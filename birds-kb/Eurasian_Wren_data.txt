<p>The <b>Eurasian wren</b> (<i>Troglodytes troglodytes</i>) is a very small <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a> bird, and the only member of the <a href="/wiki/Wren" title="Wren">wren</a> family <a href="/wiki/Troglodytidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Troglodytidae">Troglodytidae</a> found in Eurasia and Africa (<a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">Maghreb</a>). In Anglophone Europe, it is commonly known simply as the <b>wren</b>. It has a very short tail which is often held erect, a short neck and a 
relative long thin bill. It is russet brown above, paler buff-brown below and has a cream buff <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a>. The sexes are alike.
</p><p>It was once lumped with <i><a href="/wiki/Winter_wren" title="Winter wren">Troglodytes hiemalis</a></i> of eastern North America and <i><a href="/wiki/Pacific_wren" title="Pacific wren">Troglodytes pacificus</a></i> of western North America as the winter wren. The Eurasian wren occurs in Europe and across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> – including a belt of Asia from northern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> across to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> in only the northern parts of its range.<sup id="cite_ref-Brewer_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brewer-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is also highly <a href="/wiki/Polygynous" class="mw-redirect" title="Polygynous">polygynous</a>, an unusual mating system for <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerines</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian-Wren-Troglodytes-troglodytes.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Eurasian-Wren-Troglodytes-troglodytes.jpg/220px-Eurasian-Wren-Troglodytes-troglodytes.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Eurasian-Wren-Troglodytes-troglodytes.jpg/330px-Eurasian-Wren-Troglodytes-troglodytes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Eurasian-Wren-Troglodytes-troglodytes.jpg/440px-Eurasian-Wren-Troglodytes-troglodytes.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1171" data-file-height="1171" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian-Wren-Troglodytes-troglodytes.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eurasian wren in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a></div></div></div>
<p>The scientific name is taken from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> word "troglodytes" (from <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language romanization">trogle</i> a hole, and <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language romanization">dyein</i> to creep), meaning "cave-dweller", and refers to its habit of disappearing into cavities or crevices whilst hunting <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> or to roost. The <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">taxonomy</a> of the genus <i>Troglodytes</i> is currently unresolved, as recent molecular studies have suggested that  <i><a href="/wiki/Cistothorus" title="Cistothorus">Cistothorus</a></i> spp. and <i><a href="/wiki/Thryorchilus" class="mw-redirect" title="Thryorchilus">Thryorchilus</a></i> spp. are within the <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> currently defined by <i>Troglodytes</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-rice_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rice-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gomez_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gomez-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_wren&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Eurasian wren was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">tenth edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> under the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Motacilla troglodytes</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The specific epithet is from the Ancient Greek <i><i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language romanization">trōglodutēs</i></i> "cave-dweller".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In 1555 the German naturalist <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Gessner" title="Conrad Gessner">Conrad Gessner</a> had used the Latin name <i>Passer troglodyte</i> for the Eurasian wren in his <i><a href="/wiki/Historiae_animalium" class="mw-redirect" title="Historiae animalium">Historiae animalium</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The species is now placed in the genus <i>Troglodytes</i> that was introduced by the French ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Louis_Jean_Pierre_Vieillot" title="Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot">Louis Pierre Vieillot</a> in 1809.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> There are 28 recognised <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Eurasian wren was formerly considered <a href="/wiki/Conspecific" class="mw-redirect" title="Conspecific">conspecific</a> with two North American species: the <a href="/wiki/Winter_wren" title="Winter wren">winter wren</a> (<i>Troglodytes hiemalis</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_wren" title="Pacific wren">Pacific wren</a> (<i>Troglodytes pacificus</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Some ornithologists place the Eurasian wren, the winter wren and the Pacific wren in a separate genus <i>Nannus</i> that was introduced by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Gustaf_Johan_Billberg" title="Gustaf Johan Billberg">Gustaf Johan Billberg</a> in 1828 with the Eurasian wren as the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rice_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rice-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was estimated that <i>Troglodytes pacificus</i> and <i>Troglodytes troglodytes</i> last shared a <a href="/wiki/Common_ancestor" class="mw-redirect" title="Common ancestor">common ancestor</a> approximately 4.3 million years ago, long before the glacial cycles of the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>, thought to have promoted <a href="/wiki/Speciation" title="Speciation">speciation</a> in many avian lineages inhabiting the boreal forest of North America.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid15347509_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid15347509-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_wren&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Eurasian wren is a plump, sturdy bird with rounded wings and a short tail, which is usually held cocked up. The adult bird is 9 to 10&#160;cm (3.5 to 3.9&#160;in) in length and has a wingspan of 13–17&#160;cm (5.1–6.7&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1988525_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1988525-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> It weighs around 10&#160;g (0.35&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1988541_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1988541-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> It is rufous brown above, greyer beneath, and indistinctly barred with darker brown and grey, even on the wings and tail. The bill is dark brown and the legs are pale brown, the feet having strong claws and a large hind toe. Young birds are less distinctly barred and have mottled underparts.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">plumage</a> is subject to considerable variation, and where populations have been isolated, the variation has become fixed in one minor form or another. 
</p><p>There are around 28 subspecies of this taxonomically complex bird. The disputed subspecies <i><i lang="la" title="Latin language text">orii</i></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Daito_wren" title="Daito wren">Daito wren</a>, became extinct around 1940 – if it was indeed a valid taxon and not merely based on an anomaly.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Thus in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, in addition to the typical bird <i>T. t. indigenus</i>, there are three distinct insular subspecies: one, <i>T. t. hirtensis</i>, is confined to the island of <a href="/wiki/St_Kilda,_Scotland" title="St Kilda, Scotland">St. Kilda</a>; another, <i>T. t. zetlandicus</i>, to the <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland Islands</a>; and the third, <i>T. t. fridariensis</i>, to <a href="/wiki/Fair_Isle" title="Fair Isle">Fair Isle</a>. The <a href="/wiki/St_Kilda_wren" title="St Kilda wren">St. Kilda wren</a> is greyer above, whiter beneath, with more abundant bars on the back; the <a href="/wiki/Shetland_wren" title="Shetland wren">Shetland wren</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fair_Isle_wren" title="Fair Isle wren">Fair Isle wren</a> are darker.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The most common call is a sharp, repeated "tic-tic-tic". When the bird is annoyed or excited, its call runs into an emphatic <i>churr</i>, not unlike clockwork running down.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">song</a> is a gushing burst of sweet music, clear, shrill and emphatic. The male has remarkably long and complex vocalizations, with a series of tinkling trills one after the other for seconds on end. The bird has an enormous voice for its size, ten times louder, weight for weight, than a <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">cockerel</a>. The song begins with a few preliminary notes, then runs into a trill, slightly ascending, and ends in full clear notes or another trill. At all and any season, the song may be heard, though most noticeable during spring. Despite its generally mouse-like behaviour, the male may sing from an exposed low perch as its whole body quivers from the effort. Its song may sometimes be confused with that of the <a href="/wiki/Dunnock" title="Dunnock">dunnock</a>, which has a warble that is shorter and weaker. The wren's song also incorporates repeated trill sounds while the dunnock's does not.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW10_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW10-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_wren&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_Wren_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_20.10.2015.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Eurasian_Wren_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_20.10.2015.jpg/220px-Eurasian_Wren_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_20.10.2015.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Eurasian_Wren_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_20.10.2015.jpg/330px-Eurasian_Wren_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_20.10.2015.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Eurasian_Wren_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_20.10.2015.jpg/440px-Eurasian_Wren_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_20.10.2015.jpg 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_Wren_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_20.10.2015.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Subspecies <i>Troglodytes troglodytes nipalensis</i> with its very dark plumage in <a href="/wiki/Sikkim" title="Sikkim">Sikkim</a>, India<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The Eurasian wren is a <a href="/wiki/Holarctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Holarctic">Holarctic</a> species. The nominate race breeds in Europe as far north as 67°N in Norway and 64°N in Sweden, Finland and Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The bird's southern limit is northern Spain, southern France, Italy, <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> and southern Russia. It also breeds in <a href="/wiki/Western_Asia" title="Western Asia">Western Asia</a> as far east as Syria. It is replaced by other races in Iceland, the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetlands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a>, and <a href="/wiki/St_Kilda,_Scotland" title="St Kilda, Scotland">St Kilda</a>, and further south in northwestern Africa, Spain and Portugal, the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>. Other races also occur in southern Russia and Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It occupies a great variety of habitats, typically any kind of cultivated or uncultivated area with bushes and low ground cover; gardens, <a href="/wiki/Hedge" title="Hedge">hedgerows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thicket" title="Thicket">thickets</a>, plantations, woodland and <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed beds</a>. It inhabits more open locations with clumps of <a href="/wiki/Bramble" title="Bramble">brambles</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ulex_europaeus" title="Ulex europaeus">gorse</a>, rough pasture, moorland, boulder-strewn slopes, rocky coasts and sea cliffs.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_18-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The wren is an ever-active bird, constantly on the move foraging for insects, in the open or among thick vegetation. It moves with quick jerks, probing into crevices, examining old masonry, hopping onto fallen logs and delving down among them. It sometimes moves higher in the canopy, but for the most part stays near the ground, often being flushed from under overhangs on banks. Sometimes it hops up the lower part of tree trunks, behaving like a miniature <a href="/wiki/Nuthatch" title="Nuthatch">nuthatch</a>. Occasionally it flits away, its short flights swift and direct but not sustained, its tiny round wings whirring as it flies.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_18-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This small, stump-tailed wren is almost as familiar in Europe as the <a href="/wiki/European_robin" title="European robin">robin</a>. It is mouse-like, easily lost sight of when it is hunting for food, but is found everywhere from the tops of the highest moors to the sea coast.
</p><p>It is a bird of the uplands even in winter, vanishing into the heather when snow lies thick above, a troglodyte indeed. It frequents gardens and farms, but it is quite as abundant in thick woods and in reed-beds.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At night, usually in winter, it often roosts, true to its scientific name, in dark retreats, snug holes and even old nests. In hard weather, it may do so in parties, consisting of either the family or of many individuals gathered together for warmth.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_wren&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Troglodytes_troglodytes_kabylorum_Hartert,_E,_1910_T%C3%A9bessa_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_232.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Troglodytes_troglodytes_kabylorum_Hartert%2C_E%2C_1910_T%C3%A9bessa_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_232.jpg/180px-Troglodytes_troglodytes_kabylorum_Hartert%2C_E%2C_1910_T%C3%A9bessa_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_232.jpg" decoding="async" width="180" height="180" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Troglodytes_troglodytes_kabylorum_Hartert%2C_E%2C_1910_T%C3%A9bessa_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_232.jpg/270px-Troglodytes_troglodytes_kabylorum_Hartert%2C_E%2C_1910_T%C3%A9bessa_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_232.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Troglodytes_troglodytes_kabylorum_Hartert%2C_E%2C_1910_T%C3%A9bessa_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_232.jpg/360px-Troglodytes_troglodytes_kabylorum_Hartert%2C_E%2C_1910_T%C3%A9bessa_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_232.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3327" data-file-height="3334" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Troglodytes_troglodytes_kabylorum_Hartert,_E,_1910_T%C3%A9bessa_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_232.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Zaunkoenig_alleinerziehend.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Zaunkoenig_alleinerziehend.jpg/220px-Zaunkoenig_alleinerziehend.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="217" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Zaunkoenig_alleinerziehend.jpg/330px-Zaunkoenig_alleinerziehend.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Zaunkoenig_alleinerziehend.jpg/440px-Zaunkoenig_alleinerziehend.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1578" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Zaunkoenig_alleinerziehend.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult with four hatchlings; one has just been fed a spider or harvestman</div></div></div>
<p>In most of northern Europe and Asia, it nests mostly in <a href="/wiki/Coniferous_forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Coniferous forest">coniferous forests</a>, where it is often identified by its long and exuberant song.  Although it is an <a href="/wiki/Insectivore" title="Insectivore">insectivore</a>, it can remain in moderately cold and even snowy climates by foraging for insects on substrates such as bark and fallen logs. The male wren builds several <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nests</a> in his territory; these are called "cock nests" but are never lined until the female chooses one to use. The number of nests on a territory influences the female's choice of mate; she preferentially mates with a male that had constructed numerous nests.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Courtship includes display and posturing by the male. He sings with wings and tail half open, or with them drooping, sometimes with one wing extended, or the wings may be raised and lowered several times in quick succession.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_18-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The neatly-domed nest has a side entrance and is built of <a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">grass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">moss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichen</a> and dead leaves, whatever is available locally. It is often tucked into a hole in a wall or tree trunk or a crack in a rock, but it is often built in <a href="/wiki/Bramble" title="Bramble">brambles</a>, a bush or a hedge, among <a href="/wiki/Ivy" class="mw-redirect" title="Ivy">ivy</a> on a bank, in thatch, or in abandoned bird's nests such as those of the <a href="/wiki/House_sparrow" title="House sparrow">house sparrow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barn_swallow" title="Barn swallow">swallow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_house_martin" title="Common house martin">house martin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dipper" title="Dipper">dipper</a>. On making her selection, the female wren lines the nest generously with feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_18-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A clutch of five or six (range three to eleven) <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> are laid from April onwards. These average 16.6 by 12.7&#160;mm (0.65 by 0.50&#160;in) and are white with variable amounts of reddish-brown speckles, mostly on the broad end. The female alone incubates these, and they hatch after 14 to 15 days. The young are fed on insects, spiders and other small invertebrates; there is no record of the male feeding the young in the nest, but he does do so after they have fledged, which happens after about 16 days. There are usually two broods.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_18-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Wrens are highly <a href="/wiki/Polygamous" class="mw-redirect" title="Polygamous">polygamous</a>, that is to say a male can have, at any one time, more than one female with an active nest on his territory. An active nest is one in which there are eggs or nestlings. A male has been recorded with four females breeding on his territory.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Bigamy and trigamy are the most common forms of polygamy.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_feeding">Food and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_wren&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Food and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Insects form the bulk of the diet; these are chiefly the larvae of <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterflies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moths</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Geometer_moth" title="Geometer moth">geometer moths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Noctuidae" title="Noctuidae">owlet moths</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetle</a> larvae, <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">fly</a> larvae, <a href="/wiki/Caddisfly" title="Caddisfly">caddisfly</a> larvae and <a href="/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">aphids</a>. Other dietary items include spiders, and some seeds are also taken. The young are largely fed on moth larvae, with <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cabbage_moth" title="Cabbage moth">cabbage moth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crane_fly" title="Crane fly">crane fly</a> larvae having been identified.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_18-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_wren&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:British_farthing_1951_reverse.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/British_farthing_1951_reverse.png/150px-British_farthing_1951_reverse.png" decoding="async" width="150" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/British_farthing_1951_reverse.png/225px-British_farthing_1951_reverse.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/British_farthing_1951_reverse.png/300px-British_farthing_1951_reverse.png 2x" data-file-width="1900" data-file-height="1900" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:British_farthing_1951_reverse.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wren on a British farthing coin</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Faroe_stamp_345_wren_(troglodytes_troglodytes).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Faroe_stamp_345_wren_%28troglodytes_troglodytes%29.jpg/180px-Faroe_stamp_345_wren_%28troglodytes_troglodytes%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="180" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Faroe_stamp_345_wren_%28troglodytes_troglodytes%29.jpg/270px-Faroe_stamp_345_wren_%28troglodytes_troglodytes%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fd/Faroe_stamp_345_wren_%28troglodytes_troglodytes%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="295" data-file-height="363" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Faroe_stamp_345_wren_(troglodytes_troglodytes).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wrens on a stamp from the Faroe Islands</div></div></div>
<p>In European folklore, the wren is the king of the birds, according to a fable attributed to <a href="/wiki/Aesop" title="Aesop">Aesop</a> by <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> when the <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagle</a> and the wren strove to fly the highest, the wren rested on the eagle's back, and when the eagle tired, the wren flew out above him.  Thus, Plutarch implied, the wren proved that cleverness is better than strength.  The wren's majesty is recognized in such stories as the <a href="/wiki/Brothers_Grimm" title="Brothers Grimm">Grimm Brothers'</a> <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Willow-Wren_and_the_Bear&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Willow-Wren and the Bear (page does not exist)">The Willow-Wren and the Bear</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a> called the wren <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language romanization">basileus</i> (king) and <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language romanization">basiliskos</i> (little king). In German, the wren is called <i>Zaunkönig</i> (king of the fence). An old German name was “Schneekönig” (snow king), and in Dutch, it is “winterkoning” (winter king), which all refer to <i>king</i>. In Japan, the wren is labelled king of the winds, and the myth of <i>The Wren Among the Hawks</i> sees the wren successfully hunt a boar that the hawks could not, by flying into its ear and driving it mad.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was a sacred bird to the <a href="/wiki/Druid" title="Druid">druids</a>, who considered it "king of all birds",<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and used its musical notes for divination.  The shape-shifting Fairy Queen took the form of a wren, known as "Jenny Wren" in nursery rhymes.  A wren's feather was thought to be a charm against disaster or drowning.
</p><p>The wren also features in the legend of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Stephen" title="Saint Stephen">Saint Stephen</a>, the first Christian <a href="/wiki/Martyr" title="Martyr">martyr</a>, who supposedly was betrayed by the noisy bird as he attempted to hide from his enemies.  Traditionally, <a href="/wiki/St._Stephen%27s_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Stephen&#39;s Day">St. Stephen's Day</a> (26 December) has been commemorated by <i><a href="/wiki/Wren_Day" title="Wren Day">Hunting the Wren</a></i>, wherein young wrenboys would catch the bird and then ritually parade it around town, as described in the traditional "Wren Song". <i>The Wren, the Wren, the king of all birds, St. Stephen's day was caught in the furze. Although he is little, his family's great, I pray you, good landlady, give us a treat</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hymn_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hymn-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The tradition, and the significance of the wren as a symbol and sacrifice of the old year, is discussed in Sir James Frazer's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Golden_Bough" title="The Golden Bough">The Golden Bough</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Frazer_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frazer-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Suetonius" title="Suetonius">Suetonius</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_Julius_Caesar" title="Assassination of Julius Caesar">assassination of Julius Caesar</a> was foretold by an unfortunate wren. On the day before the <a href="/wiki/Ides_of_March" title="Ides of March">Ides of March</a>, a wren was seen being pursued in a frenzy by various other birds. With a conspicuous sprig of <a href="/wiki/Bay_laurel" class="mw-redirect" title="Bay laurel">laurel</a> clamped in its beak, the wren flew desperately into the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Senate" title="Roman Senate">Roman Senate</a>, but there its pursuers overtook it and tore it to pieces.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_depictions">Cultural depictions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_wren&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cultural depictions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jungle_Book" title="The Jungle Book">The Jungle Book</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Rudyard_Kipling" title="Rudyard Kipling">Rudyard Kipling</a>, Limmershin, the <a href="/wiki/Winter_wren" title="Winter wren">winter wren</a>, who was once thought of as the same species as the Eurasian wren, is the narrator of "The White <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">Seal</a>" story.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Wrens have been featured on stamps from Albania, Albany, Belarus, Belgium, Bulgaria, Faroe Islands, France, Germany, Britain, Guernsey, Hungary, Iceland, Ireland, Isle of Man, Jersey, Netherlands, San Mario, USA and The Vatican.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The Wren is one of the animals of the Celtic zodiac.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The old British <a href="/wiki/Farthing_(British_coin)" title="Farthing (British coin)">farthing</a> coin featured a wren on the reverse side from 1937 to 1960. The wren was chosen because it was thought of as Britain's smallest bird.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
