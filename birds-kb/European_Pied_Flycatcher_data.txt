<p>The <b>European pied flycatcher</b> (<i>Ficedula hypoleuca</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a> family. One of the four species of <a href="/wiki/Western_Palearctic_black-and-white_flycatchers" title="Western Palearctic black-and-white flycatchers">Western Palearctic black-and-white flycatchers</a>, it <a href="/wiki/Bird_hybrid" title="Bird hybrid">hybridizes</a> to a limited extent with the <a href="/wiki/Collared_flycatcher" title="Collared flycatcher">collared flycatcher</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Silverin_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Silverin-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It breeds in most of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and across the Western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>. It is migratory, wintering mainly in tropical <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-habitat_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-habitat-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  It usually builds its nests in holes on oak trees.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
This species practices <a href="/wiki/Polygyny" title="Polygyny">polygyny</a>, usually bigamy, with the male travelling large distances to acquire a second mate. The male will mate with the secondary female and then return to the primary female in order to help with aspects of child rearing, such as feeding.<sup id="cite_ref-Silverin_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Silverin-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vonHaartman_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vonHaartman-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The European pied flycatcher is mainly <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a>, although its diet also includes other <a href="/wiki/Arthropods" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthropods">arthropods</a>. This species commonly feeds on spiders, ants, bees and similar <a href="/wiki/Prey" class="mw-redirect" title="Prey">prey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Silverina_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Silverina-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The European pied flycatcher has a very large range and population size and so it is of least concern according to the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN).<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_pied_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European pied flycatcher is an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a>, part of a family of <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a> songbirds which typically feed by darting after insects.<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The Latin word <i>ficedula</i> means “small fig-eating bird”. The term <i>hypoleuca</i> comes from two Greek roots, <i>hupo</i>, “below”, and <i>leukos</i>, “white”.<sup id="cite_ref-habitat_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-habitat-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The species was described in <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus's</a> Fauna Svecica (1746), a work that was not binomial and that is therefore unavailable nomenclaturally. Later, in the tenth edition of his <a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a>
and the next edition of Fauna Svecica (1761), Linnaeus confounded this flycatcher with the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_blackcap" title="Eurasian blackcap">Eurasian blackcap</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Whinchat" title="Whinchat">Whinchat</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Salvador_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salvador-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> To this point, the European Pied Flycatcher still lacked a proper valid binominal name. The species was finally named as <i>Motacilla hypoleuca</i> by German naturalist <a href="/wiki/Peter_Simon_Pallas" title="Peter Simon Pallas">Peter Simon Pallas</a> in 1764. However, he described this species anonymously in the appendix of a sales catalogue of the collection of Adriaan Vroeg, popularly known simply as the “Adumbratiunculae” among ornithologists.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The authorship of the Adumbratiunculae could later be attributed to Pallas.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Given the initial anonymity of the publication and the inferred authorship by external evidence, the <a href="/wiki/International_Code_of_Zoological_Nomenclature" title="International Code of Zoological Nomenclature">International Code of Zoological Nomenclature</a> advocates that Pallas's name should appear enclosed in square brackets in the species' name. Thus, the correct form of the scientific name of the European pied plycatcher is <i>Ficedula hypoleuca</i> ([Pallas], 1764).<sup id="cite_ref-Salvador_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salvador-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Ficedula hypoleuca</i> currently has four recognized subspecies: the nominate <i>F. h. hypoleuca</i> ([Pallas], 1764), <i>F. h. speculigera</i> (Bonaparte, 1850), <i>F. h. iberiae</i> (Witherby, 1928), and <i>F. h. tomensis</i> (Johansen, 1916).<sup id="cite_ref-Salvador_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salvador-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The subspecies <i>F. h. muscipeta</i> (Bechstein, 1792) is currently considered synonymous with <i>F. h. hypoleuca</i>, but could represent an actual distinct subspecies. The name <i>F. h. atricapilla</i> (Linnaeus, 1766) is a junior subjective synonym of <i>F. h. hypoleuca</i>; and the name <i>F. h. sibirica</i> Khakhlov, 1915 is invalid, the correct form being <i>F. h. tomensis</i> (Johansen, 1916).<sup id="cite_ref-Salvador_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salvador-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_pied_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ficedula_hypoleuca_IMG_4747.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Ficedula_hypoleuca_IMG_4747.JPG/220px-Ficedula_hypoleuca_IMG_4747.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Ficedula_hypoleuca_IMG_4747.JPG/330px-Ficedula_hypoleuca_IMG_4747.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Ficedula_hypoleuca_IMG_4747.JPG/440px-Ficedula_hypoleuca_IMG_4747.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1727" data-file-height="1153" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ficedula_hypoleuca_IMG_4747.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult female in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>This is a 12–13.5 centimetres (4.7–5.3&#160;in) long bird. The breeding male is mainly black above and white below, with a large white wing patch, white tail sides and a small forehead patch. The <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian</a> <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> <i>iberiae</i> (known as <b>Iberian pied flycatcher</b>) has a larger forehead patch and a pale rump. Non-breeding males, females and juveniles have the black replaced by a pale brown, and may be very difficult to distinguish from other <i>Ficedula</i> flycatchers, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Collared_flycatcher" title="Collared flycatcher">collared flycatcher</a>, with which this species <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridizes</a> to a limited extent.<sup id="cite_ref-parkin_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-parkin-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The bill is black, and has the broad but pointed shape typical of aerial insectivores. As well as taking insects in flight, this species hunts <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a> amongst the oak foliage, and will take berries. It is therefore a much earlier spring migrant than the more aerial <a href="/wiki/Spotted_flycatcher" title="Spotted flycatcher">spotted flycatcher</a>, and its loud rhythmic and melodious song is characteristic of oak woods in spring.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><div class="mediaContainer" style="width:220px"><audio id="mwe_player_3" controls="" preload="none" style="width:220px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="12.011895833333" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="Ficedula_hypoleuca.ogg" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ef/Ficedula_hypoleuca.ogg" type="audio/ogg; codecs=&quot;vorbis&quot;" data-title="Original Ogg file (201 kbps)" data-shorttitle="Ogg source" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="201248" /><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/e/ef/Ficedula_hypoleuca.ogg/Ficedula_hypoleuca.ogg.mp3" type="audio/mpeg" data-title="MP3" data-shorttitle="MP3" data-transcodekey="mp3" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="227424" /></audio></div>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ficedula_hypoleuca.ogg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>European pied flycatcher vocalization</div></div></div> 
<p>They are birds of deciduous woodlands, parks and gardens, with a preference for <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> trees. They build an open nest in a tree hole, and will readily adapt to an open-fronted nest box. 4&#8211;10 eggs are laid.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The very similar <a href="/wiki/Atlas_pied_flycatcher" title="Atlas pied flycatcher">Atlas pied flycatcher</a>, of the mountains of north west <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> was formerly classed as subspecies of the European pied flycatcher.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_pied_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European pied flycatcher has a very large range and population size, and is thus deemed to be of least concern by the IUCN. This species occupies areas of many different countries in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, also being present in the <a href="/wiki/East_European_Plain" title="East European Plain">west Asian portion of Russia</a>. More specifically, the nominate subspecies <i>F. h. hypoleuca</i> inhabits the UK, central Europe and Scandinavia, <i>F. h. speculigera</i> inhabits Morocco, Algeria and Tunisia, <i>F. h. iberiae</i> inhabits in the Iberian Peninsula, and <i>F. h. tomensis</i> in eastern Europe and Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-Salvador_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salvador-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The species is noted as a vagrant species in places in other countries in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> This flycatcher typically spends winter in tropical Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-habitat_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-habitat-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Salvador_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salvador-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The European pied flycatcher is a terrestrial bird,<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> typically inhabiting open forests, woodlands, and towns. In 2005, the European population was listed to hold 3&#8211;7 million pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-habitat_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-habitat-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mating_systems">Mating systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_pied_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mating systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European pied flycatcher predominately practices a mixed mating system of <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polygyny" title="Polygyny">polygyny</a>. Their mating system has also been described as successive polygyny.<sup id="cite_ref-vonHaartman_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vonHaartman-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Within the latter system, the males leave their home territory once their primary mates lays their first eggs. Males then create a second territory, presumably in order to attract a secondary female to breed. Even when they succeed at acquiring a second mate, the males typically return to the first female to exclusively provide for her and her offspring.<sup id="cite_ref-Silverin_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Silverin-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Males will sometimes care for both mates if the nests of the primary and secondary female are close together. The male may also care for both mates once the offspring of the primary female have <a href="/wiki/Fledged" class="mw-redirect" title="Fledged">fledged</a>. The male bird usually does not exceed two mates, practicing <a href="/wiki/Bigamy" title="Bigamy">bigamy</a>. Only two cases of trigyny had been observed.<sup id="cite_ref-atalalo_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atalalo-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_difference_in_mating_behavior">Gender difference in mating behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_pied_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gender difference in mating behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The male mating behavior has two key characteristics: desertion of the primary female and <a href="/wiki/Polyterritoriality" class="mw-redirect" title="Polyterritoriality">polyterritoriality</a>. The males travel large distances, an average of 200–3,500 metres (660–11,480&#160;ft), to find his second mate. After breeding with the secondary female, the males return to their first mate. The males of this species are polyterritorial; the males will acquire multiple nest sites to attract a female. Upon breeding with this first female, the male will procure more nesting sites, typically some distance from the site of the primary female, in order to attract a second female for mating. The males that have better success at polygyny are typically larger, older and more experienced at arriving earlier to the mating sites.<sup id="cite_ref-jstor_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jstor-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Polyganythresholdmodel_1.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fd/Polyganythresholdmodel_1.svg/220px-Polyganythresholdmodel_1.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fd/Polyganythresholdmodel_1.svg/330px-Polyganythresholdmodel_1.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fd/Polyganythresholdmodel_1.svg/440px-Polyganythresholdmodel_1.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="282" data-file-height="257" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Polyganythresholdmodel_1.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Polygyny threshold model graph</div></div></div>
<p>The female behaviour has also been studied in depth, especially due to the fact that some females accept polygyny while others are able to maintain monogamous relationships. The first female in a polygynous relationship does not suffer much in comparison to females in monogamous situations. These primary females gain greater reproductive success because they are able to secure full-time help from the male once he returns from his search for a second mate. The second female, however, often suffers from polygyny. These females have 60% less offspring than females that are in a monogamous relationship.<sup id="cite_ref-Lundberg_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lundberg-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> These findings are consistent with the <a href="/wiki/Polygyny_threshold_model" title="Polygyny threshold model">polygyny threshold model</a>, which is depicted at the left. Additionally, the secondary female lays a smaller clutch which she is more likely to be able to rear on her own.
</p><p>Another behavior that is relatively frequent in European pied flycatchers is the practice of <a href="/wiki/Extra-pair_copulation" title="Extra-pair copulation">extra-pair copulations</a> (EPC). Thus, the male practicing EPC will have a group of offspring raised successfully without any parental investment on his part. The female may benefit from EPC if the second male is judged to have superior genes to the original male. Another benefit that EPC adds is that there is an increase in <a href="/wiki/Genetic_variability" title="Genetic variability">genetic variability</a>. However, females are not typically very welcoming of EPC. A female that is being pursued for an EPC will either passively allow the male to copulate with her, or will resist it and risk injury due to the male's aggression.<sup id="cite_ref-Alatalalundberg1987_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alatalalundberg1987-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding_dispersal">Breeding dispersal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_pied_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding dispersal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In an experiment conducted from 1948 to 1964 in the <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Dean" title="Forest of Dean">Forest of Dean</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a>, two hundred and fifty nest boxes were carefully recorded for their locations and then analyzed for their inhabitance.<sup id="cite_ref-Harveyetal_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harveyetal-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The median breeding dispersal (the distance between successive nests) of the European pied flycatcher ranges from about 52–133 metres (171–436&#160;ft), with the average distance between nest sites being about 45 metres (148&#160;ft). This distance typically depended on the breeding density in each year. The study found little evidence to suggest a difference in breeding dispersal between years or between monogamous and polygynous males. As a result, the data for the separate categories could be combined. The breeding dispersal over longer distances could result in both mate <a href="/wiki/Fidelity" title="Fidelity">fidelity</a> as well as mate change, the latter of which occurs either while the previous mate is still alive, or following the death of the mate. The breeding dispersal distances of birds that survive more than three breeding seasons were studied, and the results showed that the site fidelity increased with more successive <a href="/wiki/Animal_husbandry" title="Animal husbandry">breeding</a> attempts. The same long-term study also found that older European pied flycatchers, both male and female, were more likely to move shorter distances between breeding seasons than younger birds were. When mates were observed to re-establish their pair bond, they tended to occupy certain areas that were near the nest site established in the previous breeding season. In addition, female birds were less likely to return to a former breeding site following the death of, or divorce from, their former partner. When a pair divorces, the females have been observed to move greater distances away than the males. As a result, females that keep the same mates from year to year end up moving shorter distances for each mating period than those that divorce. Divorce has little influence on the likelihood of males moving away from their original nest site. The study found that males that keep the same mate do not move significantly smaller distances than males that divorce.<sup id="cite_ref-Harveyetal_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harveyetal-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_of_polygyny">Evolution of polygyny</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_pied_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Evolution of polygyny">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since most bird species exhibit monogamous mating behaviors, the polygynous behavior of the European pied flycatcher has sparked much research. There are three main hypotheses that seek to explain why females settle polygynously when it lowers their overall <a href="/wiki/Fitness_(biology)" title="Fitness (biology)">fitness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_success" title="Reproductive success">reproductive success</a> compared to a monogamous relationship.<sup id="cite_ref-Orians_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Orians-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id=".22Sexy_son.22_hypothesis"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Sexy_son&quot;_hypothesis">"Sexy son" hypothesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_pied_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: &quot;Sexy son&quot; hypothesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ficedula_hypoleuca_Haukipudas_20120610_02.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Ficedula_hypoleuca_Haukipudas_20120610_02.JPG/220px-Ficedula_hypoleuca_Haukipudas_20120610_02.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Ficedula_hypoleuca_Haukipudas_20120610_02.JPG/330px-Ficedula_hypoleuca_Haukipudas_20120610_02.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Ficedula_hypoleuca_Haukipudas_20120610_02.JPG/440px-Ficedula_hypoleuca_Haukipudas_20120610_02.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2027" data-file-height="1519" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ficedula_hypoleuca_Haukipudas_20120610_02.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult male in Finland</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ficedula_hypoleuca_-Finland_-female-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Ficedula_hypoleuca_-Finland_-female-8.jpg/220px-Ficedula_hypoleuca_-Finland_-female-8.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Ficedula_hypoleuca_-Finland_-female-8.jpg/330px-Ficedula_hypoleuca_-Finland_-female-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Ficedula_hypoleuca_-Finland_-female-8.jpg/440px-Ficedula_hypoleuca_-Finland_-female-8.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5184" data-file-height="3456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ficedula_hypoleuca_-Finland_-female-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult female in Finland</div></div></div>
<p>The first hypothesis is the <a href="/wiki/Sexy_son_hypothesis" title="Sexy son hypothesis">"sexy son" hypothesis</a> which asserts that although females experience an initial reproductive loss with their first generation, the reproductive success of the second generation compensates for the initial loss. The second generation of males is thought to be privileged because it will inherit the increased mating ability, or attractiveness, from their fathers and thus will have high success in procuring mates upon maturation. Since these "sexy sons" are projected to have heightened reproductive success, the secondary female's reproductive success in turn improves.<sup id="cite_ref-Weatherhead1979_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weatherhead1979-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Some researchers, however, have refuted this theory, stating that offspring born to secondary females suffered from poor nutrition, which resulted in shorter <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsi</a> and lower weights than the progeny of primary and monogamous females. These phenotypic traits contribute to lesser success in mate acquisition, rejecting the “sexy son” hypothesis.<sup id="cite_ref-atalalo_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atalalo-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Deception_hypothesis">Deception hypothesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_pied_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Deception hypothesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The second hypothesis claims that <a href="/wiki/Deception" title="Deception">deception</a> from the male flycatcher explains a female's choice to mate with an already-mated male despite the relative decrease in reproductive success.<sup id="cite_ref-jstor_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jstor-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The deception arises from the <a href="/wiki/Polyterritoriality" class="mw-redirect" title="Polyterritoriality">polyterritoriality</a> of the males, meaning that the males are able to deceive the females through the use of separate territories. This hypothesis attempts to describe why males have developed polyterritorial behavior. The typical long distances between nest sites suggest that males acquire multiple nest sites to facilitate the deception of the secondary female.<sup id="cite_ref-atalalo_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atalalo-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> A study showed that females leave the male upon discovering that he is already mated, as long as she discovers this fact before laying season.<sup id="cite_ref-jstor_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jstor-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> However, another experiment with European pied flycatchers in Norway produced results that refute the deception hypothesis.<sup id="cite_ref-stenmarks_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stenmarks-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The secondary female birds in their study raised larger clutches than primary females. The study also showed that deception is not an <a href="/wiki/Evolutionarily_stable_strategy" title="Evolutionarily stable strategy">evolutionarily stable strategy</a> for males, because secondary females would notice the frequent visits to the primary females and then elect to choose another mate. According to the deception hypothesis, already-mated males display polyterritorial behavior that increases their chances of acquiring another mate. Unmated males were shown to display mating behavior, consisting mostly of singing, at their nest site. On the other hand, already-mated males would need to disrupt their singing at their secondary territories in order to return to their primary nest. This can occur both before and after the time of their second mating.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> As a result, it decreases the chance that females would be deceived, leading to an evolutionarily unstable strategy.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Female_aggression_hypothesis">Female aggression hypothesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_pied_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Female aggression hypothesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Trauerschn%C3%A4pper_Weibchen_Ficedula_hypoleuca_Schlehdorf_Kochelsee-001.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Trauerschn%C3%A4pper_Weibchen_Ficedula_hypoleuca_Schlehdorf_Kochelsee-001.jpg/220px-Trauerschn%C3%A4pper_Weibchen_Ficedula_hypoleuca_Schlehdorf_Kochelsee-001.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Trauerschn%C3%A4pper_Weibchen_Ficedula_hypoleuca_Schlehdorf_Kochelsee-001.jpg/330px-Trauerschn%C3%A4pper_Weibchen_Ficedula_hypoleuca_Schlehdorf_Kochelsee-001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Trauerschn%C3%A4pper_Weibchen_Ficedula_hypoleuca_Schlehdorf_Kochelsee-001.jpg/440px-Trauerschn%C3%A4pper_Weibchen_Ficedula_hypoleuca_Schlehdorf_Kochelsee-001.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2840" data-file-height="2130" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Trauerschn%C3%A4pper_Weibchen_Ficedula_hypoleuca_Schlehdorf_Kochelsee-001.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult female at the <a href="/wiki/Kochelsee" title="Kochelsee">Kochelsee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schlehdorf" title="Schlehdorf">Schlehdorf</a>, Germany</div></div></div>
<p>The third hypothesis asserts that females settle for polygyny because it is hard to find unmated males.<sup id="cite_ref-stenmarks_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stenmarks-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> This theory assumes that there is aggression between females to find mates and asserts that polyterritoriality actually helps to alleviate this aggression, allowing the second female a place to settle and breed peacefully.<sup id="cite_ref-DaviesNicholas_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DaviesNicholas-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Although the deception hypothesis suggests that males are more successful at farther secondary territories because they can hide their marital status, the female-female aggression suggests that males occupy distant secondary territories to reduce aggression between the primary and secondary females. Primary females display aggression and prevent other females from settling near the initial nest to ensure that they acquire the male <a href="/wiki/Parental_care" title="Parental care">parental care</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Primary females were seen in experiments to visit the second territory and behave aggressively towards the secondary female. The number of such visits decreased with increasing distance between the nests. It is also important for the primary female to be able to detect an intruding female as soon as possible, because the longer the intruder has been present in a territory, the more difficult it will be to evict the female. Female flycatchers are known to have the capacity to identify the songs of their own mates and check if they establish a second territory. The primary male was also shown to spend less time in the second territories during incubation periods than before they acquired a secondary mate, especially with greater distances between the two territories.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parental_care">Parental care</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_pied_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Parental care">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Male_Ficedula_Hypoleuca_at_nest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Male_Ficedula_Hypoleuca_at_nest.jpg/220px-Male_Ficedula_Hypoleuca_at_nest.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="258" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Male_Ficedula_Hypoleuca_at_nest.jpg/330px-Male_Ficedula_Hypoleuca_at_nest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Male_Ficedula_Hypoleuca_at_nest.jpg/440px-Male_Ficedula_Hypoleuca_at_nest.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1145" data-file-height="1345" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Male_Ficedula_Hypoleuca_at_nest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Male flycatcher returning to nest</div></div></div>
<p>Studies were also done to examine the amount of contribution the male European pied flycatcher provided in <a href="/wiki/Parental_investment" title="Parental investment">parental care</a> as well as why some females choose to mate with mated males.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> When older and younger monogamous males were compared, there was no difference in feeding rate between each <a href="/wiki/Nest" title="Nest">nest</a>. When females were studied, scientists found that monogamous and primary females benefited significantly more from the male in terms of parental care than polygynous females did. The latter group could only partially compensate for the absence of a male, leading to secondary females and widows raising fewer offspring than the monogamous pairs did. In the study, differences in mates and the qualities of the territories were slight and therefore not considered, since they lead to no advantages for females to choose between the territories belonging to monogamous or already-mated males. The results of the study suggest that the males can control multiple territories and are thus able to deceive females into accepting polygyny, while the females do not have enough time to discover the marital status of the males.
</p><p>In terms of male parental care to clutches, the rate of male <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" class="mw-redirect" title="Avian incubation">incubation</a> feeding was directly related to the physical condition of the males, and negatively correlated with the ambient temperature. Polygynously mated females also received far less feeds than monogamously mated females, despite having no difference in the food delivery rates by the male. The reduction in delivery rate to the polygynously mated females led to a negative effect on their incubation efficiency, because the females needed to spend more time away from the nest acquiring food. This also prolonged the incubation period when compared to monogamous females. The male feeding behavior is related to the reproductive value as represented by the nests, as well as to the costs and benefits of incubation feeding.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_pied_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main diet of the European pied flycatcher is insects. In fact, their name comes from their habit of catching flying insects, but they also catch insects or arthropods from tree trunks, branches, or from the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Studies have found that the majority of food catches were made from the ground. It was also found that airborne prey were captured more during the early part of the season (May to June) than in the later part (August to September); the converse trend appeared in prey taken from trees. There are also many overlaps in the foraging techniques with the <a href="/wiki/Collared_flycatcher" title="Collared flycatcher">collared flycatcher</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Spotted_flycatcher" title="Spotted flycatcher">spotted flycatcher</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Common_redstart" title="Common redstart">common redstart</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Silverina_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Silverina-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vony_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vony-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Courtship_feeding" class="mw-redirect" title="Courtship feeding">Courtship feeding</a>, or incubation feeding, occurs when the male feeds the female in the pairing, egglaying stages, and incubation. An interpretation of this behavior is that it strengthens the pair bond between mates.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_pied_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ficedula_speculigera_MHNT_232_Oued_B%C3%A9char_Alg%C3%A9rie.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Ficedula_speculigera_MHNT_232_Oued_B%C3%A9char_Alg%C3%A9rie.jpg/220px-Ficedula_speculigera_MHNT_232_Oued_B%C3%A9char_Alg%C3%A9rie.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="189" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Ficedula_speculigera_MHNT_232_Oued_B%C3%A9char_Alg%C3%A9rie.jpg/330px-Ficedula_speculigera_MHNT_232_Oued_B%C3%A9char_Alg%C3%A9rie.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Ficedula_speculigera_MHNT_232_Oued_B%C3%A9char_Alg%C3%A9rie.jpg/440px-Ficedula_speculigera_MHNT_232_Oued_B%C3%A9char_Alg%C3%A9rie.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3481" data-file-height="2990" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ficedula_speculigera_MHNT_232_Oued_B%C3%A9char_Alg%C3%A9rie.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs off <i>Ficedula speculigera</i> <a href="/wiki/MHNT" class="mw-redirect" title="MHNT">MHNT</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ficedula_hypoleuca-chicks2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Ficedula_hypoleuca-chicks2.jpg/220px-Ficedula_hypoleuca-chicks2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="179" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Ficedula_hypoleuca-chicks2.jpg/330px-Ficedula_hypoleuca-chicks2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Ficedula_hypoleuca-chicks2.jpg/440px-Ficedula_hypoleuca-chicks2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1180" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ficedula_hypoleuca-chicks2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pied flycatcher chicks</div></div></div>
<p>The diet of the European pied flycatcher is composed nearly entirely of insects. One study analyzed the stomach contents of birds during the breeding season and found that <a href="/wiki/Ants" class="mw-redirect" title="Ants">ants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bees" class="mw-redirect" title="Bees">bees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wasps" class="mw-redirect" title="Wasps">wasps</a>  and <a href="/wiki/Beetles" class="mw-redirect" title="Beetles">beetles</a> made up the main diet.<sup id="cite_ref-Silverina_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Silverina-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Ants made up approximately 25% of the diet.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
Food given to nestlings include <a href="/wiki/Spiders" class="mw-redirect" title="Spiders">spiders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Butterflies" class="mw-redirect" title="Butterflies">butterflies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moths" class="mw-redirect" title="Moths">moths</a>, flies, mosquitoes, ants, bees, wasps, and beetles. For <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">Lepidoptera</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">Hymenoptera</a>, larvae appear to be consumed more than adult insects; the opposite is true for other insect species.<sup id="cite_ref-vony_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vony-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> There is also variation between the proportions of larvae and adult insects between different habitats. Nestlings were also found to consume more spiders, butterfly, and moth larvae, while adult flycatchers consume more ants.<sup id="cite_ref-Silverina_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Silverina-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_pied_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It has on average decreased in population by 25% within the last 25 years. It has ceased to breed in several parts of its former range within <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a>. (It is a very rare and irregular breeder in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, with only one or two pairs recorded as breeding in most years.)<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Records of its location can be found on that <a href="/wiki/National_Biodiversity_Network" title="National Biodiversity Network">National Biodiversity Network</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> it have declined by 90% due to nestlings peaks mistiming.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lifecycle">Lifecycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_pied_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Lifecycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<ul><li>mid-September to mid-April: lives in sub Saharan <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a></li>
<li>mid April to end of May: migrates and arrives in countries such as the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a></li>
<li>June to August: breeding season, one <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">brood</a> only</li>
<li>August to mid September: flies back to sub Saharan Africa</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Management_and_conservation">Management and conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_pied_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Management and conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>They breed in upland broadleaf woodland. This means that in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a> they are limited due to geography to the North and West. They prefer mature <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> woodland, but also breed in mature upland <a href="/wiki/Fraxinus" title="Fraxinus">ash</a> and <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a> woods.
</p><p>They require very high horizontal visibility - a low abundance of shrub and understorey, but with high proportion of <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">moss</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grass" class="mw-redirect" title="Grass">grass</a>. <a href="/wiki/Grazing" title="Grazing">Grazing</a> needs to be managed to maintain this open character, but also allow the occasional replacement trees.
</p><p>They will sometimes use mature open <a href="/wiki/Conifer" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifer">conifer</a> woodland where natural tree holes occur.  Generally they prefer trees that have tree holes, i.e. dead trees, or dead limbs on healthy trees.  They also like <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichens</a> that grow on trees.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Grant_funding_for_conservation">Grant funding for conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_pied_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Grant funding for conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Forestry_Commission" title="Forestry Commission">Forestry Commission</a> offers grants under a scheme called England's Woodland Improvement Grant (EWIG); as does <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Stewardship" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental Stewardship">Environmental Stewardship</a> Scheme.
</p>
