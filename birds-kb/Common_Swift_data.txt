<p>The <b>Common Swift</b> (<i>Apus apus</i>) is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a>, superficially similar to the <a href="/wiki/Barn_Swallow" title="Barn Swallow">Barn Swallow</a> or <a href="/wiki/House_Martin" title="House Martin" class="mw-redirect">House Martin</a> but somewhat larger. It is, however, completely unrelated to those <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> species, since <a href="/wiki/Swift" title="Swift">swifts</a> are in the separate order <a href="/wiki/Apodiformes" title="Apodiformes">Apodiformes</a>. The resemblances between the groups are due to <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">convergent evolution</a> reflecting similar life styles. Swifts' nearest relatives are thought to be the New World <a href="/wiki/Hummingbirds" title="Hummingbirds" class="mw-redirect">hummingbirds</a> and the Southeast Asian <a href="/wiki/Treeswift" title="Treeswift">treeswifts</a>.</p>
<p>Their scientific name comes from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> words α "without", and πούς, "feet". ἄπους, <i>apous</i>, meaning "without feet". These birds have very short legs which they use only for clinging to vertical surfaces (hence the German name <i>Mauersegler</i>, literally meaning "wall-glider"). They never settle voluntarily on the ground, where they would be vulnerable to accidents and predation.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Swift&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The Common Swift was one of the many species described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_naturae" title="Systema naturae" class="mw-redirect">Systema naturae</a></i> in 1758. The predecessor of the Central European <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> which lived during the <a href="/wiki/Last_ice_age" title="Last ice age" class="mw-redirect">last ice age</a> has been described as <i>Apus apus palapus</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Swift&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>Common Swifts are 16–17&#160;cm long with a wingspan of 38–40&#160;cm<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> and entirely blackish-brown except for a small white or pale grey patch on their chins which is not visible from a distance. They have a short forked tail and very long swept-back wings that resemble a crescent or a <a href="/wiki/Boomerang" title="Boomerang">boomerang</a>.</p>
<p>The call is a loud scream in two different tone pitches, of which the higher one is from the female and the lower one from the male. They often form 'screaming parties' during summer evenings where about 10-20 Swifts will gather and fly around their nesting area, all calling out to each other, and being answered by other Swifts within the nests. Larger "screaming parties" are formed at higher altitudes, especially late in the breeding season. The purpose of these is not known, but it appears to be the case that these parties, or many Swifts in them, will then ascend to sleep on the wing, while still breeding adults tend to spend the night in the nest.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Swift&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Swift&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Migration">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Migration">Migration</span></h3>
<p>Like Swallows, Common Swifts are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>. Their summer breeding range runs from Spain and Ireland in the West across to China and Siberia in the East. They breed as far South as North Africa (in Morocco and Algeria) with a presence in the Middle East in Israel, Lebanon and Syria, across Turkey including the whole of Europe, as far North as Norway, Finland and most of sub-Arctic Russia. They migrate down to Africa by a variety of routes, so far poorly understood, ending up in Equatorial and Sub-Equatorial Africa, excluding the Cape. The map on this page is old and probably reflects the limits of Linnaeus' knowledge at the time; in fact Common Swifts do not breed in the Indian Subcontinent.</p>
<p>A recent geolocator tracking study revealed that individuals of this species that breed in Sweden winter in the Congo region of Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Swift&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mauersegler4.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Mauersegler4.jpg/220px-Mauersegler4.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Mauersegler4.jpg/330px-Mauersegler4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0d/Mauersegler4.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mauersegler4.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Young bird, not yet able to fly</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Swifts will occasionally live in old woodpecker nest holes in ancient forests, (some 600 having been reported as nesting in such circumstances in the Bialowieska forest in North Eastern Poland while a small colony lives in woodpecker holes and tree nestboxes at the RSPB's reserve in the Ancient Caledonian Forest reserve at Abernethy in Scotland) and it can be surmised that together with cliffs this was their main nest resource, but with the almost complete removal of ancient forest from their nesting range they have adapted to occupy man-made sites and will build their <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nests</a> of air-borne material caught in flight and bonded with their saliva, in suitable hollows in buildings, under tiles, in gaps beneath window sills, but most typically in the eaves and in gables. Swifts form reasonably faithful pairs that may last for years, and they will return to the same nesting site and partner year after year, rebuilding their nest when necessary. Swift nests degrade naturally over the long period (40 weeks or so) that the birds are absent on migration. Insect species such as clothes moths, carpet and larder beetles will eat them away, sometimes leaving only the most indigestible elements, typically feather shafts, behind.</p>
<p>Young Swifts in the nest can drop their body temperature and other metabolic elements and become <a href="/wiki/Torpid" title="Torpid" class="mw-redirect">torpid</a> if bad weather prevents their parents from catching insects nearby. In this state they may survive for a few days without food.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Swift&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Apus_apus_flock_flying_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Apus_apus_flock_flying_1.jpg/220px-Apus_apus_flock_flying_1.jpg" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Apus_apus_flock_flying_1.jpg/330px-Apus_apus_flock_flying_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Apus_apus_flock_flying_1.jpg/440px-Apus_apus_flock_flying_1.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Apus_apus_flock_flying_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Several flying</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Except when breeding, Swifts spend their lives in the air, living on the insects they catch in flight. They drink, feed, and often mate and sleep on the wing.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> No other bird spends as much of its life in flight. Feeding parties can be very large indeed in suitable insect-rich areas, such as wetlands. Reports of as many as 2000 Swifts feeding over flooded gravel pits, lakes and marshy river deltas are not uncommon, and may represent an ingress of Swifts from a huge area of maybe 100 kilometres radius. It has for example been suggested that many of Western Scotland's Swifts fly to Lough Neagh in Northern Ireland to feed on the abundant and nutritious "Lough Neagh Fly".</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Swift&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Parasites">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Parasites">Parasites</span></h2>
<p>Swift nests commonly support populations of the <a href="/wiki/Chewing_louse" title="Chewing louse" class="mw-redirect">Chewing louse</a>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dennyus_hirundinis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dennyus hirundinis (page does not exist)">Dennyus hirundinis</a></i> and the Lousefly, <i><a href="/wiki/Crataerina_pallida" title="Crataerina pallida">Crataerina pallida</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Swift&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In heraldry">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="In_heraldry">In heraldry</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">heraldic</a> bird known as the "<a href="/wiki/Martlet" title="Martlet">martlet</a>", which is represented without feet, may have been based on the swift, but is generally assumed to refer to the house martin; it was used for the arms of younger sons, perhaps because it symbolized their landless wandering.</p>
