<p>The <b>mistle thrush</b> (<i>Turdus viscivorus</i>) is a bird common to much of Europe, <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">temperate Asia</a> and North Africa. It is a year-round resident in a large part of its range, but northern and eastern populations <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> south for the winter, often in small flocks. It is a large <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> with pale grey-brown upper parts, a greyish-white chin and throat, and black spots on its pale yellow and off-white under parts. The sexes are similar in <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>, and its three <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> show only minimal differences. The male has a loud, far-carrying song which is delivered even in wet and windy weather, earning the bird the old name of <b>stormcock</b>.
</p><p>Found in open woods, parks, hedges and cultivated land, the mistle thrush feeds on a wide variety of <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>, seeds and berries. Its preferred fruits include those of the <a href="/wiki/Viscum_album" title="Viscum album">mistletoe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holly" title="Holly">holly</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yew" title="Yew">yew</a>. Mistletoe is favoured where it is available, and this is reflected in the thrush's English and scientific names; the plant, a <a href="/wiki/Parasitic" class="mw-redirect" title="Parasitic">parasitic</a> species, benefits from its seeds being excreted by the thrush onto branches where they can <a href="/wiki/Germinate" class="mw-redirect" title="Germinate">germinate</a>. In winter, a mistle thrush will vigorously defend mistletoe clumps or a holly tree as a food reserve for when times are hard.
</p><p>The open <a href="/wiki/Cup_nest" class="mw-redirect" title="Cup nest">cup nest</a> is built against a trunk or in a forked branch, and is fearlessly defended against potential predators, sometimes including humans or cats. The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a>, typically of three to five eggs, is <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation" title="Egg incubation">incubated</a> for 12–15&#160;days, mainly by the female. The chicks <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> about 14–16&#160;days after hatching. There are normally two broods. There was a range expansion in the 18th and early 19th centuries, and a small decline in recent decades, perhaps due to changes in agricultural practices. Given its high numbers and very large range, this thrush is classified by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> as being of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mistle_thrush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The mistle thrush was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a> under its current <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">scientific name</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Linn_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linn-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <i>Turdus</i> is the Latin for "thrush", and <i>viscivorus</i>, "mistletoe eater", comes from <i>viscum</i> "mistletoe" and <i>vorare</i>, "to devour".<sup id="cite_ref-brookes_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brookes-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-job393_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job393-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-job404_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job404-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The bird's liking for <a href="/wiki/Viscum_album" title="Viscum album">mistletoe</a> berries is also indicated by its English name, "mistle" being an old name for the plant.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are more than 60 species of medium to large thrushes in the genus <i>Turdus</i>, characterised by rounded heads, longish pointed wings, and usually melodious songs.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement36_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement36-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> study identified the mistle thrush's closest relatives as the similarly plumaged <a href="/wiki/Song_thrush" title="Song thrush">song</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_thrush" title="Chinese thrush">Chinese thrushes</a>; these three species are early offshoots from the Eurasian lineage of <i>Turdus</i> thrushes after they spread north from Africa. They are less closely related to other European thrush species such as the <a href="/wiki/Common_blackbird" title="Common blackbird">blackbird</a> (<i>T. merula</i>) which are descended from ancestors that had colonised the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> islands from Africa and subsequently reached Europe from there.<sup id="cite_ref-reilly_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reilly-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-voelker_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-voelker-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At least eight <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> have been proposed, but the differences are mainly <a href="/wiki/Cline_(biology)" title="Cline (biology)">clinal</a>, with birds of the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> becoming paler and less densely spotted in the east of the range. The currently accepted subspecies are:<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>Turdus viscivorus viscivorus</i>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">1758</a>. The nominate subspecies.</li>
<li><i>T. v. bonapartei</i>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Cabanis" title="Jean Cabanis">Cabanis</a>, 1860.</li>
<li><i>T. v. deichleri</i>, <a href="/wiki/Carlo_von_Erlanger" title="Carlo von Erlanger">Erlanger</a>, 1897.</li></ul>
<p>An isolated population in <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a> has sometimes been separated as <i>T. v. tauricus</i>, but this is not considered to be a valid form.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Mistle thrush fossils have been found in <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> deposits from Poland and Sicily.<sup id="cite_ref-poland_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-poland-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sicily_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sicily-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mistle_thrush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Turdus_viscivorus_in_Baikonur-town_003.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Turdus_viscivorus_in_Baikonur-town_003.jpg/220px-Turdus_viscivorus_in_Baikonur-town_003.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Turdus_viscivorus_in_Baikonur-town_003.jpg/330px-Turdus_viscivorus_in_Baikonur-town_003.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Turdus_viscivorus_in_Baikonur-town_003.jpg/440px-Turdus_viscivorus_in_Baikonur-town_003.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1082" data-file-height="811" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Turdus_viscivorus_in_Baikonur-town_003.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a></div></div></div>
<p>The mistle thrush is the largest thrush native to Europe. The nominate subspecies measures 27–28&#160;cm (11–11&#160;in) in length,<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> with a 45&#160;cm (18&#160;in) wingspan.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It weighs 93 to 167&#160;g (3.3 to 5.9&#160;oz),<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> with an average of around 130&#160;g (4.6&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-bto_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It has a stocky upright posture when on the ground. It has pale grey-brown upperparts, the chin and throat are greyish-white, and the yellowish-buff breast and off-white belly are marked with round black spots. The spotting becomes denser on the lower chest, giving the appearance of a breast-band. The long tail has white tips on the outer feathers, and the underwing <a href="/wiki/Covert_feather" title="Covert feather">coverts</a> are white. The eyes are dark brown and the bill is blackish with a yellowish base to the lower <a href="/wiki/Mandible" title="Mandible">mandible</a>. The legs and feet are yellowish-brown. There are no plumage differences between the sexes. Juveniles are similar to adults, but they have paler upperparts with creamy centres to many of the feathers and smaller spots on the yellowish underparts. By their first winter they are very similar to adults, but the underparts are usually more buff-toned.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The eastern subspecies <i>T. v. bonapartei</i> is 30&#160;cm (12&#160;in) in length, and therefore slightly larger than the nominate form. It is paler grey above and whiter below, with fewer black spots. Birds of intermediate appearance are seen west of the <a href="/wiki/Ob_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Ob River">Ob River</a> where the range overlaps with <i>viscivorus</i>. The southern race <i>T. v. deichleri</i> resembles <i>bonapartei</i> in appearance, but is closer in size to the nominate <i>viscivorus</i>, although it has a more slender bill.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Adults have a full <a href="/wiki/Moult" class="mw-redirect" title="Moult">moult</a> after breeding, beginning between late May and the end of June, and completed by early October. Juvenile birds have a partial moult, replacing their head, body, and covert feathers; this is completed by October, although the start of the moult depends on when the chicks hatched.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The mistle thrush is much larger, paler and longer-tailed than the <a href="/wiki/Sympatric" class="mw-redirect" title="Sympatric">sympatric</a> song thrush. In the western Himalayas it could be confused with both the <a href="/wiki/Plain-backed_thrush" title="Plain-backed thrush">plain-backed</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Long-tailed_thrush" title="Long-tailed thrush">long-tailed thrushes</a>. These are similar to the mistle thrush, but the plain–backed thrush lacks obvious wing bars, is more <a href="/wiki/Rufous" title="Rufous">rufous</a> above than its relative, and is barred rather than spotted below. The long-tailed thrush has olive-toned upperparts, bars on its breast and two wing bars. Juvenile mistle thrushes are superficially similar to <a href="/wiki/White%27s_thrush" title="White&#39;s thrush">White's thrush</a>, but that species has golden-yellow plumage, scalloped underparts and a distinctive underwing pattern.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mistle_thrush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The male mistle thrush has a loud melodious song with fluted whistles, sounding like <i>chewee-trewuu&#160;... trureetruuruu</i> or similar, repeated three to six times,<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and used to advertise his territory, attract a mate and maintain the pair bond.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement41_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement41-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The tone resembles that of the song thrush or blackbird, but compared to its relatives the mistle thrush's repertoire is less varied and the delivery is slower. The song is, however, much louder, often audible up to 2&#160;km (2,000&#160;yd) away. The song is given from a treetop or other elevated position mainly from November to early June. The male is most vocal in the early morning, and its tendency to sing after, and sometimes during, wet and windy weather led to the old name "stormcock".<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BWP_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The song may be heard in any month, although it is uncommon from July to August while the thrush is moulting.<sup id="cite_ref-Coward_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The main call, given by both sexes, is a dry chattering <i>krrrr</i>, louder when it is alarmed or excited.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BWP_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> It is often likened to the sound of a football rattle, a form of <a href="/wiki/Ratchet_(instrument)" title="Ratchet (instrument)">musical ratchet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-btothrushes_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-btothrushes-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> There is also a squeaky <i>tuk</i> contact call.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mistle_thrush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:249px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Turdus_viscivorus_-Longbridge,_Birmingham,_England_-parent_and_chicks-8_(2).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Turdus_viscivorus_-Longbridge%2C_Birmingham%2C_England_-parent_and_chicks-8_%282%29.jpg/247px-Turdus_viscivorus_-Longbridge%2C_Birmingham%2C_England_-parent_and_chicks-8_%282%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="247" height="132" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Turdus_viscivorus_-Longbridge%2C_Birmingham%2C_England_-parent_and_chicks-8_%282%29.jpg/371px-Turdus_viscivorus_-Longbridge%2C_Birmingham%2C_England_-parent_and_chicks-8_%282%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Turdus_viscivorus_-Longbridge%2C_Birmingham%2C_England_-parent_and_chicks-8_%282%29.jpg/494px-Turdus_viscivorus_-Longbridge%2C_Birmingham%2C_England_-parent_and_chicks-8_%282%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1952" data-file-height="1040" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Turdus_viscivorus_-Longbridge,_Birmingham,_England_-parent_and_chicks-8_(2).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An adult (right) with two juveniles feeding on a lawn</div></div></div>
<p>The mistle thrush breeds in much of Europe and temperate Asia, although it is absent from the treeless far north, and its range becomes discontinuous in southeast Europe, Turkey and the Middle East. In these warmer southern areas, it tends to be found in the milder uplands and coastal regions. Nominate <i>T. v. viscivorus</i> breeds in Europe and in Asia east to the Ob, beyond which it is replaced by <i>T. v. bonapartei</i>. The southern form <i>T. v. deichleri</i> is resident in North Africa, <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The mistle thrush is a partial <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>: birds from the north and east of the range wintering in the milder areas of Europe and North Africa. Scandinavian and Russian birds start moving south from mid-September onwards, most birds wintering in Europe, western Turkey and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>. Between mid-October and November, large numbers cross the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Gibraltar" title="Strait of Gibraltar">Strait of Gibraltar</a> and others pass through Cyprus, but there is hardly any migration across the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>. Breeding birds in the British Isles and north-west Europe are resident or move only short distances. In the Himalayas, the breeding population moves to nearby lower-altitude sites in winter. Return migration starts mainly from late March, although it can be a month earlier in the Middle East, and northern breeders may not arrive back on their territories until late April or early May. Migration may be by day or night, and typically involves individuals or small groups.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Vagrant birds have occurred in the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, China, <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, Iceland, Japan, Oman, Saudi Arabia, <a href="/wiki/Sikkim" title="Sikkim">Sikkim</a> and the United Arab Emirates.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In the winter 2017/2018 the first record of a mistle thrush for North America was at <a href="/wiki/Miramichi,_New_Brunswick" title="Miramichi, New Brunswick">Miramichi</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cbc_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbc-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The mistle thrush is found in a wide range of habitats containing trees, including forests, plantations, hedges and town parks. In the south and east of its range, it inhabits upland <a href="/wiki/Coniferous" class="mw-redirect" title="Coniferous">coniferous</a> woodland and the range extends above the main <a href="/wiki/Tree_line" title="Tree line">tree line</a> where dwarf <a href="/wiki/Juniperus_communis" title="Juniperus communis">juniper</a> is present. Breeding occurs at up to 600&#160;m (2,000&#160;ft) in the mountains of North Africa, and occasionally much higher, to 1,700&#160;m (5,600&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In the highlands of Europe, its preferred altitude is from 800–1,800&#160;m (2,600–5,900&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> More open habitats, such as agricultural land, moors and grassy hills, are extensively used in winter or on migration.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is evidence that this species has changed its natural habitat in at least parts of its range. In Germany and elsewhere in central Europe, it was found only in coniferous forest until the mid-1920s when its range rapidly expanded, first into farmland, and then to suburbs and urban parks. The reasons for this expansion are unclear.<sup id="cite_ref-fuller_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fuller-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In areas of <a href="/wiki/Intensive_farming" title="Intensive farming">intensive farming</a>, such as eastern England, <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable land</a> has in turn largely been abandoned in favour of built-up areas with their greater variety of green habitats.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Perhaps the most notable find of the 118th <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Bird_Count" title="Christmas Bird Count">Christmas Bird Count</a> in Canada was a single vagrant mistle thrush found in <a href="/wiki/Miramichi,_New_Brunswick" title="Miramichi, New Brunswick">Miramichi, New Brunswick</a>. This was the first record for this species in North America.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The discovery attracted many birders from Canada and the United States; sightings continued from early December 2017 through late March 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mistle_thrush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mistle thrushes are found as individuals or pairs for much of the year, although families forage together in late summer,<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and groups may merge to form large flocks when food sources are plentiful.<sup id="cite_ref-brown_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brown-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> It is not uncommon for up to 50 thrushes to feed together at that time of year. They roost at night in trees or bushes, again typically as individuals or pairs, except in late summer or autumn when families may roost together.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The mistle thrush is quite terrestrial, hopping with its head held up and body erect; when excited, it will flick its wings and tail. The flight consists of undulating bounds interspersed with glides.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mistle_thrush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mistle_trush_male_left_pass_earthworms_to_female.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Mistle_trush_male_left_pass_earthworms_to_female.jpg/220px-Mistle_trush_male_left_pass_earthworms_to_female.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="201" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Mistle_trush_male_left_pass_earthworms_to_female.jpg/330px-Mistle_trush_male_left_pass_earthworms_to_female.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Mistle_trush_male_left_pass_earthworms_to_female.jpg/440px-Mistle_trush_male_left_pass_earthworms_to_female.jpg 2x" data-file-width="517" data-file-height="472" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mistle_trush_male_left_pass_earthworms_to_female.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Male (left) passing earthworms to female on nest</div></div></div>
<p>Mistle thrushes breed in the year subsequent to their hatching;<sup id="cite_ref-bto_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> they are <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" class="mw-redirect" title="Monogamous">monogamous</a> and stay as a pair throughout the year in areas where they are not migratory.<sup id="cite_ref-btoturdus_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-btoturdus-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Their territories are much larger than those of blackbirds or song thrushes;<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> although the nest territory is only about 0.6 hectares (1.5 acres), around 15–17&#160;ha (37–42 acres) is used for feeding. Territories are normally reoccupied in subsequent years.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Territories are larger in woods than in farmland.<sup id="cite_ref-brown_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brown-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The male will attack intruders into its breeding area, including <a href="/wiki/Birds_of_prey" class="mw-redirect" title="Birds of prey">birds of prey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corvids" class="mw-redirect" title="Corvids">corvids</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and sometimes cats or humans.<sup id="cite_ref-Coward_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Courtship feeding of the female by her partner has sometimes been observed.<sup id="cite_ref-young_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-young-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
Breeding typically commences in mid-March in the south and west of Europe (late February in Britain), but not till early May in Finland.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The nest is usually built in a tree in the fork of a branch or against the trunk, although hedges, ledges on buildings and cliff faces may also be used. The nest site may be up to 20&#160;m (66&#160;ft) above the ground, although 2–9&#160;m (6.6–29.5&#160;ft) is more typical. The <a href="/wiki/Common_chaffinch" title="Common chaffinch">common chaffinch</a> often nests close to a mistle thrush, the vigilance of the chaffinch and the aggressive behaviour of the thrush benefiting both species. The thrush's nest is a large cup of sticks, dry grass, roots and moss, coated on the inside with a layer of mud and lined with fine grass and leaves. The nest is built by the female, although the male may help.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BWP_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Nests built early in the breeding season may be destroyed by bad weather.<sup id="cite_ref-Coward_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is typically three to five eggs (range two to six), which are usually whitish-buff or greenish-blue and are spotted with red, purple or brown.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_16-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The average size of the egg is 30&#160;mm ×&#160;22&#160;mm (1.18&#160;in ×&#160;0.87&#160;in), and weighs 7.8&#160;g (0.28&#160;oz), of which 6% is shell.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The eggs are <a href="/wiki/Incubate_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Incubate (bird)">incubated</a> for 12–15&#160;days, mainly by the female.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The chicks are <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a> and downy,<sup id="cite_ref-bto_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and are fed by both parents. They <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> about 14–16&#160;days after hatching. There are normally two broods, except in Siberia, where there is only one, the male feeding the fledglings from the first brood while the female sits on the second clutch.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Sometimes the same nest is reused for both broods.<sup id="cite_ref-btoaz_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-btoaz-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The young are dependent on their parents for 15–20 days after fledging.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In a study carried out in Britain, the survival rate for juveniles in their first year is 57&#160;per cent, and the adult annual survival rate is 62&#160;per cent. Life expectancy is typically three years,<sup id="cite_ref-bto_14-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> but the maximum age recorded from <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">bird ringing</a> recoveries is 21&#160;years and 3&#160;months for a bird shot in Switzerland.<sup id="cite_ref-euring_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euring-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mistle_thrush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Viscum_album_fruit.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Viscum_album_fruit.jpg/220px-Viscum_album_fruit.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Viscum_album_fruit.jpg/330px-Viscum_album_fruit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Viscum_album_fruit.jpg/440px-Viscum_album_fruit.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Viscum_album_fruit.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The mistle thrush derives its English and scientific names from mistletoe, a favourite food.</div></div></div>
<p>Mistle thrushes feed mainly on <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>, fruit and berries. Animal prey include earthworms, insects and other <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a>, slugs and snails.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Snails are sometimes smashed on a stone "anvil", a technique also used by the song thrush.<sup id="cite_ref-Coward_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The mistle thrush has been known to kill <a href="/wiki/Slowworms" class="mw-redirect" title="Slowworms">slowworms</a> and the young of the song thrush, blackbird and <a href="/wiki/Dunnock" title="Dunnock">dunnock</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Plant food includes the fruits and seeds of bushes and trees, mainly holly, yew, ivy and mistletoe, but also, for example, blackberry, cherry, elder, hawthorn, olive and rose. It may eat the flowers and shoots of grasses and other plants, and will take fallen apples and plums. It forages within its breeding habitat and in open fields, sometimes sharing these feeding areas with <a href="/wiki/Redwing" title="Redwing">redwings</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fieldfare" title="Fieldfare">fieldfares</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement397_11-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement397-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Young birds are initially mainly fed on invertebrates, often collected from low foliage or under bushes rather than in the grassland preferred by the adults. Adults will roam up to 1&#160;km (approximately 1,100&#160;yards) from the nest on pasture or ploughed land. After fledging the young may accompany their parents until the onset of winter.<sup id="cite_ref-brown_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brown-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Individuals or pairs will defend one or more fruit-bearing trees throughout the winter, with preference shown for trees which host mistletoe, the parasitic plant from which the bird derives its name. Where mistletoe is not present, holly is the most common tree chosen.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Although the thrush normally feeds on the ground and from low bushes, the defence of this resource conserves fruit for later in the season when other food items become scarce.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_16-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The trees are defended against other thrushes as well as birds such as the <a href="/wiki/Bullfinch" title="Bullfinch">bullfinch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_spotted_woodpecker" title="Great spotted woodpecker">great spotted woodpecker</a>. In milder winters with an abundance of fruit, however, this strategy is less used and thrushes can be observed foraging in flocks.<sup id="cite_ref-skorka_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-skorka-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Conversely, in hard winters, the defender may be overwhelmed by large flocks of fieldfares, redwings or <a href="/wiki/Bohemian_waxwing" title="Bohemian waxwing">Bohemian waxwings</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As its name implies, the mistle thrush is important in <a href="/wiki/Plant_propagation" title="Plant propagation">propagating</a> the mistletoe, an aerial parasite, which needs its seeds to be deposited on the branches of suitable trees. The highly nutritious fruits are favoured by the thrush, which digests the flesh leaving the sticky seeds to be excreted, possibly in a suitable location for germination.<sup id="cite_ref-fleas28_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fleas28-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators_and_parasites">Predators and parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mistle_thrush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Predators and parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ixodus_ricinus_5x.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Ixodus_ricinus_5x.jpg/220px-Ixodus_ricinus_5x.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Ixodus_ricinus_5x.jpg/330px-Ixodus_ricinus_5x.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Ixodus_ricinus_5x.jpg/440px-Ixodus_ricinus_5x.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2200" data-file-height="1500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ixodus_ricinus_5x.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Castor_bean_tick" class="mw-redirect" title="Castor bean tick">castor bean tick</a> swollen with the blood of its host</div></div></div>
<p>The mistle thrush is predated upon by a wide variety of birds of prey, including the <a href="/wiki/Boreal_owl" title="Boreal owl">boreal owl</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-owl_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-owl-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Short-eared_owl" title="Short-eared owl">short-eared owl</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tawny_owl" title="Tawny owl">tawny owl</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ural_owl" title="Ural owl">Ural owl</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_eagle-owl" title="Eurasian eagle-owl">Eurasian eagle-owl</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">golden eagle</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Watson_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Common_kestrel" title="Common kestrel">kestrel</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Coward_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Common_buzzard" title="Common buzzard">common buzzard</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Red_kite" title="Red kite">red kite</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Northern_goshawk" title="Northern goshawk">northern goshawk</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Kenward_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kenward-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">peregrine falcon</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Ratcliffe_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ratcliffe-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">sparrowhawk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Newton_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newton-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The eggs and chicks may be targeted by <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a> and corvids. Parent birds exhibit fearlessness in defence of their nests, occasionally even attacking humans.<sup id="cite_ref-Coward_17-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The mistle thrush is not normally a host of the <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common cuckoo</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasite</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-glue_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glue-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>External parasites of the mistle thrush include the <a href="/wiki/Hen_flea" class="mw-redirect" title="Hen flea">hen flea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Moorhen_flea" title="Moorhen flea">moorhen flea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Castor_bean_tick" class="mw-redirect" title="Castor bean tick">castor bean tick</a> and the brightly coloured <a href="/wiki/Trombicula_autumnalis" title="Trombicula autumnalis">harvest mite</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-fleas84_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fleas84-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fleas227_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fleas227-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Falchi_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Falchi-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Internally, they can suffer from parasites including <a href="/wiki/Cestoda" title="Cestoda">tapeworm</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-fleas197_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fleas197-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mettrick_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mettrick-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nematode" title="Nematode">nematodes</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-fleas189_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fleas189-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Supperer_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Supperer-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Syngamus_merulae" title="Syngamus merulae">Syngamus merulae</a></i> (a species of Gapeworm).<sup id="cite_ref-fleas181_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fleas181-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Blood parasites can include <i><a href="/wiki/Trypanosoma" title="Trypanosoma">Trypanosoma</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Plasmodium" title="Plasmodium">Plasmodium</a></i> species.<sup id="cite_ref-fleas171_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fleas171-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fleas165_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fleas165-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mistle_thrush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The mistle thrush has an extensive distribution in Europe and western Asia, and its European breeding population is estimated at 9–22.2&#160;million birds. When Asian breeders are added, this gives a global total of 12.2–44.4&#160;million.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The species was formerly more restricted in range, and rarely bred even in northern England in the 1700s.<sup id="cite_ref-brown_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brown-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> It expanded rapidly into lowland and coastal areas of Europe during the 18th century and the first half of the 19th century, colonising areas where it was formerly rare or absent, such as Ireland (where it first bred in 1807), Scotland and the Netherlands. The range also increased in Denmark, Norway, Hungary and Austria.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_16-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the population now appears to be declining, the decrease is not rapid or large enough to trigger conservation vulnerability criteria. Given its high numbers and very large range, this thrush is therefore classified by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> as being of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The perceived decline may be due to the loss of invertebrate-rich pastures and mixed farms through conversion to arable agriculture or more intensively managed grassland. Adult survival, clutch size and fledging success are all lower in arable landscapes than in areas with extensive pasture.<sup id="cite_ref-brown_24-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brown-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In Finland, the loss of ancient forests is thought to have led to a local decline.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mistle_thrush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mistlethrushbonna.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Mistlethrushbonna.png/170px-Mistlethrushbonna.png" decoding="async" width="170" height="236" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Mistlethrushbonna.png/255px-Mistlethrushbonna.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Mistlethrushbonna.png/340px-Mistlethrushbonna.png 2x" data-file-width="407" data-file-height="566" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mistlethrushbonna.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Mistle Thrush and Alpine Chough</i> by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_da_Udine" title="Giovanni da Udine">Giovanni da Udine</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Desiderius_Erasmus" class="mw-redirect" title="Desiderius Erasmus">Desiderius Erasmus</a>'s early sixteenth-century collection of Latin proverbs included <i>Turdus malum sibi ipse cacat</i>, (the thrush himself excretes his own trouble), which refers to the use of the sticky mistletoe berries favoured by this species as an ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Birdlime" title="Birdlime">birdlime</a>, used to trap birds. The thrush was seen to be thus spreading the seeds of his own destruction.<sup id="cite_ref-job404_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job404-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-erasmus_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-erasmus-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Mistle Thrush and Alpine Chough</i>, by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_da_Udine" title="Giovanni da Udine">Giovanni da Udine</a>, an artist who worked in <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">Raphael</a>'s studio in the 16th century, was a sketch for his <i>Bird with Garland and Fruit</i>, and this in turn was the basis for a Raphael <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">fresco</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Palace" title="Apostolic Palace">Apostolic Palace</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hersey_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hersey-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Alsteens_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alsteens-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The early <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> poem "The Harmony of Birds" features a <b>thrusshe</b> (mistle thrush) singing the phrase "sanctus, sanctus", distinguishing the bird from the song thrush, the <i>mauys</i> or <i>throstle</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-andrew_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-andrew-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> The song of the mistle thrush is also described in <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hardy" title="Thomas Hardy">Thomas Hardy</a>'s "Darkling Thrush" and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Thomas_(poet)" title="Edward Thomas (poet)">Edward Thomas's</a> "The Thrush".<sup id="cite_ref-Armitage_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Armitage-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> The loud call of this common and conspicuous bird also led to many old or local names, including "screech", "shrite" and "gawthrush".<sup id="cite_ref-swainson_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swainson-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lockwood_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lockwood-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> Other names, including "stormcock" referred to its willingness to sing in wind and rain. "Holm thrush", "hollin cock" and "holm cock" are based on obsolete names for the holly tree, which may be defended by the thrush in winter for its berries.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Frances_Hodgson_Burnett" title="Frances Hodgson Burnett">Frances Hodgson Burnett</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Secret_Garden" title="The Secret Garden">The Secret Garden</a></i>, Dickon reassures Mary Lennox that he will keep his knowledge of the garden secret by comparing her to a mistle thrush in defence of its nest, recognising his privilege in sharing her secret: "If tha' was a missel thrush an' showed me where thy nest was, does tha' think I'd tell any one? Not me," he said. "Tha' art as safe as a missel thrush."
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Stormcock_(album)" title="Stormcock (album)">Stormcock</a></i>, released in 1971, is the fifth album by English folk/rock singer-songwriter and guitarist <a href="/wiki/Roy_Harper_(singer)" title="Roy Harper (singer)">Roy Harper</a>.
</p><p>The final verse of the <a href="/wiki/Jethro_Tull_(band)" title="Jethro Tull (band)">Jethro Tull</a> song "Jack-in-the-Green" from their album <i><a href="/wiki/Songs_from_the_Wood" title="Songs from the Wood">Songs from the Wood</a></i> mentions the bird in the lines "Oh, the mistlethrush is coming. Jack, put out the light." The bird also features in the lyrics of <a href="/wiki/The_Decemberists" title="The Decemberists">The Decemberists</a>' song "Won't Want For Love (Margaret In The Taiga)" from their 2009 album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hazards_of_Love" title="The Hazards of Love">The Hazards of Love</a></i>: "Mistlethrush, Mistlethrush, Lay me down in the underbrush, My naked feet grow weary with the dusk".<sup id="cite_ref-HoL_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HoL-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
