<p>The <b>common redstart</b> (<i>Phoenicurus phoenicurus</i>), or often simply <b>redstart</b>, is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Redstart" title="Redstart">redstart</a> genus <i><a href="/wiki/Phoenicurus" title="Phoenicurus">Phoenicurus</a></i>. Like its relatives, it was formerly classed as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family, (Turdidae), but is now known to be an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a> (family Muscicapidae).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_redstart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">formal description</a> of the common redstart was by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">tenth edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> under the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Motacilla phoenicurus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The genus <i>Phoenicurus</i> was introduced by the English naturalist <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Ignatius_Maria_Forster" title="Thomas Ignatius Maria Forster">Thomas Forster</a> in 1817.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The genus and species name <i>phoenicurus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>phoinix</i>, "red", and <i>-ouros</i> -"tailed".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are accepted. The <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate</a> <i>P. p. phoenicurus</i> is found all over Europe and reaches into <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. To the southeast, subspecies <i>P. p. samamisicus</i> is found from the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_Peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Crimean Peninsula">Crimean Peninsula</a> through <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, and into <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>. It is slightly smaller than <i>P. p. phoenicurus</i> and in adult males has white outer webs in the <a href="/wiki/Remiges" class="mw-redirect" title="Remiges">remiges</a> to some extent, forming a pale to whitish wing-patch similar to the one seen in <a href="/wiki/Black_redstart" title="Black redstart">black redstart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daurian_redstart" title="Daurian redstart">Daurian redstart</a>. This patch is also present but less conspicuous in immature males, and sometimes in adult females. The subspecies intergrade widely in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and the southern <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Snow_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The closest genetic relative of the common redstart may be the <a href="/wiki/Moussier%27s_redstart" title="Moussier&#39;s redstart">Moussier's redstart</a>, though incomplete sampling of the genus gives some uncertainty to this.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Its ancestors were apparently the first redstarts to spread to Europe; they seem to have diverged from the black redstart group about 3 <a href="/wiki/Mya_(unit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mya (unit)">mya</a>, during the <a href="/wiki/Piacenzian" title="Piacenzian">Piacenzian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ertan_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ertan-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Genetically, common and black redstarts are still fairly compatible and can produce <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrids</a> that appear to be healthy and fertile, but they are separated by different <a href="/wiki/Behaviour" class="mw-redirect" title="Behaviour">behaviour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a> requirements so hybrids are very rare in nature.<sup id="cite_ref-Grosch_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grosch-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_redstart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common redstart shows some affinity to the <a href="/wiki/European_robin" title="European robin">European robin</a> in many of its habits and actions. It has the same general carriage, and <a href="/wiki/Chat_(bird)" title="Chat (bird)">chat</a>-like behaviour, and is the same length at 13–14.5&#160;cm long but slightly slimmer and not quite as heavy, weighing 11–23 g. The orange-red tail, from which it and other redstarts get their names ("start" is an old word for "tail"), is frequently quivered. Among common European birds, only the <a href="/wiki/Black_redstart" title="Black redstart">black redstart</a> (<i>Phoenicurus ochrurus</i>) has a similarly coloured tail.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Snow_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Phoenicurus_phoenicurus_female.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Phoenicurus_phoenicurus_female.jpg/220px-Phoenicurus_phoenicurus_female.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Phoenicurus_phoenicurus_female.jpg/330px-Phoenicurus_phoenicurus_female.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ae/Phoenicurus_phoenicurus_female.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="267" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Phoenicurus_phoenicurus_female.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Female</div></div></div>
<p>The male in summer has a slate-grey head and upperparts, except the rump and tail, which, like the flanks, underwing coverts and axillaries are orange-chestnut. The forehead is white; the sides of the face and throat are black. The wings and the two central tail feathers are brown, the other tail feathers bright orange-red. The orange on the flanks shades to almost white on the belly. The bill and legs are black. In autumn, pale feather fringes on the body feathering obscures the colours of the male, giving it a washed-out appearance. The female is browner, with paler underparts; it lacks the black and slate, and the throat is whitish.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_redstart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Distribution_ph_phoenicurus.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Distribution_ph_phoenicurus.png/220px-Distribution_ph_phoenicurus.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="134" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Distribution_ph_phoenicurus.png/330px-Distribution_ph_phoenicurus.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Distribution_ph_phoenicurus.png/440px-Distribution_ph_phoenicurus.png 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="730" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Distribution_ph_phoenicurus.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Breeding (orange) and wintering (blue) distribution</div></div></div>
<p>Common redstarts prefer open mature <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodland</a> with a high horizontal visibility and low amounts of shrub and understorey especially where the trees are old enough to have holes suitable for its nest. They prefer to nest on the edge of woodland clearings. In Britain it occurs primarily in upland areas less affected by <a href="/wiki/Intensive_farming" title="Intensive farming">agricultural intensification</a>, but further east in Europe also commonly in lowland areas, including parks and old gardens in urban areas. They nest in natural tree holes, so dead trees or those with dead limbs are beneficial to the species; nestboxes are sometimes used. A high cover of <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">moss</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichen</a> is also preferred. They also use mature open <a href="/wiki/Conifer" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifer">conifer</a> woodland, particularly in the north of the breeding range. Management to thin out the trees is thus favoured.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Snow_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, where it has declined by 55% in the past 25 years, the <a href="/wiki/Forestry_Commission" title="Forestry Commission">Forestry Commission</a> offers grants under a scheme called England's Woodland Improvement Grant (EWIG); as does  <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural Englands</a> <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Stewardship" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental Stewardship">Environmental Stewardship</a> Scheme.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It is a very rare breeding bird in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, with between one and five pairs breeding in most years, nearly all of them in <a href="/wiki/County_Wicklow" title="County Wicklow">County Wicklow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_redstart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Phoenicurus_phoenicurus_eggs.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Phoenicurus_phoenicurus_eggs.jpg/220px-Phoenicurus_phoenicurus_eggs.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="194" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Phoenicurus_phoenicurus_eggs.jpg/330px-Phoenicurus_phoenicurus_eggs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Phoenicurus_phoenicurus_eggs.jpg/440px-Phoenicurus_phoenicurus_eggs.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1128" data-file-height="993" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Phoenicurus_phoenicurus_eggs.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nest with a clutch of eggs.</div></div></div>

<p>It is a summer visitor throughout most of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> (east to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a>), and also in northwest <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>. It winters in central Africa and <a href="/wiki/Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabia">Arabia</a>, south of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" class="mw-redirect" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a> but north of the Equator, from <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> east to <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a>. It is widespread as a breeding bird in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, particularly in upland broadleaf woodlands and hedgerow trees, but in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> it is very local, and may not breed every year.
</p><p>The males first arrive in early to mid April,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> often a few days in advance of the females. Five or six light blue eggs are laid during May, with a second brood in mid summer in the south of the breeding range. It departs for Africa between mid-August and early October. It often feeds like a <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">flycatcher</a>, making <a href="/wiki/Hawking_(birds)" title="Hawking (birds)">aerial sallies</a> after passing insects, and most of its food consists of winged <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>. The call is chat-like and the alarm a plaintive single note, <i>wheet</i>, like that of many other chats.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The male's song is similar to that of the Robin, but never more than a prelude, since it has an unfinished, feeble ending.
</p><p>Common redstarts are sometimes parasitized by <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common cuckoos</a>. Surprisingly, redstart chicks did not suffer from sharing the nest with a Cuckoo chick. The presence of a cuckoo might even be beneficial for the nestlings. The large size of the cuckoo chick affects the thermoregulation in the nest. In some sense, the Cuckoo chick is ‘brooding’ the Redstart nestlings. Moreover, food provision might be better for redstart chicks in a mixed brood.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
