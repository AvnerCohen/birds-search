<p>The <b>Tundra Swan</b> (<i>Cygnus columbianus</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Holarctic" title="Holarctic">Holarctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Swan" title="Swan">swan</a>. The two <a href="/wiki/Taxa" title="Taxa" class="mw-redirect">taxa</a> within it are usually regarded as <a href="/wiki/Conspecific" title="Conspecific" class="mw-redirect">conspecific</a>, but are also sometimes<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> split into two <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, <i><b>Cygnus bewickii</b></i> (<b>Bewick's Swan</b>) of the <a href="/wiki/Palaearctic" title="Palaearctic" class="mw-redirect">Palaearctic</a> and the <b>Whistling Swan</b>, <i>C. columbianus</i> proper, of the <a href="/wiki/Nearctic" title="Nearctic" class="mw-redirect">Nearctic</a>. Birds from eastern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> (roughly east of the <a href="/wiki/Taimyr_Peninsula" title="Taimyr Peninsula" class="mw-redirect">Taimyr Peninsula</a>) are sometimes separated as the subspecies <i>C. c. jankowskii</i>, but this is not widely accepted as distinct, most authors including them in <i>C. c. bewickii</i>. Tundra Swans are sometimes separated in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i>Olor</i> together with the other <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> swan species.<sup id="cite_ref-mbc8792_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mbc8792-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Bewick's Swan is named after the engraver <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bewick" title="Thomas Bewick">Thomas Bewick</a>, who specialised in illustrations of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> and animals.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Tundra_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p><i>C. columbianus</i> is the smallest of the <a href="/wiki/Holarctic" title="Holarctic">Holarctic</a> swans, at 115–150 cm (45–59 in) in length, 168–211 cm (66–83 in) in wingspan and a weight range of 3.4–9.6 kg (7.5–21 lb).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> In adult birds, the plumage of both <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> is entirely white, with black feet, and a bill that is mostly black, with a thin salmon-pink streak running along the mouthline and – depending on the subspecies – more or less yellow in the <a href="/wiki/Proximal" title="Proximal" class="mw-redirect">proximal</a> part. The <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">iris</a> is dark brown. In birds living in waters that contains large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bog</a> lakes), the head and neck plumage acquires a golden or rusty hue. Pens (females) are slightly smaller than cobs (males), but do not differ in appearance otherwise.<sup id="cite_ref-mbc8792_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mbc8792-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

</div>
</div>
<p>Immatures of both subspecies are white mixed with some dull grey feathering, mainly on the head and upper neck, which are often entirely light grey; their first-summer plumage is quite white already, and in their second winter they <a href="/wiki/Moult" title="Moult" class="mw-redirect">moult</a> into the adult plumage. Their bills are black with a large dirty-pink patch taking up most of the proximal half and often black <a href="/wiki/Nostril" title="Nostril">nostrils</a>, and their feet are dark grey with a pinkish hue. <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">Downy</a> young are silvery grey above and white below.<sup id="cite_ref-mbc8792_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mbc8792-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><b>Bewick's Swans</b> are the smaller subspecies. There is a slight size <a href="/wiki/Cline_(population_genetics)" title="Cline (population genetics)" class="mw-redirect">cline</a>, with the eastern birds being slightly larger; good measurement data only exists for the western populations however. These weigh 3.4–7.8 kg (7.5–17 lb), 6.4 kilograms (14&#160;lb) on average in males and 5.7 kilograms (13&#160;lb) in females. They measure 115–140 cm (45–55 in) in overall length; each wing is 46.9–54.8 cm (18.5–21.6 in) long, on average 51.9 centimetres (20.4&#160;in) in males and 50.4 centimetres (19.8&#160;in) in females. The <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> measures 9.2–11.6 cm (3.6–4.6 in) in length, the bill 8.2–10.2 cm (3.2–4.0 in), averaging 9.1 centimetres (3.6&#160;in). Bewick's Swan is similar in appearance to the <a href="/wiki/Parapatric" title="Parapatric" class="mw-redirect">parapatric</a> <a href="/wiki/Whooper_Swan" title="Whooper Swan">Whooper Swan</a> (<i>C. cygnus</i>), but is smaller, shorter-necked and has a more rounded head shape, with variable <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> pattern, but always showing more black than yellow and having a blunt forward edge of the yellow base patch. Whooper Swans have a bill that has more yellow than black and the forward edge of the yellow patch is usually pointed. The bill pattern for every individual Bewick's Swan is unique, and scientists often make detailed drawings of each bill and assign names to the swans to assist with studying these <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>. The eastern birds, apart from being larger, tend towards less yellow on the bill, perhaps indicating that <a href="/wiki/Gene_flow" title="Gene flow">gene flow</a> across <a href="/wiki/Beringia" title="Beringia">Beringia</a>, while marginal, never entirely ceased. An apparent case of <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridization</a> between a Bewick's and a vagrant Whistling Swan has been reported from eastern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mbc8792_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mbc8792-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><b>Whistling Swans</b> weigh 9.5–21 lb (4.3–9.5 kg) – 16 pounds (7.3&#160;kg) on average in males and 14 pounds (6.4&#160;kg) in females –, and measure 47–59 in (120–150 cm) in length. Each wing is 19.7–22.4 in (50–57 cm) long; the tarsus measures 3.7–4.5 in (9.4–11 cm) in length, and the bill is 3.6–4.2 in (9.1–11 cm) long. <i>C. c. columbianus</i> is distinguished from <i>C. c. bewickii</i> by its larger size and the mostly black bill, with just a small and usually hard to see yellow spot of variable size at the base. It is distinguished from the largely <a href="/wiki/Allopatric" title="Allopatric" class="mw-redirect">allopatric</a> <a href="/wiki/Trumpeter_Swan" title="Trumpeter Swan">Trumpeter Swan</a> (<i>C. buccinator</i>) of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> by that species' much larger size and particularly long bill, which is black all over except for the pink mouthline, which is stronger than in the Whistling Swan.<sup id="cite_ref-mbc8792_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mbc8792-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Note that color variations with more or less yellow, or pink instead of yellow or black, are not exceptional, especially in Bewick's Swans, which very rarely may even have yellowish feet. The small size and particularly the rather short neck, which make it look like a large white <a href="/wiki/Goose" title="Goose">goose</a>, are still distinguishing marks.<sup id="cite_ref-mbc8792_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mbc8792-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Tundra Swans have high-pitched honking calls and sound similar to a <a href="/wiki/Black_goose" title="Black goose" class="mw-redirect">black goose</a> (<i>Branta</i>). They are particularly vocal when foraging in flocks on their wintering grounds; any <a href="/wiki/Conspecific" title="Conspecific" class="mw-redirect">conspecific</a> arriving or leaving will elicit a bout of loud excited calling from its fellows. Contrary to its common name, the ground calls of the Whistling Swan are not a whistle and neither notably different from that of Bewick's Swan. The flight call of the latter is a low and soft ringing bark, <i>bow-wow...</i>; the Whistling Swan gives a markedly high-pitched trisyllabic bark like <i>wow-wow-wow</i> in flight. By contrast, the Whooper and Trumpeter Swans' names accurately describe their calls – a deep hooting and a higher-pitched <a href="/wiki/French_horn" title="French horn" class="mw-redirect">French horn</a>-like honk, respectively. Flying birds of these species are shorter-necked and have a quicker wingbeat than their relatives, but they are often impossible to tell apart except by their calls.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Tundra_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Flock-of-tundra-swans.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Flock-of-tundra-swans.jpg/220px-Flock-of-tundra-swans.jpg" width="220" height="137" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flock-of-tundra-swans.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Flock of adult and young Whistling Swans</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As their common name implies, the Tundra Swan breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subarctic" title="Subarctic">subarctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>, where they inhabit shallow <a href="/wiki/Stream_pool" title="Stream pool">pools</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rivers" title="Rivers" class="mw-redirect">rivers</a>. These birds, unlike <a href="/wiki/Mute_Swans" title="Mute Swans" class="mw-redirect">Mute Swans</a> (<i>C. olor</i>) but like the other Arctic swans, are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory birds</a>. The winter <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> of both subspecies is <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marshland" title="Marshland" class="mw-redirect">marshland</a>, often near the coast; they like to visit fields after harvest to feed on discarded grains and while on migration may stop over on <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountain</a> lakes.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from March 2012">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> According to <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">National Geographic</a>, when migrating these birds can fly at altitudes of 8&#160;km (nearly 27,000&#160;ft);<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from October 2007">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Tundra Swan flocks usually fly in <a href="/wiki/V_formation" title="V formation">V formation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mbc8792_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mbc8792-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The breeding range of <i>C. c. bewickii</i> extends across the coastal lowlands of <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, from the <a href="/wiki/Kola_Peninsula" title="Kola Peninsula">Kola Peninsula</a> east to the <a href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific" class="mw-redirect">Pacific</a>. They start to arrive on the breeding grounds around mid-May, and leave for winter quarters around the end of September. The populations west of the <a href="/wiki/Taimyr_Peninsula" title="Taimyr Peninsula" class="mw-redirect">Taimyr Peninsula</a> migrate via the <a href="/wiki/White_Sea" title="White Sea">White Sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Elbe" title="Elbe">Elbe</a> <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary</a> to winter in <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>. They are common in winter in the <a href="/wiki/Wildfowl" title="Wildfowl" class="mw-redirect">wildfowl</a> <a href="/wiki/Nature_reserve" title="Nature reserve">nature reserves</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Protection_of_Birds" title="Royal Society for the Protection of Birds">Royal Society for the Protection of Birds</a> and of the <a href="/wiki/Wildfowl_and_Wetlands_Trust" title="Wildfowl and Wetlands Trust" class="mw-redirect">Wildfowl and Wetlands Trust</a>. Some birds also winter elsewhere on the southern shores of the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>. Bewick's Swans breeding in eastern Russia migrate via <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> to winter in the coastal regions of <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, and southern China, south to <a href="/wiki/Guangdong" title="Guangdong">Guangdong</a> and occasionally as far as <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>. A few birds from the central Siberian range also winter in <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> at the south of the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>; in former times these flocks also migrated to the <a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral Sea</a> before the late 20th century ecological catastrophe turned most of the habitat there into inhospitable wasteland. Arrival in winter quarters starts about mid-October, though most spend weeks or even months at favorite resting locations and will only arrive in winter quarters by November or even as late as January. The birds leave winter quarters to breed starting in mid-February. Vagrants may occur south of the main wintering range in cold years and have been recorded from most <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> countries where the birds do not regularly winter, as well as <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, NW <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, and on the <a href="/wiki/Marianas" title="Marianas" class="mw-redirect">Marianas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volcano_Islands" title="Volcano Islands">Volcano Islands</a> in the western <a href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific" class="mw-redirect">Pacific</a>. Vagrants on the spring migration have been sighted on <a href="/wiki/Bear_Island_(Norway)" title="Bear Island (Norway)">Bear Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">Svalbard</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sasketchewan" title="Sasketchewan" class="mw-redirect">Sasketchewan</a> in North America.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>C. c. columbianus</i> breeds in the coastal plains of Alaska and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, leaving for winter quarters about October. They arrive in winter quarters by November/December. Birds breeding in western Alaska winter along the <a href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific" class="mw-redirect">Pacific</a> coast from southern Alaska to <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>; they often move inland – particularly to the rich feeding grounds in the <a href="/wiki/Californian_Central_Valley" title="Californian Central Valley" class="mw-redirect">Californian Central Valley</a> – and some cross the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a> again and winter as far east as <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a> and south to <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. The birds breeding along the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> coast migrate via Canada and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> region to winter at the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic" class="mw-redirect">Atlantic</a> coast of the USA, mainly from <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> to <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, but some move as far south as <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>. Whistling Swans start leaving for the breeding grounds again by mid-March, and arrive by late May. Vagrants have been recorded on the <a href="/wiki/Bermudas" title="Bermudas" class="mw-redirect">Bermudas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, northeastern Siberia and <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mbc8792_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mbc8792-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Tundra_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cygnus_bewickii_03.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Cygnus_bewickii_03.jpg/220px-Cygnus_bewickii_03.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cygnus_bewickii_03.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A Bewick's Swan feeding by upending</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In summer, their diet consists mainly of aquatic vegetation – e.g. <a href="/wiki/Mannagrass" title="Mannagrass" class="mw-redirect">mannagrass</a> (<i>Glyceria</i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Potamogeton" title="Potamogeton">Potamogeton</a></i> pondweeds and <a href="/wiki/Marine_eelgrass" title="Marine eelgrass" class="mw-redirect">marine eelgrass</a> (<i>Zostera</i>), acquired by sticking the head underwater or upending while swimming; they also eat some <a href="/wiki/Grass" title="Grass">grass</a> growing on dry land. At other times of year, leftover <a href="/wiki/Grain" title="Grain" class="mw-redirect">grains</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Crop" title="Crop">crops</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potatoes</a>, picked up in open fields after harvest, make up much of their diet. Tundra Swans forage mainly by day. In the breeding season, they tend to be territorial and are aggressive to many animals who pass by; outside the breeding season they are rather gregarious birds.<sup id="cite_ref-mbc8792_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mbc8792-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Healthy adult birds have few natural predators. <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Fox" title="Arctic Fox" class="mw-redirect">Arctic Foxes</a> (<i>Vulpes lagopus</i>) may threaten breeding females and particular the eggs and hatchlings; they can be quite hard to scare away<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from January 2009">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">Raccoons</a> (<i>Procyon lotor</i>), very common in Canada, <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, eat eggs and young on occasion<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from January 2009">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. About 15% of the adults die each year from various causes, and thus the average lifespan in the wild is about 10 years. The oldest recorded Tundra Swan was over 24 years old.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Tundra_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span></h3>
<p>The Tundra Swans mate in the late spring, usually after they have returned to the <a href="/wiki/Nesting" title="Nesting">nesting</a> grounds; as usual for swans, they pair <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" title="Monogamous" class="mw-redirect">monogamously</a> until one partner dies. Should one partner die long before the other, the surviving bird often will not mate again for some years, or even for its entire life. The nesting season starts at the end of May. The pair build the large mound-shaped nest from plant material at an elevated site near open water, and defend a large territory around it. The pen (female) lays and incubates a <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> of 2-7 (usually 3-5) eggs, watching for danger while sitting on the nest. The cob (male) keeps a steady lookout for potential <a href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator" class="mw-redirect">predators</a> heading towards his mate and offspring<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from January 2009">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. When either of them spots a threat, they give a warning sound to let their partner know that danger is approaching<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from January 2009">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Sometimes the cob will use his wings to run faster and appear larger in order to scare away a predator<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from January 2009">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-mbc8792_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mbc8792-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The time from laying to hatching is 29–30 days for Bewick's Swan and 30–32 days for the Whistling Swan. Since they nest in cold regions, Tundra Swan <a href="/wiki/Cygnet_(bird)" title="Cygnet (bird)" class="mw-redirect">cygnets</a> grow faster than those of swans breeding in warmer climates; those of the Whistling Swan take about 60–75 days to fledge – twice as fast as those of the Mute Swan for example – while those of Bewick's Swan, about which little breeding data is known, may fledge a record 40–45 days after hatching already. The fledglings stay with their parents for the first winter migration. The family is sometimes even joined by their offspring from previous breeding seasons while on the wintering grounds; Tundra Swans do not reach sexual maturity until 3 or 4 years of age.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Tundra_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span></h3>
<p>The Whistling Swan is the most common swan species of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, estimated to number almost 170,000 individuals around 1990. Its numbers seem to be slowly declining in the west of its range since the late 19th century, coincident with the expansion of human settlement and habitat conversion in the birds' wintering areas; the eastern Whistling Swan populations on the other hand seem to be increasing somewhat, and altogether its numbers seem to have slightly risen in the late 20th century (the population was estimated at about 146,000 in 1972). Bewick's Swan remains far less known; the European winter population was estimated at 16,000-17,000 about 1990, with about 20,000 birds wintering in <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>. The Iranian wintering population is small – 1,000 birds or so at most – but they usually disperse to several sites, some of which are still unknown to scientists.<sup id="cite_ref-mbc8792_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mbc8792-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Although Tundra Swan numbers are stable over most of its range, they are increasingly dependent on agricultural <a href="/wiki/Crop" title="Crop">crops</a> to supplement their winter diet, as aquatic vegetation in their winter habitat dwindles due to <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a>. But the main cause of adult mortality is hunting; 4,000 Whistling Swans are bagged officially each year, while a further 6,000-10,000 are killed by <a href="/wiki/Poacher" title="Poacher" class="mw-redirect">poachers</a> and native <a href="/wiki/Subsistence" title="Subsistence" class="mw-redirect">subsistence</a> hunter-gatherers. Bewick's Swan cannot be hunted legally, but almost half the birds studied contained <a href="/wiki/Lead_shot" title="Lead shot">lead shot</a> in their body, indicating they were shot at by poachers. <a href="/wiki/Lead_poisoning" title="Lead poisoning">Lead poisoning</a> by ingestion of lead shot is a very significant cause of mortality also, particularly in the Whistling Swan. The Tundra Swan is not considered threatened by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN" class="mw-redirect">IUCN</a> due to its large range and population.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The proposed <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> <i>jankowskii</i> was for some time placed on <a href="/wiki/CITES" title="CITES">CITES</a> Appendix II; it was eventually removed since it is not generally accepted as valid.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Bewick's Swan is one of the birds to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>
<p>Toxic mining wastes in the <a href="/wiki/Silver_Valley,_Idaho" title="Silver Valley, Idaho">Silver Valley, Idaho</a> in the United States is responsible for the death of migrating tundra swans.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
