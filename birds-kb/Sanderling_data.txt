<p>The <b>sanderling</b> (<i>Calidris alba</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wading</a> bird. The name derives from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>sand-yrðling</i>, "sand-ploughman".<sup id="cite_ref-OED_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The genus name is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>kalidris</i> or <i>skalidris</i>, a term used by <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> for some grey-coloured waterside birds. The specific <i>alba</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "white".<sup id="cite_ref-job_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is a circumpolar <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> breeder, and is a long-distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>, wintering south to <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, South <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. It is highly gregarious in winter, sometimes forming large flocks on coastal mudflats or sandy beaches.
</p><p>It is somewhat unlike other <a href="/wiki/Sandpiper" title="Sandpiper">sandpipers</a> in appearance, which has led to the suggestion that it should be placed into a <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monotypic">monotypic</a> genus <i>Crocethia</i>. A more recent review (Thomas <i>et al.</i>, 2004) indicates, however, that the sanderling is a fairly typical "<a href="/wiki/Stint" title="Stint">stint</a>" or small sandpiper and should be separated from the large knots with its closest relatives in a distinct genus.
</p><p>This bird is similar in size to a <a href="/wiki/Dunlin" title="Dunlin">dunlin</a>, but stouter, with a thick bill. It shows a strong white wingbar in flight, and runs along the sandy beaches it prefers with a characteristic "bicycling" action of its legs, stopping frequently to pick small food items. It eats small <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a> and other small <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>. In spring, birds migrating north from South America consume large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe_crab" title="Horseshoe crab">horseshoe crab</a> eggs in the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Bay" title="Delaware Bay">Delaware Bay</a> area.
</p><p>In spring, the birds arrive on the High Arctic breeding grounds (see map), where they lay 3–4 eggs in a ground scrape. On the nesting grounds, these birds mainly eat <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and some plant material.
</p><p>The sanderling was described by the German naturalist <a href="/wiki/Peter_Simon_Pallas" title="Peter Simon Pallas">Peter Simon Pallas</a> in 1764 and given the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> <i>Trynga alba</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The sanderling is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanderling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The sanderling is a small plump sandpiper, 18–20&#160;cm (7.1–7.9&#160;in) in length. Its weight ranges from 40–100&#160;g (1.4–3.5&#160;oz). The winter <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> is very pale, almost white apart from a dark shoulder patch. This is the source of the specific name, <i>alba</i>, which is the Latin for "white". Later in the summer, the face and throat become brick-red. The juvenile bird is spangled black and white, and shows much more contrast than the adult.

<p>If its size is misjudged, a sanderling in breeding plumage can be mistaken for some varieties of <a href="/wiki/Stint" title="Stint">stint</a>, or a sanderling in winter plumage can be mistaken for a <a href="/wiki/Dunlin" title="Dunlin">dunlin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Red_knot" title="Red knot">red knot</a>. It can be told from other small wading birds, given good views, by its lack of a hind toe. Its behavior is also distinctive.
</p>
<h2><span id="Distribution.2C_habitat_and_migration"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution,_habitat_and_migration">Distribution, habitat and migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanderling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution, habitat and migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sanderlin_feeding.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Sanderlin_feeding.jpg/220px-Sanderlin_feeding.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="194" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Sanderlin_feeding.jpg/330px-Sanderlin_feeding.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Sanderlin_feeding.jpg/440px-Sanderlin_feeding.jpg 2x" data-file-width="873" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sanderlin_feeding.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>at Kutch</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Calidris_alba_at_Ocean_Beach,_San_Francisco,_California_-_20101116.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Calidris_alba_at_Ocean_Beach%2C_San_Francisco%2C_California_-_20101116.jpg/300px-Calidris_alba_at_Ocean_Beach%2C_San_Francisco%2C_California_-_20101116.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="118" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Calidris_alba_at_Ocean_Beach%2C_San_Francisco%2C_California_-_20101116.jpg/450px-Calidris_alba_at_Ocean_Beach%2C_San_Francisco%2C_California_-_20101116.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Calidris_alba_at_Ocean_Beach%2C_San_Francisco%2C_California_-_20101116.jpg/600px-Calidris_alba_at_Ocean_Beach%2C_San_Francisco%2C_California_-_20101116.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2777" data-file-height="1096" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Calidris_alba_at_Ocean_Beach,_San_Francisco,_California_-_20101116.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sanderlings at Ocean Beach, San Francisco</div></div></div>
<p>The sanderling breeds in the High Arctic areas of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. In North America, it breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Arctic_Archipelago" class="mw-redirect" title="Canadian Arctic Archipelago">Canadian Arctic Archipelago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nunavut" title="Nunavut">Nunavut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> (and to a lesser extent <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>). In <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurasia</a>, it breeds in <a href="/wiki/Spitsbergen" title="Spitsbergen">Spitsbergen</a> and areas of northern Russia from the <a href="/wiki/Taymyr_Peninsula" title="Taymyr Peninsula">Taymyr Peninsula</a> to the <a href="/wiki/New_Siberian_Islands" title="New Siberian Islands">New Siberian Islands</a>. In the northern winter, it has a nearly <a href="/wiki/Cosmopolitan_distribution" title="Cosmopolitan distribution">cosmopolitan distribution</a> across the world's marine coasts. It is a complete <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>, travelling between 3,000 to 10,000&#160;km (1,900 to 6,200&#160;mi) from its breeding grounds to its wintering sites. Birds that travel further also arrive later and leave sooner. Most adults leave the breeding grounds in July and early August, whereas juvenile birds leave in late August and early September. The northward migration begins in March at the southern end of their winter distribution.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The breeding habitat of the sanderling is coastal <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> north of 5&#160;°C (41&#160;°F) July <a href="/wiki/Isotherm_(contour_line)" class="mw-redirect" title="Isotherm (contour line)">isotherm</a>. The species typically chooses nesting sites on dry stony areas near wet areas, from 60&#160;m (200&#160;ft) above sea level to 800&#160;m (2,600&#160;ft). During the winter and its migration, it is most commonly found on coastal sandy <a href="/wiki/Beach" title="Beach">beaches</a>, but also occurs on <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tidal</a> sand flats, mud flats and the shores of lakes and rivers. More infrequently, it may occur on rocky shores.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanderling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The sanderling consists of two subspecies:
</p>
<ul><li><i>C. a. alba</i>, <small>(<a href="/wiki/Peter_Simon_Pallas" title="Peter Simon Pallas">Pallas</a>, 1764)</small>, breeds on <a href="/wiki/Ellesmere_Island" title="Ellesmere Island">Ellesmere Island</a>, north &amp; east <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">Svalbard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franz_Josef_Land" title="Franz Josef Land">Franz Josef Land</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Taymyr_Peninsula" title="Taymyr Peninsula">Taymyr Peninsula</a></li>
<li><i>C. a. rubida</i>, <small>(<a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Gmelin" title="Johann Friedrich Gmelin">Gmelin</a>, 1789)</small>, breeds in northeast <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanderling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding_behaviour">Feeding behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanderling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Feeding behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sanderlings feed on invertebrate <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">prey</a> buried in the sand in the upper <a href="/wiki/Intertidal_zone" title="Intertidal zone">intertidal zone</a>. In North America, this diet largely consists of the <a href="/wiki/Isopoda" title="Isopoda">isopods</a> <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Excirolana_linguifrons&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Excirolana linguifrons (page does not exist)">Excirolana linguifrons</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Excirolana_kincaidii&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Excirolana kincaidii (page does not exist)">Excirolana kincaidii</a></i>, and the mole crab, <i><a href="/wiki/Emerita_analoga" title="Emerita analoga">Emerita analoga</a></i>. When the tide is out, these <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> live in burrows some way beneath the surface. When the tide comes in, they move into the upper layers of sand and feed on the <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Detritus" title="Detritus">detritus</a> that washes over them with each wave. They then burrow rapidly down again as the water retreats. They leave no marks on the surface, so the sanderlings hunt for them by plunging their beaks into the sand at random, consuming whatever they find. Their bills can penetrate only 2 or 3&#160;cm (0.79 or 1.18&#160;in) and as the water swirls around and retreats, the sand is softer; this makes it easier for the birds' beaks to penetrate further. In the spring, when much breeding activity is taking place in the <a href="/wiki/Benthos" title="Benthos">benthic</a> community, there may be as many as 4000 invertebrates per square metre, but their average size is smaller than later in the year. The birds appear to rush madly around at the edge of the surf, but in reality they are maximising their chances of catching as many prey animals as possible when they are at their most vulnerable near the surface.<sup id="cite_ref-Schultz1990_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schultz1990-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding_behaviour">Breeding behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanderling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At breeding time sanderlings are <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territorial</a>, with the male aggressively defending its territory. They may either form <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous pairs</a> or <a href="/wiki/Polyandrous" class="mw-redirect" title="Polyandrous">polyandrous</a> (one female and two male) pairings.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
