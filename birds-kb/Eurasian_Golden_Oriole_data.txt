<p>The <b>Eurasian golden oriole</b> (<i>Oriolus oriolus</i>) or simply <b>golden oriole</b>, is the only member of the <a href="/wiki/Old_World_oriole" title="Old World oriole">oriole</a> family of <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> breeding in Northern Hemisphere temperate regions. It is a summer <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a> in Europe and <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">western Asia</a> and spends the winter season in central and southern Africa.
</p><p>Golden orioles have an extremely large range with large populations that are apparently stable. Therefore, they are evaluated as <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a> by <a href="/wiki/BirdLife_International" title="BirdLife International">BirdLife International</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_golden_oriole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Eurasian golden oriole was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">tenth edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> and given the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> <i>Coracias oriolus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Eurasian golden oriole and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_golden_oriole" title="Indian golden oriole">Indian golden oriole</a> were formerly considered as <a href="/wiki/Conspecific" class="mw-redirect" title="Conspecific">conspecific</a>, but in 2005 they were treated as separate species by the ornithologists <a href="/wiki/Pamela_C._Rasmussen" title="Pamela C. Rasmussen">Pamela Rasmussen</a> and John Anderton in the first edition of their <i><a href="/wiki/Birds_of_South_Asia._The_Ripley_Guide" class="mw-redirect" title="Birds of South Asia. The Ripley Guide">Birds of South Asia</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Support for this split was provided by a <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular phylogenetic">molecular phylogenetic</a> study published in 2010,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and most ornitholists now treat the Indian golden oriole as a separate species.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Alternate names for the Eurasian golden oriole include the <b>European golden oriole</b> and <b>western Eurasian golden oriole</b>. The species is <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monotypic">monotypic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_golden_oriole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The name "oriole" was first used in the 18th century and is an adaptation of the scientific <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> genus name, which is derived from the Classical Latin "aureolus" meaning <i>golden</i>. Various forms of "oriole" have existed in <a href="/wiki/Romance_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Romance language">Romance languages</a> since the 12th and 13th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Albertus_Magnus" title="Albertus Magnus">Albertus Magnus</a> used the Latin form <i>oriolus</i> in about 1250 and erroneously stated that it was <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeic</a> because of the golden oriole's song. In medieval England its name, derived from the song, was the <i>woodwele</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_golden_oriole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The male is striking in the typical oriole black and yellow plumage, but the female is a drabber green bird. Orioles are shy, and even the male is remarkably difficult to see in the dappled yellow and green leaves of the canopy. In flight they look somewhat like a <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a>, strong and direct with some shallow dips over longer distances. The <a href="/wiki/New_World_oriole" title="New World oriole">New World orioles</a> are similar in appearance to the Oriolidae, but are <a href="/wiki/Icterid" title="Icterid">icterids</a> and unrelated to the Old World birds.
</p><p>The male of the Indian golden oriole (<i>Oriolus </i>) has the black eye-stripe extending behind the eye, has a longer and paler red bill and has more yellow in the plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Their call is a screech like a <a href="/wiki/Jay" title="Jay">jay</a>, but the song is a beautiful fluting <i>weela-wee-ooo</i> or <i>or-iii-ole</i>, unmistakable once heard.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_golden_oriole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The breeding range of this species spans from western Europe and <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> east to China. They winter in central and southern Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> They generally migrate during the night, but may travel during the day in the spring migration. During the autumn migration they migrate via the Eastern Mediterranean where they feed on fruit; they are often considered a pest in this region because of this.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Eurasian golden oriole inhabits a range of habitats. In Western Europe they prefer open broadleaf forests and plantations, copses, riverine forest, orchards, large gardens; in Eastern Europe they may inhabit more continuous forest as well as mixed or coniferous forests. They generally avoid treeless habitats but may forage there. In their wintering habitat they are found in semi-arid to humid woodland, tall forests, riverine forest, woodland/savanna mosaic and savanna.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_golden_oriole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_golden_oriole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Eurasian golden orioles may delay breeding until they are 2 or 3 years of age. Males usually arrive at breeding area several days before the females. The fidelity to a territory or even to a specific nest site suggests that the pair-bond may continue from one breeding season to the next.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1993421_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1993421-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The nest is placed high in a tree towards the edge of the crown. The deep cup-shaped nest is suspended below a horizontal fork of thin branches. It is built by the female, but the male will sometimes gather some of the material. The nest is held in place by plant fibres up to 40&#160;cm (16&#160;in) in length and lined with fine grass, feathers and wool. The clutch is usually between 3 and 5 eggs. These are laid at daily intervals early in the morning. The eggs are on average 30.4&#160;mm ×&#160;21.3&#160;mm (1.20&#160;in ×&#160;0.84&#160;in) with a calculated weight of 7.3&#160;g (0.26&#160;oz). They can be white, cream or very pale pink and are decorated with black marks which are sometimes concentrated at the larger end. The eggs are mainly incubated by the female but the male will incubate for short periods to allow the female to feed. The eggs hatch after 16–17 days. The young are fed by both parents but are mostly brooded by the female. The young fledge after 16–17 days. The clutch is only rarely lost to predators as the parents vigorously defend their nest.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1993429-430_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1993429-430-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The greatest recorded age for a Eurasian golden oriole is 10 years and 1 month for a male that was ringed in Lincolnshire in 1986 and seen alive in Cambridgeshire in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_golden_oriole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>They feed on insects and fruit, using their bills to pick insects out of crevices.
</p>
