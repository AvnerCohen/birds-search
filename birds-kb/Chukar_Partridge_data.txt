<p>The <b>chukar partridge</b> (<i>Alectoris chukar</i>), or simply <b>chukar</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> upland <a href="/wiki/Upland_game" class="mw-redirect" title="Upland game">gamebird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Pheasant" title="Pheasant">pheasant</a> family <a href="/wiki/Phasianidae" title="Phasianidae">Phasianidae</a>. It has been considered to form a superspecies complex along with the <a href="/wiki/Rock_partridge" title="Rock partridge">rock partridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philby%27s_partridge" title="Philby&#39;s partridge">Philby's partridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Przevalski%27s_partridge" title="Przevalski&#39;s partridge">Przevalski's partridge</a> and treated in the past as conspecific particularly with the first. This <a href="/wiki/Partridge" title="Partridge">partridge</a> has well marked black and white bars on the flanks and a black band running from the forehead across the eye and running down the head to form a necklace that encloses a white throat. The species has been introduced into many other places and feral populations have established themselves in parts of North America and New Zealand. This bird can be found in parts of the Middle East and temperate Asia.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukar_partridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alectoris_chukar_hm.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Alectoris_chukar_hm.jpg/220px-Alectoris_chukar_hm.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="202" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Alectoris_chukar_hm.jpg/330px-Alectoris_chukar_hm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Alectoris_chukar_hm.jpg/440px-Alectoris_chukar_hm.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="587" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alectoris_chukar_hm.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration from <a href="/wiki/Allan_Octavian_Hume" title="Allan Octavian Hume">Hume</a> and Marshall's <i>Game Birds of India, Burma and Ceylon</i></div></div></div>
<p>The chukar is a rotund 32–35&#160;cm (13–14&#160;in) long partridge, with a light brown back, grey breast, and buff belly. The shades vary across the various populations. The face is white with a black <a href="/wiki/Gorget_(birds)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gorget (birds)">gorget</a>. It has <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rufous" class="extiw" title="wikt:rufous">rufous</a>-streaked flanks, red legs and coral red bill. Sexes are similar, the female slightly smaller in size and lacking the spur.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The tail has 14 feathers, the third primary is the longest while the first is level with the fifth and sixth primaries.<sup id="cite_ref-fbi_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fbi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is very similar to the <a href="/wiki/Rock_partridge" title="Rock partridge">rock partridge</a> (<i>Alectoris graeca</i>) with which it has been lumped in the past<sup id="cite_ref-watson_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-watson-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> but is browner on the back and has a yellowish tinge to the foreneck. The sharply defined gorget distinguishes this species from the <a href="/wiki/Red-legged_partridge" title="Red-legged partridge">red-legged partridge</a> which has the black collar breaking into dark streaks near the breast. Their song is a noisy <i>chuck-chuck-chukar-chukar</i> from which the name is derived.<sup id="cite_ref-fbi2_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fbi2-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Barbary_partridge" title="Barbary partridge">Barbary partridge</a> (<i>Alectoris barbara</i>) has a reddish-brown rather than black collar with a grey throat and face with a chestnut crown.<sup id="cite_ref-johnsgard_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-johnsgard-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other common names of this bird include <b>chukker</b> (<b>chuker</b> or <b>chukor</b>), <b>Indian chukar</b> and <b>keklik</b>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukar_partridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Partridge" title="Partridge">partridge</a> has its native range in Asia, including <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel_(region)" class="mw-redirect" title="Israel (region)">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/State_of_Palestine" title="State of Palestine">Palestine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, along the inner ranges of the western <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> to <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>. Further west in southeastern Europe it is replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Red-legged_partridge" title="Red-legged partridge">red-legged partridge</a>, <i>Alectoris rufa</i>. It barely ranges into Africa on the <a href="/wiki/Sinai_Peninsula" title="Sinai Peninsula">Sinai Peninsula</a>. The habitat in the native range is rocky open hillsides with grass or scattered scrub or cultivation. In Israel and <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a> it is found at low altitudes, starting at 400&#160;m (1,300&#160;ft) below sea level in the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a> area, whereas in the more eastern areas it is mainly found at an altitude of 2,000 to 4,000&#160;m (6,600 to 13,100&#160;ft) except in Pakistan, where it occurs at 600&#160;m (2,000&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-whistler_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whistler-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> They are not found in areas of high humidity or rainfall.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_gbi_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker_gbi-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It has been <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced</a> widely as a game bird, and feral populations have become established in the United States (<a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Basin" title="Great Basin">Great Basin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mojave_Desert" title="Mojave Desert">high desert areas of California</a>), <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Initial introductions into the US were from the nominate populations collected from Afghanistan and Nepal.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It has also been introduced to <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> in Australia but breeding populations have not persisted and are probably extinct.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> A small population exists on <a href="/wiki/Robben_Island" title="Robben Island">Robben Island</a> in South Africa since it was introduced there in 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The chukar readily interbreeds with the <a href="/wiki/Red-legged_partridge" title="Red-legged partridge">red-legged partridge</a> (<i>Alectoris rufa</i>), and the practice of breeding and releasing captive-bred hybrids has been banned in various countries including the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, as it is a threat to wild populations.<sup id="cite_ref-GWCT_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GWCT-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Systematics_and_taxonomy">Systematics and taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukar_partridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Systematics and taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alectoris_chukar_7_-_Israel.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Alectoris_chukar_7_-_Israel.jpg/220px-Alectoris_chukar_7_-_Israel.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Alectoris_chukar_7_-_Israel.jpg/330px-Alectoris_chukar_7_-_Israel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Alectoris_chukar_7_-_Israel.jpg/440px-Alectoris_chukar_7_-_Israel.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1404" data-file-height="936" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alectoris_chukar_7_-_Israel.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Alectoris chukar</i></div></div></div>
<p>The chukar partridge is part of a confusing group of "red-legged partridges". Several plumage variations within the widespread distribution of the chukar partridge have been described and designated as subspecies. In the past the chukar group was included with the <a href="/wiki/Rock_partridge" title="Rock partridge">rock partridge</a> (also known as the Greek partridge). The species from Turkey and farther east was subsequently separated from <i>A. graeca</i> of Greece and Bulgaria and western Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-christensen_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-christensen-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukar_partridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are fourteen recognized <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:
</p>
<ul><li><i>A. c. chukar</i> (J. E. Gray, 1830) – nominate – eastern <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> to eastern <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a></li>
<li><i>A. c. cypriotes</i> (<a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a>, 1917) – island chukar – southeastern <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a> to southern <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhodes" title="Rhodes">Rhodes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a></li>
<li><i>A. c. dzungarica</i> (Sushkin, 1927) – northwestern <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Altai_Mountains" title="Altai Mountains">Russian Altai</a> and eastern <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a></li>
<li><i>A. c. falki</i> (Hartert, 1917) – north central Afghanistan to <a href="/wiki/Pamir_Mountains" title="Pamir Mountains">Pamir Mountains</a> and western <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a></li>
<li><i>A. c. kleini</i> (Hartert, 1925)</li>
<li><i>A. c. koroviakovi</i> (<a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Zarudny" title="Nikolai Zarudny">Zarudny</a>, 1914) – Persian chukar – eastern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> to <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a></li>
<li><i>A. c. kurdestanica</i> (<a href="/wiki/Richard_Meinertzhagen" title="Richard Meinertzhagen">Meinertzhagen</a>, 1923) – Kurdestan chukar – <a href="/wiki/Caucasus_Mountains" title="Caucasus Mountains">Caucasus Mountains</a> to Iran</li>
<li><i>A. c. pallescens</i> (<a href="/wiki/Allan_Octavian_Hume" title="Allan Octavian Hume">Hume</a>, 1873) – northern chukar – northeastern Afghanistan to <a href="/wiki/Ladakh" title="Ladakh">Ladakh</a> and western Tibet</li>
<li><i>A. c. pallida</i> (Hume, 1873) – northwestern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a></li>
<li><i>A. c. potanini</i> (Sushkin, 1927) – western Mongolia</li>
<li><i>A. c. pubescens</i> (<a href="/wiki/Robert_Swinhoe" title="Robert Swinhoe">R. Swinhoe</a>, 1871) – inner Mongolia to northwestern <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">Sichuan</a> and eastern <a href="/wiki/Qinghai" title="Qinghai">Qinghai</a></li>
<li><i>A. c. sinaica</i> (<a href="/wiki/Charles_Lucien_Bonaparte" title="Charles Lucien Bonaparte">Bonaparte</a>, 1858) – northern <a href="/wiki/Syrian_Desert" title="Syrian Desert">Syrian Desert</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sinai_Peninsula" title="Sinai Peninsula">Sinai Peninsula</a></li>
<li><i>A. c. subpallida</i> (Zarudny, 1914) – <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a> (Kyzyl Kum and Kara Kum mountains)</li>
<li><i>A. c. werae</i> (Zarudny and Loudon, 1904) – Iranian chukar – eastern <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> and southwestern Iran</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_and_status">Population and status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukar_partridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Population and status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:AntelopeIsland_Chukar.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/AntelopeIsland_Chukar.JPG/220px-AntelopeIsland_Chukar.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/AntelopeIsland_Chukar.JPG/330px-AntelopeIsland_Chukar.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/AntelopeIsland_Chukar.JPG/440px-AntelopeIsland_Chukar.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:AntelopeIsland_Chukar.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chukar partridge (right) in the <a href="/wiki/Antelope_Island_State_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="Antelope Island State Park">Antelope Island State Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>, US</div></div></div>
<p>This species is relatively unaffected by hunting or loss of habitat. Its numbers are largely affected by weather patterns during the breeding season. The release of captive stock in some parts of southern Europe can threaten native populations of rock partridge and red-legged partridge with which they may hybridize.<sup id="cite_ref-Barilani_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barilani-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Duarte_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duarte-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>British sportsmen in India considered the chukar as good sport although they were not considered to be particularly good in flavour. Their fast flight and ability to fly some distance after being shot made recovery of the birds difficult without retriever dogs.<sup id="cite_ref-humegame_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-humegame-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> During cold winters, when the higher areas are covered in snow, people in <a href="/wiki/Kashmir" title="Kashmir">Kashmir</a> have been known to use a technique to tire the birds out to catch them.<sup id="cite_ref-Ludlow_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ludlow-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukar_partridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the non-breeding season, chukar partridge are found in small coveys of 10 or more (up to 50) birds. In summer, chukars form pairs to breed. During this time, the cocks are very pugnacious calling and fighting.<sup id="cite_ref-whistler_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whistler-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-baker_gbi_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker_gbi-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-finn_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-finn-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbk_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> During winter they descend into the valleys and feed in fields. They call frequently during the day and especially in the mornings and evenings. The call is loud and includes loud repeated <i>chuck</i> notes and sometimes duetting <i>chuker</i> notes. Several calls varying with context have been noted.<sup id="cite_ref-Stokes_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stokes-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The most common call is a "rallying call" which when played back elicits a response from birds and has been used in surveys, although the method is not very reliable.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bohl_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bohl-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> When disturbed, it prefers to run rather than fly, but if necessary it flies a short distance often down a slope on rounded wings, calling immediately after alighting.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-humegame_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-humegame-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-oates_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oates-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In Utah, birds were found to forage in an area of about 2.6&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (1.0&#160;sq&#160;mi) and travel up to 4.8&#160;km (3.0&#160;mi) to obtain water during the dry season. The home range was found to be even smaller in Idaho.<sup id="cite_ref-Walter_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walter-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bump_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bump-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Phelps_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phelps-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The breeding season is summer. Males perform tidbitting displays, a form of <a href="/wiki/Courtship_feeding" class="mw-redirect" title="Courtship feeding">courtship feeding</a> where the male pecks at food and a female may visit to peck in response. The males may chase females with head lowered, wing lowered and neck fluffed. The male may also perform a high step stiff walk while making a special call. The female may then crouch in acceptance and the male mounts to copulate, while grasping the nape of the female. Males are monogamous.<sup id="cite_ref-christensen_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-christensen-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The nest is a scantily lined ground scrape, though occasionally a compact pad is created with a depression in the centre. Generally, the nests are sheltered by ferns and small bushes, or placed in a dip or rocky hillside under an overhanging rock. About 7 to 14 eggs are laid.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_gbi_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker_gbi-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbk_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hume_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hume-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The eggs hatch in about 23–25 days. In captivity they can lay an egg each day during the breeding season if eggs are collected daily.<sup id="cite_ref-Woodard_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Woodard-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Chicks join their parents in foraging and will soon join the chicks of other members of the covey.<sup id="cite_ref-johnsgard_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-johnsgard-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As young chukars grow, and before flying for the first time, they utilize <a href="/wiki/Wing-assisted_incline_running" title="Wing-assisted incline running">wing-assisted incline running</a> as a transition to adult flight. This behaviour is found in several bird species, but has been extensively studied in chukar chicks, as a model to explain the <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_avian_flight" title="Origin of avian flight">evolution of avian flight</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-TobalskeDial2007_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TobalskeDial2007-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dialetal2006_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dialetal2006-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dial2003WAIR_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dial2003WAIR-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BundleDial2003MechanicsOfWAIR_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BundleDial2003MechanicsOfWAIR-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:FarahChukar.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/FarahChukar.png/150px-FarahChukar.png" decoding="async" width="150" height="250" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/FarahChukar.png/225px-FarahChukar.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/FarahChukar.png/300px-FarahChukar.png 2x" data-file-width="717" data-file-height="1194" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:FarahChukar.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A chukar in a 17th-century Persian encyclopedia</div></div></div>
<p>Chukar will take a wide variety of seeds and some <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> as food. It also ingests grit.<sup id="cite_ref-oates_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oates-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In Kashmir, the seeds of a species of <i><a href="/wiki/Eragrostis" title="Eragrostis">Eragrostis</a></i> was particularly dominant in their diet<sup id="cite_ref-Oakleaf_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oakleaf-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> while those in the US favoured <i><a href="/wiki/Bromus_tectorum" title="Bromus tectorum">Bromus tectorum</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-johnsgard_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-johnsgard-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Birds feeding on succulent vegetation make up for their water needs but visit open water in summer.<sup id="cite_ref-Degen_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Degen-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chukar roost on rocky slopes or under shrubs. In the winter, birds in the US selected protected niches or caves. A group may roost in a tight circle with their heads pointed outwards to conserve heat and keep a look out for predators.<sup id="cite_ref-johnsgard_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-johnsgard-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chukar are sometimes preyed on by <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">golden eagles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bbb_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbb-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Birds in captivity can die from <i><a href="/wiki/Mycoplasma" title="Mycoplasma">Mycoplasma</a></i> infection and outbreaks of other diseases such as <a href="/wiki/Erysipelas" title="Erysipelas">erysipelas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lateef_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lateef-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pettit_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pettit-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dubey_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dubey-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukar_partridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The chukar is the <a href="/wiki/National_bird" class="mw-redirect" title="National bird">national bird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Kurdistan_Region_of_Iraq" class="mw-redirect" title="Kurdistan Region of Iraq">Kurdistan Region of Iraq</a>. The name is <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeic" class="mw-redirect" title="Onomatopoeic">onomatopoeic</a> and mentions of <i>chakor</i> in Sanskrit, from northern Indian date back to the <i><a href="/wiki/Markandeya_Purana" title="Markandeya Purana">Markandeya Purana</a></i> (c. 250-500 AD).<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/North_India" title="North India">North Indian</a> and Pakistani culture, as well as in <a href="/wiki/Hindu_mythology" title="Hindu mythology">Indian mythology</a>, the chukar sometimes symbolizes intense, and often unrequited, love.<sup id="cite_ref-temple1884_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-temple1884-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jasb1881_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jasb1881-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> It is said to be in love with the moon and to gaze at it constantly.<sup id="cite_ref-balfour1871_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-balfour1871-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Because of their pugnacious behaviour during the breeding season they are kept in some areas as fighting birds.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_gbi_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker_gbi-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-finn_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-finn-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
