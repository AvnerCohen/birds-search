<p>The <b>Sand Partridge</b>, <i>Ammoperdix heyi</i>, is a <a href="/wiki/Gamebird" title="Gamebird" class="mw-redirect">gamebird</a> in the pheasant family <a href="/wiki/Phasianidae" title="Phasianidae">Phasianidae</a> of the order <a href="/wiki/Galliformes" title="Galliformes">Galliformes</a>, gallinaceous birds.</p>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Partridge" title="Partridge">partridge</a> has its main native range from <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palestine" title="Palestine">Palestine</a> east to south <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabia</a>. It is closely related and similar to its counterpart in southeast <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and east to <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/See-see_Partridge" title="See-see Partridge">See-see Partridge</a>, <i>Ammoperdix griseogularis</i>.</p>
<p>This 22-25 cm <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> is a resident breeder in dry, open and often hilly country. It nests in a scantily lined ground scrape laying 5-7 eggs. The Sand Partridge takes a wide variety of seeds and some insect food.</p>
<p>The Sand Partridge is a rotund bird, mainly sandy-brown with wavy white and brown flank stripes. The male has a grey head with a white stripe in front of the eye and a white cheek patch. The neck sides are plain, and not speckled with white. The head pattern is the best distinction from See-see Partridge.</p>
<p>The female is a very washed-out version of the male, and is more difficult to distinguish from its relative due to the weak head pattern.</p>
<p>When disturbed, Sand Partridge prefers to run rather than fly, but if necessary it flies a short distance on rounded wings. The song is a slurred <i>kwa-kwa-kwa</i>.</p>
