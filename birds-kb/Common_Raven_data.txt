<p>The <b>Common Raven</b> (<i>Corvus corax</i>), also known as the <b>Northern Raven</b>, is a large, all-black <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird. Found across the <a href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere" class="mw-redirect">northern hemisphere</a>, it is the most widely distributed of all <a href="/wiki/Corvidae" title="Corvidae">corvids</a>. There are at least eight <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> with little variation in appearance—although recent research has demonstrated significant genetic differences among populations from various regions. It is one of the two largest corvids, alongside the <a href="/wiki/Thick-billed_Raven" title="Thick-billed Raven">Thick-billed Raven</a>, and is possibly the heaviest passerine bird; at maturity, the Common Raven averages 63&#160;centimetres (25 inches) in length and 1.2 <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kilograms</a> (2.6&#160;<a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)" title="Pound (mass)">pounds</a>) in mass. Common Ravens can live up to 21 years in the wild,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> a lifespan exceeded among passerines by only a few <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasian</a> species such as the <a href="/wiki/Satin_Bowerbird" title="Satin Bowerbird">Satin Bowerbird</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and probably the <a href="/wiki/Lyrebird" title="Lyrebird">lyrebirds</a>. Young birds may travel in flocks but later <a href="/wiki/Mate_for_life" title="Mate for life" class="mw-redirect">mate for life</a>, with each <a href="/wiki/Mated_pair" title="Mated pair" class="mw-redirect">mated pair</a> defending a <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territory</a>.</p>
<p>The Common Raven has coexisted with humans for thousands of years and in some areas have been so numerous that they are considered a <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">pest</a>. Part of its success comes from its <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivorous</a> diet; Common Ravens are extremely versatile and opportunistic in finding sources of nutrition, feeding on <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">carrion</a>, insects, cereal grains, berries, fruit, small animals, and food waste.</p>
<p>Some notable feats of problem-solving have been observed in the species, leading to the belief that it is highly <a href="/wiki/Intelligence" title="Intelligence">intelligent</a>. Over the centuries, it has been the subject of mythology, folklore, <a href="/wiki/Cultural_depictions_of_ravens" title="Cultural depictions of ravens">art, and literature</a>. In many indigenous cultures, including those of <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Scandinavia</a>, ancient Ireland and Wales, Bhutan, the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Pacific_Northwest_Coast" title="Indigenous peoples of the Pacific Northwest Coast">northwest coast of North America</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_Siberia" title="Indigenous peoples of Siberia">Siberia</a> and northeast Asia, the Common Raven has been revered as a spiritual figure or god.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Raven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The Common Raven was one of the many species originally described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in his 18th century work, <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>, and it still bears its original name of <i>Corvus corax</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> It is the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a> of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Corvus_(genus)#List_of_Species_in_the_genus_Corvus" title="Corvus (genus)" class="mw-redirect">Corvus</a></i>, derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin_(language)" title="Latin (language)" class="mw-redirect">Latin</a> for "Raven".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The specific epithet, <i>corax</i>/κοραξ, is the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> word for "raven" or "crow".<sup id="cite_ref-Liddell1980_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Liddell1980-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The name "raven" has been applied to several other (generally large) species of the genus <i>Corvus</i>, though they are not necessarily closely related to <i>Corvus corax</i>. Some, such as the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Raven" title="Australian Raven">Australian Raven</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forest_Raven" title="Forest Raven">Forest Raven</a>, are clearly closer to the other Australian crows.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The original raven is now called the Common or Northern Raven.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The modern English word <i>raven</i> has cognates in all other <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic languages</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" title="Old Norse language" class="mw-redirect">Old Norse</a> <i>hrafn</i><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> <i>(h)raban</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> all which descend from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" title="Proto-Germanic" class="mw-redirect">Proto-Germanic</a> <i>*khrabanas</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> An old <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scottish</a> word <i>corby</i> or <i>corbie</i>, akin to the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> <i>corbeau</i>, has been used for both this bird and the <a href="/wiki/Carrion_Crow" title="Carrion Crow">Carrion Crow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Obsolete <a href="/wiki/Collective_noun" title="Collective noun">collective nouns</a> for a group of ravens (or at least the Common Raven) include "unkindness"<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> and "conspiracy".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> In practice, most people use the more generic "flock".</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Raven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span></h3>
<p>The closest relatives of the Common Raven are the <a href="/wiki/Brown-necked_Raven" title="Brown-necked Raven">Brown-necked Raven</a> <i>(C. ruficollis)</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Pied_Crow" title="Pied Crow">Pied Crow</a> <i>(C. albus)</i> of Africa, and the <a href="/wiki/Chihuahuan_Raven" title="Chihuahuan Raven">Chihuahuan Raven</a> <i>(C. cryptoleucus)</i> of the North American southwest.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> While some authorities recognized as many as 11 <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> others only recognize eight:<sup id="cite_ref-Clements_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clements-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>C. c. corax</i> (the nominate subspecies) occurs from Europe eastwards to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a>, south to the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> region and northern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. It has a relatively short, arched bill. The population in south-western Europe (including the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a>) has an even more arched bill and shorter wings than "typical" nominate, leading some authorities to recognize it as a separate subspecies, <i>C. c. hispanus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>C. c. varius</i> occurs in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>. It is less glossy than <i>C. c. principalis</i> or nominate <i>corax</i>, is intermediate in size, and the bases of its neck feathers are whitish (not visible at a distance). An <a href="/wiki/Extinct" title="Extinct" class="mw-redirect">extinct</a> <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(biology)" title="Polymorphism (biology)">color morph</a> found only on the Faroes is known as <a href="/wiki/Pied_Raven" title="Pied Raven">Pied Raven</a></li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li><i>C. c. subcorax</i> occurs from Greece eastwards to north-west India, <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> and western China though not the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayan</a> region. It is larger than the nominate form, but has relatively short throat feathers (<i>hackles</i>). Its plumage is generally all black, though its neck and breast have a brownish tone similar to that of the Brown-necked Raven; this more evident when the plumage is worn. The bases of its neck feathers, although somewhat variable in colour, are often almost whitish. (The name <i>C. c. laurencei</i> is sometimes used instead of <i>C. c. subcorax</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> It is based on the population from <a href="/wiki/Sindh" title="Sindh">Sindh</a> described by <a href="/wiki/Allan_Octavian_Hume" title="Allan Octavian Hume">Hume</a> in 1873<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> and is sometimes preferred since the type specimen of <i>subcorax</i> collected by <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Severtzov" title="Nikolai Severtzov">Nikolai Severtzov</a> is possibly a <a href="/wiki/Brown-necked_Raven" title="Brown-necked Raven">Brown-necked Raven</a><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup>)</li>
<li><i>C. c. tingitanus</i> occurs in North Africa and the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>. It is the smallest subspecies, with the shortest throat hackles and a distinctly oily plumage gloss. Its bill is short but markedly stout, and the <a href="/wiki/Culmen_(bird)" title="Culmen (bird)" class="mw-redirect">culmen</a> is strongly arched. Canary Ravens are browner than the North African Ravens, leading some authorities to treat them as separate subspecies, with the latter maintaining the name <i>C. c. tingitanus</i> and the former known as <i>C. c. canariensis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>C. c. tibetanus</i> occurs in the Himalayas. It is the largest and glossiest subspecies, with the longest throat hackles. Its bill is large but less imposing than that of <i>C. c. principalis</i>, and the bases of its neck feathers are grey.</li>
<li><i>C. c. kamtschaticus</i> occurs in north-eastern Asia, intergrading into the nominate subspecies in the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Baikal</a> region. It is intermediate in size between <i>C. c. principalis</i> and <i>C. c. corax</i> and has a distinctly larger and thicker bill than does the nominate race.</li>
<li><i>C. c. principalis</i> occurs in northern North America and <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>. It has a large body and the largest bill, its plumage is strongly glossed, and its throat hackles are well developed.</li>
<li><i>C. c. sinuatus</i>, the <b>Western Raven</b>, occurs in south-central USA and Central America. It is smaller, with a smaller and narrower bill than <i>C. c. principalis</i>. Populations in far south-western USA and north-western Mexico (including the <a href="/wiki/Revillagigedo_Islands" title="Revillagigedo Islands">Revillagigedo Islands</a>) are the smallest in North America. They are sometimes included in <i>C. c. sinuatus</i>, while other authorities recognize them as a distinct subspecies, <i>C. c. clarionensis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Raven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Evolutionary history">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Evolutionary_history">Evolutionary history</span></h3>
<p>The Common Raven evolved in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> and crossed the <a href="/wiki/Bering_land_bridge" title="Bering land bridge" class="mw-redirect">Bering land bridge</a> into North America.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Recent genetic studies, which examined the <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> of Common Ravens from across the world, have determined that the birds fall into at least two <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clades</a>: a <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> clade, found only in the southwestern United States, and a <a href="/wiki/Holarctic" title="Holarctic">Holarctic</a> clade, found across the rest of the northern hemisphere. Birds from both clades look alike, but the groups are genetically distinct and began to diverge about two million years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-USGS_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The findings indicate that based on mitochondrial DNA, Common Ravens from the rest of the United States are more closely related to those in Europe and Asia than to those in the California clade, and that Common Ravens in the California clade are more closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Chihuahuan_Raven" title="Chihuahuan Raven">Chihuahuan Raven</a> <i>(C. cryptoleucus)</i> than to those in the Holarctic clade.<sup id="cite_ref-USGS_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Ravens in the Holarctic clade are more closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Pied_Crow" title="Pied Crow">Pied Crow</a> <i>(C. albus)</i> than they are to the California clade.<sup id="cite_ref-Feld05_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Feld05-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Thus, the Common Raven species as traditionally delimited is considered to be <a href="/wiki/Paraphyletic" title="Paraphyletic" class="mw-redirect">paraphyletic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Feld05_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Feld05-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>One explanation for these surprising genetic findings is that Common Ravens settled in California at least two million years ago and became separated from their relatives in Europe and Asia during an <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>. One million years ago, a group from the California clade evolved into a new species, the Chihuahuan Raven. Other members of the Holarctic clade arrived later in a separate migration from Asia, perhaps at the same time as humans.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A recent study of raven <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> showed that the isolated population from the Canary Islands is distinct from other populations.<sup id="cite_ref-Bakom06_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bakom06-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> The study did not include any individuals from the North African population,<sup id="cite_ref-Bakom06_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bakom06-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> and its position is therefore unclear, though its <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a> is very close to the population of the Canaries (to the extent that the two are often considered part of a single subspecies).<sup id="cite_ref-Clements_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clements-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Raven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>

<p>A mature Common Raven ranges between 56 and 78&#160;cm (22 to 30&#160;inches) in length, with a <a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">wingspan</a> of 100 to 150&#160;cm (40 to 59 in).<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> Recorded weights range from 0.69 to 2&#160;kg (1.5 to 4.4&#160;lb),<sup id="cite_ref-Boarman_Heinrich_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boarman_Heinrich-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> thus making the Common Raven one of the heaviest <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerines</a>. Birds from colder regions such as the Himalayas and Greenland are generally larger with slightly larger bills, while those from warmer regions are smaller with proportionally smaller bills.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> is large and slightly curved, with a <a href="/wiki/Culmen_(bird)" title="Culmen (bird)" class="mw-redirect">culmen</a> length of 5.7 to 8.5 cm (2.2 to 3.3 in), easily one of the largest bills amongst passerines (perhaps only the <a href="/wiki/Thick-billed_Raven" title="Thick-billed Raven">Thick-billed Raven</a> has a noticeably larger bill). It has a longish, strongly graduated tail, at 20 to 26.3 cm (7.9 to 10.4 in), and mostly black <a href="/wiki/Iridescent" title="Iridescent" class="mw-redirect">iridescent</a> plumage, and a dark brown <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">iris</a>. The throat feathers are elongated and pointed and the bases of the neck feathers are pale brownish-grey. The legs and feet are good-sized, with a <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> length of 6 to 7.2 cm (2.4 to 2.8 in).<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> Juvenile plumage is similar but duller with a blue-grey iris.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Apart from its greater size, the Common Raven differs from its cousins, the <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crows</a>, by having a larger and heavier, black beak, shaggy feathers around the throat and above the beak, and a wedge-shaped tail.<sup id="cite_ref-observerbook_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-observerbook-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> The species has a distinctive, deep, resonant <i>prruk-prruk-prruk</i> call, which to experienced listeners is unlike that of any other corvid. Its very wide and complex vocabulary includes a high, knocking <i>toc-toc-toc</i>, a dry, grating <i>kraa</i>, a low guttural rattle and some calls of an almost musical nature.<sup id="cite_ref-Gwinner64_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gwinner64-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> In flight the feathers produce a creaking sound that has been likened to the rustle of silk.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Common Ravens can be very long-lived, especially in captive or protected conditions; <a href="/wiki/Ravens_of_the_Tower_of_London" title="Ravens of the Tower of London">individuals at the Tower of London</a> have lived for more than 40 years.<sup id="cite_ref-Boarman_Heinrich_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boarman_Heinrich-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Lifespans in the wild are considerably shorter at typically 10 to 15 years. The longest known lifespan of a <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">banded</a> wild Common Raven was 23 years, 3 months.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Although ravens are almost always black, there do exist exceptionally rare white ravens.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

</div>
<p>Common Ravens can thrive in varied climates; indeed this species has the largest range of any member of the genus.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Helm_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helm-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> They range throughout the <a href="/wiki/Holarctic" title="Holarctic">Holarctic</a> from <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate" class="mw-redirect">temperate</a> habitats in North America and <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> to the deserts of North Africa, and to islands in the Pacific Ocean. In the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, they are more common in Scotland, Wales, northern England and the west of Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-observerbook_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-observerbook-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>, they have been recorded at altitudes up to 5,000&#160;m (16,400&#160;ft), and as high as 6,350&#160;m (20,600&#160;ft) on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Helm_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helm-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> The population sometimes known as the Punjab Raven — described as <i>Corvus corax laurencei</i> (also spelt <i>lawrencii</i> or <i>laurencii</i>) by <a href="/wiki/Allan_Octavian_Hume" title="Allan Octavian Hume">Allan Octavian Hume</a> but more often considered synonymous with <i>subcorax</i><sup id="cite_ref-pcr_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> — is restricted to the <a href="/wiki/Sindh" title="Sindh">Sindh</a> district of Pakistan and adjoining regions of northwestern India.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Except in Arctic habitats,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> they are generally resident within their range for the whole year. Young birds may disperse locally.<sup id="cite_ref-Goodwin._p139_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goodwin._p139-45"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a> a now extinct colour-morph of this species existed, known as the <a href="/wiki/Pied_Raven" title="Pied Raven">Pied Raven</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Most Common Ravens prefer wooded areas, with large expanses of open land nearby, or coastal regions for their nesting sites and feeding grounds. In some areas of dense human population, such as <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> in the United States, they take advantage of a plentiful food supply and have seen a surge in their numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-mojave_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mojave-47"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Raven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<p>Common Ravens usually travel in mated pairs, although young birds may form <a href="/wiki/Flock_(birds)" title="Flock (birds)">flocks</a>. Relationships between Common Ravens are often quarrelsome, yet they demonstrate considerable devotion to their families.<sup id="cite_ref-Oregon_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oregon-48"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Raven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Common_Raven_Nationalpark_Bayerischer_Wald_01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Common_Raven_Nationalpark_Bayerischer_Wald_01.jpg/220px-Common_Raven_Nationalpark_Bayerischer_Wald_01.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Common_Raven_Nationalpark_Bayerischer_Wald_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Feeding</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Common Ravens are <a href="/wiki/Omnivorous" title="Omnivorous" class="mw-redirect">omnivorous</a> and highly opportunistic: their diet may vary widely with location, season and <a href="/wiki/Serendipity" title="Serendipity">serendipity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nogales_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nogales-49"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> For example, those foraging on <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_North_Slope" title="Arctic North Slope" class="mw-redirect">Arctic North Slope</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> obtained about half their energy needs from predation, mainly of <a href="/wiki/Microtus" title="Microtus">microtine rodents</a>, and half by scavenging, mainly of <a href="/wiki/Caribou" title="Caribou" class="mw-redirect">caribou</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rock_Ptarmigan" title="Rock Ptarmigan">Ptarmigan</a> carcasses.<sup id="cite_ref-Temple_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Temple-50"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In some places they are mainly <a href="/wiki/Scavenger" title="Scavenger">scavengers</a>, feeding on <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">carrion</a> as well as the associated <a href="/wiki/Maggots" title="Maggots" class="mw-redirect">maggots</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carrion_beetle" title="Carrion beetle" class="mw-redirect">carrion beetles</a>. With large-bodied carrion, which they are not equipped to tear through as well as birds such as hook-billed <a href="/wiki/Vulture" title="Vulture">vultures</a>, they must wait for the prey to be torn open by another predator or flayed by other means before they can eat themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> Plant food includes <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereal grains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berries" title="Berries" class="mw-redirect">berries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a>. They prey on small <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amphibians" title="Amphibians" class="mw-redirect">amphibians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Mammals" title="Mammals" class="mw-redirect">mammals</a> and birds.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> Ravens may also consume the undigested portions of animal <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a>, and human <a href="/wiki/Food_waste" title="Food waste">food waste</a>. They store surplus food items, especially those containing fat, and will learn to hide such food out of the sight of other Common Ravens.<sup id="cite_ref-Goodwin._p139_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goodwin._p139-45"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> Ravens also raid the food caches of other species, such as the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Fox" title="Arctic Fox" class="mw-redirect">Arctic Fox</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> They sometimes associate with another canine, the <a href="/wiki/Grey_Wolf" title="Grey Wolf" class="mw-redirect">Grey Wolf</a>, as a <a href="/wiki/Kleptoparasite" title="Kleptoparasite" class="mw-redirect">kleptoparasite</a>, following to scavenge wolf-kills in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> Ravens are regular predators at bird nests, brazenly picking off eggs, nestings and sometimes adult birds when they spot an opportunity. They are considered perhaps the primary natural threat to the nesting success of the critically endangered <a href="/wiki/California_Condor" title="California Condor">California Condor</a>, since they readily take condor eggs and are very common in the areas where the species is being re-introduced.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Common_raves_landfill.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Common_raves_landfill.jpg/220px-Common_raves_landfill.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Common_raves_landfill.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Flock feeding at a garbage dump</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Common Ravens nesting near sources of human <a href="/wiki/Food_waste" title="Food waste">garbage</a> included a higher percentage of food waste in their diet, birds nesting near roads consumed more <a href="/wiki/Roadkill" title="Roadkill">road-killed</a> <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a>, and those nesting far from these sources of food ate more <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> and plant material. <a href="/wiki/Fledging" title="Fledging" class="mw-redirect">Fledging</a> success was higher for those using human garbage as a food source.<sup id="cite_ref-Kristan_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kristan-56"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> In contrast, a 1984–1986 study of Common Raven diet in an agricultural region of south-western <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a> found that cereal grains were the principal constituent of <a href="/wiki/Pellet_(ornithology)" title="Pellet (ornithology)">pellets</a>, though small mammals, grasshoppers, cattle carrion and birds were also eaten.<sup id="cite_ref-Engel_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Engel-57"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>One behavior is recruitment, where juvenile ravens call other ravens to a food bonanza, usually a carcass, with a series of loud yells. In <i>Ravens in Winter,</i> <a href="/wiki/Bernd_Heinrich" title="Bernd Heinrich">Bernd Heinrich</a> posited that this behavior evolved to allow the juveniles to outnumber the resident adults, thus allowing them to feed on the carcass without being chased away.<sup id="cite_ref-Bernd89_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bernd89-58"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> A more mundane explanation is that individuals co-operate in sharing information about carcasses of large mammals because they are too big for just a few birds to exploit.<sup id="cite_ref-Heinrich.2C_B._1999_pp.C2.A0119.E2.80.93120_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heinrich.2C_B._1999_pp.C2.A0119.E2.80.93120-59"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> Experiments with baits however show that such recruitment behaviour is independent of the size of the bait.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Raven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Predation">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Predation">Predation</span></h3>
<p>Due to its size, gregariousness and its defensive abilities, the Common Raven has few natural predators. Predators of its eggs include <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marten" title="Marten">martens</a>, and other ravens. Ravens are quite vigorous at defending their young and are usually successful at driving off perceived threats. They attack potential predators by flying at them and lunging with their large bills, occasionally also using weapons such as small rocks which they drop on threatening animals while in flight. Humans are occasionally attacked if they get close to a raven nest, though serious injuries are unlikely. There are a few records of predation by <a href="/wiki/Golden_Eagle" title="Golden Eagle">Golden Eagles</a> and large owls such as <a href="/wiki/Great_Horned_Owl" title="Great Horned Owl">Great Horned Owls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Eagle_Owl" title="Eurasian Eagle Owl" class="mw-redirect">Eurasian Eagle Owls</a>. Due to the fact that they are potential hazardous prey for raptorial birds, raptors usually must take them by surprise and most attacks are on fledgling ravens. More rarely still, large mammalian predators such as <a href="/wiki/Lynx" title="Lynx">lynxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coyote" title="Coyote">coyotes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf" class="mw-redirect">wolves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cougar" title="Cougar">cougars</a> have also attacked ravens. This principally occurs at a nest site and when other prey for the carnivores are scarce. Ravens are highly wary around novel carrion sites and, in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, have been recorded waiting for the presence of <a href="/wiki/American_Crow" title="American Crow">American Crows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blue_Jay" title="Blue Jay">Blue Jays</a> before approaching to eat.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Raven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ravens_nest_Lastef.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Ravens_nest_Lastef.jpg/220px-Ravens_nest_Lastef.jpg" width="220" height="181" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ravens_nest_Lastef.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Young on a nest - Hvítserkur, Iceland</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Juveniles begin to court at a very early age, but may not bond for another two or three years. Aerial acrobatics, demonstrations of intelligence, and ability to provide food are key behaviors of courting. Once paired, they tend to nest together for life, usually in the same location.<sup id="cite_ref-Oregon_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oregon-48"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> Instances of non-monogamy have been observed in Common Ravens, by males visiting a female's nest when her mate is away.<sup id="cite_ref-Heinrich.2C_B._1999_pp.C2.A0119.E2.80.93120_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heinrich.2C_B._1999_pp.C2.A0119.E2.80.93120-59"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Breeding pairs must have a territory of their own before they begin nest-building and reproduction, and thus aggressively defend a territory and its food resources. Nesting territories vary in size according to the density of food resources in the area.<sup id="cite_ref-Boarman_Heinrich_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boarman_Heinrich-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> The nest is a deep bowl made of large sticks and twigs, bound with an inner layer of roots, mud, and bark and lined with a softer material, such as <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a> fur. The nest is usually placed in a large tree or on a cliff ledge, or less frequently in old buildings or utility poles.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Females lay between three to seven pale bluish-green, brown-blotched eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-observerbook_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-observerbook-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> Incubation is about 18 to 21 days, by the female only. However, the male may stand or crouch over the young, sheltering but not actually <a href="/wiki/Brooding" title="Brooding" class="mw-redirect">brooding</a> them.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> Young <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> at 35 to 42 days, and are fed by both parents. They stay with their parents for another six months after fledging.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In most of their range, egg laying begins in late February. In colder climates, it is later, e.g. April in <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>. In Pakistan, egg-laying takes place in December.<sup id="cite_ref-Helm_41-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helm-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> Eggs and hatchlings are rarely preyed on by large <a href="/wiki/Hawk" title="Hawk">hawks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a>, large <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marten" title="Marten">martens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canid" title="Canid" class="mw-redirect">canids</a>. The adults, which are very rarely predated, are often successful in defending their young from these predators, due to their numbers, large size and cunning.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> They have been observed dropping stones on potential predators that venture close to their nests.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Raven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Vocalization">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Vocalization">Vocalization</span></h3>
<p>Like other corvids, Ravens can mimic sounds from their environment, including human speech. They have a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">vocalizations</a>, which remain an object of interest to <a href="/wiki/Ornithologist" title="Ornithologist" class="mw-redirect">ornithologists</a>. Gwinner carried out important studies in the early 1960s, recording and photographing his findings in great detail.<sup id="cite_ref-Gwinner64_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gwinner64-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Fifteen to 30 categories of vocalization have been recorded for this species, most of which are used for social interaction. Calls recorded include alarm calls, chase calls, and flight calls. Non-vocal sounds produced by the Common Raven include wing whistles and bill snapping. Clapping or clicking has been observed more often in females than in males. If a member of a pair is lost, its mate reproduces the calls of its lost partner to encourage its return.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Raven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Intelligence">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Intelligence">Intelligence</span></h3>
<div class="rellink boilerplate seealso">See also: <a href="/wiki/Bird_intelligence" title="Bird intelligence">Bird intelligence</a>&#160;and <a href="/wiki/Corvidae#Intelligence" title="Corvidae">Corvidae#Intelligence</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Corvuscorax001.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Corvuscorax001.jpg/220px-Corvuscorax001.jpg" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Corvuscorax001.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<blockquote class="templatequote">
<div class="Bug6200">Crows, ravens, magpies, and jays are not just feathered machines, rigidly programmed by their genetics. Instead, they are beings that, within the constraints of their molecular inheritance, make complex decisions and show every sign of enjoying a rich awareness.</div>
<div class="templatequotecite">—<a href="/wiki/Candace_Savage" title="Candace Savage">Candace Savage</a><sup id="cite_ref-birdinblack_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdinblack-68"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The brains of Common Ravens count among the largest of any bird species. Specifically, their <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyperpallium&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hyperpallium (page does not exist)">hyperpallium</a> is large (see <a href="/wiki/Avian_pallium" title="Avian pallium">avian pallium</a>). For a bird, they display ability in problem solving, as well as other <a href="/wiki/Cognitive" title="Cognitive" class="mw-redirect">cognitive</a> processes such as <a href="/wiki/Imitation" title="Imitation">imitation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insight_phenomenology" title="Insight phenomenology">insight</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-birdinblack_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdinblack-68"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Linguist <a href="/wiki/Derek_Bickerton" title="Derek Bickerton">Derek Bickerton</a>, building on the work of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bernd_Heindrich&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bernd Heindrich (page does not exist)">Bernd Heindrich</a>, has recently argued that Ravens are one of only four known animals (the others beings <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ants</a>, and humans) who have demonstrated <a href="/wiki/Displacement_(linguistics)" title="Displacement (linguistics)">displacement</a>, the capacity to communicate about objects or events that are distant in space or time from the communication. Young, unmated Common Ravens roost together at night, but usually forage alone during the day. However, when one discovers a large carcass guarded by a pair of adult Ravens, he will return to the roost and communicate his find. The next day, a flock of young Ravens will fly to the carcass, and chase off the adults. Bickerton argues that the advent of linguistic displacement was perhaps the most important event in the evolution of human language, and that Ravens are the only other <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrate</a> to share this with humans.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>One experiment designed to evaluate insight and <a href="/wiki/Problem-solving" title="Problem-solving" class="mw-redirect">problem-solving</a> ability involved a piece of meat attached to a string hanging from a perch. To reach the food, the bird needed to stand on the perch, pull the string up a little at a time, and step on the loops to gradually shorten the string. Four of five Common Ravens eventually succeeded, and "the transition from no success (ignoring the food or merely yanking at the string) to constant reliable access (pulling up the meat) occurred with no demonstrable <a href="/wiki/Trial-and-error" title="Trial-and-error" class="mw-redirect">trial-and-error</a> learning." This supports the hypothesis that Common Ravens are 'inventors'; that is, they have the ability to solve problems presented to them. Many of the Common Raven's <a href="/wiki/Problem-solving" title="Problem-solving" class="mw-redirect">problem-solving</a> skills were formerly thought to be instinctive, but it is becoming clear that Common Ravens are actually quite intelligent.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Common Ravens have been observed to manipulate others into doing work for them, such as by calling <a href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf" class="mw-redirect">wolves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coyote" title="Coyote">coyotes</a> to the site of dead animals. The canines open the carcass, making it more accessible to the birds.<sup id="cite_ref-birdinblack_68-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdinblack-68"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> They watch where other Common Ravens bury their food and remember the locations of each other's food caches, so they can steal from them. This type of theft occurs so regularly that Common Ravens will fly extra distances from a food source to find better hiding places for food.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> They have also been observed pretending to make a cache without actually depositing the food, presumably to confuse onlookers.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_NIKE_Missile_radar_dome_with_ravens.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Old_NIKE_Missile_radar_dome_with_ravens.JPG/220px-Old_NIKE_Missile_radar_dome_with_ravens.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_NIKE_Missile_radar_dome_with_ravens.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Dilapidated NIKE Missile radar dome in Alaska with an evening roost</div>

<p>Common Ravens are known to steal and cache shiny objects such as pebbles, pieces of metal, and golf balls. One theory is that they hoard shiny objects to impress other ravens.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup> Other research indicates that juveniles are deeply curious about all new things, and that Common Ravens retain an attraction to bright, round objects based on their similarity to bird eggs. Mature birds lose their intense interest in the unusual, and become highly <a href="/wiki/Neophobia" title="Neophobia">neophobic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Raven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Playful behavior">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Playful_behavior">Playful behavior</span></h3>
<p>In recent years, biologists have recognized that birds engage in <a href="/wiki/Play_(activity)" title="Play (activity)">play</a>. Juvenile Common Ravens are among the most playful of bird species. They have been observed to slide down snowbanks, apparently purely for fun. They even engage in games with other species, such as playing catch-me-if-you-can with wolves, otters and dogs.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup> Common Ravens are known for spectacular aerobatic displays, such as flying in loops or interlocking talons with each other in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>They are also one of only a few wild animals who make their own toys. They have been observed breaking off twigs to play with socially.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Raven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Raven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Conservation and management">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_and_management">Conservation and management</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Corvus_corax_and_half_dome.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Corvus_corax_and_half_dome.jpg/220px-Corvus_corax_and_half_dome.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Corvus_corax_and_half_dome.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
By <a href="/wiki/Half_Dome" title="Half Dome">Half Dome</a> Yosemite</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Common Ravens are widely distributed and are not currently in danger of <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a>; however, there have been some localised declines in populations due to <a href="/wiki/Habitat_loss" title="Habitat loss" class="mw-redirect">habitat loss</a> and direct persecution. Compared to many smaller <i><a href="/wiki/Corvus_(genus)" title="Corvus (genus)" class="mw-redirect">Corvus</a></i> species (such as <a href="/wiki/American_Crow" title="American Crow">American Crow</a>), ravens prefer undisturbed montane or forest habitat or rural areas over urban areas.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup> In other areas, their numbers have increased dramatically and they have become <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">pests</a>. Common Ravens can cause damage to crops, such as nuts and grain, or can harm livestock, particularly by killing young <a href="/wiki/Goat" title="Goat">goat kids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Domestic_sheep" title="Domestic sheep" class="mw-redirect">lambs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">calves</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup> Ravens generally attack the faces of young livestock, but the more common Raven behavior of scavenging may be misidentified as predation by ranchers.<sup id="cite_ref-nass_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nass-81"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the western <a href="/wiki/Mojave_desert" title="Mojave desert" class="mw-redirect">Mojave desert</a>, human settlement and land development have led to an estimated 16-fold increase in the Common Raven population over 25 years. Towns, landfills, sewage treatment plants and artificial ponds create sources of food and water for scavenging birds. Ravens also find nesting sites in utility poles and ornamental trees, and are attracted to roadkill on highways. The explosion in the Common Raven population in the Mojave has raised concerns for the <a href="/wiki/Desert_tortoise" title="Desert tortoise">desert tortoise</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Threatened_species" title="Threatened species">threatened species</a>. Common Ravens prey upon juvenile tortoises, which have soft shells and are slow-moving.<sup id="cite_ref-mojave_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mojave-47"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> Plans to control the population have included shooting and trapping birds, as well as contacting landfill operators to ask that they reduce the amount of exposed garbage.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup> A hunting bounty as a method of control was historically used in Finland from the mid-18th century until 1923.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup> Culling has taken place to a limited extent in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, where the population increase in Common Ravens is threatening the vulnerable <a href="/wiki/Steller%27s_Eider" title="Steller's Eider">Steller's Eider</a> <i>(Polysticta stelleri)</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-MMS2007_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MMS2007-84"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Raven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cultural depictions">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_depictions">Cultural depictions</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bill_Reid_raven.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Bill_Reid_raven.jpg/220px-Bill_Reid_raven.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>

<a href="/wiki/Bill_Reid" title="Bill Reid">Bill Reid</a>'s sculpture <i>The Raven and The First Men</i>, showing part of a <a href="/wiki/Haida_mythology" title="Haida mythology">Haida</a> creation myth. <p>Across its range in the northern hemisphere, and throughout human history, the Common Raven has been a powerful symbol and a popular subject of mythology and folklore.</p>
<p>In many post-conversion <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western traditions</a>, ravens have long been considered to be birds of ill omen and death, in part because of the negative symbolism of their all-<a href="/wiki/Black#Beliefs.2C_religions_and_superstitions" title="Black">black</a> plumage and the eating of <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">carrion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup> In Sweden, ravens are known as the ghosts of murdered people, and in Germany as the souls of the damned. In <a href="/wiki/Danish_folklore" title="Danish folklore">Danish folklore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Valravn" title="Valravn">valravne</a> that ate a king's heart gained human knowledge, could perform great malicious acts, could lead people astray, had superhuman powers, and were "terrible animals".<sup id="cite_ref-KRISTENSEN132_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KRISTENSEN132-86"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>As in traditional mythology and folklore, the Common Raven features frequently in more modern writings such as the works of <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>, and, perhaps most famously, in the poem "<a href="/wiki/The_Raven" title="The Raven">The Raven</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</a>. Ravens have appeared in the works of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87"><span>[</span>88<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88"><span>[</span>89<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stephen_King" title="Stephen King">Stephen King</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89"><span>[</span>90<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Joan_Aiken" title="Joan Aiken">Joan Aiken</a><sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90"><span>[</span>91<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91"><span>[</span>92<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92"><span>[</span>93<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93"><span>[</span>94<span>]</span></a></sup> among others.</p>
<p>It continues to be used as a symbol in areas where it once had mythological status: as the <a href="/wiki/List_of_national_birds" title="List of national birds">National Bird</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ravenbhutan_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ravenbhutan-94"><span>[</span>95<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Druk_Gyalpo" title="Druk Gyalpo">Kings of Bhutan</a> wear the <a href="/wiki/Raven_Crown" title="Raven Crown">Raven Crown</a>, Official Bird of the <a href="/wiki/Yukon" title="Yukon">Yukon</a> territory,<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95"><span>[</span>96<span>]</span></a></sup> and on the Coat of Arms of the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> (once a Viking colony).<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96"><span>[</span>97<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Ravens" title="Baltimore Ravens">Baltimore Ravens</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a> have had a raven named "Poe" as their official mascot since the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Browns" title="Cleveland Browns">Cleveland Browns</a> relocated in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97"><span>[</span>98<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sussex_Technical_High_School" title="Sussex Technical High School">Sussex Technical High School</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Georgetown,_Delaware" title="Georgetown, Delaware">Georgetown, Delaware</a>, has adapted the Raven as its mascot.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98"><span>[</span>99<span>]</span></a></sup> As a reference to Edgar Allan Poe, the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Ribbon_School" title="Blue Ribbon School" class="mw-redirect">Blue Ribbon School</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Yearbook" title="Yearbook">yearbooks</a> are named <a href="/wiki/Evermore" title="Evermore">Evermore</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99"><span>[</span>100<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The modern <a href="/wiki/Unisex_given_name" title="Unisex given name" class="mw-redirect">unisex given name</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Raven_(given_name)" title="Raven (given name)">Raven</a></i> is derived from the English word "raven". As a masculine name, <i>Raven</i> parallels the Old Norse <i><a href="/wiki/Hrafn" title="Hrafn">Hrafn</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Hanks-226_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hanks-226-100"><span>[</span>101<span>]</span></a></sup> and Old English <i>*Hræfn</i>, which were both <a href="/wiki/Byname" title="Byname" class="mw-redirect">bynames</a> and <a href="/wiki/Personal_names" title="Personal names" class="mw-redirect">personal names</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Reaney_101-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reaney-101"><span>[</span>102<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Old_Frankish" title="Old Frankish">Old Frankish</a> name for a raven was <a href="/wiki/Chram" title="Chram">Chram</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Raven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Mythology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Mythology">Mythology</span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Raven_in_mythology" title="Raven in mythology" class="mw-redirect">Raven in mythology</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Valkyrie_and_raven.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Valkyrie_and_raven.jpg/220px-Valkyrie_and_raven.jpg" width="220" height="380" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Valkyrie_and_raven.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Valkyrie" title="Valkyrie">valkyrie</a> speaks with a raven in a 19th century illustration of the <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> poem <i><a href="/wiki/Hrafnsm%C3%A1l" title="Hrafnsmál">Hrafnsmál</a></i> ("raven song") by <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Frederick_Augustus_Sandys" title="Anthony Frederick Augustus Sandys" class="mw-redirect">Anthony Frederick Augustus Sandys</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Tlingit#Creation_story_and_the_Raven_Cycle" title="History of the Tlingit">Tlingit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haida_mythology" title="Haida mythology">Haida cultures</a>, Raven was both a Trickster and Creator god. Related beliefs are widespread among the peoples of <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> and northeast Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102"><span>[</span>103<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Kamchatka_peninsula" title="Kamchatka peninsula" class="mw-redirect">Kamchatka peninsula</a>, for example, was supposed to have been created by the raven god <a href="/wiki/Kutkh" title="Kutkh">Kutkh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103"><span>[</span>104<span>]</span></a></sup> There are several references to Common Ravens in the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> and it is an aspect of <a href="/wiki/Mahakala" title="Mahakala" class="mw-redirect">Mahakala</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutanese</a> mythology.<sup id="cite_ref-ravenbhutan_94-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ravenbhutan-94"><span>[</span>95<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse mythology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huginn_and_Muninn" title="Huginn and Muninn">Huginn</a> (from Old Norse "thought") and <a href="/wiki/Huginn_and_Muninn" title="Huginn and Muninn">Muninn</a> (Old Norse "memory"or "mind") are a pair of ravens that fly all over the world, <a href="/wiki/Midgard" title="Midgard">Midgard</a>, and bring the god <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a> information. Additionally among the Norse, <a href="/wiki/Raven_banner" title="Raven banner">Raven banner</a> standards were carried by such figures as the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Orkney" title="Earl of Orkney">Jarls of Orkney</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104"><span>[</span>105<span>]</span></a></sup> King <a href="/wiki/Canute_the_Great" title="Canute the Great" class="mw-redirect">Canute the Great</a> of England, Norway and Denmark,<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105"><span>[</span>106<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Harald_III_of_Norway" title="Harald III of Norway" class="mw-redirect">Harald Hardrada</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106"><span>[</span>107<span>]</span></a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, ravens also were symbolic to the <a href="/wiki/Celt" title="Celt" class="mw-redirect">Celts</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Irish_mythology" title="Irish mythology">Irish mythology</a>, the goddess <a href="/wiki/Morr%C3%ADgan" title="Morrígan" class="mw-redirect">Morrígan</a> alighted on the hero <a href="/wiki/C%C3%BA_Chulainn" title="Cú Chulainn">Cú Chulainn</a>'s shoulder in the form of a raven after his death.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107"><span>[</span>108<span>]</span></a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Welsh_mythology" title="Welsh mythology">Welsh mythology</a> they were associated with the Welsh god <a href="/wiki/Bran_the_Blessed" title="Bran the Blessed">Bran the Blessed</a>, whose name translates to "raven." According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Mabinogion" title="Mabinogion">Mabinogion</a></i>, Bran's head was buried in the White Hill of London as a talisman against invasion.<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108"><span>[</span>109<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Raven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Tower of London">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Tower_of_London">Tower of London</span></h4>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ravens_of_the_Tower_of_London" title="Ravens of the Tower of London">Ravens of the Tower of London</a></div>
<p>A legend developed that England would not fall to a foreign invader so long as there were ravens at the <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_London" title="Tower of London">Tower of London</a>; although this is often thought to be an ancient belief, the official Tower of London historian, Geoff Parnell, believes that this is actually a romantic Victorian invention.<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109"><span>[</span>110<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110"><span>[</span>111<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Raven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Christianity">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Christianity">Christianity</span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Judeo-Christian" title="Judeo-Christian">Judeo-Christian</a> tradition, the raven was the first animal to be released from <a href="/wiki/Noah%27s_ark" title="Noah's ark" class="mw-redirect">Noah's ark</a>. "So it came to pass, at the end of forty days, that Noah opened the window of the ark which he had made. Then he sent out a raven, which kept going to and fro until the waters had dried up from the earth. He also sent out from himself a dove, to see if the waters had receded from the face of the ground." Genesis 8:6-8 NKJV.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111"><span>[</span>112<span>]</span></a></sup> In Christianity the raven appears throughout the Bible in twelve different verses.
