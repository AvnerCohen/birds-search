<p>The <b>red-wattled lapwing</b> (<i>Vanellus indicus</i>) is an Asian <a href="/wiki/Lapwing" title="Lapwing">lapwing</a> or large <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">plover</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Charadriidae" title="Charadriidae">Charadriidae</a>. Like other lapwings they are ground birds that are incapable of perching. Their characteristic loud alarm calls are indicators of human or animal movements and the sounds have been variously rendered as <i>did he do it</i> or <i>pity to do it</i><sup id="cite_ref-jerdon_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jerdon-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> leading to the colloquial name of <i>did-he-do-it</i> bird.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Usually seen in pairs or small groups and usually not far from water they sometimes form large aggregations in the non-breeding season (winter). They nest in a ground scrape laying three to four camouflaged eggs. Adults near the nest fly around, diving at potential predators while calling noisily. The cryptically patterned chicks hatch and immediately follow their parents to feed, hiding by lying low on the ground or in the grass when threatened.<sup id="cite_ref-marchant_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marchant-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-wattled_lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_wattled_lapwing.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Red_wattled_lapwing.jpg/220px-Red_wattled_lapwing.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Red_wattled_lapwing.jpg/330px-Red_wattled_lapwing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Red_wattled_lapwing.jpg/440px-Red_wattled_lapwing.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="462" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_wattled_lapwing.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View of the wattles</div></div></div>
<p>Traditionally well known to native hunters, the red-wattled lapwing was first described in a book by the French polymath <a href="/wiki/Georges-Louis_Leclerc,_Comte_de_Buffon" title="Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon">Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon</a> in his <i>Histoire Naturelle des Oiseaux</i> in 1781.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The bird was also illustrated in a hand-coloured plate engraved by <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Nicolas_Martinet" title="François-Nicolas Martinet">François-Nicolas Martinet</a> in the <i>Planches Enluminées D'Histoire Naturelle</i> produced under the supervision of <a href="/wiki/Edme-Louis_Daubenton" title="Edme-Louis Daubenton">Edme-Louis Daubenton</a> to accompany Buffon's text.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  Neither the plate nor Buffon's description included a scientific name but in 1783 the Dutch naturalist <a href="/wiki/Pieter_Boddaert" title="Pieter Boddaert">Pieter Boddaert</a> used the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> <i>Tringa indica</i> in his catalogue of the <i>Planches Enluminées</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Type_locality_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Type locality (biology)">type locality</a> is <a href="/wiki/Goa" title="Goa">Goa</a> in western India.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It was subsequently placed in various other <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genera</a> such as <i>Sarcogrammus</i> and <i>Lobivanellus</i> before being merged into <i><a href="/wiki/Vanellus" title="Vanellus">Vanellus</a></i> which was erected by the French zoologist <a href="/wiki/Mathurin_Jacques_Brisson" title="Mathurin Jacques Brisson">Mathurin Jacques Brisson</a> in 1760.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <i>Vanellus</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> for a "lapwing". It is a diminutive of the Latin <i>vanus</i> meaning "winnowing" or "fan". The specific epithet <i>indicus</i> is the Latin for "India".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Across their wide range there are slight differences in the plumage and there are four recognized <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>V. i. aigneri</i> (<a href="/wiki/Alfred_Laubmann" title="Alfred Laubmann">Laubmann</a>, 1913) – southeast Turkey to Pakistan</li>
<li><i>V. i. indicus</i> (Boddaert, 1783) – central Pakistan to Nepal, northeast India and Bangladesh</li>
<li><i>V. i. lankae</i> (<a href="/wiki/Walter_Koelz" title="Walter Koelz">Koelz</a>, 1939) – Sri Lanka</li>
<li><i>V. i. atronuchalis</i> (<a href="/wiki/Thomas_C._Jerdon" title="Thomas C. Jerdon">Jerdon</a>, 1864) – northeast India to south China, southeast Asia, Malay Peninsula and north Sumatra</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-wattled_lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Red-wattled_lapwing-wings-spread-tail-hyderabad-India.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Red-wattled_lapwing-wings-spread-tail-hyderabad-India.jpg/220px-Red-wattled_lapwing-wings-spread-tail-hyderabad-India.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Red-wattled_lapwing-wings-spread-tail-hyderabad-India.jpg/330px-Red-wattled_lapwing-wings-spread-tail-hyderabad-India.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Red-wattled_lapwing-wings-spread-tail-hyderabad-India.jpg/440px-Red-wattled_lapwing-wings-spread-tail-hyderabad-India.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1653" data-file-height="930" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Red-wattled_lapwing-wings-spread-tail-hyderabad-India.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>V. i. indicus</i> showing the diagnostic white wing bar and a broad black band on the white tail.</div></div></div>
<p>Red-wattled lapwings are large <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">waders</a>, about 35&#160;cm (14&#160;in) long. The wings and back are light brown with a purple to green sheen, but the head, a bib on the front and back of the neck are black.  Prominently white patch runs between these two colours, from belly and tail, flanking the neck to the sides of crown.  Short tail is tipped black.  A red fleshy <a href="/wiki/Wattle_(anatomy)" title="Wattle (anatomy)">wattle</a> in front of each eye, black-tipped red bill, and the long legs are yellow. In flight, prominent white wing bars formed by the white on the secondary coverts.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vanellus_indicus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Vanellus_indicus.jpg/220px-Vanellus_indicus.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="127" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Vanellus_indicus.jpg/330px-Vanellus_indicus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Vanellus_indicus.jpg/440px-Vanellus_indicus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="577" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vanellus_indicus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>V. i. aigneri</i> from Turkey</div></div></div>
<p>Race <i>aigneri</i> is slightly paler and larger than the nominate race and is found in Turkey, Iran, Iraq, Afghanistan and the Indus valley. The nominate race is found all over India. The Sri Lankan race <i>lankae</i> is smaller and dark while <i>atronuchalis</i> the race in north-eastern India and eastern Bangladesh has a white cheek surrounded by black.<sup id="cite_ref-ras_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ras-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Males and females are similar in plumage but males have a 5% longer wing and tend to have a longer carpal spur. The length of the birds is 320–350&#160;mm, wing of 208–247&#160;mm with the nominate averaging 223&#160;mm, Sri Lanka 217&#160;mm. The Bill is 31–36&#160;mm and tarsus of 70–83&#160;mm. Tail length is 104–128&#160;mm.<sup id="cite_ref-marchant_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marchant-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It usually keeps in pairs or trios in well-watered open country, ploughed fields, grazing land, and margins and dry beds of tanks and puddles. They occasionally form large flocks, ranging from 26 to 200 birds.<sup id="cite_ref-vyas_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vyas-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It is also found in forest clearings around rain-filled depressions. It runs about in short spurts and dips forward obliquely (with unflexed legs) to pick up food in a typical plover manner.<sup id="cite_ref-ali-cent_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ali-cent-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> They are said to feed at night being especially active around the full moon.<sup id="cite_ref-marchant_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marchant-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Is uncannily and ceaselessly vigilant, day or night, and is the first to detect intrusions and raise an alarm, and was therefore considered a nuisance by hunters. Flight rather slow, with deliberate flaps, but capable of remarkable agility when defending nest or being hunted by a hawk.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vanellus_indicus_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Vanellus_indicus_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg/170px-Vanellus_indicus_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="213" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Vanellus_indicus_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg/255px-Vanellus_indicus_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Vanellus_indicus_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg/340px-Vanellus_indicus_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1760" data-file-height="2200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vanellus_indicus_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>V. i. atronuchalis</i> showing the white ear patch bounded by black</div></div></div>
<p>Its striking appearance is supplemented by its noisy nature, with a loud and scolding <i>did-he-do-it</i> call, uttered both in the day and night.<sup id="cite_ref-ras_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ras-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Leucism" title="Leucism">Leucistic</a> abnormal <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumages</a> have been noted.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The local names are mainly onomatopoeic in origin and include <i>titeeri</i> (Hindi), <i>titawi</i> (Marathi), <i>tittibha</i> (Kannada), <i>tateehar</i> (Sindhi), <i>titodi</i> (Gujarati), <i>hatatut</i> (Kashmiri), <i>balighora</i> (Assamese), <i>yennappa chitawa</i> (Telugu),<sup id="cite_ref-jerdon_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jerdon-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <i>aal-kaati</i> (Tamil, meaning "human indicator").<sup id="cite_ref-jerdon_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jerdon-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-wattled_lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Red-wattled_Lapwing_Sri_Lanka.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Red-wattled_Lapwing_Sri_Lanka.jpg/220px-Red-wattled_Lapwing_Sri_Lanka.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Red-wattled_Lapwing_Sri_Lanka.jpg/330px-Red-wattled_Lapwing_Sri_Lanka.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Red-wattled_Lapwing_Sri_Lanka.jpg/440px-Red-wattled_Lapwing_Sri_Lanka.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4907" data-file-height="3229" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Red-wattled_Lapwing_Sri_Lanka.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wading in shallow water, Sri Lanka</div></div></div>
<p>It breeds from West Asia (<a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, SW <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, Persian Gulf) eastwards across South Asia (<a href="/wiki/Balochistan_(region)" class="mw-redirect" title="Balochistan (region)">Baluchistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>,Afghanistan, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, the entire <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> subcontinent up to <a href="/wiki/Kanyakumari_district" title="Kanyakumari district">Kanyakumari</a> and up to 1800m in <a href="/wiki/Kashmir" title="Kashmir">Kashmir</a>/<a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>), with another sub-species further east in Southeast Asia. May <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> altitudinally in spring and autumn (e.g. in N. <a href="/wiki/Balochistan_(region)" class="mw-redirect" title="Balochistan (region)">Baluchistan</a> or NW <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>), and spreads out widely in the monsoons<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> on creation of requisite habitats, but by and large the populations are resident.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species is declining in its western range, but is abundant in much of South Asia, being seen at almost any wetland habitat in its range.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-wattled_lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vanellus_indicus_eggs_and_chicks.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Vanellus_indicus_eggs_and_chicks.jpg/220px-Vanellus_indicus_eggs_and_chicks.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Vanellus_indicus_eggs_and_chicks.jpg/330px-Vanellus_indicus_eggs_and_chicks.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Vanellus_indicus_eggs_and_chicks.jpg/440px-Vanellus_indicus_eggs_and_chicks.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vanellus_indicus_eggs_and_chicks.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chicks and eggs on a scrape nest. The young hatch in synchrony and the cryptically plumaged chick typically lies still when alarmed.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The breeding season is mainly March to August. The courtship involves the male puffing its feathers and pointing its beak upwards. The male then shuffles around the female. Several males may display to females and they may be close together.<sup id="cite_ref-vyas_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vyas-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The eggs are laid in a ground scrape or depression sometimes fringed with pebbles, goat or hare droppings.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> About 3–4 black-blotched buff eggs shaped a bit like a peg-top (<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pyriform" class="extiw" title="wikt:pyriform">pyriform</a>), 42x30 mm on average. Nests are difficult to find since the eggs are cryptically coloured and usually matches the ground pattern.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In residential areas, they sometimes take to nesting on roof-tops.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> They have been recorded nesting on the stones between the rails of a railway track, the adult leaving the nest when trains passed.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Nests that have been threatened by agricultural operations have been manually translocated by gradually shifting the eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> When nesting they will attempt to dive bomb or distract potential predators.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Both the male and female incubate the eggs and divert predators using <a href="/wiki/Distraction_display" title="Distraction display">distraction displays</a> or flash their wings to deter any herbivores that threaten the nest. Males appear to relieve females incubating at the nest particularly towards the hot part of noon.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The eggs hatch in 28 to 30 days. The reproductive success is about 40%. Egg mortality is high (~43%) due to predation by mongooses, crows and kites. Chicks have a lower mortality (8.3%) and their survival improves after the first week.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Like other lapwings, they soak their belly feathers to provide water to their chicks as well as to cool the eggs during hot weather.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_wattled_lapwing_immature.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Red_wattled_lapwing_immature.jpg/170px-Red_wattled_lapwing_immature.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="224" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Red_wattled_lapwing_immature.jpg/255px-Red_wattled_lapwing_immature.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Red_wattled_lapwing_immature.jpg/340px-Red_wattled_lapwing_immature.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="792" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_wattled_lapwing_immature.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The chick leaves the nest and follows the parents soon after hatching</div></div></div>
<p>They bathe in pools of water when available and will often spend time on preening when leaving the nest or after copulation. They sometimes rest on the ground with the tarsi laid flat on the ground and at other times may rest on one leg.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Healthy adult birds have few predators and are capable of rapid and agile flight when pursued by hawks or falcons.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_13-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hugh_B._Cott" title="Hugh B. Cott">Hugh B. Cott</a> claimed that the flesh of the bird was unpalatable based on evidence from an Indian geologist who noted that a hungry tiger cub refused to eat their meat.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Some endoparasitic tapeworms, nematodes, and trematodes have been described from the species.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Mortality caused by respiratory infection by <i><a href="/wiki/Ornithobacterium_rhinotracheale" title="Ornithobacterium rhinotracheale">Ornithobacterium rhinotracheale</a></i> has been recorded in captive birds in Pakistan.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-wattled_lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The diet of the lapwing includes a range of insects, <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a> and other invertebrates, mostly picked from the ground. They may also feed on some grains. They feed mainly during the day but they may also feed at night. They may sometimes make use of the legs to disturb insect prey from soft soil.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-wattled_lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In parts of India, a local belief is that the bird sleeps on its back with the legs upwards and an associated Hindi metaphor <i>Tithiri se asman thama jayega</i> ("can the lapwing support the heavens?") is used to refer to persons undertaking tasks beyond their ability or strength.<sup id="cite_ref-jerdon_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jerdon-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In parts of Rajasthan it is believed that the laying of eggs by the lapwing on high ground was an indication of good rains to come.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The eggs are known to be collected by practitioners of <a href="/wiki/Traditional_medicine" title="Traditional medicine">folk medicine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The Bhils of Malwa believed that the laying of eggs by red-wattled lapwings in the dry beds of streams as forewarnings of delayed rains or droughts. Eggs laid on the banks on the other hand were taken as indications of normal rains.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
