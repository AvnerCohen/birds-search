<p>The <b>Red-wattled Lapwing</b> (<i>Vanellus indicus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Lapwing" title="Lapwing">lapwing</a> or large <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">plover</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Charadriidae" title="Charadriidae">Charadriidae</a>. It has characteristic loud alarm calls which are variously rendered as <i>did he do it</i> or <i>pity to do it</i><sup id="cite_ref-jerdon_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jerdon-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> leading to colloquial names like the <i>did-he-do-it</i> bird.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Usually seen in pairs or small groups not far from water but may form large flocks in the non-breeding season (winter).<sup id="cite_ref-marchant_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marchant-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-wattled_Lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>Red-wattled Lapwings are large <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">waders</a>, about 35&#160;cm long. The wings and back are light brown with a purple sheen, but head and chest and front part of neck are black. Prominently white patch runs between these two colours, from belly and tail, flanking the neck to the sides of crown. Short tail is tipped black. A red fleshy <a href="/wiki/Wattle_(anatomy)" title="Wattle (anatomy)">wattle</a> in front of each eye, black-tipped red bill, and the long legs are yellow. In flight, prominent white wing bars formed by the white on the secondary coverts.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vanellus_indicus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Vanellus_indicus.jpg/220px-Vanellus_indicus.jpg" width="220" height="127" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Vanellus_indicus.jpg/330px-Vanellus_indicus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Vanellus_indicus.jpg/440px-Vanellus_indicus.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vanellus_indicus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>V. i. aigneri</i> from Turkey</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Race <i>aigneri</i> is slightly paler and larger than the nominate race and is found in Turkey, Iran, Iraq, Afghanistan and the Indus valley. The nominate race is found all over India. The Sri Lankan race <i>lankae</i> is smaller and dark while <i>atronuchalis</i> the race in north-eastern India and eastern Bangladesh has a white cheek surrounded by black.<sup id="cite_ref-ras_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ras-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Males and females are similar in plumage but males have a 5% longer wing and tend to have a longer carpal spur. The length of the birds is 320-350mm, wing of 208-247mm with the nominate averaging 223mm, Sri Lanka 217mm. The Bill is 31-36mm and tarsus of 70-83mm. Tail length is 104-128mm.<sup id="cite_ref-marchant_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marchant-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It usually keeps in pairs or trios in well-watered open country, ploughed fields, grazing land, and margins and dry beds of tanks and puddles. They occasionally form large flocks, ranging from 26 to 200 birds.<sup id="cite_ref-vyas_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vyas-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> It is also found in forest clearings around rain-filled depressions. It runs about in short spurts and dips forward obliquely (with unflexed legs) to pick up food in a typical plover manner.<sup id="cite_ref-ali-cent_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ali-cent-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> They are said to feed at night being especially active around the full moon.<sup id="cite_ref-marchant_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marchant-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Is uncannily and ceaselessly vigilant, day or night, and is the first to detect intrusions and raise an alarm, and was therefore considered a nuisance by hunters. Flight rather slow, with deliberate flaps, but capable of remarkable agility when defending nest or being hunted by a hawk.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vanellus_indicus_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Vanellus_indicus_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg/220px-Vanellus_indicus_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg" width="220" height="275" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Vanellus_indicus_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg/330px-Vanellus_indicus_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Vanellus_indicus_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg/440px-Vanellus_indicus_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vanellus_indicus_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>V. i. atronuchalis</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Its striking appearance is supplemented by its noisy nature, with a loud and scolding <i>did-he-do-it</i> call, often uttered at night.<sup id="cite_ref-ras_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ras-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Leucism" title="Leucism">Leucistic</a> abnormal <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> is rare.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The local names include <i>titeeri</i> (Hindi), <i>tateehar</i> (Sindhi), <i>titodi</i> (Gujarati), <i>hatatut</i> (Kashmiri), <i>balighora</i> (Assamese), <i>yennappa chitawa</i> (Telugu),<sup id="cite_ref-jerdon_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jerdon-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>aal-kaati</i> (Tamil, meaning "human indicator").<sup id="cite_ref-jerdon_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jerdon-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-wattled_Lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_wattled_lapwing.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Red_wattled_lapwing.jpg/170px-Red_wattled_lapwing.jpg" width="170" height="131" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Red_wattled_lapwing.jpg/255px-Red_wattled_lapwing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Red_wattled_lapwing.jpg/340px-Red_wattled_lapwing.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_wattled_lapwing.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
View of the wattles</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It breeds from West Asia (<a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, SW <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, the Arabian/Persian Gulf) eastwards across South Asia (<a href="/wiki/Balochistan_(region)" title="Balochistan (region)" class="mw-redirect">Baluchistan</a>, Afghanistan, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, the entire <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> subcontinent up to <a href="/wiki/Kanyakumari_district" title="Kanyakumari district">Kanyakumari</a> and up to 1800m in <a href="/wiki/Kashmir" title="Kashmir">Kashmir</a>/<a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>), with another sub-species further east in Southeast Asia. May <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> altitudinally in spring and autumn (e.g. in N. <a href="/wiki/Balochistan_(region)" title="Balochistan (region)" class="mw-redirect">Baluchistan</a> or NW <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>), and spreads out widely in the monsoons<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> on creation of requisite habitats, but by and large the populations are resident.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species is declining in its western range, but is abundant in much of South Asia, being seen at almost any wetland habitat in its range.</p>

<p>The breeding season is mainly March to August. The courtship involves the male puffing its feathers and pointing its beak upwards. The male then shuffles around the female. Several males may display to females and they may be close together.<sup id="cite_ref-vyas_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vyas-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The eggs are laid in a ground scrape or depression sometimes fringed with pebbles, goat or hare droppings.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> About 3–4 black-blotched buff eggs shaped a bit like a peg-top (<a href="/wiki/Pyriform" title="Pyriform" class="mw-redirect">pyriform</a>), 42x30 mm on average. Nests are difficult to find since the eggs are cryptically coloured and usually matches the ground pattern.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> In residential areas, they sometimes take to nesting on roof-tops.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> They have been recorded nesting on the stones between the rails of a railway track, the adult leaving the nest when trains passed.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Nests that have been threatened by agricultural operations have been manually translocated by gradually shifting the eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> When nesting they will attempt to dive bomb or distract potential predators.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Both the male and female incubate the eggs and divert predators using <a href="/wiki/Distraction_display" title="Distraction display">distraction displays</a> or flash their wings to deter any herbivores that threaten the nest. Males appear to relieve the females incubating at the nest particularly towards the hot part of noon.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The eggs hatch in 28 to 30 days. The reproductive success is about 40%. Egg mortality is high (~43%) due to predation by mongooses, crows and kites. Chicks had a lower mortality (8.3%) and their survival improved after the first week.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Like other lapwings, they soak their belly feathers to provide water to their chicks as well as to cool the eggs during hot weather.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>They bathe in pools of water when available and will often spend time on preening when leaving the nest or after copulation. They sometimes rest on the ground with the tarsi laid flat on the ground and at other times may rest on one leg.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

</div>
<p>Healthy adult birds have few predators. Some species of tapeworm and trematodes have been described as endoparasites.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-wattled_Lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span></h3>
<p>The diet of the lapwing includes a range of insects, <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a> and other invertebrates, mostly picked from the ground. They may also feed on some grains. They feed mainly during the day but they may also feed at night. They may sometimes make use of the legs to disturb insect prey.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-wattled_Lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span></h2>
<p>In parts of India, a local belief is that the bird sleeps on its back with the legs upwards and an associated Hindi metaphor <i>Tithiri se asman thama jayega</i> ("can the pee-wit support the heavens?") is used when referring to persons undertaking tasks beyond their ability or strength.<sup id="cite_ref-jerdon_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jerdon-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In parts of Rajasthan it is believed that the laying of eggs by the lapwing on high ground was an indication of good rains to come.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> The eggs are known to be collected by practitioners of <a href="/wiki/Traditional_medicine" title="Traditional medicine">folk medicine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
