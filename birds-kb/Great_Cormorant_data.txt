<p>The <b>great cormorant</b> (<i>Phalacrocorax carbo</i>), known as the <b>black shag</b> in New Zealand and formerly also known as the <b>great black cormorant</b> across the Northern Hemisphere, the <b>black cormorant</b> in Australia, and the <b>large cormorant</b> in India, is a widespread member of the <a href="/wiki/Cormorant" title="Cormorant">cormorant</a> family of <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ali1993_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ali1993-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> name is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latinised</a> <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>, from φαλακρός (<i>phalakros</i>, "bald") and κόραξ (<i>korax</i>, "raven"), and <i>carbo</i> is Latin for "<a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-job90_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job90-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It breeds in much of the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> coast of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_etymology">Taxonomy and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_cormorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 80–100&#160;cm (31–39&#160;in) long <a href="/wiki/White-breasted_cormorant" title="White-breasted cormorant">white-breasted cormorant</a> <i>P. c. lucidus</i> found in sub-Saharan Africa, has a white neck and breast. It is often treated as a full species, <i>Phalacrocorax lucidus</i> (e.g. <a href="#CITEREFSibleyMonroe1990">Sibley &amp; Monroe 1990</a>, <a href="#CITEREFSinclairHockeyTarboton2002">Sinclair, Hockey &amp; Tarboton 2002</a>).
</p><p>In addition to the Australasian and African forms, <i>Phalacrocorax carbo novaehollandiae</i> and <i>P. c. lucidus</i> mentioned above, other geographically distinct subspecies are recognised, including <i>P. c. sinensis</i> (western Europe to east <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>), <i>P. c. maroccanus</i> (north-western Africa), and <i>P. c. hanedae</i> (<a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>).
</p><p>Some authors<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (December 2011)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> treat all these as <a href="/wiki/Allospecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Allospecies">allospecies</a> of a <i>P. carbo</i> superspecies group.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, the subspecies <i>P. c. novaehollandiae</i> is known as the black shag or by its <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="Māori language">Māori</a> name; "kawau".<sup id="cite_ref-FGBNZ_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FGBNZ-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Syntype" title="Syntype">syntype</a> is in the collection of the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_New_Zealand_Te_Papa_Tongarewa" title="Museum of New Zealand Te Papa Tongarewa">Museum of New Zealand Te Papa Tongarewa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tepapa_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tepapa-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_cormorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The great cormorant is a large black bird, but there is a wide variation in size in the species' wide range. Weight is reported to vary from 1.5&#160;kg (3.3&#160;lb)<sup id="cite_ref-Ribak2005_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ribak2005-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> to 5.3&#160;kg (12&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-CanadianEncyclopedia_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CanadianEncyclopedia-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Males are typically larger and heavier than females, with the nominate race (<i>P. c. carbo</i>) averaging about 10% larger in linear measurements than the smallest race in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> (<i>P. c. sinensis</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Koffijberg1995_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koffijberg1995-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The lightest average weights cited are in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> (<i>P. c. sinensis</i>), where 36 males averaged 2.28&#160;kg (5.0&#160;lb) and 17 females averaged 1.94&#160;kg (4.3&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The highest come from <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward_Island" title="Prince Edward Island">Prince Edward Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> (<i>P. c. carbo</i>), where 11 males averaged 3.68&#160;kg (8.1&#160;lb) and 11 females averaged 2.94&#160;kg (6.5&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-Hogan1979_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hogan1979-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hatch_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hatch-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Length can vary from 70 to 102&#160;cm (28 to 40&#160;in) and wingspan from 121 to 160&#160;cm (48 to 63&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Hatch_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hatch-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Stevenson_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stevenson-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> They are tied as the second largest extant species of cormorant after the <a href="/wiki/Flightless_cormorant" title="Flightless cormorant">flightless cormorant</a>, with the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cormorant" title="Japanese cormorant">Japanese cormorant</a> averaging at a similar size. In bulk if not in linear dimensions, the <a href="/wiki/Blue-eyed_shag" title="Blue-eyed shag">Blue-eyed shag</a> species complex of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Oceans</a> are scarcely smaller at average.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> It has a longish tail and yellow throat-patch. Adults have white patches on the thighs and on the throat in the breeding season. In <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> waters it can be distinguished from the <a href="/wiki/Common_shag" class="mw-redirect" title="Common shag">common shag</a> by its larger size, heavier build, thicker bill, lack of a crest and <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">plumage</a> without any green tinge. In eastern North America, it is similarly larger and bulkier than <a href="/wiki/Double-crested_cormorant" title="Double-crested cormorant">double-crested cormorant</a>, and the latter species has more yellow on the throat and bill and lack the white thigh patches frequently seen on great cormorants. Great cormorants are mostly silent, but they make various guttural noises at their breeding colonies.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">Variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_cormorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A very rare variation of the great cormorant is caused by <a href="/wiki/Albinism" title="Albinism">albinism</a>. The <i>Phalacrocorax carbo</i> albino suffers from poor eyesight and/or hearing, thus it rarely manages to survive in the wild.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_cormorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is a very common and widespread <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> species. It feeds on the sea, in <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuaries</a>, and on freshwater lakes and rivers. Northern birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> south and winter along any coast that is well-supplied with <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>.
</p><p>In Serbia, the cormorant lives in Vojvodina. However, after 1945 many artificial lakes were formed in Serbia; some of them became potential habitats for cormorants. Currently, on the Lake <a href="/wiki/Rasina_(river)" title="Rasina (river)">Ćelije</a>, formed in 1980, there is a resident colony of cormorants, who nest there and are present throughout the year, except January–February 1985 and February 2012 when the lake surface was completely frozen.
</p><p>The type subspecies, <i>P. c. carbo</i>, is found mainly in <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> waters and nearby inland areas: on western European coasts and east across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> to <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> and to North Africa, the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>; and on the eastern seaboard of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, though in America it breeds only in the north of its range, in the <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> <a href="/wiki/Maritime_provinces" class="mw-redirect" title="Maritime provinces">maritime provinces</a>. The subspecies <i>P. c. novaehollandiae</i> is found in Australian waters.<sup id="cite_ref-FGBNZ_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FGBNZ-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_cormorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The great cormorant often nests in colonies near wetlands, rivers, and sheltered inshore waters. Pairs will use the same nest site to breed year after year. It builds its nest, which is made from sticks, in trees, on the ledges of cliffs, and on the ground on rocky islands that are free of predators.<sup id="cite_ref-Hauber2014_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hauber2014-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This cormorant lays a <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> of three to five eggs that measure 63 by 41 millimetres (2.5 by 1.6&#160;in) on average. The eggs are a pale blue or green, and sometimes have a white chalky layer covering them. These eggs are incubated for a period of about 28 to 31 days.<sup id="cite_ref-Hauber2014_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hauber2014-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_cormorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The great cormorant feeds on fish caught through diving.<sup id="cite_ref-Hauber2014_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hauber2014-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> This bird feeds primarily on <a href="/wiki/Wrasse" title="Wrasse">wrasses</a>, but it also takes <a href="/wiki/Sand_smelt" title="Sand smelt">sand smelt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flathead_(fish)" title="Flathead (fish)">flathead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_sole" title="Common sole">common soles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-GrémilletArgentin2008_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GrémilletArgentin2008-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The average weight of fish taken by great cormorants increased with decreasing air and water temperature, being 30 g during summer, 109 g during a warm winter and 157 g during the cold winter (all values for non-breeding birds). Cormorants consume all fish of appropriate size that they are able to catch in summer and noticeably select for larger, mostly torpedo-shaped fish in winter. Thus, the winter elevation of foraging efficiency described for cormorants by various researchers is due to capturing larger fish not due to capturing more fish.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In some freshwater systems, the losses of fish due to overwintering great cormorants were estimated to be up to 80&#160;kg per ha each year (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Vltava" title="Vltava">Vltava</a> River, <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This cormorant forages by diving and capturing its prey in its beak.<sup id="cite_ref-Hauber2014_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hauber2014-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The duration of its dives is around 28 seconds, with the bird diving to depths of about 5.8 metres (19&#160;ft). About 60% of dives are to the <a href="/wiki/Benthic_zone" title="Benthic zone">benthic zone</a> and about 10% are to the <a href="/wiki/Pelagic_zone" title="Pelagic zone">pelagic zone</a>, with the rest of the dives being to zones in between the two.<sup id="cite_ref-GrémilletArgentin2008_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GrémilletArgentin2008-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Studies suggest that their hearing has evolved for underwater usage, possibly aiding their detection of fish.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> These adaptations also have a cost on their hearing ability in air which is of lowered sensitivity.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships_with_humans">Relationships with humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_cormorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Relationships with humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many fishermen see in the great cormorant a competitor for fish. Because of this, it was hunted nearly to extinction in the past. Due to conservation efforts, its numbers increased. At the moment, there are about 1.2 million birds in Europe (based on winter counts; late summer counts would show higher numbers).<sup id="cite_ref-WICRG_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WICRG-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Increasing populations have once again brought the cormorant into conflict with fisheries.<sup id="cite_ref-FAO_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-europarl_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-europarl-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> For example, in Britain, where inland breeding was once uncommon, there are now increasing numbers of birds breeding inland, and many inland fish farms and fisheries now claim to be suffering high losses due to these birds. In the UK each year, some licences are issued to <a href="/wiki/Culling" title="Culling">cull</a> specified numbers of cormorants in order to help reduce predation; it is, however, still illegal to kill a bird without such a licence.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cormorant_fishing_-Suzhou_-China-6July2005.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Cormorant_fishing_-Suzhou_-China-6July2005.jpg/220px-Cormorant_fishing_-Suzhou_-China-6July2005.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Cormorant_fishing_-Suzhou_-China-6July2005.jpg/330px-Cormorant_fishing_-Suzhou_-China-6July2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Cormorant_fishing_-Suzhou_-China-6July2005.jpg/440px-Cormorant_fishing_-Suzhou_-China-6July2005.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cormorant_fishing_-Suzhou_-China-6July2005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Cormorant_fishing" title="Cormorant fishing">Cormorant fishing</a> in <a href="/wiki/Suzhou" title="Suzhou">Suzhou</a>, China</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cormorant_fishing" title="Cormorant fishing">Cormorant fishing</a> is practised in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, and elsewhere around the globe. In this practice, fishermen tie a line around the throats of cormorants, tight enough to prevent swallowing the larger fish they catch, and deploy them from small boats. The cormorants catch fish without being able to fully swallow them, and the fishermen are able to retrieve the fish simply by forcing open the cormorants' mouths, apparently engaging the regurgitation reflex.
</p><p>In Norway, the cormorant is a traditional game bird. Each year approximately 10,000 cormorants are shot to be eaten.<sup id="cite_ref-intercafeproject_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-intercafeproject-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In North Norway, cormorants are traditionally seen as semi-sacred.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It is regarded as good luck to have cormorants gather near your village or settlement. An old legend states that for people who die far out at sea, whose bodies are never recovered, spend eternity on the island Utrøst – which can only occasionally be found by mortals. The inhabitants of Utrøst can only visit their homes in the shape of cormorants.
</p>
