<p>The <b>Great Cormorant</b> (<i>Phalacrocorax carbo</i>), known as the <b>Great Black Cormorant</b> across the Northern Hemisphere, the <b>Black Cormorant</b> in Australia, the <b>Large Cormorant</b> in India and the <b>Black Shag</b> further south in New Zealand, is a widespread member of the <a href="/wiki/Cormorant" title="Cormorant">cormorant</a> family of <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> It breeds in much of the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> coast of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Cormorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>

<p>The Great Cormorant is a large black bird, but there is a wide variation in size in the species wide range. Weight is reported from 1.5&#160;kg (3.3&#160;lbs)<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> to 5.3&#160;kg (11.7&#160;lbs),<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> with a typical range from 2.6 to 3.7&#160;kg (5.7–8.2&#160;lbs).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Length can vary from 70 to 102&#160;cm (28–40&#160;in) and wingspan from 121 to 160&#160;cm (48–63&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> It has a longish tail and yellow throat-patch. Adults have white thigh patches in the breeding season. In <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> waters it can be distinguished from the <a href="/wiki/Common_Shag" title="Common Shag" class="mw-redirect">Common Shag</a> by its larger size, heavier build, thicker bill, lack of a crest and <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">plumage</a> without any green tinge. In eastern North America, it is similarly larger and bulkier than <a href="/wiki/Double-crested_Cormorant" title="Double-crested Cormorant">Double-crested Cormorant</a>, and the latter species has more yellow on the throat and bill. Great Cormorants are mostly silent, but they make various guttural noises at their breeding colonies.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Cormorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Variations">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">Variations</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Phalacrocorax_Carbo_Albino_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>A very rare variation of the Great Cormorant is caused by <a href="/wiki/Albinism" title="Albinism">albinism</a>. The <i>Phalacrocorax carbo</i> albino suffers from loss of eyesight and/or hearing, thus it rarely manages to survive in the wild.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from June 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Cormorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Cormorants_Nesting_Feb09.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Great_Cormorants_Nesting_Feb09.jpg/220px-Great_Cormorants_Nesting_Feb09.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Cormorants_Nesting_Feb09.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Nesting in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne,_Australia" title="Melbourne, Australia" class="mw-redirect">Melbourne, Australia</a></div>

<p>This is a very common and widespread <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> species. It feeds on the sea, in <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuaries</a>, and on freshwater lakes and rivers. Northern birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> south and winter along any coast that is well-supplied with <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>.</p>
<p>The type subspecies, <i>P. c. carbo</i>, is found mainly in <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic" class="mw-redirect">Atlantic</a> waters and nearby inland areas: on western European coasts and south to North Africa, the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>; and on the eastern seaboard of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, though in America it breeds only in the north of its range, in the <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> <a href="/wiki/Maritime_provinces" title="Maritime provinces" class="mw-redirect">maritime provinces</a>.<br />
The subspecies found in <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasian</a> waters, <i>P. carbo novaehollandiae</i>, has a crest. In <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> it is known as the <b>Black Shag</b> or by its <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="Māori language">Māori</a> name; <b>Kawau</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-FGBNZ_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FGBNZ-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Syntype" title="Syntype" class="mw-redirect">syntype</a> is in the collection of the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_New_Zealand_Te_Papa_Tongarewa" title="Museum of New Zealand Te Papa Tongarewa">Museum of New Zealand Te Papa Tongarewa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The 80–100&#160;cm long <a href="/wiki/White-breasted_Cormorant" title="White-breasted Cormorant">White-breasted Cormorant</a> <i>P. c. lucidus</i> found in sub-Saharan Africa, has a white neck and breast. It is often treated as a full species, <i>Phalacrocorax lucidus</i> (e.g. Sibley &amp; Monroe, 1990, Sinclair, Hockey and Tarboton, 2002)</p>
<p>In addition to the Australasian and African forms, <i>Phalacrocorax carbo novaehollandiae</i> and <i>P. carbo lucidus</i> mentioned above, other geographically distinct subspecies are recognised, including <i>P. c. sinensis</i> (western Europe to east <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>), <i>P. c. maroccanus</i> (north-western Africa), and <i>P. c. hannedae</i> (<a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>).</p>
<p>Some authors<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words" class="mw-redirect"><span title="The material in the vicinity of this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. from December 2011">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> treat all these as <a href="/wiki/Allospecies" title="Allospecies" class="mw-redirect">allospecies</a> of a <i>P. carbo</i> superspecies group.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Cormorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Cormorants_with_Darter,_other_Cormorants_%26_Great_Egret_I2_IMG_9369.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Great_Cormorants_with_Darter%2C_other_Cormorants_%26_Great_Egret_I2_IMG_9369.jpg/220px-Great_Cormorants_with_Darter%2C_other_Cormorants_%26_Great_Egret_I2_IMG_9369.jpg" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Cormorants_with_Darter,_other_Cormorants_%26_Great_Egret_I2_IMG_9369.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Great Cormorants resting with <a href="/wiki/Darter" title="Darter">Darter</a> (Ahinga), other cormorants &amp; <a href="/wiki/Great_Egret" title="Great Egret">Great Egret</a> near <a href="/wiki/Hodal" title="Hodal">Hodal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Faridabad" title="Faridabad">Faridabad</a> District of <a href="/wiki/Haryana" title="Haryana">Haryana</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Great Cormorant breeds mainly on coasts, nesting on cliffs or in trees (which are eventually killed by the droppings), but also increasingly inland. 3–4 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> are laid in a nest of <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweed</a> or twigs.</p>
<p>The Great Cormorant can dive to considerable depths, but often feeds in shallow water. It frequently brings prey to the surface. A wide variety of fish are taken: cormorants are often noticed eating <a href="/wiki/Eel" title="Eel">eels</a>, but this may reflect the considerable time taken to subdue an eel and position it for swallowing, rather than any dominance of eels in the diet. In <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> waters, dive times of 20–30 seconds are common, with a recovery time on the surface around a third of the dive time.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Cormorant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relationships with humans">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships_with_humans">Relationships with humans</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cormorant_fishing_-Suzhou_-China-6July2005.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Cormorant_fishing_-Suzhou_-China-6July2005.jpg/220px-Cormorant_fishing_-Suzhou_-China-6July2005.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cormorant_fishing_-Suzhou_-China-6July2005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Cormorant_fishing" title="Cormorant fishing">Cormorant fishing</a> in <a href="/wiki/Suzhou" title="Suzhou">Suzhou</a>, China</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Many fishermen see in the Great Cormorant a competitor for fish. Because of this it was nearly hunted to extinction in the past. Thanks to conservation efforts its numbers increased. At the moment there are about 1.2 million birds in Europe (based on winter counts. Late summer counts would show higher numbers).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Increasing populations have once again brought the cormorant into conflict with fisheries.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> For example, in Britain, where inland breeding was once uncommon, there are now increasing numbers of birds breeding inland, and many inland fish farms and fisheries now claim to be suffering high losses due to these birds. In the UK each year some licences are issued to shoot specified numbers of cormorants in order to help reduce predation, it is however still illegal to kill a bird without such a licence.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cormorant_fishing" title="Cormorant fishing">Cormorant fishing</a> is practiced in China, Japan, and elsewhere around the globe. In it, fishermen tie a line around the throats of cormorants, tight enough to prevent swallowing, and deploy them from small boats. The cormorants catch fish without being able to fully swallow them, and the fishermen are able to retrieve the fish simply by forcing open the cormorants' mouths, apparently engaging the regurgitation reflex.</p>
<p>In Norway cormorant is a traditional game bird. Each year ca. 10,000 cormorants are shot to be eaten.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> In North Norway, cormorants are traditionally seen as semi-sacred.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from June 2009">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It is regarded as good luck to have cormorants gather near your village or settlement. An old legend states that people who die far out at sea, their bodies never recovered, spend eternity on the island Utrøst – which can only occasionally be found by mortals. The inhabitants of Utrøst can only visit their homes in the shape of cormorants.<br style="clear:both;" /></p>
