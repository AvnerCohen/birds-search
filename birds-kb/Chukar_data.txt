<p>The <b>Chukar Partridge</b> or <b>Chukar</b> (<i>Alectoris chukar</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasian</a> upland <a href="/wiki/Upland_game" title="Upland game" class="mw-redirect">gamebird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Pheasant" title="Pheasant">pheasant</a> family <a href="/wiki/Phasianidae" title="Phasianidae">Phasianidae</a>. It has been considered to form a superspecies complex along with the <a href="/wiki/Rock_Partridge" title="Rock Partridge">Rock Partridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philby%27s_Partridge" title="Philby's Partridge">Philby's Partridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Przevalski%27s_Partridge" title="Przevalski's Partridge">Przevalski's Partridge</a> and treated in the past as conspecific particularly with the first. This partridge has well marked black and white bars on the flanks and a black band running from the forehead across the eye and running down the head to form a necklace that encloses a white throat. The species has been introduced into many other places and feral populations have established themselves in parts of North America and New Zealand.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukar_Partridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alectoris_chukar_hm.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Alectoris_chukar_hm.jpg/220px-Alectoris_chukar_hm.jpg" width="220" height="202" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alectoris_chukar_hm.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Illustration from <a href="/wiki/Allan_Octavian_Hume" title="Allan Octavian Hume">Hume</a> and Marshall's Game birds of India, Burma and Ceylon</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Chukar is a rotund 32–35&#160;cm (13–14&#160;in) long partridge, with a light brown back, grey breast, and buff belly. The shades vary across the various populations. The face is white with a black <a href="/wiki/Gorget_(birds)" title="Gorget (birds)" class="mw-redirect">gorget</a>. It has rufous-streaked flanks, red legs and coral red bill. Sexes are similar, the female slightly smaller in size and lacking the spur.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The tail has 14 feathers, the third primary is the longest while the first is level with the fifth and sixth primaries.<sup id="cite_ref-fbi_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fbi-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is very similar to the <a href="/wiki/Rock_Partridge" title="Rock Partridge">Rock Partridge</a> (<i>Alectoris graeca</i>) with which it has been lumped in the past<sup id="cite_ref-watson_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-watson-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> but is browner on the back and has a yellowish tinge to the foreneck. The sharply defined gorget distinguishes this species from the <a href="/wiki/Red-legged_Partridge" title="Red-legged Partridge">Red-legged Partridge</a> which has the black collar breaking into dark streaks near the breast. Their song is a noisy <i>chuck-chuck-chukar-chukar</i> from which the name is derived.<sup id="cite_ref-fbi2_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fbi2-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The Barbary partridge (<i>Alectoris barbara</i>) has a reddish brown rather than black collar with a grey throat and face with a chestnut crown.<sup id="cite_ref-johnsgard_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-johnsgard-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Other common names of this bird include Chukker (sometimes spelled as 'Chuker' or 'Chukor'), Indian Chukar and Keklik.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukar_Partridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Partridge" title="Partridge">partridge</a> has its native range in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, from Israel and Turkey through Afghanistan to India, along the inner ranges of the Western Himalayas to Nepal. Further west in southeastern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> it is replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Red-legged_Partridge" title="Red-legged Partridge">Red-legged Partridge</a>, <i>Alectoris rufa</i>. It barely ranges into <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Sinai_Peninsula" title="Sinai Peninsula">Sinai Peninsula</a>. The habitat in the native range is rocky open hillsides with grass or scattered scrub or cultivation. In Israel and Jordan it is found at low altitudes, starting at 400 metres below sea level in the Dead Sea area, whereas in the more eastern areas it is mainly found at an altitude of 2000 to 4000 m except in Pakistan, where it occurs at 600m.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-whistler_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whistler-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> They are not found in areas of high humidity or rainfall.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_gbi_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker_gbi-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It has been <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced</a> widely as a game bird, and feral populations have become established in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Basin" title="Great Basin">Great Basin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mojave_Desert" title="Mojave Desert">high desert areas of California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>. Initial introductions into the US were from the nominate populations collected from Afghanistan and Nepal.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> It has also been introduced to New South Wales in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> but breeding populations have not persisted and are probably extinct.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> A small population exists on <a href="/wiki/Robben_Island" title="Robben Island">Robben Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> since it was introduced there in 1964. <sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukar_Partridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Systematics and taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Systematics_and_taxonomy">Systematics and taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The Chukar Partridge is part of a confusing group of "Red-legged Partridges". Several plumage variations within the widespread distribution of the Chukar Partridge have been described and designated as subspecies. In the past the Chukar group was included with the <a href="/wiki/Rock_Partridge" title="Rock Partridge">Rock Partridge</a> (also known as the Greek Partridge). The species from Turkey and farther east was subsequently separated from <i>A. graeca</i> of Greece and Bulgaria and western Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-christensen_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-christensen-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukar_Partridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span></h3>
<p>There are fourteen recognized <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>A. c. chukar</i> (JE Gray, 1830) – nominate – eastern <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> to eastern <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a></li>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukar_Partridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Population and status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Population_and_status">Population and status</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:AntelopeIsland_Chukar.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/AntelopeIsland_Chukar.JPG/220px-AntelopeIsland_Chukar.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>

<p>This species is relatively unaffected by hunting or loss of habitat. Its numbers are largely affected by weather patterns during the breeding season. The release of captive stock in some parts of southern Europe can threaten native populations of Rock Partridge and Red-legged Partridge with which they may hybridize.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukar_Partridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chukarhuhn_Weltvogelpark_Walsrode_2010.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Chukarhuhn_Weltvogelpark_Walsrode_2010.jpg/240px-Chukarhuhn_Weltvogelpark_Walsrode_2010.jpg" width="240" height="159" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chukarhuhn_Weltvogelpark_Walsrode_2010.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Chukar Partridge at <a href="/wiki/Weltvogelpark_Walsrode" title="Weltvogelpark Walsrode">Weltvogelpark Walsrode</a> (<a href="/wiki/Walsrode" title="Walsrode">Walsrode</a> Bird Park, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>)</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Perdrix_choukar_MHNT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Perdrix_choukar_MHNT.jpg/220px-Perdrix_choukar_MHNT.jpg" width="220" height="158" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Perdrix_choukar_MHNT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>Alectoris chukar falki</i> - <a href="/wiki/MHNT" title="MHNT" class="mw-redirect">MHNT</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the non-breeding season, Chukar Partridge are found in small coveys of 10 or more (up to 50) birds. In summer, Chukars form pairs to breed. During this time, the cocks are very pugnacious calling and fighting.<sup id="cite_ref-whistler_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whistler-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-baker_gbi_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker_gbi-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-finn_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-finn-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbk_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> During winter they descend into the valleys and feed in fields. The call frequently during the day especially in the mornings and evenings. The call is loud and includes loud repeated "Chuck" notes and sometimes duetting "Chuker" notes. Several calls varying with context have been noted.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> The commonest call is a "rallying call" which when played back elicits a response from birds and has been used in surveys, although the method is not very reliable.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> When disturbed, it prefers to run rather than fly, but if necessary it flies a short distance often down a slope on rounded wings, calling immediately after alighting.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-oates_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oates-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-humegame_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-humegame-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> In Utah, birds were found to forage in an area of about 2.6&#160;km<sup>2</sup>. and travel up to 4.8&#160;km to obtain water during the dry season. The home range was found to be even smaller in Idaho.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The breeding season is summer. Males perform tidbitting displays, a form of <a href="/wiki/Courtship_feeding" title="Courtship feeding" class="mw-redirect">courtship feeding</a> where the male pecks at food and a female may visit to peck in response. The males may chase females with head lowered, wing lowered and neck fluffed. The male may also performs a high step stiff walk while making a special call. The female may then crouch in acceptance and the male mounts to copulate, while grasping the nape of the female. Males are monogamous.<sup id="cite_ref-christensen_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-christensen-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The nest is a scantily lined ground scrape, though occasionally a compact pad is created with a depression in the center. Generally, the nests are sheltered by ferns and small bushes, or placed in a dip or rocky hillside under a overhanging rock. About 7 to 14 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> are laid.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_gbi_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker_gbi-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbk_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> The eggs hatch in about 23–25 days. In captivity they can lay an egg each day during the breeding season if eggs are collected daily.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Chicks join their parents in foraging and will soon join the chicks of other members of the covey.<sup id="cite_ref-johnsgard_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-johnsgard-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Chukar will take a wide variety of seeds and some <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> as food. It also ingests grit.<sup id="cite_ref-oates_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oates-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> In Kashmir, the seeds of a species of <i><a href="/wiki/Eragrostis" title="Eragrostis">Eragrostis</a></i> was particularly dominant in their diet<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> while those in the US favoured <i><a href="/wiki/Bromus_tectorum" title="Bromus tectorum">Bromus tectorum</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-johnsgard_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-johnsgard-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Birds feeding on succulent vegetation make up for their water needs but visit open water in summer.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Chukar roost on rocky slopes or under shrubs. In winter, birds in the US selected protected niches or caves. A group may roost in a tight circle with their heads pointed outwards to conserve heat and keep a look out for predators.<sup id="cite_ref-johnsgard_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-johnsgard-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Chukar are sometimes preyed on by Golden Eagles.<sup id="cite_ref-bbb_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbb-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Birds in captivity can die from mycoplasma infection and outbreaks of other diseases such as <i>Erysipelas</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukar_Partridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span></h2>
<p>The Chukar is the <a href="/wiki/National_bird" title="National bird" class="mw-redirect">National bird</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>. In Punjab, the Chukar has been considered as a symbol of intense, and often unrequited, love. It was considered to be enamoured by the moon and said to constantly gaze at it.<sup id="cite_ref-temple1884_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-temple1884-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> Due to their pugnacious behaviour during the breeding season they are kept in some areas as fighting birds.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_gbi_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker_gbi-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-finn_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-finn-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>British sportsmen in India considered the Chukar as good sport although they were not considered to be particularly good in flavour. Their fast flight and ability to fly some distance after being shot made recovery of the birds difficult without retriever dogs.<sup id="cite_ref-humegame_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-humegame-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> During cold winters, when the higher areas are covered in snow, people in Kashmir have been known to use a technique to tire the birds out to catch them.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Idaho_Falls_Chukars" title="Idaho Falls Chukars">Idaho Falls Chukars</a> are a <a href="/wiki/Minor_league_baseball" title="Minor league baseball" class="mw-redirect">minor league baseball</a> affiliate of the <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Royals" title="Kansas City Royals">Kansas City Royals</a>.</p>
