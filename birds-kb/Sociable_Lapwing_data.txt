<p>The <b>Sociable Lapwing</b> or <b>Sociable Plover</b> (<i>Vanellus gregarius</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Lapwing" title="Lapwing">lapwing</a> family of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>.</p>
<p>It breeds on open grassland in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>. Three to five eggs are laid in a ground nest. These birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> south through <a href="/wiki/Kyrgyzstan" title="Kyrgyzstan">Kyrgyzstan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkmenistan" title="Turkmenistan">Turkmenistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, to key wintering sites in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eritrea" title="Eritrea">Eritrea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> and north-west <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. Birds winter occasionally in <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a>. This lapwing is a very rare vagrant in western and northern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, where this gregarious bird is usually found with <a href="/wiki/Northern_Lapwing" title="Northern Lapwing">Northern Lapwings</a>. It feeds in a similar way, picking <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and other small prey mainly from grassland or arable.</p>
<p>This attractive medium-sized lapwing has longish black legs and a short black bill. Summer adults have grey backs and breast, dark belly and white undertail. The head has a striking pattern, with a black crown and eyestripe, the latter being bordered above and below with white. The upper neck is ochre. Its longish black legs, white tail with a black terminal band and distinctive brown, white and grey wings make it almost unmistakable in flight. Length is 27–30&#160;cm (10.5–12&#160;in). The call is a harsh <i>kereck</i>.</p>
<p>Winter adults have a less distinct head pattern, slightly browner back and breast, and white belly. Young birds have a scaly back, and only vestiges of the head pattern.</p>
<p>In 2004 <a href="/wiki/BirdLife_International" title="BirdLife International">BirdLife International</a> categorised this bird as critically endangered, due to a very rapid population decline for poorly understood reasons. The main decline took place between 1960 and 1987, when the known population halved; it is presently some 20-25% of the 1930s population levels (BirdLife International 2006). The current population was estimated to be between 600 and 1,800 mature birds in 2006 (BirdLife International), but is currently being revised to the upward end of that scale, possibly more, following the discovery of the species' previously unknown main wintering grounds in <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, where 1500 birds of all ages were encountered <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.birdlife.org/news/pr/2007/03/sociable_lapwing_discovery.html">[1]</a>. Additionally, in October 2007, a superflock of approximately 3,200 Sociable Lapwing were discovered in Turkey, according to Guven Eken, director of the Turkish Nature Association <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.npr.org/templates/story/story.php?storyId=15483243">[2]</a>. The current <a href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN" class="mw-redirect">IUCN</a> classification is <b>CR A3bc</b> - meaning that the population is expected to decline in the next decade or so by 80%, but based on theoretical considerations and the known <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a> rather than direct observation of the birds. Thus, the new discoveries might mean that as more data becomes available, the species could be downlisted to <a href="/wiki/Endangered" title="Endangered" class="mw-redirect">Endangered</a>.</p>
