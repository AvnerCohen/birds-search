<p>The <b>Lanner Falcon</b> (<i>Falco biarmicus</i><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> that breeds in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, southeast <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and just into <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. It is mainly resident, but some <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">disperse</a> more widely after the breeding season.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanner_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Falco_biarmicus_-near_Tugela_Ferry,_KwaZulu-Natal,_South_Africa_-flying-8a.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Falco_biarmicus_-near_Tugela_Ferry%2C_KwaZulu-Natal%2C_South_Africa_-flying-8a.jpg/220px-Falco_biarmicus_-near_Tugela_Ferry%2C_KwaZulu-Natal%2C_South_Africa_-flying-8a.jpg" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Falco_biarmicus_-near_Tugela_Ferry,_KwaZulu-Natal,_South_Africa_-flying-8a.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Flying in South Africa</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It is a large <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon</a>, at 43–50&#160;cm length with a wingspan of 95–105&#160;cm. European Lanner Falcons (<i>Falco biarmicus feldeggi</i>, also called <b>Feldegg's Falcon</b>) have slate grey or brown-grey upperparts; most African <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are a paler blue grey above. The breast is streaked in northern birds, resembling greyish <a href="/wiki/Saker_Falcon" title="Saker Falcon">Saker Falcons</a>, but the Lanner has a reddish back to the head. Sexes are similar, but the browner young birds resemble Saker Falcons even more. However, Sakers have a lighter top of the head and less clear head-side patterns. The Lanner's call is a harsh "<i>wray-e</i>".</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanner_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span></h2>
<p>The Lanner Falcon is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of open country and <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a>. It usually hunts by horizontal pursuit, rather than the <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_Falcon" title="Peregrine Falcon">Peregrine Falcon</a>'s stoop from a height, and takes mainly <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> prey in flight. It lays 3-4 <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> on a cliff ledge <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a>, or occasionally in an old stick nest in a <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">tree</a>.</p>
<p>This is presumably the oldest living <a href="/wiki/Hierofalcon" title="Hierofalcon">hierofalcon</a> species. Support for this assumption comes mainly from <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a> agreeing better with the confusing pattern of <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">DNA sequence</a> data in this case than in others. Nonetheless, there is rampant <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridization</a> (see also <a href="/wiki/Perilanner" title="Perilanner">Perilanner</a>) and incomplete <a href="/wiki/Lineage_sorting" title="Lineage sorting" class="mw-redirect">lineage sorting</a> which confounds the data to a massive extent; molecular studies with small <a href="/wiki/Sample_size" title="Sample size" class="mw-redirect">sample sizes</a> can simply not be expected to yield reliable conclusions in the entire hierofalcon group. In any case, the <a href="/wiki/Radiation_(biology)" title="Radiation (biology)" class="mw-redirect">radiation</a> of the entire living diversity of hierofalcons seems to have taken place in the <a href="/wiki/Eemian_interglacial" title="Eemian interglacial" class="mw-redirect">Eemian interglacial</a> at the start of the <a href="/wiki/Late_Pleistocene" title="Late Pleistocene">Late Pleistocene</a>, a mere 130,000-115,000 years ago; the Lanner Falcons would thus represent the lineage that became isolated in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a> at some time during the <a href="/wiki/Riss_glaciation" title="Riss glaciation" class="mw-redirect">Riss glaciation</a> (200,000 to 130,000 years ago) already.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>They are bred in <a href="/wiki/Captivity_(animal)" title="Captivity (animal)">captivity</a> for falconry; hybrids with the Peregrine ("<a href="/wiki/Perilanner" title="Perilanner">perilanners</a>") are also often seen. Merret (1666) claimed that the "lanar" lived in <a href="/wiki/Sherwood_Forest" title="Sherwood Forest">Sherwood Forest</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Dean" title="Forest of Dean">Forest of Dean</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>; such populations would seem to derive from escaped hunting birds of the nobility.</p>
<p>In the wild Lanner Falcon numbers are somewhat declining in Europe, though the species remains relatively common in parts of Africa.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jackdaw" title="Jackdaw" class="mw-redirect">Jackdaw</a> flocks are targets of coordinated hunting by pairs of Lanner Falcons, although larger flocks are more able to elude becoming prey.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
