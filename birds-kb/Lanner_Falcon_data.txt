<p>The <b>lanner falcon</b> (<i>Falco biarmicus</i>) is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> that breeds in Africa, southeast Europe and just into Asia. It prefers open habitat and is mainly resident, but some birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">disperse</a> more widely after the breeding season. A large falcon, it preys on birds and bats.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanner_falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lanner falcon was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> by the Dutch zoologist <a href="/wiki/Coenraad_Jacob_Temminck" title="Coenraad Jacob Temminck">Coenraad Jacob Temminck</a> in 1825 under the current <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> <i>Falco biarmicus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Type_locality_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Type locality (biology)">type locality</a> is <a href="/wiki/Kaffraria" title="Kaffraria">Caffraria</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <i>Falco</i> is <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">Late Latin</a> for a "falcon", from <i>falx</i>, <i>falcis</i> "sickle". The Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> had used the specific epithet <i>biarmicus</i> for the <a href="/wiki/Bearded_reedling" title="Bearded reedling">bearded reedling</a> and Temminck clearly believed that the word meant "bearded" but it is likely that Linnaeus was using the Latinized form for <a href="/wiki/Bjarmaland" title="Bjarmaland">Bjarmaland</a>, a district in northern Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The English word "lanner" is believed to come from the Old French <i>lanier</i> meaning "cowardly". The first recorded use of the word in English is from around 1400.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This is presumably the oldest living <a href="/wiki/Hierofalcon" title="Hierofalcon">hierofalcon</a> species. Support for this assumption comes mainly from <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a> agreeing better with the confusing pattern of <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" class="mw-redirect" title="DNA sequence">DNA sequence</a> data in this case than in others. Nonetheless, there is rampant <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridization</a> (see also <a href="/wiki/Perilanner" title="Perilanner">perilanner</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Incomplete_lineage_sorting" title="Incomplete lineage sorting">incomplete lineage sorting</a> which confounds the data to a massive extent; molecular studies with small <a href="/wiki/Sample_size" class="mw-redirect" title="Sample size">sample sizes</a> can simply not be expected to yield reliable conclusions in the entire hierofalcon group. 
</p><p>In any case, the <a href="/wiki/Radiation_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Radiation (biology)">radiation</a> of the entire living diversity of hierofalcons seems to have taken place in the <a href="/wiki/Eemian_interglacial" class="mw-redirect" title="Eemian interglacial">Eemian interglacial</a> at the start of the <a href="/wiki/Late_Pleistocene" title="Late Pleistocene">Late Pleistocene</a>, a mere 130,000–115,000 years ago; the lanner falcons would thus represent the lineage that became isolated in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a> at some time during the <a href="/wiki/Riss_glaciation" title="Riss glaciation">Riss glaciation</a> (200,000 to 130,000 years ago) already.<sup id="cite_ref-Helbig1994_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helbig1994-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wink1998_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wink1998-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Nittinger2005_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nittinger2005-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wink2004_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wink2004-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are five recognised <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>F. b. biarmicus</i> Temminck, 1825 – The nominate subspecies, ranges from the Democratic Republic of the Congo to southern Kenya south to South Africa</li>
<li><i>F. b. feldeggii</i> <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Schlegel" title="Hermann Schlegel">Schlegel</a>, 1843 – Italy to Turkey, Azerbaijan and northwestern Iran</li>
<li><i>F. b. tanypterus</i> Schlegel, 1843 – northeastern Africa to Arabia, Israel and Iraq</li>
<li><i>F. b. erlangeri</i> <a href="/wiki/Otto_Kleinschmidt" title="Otto Kleinschmidt">Kleinschmidt, O.</a>, 1901 – northwestern Africa</li>
<li><i>F. b. abyssinicus</i> <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Neumann" title="Oscar Neumann">Neumann</a>, 1904 – southern Mauritania to Ethiopia and Somalia south to Cameroon and northern Kenya</li></ul>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Falco_biarmicus_MWNH_0677.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Falco_biarmicus_MWNH_0677.JPG/220px-Falco_biarmicus_MWNH_0677.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Falco_biarmicus_MWNH_0677.JPG/330px-Falco_biarmicus_MWNH_0677.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Falco_biarmicus_MWNH_0677.JPG/440px-Falco_biarmicus_MWNH_0677.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3509" data-file-height="2491" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Falco_biarmicus_MWNH_0677.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>It is a large <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon</a>, at 43–50&#160;cm (17–20&#160;in) length with a wingspan of 95–105&#160;cm (37–41&#160;in). European lanner falcons (<i>Falco biarmicus feldeggi</i>, also called <b>Feldegg's falcon</b>) have slate grey or brown-grey upperparts; most African <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are a paler blue grey above. The breast is streaked in northern birds, resembling greyish <a href="/wiki/Saker_falcon" title="Saker falcon">saker falcons</a>, but the lanner has a reddish back to the head. Sexes are similar, but the browner young birds resemble saker falcons even more. However, sakers have a lighter top of the head and less clear head-side patterns. The lanner's call is a harsh "<i>wray-e</i>".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanner_falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lanner falcon is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of open country and <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a>. It usually hunts by horizontal pursuit, rather than the <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">peregrine falcon</a>'s stoop from a height, and takes mainly <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> prey in flight. It lays three to four <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> on a cliff ledge <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a>, or occasionally in an old stick nest in a <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">tree</a>.
</p><p>They are bred in <a href="/wiki/Captivity_(animal)" title="Captivity (animal)">captivity</a> for falconry; hybrids with the peregrine ("<a href="/wiki/Perilanner" title="Perilanner">perilanners</a>") are also often seen. Merret (1666) claimed that the "lanar" lived in <a href="/wiki/Sherwood_Forest" title="Sherwood Forest">Sherwood Forest</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Dean" title="Forest of Dean">Forest of Dean</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>; such populations would seem to have derived from escaped hunting birds of the nobility.<sup id="cite_ref-Merret1666_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merret1666-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the wild, lanner falcon numbers are somewhat declining in Europe, though the species remains relatively common in parts of Africa. In the <a href="/wiki/Degua_Tembien" class="mw-redirect" title="Degua Tembien">Degua Tembien</a> mountains of <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, it was observed to contribute to controlling <a href="/wiki/Pest_rodent" class="mw-redirect" title="Pest rodent">pest rodents</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Meheretu2019_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meheretu2019-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Jackdaw" class="mw-redirect" title="Jackdaw">Jackdaw</a> flocks are targets of coordinated hunting by pairs of lanner falcons, although larger flocks are more able to elude becoming prey.<sup id="cite_ref-Leonardi1999_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leonardi1999-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, lanner falcons were observed as hunting <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
