<p>The <b>eastern black-eared wheatear</b> (<i>Oenanthe melanoleuca</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Wheatear" title="Wheatear">wheatear</a>, a small <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> that was formerly classed as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family <a href="/wiki/Turdidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Turdidae">Turdidae</a>, but is now considered to be an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a> (family <a href="/wiki/Muscicapidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Muscicapidae">Muscicapidae</a>). It was formerly (and still is by some authorities) considered <a href="/wiki/Conspecific" class="mw-redirect" title="Conspecific">conspecific</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Western_black-eared_wheatear" title="Western black-eared wheatear">western black-eared wheatear</a>. 
</p><p>The breeding male has the forehead, crown, and mantle white or nearly white with a buff tinge, and the wings blacker than those of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_wheatear" title="Northern wheatear">northern wheatear</a>. The underparts are white tinged with buff. The back, upper tail coverts and most of the tail are white. A black mask extends from the ear coverts to the bill. The throat can be either black or white.
</p><p>In autumn and winter the head and mantle are distinctly buff, as are the underparts (including the throat on non-black-throated individuals), but the buff varies in intensity. Except for the central pair, the tail feathers are much whiter than in the northern wheatear, the white on the inner web often extending to the tip.
</p><p>The female is a more gray-brown bird, but has the characteristic white lower back, and her seasonal changes are less marked.
</p><p>It is found breeding in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> , Southeast Europe to the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a> and Iran and migrates to winter quarters in the <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>.
</p><p>The male eastern black-eared wheatear can be distinguished from the male <a href="/wiki/Western_black-eared_wheatear" title="Western black-eared wheatear">western black-eared wheatear</a> by its whiter, less buff-tinted upperparts than the latter species, giving it a more distinctly black-and-white appearance, as well as by having the black of the mask reaching just above the base of the bill. Black-throated individuals of this species have a greater amount of black on the throat and face than on the western birds, and the black generally terminates more abruptly or in a straighter line. Females of this species differ from their western counterparts in being overall colder-colored and duller. 
</p>
