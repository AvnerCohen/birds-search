<p>The <b>common cuckoo</b> (<i>Cuculus canorus</i>) is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Cuckoo" title="Cuckoo">cuckoo</a> order of birds, Cuculiformes, which includes the <a href="/wiki/Geococcyx" class="mw-redirect" title="Geococcyx">roadrunners</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ani_(bird)" title="Ani (bird)">anis</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Coucal" title="Coucal">coucals</a>.
</p><p>This species is a widespread summer <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a> to Europe and Asia, and winters in Africa. It is a <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasite</a>, which means it lays eggs in the nests of other bird species, particularly of <a href="/wiki/Dunnock" title="Dunnock">dunnocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meadow_pipit" title="Meadow pipit">meadow pipits</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_reed_warbler" title="Eurasian reed warbler">reed warblers</a>. Although its eggs are larger than those of its hosts, the eggs in each type of host nest resemble the host's eggs. The adult too is a <a href="/wiki/Mimicry" title="Mimicry">mimic</a>, in its case of the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">sparrowhawk</a>; since that species is a predator, the mimicry gives the female time to lay her eggs without being seen to do so.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The species' <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a> <i>cuculus</i> (the cuckoo) and <i>canorus</i> (melodious; from <i>canere</i>, meaning to sing).<sup id="cite_ref-bto_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The cuckoo family gets its common name and <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">genus name</a> by <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeia</a> for the call of the male common cuckoo.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> word "cuckoo" comes from the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i>cucu</i>, and its earliest recorded usage in English is from around 1240, in the song <i><a href="/wiki/Sumer_Is_Icumen_In" class="mw-redirect" title="Sumer Is Icumen In">Sumer Is Icumen In</a></i>. The song is written in <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a>, and the first two lines are: "Svmer is icumen in / Lhude sing cuccu." In modern <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, this translates to "Summer has come in / Loudly sing, Cuckoo!".<sup id="cite_ref-OED_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are four subspecies worldwide:<sup id="cite_ref-ibc_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibc-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>C. c. canorus</i>, the nominate subspecies, was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his landmark 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a>. It occurs from <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> through <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> in the east, and from the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a> through <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>. It winters in Africa and South Asia.</li>
<li><i>C. c. bakeri</i>, first described by <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a> in 1912, breeds in western China to the <a href="/wiki/Himalaya" class="mw-redirect" title="Himalaya">Himalayan</a> foothills in northern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>, northwestern <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> and southern China. During the winter it is found in <a href="/wiki/Assam" title="Assam">Assam</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Bengal" title="East Bengal">East Bengal</a> and southeastern Asia.</li>
<li><i>C. c. bangsi</i> was first described by <a href="/wiki/Harry_Church_Oberholser" class="mw-redirect" title="Harry Church Oberholser">Oberholser</a> in 1919 and breeds in <a href="/wiki/Iberia" class="mw-redirect" title="Iberia">Iberia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a> and North Africa, spending the winter in Africa.</li>
<li><i>C. c. subtelephonus</i>, first described by <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Zarudny" title="Nikolai Zarudny">Zarudny</a> in 1914, breeds in Central Asia from <a href="/wiki/Turkestan" title="Turkestan">Turkestan</a> to southern <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>. It migrates to southern Asia and Africa for the winter.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lifespan_and_demography">Lifespan and demography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lifespan and demography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the common cuckoo's global population appears to be declining, it is classified of being of least concern by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a>. It is estimated that the species numbers between 25 million and 100 million individuals worldwide, with around 12.6 million to 25.8 million of those birds breeding in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The maximum recorded lifespan of a common cuckoo in the United Kingdom is 6 years, 11 months and 2 days.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo.jpg/220px-Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="152" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo.jpg/330px-Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo.jpg/440px-Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3450" data-file-height="2388" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Common cuckoo in flight</div></div></div>
<p>The common cuckoo is 32–34 centimetres (13–13&#160;in) long from bill to tail (with a tail of 13–15 centimetres (5.1–5.9&#160;in) and a wingspan of 55–60 centimetres (22–24&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The legs are short.<sup id="cite_ref-collins_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It has a greyish, slender body and long tail, similar to a sparrowhawk in flight, where the wingbeats are regular. During the breeding season, common cuckoos often settle on an open perch with drooped wings and raised tail.<sup id="cite_ref-collins_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> There is a <a href="/wiki/Rufous" title="Rufous">rufous</a> <a href="/wiki/Color_morph" class="mw-redirect" title="Color morph">colour morph</a>, which occurs occasionally in adult females but more often in juveniles.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_Cuckoo_Rongli_East_Sikkim_India_10.04.2016.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Eurasian_Cuckoo_Rongli_East_Sikkim_India_10.04.2016.jpg/220px-Eurasian_Cuckoo_Rongli_East_Sikkim_India_10.04.2016.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="308" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Eurasian_Cuckoo_Rongli_East_Sikkim_India_10.04.2016.jpg/330px-Eurasian_Cuckoo_Rongli_East_Sikkim_India_10.04.2016.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/02/Eurasian_Cuckoo_Rongli_East_Sikkim_India_10.04.2016.jpg 2x" data-file-width="428" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_Cuckoo_Rongli_East_Sikkim_India_10.04.2016.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Eurasian cuckoo (<i>C. c. bakeri</i>) from <a href="/wiki/Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary" title="Pangolakha Wildlife Sanctuary">Pangolakha Wildlife Sanctuary</a> in <a href="/wiki/East_Sikkim" class="mw-redirect" title="East Sikkim">East Sikkim</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>All adult males are slate-grey; the grey throat extends well down the bird's breast with a sharp demarcation to the barred underparts.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The iris, orbital ring, the base of the bill and feet are yellow.<sup id="cite_ref-collins_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Grey adult females have a pinkish-buff or buff background to the barring and neck sides, and sometimes small rufous spots on the <a href="/wiki/Covert_(feather)" class="mw-redirect" title="Covert (feather)">median</a> and greater coverts and the outer webs of the <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Secondaries" title="Flight feather">secondary feathers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Rufous morph adult females have reddish-brown upperparts with dark grey or black bars. The black upperpart bars are narrower than the rufous bars, as opposed to rufous juvenile birds, where the black bars are broader.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Common cuckoos in their first autumn have variable plumage. Some have strongly-barred chestnut-brown upperparts, while others are plain grey. Rufous-brown birds have heavily barred upperparts with some feathers edged with creamy-white. All have whitish edges to the upper wing-coverts and <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Primaries" title="Flight feather">primaries</a>. The secondaries and greater coverts have chestnut bars or spots. In spring, birds hatched in the previous year may retain some barred secondaries and wing-coverts.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The most obvious identification features of juvenile common cuckoos are the white nape patch and white feather fringes.<sup id="cite_ref-collins_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Common cuckoos <a href="/wiki/Moult#Birds" class="mw-redirect" title="Moult">moult</a> twice a year: a partial moult in summer and a complete moult in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Males weigh around 130 grams (4.6&#160;oz) and females 110 grams (3.9&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-bto_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The common cuckoo looks very similar to the <a href="/wiki/Oriental_cuckoo" title="Oriental cuckoo">Oriental cuckoo</a>, which is slightly shorter-winged on average.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mimicry_in_adult">Mimicry in adult</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mimicry in adult">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:European_Cuckoo_Mimics_Sparrowhawk.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Photo of sparrowhawk and cuckoo, looking similar" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/European_Cuckoo_Mimics_Sparrowhawk.jpg/220px-European_Cuckoo_Mimics_Sparrowhawk.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="264" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/European_Cuckoo_Mimics_Sparrowhawk.jpg/330px-European_Cuckoo_Mimics_Sparrowhawk.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/33/European_Cuckoo_Mimics_Sparrowhawk.jpg 2x" data-file-width="353" data-file-height="424" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:European_Cuckoo_Mimics_Sparrowhawk.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cuckoo adult (top) mimics sparrowhawk, giving female time to lay eggs parasitically</div></div></div>
<p>A study using <a href="/wiki/Taxidermy" title="Taxidermy">stuffed</a> bird models found that small birds are less likely to approach common cuckoos that have barred underparts similar to the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">Eurasian sparrowhawk</a>, a predatory bird. Eurasian reed warblers were found more aggressive to cuckoos that looked less hawk-like, meaning that the resemblance to the hawk helps the cuckoo to access the nests of potential hosts.<sup id="cite_ref-Welbergen2011_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Welbergen2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Other small birds, <a href="/wiki/Great_tit" title="Great tit">great tits</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blue_tit" class="mw-redirect" title="Blue tit">blue tits</a>, showed alarm and avoided attending feeders on seeing either (mounted) sparrowhawks or cuckoos; this implies that the cuckoo's hawklike appearance functions as protective mimicry, whether to reduce attacks by hawks or to make brood parasitism easier.<sup id="cite_ref-Davies_and_Welbergen_2008_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies_and_Welbergen_2008-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hosts attack cuckoos more when they see neighbors <a href="/wiki/Mobbing_(animal_behavior)" title="Mobbing (animal behavior)">mobbing</a> cuckoos.<sup id="cite_ref-ccevo2012_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccevo2012-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The existence of the two plumage morphs in females may be due to frequency-dependent selection if this learning applies only to the morph that hosts see neighbors mob. In an experiment with dummy cuckoos of each morph and a sparrowhawk, reed warblers were more likely to attack both cuckoo morphs than the sparrowhawk, and even more likely to mob a certain cuckoo morph when they saw neighbors mobbing that morph, decreasing the reproductive success of that morph and selecting for the less common morph.<sup id="cite_ref-ccevo2012_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccevo2012-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><div class="mediaContainer" style="width:200px"><audio id="mwe_player_0" controls="" preload="none" style="width:200px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="18.475782312925" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="Cuculus_canorus.ogg" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/0/01/Cuculus_canorus.ogg/Cuculus_canorus.ogg.mp3" type="audio/mpeg" data-title="MP3" data-shorttitle="MP3" data-transcodekey="mp3" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="107992" /><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/01/Cuculus_canorus.ogg" type="audio/ogg; codecs=&quot;vorbis&quot;" data-title="Original Ogg file (131 kbps)" data-shorttitle="Ogg source" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="131456" /></audio></div>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cuculus_canorus.ogg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Common cuckoo song, Kaluga region, Russia</div></div></div>
<p>The male's song,  <i>goo-ko</i>, is usually given from an open perch. During the breeding season the male typically gives this vocalisation with intervals of 1&#8211;1.5 seconds, in groups of 10&#8211;20 with a rest of a few seconds between groups. The female has a loud bubbling call.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The song starts as a descending minor third early in the year in April, and the interval gets wider, through a major third to a fourth as the season progresses, and in June the cuckoo "forgets its tune" and may make other calls such as ascending intervals. The wings are drooped when calling intensely and when in the vicinity of a potential female, the male often wags its tail from side to side or the body may pivot from side to side.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Essentially a bird of open land, the common cuckoo is a widespread summer <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a> to Europe and Asia, and winters in Africa. Birds arrive in Europe in April and leave in September.<sup id="cite_ref-collins_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The common cuckoo has also occurred as a vagrant in countries including <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>, the United States, <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Faroe Island">Faroe Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">Palau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">Seychelles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> and China.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Between 1995 and 2015, the distribution of cuckoos within the UK has shifted towards the north, with a decline by 69% in England but an increase by 33% in Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_feeding">Food and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Food and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The common cuckoo's diet consists of <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, with hairy <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a>, which are distasteful to many birds, being a specialty of preference.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It also occasionally eats eggs and chicks.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg/220px-Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="310" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg/330px-Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg/440px-Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg 2x" data-file-width="446" data-file-height="628" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_reed_warbler" title="Eurasian reed warbler">Eurasian reed warbler</a> is raising a common cuckoo.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasitism" class="mw-redirect" title="Brood parasitism">Brood parasitism</a></div>
<p>The common cuckoo is an obligate <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasite</a>; it lays its eggs in the nests of other birds. At the appropriate moment, the hen cuckoo flies down to the host's nest, pushes one egg out of the nest, lays an egg and flies off. The whole process takes about 10 seconds. A female may visit up to 50 nests during a breeding season. Common cuckoos first breed at the age of two years.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Egg_mimicry">Egg mimicry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Egg mimicry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>More than 100 host species have been recorded: <a href="/wiki/Meadow_pipit" title="Meadow pipit">meadow pipit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dunnock" title="Dunnock">dunnock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_reed_warbler" title="Eurasian reed warbler">Eurasian reed warbler</a> are the most common hosts in northern Europe; <a href="/wiki/Garden_warbler" title="Garden warbler">garden warbler</a>, meadow pipit, <a href="/wiki/Pied_wagtail" class="mw-redirect" title="Pied wagtail">pied wagtail</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_robin" title="European robin">European robin</a> in central Europe; <a href="/wiki/Brambling" title="Brambling">brambling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_redstart" title="Common redstart">common redstart</a> in <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Great_reed_warbler" title="Great reed warbler">great reed warbler</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Female common cuckoos are divided into <a href="/wiki/Gens_(behaviour)" title="Gens (behaviour)">gentes</a> – groups of females favouring a particular host species' nest and laying <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> that match those of that species in color and pattern. Evidence from mitochondrial DNA analyses suggest that each gene may have multiple independent origins due to parasitism of specific hosts by different ancestors.<sup id="cite_ref-ccevo2011_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccevo2011-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> One hypothesis for the inheritance of egg appearance mimicry is that this trait is inherited from the female only, suggesting that it is carried on the sex-determining W chromosome (females are WZ, males ZZ). A genetic analysis of gentes supports this proposal by finding significant differentiation in mitochondrial DNA, but not in microsatellite DNA.<sup id="cite_ref-ccevo2011_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccevo2011-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> A second proposal for the inheritance of this trait is that the genes controlling egg characteristics are carried on <a href="/wiki/Autosome" title="Autosome">autosomes</a> rather than just the W chromosome. Another genetic analysis of <a href="/wiki/Sympatry" title="Sympatry">sympatric</a> gentes supports this second proposal by finding significant genetic differentiation in both microsatellite DNA and mitochondrial DNA.<sup id="cite_ref-ccevo20112_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccevo20112-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Considering the tendency for common cuckoo males to mate with multiple females and produce offspring raised by more than one host species, it appears as though males do not contribute to the maintenance of common cuckoo gentes. However, it was found that only nine percent of offspring were raised outside of their father's presumed host species.<sup id="cite_ref-ccevo20112_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccevo20112-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Therefore, both males and females may contribute to the maintenance of common cuckoo egg mimicry polymorphism.<sup id="cite_ref-ccevo2011_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccevo2011-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ccevo20112_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccevo20112-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> It is notable that most non-parasitic cuckoo species lay white eggs, like most non-passerines other than ground-nesters.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cuckoo_Eggs_Mimicking_Reed_Warbler_Eggs.JPG" class="image"><img alt="photo of box of cuckoo and reed warbler eggs" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Cuckoo_Eggs_Mimicking_Reed_Warbler_Eggs.JPG/220px-Cuckoo_Eggs_Mimicking_Reed_Warbler_Eggs.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="217" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Cuckoo_Eggs_Mimicking_Reed_Warbler_Eggs.JPG/330px-Cuckoo_Eggs_Mimicking_Reed_Warbler_Eggs.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Cuckoo_Eggs_Mimicking_Reed_Warbler_Eggs.JPG/440px-Cuckoo_Eggs_Mimicking_Reed_Warbler_Eggs.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1897" data-file-height="1872" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cuckoo_Eggs_Mimicking_Reed_Warbler_Eggs.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cuckoo eggs mimicking smaller eggs, in this case of <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_reed_warbler" title="Eurasian reed warbler">reed warbler</a></div></div></div>
<p>As the common cuckoo evolves to lay eggs that better imitate the host's eggs, the host species adapts and is more able to distinguish the cuckoo egg. A study of 248 common cuckoo and host eggs demonstrated that female cuckoos that parasitised common redstart nests laid eggs that matched better than those that targeted dunnocks. <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">Spectroscopy</a> was used to model how the host species saw the cuckoo eggs. Cuckoos that target dunnock nests lay white, brown-speckled eggs, in contrast to the dunnock's own blue eggs. The theory suggests that common redstarts have been parasitised by common cuckoos for longer, and so have evolved to be better than the dunnocks at noticing the cuckoo eggs. The cuckoo, over time, has needed to evolve more accurate mimicking eggs to successfully parasitise the redstart. In contrast, cuckoos do not seem to have experienced evolutionary pressure to develop eggs which closely mimic the dunnock's, as dunnocks do not seem to be able to distinguish between the two species' eggs, despite the significant colour differences. The dunnock's inability to distinguish the eggs suggests that they have not been parasitised for very long, and have not yet evolved defences against it, unlike the redstart.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Studies performed on great reed warbler nests in central Hungary, showed an <i>"unusually high"</i> frequency of common cuckoo parasitism, with 64% of the nests parasitised. Of the nests targeted by cuckoos, 64% contained one cuckoo egg, 23% had two, 10% had three and 3% had four common cuckoo eggs. In total, 58% of the common cuckoo eggs were laid in nests that were multiply parasitised. When laying eggs in nests already parasitised, the female cuckoos removed one egg at random, showing no discrimination between the great reed warbler eggs and those of other cuckoos.<sup id="cite_ref-ibis2002_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibis2002-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was found that nests close to cuckoo perches were most vulnerable: multiple parasitised nests were closest to the vantage points, and unparasitised nests were farthest away. Nearly all the nests <i>"in close vicinity"</i> to the vantage points were parasitised. More visible nests were more likely to be selected by the common cuckoos. Female cuckoos use their vantage points to watch for potential hosts and find it easier to locate the more visible nests while they are egg-laying,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> however, novel studies highlight that host alarm calls might also play an important role during nest searching.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In addition, cuckoos tend to lay the eggs on the host clutch initiation day or one day before.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The great reed warblers' responses to the common cuckoo eggs varied: 66% accepted the egg(s); 12% ejected them; 20% abandoned the nests entirely; 2% buried the eggs. 28% of the cuckoo eggs were described as "<i>almost perfect</i>" in their mimesis of the host eggs, and the warblers rejected <i>"poorly mimetic"</i> cuckoo eggs more often. The degree of mimicry made it difficult for both the great reed warblers and the observers to tell the eggs apart.<sup id="cite_ref-ibis2002_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibis2002-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The egg measures 22 by 16 millimetres (0.87&#160;in ×&#160;0.63&#160;in) and weighs 3.2 grams (0.11&#160;oz), of which 7% is shell.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Research has shown that the female common cuckoo is able to keep its egg inside its body for an extra 24 hours before laying it in a host's nest. This means the cuckoo chick can hatch before the host's chicks do, and it can eject the unhatched eggs from the nest. Scientists incubated common cuckoo eggs for 24 hours at the bird's body temperature of 40&#160;°C (104&#160;°F), and examined the embryos, which were found "much more advanced" than those of other species studied. The idea of 'internal incubation' was first put forward in 1802 and 18th- and 19th-century egg collectors had reported finding that cuckoo embryos were more advanced than those of the host species.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chicks">Chicks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Chicks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cuculus_canorus_chick1.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Cuculus_canorus_chick1.JPG/220px-Cuculus_canorus_chick1.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Cuculus_canorus_chick1.JPG/330px-Cuculus_canorus_chick1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Cuculus_canorus_chick1.JPG/440px-Cuculus_canorus_chick1.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1160" data-file-height="870" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cuculus_canorus_chick1.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A chick of the common cuckoo in the nest of a <a href="/wiki/Tree_pipit" title="Tree pipit">tree pipit</a></div></div></div><p>The naked, <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a> chick hatches after 11–13 days.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It methodically evicts all host progeny from host nests. It is a much larger bird than its hosts, and needs to monopolize the food supplied by the parents. The chick will roll the other eggs out of the nest by pushing them with its back over the edge. If the host's eggs hatch before the cuckoo's, the cuckoo chick will push the other chicks out of the nest in a similar way. At 14 days old, the common cuckoo chick is about three times the size of an adult Eurasian reed warbler.
</p><p>The necessity of eviction behavior is unclear. One hypothesis is that competing with host chicks leads to decreased cuckoo chick weight, which is selective pressure for eviction behavior. An analysis of the amount of food provided to common cuckoo chicks by host parents in the presence and absence of host siblings showed that when competing against host siblings, cuckoo chicks did not receive enough food, showing an inability to compete.<sup id="cite_ref-ccevo20122_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccevo20122-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Selection pressure for eviction behavior may come from cuckoo chicks lacking the correct visual begging signals, hosts distributing food to all nestlings equally, or host recognition of the parasite.<sup id="cite_ref-ccevo20122_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccevo20122-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ABC_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Another hypothesis is that decreased cuckoo chick weight is not selective pressure for eviction behavior. An analysis of resources provided to cuckoo chick in the presence and absence of host siblings also showed that the weights of cuckoos raised with host chicks were much smaller upon fledging than cuckoos raised alone, but within 12 days cuckoos raised with siblings grew faster than cuckoos raised alone and made up for developmental differences, showing a flexibility that would not necessarily select for eviction behavior.<sup id="cite_ref-ccevo20123_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccevo20123-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Species whose broods are parasitised by the common cuckoo have evolved to discriminate against cuckoo eggs but not chicks.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Experiments have shown that common cuckoo chicks persuade their host parents to feed them by making a rapid <a href="/wiki/Begging_behavior_in_animals" class="mw-redirect" title="Begging behavior in animals">begging call</a> that sounds "remarkably like a whole brood of host chicks." The researchers suggested that "the cuckoo needs vocal trickery to stimulate adequate care to compensate for the fact that it presents a visual stimulus of just one gape."<sup id="cite_ref-ABC_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> However, a cuckoo chick needs the amount of food of a whole brood of host nestlings, and it struggles to elicit that much from the host parents with only the vocal stimulus. This may reflect a tradeoff—the cuckoo chick benefits from eviction by receiving all the food provided, but faces a cost in being the only one influencing feeding rate. For this reason, cuckoo chicks exploit host parental care by remaining with the host parent longer than host chicks do, both before and after fledging.<sup id="cite_ref-ABC_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Common cuckoo chicks fledge about 17–21 days after hatching,<sup id="cite_ref-bto_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> compared to 12–13 days for Eurasian reed warblers.<sup id="cite_ref-btoreewa_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-btoreewa-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> If the hen cuckoo is out-of-phase with a clutch of Eurasian reed warbler eggs, she will eat them all so that the hosts are forced to start another brood.
</p><p>The common cuckoo's behaviour was firstly observed and described by <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> and the combination of behaviour and anatomical adaptation by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Jenner" title="Edward Jenner">Edward Jenner</a>, who was elected as Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> in 1788 for this work rather than for his development of the <a href="/wiki/Smallpox_vaccine" title="Smallpox vaccine">smallpox vaccine</a>. It was first documented on film in 1922 by <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Chance" title="Edgar Chance">Edgar Chance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oliver_G_Pike" class="mw-redirect" title="Oliver G Pike">Oliver G Pike</a>, in their film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cuckoo%27s_Secret" class="mw-redirect" title="The Cuckoo&#39;s Secret">The Cuckoo's Secret</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-WildFilmHistory-bio_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WildFilmHistory-bio-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
A study in Japan found that young common cuckoos probably acquire species-specific <a href="/wiki/Bird_louse" title="Bird louse">feather lice</a> from body-to-body contact with other cuckoos between the time of leaving the nest and returning to the breeding area in spring. A total of 21 nestlings were examined shortly before they left their hosts' nests and none carried feather lice. However, young birds returning to Japan for the first time were found just as likely as older individuals to be lousy.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_biodiversity_indicator">As a biodiversity indicator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: As a biodiversity indicator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The occurrence of common cuckoo in Europe is a good surrogate for <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> facets including taxonomic diversity and functional diversity in bird communities, and better than the traditional use of top predators as <a href="/wiki/Bioindicator" title="Bioindicator">bioindicators</a>. The reason for this is the strong correlation between the cuckoo's host species richness and overall bird species richness, due to co-evolutionary relationships.<sup id="cite_ref-Tryjanowski_&amp;_Morelli_2015_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tryjanowski_&amp;_Morelli_2015-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> This may be useful for <a href="/wiki/Citizen_science" title="Citizen science">citizen science</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Morelli_et_al._2015_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morelli_et_al._2015-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Birds_in_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Birds in culture">Birds in culture</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> was aware of the old tale that cuckoos turned into hawks in winter. The tale was an explanation for their absence outside the summer season, later accepted by <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Natural_History_(Pliny)" title="Natural History (Pliny)">Natural History</a></i>. Aristotle rejected the claim, observing in his <i><a href="/wiki/History_of_Animals" title="History of Animals">History of Animals</a></i> that cuckoos do not have the predators' talons or hooked bills. These <a href="/wiki/Classical_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical era">Classical era</a> accounts were known to the <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Modern">Early Modern</a> English naturalist, <a href="/wiki/William_Turner_(naturalist)" title="William Turner (naturalist)">William Turner</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Davies_and_Welbergen_2008_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies_and_Welbergen_2008-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The  13th-century <a href="/wiki/Medieval_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval England">medieval English</a> <a href="/wiki/Round_(music)" title="Round (music)">round</a>, "<a href="/wiki/Sumer_Is_Icumen_In" class="mw-redirect" title="Sumer Is Icumen In">Sumer Is Icumen In</a>", celebrates the cuckoo as a sign of spring, the beginning of summer, in the first stanza, and in the chorus:<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div>

&#32;
<table class="multicol" role="presentation" style="border-collapse: collapse; padding: 0; border: 0; background:transparent; width:100%;"><tbody><tr>
<td style="text-align: left; vertical-align: top;">

<div class="poem">
<p>Svmer is icumen in<br />
Lhude sing cuccu<br />
Groweþ sed<br />
and bloweþ med<br />

</p>
</div>

</p>

</p>

</p>
</td></tr></tbody></table></div>
<p>In England, <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> alludes to the common cuckoo's association with spring, and with <a href="/wiki/Cuckoldry" class="mw-redirect" title="Cuckoldry">cuckoldry</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> in the courtly springtime song in his play <i><a href="/wiki/Love%27s_Labours_Lost" class="mw-redirect" title="Love&#39;s Labours Lost">Love's Labours Lost</a></i>:<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RhodesGillespie2014_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RhodesGillespie2014-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>When daisies pied and violets blue
<dl><dd><dl><dd>And lady-smocks all silver-white</dd></dl></dd></dl></dd>
<dd>And cuckoo-buds of yellow hue
<dl><dd><dl><dd>Do paint the meadows with delight,</dd></dl></dd></dl></dd>
<dd>The cuckoo then, on every tree,</dd>
<dd>Mocks married men; for thus sings he:&#160;
<dl><dd><dl><dd>"Cuckoo;</dd></dl></dd></dl></dd>
<dd>Cuckoo, cuckoo!" O, word of fear,
<dl><dd><dl><dd>Unpleasing to a married ear!</dd></dl></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>In Europe, hearing the call of the common cuckoo is regarded as the first harbinger of spring. Many local legends and traditions are based on this.  In <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gowk_stane" title="Gowk stane">gowk stanes</a> (cuckoo stones) sometimes associated with the arrival of the first cuckoo of spring. "Gowk" is an old name for the common cuckoo in northern <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ODBBN_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODBBN-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> derived from the harsh repeated <i>"gowk"</i> call the bird makes when excited.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The well-known <a href="/wiki/Cuckoo_clock" title="Cuckoo clock">cuckoo clock</a> features a mechanical bird and is fitted with bellows and pipes that imitate the call of the common cuckoo.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Cuckoos feature in traditional rhymes, such as '"In April the cuckoo comes, In May she'll stay, In June she changes her tune, In July she prepares to fly, Come August, go she must,"' quoted Peggy. 'But you haven't said it all,' put in Bobby. '"And if the cuckoo stays till September, It's as much as the oldest man can remember."'<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/On_Hearing_the_First_Cuckoo_in_Spring" title="On Hearing the First Cuckoo in Spring">On Hearing the First Cuckoo in Spring</a></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Symphonic_poem" title="Symphonic poem">symphonic poem</a> from Norway composed for orchestra by <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Delius" title="Frederick Delius">Frederick Delius</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Delius_IMSLP_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Delius_IMSLP-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Two English folk songs feature cuckoos. One usually called <i>The Cuckoo</i> starts:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The cuckoo is a fine bird and she sings as she flies,<br />
She brings us good tidings, she tells us no lies.<br />
She sucks little birds' eggs to make her voice clear,<br />
And never sings cuckoo till the summer draws near<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><br />
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The second, "The Cuckoo's Nest" is a song about a courtship, with the eponymous (and of course, non-existent) nest serving as a metaphor for the <a href="/wiki/Vulva" title="Vulva">vulva</a> and its tangled "nest" of <a href="/wiki/Pubic_hair" title="Pubic hair">pubic hair</a>.
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Some like a girl who is pretty in the face<br />
and some like a girl who is slender in the waist<br />
But give me a girl who will wriggle and will twist<br />
At the bottom of the belly lies the cuckoo's nest...<br />
</p><p>...Me darling, says she, I can do no such thing<br />
For me mother often told me it was committing sin<br />
Me maidenhead to lose and me sex to be abused<br />
So have no more to do with me cuckoo's nest<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><br />
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>One of the tales of the <a href="/wiki/Wise_Men_of_Gotham" title="Wise Men of Gotham">Wise Men of Gotham</a> tells how they built a hedge round a tree in order to trap a cuckoo so that it would always be summer.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
