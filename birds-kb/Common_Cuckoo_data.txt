<p>The <b>Common Cuckoo</b> (<i>Cuculus canorus</i>) (formerly European Cuckoo) is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Cuckoo" title="Cuckoo">cuckoo</a> order of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, <i>Cuculiformes</i>, which includes the <a href="/wiki/Geococcyx" title="Geococcyx">roadrunners</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ani_(bird)" title="Ani (bird)">anis</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Coucal" title="Coucal">coucals</a>.</p>
<p>This species is a widespread summer <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a> to <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, and winters in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasite</a>, which means it lays eggs in the nests of other bird species, particularly of <a href="/wiki/Dunnock" title="Dunnock">Dunnocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meadow_Pipit" title="Meadow Pipit">Meadow Pipits</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Reed_Warbler" title="Eurasian Reed Warbler">Eurasian Reed Warblers</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The Common Cuckoo (formerly European Cuckoo) is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Cuckoo" title="Cuckoo">cuckoo</a> order of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, the <i>Cuculiformes</i>, which also includes the <a href="/wiki/Geococcyx" title="Geococcyx">roadrunners</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ani_(bird)" title="Ani (bird)">anis</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Coucal" title="Coucal">coucals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The species' <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" title="Binomial name" class="mw-redirect">binomial name</a> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language" class="mw-redirect">Latin</a> <i>cuculus</i> (the cuckoo) and <i>canorus</i> (melodious; from <i>canere</i>, meaning to sing).<sup id="cite_ref-bto_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The cuckoo family gets its common name and <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">genus name</a> by <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeia</a> for the call of the male Common Cuckoo.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> word "cuckoo" comes from the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i>cucu</i> and it first appears about 1240<sup id="cite_ref-ODBBN_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODBBN-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> in the poem <i><a href="/wiki/Sumer_Is_Icumen_In" title="Sumer Is Icumen In">Sumer Is Icumen In</a></i> - "Summer has come in / Loudly sing, Cuckoo!" in modern <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>.</p>
<p>There are four subspecies worldwide:<sup id="cite_ref-ibc_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibc-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>C. c. canorus</i>, the nominate subspecies, was first described by <a href="/wiki/Linnaeus" title="Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in 1758. It occurs from the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> through <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, north <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> in the east, and from the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a> through <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>. It winters in Africa and south Asia.</li>
<li><i>C. c. bakeri</i>, first described by <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a> in 1912, breeds in western China to the <a href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya" class="mw-redirect">Himalayan</a> foothills in northern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar" class="mw-redirect">Myanmar</a>, north-west <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> and southern China. During winter it is found in <a href="/wiki/Assam" title="Assam">Assam</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Bengal" title="East Bengal">East Bengal</a> and south-east Asia.</li>
<li><i>C. c. bangsi</i> was first described by <a href="/wiki/Harry_Church_Oberholser" title="Harry Church Oberholser">Oberholser</a> in 1919 and breeds in <a href="/wiki/Iberia" title="Iberia" class="mw-redirect">Iberia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a> and north Africa, spending winter in Africa.</li>
<li><i>C. c. subtelephonus</i>, first described by <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Zarudny" title="Nikolai Zarudny">Zarudny</a> in 1914, breeds in central Asia from <a href="/wiki/Turkestan" title="Turkestan">Turkestan</a> to southern <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>. It migrates to southern Asia and Africa for winter.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lifespan and demography">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Lifespan_and_demography">Lifespan and demography</span></h2>
<p>Although the Common Cuckoo's global population appears to be declining, it is classified of being of Least Concern by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a>. It is estimated that the species numbers between 25 million and 100 million individuals worldwide, with around 12.6 million to 25.8 million of those birds breeding in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The maximum recorded lifespan of a Common Cuckoo in the United Kingdom is 6 years, 11 months and 2 days.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The Common Cuckoo is 32–34 centimetres (13–13 in) long from bill to tail (with a tail of 13–15 centimetres (5.1–5.9 in) and a wingspan of 55–60 centimetres (22–24 in).<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The legs are short.<sup id="cite_ref-collins_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> It is greyish with a slender body and long tail and can be mistaken for a <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon</a> in flight, where the wingbeats are regular. During the breeding season, Common Cuckoos often settle on an open perch with drooped wings and raised tail.<sup id="cite_ref-collins_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> There is a <a href="/wiki/Rufous" title="Rufous">rufous</a> <a href="/wiki/Color_morph" title="Color morph" class="mw-redirect">colour morph</a>, which occurs occasionally in adult females but more often in juveniles.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>All adult males are slate-grey; the grey throat extends well down the bird's breast with a sharp demarcation to the barred underparts.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The iris, orbital ring, the base of the bill and feet are yellow.<sup id="cite_ref-collins_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Grey adult females have a pinkish-buff or buff background to the barring and neck sides, and sometimes small rufous spots on the <a href="/wiki/Covert_(feather)" title="Covert (feather)" class="mw-redirect">median</a> and greater coverts and the outer webs of the <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Secondaries" title="Flight feather">secondary feathers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Rufous morph adult females have reddish-brown upperparts with dark grey or black bars. The black upperpart bars are narrower than the rufous bars, as opposed to rufous juvenile birds, where the black bars are broader.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Common Cuckoos in their first autumn have variable plumage. Some are have strongly-barred chestnut-brown upperparts, while others are plain grey. Rufous-brown birds have heavily-barred upperparts with some feathers edged with creamy-white. All have whitish edges to the upper wing-coverts and <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Primaries" title="Flight feather">primaries</a>. The secondaries and greater coverts have chestnut bars or spots. In spring, birds hatched in the previous year may retain some barred secondaries and wing-coverts.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The most obvious identification features of juvenile Common Cuckoos are the white nape patch and white feather fringes.<sup id="cite_ref-collins_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Common Cuckoos <a href="/wiki/Moult#Birds" title="Moult" class="mw-redirect">moult</a> twice a year: a partial moult in summer and a complete moult in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Males weigh around 130 grams (4.6&#160;oz) and females 110 grams (3.9&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-bto_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The Common Cuckoo looks very similar to the <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Cuckoo" title="Oriental Cuckoo">Oriental Cuckoo</a>, which is slightly shorter-winged on average.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>A study using <a href="/wiki/Taxidermy" title="Taxidermy">stuffed</a> bird models found that small birds are less likely to approach Common Cuckoos that have barred underparts similar to the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian Sparrowhawk">Eurasian Sparrowhawk</a>, a predatory bird. Eurasian Reed Warblers were found more aggressive to cuckoos that looked less hawk-like, meaning that the resemblance to the hawk helps the cuckoo to access the nests of potential hosts.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The male's call, <i>goo-ko</i>, is usually given from an open perch. During the breeding season the male typically gives this call with intervals of 1–1.5 seconds, in groups of 10–20 with a rest of a few seconds between groups. The female has a loud bubbling call.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The song starts as a descending minor third early in the year in April, and the interval gets wider, through a major third to a fourth as the season progresses, and in June the cuckoo "forgets its tune" and may make other calls such as ascending intervals. Also the cuckoo seems to have a form of absolute pitch as it tends to sing in the key of C.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>Essentially a bird of open land, the Common Cuckoo is a widespread summer <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a> to <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, and winters in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. Birds arrive in Europe in April and leave in September.<sup id="cite_ref-collins_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Common Cuckoo has also occurred as a vagrant in countries including <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" title="United States of America" class="mw-redirect">United States of America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Island" title="Faroe Island" class="mw-redirect">Faroe Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">Palau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">Seychelles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Food and feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_feeding">Food and feeding</span></h3>
<p>The Common Cuckoo's diet consists of <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, with hairy <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a>, which are distasteful to many birds, being a speciality of preference. It also occasionally eats eggs and chicks.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg/220px-Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg" width="220" height="310" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg/330px-Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg/440px-Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
This <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Reed_Warbler" title="Eurasian Reed Warbler">Eurasian Reed Warbler</a> is raising a Common Cuckoo.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Common Cuckoo is a <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasite</a>; it lays its eggs in the nests of other birds. At the appropriate moment, the hen cuckoo flies down to the host's nest, pushes one egg out, lays an egg and flies off. The whole process takes about 10 seconds. A female may visit up to 50 nests during a breeding season. Common Cuckoos first breed at two years old.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Eggs">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Eggs">Eggs</span></h3>
<p>More than 100 host species have been recorded: <a href="/wiki/Meadow_Pipit" title="Meadow Pipit">Meadow Pipit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dunnock" title="Dunnock">Dunnock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Reed_Warbler" title="Eurasian Reed Warbler">Eurasian Reed Warbler</a> are the most common hosts in northern Europe; <a href="/wiki/Garden_Warbler" title="Garden Warbler">Garden Warbler</a>, Meadow Pipit, <a href="/wiki/Pied_Wagtail" title="Pied Wagtail" class="mw-redirect">Pied Wagtail</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_Robin" title="European Robin">European Robin</a> in central Europe; <a href="/wiki/Brambling" title="Brambling">Brambling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_Redstart" title="Common Redstart">Common Redstart</a> in <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Great_Reed_Warbler" title="Great Reed Warbler">Great Reed Warbler</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Female Common Cuckoos are divided into <a href="/wiki/Gens_(behaviour)" title="Gens (behaviour)">gentes</a> – populations favouring a particular host species' nest and laying <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> that match those of that species in colour and pattern. Evidence from mitochondrial DNA analyses suggest that each gens may have multiple independent origins due to parasitism of specific hosts by different ancestors.<sup id="cite_ref-ccevo2011_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccevo2011-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> One hypothesis for the inheritance of egg appearance mimicry is that this trait is inherited from the female only, suggesting that it is carried on the sex-determining W chromosome (females are WZ, males ZZ). A genetic analysis of gentes supports this proposal by finding significant differentiation in mitochondrial DNA, but not in microsatellite DNA.<sup id="cite_ref-ccevo2011_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccevo2011-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> A second proposal for the inheritance of this trait is that the genes controlling egg characteristics are carried on autosomes rather than just the W chromosome. Another genetic analysis of sympatric gentes supports this second proposal by finding significant genetic differentiation in both microsatellite DNA and mitochondrial DNA.<sup id="cite_ref-ccevo20112_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccevo20112-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Considering the tendency for Common Cuckoo males to mate with multiple females and produce offspring raised by more than one host species, it appears as though males do not contribute to the maintenance of Common Cuckoo gentes. However, it was found that only nine percent of offspring were raised outside of their father's presumed host species.<sup id="cite_ref-ccevo20112_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccevo20112-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Therefore, both males and females may contribute to the maintenance of Common Cuckoo egg mimicry polymorphism.<sup id="cite_ref-ccevo2011_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccevo2011-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ccevo20112_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccevo20112-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> It is notable that most non-parasitic cuckoo species lay white eggs, like most non-passerines other than ground-nesters.</p>
<p>As the Common Cuckoo evolves to lay eggs that better imitate the host's eggs, the host species adapts and is more able to distinguish the cuckoo egg. A study of 248 Common Cuckoo and host eggs demonstrated that female cuckoos that parasitised Common Redstart nests laid eggs that matched better than those that targeted Dunnocks. <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">Spectroscopy</a> was used to model how the host species saw the cuckoo eggs. Cuckoos that target Dunnock nests lay white, brown-speckled eggs, in contrast to the Dunnock's own blue eggs. The theory suggests that Common Redstarts have been parasitised by Common Cuckoos for longer, and so have evolved to be better than the Dunnocks at noticing the cuckoo eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Studies were made of 90 Great Reed Warbler nests in central Hungary. There was an <i>"unusually high"</i> frequency of Common Cuckoo parasitism, with 64% of the nests parasitised. Of the nests targeted by cuckoos, 64% contained one cuckoo egg, 23% had two, 10% had three and 3% had four Common Cuckoo eggs. In total, 58% of the Common Cuckoo eggs were laid in nests that were multiply parasitised. When laying eggs in nests already parasitised, the female cuckoos removed one egg at random, showing no discrimination between the Great Reed Warbler eggs and those of other cuckoos.<sup id="cite_ref-ibis2002_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibis2002-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It was found that nests close to cuckoo perches were most vulnerable: multiple parasitised nests were closest to the vantage points, and unparasitised nests were farthest away. Nearly all the nests <i>"in close vicinity"</i> to the vantage points were parasitised. More visible nests were more likely to be selected by the Common Cuckoos. Female cuckoos use their vantage points to watch for potential hosts and find it easier to locate the more visible nests while they are egg-laying.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Great Reed Warblers' responses to the Common Cuckoo eggs varied: 66% accepted the egg(s); 12% ejected them; 20% abandoned the nests entirely; 2% buried the eggs. 28% of the cuckoo eggs were described as "<i>almost perfect</i>" in their mimesis of the host eggs, and the warblers rejected <i>"poorly mimetic"</i> cuckoo eggs more often. The degree of mimicry made it difficult for both the Great Reed Warblers and the observers to tell the eggs apart.<sup id="cite_ref-ibis2002_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibis2002-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The egg measures 22 by 16 millimetres (0.87&#160;in ×&#160;0.63&#160;in) and weighs 3.2 grams (0.11&#160;oz), of which 7% is shell.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Research has shown that the female Common Cuckoo is able to keep its egg inside its body for an extra 24 hours before laying it in a host's nest. This means the cuckoo chick can hatch before the host's chicks do, and it can eject the unhatched eggs from the nest. Scientists incubated Common Cuckoo eggs for 24 hours at the bird's body temperature of <span style="white-space:nowrap;">40 °C</span> <span style="white-space:nowrap;">(104&#160;°F)</span>, and examined the embryos, which were found "much more advanced" than those of other species studied. The idea of 'internal incubation' was first put forward in 1802 and 18th and 19th Century egg collectors had reported finding that cuckoo embryos were more advanced than those of the host species.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Chicks">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Chicks">Chicks</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cuculus_canorus_chick1.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Cuculus_canorus_chick1.JPG/220px-Cuculus_canorus_chick1.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Cuculus_canorus_chick1.JPG/330px-Cuculus_canorus_chick1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Cuculus_canorus_chick1.JPG/440px-Cuculus_canorus_chick1.JPG 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cuculus_canorus_chick1.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Chick of Common Cuckoo in nest of <a href="/wiki/Tree_Pipit" title="Tree Pipit">Tree Pipit</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The naked, <a href="/wiki/Altricial" title="Altricial">altricial</a> chick hatches after 11–13 days.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> It methodically evicts all host progeny from host nests. It is a much larger bird than its hosts, and needs to monopolise the food supplied by the parents. The chick will roll the other eggs out of the nest by pushing them with its back over the edge. If the host's eggs hatch before the cuckoo's, the cuckoo chick will push the other chicks out of the nest in a similar way. At 14 days old, the Common Cuckoo chick is about three times the size of an adult Eurasian Reed Warbler.</p>
<p>Species whose broods are parasitised by the Common Cuckoo have evolved to discriminate against cuckoo eggs but not chicks.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Experiments have shown that Common Cuckoo chicks persuade their host parents to feed them by making a rapid begging call that sounds <i>"remarkably like a whole brood of host chicks."</i> The researchers suggested that <i>"the cuckoo needs vocal trickery to stimulate adequate care to compensate for the fact that it presents a visual stimulus of just one gape."</i><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Common Cuckoo chicks fledge about 17–21 days after hatching,<sup id="cite_ref-bto_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> compared to 12–13 days for Eurasian Reed Warblers.<sup id="cite_ref-btoreewa_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-btoreewa-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> If the hen cuckoo is out-of-phase with a clutch of Eurasian Reed Warbler eggs, she will eat them all so that the hosts are forced to start another brood.</p>
<p>The Common Cuckoo's behaviour was firstly observed and described by <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> and the combination of behaviour and anatomical adaptation by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Jenner" title="Edward Jenner">Edward Jenner</a>, who was elected as Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> in 1788 for this work. It was first documented on film in 1922 by <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Chance" title="Edgar Chance">Edgar Chance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oliver_G_Pike" title="Oliver G Pike" class="mw-redirect">Oliver G Pike</a>, in their film 'The Cuckoo's Secret'.<sup id="cite_ref-WildFilmHistory-bio_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WildFilmHistory-bio-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A study in Japan found that young Common Cuckoos probably acquire species-specific feather <a href="/wiki/Louse" title="Louse">lice</a> from body-to-body contact with other cuckoos between the time of leaving the nest and returning to the breeding area in spring. A total of 21 nestlings were examined shortly before they left their hosts' nests and none carried feather lice. However, young birds returning to Japan for the first time were found just as likely as older individuals to be lousy.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, hearing the call of the Common Cuckoo is regarded as the first harbinger of spring. Many local legends and traditions are based on this. In <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, a number of <a href="/wiki/Gowk_Stone" title="Gowk Stone">Gowk Stones</a> exist, sometimes associated with the arrival of the first cuckoo of spring. "Gowk" is an old name for the Common Cuckoo in northern <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ODBBN_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODBBN-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> derived from a harsh repeated <i>"gowk"</i> call the bird makes when excited.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The well-known <a href="/wiki/Cuckoo_clock" title="Cuckoo clock">cuckoo clock</a> features a mechanical bird and is fitted with bellows and pipes that imitate the call of the Common Cuckoo.</p>
