</tbody></table><p>The <b>whooper swan</b> (<i>Cygnus cygnus</i>), also known as the <b>common swan</b>, pronounced <i>hooper swan</i>, is a large northern hemisphere <a href="/wiki/Swan" title="Swan">swan</a>. It is the Eurasian counterpart of the North American <a href="/wiki/Trumpeter_swan" title="Trumpeter swan">trumpeter swan</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a> for the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Cygnus_(genus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cygnus (genus)">Cygnus</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Francis_Willughby" title="Francis Willughby">Francis Willughby</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Ray" title="John Ray">John Ray</a>'s <i>Ornithology</i> of 1676 referred to this swan as "the Elk, Hooper, or wild Swan".<sup id="cite_ref-Willughby1676_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Willughby1676-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>23</span></sup> The scientific name is from <i>cygnus</i>, the Latin for "swan".<sup id="cite_ref-job90_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job90-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" aria-labelledby="mw-toc-heading"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2 id="mw-toc-heading">Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whooper_swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cygnus_cygnus,_Whooper_Swan,_Singschwan.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Cygnus_cygnus%2C_Whooper_Swan%2C_Singschwan.jpg/220px-Cygnus_cygnus%2C_Whooper_Swan%2C_Singschwan.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Cygnus_cygnus%2C_Whooper_Swan%2C_Singschwan.jpg/330px-Cygnus_cygnus%2C_Whooper_Swan%2C_Singschwan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Cygnus_cygnus%2C_Whooper_Swan%2C_Singschwan.jpg/440px-Cygnus_cygnus%2C_Whooper_Swan%2C_Singschwan.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3214" data-file-height="2442" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cygnus_cygnus,_Whooper_Swan,_Singschwan.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Head detail</div></div></div>
<p>The whooper swan is similar in appearance to <a href="/wiki/Bewick%27s_swan" class="mw-redirect" title="Bewick&#39;s swan">Bewick's swan</a>. It is larger, however, at a length of 140–165 centimetres (55–65 inches) and a wingspan of 205–275&#160;cm (81–108&#160;in). The weight is typically in the range of 7.4–14.0 kilograms (<span class="frac nowrap">16<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span>–<span class="frac nowrap">30<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>3</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span> pounds), with an average of 9.8–11.4&#160;kg (<span class="frac nowrap">21<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span>–<span class="frac nowrap">25<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span>&#160;lb) for males and 8.2–9.2&#160;kg (18–<span class="frac nowrap">20<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span>&#160;lb) for females. The verified record mass was 15.5&#160;kg (<span class="frac nowrap">34<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span>&#160;lb) for a wintering male from <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>. It is considered to be amongst the heaviest flying birds.<sup id="cite_ref-Brazil2003_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brazil2003-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CRC_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Among standard measurements, the wing chord is 56.2–63.5&#160;cm (<span class="frac nowrap">22<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>8</sub></span>–25&#160;in), the tarsus is 10.4–13.0&#160;cm (<span class="frac nowrap">4<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>3</sup>&#8260;<sub>32</sub></span>–<span class="frac nowrap">5<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>8</sub></span>&#160;in) and the bill is 9.2–11.6&#160;cm (3.6–4.6&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It has a more angular head shape and a more variable <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> pattern that always shows more yellow than black (Bewick's swans have more black than yellow). Like their close relatives, whooper swans are vocal birds with a call similar to the trumpeter swan.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_behaviour">Distribution and behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whooper_swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cygnus_cygnus_MWNH_1960.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Cygnus_cygnus_MWNH_1960.JPG/220px-Cygnus_cygnus_MWNH_1960.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Cygnus_cygnus_MWNH_1960.JPG/330px-Cygnus_cygnus_MWNH_1960.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Cygnus_cygnus_MWNH_1960.JPG/440px-Cygnus_cygnus_MWNH_1960.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cygnus_cygnus_MWNH_1960.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>Whooper swans require large areas of water to live in, especially when they are still growing, because their body weight cannot be supported by their legs for extended periods of time. The whooper swan spends much of its time swimming, straining the water for food, or eating plants that grow on the bottom.<sup id="cite_ref-Mondadori_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mondadori-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Whooper swans have a deep honking call and, despite their size, are powerful fliers. Whooper swans can <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> hundreds or even thousands of miles to their wintering sites in southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and eastern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. They breed in subarctic <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurosiberia</a>, further south than Bewicks in the <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a> zone. They are rare breeders in northern <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a>, and no more than five pairs have bred there in recent years; a handful of pairs have also bred in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> in recent years. This bird is an occasional vagrant to the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Subcontinent" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian Subcontinent">Indian Subcontinent</a><sup id="cite_ref-Hillpost_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hillpost-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and western <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Icelandic</a> breeders overwinter in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Wildfowl" class="mw-redirect" title="Wildfowl">wildfowl</a> <a href="/wiki/Nature_reserve" title="Nature reserve">nature reserves</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Protection_of_Birds" title="Royal Society for the Protection of Birds">Royal Society for the Protection of Birds</a> and of the <a href="/wiki/Wildfowl_and_Wetlands_Trust" class="mw-redirect" title="Wildfowl and Wetlands Trust">Wildfowl and Wetlands Trust</a>.
</p><p>Whooper swans pair for life, and their cygnets stay with them all winter; they are sometimes joined by offspring from previous years. Their preferred breeding habitat is <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetland</a>, but semi-domesticated birds will build a nest anywhere close to water. Both the male and female help build the nest, and the male will stand guard over the nest while the female incubates. The female will usually lay 4–7 eggs (exceptionally 12). The cygnets hatch after about 36 days and have a grey or brown plumage. The cygnets can fly at an age of 120 to 150 days.
</p><p>When whooper swans prepare to go on a flight as a flock, they use a variety of signaling movements to communicate with each other. These movements include head bobs, head shakes, and wing flaps and influence whether the flock will take flight and if so, which individual will take the lead.<sup id="cite_ref-Black_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Black-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Whooper swans that signaled with these movements in large groups were found to be able to convince their flock to follow them 61% of the time.<sup id="cite_ref-Black_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Black-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In comparison, swans that did not signal were only able to create a following 35% of the time.<sup id="cite_ref-Black_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Black-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In most cases, the whooper swan in the flock that makes the most movements (head bobs) is also the swan that initiates the flight of the flock – this initiator swan can be either male or female, but is more likely to be a parent than a cygnet.<sup id="cite_ref-Black_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Black-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, this signaling method may be a way for paired mates to stay together in flight. Observational evidence indicates that a swan whose mate is paying attention to and participates in its partner's signals will be more likely to follow through with the flight. Thus, if a whooper swan begins initiating flight signals, it will be less likely to actually carry through with the flight if its mate is not paying attention and is therefore less likely to join it.<sup id="cite_ref-Black_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Black-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>They are very noisy; the calls are strident, similar to those of Bewick's swan but more resonant and lower-pitched on average: <i>kloo-kloo-kloo</i> in groups of three or four.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whooper_swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Whooper swans are much admired in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Mondadori_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mondadori-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The whooper swan is the national bird of <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and is featured on the Finnish 1 <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a> coin. The whooper swan is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies. Musical utterances by whooper swans at the moment of death have been suggested as the origin of the <a href="/wiki/Swan_song" title="Swan song">swan song</a> legend.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Global_spread_of_H5N1" title="Global spread of H5N1">global spread of H5N1</a> reached the <a href="/wiki/UK" class="mw-redirect" title="UK">UK</a> in April 2006 in the form of a dead whooper swan found in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
