<p>The <b>pied bush chat</b> (<i>Saxicola caprata</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> found ranging from <a href="/wiki/West_Asia" class="mw-redirect" title="West Asia">West Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>. About sixteen subspecies are recognized through its wide range with many island forms. It is a familiar bird of countryside and open scrub or grassland where it is found perched at the top of short thorn trees or other shrubs, looking out for insect prey. They pick up insects mainly from the ground, and were, like other <a href="/wiki/Chat_(bird)" title="Chat (bird)">chats</a>, placed in the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family Turdidae, but are now considered as <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatchers</a>.
</p><p>They nest in cavities in stone walls or in holes in an embankment, lining the nest with grass and animal hair. The males are black with white shoulder and vent patches whose extent varies among populations. Females are predominantly brownish while juveniles are speckled.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pied_bush_chat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1760 the French zoologist <a href="/wiki/Mathurin_Jacques_Brisson" title="Mathurin Jacques Brisson">Mathurin Jacques Brisson</a> included a description of the pied bush chat in his <i>Ornithologie</i> based on a specimen collected from the island of <a href="/wiki/Luzon" title="Luzon">Luzon</a> in the Philippines. He used the French name <i>Le traquet de l'Isle de Luçon</i> and the Latin <i>Rubetra Lucionensis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-brisson_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brisson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Although Brisson coined Latin names, these do not conform to the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial system</a> and are not recognised by the <a href="/wiki/International_Commission_on_Zoological_Nomenclature" title="International Commission on Zoological Nomenclature">International Commission on Zoological Nomenclature</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-allen_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allen-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> When in 1766 the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> updated his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/12th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="12th edition of Systema Naturae">twelfth edition</a>, he added 240 species that had been previously described by Brisson.<sup id="cite_ref-allen_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allen-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> One of these was the pied bush chat. Linnaeus included a brief description, coined the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> <i>Motacilla caprata</i> and cited Brisson's work.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific epithet</a> <i>caprata</i> is from the local name which according to Brisson was <i>Maria-capra</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-brisson_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brisson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbwkey_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwkey-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This species is now placed in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Saxicola" title="Saxicola">Saxicola</a></i> that was introduced by the German naturalist <a href="/wiki/Johann_Matth%C3%A4us_Bechstein" title="Johann Matthäus Bechstein">Johann Matthäus Bechstein</a> in 1802.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> There are 16 recognised <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pied_bush_chat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>At 13&#160;cm, the pied bush chat is slightly smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Siberian_stonechat" title="Siberian stonechat">Siberian stonechat</a>, <i>Saxicola maurus</i>, although it has a similar dumpy structure and upright stance. The male is black except for a white rump, wing patch and lower belly. The iris is dark brown, the bill and legs black. The female is drab brown and slightly streaked. Juveniles have a scaly appearance on the underside but dark above like the females.
</p><p>A number of geographic populations have been given subspecies status:
</p>
<ul><li>nominate <i>caprata</i> (Linnaeus, 1766) is found in Luzon and Mindoro, in the Philippines.</li>
<li><i>rossorum</i>  (<a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a>, 1910) is found in NE Iran SC Kazakhstan S to Afghanistan and Baluchistan; migrant in SW Asia (vagrant in Arabia, Israel).(shows more white below than <i>bicolor</i>, described by Hartert, Jour. f. Orn. 1910:180 but not always recognized<sup id="cite_ref-rasmussen_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rasmussen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>)</li>
<li><i>bicolor</i> <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Sykes" title="William Henry Sykes">Sykes</a>, 1832 is found in SE Iran, Afghanistan, Pakistan and N India; migrating to central India and possibly southern India.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-whistler_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whistler-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>burmanicus</i> <a href="/wiki/E._C._Stuart_Baker" title="E. C. Stuart Baker">Stuart Baker</a>, 1922 is found in peninsular India E to Myanmar and S China (S Sichuan, Yunnan), S to Thailand and Indochina. This has the white on the abdomen restricted towards the vent.</li>
<li><i>nilgiriensis</i> <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Whistler" title="Hugh Whistler">Whistler</a>, 1940 is found in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Ghats" title="Western Ghats">Western Ghats</a> and the Nilgiri Hills.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>atratus</i> (Blyth, 1851) is restricted to Sri Lanka. This has a large bill.<sup id="cite_ref-rasmussen_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rasmussen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Some of these isolated populations are found on islands and they include:
</p>
<ul><li><i>randi</i> Parkes, 1960 found in the central Philippines (Panay, Negros, Cebu, Bohol, Siquijor).</li>
<li><i>anderseni</i> <a href="/wiki/Finn_Salomonsen" title="Finn Salomonsen">Salomonsen</a>, 1953 found on Leyte and Mindanao, in the Philippines.</li>
<li><i>fruticola</i> <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Horsfield" title="Thomas Horsfield">Horsfield</a>, 1821 found in Java E to Flores and Alor.</li>
<li><i>francki</i> <a href="/wiki/Bernhard_Rensch" title="Bernhard Rensch">Rensch</a>, 1931 is found on the Sumba Islands</li>
<li><i>pyrrhonotus</i> (Vieillot, 1818) found in the E Lesser Sundas (Wetar, Kisar, Timor, Savu, Roti).</li>
<li><i>albonotatus</i> (<a href="/wiki/Erwin_Stresemann" title="Erwin Stresemann">Stresemann</a>, 1912) found in Sulawesi (except N peninsula) and Salayer I.</li>
<li><i>cognatus</i> <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Mayr" title="Ernst Mayr">Mayr</a>, 1944 on Babar Island.</li>
<li><i>belensis</i> Rand, 1940 in WC New Guinea.</li>
<li><i>aethiops</i> (<a href="/wiki/Philip_Sclater" title="Philip Sclater">P. L. Sclater</a>, 1880) in N New Guinea and Bismarck Archipelago.</li>
<li><i>wahgiensis</i> Mayr &amp; Gilliard, 1951 in EC &amp; E New Guinea.</li></ul>
<p>This species is closely related to the <a href="/wiki/European_stonechat" title="European stonechat">European</a>-<a href="/wiki/African_stonechat" title="African stonechat">African stonechat</a> complex.
<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <i>S. c. fruticola</i> from Indonesia (Moyo Island population appeared to be well differentiated from specimens from Lembata Island with a divergence estimated to about 360,000 years ago.), <i>S. c. pyrrhonota</i> from West Timor (Indonesia).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Local names include <i>Kala pidda</i> in Hindi <i>Shyama</i> in Gujarati <i>Kavda gapidda</i> in Marathi <i>Kallu kuruvi</i> in Tamil, <i>Kampa nalanchi</i> in Telugu.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Fore_people" title="Fore people">Fore people</a> of New Guinea called it <i>pobogile</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> They were once popular in Bengal as cage birds.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> They are still found in the local bird trade of some parts of Southeast Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pied_bush_chat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The pied bush chat is a resident breeder in tropical southern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Middle_East" title="Greater Middle East">Greater Middle East</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> eastwards to <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>. They colonized <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a> around 1950.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> It is found in open habitats including scrub, grassland and cultivation.
</p><p>Some populations are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">partially migratory</a>. A ringed individual of subspecies <i>rossorum</i> has been recovered from Israel.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The populations in India also appear to show seasonal movements but the patterns are unclear. Subspecies <i>bicolor</i> is found in peninsular India in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In Karwar on the western coast, it is said to appear in October and stay till May but not seen during the rainy season.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Said to be absent in the Baroda district of Gujarat from April to September.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ticehurst_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ticehurst-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Claud_Buchanan_Ticehurst" class="mw-redirect" title="Claud Buchanan Ticehurst">Claud Buchanan Ticehurst</a> noted that it was a summer visitor to Baluchistan leaving in October and further that the birds from Baluchistan were indistinguishable from <i>rossorum</i> of Turkestan.<sup id="cite_ref-ticehurst_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ticehurst-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:left;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pied_bush_chat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><div class="mediaContainer" style="width:220px"><audio id="mwe_player_0" controls="" preload="none" style="width:220px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="10.762625" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="SaxicolaCaprataCall.ogg" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/09/SaxicolaCaprataCall.ogg" type="audio/ogg; codecs=&quot;vorbis&quot;" data-title="Original Ogg file (38 kbps)" data-shorttitle="Ogg source" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="38206" /><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/0/09/SaxicolaCaprataCall.ogg/SaxicolaCaprataCall.ogg.mp3" type="audio/mpeg" data-title="MP3" data-shorttitle="MP3" data-transcodekey="mp3" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="63176" /></audio></div>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SaxicolaCaprataCall.ogg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Calls</div></div></div>
<p>The breeding season is mainly February to August with a peak in March to June. Males sing from prominent perches. The whistling call is somewhat like that of an <a href="/wiki/Indian_robin" title="Indian robin">Indian robin</a> and has been transcribed as <i>we are tea for two</i> with <i>tea</i> at higher note. The nest is built in a hole in a wall or similar site lined with grass and hair, and 2-5 eggs are laid.<sup id="cite_ref-rasmussen_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rasmussen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Paired males did not reduce their dawn singing behaviour when their mates where trapped and temporarily excluded from the territory. This study suggests that males use dawn chorus to mediate social relationships with neighbouring males to proclaim an established territory.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The eggs are small and broadly oval with pale bluish-white or pinkish ground colour and speckles and blotches towards the broad end. They measure about 0.67 by 0.55&#160;inches.<sup id="cite_ref-whistler_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whistler-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Eggs are incubated chiefly by the female for 12 to 13 days.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">Brood parasitism</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common cuckoo</a> (race <i>bakeri</i>) has been noted to be common in the <a href="/wiki/Shan_State" title="Shan State">Shan State</a> of Burma, with the cuckoo visiting the nest at dusk and removing an egg before quickly laying its own.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
The female has dark brown upperparts and rufous underparts and rump. She has no white wing patches. Juveniles are similar to females. Males display during the breeding season by splaying the tail, fluttering and puffing up the white scapular feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-whistler_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whistler-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species is insectivorous, and like other chats hunts from a prominent low perch. They have been noted to feed on <a href="/wiki/Pyralidae" title="Pyralidae">Pyralid</a> moths and <a href="/wiki/Whitefly" title="Whitefly">whitefly</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Nematode parasites in the genus <i>Acuaria</i> have been noted.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Adult birds have few predators although bats (<i><a href="/wiki/Megaderma_lyra" class="mw-redirect" title="Megaderma lyra">Megaderma lyra</a></i>)<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and wintering <i><a href="/wiki/Asio_flammeus" class="mw-redirect" title="Asio flammeus">Asio flammeus</a></i> have been noted<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> to prey on them.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pied_bush_chat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Among the <a href="/wiki/Toda_people" title="Toda people">Toda people</a> in the Nilgiris, the pied bushchat or kāŗpiłc, is a bird of omen and the origin of its white wing patches is described in a story of a dairy priest <i>Piu.f</i> who in the ritual of churning milk forgot to remove the churning stick before fetching water from a stream. The bird attempted to obstruct him by blocking his path but he disregarded it and flicked off butter from his hands. The white spots remained but <i>Piu.f</i> met his death. The <a href="/wiki/Kota_people_(India)" title="Kota people (India)">Kotas</a> of the Nilgiris have an origin story to explain the sexual dimorphism of the pied bushchat but they consider the "karyvaky" bird to be one that foretells good omen.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The Kalam people of Papua New Guinea consider the birds as messengers.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
