<p>The <b>Desert Finch</b> (<i>Carduelis obsoleta</i>), sometimes called <b>Lichtenstein's Desert Finch</b>, is a large brown <a href="/wiki/Fringillidae" title="Fringillidae" class="mw-redirect">true finch</a> found in southern <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>. Its <a href="/wiki/Linnaean_taxonomy" title="Linnaean taxonomy">taxonomy</a> is confused, and it has formerly been placed in <i>Fringilla</i>, <i>Bucanetes</i>, <i>Rhodospiza</i> and <i>Rhodopechys</i>.</p>
<p>It has an average wingspan of 26 centimetres (10&#160;in). It has a stout black bill, black and white <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">remiges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">rectrices</a>, and a slash of rosy-pink on each wing. The female is more dull in color than the male, but other than that the adult sexes are similar in color pattern.</p>
<p>The bird is indeed a <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> resident in areas where water is readily available, but it can also be found in low mountains and foothills, and in <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">cultivated</a> valleys. It feeds on seeds and the occasional insect. Nesting occurs in trees in the spring, often in fruit trees in orchards, and the female lays and incubates 4 to 6 pale green, lightly speckled eggs.</p>
<p>This species does not migrate except locally. The Desert Finch congregates near rural and remote human settlements, and the well-watered orchard in otherwise arid land is an ideal habitat. It can be found in feeding in large flocks of its own species or mixed finch flocks.</p>
<p>Recent research by Zamora <i>et al.</i> (2006) has revealed that the Desert Finch is more closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Greenfinch" title="Greenfinch">greenfinches</a> of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Carduelis" title="Carduelis">Carduelis</a></i> (or <i>Chloris</i>, if <i>Carduelis</i> is split up) as indicated by <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> sequence analysis, vocalizations, and the presence of a black eye-stripe. Genetically, it seems very close to the common ancestor of the greenfinches. It may be that the latter evolved from a desert form and later developed the green plumage, or that the common ancestor of the greenfinches and the Desert Finch (which lived around 6 million years ago) was a species of <a href="/wiki/Semiarid" title="Semiarid" class="mw-redirect">semiarid</a> habitat which subsequently diverged into a truly desert-adapted lineage, today represented by the Desert Finch, and the ancestor of a woodlands lineage, the greenfinches. The distinctness of the Desert Finch from the other <i>Rhodopechys</i> species was recognized as early as 1888 by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bowdler_Sharpe" title="Richard Bowdler Sharpe">Sharpe</a>,<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from February 2007">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but it was not until recently that its true affinities were determined.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Desert_Finch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin of greenfinches">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_greenfinches">Origin of greenfinches</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Origin_of_greenfinches.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Origin_of_greenfinches.jpg/320px-Origin_of_greenfinches.jpg" width="320" height="335" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Origin_of_greenfinches.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Hypothetical origin of greenfinches</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The common ancestor of all <a href="/wiki/Greenfinch" title="Greenfinch">greenfinches</a>, which are among the oldest <i>Carduelis</i> species, appeared on Earth around 9 <a href="/wiki/Mya_(unit)#Symbols_y_and_yr" title="Mya (unit)" class="mw-redirect">MYA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> One of these common ancestors is the desert finch, which now thrives in Asian deserts, but could have also inhabited African deserts.<br />
This finch, with very pale colors and a breeding season mask similar to that of the common greenfinch, used to be classified, according to phenotypical (external) characters, close to the trumpeteer finch, but it belongs to genus <i>Carduelis</i> according to several molecular studies.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Two subfossil greenfinches, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Slender-billed_Greenfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Slender-billed Greenfinch (page does not exist)">Slender-billed Greenfinch</a>, <i>Carduelis aurelioi</i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Trias_Greenfinch" title="Trias Greenfinch">Trias Greenfinch</a>, <i>Carduelis triasi</i>, are known from the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>.</p>
