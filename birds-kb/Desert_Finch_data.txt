<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rhodospiza_obsoleta-Ku.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Rhodospiza_obsoleta-Ku.jpg/250px-Rhodospiza_obsoleta-Ku.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Rhodospiza_obsoleta-Ku.jpg/375px-Rhodospiza_obsoleta-Ku.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Rhodospiza_obsoleta-Ku.jpg/500px-Rhodospiza_obsoleta-Ku.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1344" data-file-height="898" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rhodospiza_obsoleta-Ku.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rhodospiza obsoleta</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>desert finch</b> (<i>Rhodospiza obsoleta</i>), sometimes called <b>Lichtenstein's desert finch</b>, is a large brown <a href="/wiki/Fringillidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Fringillidae">true finch</a> found in southern <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>. Its <a href="/wiki/Linnaean_taxonomy" title="Linnaean taxonomy">taxonomy</a> is confused, and it has formerly been placed in <i><a href="/wiki/Fringilla" title="Fringilla">Fringilla</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bucanetes" title="Bucanetes">Bucanetes</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Carduelis" title="Carduelis">Carduelis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Rhodopechys" title="Rhodopechys">Rhodopechys</a></i>.
</p><p>It has an average wingspan of 26 centimetres (10&#160;in). It has a stout black bill, black and white <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">remiges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">rectrices</a>, and a slash of rosy-pink on each wing. The female is more dull in color than the male, but other than that the adult sexes are similar in color pattern.
</p><p>The bird is indeed a <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> resident in areas where water is readily available, but it can also be found in low mountains and foothills, and in <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">cultivated</a> valleys. It feeds on seeds and the occasional insect. Nesting occurs in trees in the spring, often in fruit trees in orchards, and the female lays and incubates 4 to 6 pale green, lightly speckled eggs.
</p><p>This species does not migrate except locally. The desert finch congregates near rural and remote human settlements, and the well-watered orchard in otherwise arid land is an ideal habitat. It can be found in feeding in large flocks of its own species or mixed finch flocks.
</p><p>Recent research by Zamora <i>et al.</i> (2006) has revealed that the desert finch is more closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Greenfinch" title="Greenfinch">greenfinches</a> of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Carduelis" title="Carduelis">Carduelis</a></i> (or <i><a href="/wiki/Chloris_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chloris (bird)">Chloris</a></i>, if <i>Carduelis</i> is split up) as indicated by <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> sequence analysis, vocalizations, and the presence of a black eye-stripe. Genetically, it seems very close to the common ancestor of the greenfinches. It may be that the latter evolved from a desert form and later developed the green plumage, or that the common ancestor of the greenfinches and the desert finch (which lived around 6 million years ago) was a species of <a href="/wiki/Semiarid" class="mw-redirect" title="Semiarid">semiarid</a> habitat which subsequently diverged into a truly desert-adapted lineage, today represented by the desert finch, and the ancestor of a woodlands lineage, the greenfinches.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_greenfinches">Origin of greenfinches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desert_finch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin of greenfinches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rhodospiza_obsoleta_MHNT_224_Ferouzi_Homs_Syrie.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Rhodospiza_obsoleta_MHNT_224_Ferouzi_Homs_Syrie.jpg/220px-Rhodospiza_obsoleta_MHNT_224_Ferouzi_Homs_Syrie.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="224" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Rhodospiza_obsoleta_MHNT_224_Ferouzi_Homs_Syrie.jpg/330px-Rhodospiza_obsoleta_MHNT_224_Ferouzi_Homs_Syrie.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Rhodospiza_obsoleta_MHNT_224_Ferouzi_Homs_Syrie.jpg/440px-Rhodospiza_obsoleta_MHNT_224_Ferouzi_Homs_Syrie.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4539" data-file-height="4614" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rhodospiza_obsoleta_MHNT_224_Ferouzi_Homs_Syrie.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs of <i>Rhodospiza obsoleta</i> <a href="/wiki/MHNT" class="mw-redirect" title="MHNT">MHNT</a></div></div></div>
<p>The common ancestor of all <a href="/wiki/Greenfinch" title="Greenfinch">greenfinches</a>, which are among the oldest <i>Carduelis</i> species, appeared on Earth around 9 <a href="/wiki/Mya_(unit)#Symbols_y_and_yr" class="mw-redirect" title="Mya (unit)">MYA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
One of these common ancestors is the desert finch, which now thrives in Asian deserts, but could have also inhabited African deserts.<br />
This finch, with very pale colors and a breeding season mask similar to that of the common greenfinch, used to be classified, according to phenotypical (external) characters, close to the trumpeter finch, but it belongs to genus <i>Carduelis</i> according to several molecular studies.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Two subfossil greenfinches, the <a href="/wiki/Slender-billed_greenfinch" title="Slender-billed greenfinch">slender-billed greenfinch</a>, <i>Carduelis aurelioi</i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Trias_greenfinch" title="Trias greenfinch">Trias greenfinch</a>, <i>Carduelis triasi</i>, are known from the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>.
</p>
