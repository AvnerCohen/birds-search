<p>The <b>common myna</b> or <b>Indian myna</b> (<i>Acridotheres tristis</i>), sometimes spelled <b>mynah</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> is a member of the family <a href="/wiki/Sturnidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Sturnidae">Sturnidae</a> (<a href="/wiki/Starling" title="Starling">starlings</a> and mynas) native to Asia. An omnivorous open woodland bird with a strong territorial instinct, the common myna has adapted extremely well to <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban</a> environments.
</p><p>The range of the common myna is increasing at such a rapid rate that in 2000 the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Species_Survival_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN Species Survival Commission">IUCN Species Survival Commission</a> declared it one of the world's most <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> and one of only three birds listed among "100 of the World's Worst Invasive Species" that pose a threat to biodiversity, agriculture and human interests.<sup id="cite_ref-Lowe_et_al_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lowe_et_al-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In particular, the species poses a serious threat to the <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, where it was named "The Most Important Pest/Problem".<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_myna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1760 the French zoologist <a href="/wiki/Mathurin_Jacques_Brisson" title="Mathurin Jacques Brisson">Mathurin Jacques Brisson</a> included a description of the common myna in his <i>Ornithologie</i> based on a specimen that he mistakenly believed had been collected in the Philippines. He used the French name <i>Le merle des Philippines</i> and the Latin <i>Merula Philippensis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-brisson_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brisson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Although Brisson coined Latin names, these do not conform to the <a href="/wiki/Binominal_nomenclature" class="mw-redirect" title="Binominal nomenclature">binominal system</a> and are not recognised by the <a href="/wiki/International_Commission_on_Zoological_Nomenclature" title="International Commission on Zoological Nomenclature">International Commission on Zoological Nomenclature</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-allen_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allen-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> When in 1766 the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> updated his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/12th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="12th edition of Systema Naturae">12th edition</a>, he added 240 species that had been previously described by Brisson.<sup id="cite_ref-allen_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allen-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> One of these was the common myna. Linnaeus included a brief description, coined the <a href="/wiki/Binominal_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binominal name">binominal name</a> <i>Paradisea tristis</i> and cited Brisson's work.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Type_location_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Type location (biology)">type location</a> was subsequently corrected to <a href="/wiki/Pondicherry" title="Pondicherry">Pondicherry</a> in southern India.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific name</a> <i>tristis</i> is Latin for "sad" or "gloomy".<sup id="cite_ref-hbwkey_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwkey-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> This species is now placed in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Acridotheres" title="Acridotheres">Acridotheres</a></i> that was introduced by the French ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Louis_Jean_Pierre_Vieillot" title="Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot">Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot</a> in 1816.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are recognised:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>the <b>Indian myna</b> (<i>A. t. tristis</i>) (Linnaeus, 1766) – southern Kazakhstan, Turkmenistan and eastern Iran to southern China, Indochina, the Malay Peninsula and southern India</li>
<li>the <b>Sri Lankan myna</b> (<i>A. t. melanosternus</i>) <a href="/wiki/William_Vincent_Legge" title="William Vincent Legge">Legge</a>, 1879 – Sri Lanka</li></ul>
<p>The Sri Lankan subspecies <i>melanosternus</i> is darker than the Indian subspecies <i>tristis</i> and has half-black and half-white primary coverts and a larger yellow cheek-patch.<sup id="cite_ref-HBIP5_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBIP5-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RasmussenBSA2_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RasmussenBSA2-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_myna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common myna is readily identified by the brown body, black hooded head and the bare yellow patch behind the eye. The bill and legs are bright yellow. There is a white patch on the outer primaries and the wing lining on the underside is white. The sexes are similar and birds are usually seen in pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-rasmussen_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rasmussen-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common myna obeys <a href="/wiki/Gloger%27s_rule" title="Gloger&#39;s rule">Gloger's rule</a> in that the birds from northwestern India tend to be paler than their darker counterparts in southern India.<sup id="cite_ref-HBIP5_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBIP5-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RasmussenBSA2_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RasmussenBSA2-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocalization">Vocalization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_myna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vocalization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">calls</a> includes croaks, squawks, chirps, clicks, whistles and 'growls', and the bird often fluffs its feathers and bobs its head in singing. The common myna screeches warnings to its mate or other birds in cases of predators in proximity or when it is about to take off flying.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Common mynas are popular as cage birds for their singing and "speaking" abilities. Before sleeping in communal roosts, common mynas vocalise in unison, which is known as "communal noise".<sup id="cite_ref-Mahabal&amp;Vaidya_1989_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mahabal&amp;Vaidya_1989-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_myna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is a species of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> native to <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> with its initial home range spanning from <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>; as well as <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkmenistan" title="Turkmenistan">Turkmenistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, peninsular <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indochina" class="mw-redirect" title="Indochina">Indochina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> (both <a href="/wiki/Mainland_Japan" title="Mainland Japan">mainland Japan</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ryukyu_Islands" title="Ryukyu Islands">Ryukyu Islands</a>) and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBIP5_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBIP5-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-oiseaux_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oiseaux-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common myna has been introduced in many other parts of the world such as <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kyrgyzstan" title="Kyrgyzstan">Kyrgyzstan</a><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a>, the Cayman Islands, islands in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">Seychelles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>, <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">Réunion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Andaman_and_Nicobar_Islands" title="Andaman and Nicobar Islands">Andaman and Nicobar Islands</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lakshadweep" title="Lakshadweep">Lakshadweep</a> archipelago) and also in islands of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Ascension_Island" title="Ascension Island">Ascension</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Helena" title="Saint Helena">Saint Helena</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Oceans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBIP5_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBIP5-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The range of the common myna is increasing to the extent that in 2000 the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Species_Survival_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN Species Survival Commission">IUCN Species Survival Commission</a> declared it among the World's 100 worst <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lowe_et_al_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lowe_et_al-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CommonMynah1-aw6789.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/CommonMynah1-aw6789.JPG/220px-CommonMynah1-aw6789.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/CommonMynah1-aw6789.JPG/330px-CommonMynah1-aw6789.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/CommonMynah1-aw6789.JPG/440px-CommonMynah1-aw6789.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CommonMynah1-aw6789.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A common myna visits a home garden in <a href="/wiki/Colombo" title="Colombo">Colombo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a></div></div></div>
<p>This abundant <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> is typically found in open woodland, cultivation and around habitation. Although this is an adaptable species, its population is abnormal and very much considered a pest in <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> (where it is locally called as <i>gembala kerbau</i>, literally 'buffalo shepherd') due to competition with its cousin, the introduced <a href="/wiki/Javan_myna" title="Javan myna">Javan myna</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common myna thrives in urban and suburban environments; in <a href="/wiki/Canberra" title="Canberra">Canberra</a>, for instance, 110 common mynas were released between 1968 and 1971. By 1991, common myna population density in Canberra averaged 15 birds per square kilometer.<sup id="cite_ref-pell_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pell-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Only three years later, a second study found an average population density of 75 birds per square kilometer in the same area.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The bird likely owes its success in the urban and suburban settings of Sydney and Canberra to its <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolutionary</a> origins; having evolved in the open <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodlands</a> of India, the common myna is pre-adapted to habitats with tall vertical structures and little to no vegetative ground cover,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> features characteristic of city streets and urban nature preserves.
</p><p>The common myna (along with <a href="/wiki/Common_starling" title="Common starling">common starlings</a>, <a href="/wiki/House_sparrow" title="House sparrow">house sparrows</a>, and feral <a href="/wiki/Rock_dove" title="Rock dove">rock doves</a>) is a nuisance to city buildings; its nests block gutters and drainpipes, causing water damage to building exteriors.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_myna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:194267_10150203084606632_6641876_o.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/194267_10150203084606632_6641876_o.jpg/220px-194267_10150203084606632_6641876_o.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="131" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/194267_10150203084606632_6641876_o.jpg/330px-194267_10150203084606632_6641876_o.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/194267_10150203084606632_6641876_o.jpg/440px-194267_10150203084606632_6641876_o.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="476" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:194267_10150203084606632_6641876_o.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A common myna evicting a nest of <a href="/wiki/Jungle_babbler" title="Jungle babbler">jungle babblers</a> by breaking their eggs</div></div></div>
<p>Common mynas are believed to pair for life. They breed through much of the year depending on the location, building their nest in a hole in a tree or wall. They breed from sea-level to 3,000 m in the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBIP5_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBIP5-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The normal clutch size is 4–6 eggs. The average size of the egg is 30.8 x 21.99&#160;mm. The incubation period is 17 to 18 days and fledging period is 22 to 24 days.<sup id="cite_ref-HBIP5_12-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBIP5-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Asian_koel" title="Asian koel">Asian koel</a> is sometimes <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasitism" class="mw-redirect" title="Brood parasitism">brood parasitic</a> on this species.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Nesting material used by common mynas includes twigs, roots, tow and rubbish. Common mynas have been known to use tissue paper, tin foil and sloughed off snake-skin.<sup id="cite_ref-HBIP5_12-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBIP5-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the breeding season, the daytime activity-time budget of the common myna in Pune in April to June 1978 has been recorded to comprise the following: nesting activity (42%), scanning the environment (28%), locomotion (12%), feeding (4%), vocalisation (7%) and preening-related activities, interactions and other activities (7%).<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common myna uses the nests of woodpeckers, parakeets, etc. and easily takes to nest boxes; it has been recorded evicting the chicks of previously nesting pairs by holding them in the beak and later sometimes not even using the emptied nest boxes. This aggressive behaviour contributes to its success as an invasive species.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is also some evidence that shows that in introduced environments, the species chooses to nest in more modified and artificial structures than in natural tree cavities when compared to native species.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_feeding">Food and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_myna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Food and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like most starlings, the common myna is omnivorous. It feeds on <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arachnid" title="Arachnid">arachnids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, small mammals, seeds, grain and fruits and discarded waste from human habitation. It forages on the ground among grass for insects, and especially for <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a>, from which it gets the generic name <i>Acridotheres</i>, "grasshopper hunter". It, however, feeds on a wide range of insects, mostly picked from the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-HBIP5_12-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBIP5-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> It is a cross-pollinator of flowers such as <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salmalia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Salmalia (page does not exist)">Salmalia</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Erythrina" title="Erythrina">Erythrina</a></i>. It walks on the ground with occasional hops and is an opportunistic feeder on the insects disturbed by grazing cattle as well as fired grass fields.<sup id="cite_ref-HBIP5_12-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBIP5-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roosting_behaviour">Roosting behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_myna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Roosting behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Common mynas roost communally throughout the year, either in pure or mixed flocks with <a href="/wiki/Jungle_myna" title="Jungle myna">jungle mynas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rosy_starling" title="Rosy starling">rosy starlings</a>, <a href="/wiki/House_crow" title="House crow">house crows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jungle_crow" title="Jungle crow">jungle crows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cattle_egret" title="Cattle egret">cattle egrets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rose-ringed_parakeet" title="Rose-ringed parakeet">rose-ringed parakeets</a> and other birds.  The roost population can range from less than one hundred to thousands.<sup id="cite_ref-Mahabal&amp;Bastawade1991_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mahabal&amp;Bastawade1991-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The time of arrival of mynas at the roost starts before and ends just after sunset. The mynas depart before sunrise. The time and timespan of arrival and departure, time taken for final settlement at the roost, duration of communal sleep, flock size and population vary seasonally.<sup id="cite_ref-Mahabal&amp;Vaidya_1989_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mahabal&amp;Vaidya_1989-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The function of <a href="/wiki/Communal_roosting" title="Communal roosting">communal roosting</a> is to synchronise various social activities, avoid predators, exchange information about food sources.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Communal displays (pre-roosting and post-roosting) consist of aerial maneuvers which are exhibited in the pre-breeding season (November to March). It is assumed that this behaviour is related to pair formation.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Invasive_species">Invasive species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_myna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Invasive species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The IUCN declared the common myna as one of only three birds among the <a href="/wiki/List_of_globally_invasive_species" class="mw-redirect" title="List of globally invasive species">world's 100 worst invasive species</a><sup id="cite_ref-Lowe_et_al_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lowe_et_al-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> (the other two being the <a href="/wiki/Red-vented_bulbul" title="Red-vented bulbul">red-vented bulbul</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Common_starling" title="Common starling">common starling</a>).
The French introduced it in the 18th century from Pondicherry to Mauritius with the aim of controlling insects, even levying a fine on anyone persecuting the bird.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> It has since been introduced widely elsewhere, including adjacent areas in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, Spain and Portugal,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>  the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and various oceanic islands in the Indian and Pacific Oceans, including prominent populations in <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-oiseaux_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oiseaux-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common myna is regarded as a pest in South Africa, North America, the Middle East, Australia, New Zealand and many Pacific islands.  It is particularly problematic in Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>  Several methods have been tried to control the bird's numbers and protect native species.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_myna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Acridotheres_tristis_-Sydney,_Australia-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Acridotheres_tristis_-Sydney%2C_Australia-8.jpg/220px-Acridotheres_tristis_-Sydney%2C_Australia-8.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="172" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Acridotheres_tristis_-Sydney%2C_Australia-8.jpg/330px-Acridotheres_tristis_-Sydney%2C_Australia-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Acridotheres_tristis_-Sydney%2C_Australia-8.jpg/440px-Acridotheres_tristis_-Sydney%2C_Australia-8.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="999" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Acridotheres_tristis_-Sydney,_Australia-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mynahs_Nesting_019aa.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Mynahs_Nesting_019aa.jpg/220px-Mynahs_Nesting_019aa.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="162" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Mynahs_Nesting_019aa.jpg/330px-Mynahs_Nesting_019aa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Mynahs_Nesting_019aa.jpg/440px-Mynahs_Nesting_019aa.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1556" data-file-height="1143" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mynahs_Nesting_019aa.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A common myna nesting in a shop awning in Sydney</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, the common myna is an <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive</a> <a href="/wiki/Pest_(animal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pest (animal)">pest</a>.  They are now often the predominant bird in urban areas all along the East Coast.  In a 2008 popular vote, the bird was named "The Most Important Pest/Problem" in Australia, also earning the nickname "flying rats" due to their scavenging resembling that of rats. It is also known as "the cane toad of the sky".<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common myna was first introduced to Australia in <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a> between 1863 and 1872 into Melbourne's market gardens to control insects. The bird is likely to have spread to <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> (where it is currently most populous) at around the same time, but documentation is uncertain.<sup id="cite_ref-H227_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H227-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The bird was later introduced to Queensland as a predator of <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cane_beetle" class="mw-redirect" title="Cane beetle">cane beetles</a>. Currently, common myna populations in Australia are concentrated along the eastern coast around <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a> and its surrounding suburbs, with sparser populations in Victoria and a few isolated communities in <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> During 2009 several municipal councils in New South Wales began trials of catching myna birds in an effort to reduce numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The bird can live and breed in a wide range of temperatures, ranging from the harsh winters of Canberra to the tropical climate of Cairns. Self-sustaining populations of common myna have been found in regions of mean warmest month temperature no less than 23.2&#160;°C and mean coldest month temperature no less than -0.4&#160;°C, implying that the common myna could potentially spread from Sydney northward along the eastern coast to <a href="/wiki/Cairns" title="Cairns">Cairns</a> and westward along the southern coast to <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a> (though not to <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Darwin,_Northern_Territory" title="Darwin, Northern Territory">Darwin</a>, or the arid interior regions).<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_myna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2019, common mynas were added to the <a href="/wiki/List_of_invasive_alien_species_of_Union_concern" title="List of invasive alien species of Union concern">List of Invasive Alien Species of Union concern</a><a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="https://ec.europa.eu/environment/nature/invasivealien/list/index_en.htm">[1]</a>. They have established in Spain and Portugal<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> and were introduced to France, where they occasionally bred.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_myna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The common myna was introduced to both the North Island and South Island of New Zealand in the 1870s. However, the cooler summer temperatures in the South Island appear to have impeded the breeding success rate of the southern populations, preventing the proliferation of the species, which was largely non-existent there by the 1890s. In contrast, the North Island population was able to breed more successfully and large portions of the North Island are now populated. However, in the southern reaches of the North Island, the cooler summer temperatures, like those of the South Island, have prevented the establishment of large myna populations.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_myna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In South Africa where it escaped into the wild in 1902, it has become very common and its distribution is greater where human populations are greater or where there is more human disturbance.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The bird is also notorious for being a pest, kicking other birds out of their nests and killing their young due to the myna's strong territorial instinct. In South Africa it is considered somewhat of a major pest and disturbance of the natural habitat; as a result, they are frequently shot and killed or poisoned by people in urban environments and farmers alike. Bylaws in South Africa pertaining to the protection of most animal species specifically exclude mynas from this protection.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Morphological studies show that the process of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spatial_sorting&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Spatial sorting (page does not exist)">spatial sorting</a> is at work on the range expansion of <i>A. tristis</i> in South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Dispersal-relevant traits are significantly correlated with distance from the range core, with strong sexual dimorphism, indicative of sex-biased dispersal. Morphological variations are significant in wing and head traits of females, suggesting females as the primary dispersing sex. In contrast, traits not related to dispersal such as those associated with foraging show no signs of spatial sorting but are significantly affected by environmental variables such as vegetation and intensity of urbanisation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_United_States">The United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_myna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: The United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Hawaii, it is out-competing many native birds for food and nesting areas.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://hbs.bishopmuseum.org/birds/rlp-monograph/pdfs/07-ZOST-ESTR/COMY.pdf">[2]</a>
</p><p>To study the invasion genetics and landscape-scale dynamics of <i>A. tristis</i>, scientists have recently developed 16 polymorphic nuclear microsatellite markers <sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> using the <a href="/wiki/Next_generation_sequencing" class="mw-redirect" title="Next generation sequencing">next generation sequencing</a> (NGS) approach.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_ecosystems_and_humans">Effect on ecosystems and humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_myna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Effect on ecosystems and humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Threat_to_native_birds">Threat to native birds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_myna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Threat to native birds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The common myna is a hollow-nesting species; that is, it nests and breeds in protected hollows found either naturally in trees or artificially on buildings (for example, recessed windowsills or low eaves).<sup id="cite_ref-B34_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B34-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Compared to native hollow-nesting species, the common myna is extremely aggressive, and breeding males will actively defend areas ranging up to 0.83 <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectares</a> in size (though males in densely populated urban settings tend to only defend the area immediately surrounding their nests).<sup id="cite_ref-P148_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P148-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This aggressiveness has enabled the common myna to displace many breeding pairs of native hollow-nesters, thereby reducing their reproductive success. In Australia, their aggressiveness has enabled them to chase native birds as large as <a href="/wiki/Galah" title="Galah">galahs</a> out of their nests.
</p><p>The common myna is also known to maintain up to two <a href="/wiki/Bird#Resting_and_roosting" title="Bird">roosts</a> simultaneously; a temporary summer roost close to a breeding site (where the entire local male community sleeps during the summer, the period of highest aggression), and a permanent all-year roost where the female broods and incubates overnight. Both male and female common mynas will fiercely protect both roosts at all times, leading to further exclusion of native birds.<sup id="cite_ref-P148_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P148-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Threat_to_crops_and_pasture">Threat to crops and pasture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_myna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Threat to crops and pasture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The common myna (which feeds mostly on ground-dwelling <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, tropical fruits such as grapes, plums and some <a href="/wiki/Berry" title="Berry">berries</a> and, in urban areas, discarded human food)<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> poses a serious threat to Australian <a href="/wiki/Blueberry" title="Blueberry">blueberry</a> crops, though its main threat is to native bird species.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Hawaii, where the common myna was introduced to control pest <a href="/wiki/Mythimna_separata" title="Mythimna separata">armyworms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cutworm" title="Cutworm">cutworms</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a> crops, the bird has helped to spread the robust <i><a href="/wiki/Lantana_camara" title="Lantana camara">Lantana camara</a></i> weed across the islands’ open <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grasslands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> It also has been recorded as the fourth-ranking avian pest in the fruit industry by a 2004 survey of the Hawaiian Farm Bureau and the sixth in number of complaints of avian pests overall.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Common mynas can cause considerable damage to ripening fruit, particularly grapes, but also figs, apples, pears, strawberries, blueberries, guava, mangoes and breadfruit. Cereal crops such as maize, wheat and rice are susceptible where they occur near urban areas. Roosting and nesting commensal with humans create aesthetic and health concerns. Common mynas are known to carry avian malaria and exotic parasites such as the <i>Ornithonyssus bursia</i> mite, which can cause dermatitis in humans. The common myna can help spread agricultural weeds: for example, it spreads the seeds of <i>Lantana camara</i>, which has been classed as a Weed of National Significance because of its invasiveness. Common mynas are regularly observed to usurp nests and hollows, destroy the eggs and kill the young of native bird species, including seabirds and parrots. There is evidence that common mynas have killed small land
mammals such as mice, squirrels and possums, but further research on these occurrences is under consideration.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_myna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> literature, the common myna has a number of names, most are descriptive of the appearance or behaviour of the bird. In addition to <i>saarika</i>, the names for the common myna include <i>kalahapriya</i>, which means "one who is fond of arguments" referring to the quarrelsome nature of this bird; <i>chitranetra</i>, meaning "picturesque eyes"; <i>peetanetra</i> (one with yellow eyes) and <i>peetapaad</i> (one with yellow legs).<sup id="cite_ref-Birds_in_Sanskrit_Lit_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birds_in_Sanskrit_Lit-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
