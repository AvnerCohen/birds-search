<p>The <b>Egyptian Goose</b> (<i>Alopochen aegyptiacus</i>) is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">duck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goose" title="Goose">goose</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Swan" title="Swan">swan</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Anatidae" title="Anatidae">Anatidae</a>. It is native to Africa south of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nile_Valley" title="Nile Valley" class="mw-redirect">Nile Valley</a>, and has been introduced to parts of <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a>.</p>
<p>Egyptian Geese were considered sacred by the ancient Egyptians, and appeared in much of their artwork.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Egyptian_Goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>It swims well, and in flight looks heavy, more like a goose than a duck, hence the English name.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from November 2007">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It is 63–73&#160;cm long.</p>
<p>The sexes of this striking species are identical in plumage, though the males average slightly larger. There is a fair amount of variation in plumage tone, with some birds greyer and others browner, but this is not sex or age related. A large part of the wings of mature birds is white, but in repose the white is hidden by the wing coverts. When it is aroused, either in alarm or aggression, the white begins to show. In flight or when the wings are fully spread in aggression the white is conspicuous. The voices and vocalisations of the sexes differ, the male having a hoarse, subdued duck-like quack which seldom sounds unless it is aroused. The male Egyptian goose attracts its mate with an elaborate, noisy courtship display that includes honking, neck stretching and feather displays.<sup id="cite_ref-LPZEgyptianGoose_0-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LPZEgyptianGoose-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The female has a far noisier raucous quack that frequently sounds in aggression and almost incessantly at the slightest disturbance when tending her young. Both sexes are aggressively territorial towards their own species when breeding and frequently pursue intruders into the air, attacking them in aerial "dogfights".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Egyptian_Goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>It is in the <a href="/wiki/Shelduck" title="Shelduck">shelduck</a> <a href="/wiki/Subfamily" title="Subfamily" class="mw-redirect">subfamily</a> <a href="/wiki/Tadorninae" title="Tadorninae">Tadorninae</a>, and is the only extant member of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Alopochen" title="Alopochen">Alopochen</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA" class="mw-redirect">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome <i>b</i></a> <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">sequence</a> data suggest that the relationships of <i>Alopochen</i> to <i><a href="/wiki/Tadorna" title="Tadorna" class="mw-redirect">Tadorna</a></i> need further investigation.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Its generic name looks like Greek ἀλώπηξ + χήν = "fox-goose", referring to the colour of its back, but with a Greek language error: the linguistically correct form would have been *<i>Alopecchen</i> or *<i>Alopecochen</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Egyptian_Goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<p>This species breeds widely in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> except in deserts and dense forests, and is locally abundant. They are found mostly in the <a href="/wiki/Nile_Valley" title="Nile Valley" class="mw-redirect">Nile Valley</a> and south of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>. It has also been introduced elsewhere: <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> have self-sustaining <a href="/wiki/Feral" title="Feral">feral</a> populations. The British population dates back to the 18th century, though only formally added to the British list in 1971. In Britain, it is found mainly in <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a>, in parkland with lakes. It was officially declared a pest in the U.K. in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Egyptian Goose is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Egyptian_Goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<p>This is a largely terrestrial species, which will also perch readily on trees and buildings. Egyptian geese typically eat seeds, leaves, grasses, and plant stems. Occasionally, they will eat locusts, worms, or other small animals.</p>
<p>They are extremely territorial and often fight with other pairs for a breeding ground. Neighbouring pairs may even kill another's offspring for their own offsprings' survival as well as for more resources. <sup id="cite_ref-HonoluluZoo_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HonoluluZoo-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species will nest in a large variety of situations, especially in holes in mature trees in parkland. The female builds the nest from reeds, leaves and grass, and both parents take turns incubating eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-LPZEgyptianGoose_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LPZEgyptianGoose-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Egyptian Geese usually pair for life. Both the male and female care for the offspring until they are old enough to care for themselves. <sup id="cite_ref-HonoluluZoo_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HonoluluZoo-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
