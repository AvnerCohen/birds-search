<p>The <b>Great Bustard</b> (<i>Otis tarda</i>) is in the <a href="/wiki/Bustard" title="Bustard">bustard</a> family, the only member of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i>Otis</i>. It breeds in southern and central <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, where it is the largest species of bird, and across temperate <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. European populations are mainly resident, but Asian birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">move</a> further south in winter. Spain now contains about 60% of the world's population.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Bustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and etymology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_etymology">Taxonomy and etymology</span></h2>
<p>The Great Bustard was classified with its scientific name in 1758 by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a>, though the species was referred to as <i>avis tarda</i> in the much older writings of <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>. <i>Otis</i> is an <a href="/wiki/Old_Greek" title="Old Greek">Old Greek</a> name for "bustard". The specific name, <i>tarda</i>, has been traced to an <a href="/wiki/Old_Spanish_language" title="Old Spanish language">Old Spanish</a> name for "tread", although it has also been used in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "slow" and "deliberate", which is apt to describe the typical walking style of the species.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The Great Bustard <i>túzok</i> in <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a> and it is the <a href="/wiki/National_bird" title="National bird" class="mw-redirect">national bird</a> of Hungary and a subject of several <a href="/wiki/Proverb" title="Proverb">proverbs</a> of that region.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Bustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The male of this huge bird is possibly the <a href="/wiki/Flying_and_gliding_animals#Flying.2Fsoaring" title="Flying and gliding animals">heaviest living flying animal</a>. It also arguably the most <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphic</a> extant bird species, in terms of the size difference between males and females. Among both bustards and all living birds, the mass of this species is rivaled by that of the <a href="/wiki/Kori_Bustard" title="Kori Bustard">Kori Bustard</a>, which, thanks to its relatively longer tarsi and tail, is both longer and taller on average and is less sexually dimorphic.<sup id="cite_ref-Alonso_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alonso-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> A male is typically 90–105 cm (2&#160;ft 10&#160;in–3&#160;ft 5&#160;in) tall, with a length of around 115&#160;cm (3&#160;ft&#160;9&#160;in) and has a 2.1–2.7 m (6&#160;ft 10&#160;in–8&#160;ft 10&#160;in) wingspan. The male can range in weight from 5.8 to 18 kg (13 to 40 lb), with an average of 9.65 to 13.5 kg (21.3 to 30 lb).<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Alonso_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alonso-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gbgroup_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gbgroup-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wood_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Letters_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Letters-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The heaviest verified specimen was about 21&#160;kg (46&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-Wood_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Tetrapod_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tetrapod-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> In a recent study in Spain, another giant male tipped the scales at 19&#160;kg (42&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-Alonso_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alonso-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Larger specimens have been reported but remain unverified.<sup id="cite_ref-Wood_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Tetrapod_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tetrapod-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The female is about a third smaller in linear dimensions, typically measuring 75 to 85 cm (2&#160;ft 6&#160;in to 2&#160;ft 9&#160;in) in height, about 90&#160;cm (2&#160;ft&#160;11&#160;in)<sup id="cite_ref-Letters_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Letters-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> in length and 180&#160;cm (5&#160;ft&#160;11&#160;in) across the wings.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gbgroup_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gbgroup-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> At an average of 4.4&#160;kg (9.7&#160;lb), the females are roughly one-third the weight of the male. Overall, the female's weight can range from 3.3 to 8 kg (7.3 to 18 lb).<sup id="cite_ref-gbgroup_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gbgroup-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CRC_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Perhaps because of this physical sexual dimorphism, there is a skewed sex ratio of about 1.5:1 female to male.</p>
<p>An adult male is brown above, barred with blackish coloration, and white below, with a long grey neck and head. His breast and lower neck sides are chestnut and there is a golden wash to the back and the extent of these bright colors tending to increase as the male ages. In the breeding season, the male has long white neck bristles, which measure up to 12–15 cm (4.7–5.9 in) in length, continually growing from the third to the sixth year of life.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> In flight, the long wings are predominantly white with brown showing along the edges of the lower primary and secondary feathers and a dark brown streak along the upper-edge of the wing. The breast and neck of the female are buff, with brown and pale coloration over the rest of the plumage rendering it well camouflaged in open habitats. Immature birds resemble the female. The Eastern subspecies (<i>O. t. dybowskii</i>) is more extensively gray in color in both sexes, with more extensive barring on the back.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The Great Bustard has long legs, a long neck and a heavy, barrel-chested body. It is fairly typical of the family in its overall shape and habitat preferences. Three other bustard species overlap in range with this species: the <a href="/wiki/Macqueen%27s_Bustard" title="Macqueen's Bustard" class="mw-redirect">Macqueen's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Houbara_Bustard" title="Houbara Bustard">Houbara</a> and <a href="/wiki/Little_Bustard" title="Little Bustard">Little Bustards</a>. However, none of these attain the huge sizes, bulky form and plumage coloration of this species. Thus, the Great Bustard is essentially unmistakable.</p>
<p>Among standard measurements in the Eastern race, males have a <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> length of 59.5 to 63.5 cm (23.4 to 25.0 in), a <a href="/wiki/Tail" title="Tail">tail</a> of 21 to 23.5 cm (8.3 to 9.3 in), a <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> of 14.2 to 15 cm (5.6 to 5.9 in) and a <a href="/wiki/Culmen_(bird)" title="Culmen (bird)" class="mw-redirect">exposed culmen</a> of 4.1 to 4.7 cm (1.6 to 1.9 in). In comparison, the Eastern female measures 45.5 to 49.5 cm (17.9 to 19.5 in) across the wing chord, has a tail of 17.8 to 21 cm (7.0 to 8.3 in), a tarsus of 11.3 to 12.6 cm (4.4 to 5.0 in) and a culmen of 3.5 to 4 cm (1.4 to 1.6 in), respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Standard measurements indicate the nominate subspecies is slightly larger than the Eastern subspecies, in adult males, culmen length is to 9.8&#160;cm (3.9&#160;in), wing chord length to 69&#160;cm (27&#160;in), tail length to 29.5&#160;cm (11.6&#160;in) and tarsal length to 22&#160;cm (8.7&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Alonso_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alonso-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Bustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span></h2>
<p>This bird's <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> is <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a> or <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a> defined by open, flat or somewhat rolling landscapes. It can be found on undisturbed cultivation and seems to prefer areas with wild or cultivated crops such as cereals, vineyards and fodder plants. However, during the breeding season, they actively avoid areas with regular human activity and can be distributed by agricultural practices. Great Bustards are often attracted to areas with considerable insect activity.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Bustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span></h2>
<p>This species is gregarious, especially in winter when gatherings of several dozen birds may occur. Male and female groups do not mix outside of the breeding season. The Great Bustard has a stately slow walk but tends to run when disturbed rather than fly. Running speeds have not been measured but adult females have been known to outrun <a href="/wiki/Red_fox" title="Red fox">red foxes</a>, which can reach a trotting speed of 48&#160;km/h (30&#160;mph).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> However, they can be fairly strong fliers as well, especially during seasonal movements, and can reach speeds of up to 80&#160;km/h (50&#160;mph) in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Both sexes are usually silent but can engage in deep grunts when alarmed or angered. The displaying adult male may produce some booming, grunting and raucous noises. The female may utter some guttural calls at the nest and brooded young make a soft, trilling call in communication with their mothers. The Asian and Russian populations of the species are migratory and will gather in large numbers at pre-migratory sites in order to move collectively to winter grounds. In the remainder of the range, such as Central Asia, only partial migrations may occur based on seasonal food availability. In the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>, bustards that engage in migration seem to choose different periods for movements based on sex.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> No population is known to use the same grounds for wintering and summering.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Bustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span></h3>
<p>The Great Bustard breeds in March, and a single male may mate with up to five females. Before mating, the males moult into their breeding plumage around January. Males establish dominance in their groups during winter, clashing violently by ramming into and hitting each other with their bills.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Like other bustards, the male Great Bustard displays and competes for the attention of females on what is known as a <a href="/wiki/Lek_(biology)" title="Lek (biology)" class="mw-redirect">lek</a>. In this species, the male has a flamboyant display beginning with the strutting male puffing up his throat to the size of a football. He then tilts forwards and pulls his head in so that the long whiskery chin feathers point upwards and the head is no longer visible. He next cocks his tail flat along his back, exposing the normally hidden bright white plumage then he lowers his wings, with the primary flight feathers folded but with the white secondaries fanning out.<sup id="cite_ref-NHM_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHM-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> The displaying males, whom may walk around for several minutes at a time with feathers flared and head buried, has been described as a "foam-bath" due to its appearance.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> All breeding Great Bustards also moult again from June to September.</p>

<p>One to three olive or tan coloured, glossy eggs (two eggs being the average) are laid by the female in May or June. The nests, which are shallow scrapes by the female on dry, soft slopes and plains, are usually situated close to the prior lek location. Nests are situated in sparse clusters, with a study in <a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Inner Mongolia</a> finding nests at a minimal 9&#160;m (30&#160;ft) apart from each other. In the same study, nests were placed at mid-elevation on a hill, at about 190 to 230 m (620 to 750 ft). Nesting sites are typically in dense grassy vegetation about 15 to 35 cm (5.9 to 14 in), likely for protection against predation, with extensive exposure to sunlight.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Eggs weigh about 150 g (5.3&#160;oz) and are on average 79.4&#160;mm (3.1&#160;in) tall by 56.8&#160;mm (2.2&#160;in) wide. The female incubates the eggs alone for 21 to 28 days. The chicks almost immediately leave the nest after they hatch, although they do not move very far from their mother until they are at least 1 year old. Young Great Bustards begin developing their adult plumage at about 2 months, and begin to develop flying skills at the same time. They practice by stretching, running, flapping, and making small hops and jumps to get airborne. By three months they are able to fly reasonable distances. If threatened, the young stand still, using their downy plumage, mainly sepia in color with paler buffy streaks, as camouflage. Juveniles are independent by their first winter, but normally stay with their mother until the next breeding season. Males usually start to mate from 5 to 6 years of age, although may engage in breeding display behavior at a younger age. Females usually first breed at 2 to 3 years old.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NHM_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHM-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Bustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span></h3>
<p>This species is <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivorous</a> taking different foods in differing seasons. In northwestern Spain in August, 48.4% of the diet of adult birds was comprised by green plant material, 40.9% was <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> and 10.6% was seeds. In the same population during winter, seeds and green plant material compromised almost the entirety of the diet. <a href="/wiki/Alfalfa" title="Alfalfa">Alfalfa</a> are seemingly preferred in the diet of birds from Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Other favored plant life in the diet can including <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legumes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brassicaceae" title="Brassicaceae">crucifers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taraxacum_officinale" title="Taraxacum officinale">common dandelion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grape" title="Grape">grapes</a> and the dry seeds of <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Among animal prey, <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> are generally eaten and are the main food for young bustards in their first summer, though they then switching to the seasonal herbivorous preferences of adults by winter. <a href="/wiki/Coleoptera" title="Coleoptera" class="mw-redirect">Coleoptera</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>), <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">Hymenoptera</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">wasps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ants</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Orthoptera" title="Orthoptera">Orthoptera</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">crickets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Locust" title="Locust">locusts</a>) are mainly taken, largely based on availability and abundance. Small <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a>, including small <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a> and chicks of other <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, may supplement the diet when the opportunity arises.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Bustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mortality">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Mortality">Mortality</span></h3>
<p>Great Bustards typically live for around 10 years, but some have been known to live up to 15 years or more. The maximum known life span for the species was 28 years. Adult males seem to have a higher mortality rate than females due mainly to fierce intraspecies fighting with other males during the breeding season. Many males may perish in their first couple of years of maturity due to this cause.<sup id="cite_ref-gbgroup_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gbgroup-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Over 80% of Great Bustards die in the first year of life. Chicks are endangered to <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predation</a> by the fact that they are ground-dwelling birds which are reluctant to fly. Predators of <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> and hatchlings include <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corvid" title="Corvid" class="mw-redirect">corvids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hedgehog" title="Hedgehog">hedgehogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">foxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Badger" title="Badger">badgers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marten" title="Marten">martens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">wild boars</a>. The most serious natural predators of nests are perhaps <a href="/wiki/Red_fox" title="Red fox">red foxes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hooded_crow" title="Hooded crow" class="mw-redirect">hooded crows</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Chicks grow very quickly, by 6 months being nearly two-thirds of their adult size, and are predated by <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">foxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lynx" title="Lynx">lynxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gray_Wolf" title="Gray Wolf" class="mw-redirect">wolves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jackal" title="Jackal">jackals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a>. The bold, conspicuous behavior of the breeding adult male bustard may attract the same large mammalian predators that predate chicks, such as wolves and lynx, while the more inconspicuous female may sometimes be attacked by large eagles. However, predation is rare for adults due to their size, nimbleness and safety in numbers due to their social behavior.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gbgroup_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gbgroup-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CMS_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CMS-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Occasionally, other natural causes may contribute to mortality in the species, especially starvation in harsh winter months.<sup id="cite_ref-CMS_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CMS-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> However, major causes of mortality in recent centuries have been largely linked to human activity, as described below.</p>

<p>Sizeable populations exist in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> (23,055 birds), <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> (8,000 birds), <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> (800–3,000 birds), <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> (1,435 birds) and <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> (1,000 birds). Elsewhere, the populations are declining due to habitat loss throughout its range. A sizeable population also exists in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> (1,100–1,300 birds) where the Eastern European steppe zone ends, near <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9vav%C3%A1nya" title="Dévaványa">Dévaványa</a> town and also in the <a href="/wiki/Hortob%C3%A1gy" title="Hortobágy">Hortobágy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nagykuns%C3%A1g" title="Nagykunság">Nagykunság</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagy-S%C3%A1rr%C3%A9t&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nagy-Sárrét (page does not exist)">Nagy-Sárrét</a> regions. The population is down from a population of 10,000–12,000 before the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War" class="mw-redirect">Second World War</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Bustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Threats and conservation status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Threats_and_conservation_status">Threats and conservation status</span></h2>
<p>The Great Bustard is classified as Vulnerable at the species level. There are a myriad of threats faced by Great Bustards. Increasing human disturbance and land privatisation is expected to lead to habitat loss caused by the ploughing of grasslands, intensive agriculture, <a href="/wiki/Afforestation" title="Afforestation">afforestation</a>, increased development of irrigation schemes, and the construction of roads, power lines, fencing and ditches. Mechanisation, chemical <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, fire and predation by dogs are serious threats for chicks and juveniles, and hunting of adults contributes to high mortality in some of their range countries. Agricultural activity is a major disturbance at nest and, in Hungary, few successful nests are found outside of protected areas.<sup id="cite_ref-CMS_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CMS-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Two very rare <a href="/wiki/Albinism" title="Albinism">albino</a> Great Bustards from the same nest were killed by electricity cables in Hungary in 2000 and 2003<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from May 2010">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The bustards, despite their large size, are able to fly at a high velocity and are often mutilated or killed by the cables which are placed in Hungary just at their flying heights. The electricity companies affected will bury only part of the dangerous cables, therefore the authorities are experimenting with fixing fluorescent "Firefly" devices on the most dangerous cables to provide the birds with warning lights. Bustards also occasionally killed by collisions with automobiles or by entanglement in wires.<sup id="cite_ref-CMS_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CMS-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Great Bustard was formerly native in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and a bustard forms part of the design of the <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a> <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">Coat of Arms</a>. It was hunted out of existence by the 1840s. In 2004 a project overseeing the reintroduction to <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Plain" title="Salisbury Plain">Salisbury Plain</a> in Wiltshire using eggs taken from Saratov in Russia was undertaken by The Great Bustard Group, a UK Registered Charity that aims to establish a self-sustaining population of Great Bustards in the UK. They have laid eggs and raised chicks in Britain in 2009 and 2010. Although the Great Bustard was once native to Britain, Great Bustards are considered an alien species under English law.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> The reintroduction of the Great Bustard to the UK by the Great Bustard Group is being carried out in parallel with researchers from the University of Bath that are providing insight into the habitat of native Great Bustard populations in Russia and Hungary. On January 19, 2011 it was announced that the Great Bustard Project had been awarded EU <a href="/wiki/The_LIFE_Programme" title="The LIFE Programme">LIFE+</a> funding, reportedly to the tune of £1.8 million.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> In Hungary, where the species is the National Bird, Great Bustards are actively protected. The Hungarian authorities are seeking to preserve the long-term future of the population by active protection measures: the area affected by the special ecological treatment had grown to 15&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (5.8&#160;sq&#160;mi) by the summer of 2006.</p>
<p>Under the auspices of the <a href="/wiki/Bonn_Convention" title="Bonn Convention">Convention on Migratory Species of Wild Animals (CMS)</a>, also known as the <a href="/wiki/Bonn_Convention" title="Bonn Convention">Bonn Convention</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Bustard_Memorandum_of_Understanding" title="Great Bustard Memorandum of Understanding">the Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) on the Conservation and Management of Middle-European Populations of the Great Bustard</a> was concluded and came into effect on June 1, 2001. The MoU provides a framework for governments, scientists, conservation bodies and others to monitor and coordinate conservation efforts in order to protect the middle-European populations of the Great Bustard.</p>
