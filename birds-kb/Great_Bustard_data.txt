<p>The <b>great bustard</b> (<i>Otis tarda</i>) is a bird in the <a href="/wiki/Bustard" title="Bustard">bustard</a> family, the only member of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i>Otis</i>. It breeds in open grasslands and farmland from northern Morocco, <a href="/wiki/South_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="South Europe">South</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a>, to temperate <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>. European populations are mainly resident, but Asian populations <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> farther south in winter. It has been listed as <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">Vulnerable</a> on the IUCN Red List since 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Portugal and Spain now have about 60% of the world's population.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It became extinct in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> when the last bird was shot in 1832. Recent attempts to reintroduce it into England have met with some success<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and there is a population of 40 birds on <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Plain" title="Salisbury Plain">Salisbury Plain</a>, a British Army training area. Here the lack of public access allows them the freedom needed as a large ground-nesting bird.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_etymology">Taxonomy and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_bustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The great bustard was classified with its scientific name in 1758 by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a>, though the species was referred to as <i>avis tarda</i> in the much older writings of <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>, and hence its names in English (from Old French <i>bistarda</i>) and some other languages: <i>abetarda</i> (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">pt</a>), <i>abetarda</i> (<a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">gl</a>), <i>avutarda</i> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">es</a>). <i>Otis</i> is an <a href="/wiki/Old_Greek" title="Old Greek">Old Greek</a> name for "bustard". The specific name, <i>tarda</i>, is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "slow" and "deliberate", which is apt to describe the typical walking style of the species.<sup id="cite_ref-eol_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eol-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_bustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dabao.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Dabao.jpg/220px-Dabao.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="213" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Dabao.jpg/330px-Dabao.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Dabao.jpg/440px-Dabao.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1344" data-file-height="1302" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dabao.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Captive specimen of a male great bustard, showing the characteristic long, beard-like feathers and heavy build.</div></div></div>
<p>The adult male great bustard is amongst the <a href="/wiki/Flying_and_gliding_animals#Flying/soaring" title="Flying and gliding animals">heaviest living flying animals</a>. A male is typically 90–105&#160;cm (2&#160;ft 11&#160;in–3&#160;ft 5&#160;in) tall, with a length of around 115&#160;cm (3&#160;ft 9&#160;in) and has a 2.1–2.7&#160;m (6&#160;ft 11&#160;in–8&#160;ft 10&#160;in) wingspan. The male can range in weight from 5.8 to 18&#160;kg (13 to 40&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wood_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The heaviest verified specimen, collected in <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a>, was about 21&#160;kg (46&#160;lb),<sup id="cite_ref-Wood_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Tetrapod_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tetrapod-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> a world record for heaviest flying bird.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In a study in Spain, one male weighed as much as 19&#160;kg (42&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-Alonso2010_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alonso2010-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Larger specimens have been reported but remain unverified. Average male weights as reported have been fairly variable: in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, males weighed a median of 9.2&#160;kg (20&#160;lb); in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, males weighed a mean of 9.82&#160;kg (21.6&#160;lb) during breeding season and 10.62&#160;kg (23.4&#160;lb) during non-breeding; in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, males weighed a mean of 11.97&#160;kg (26.4&#160;lb); and the <a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness World Records</a> has indicated that prior to their <a href="/wiki/Local_extinction" title="Local extinction">extirpation</a> male bustards in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> weighed an average of 13.5&#160;kg (30&#160;lb). Average weight of males is almost an exact match to that of male Kori bustards. Among all flying animals and land birds, male <a href="/wiki/Andean_condor" title="Andean condor">Andean condors</a> (<i>Vultur gryphus</i>)  may match or exceed the mean body masses of these male bustards but not their maximum weights. Furthermore, male <a href="/wiki/Swan" title="Swan">swans</a> of the two largest species (<a href="/wiki/Trumpeter_swan" title="Trumpeter swan">trumpeter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mute_swan" title="Mute swan">mute</a>) may attain a similar average mass depending on season and region.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC2_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Alonso2010_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alonso2010-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CRC_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wood_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gbgroup_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gbgroup-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Letters_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Letters-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  Among both bustards and all living birds, the upper reported mass of this species is rivaled by that of the <a href="/wiki/Kori_bustard" title="Kori bustard">kori bustard</a> (<i>Ardeotis kori</i>), which, due to its relatively longer tarsi and tail, is both longer and taller on average and is less sexually dimorphic.<sup id="cite_ref-Alonso2010_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alonso2010-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In terms of weight ranges reported, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Indian_bustard" title="Great Indian bustard">great Indian bustard</a> (<i>Ardeotis nigriceps</i>) also only lags slightly behind these species.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC2_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CRC_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wood_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The great bustard is also arguably the most <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphic</a> extant bird species, in terms of the size difference between males and females. Adult male great bustards measured in Spain weighed on average 2.48 times more than females.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Going on mass, the only known bird with a higher dimorphism is the <a href="/wiki/Green_peafowl" title="Green peafowl">green peafowl</a> (<i>Pavo muticus</i>) as the males are apparently near four times as heavy as females.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC2_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Tetrapod_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tetrapod-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The female is about a third smaller in linear dimensions, typically measuring 75 to 85&#160;cm (2&#160;ft 6&#160;in to 2&#160;ft 9&#160;in) in height, about 90&#160;cm (2&#160;ft 11&#160;in)<sup id="cite_ref-Letters_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Letters-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> in length and 180&#160;cm (5&#160;ft 11&#160;in) across the wings.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gbgroup_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gbgroup-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Overall, the female's weight can range from 3.1 to 8&#160;kg (6.8 to 17.6&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-gbgroup_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gbgroup-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jrank_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jrank-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Like male weights, females weights are quite variable as reported: in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, females had a mean weight of 3.82&#160;kg (8.4&#160;lb), in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, females had a mean weight of 4.35&#160;kg (9.6&#160;lb) and in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, females reportedly had a median weight of 6&#160;kg (13&#160;lb). The latter figure indicates that eastern birds (presumably <i>O. t. dybowskii</i>) are considerably less sexually dimorphic in body mass than in other populations.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC2_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Alonso2010_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alonso2010-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CRC_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Perhaps because of this physical sexual dimorphism, there is a skewed sex ratio of about 1.5:1 female to male.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_BustardLg1438.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Great_BustardLg1438.jpeg/220px-Great_BustardLg1438.jpeg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Great_BustardLg1438.jpeg/330px-Great_BustardLg1438.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Great_BustardLg1438.jpeg/440px-Great_BustardLg1438.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="450" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_BustardLg1438.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mounted specimen of a female, with somewhat more muted tones and a more slender, smaller build than the adult male.</div></div></div>
<p>An adult male is brown above, barred with blackish colouration, and white below, with a long grey neck and head. His breast and lower neck sides are chestnut and there is a golden wash to the back and the extent of these bright colours tending to increase as the male ages. In the breeding season, the male has long white neck bristles, which measure up to 12–15&#160;cm (4.7–5.9&#160;in) in length, continually growing from the third to the sixth year of life.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In flight, the long wings are predominantly white with brown showing along the edges of the lower primary and secondary feathers and a dark brown streak along the upper-edge of the wing. The breast and neck of the female are buff, with brown and pale colouration over the rest of the plumage rendering it well camouflaged in open habitats. Immature birds resemble the female. The eastern subspecies (<i>O. t. dybowskii</i>) is more extensively grey in colour in both sexes, with more extensive barring on the back.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The great bustard has long legs, a long neck and a heavy, barrel-chested body. It is fairly typical of the family in its overall shape and habitat preferences. Three other bustard species overlap in range with this species: the <a href="/wiki/Macqueen%27s_bustard" class="mw-redirect" title="Macqueen&#39;s bustard">Macqueen's</a> (<i>Chlamydotis macqueenii</i>), <a href="/wiki/Houbara_bustard" title="Houbara bustard">houbara</a> (<i>Chlamydotis undulata</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Little_bustard" title="Little bustard">little bustards</a> (<i>Tetrax tetrax</i>). However, none of these attains the plumage coloration nor approach the body sizes of this species. Thus, the great bustard is essentially unmistakable.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_bustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Las_Avutardas_de_Madrid.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Las_Avutardas_de_Madrid.jpg/220px-Las_Avutardas_de_Madrid.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Las_Avutardas_de_Madrid.jpg/330px-Las_Avutardas_de_Madrid.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Las_Avutardas_de_Madrid.jpg/440px-Las_Avutardas_de_Madrid.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2908" data-file-height="2192" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Las_Avutardas_de_Madrid.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Great bustards in Spain in fairly typical habitat for the species.</div></div></div>
<p>These birds' <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> is <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a> or <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a> defined by open, flat or somewhat rolling landscapes. They can be found on undisturbed cultivation and seem to prefer areas with wild or cultivated crops such as cereals, vineyards and fodder plants. However, during the breeding season, they actively avoid areas with regular human activity and can be disturbed by agricultural practices. Great bustards are often attracted to areas with considerable insect activity.
</p><p>The breeding range of the great bustard currently stretches from Portugal to Manchuria, though previously the species bred even further east in Russian Primorsky Krai. Due to population declines across much of the range, more than half of the global population is now found in central Spain with around 30,000 individuals. Smaller populations are in southern Russia and the Great Hungarian Plain.<sup id="cite_ref-raab_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-raab-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_bustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The species is gregarious, especially in winter when gatherings of several dozen birds may occur. Male and female groups do not mix outside of the breeding season. The great bustard has a stately slow walk but tends to run when disturbed rather than fly. Running speeds have not been measured but adult females have been known to outrun <a href="/wiki/Red_fox" title="Red fox">red foxes</a> (<i>Vulpes vulpes</i>), which can reach a trotting speed of 48&#160;km/h (30&#160;mph).<sup id="cite_ref-westcountrywildlife_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-westcountrywildlife-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-biokids_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biokids-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Both sexes are usually silent but can engage in deep grunts when alarmed or angered. The displaying adult male may produce some booming, grunting and raucous noises. The female may utter some guttural calls at the nest and brooded young make a soft, trilling call in communication with their mothers.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration">Migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_bustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some individuals in Iberian populations make short seasonal movements of 5 – 200&#160;km, particularly males which appear to be move in response to higher summer temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> European populations are sedentary or make irregular movements in response to severe winter weather.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Populations breeding along the Volga River in Russia migrate 1000&#160;km to overwinter in Crimea and Kherson Oblast.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Populations breeding in northern Mongolia migrate over 2000&#160;km to overwinter in Shaanxi Province of China.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In migratory populations, great bustards often gather in larger numbers at pre-migratory sites in order to move collectively to winter grounds. In the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>, bustards that engage in migration seem to choose different periods for movements based on sex.<sup id="cite_ref-MoralesAlonso2000_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoralesAlonso2000-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Alonso2000_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alonso2000-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Alonso2001_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alonso2001-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PalacínAlonso2009_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PalacínAlonso2009-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PalacínAlonso2011_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PalacínAlonso2011-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> No population is known to use the same grounds for wintering and summering.<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Great Bustards are strong fliers and reach speeds of 48&#160;km/hr to 98&#160;km/hr during migration.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_bustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BustardDisplay.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/BustardDisplay.jpg/220px-BustardDisplay.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="341" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/BustardDisplay.jpg/330px-BustardDisplay.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/BustardDisplay.jpg/440px-BustardDisplay.jpg 2x" data-file-width="724" data-file-height="1122" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BustardDisplay.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Male Bustard display</div></div></div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Otis_tarda_MWNH_0041.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Otis_tarda_MWNH_0041.JPG/220px-Otis_tarda_MWNH_0041.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="331" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Otis_tarda_MWNH_0041.JPG/330px-Otis_tarda_MWNH_0041.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Otis_tarda_MWNH_0041.JPG/440px-Otis_tarda_MWNH_0041.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2848" data-file-height="4288" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Otis_tarda_MWNH_0041.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>The great bustard breeds in March, and a single male may mate with up to five females. Before mating, the males moult into their breeding plumage around January. Males establish dominance in their groups during winter, clashing violently by ramming into and hitting each other with their bills.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Like other bustards, the male great bustard displays and competes for the attention of females on what is known as a <a href="/wiki/Lek_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lek (biology)">lek</a>. In this species, the male has a flamboyant display beginning with the strutting male puffing up his throat to the size of a football. He then tilts forwards and pulls his head in so that the long whiskery chin feathers point upwards and the head is no longer visible. He next cocks his tail flat along his back, exposing the normally hidden bright white plumage then he lowers his wings, with the primary flight feathers folded but with the white secondaries fanning out.<sup id="cite_ref-NHM_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHM-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The displaying males, who may walk around for several minutes at a time with feathers flared and head buried waiting for hens to arrive, have been described as a "foam-bath" because of their appearance.<sup id="cite_ref-Alonso2010_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alonso2010-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> All breeding great bustards also moult again from June to September.
</p><p>One to three olive or tan coloured, glossy eggs (two eggs being the average) are laid by the female in May or June. The nests, which are shallow scrapes made by the female on dry, soft slopes and plains, are usually situated close to the prior lek location. Nests are situated in sparse clusters, with a study in <a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Inner Mongolia</a> finding nests at a minimal 9&#160;m (30&#160;ft) apart from each other. In the same study, nests were placed at mid-elevation on a hill, at about 190 to 230&#160;m (620 to 750&#160;ft). Nesting sites are typically in dense grassy vegetation about 15 to 35&#160;cm (5.9 to 13.8&#160;in), likely for protection against predation, with extensive exposure to sunlight.<sup id="cite_ref-Wan2002_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wan2002-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Eggs weigh about 150&#160;g (5.3&#160;oz) and are on average 79.4&#160;mm (3.13&#160;in) tall by 56.8&#160;mm (2.24&#160;in) wide. The female incubates the eggs alone for 21 to 28 days. The chicks almost immediately leave the nest after they hatch, although they do not move very far from their mother until they are at least 1 year old. Young great bustards begin developing their adult plumage at about 2 months, and begin to develop flying skills at the same time. They practice by stretching, running, flapping, and making small hops and jumps to get airborne. By three months they are able to fly reasonable distances. If threatened, the young stand still, using their downy plumage, mainly sepia in colour with paler buffy streaks, as camouflage. Juveniles are independent by their first winter, but normally stay with their mother until the next breeding season. Males usually start to mate from 5 to 6 years of age, although may engage in breeding display behaviour at a younger age. Females usually first breed at 2 to 3 years old.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NHM_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHM-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_bustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The species is <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivorous</a>, taking different foods in differing seasons. In northwestern Spain in August, 48.4% of the diet of adult birds was green plant material, 40.9% was <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> and 10.6% was seeds. In the same population during winter, seeds and green plant material constituted almost the entirety of the diet. <a href="/wiki/Alfalfa" title="Alfalfa">Alfalfa</a> is seemingly preferred in the diet of birds from Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-Lane1999_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lane1999-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Other favoured plant life in the diet can including <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legumes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brassicaceae" title="Brassicaceae">crucifers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taraxacum_officinale" title="Taraxacum officinale">common dandelion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grape" title="Grape">grapes</a> and the dry seeds of <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Among animal prey, <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> are generally eaten and are the main food for young bustards in their first summer, though they then switch to the seasonal herbivorous preferences of adults by winter. <a href="/wiki/Coleoptera" class="mw-redirect" title="Coleoptera">Coleoptera</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>), <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">Hymenoptera</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">wasps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ants</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Orthoptera" title="Orthoptera">Orthoptera</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Cricket_(insect)" title="Cricket (insect)">crickets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Locust" title="Locust">locusts</a>) are mainly taken, largely based on availability and abundance. Small <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a>, including small <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a> and chicks of other <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, may supplement the diet when the opportunity arises.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bravo2012_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bravo2012-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Great bustards may eat toxic <a href="/wiki/Blister_beetle" title="Blister beetle">blister beetles</a> of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Meloe" title="Meloe">Meloe</a></i> to self-medicate (<a href="/wiki/Zoopharmacognosy" title="Zoopharmacognosy">Zoopharmacognosy</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-Bravo2014_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bravo2014-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> increasing sexual arousal of males.<sup id="cite_ref-Heneberg2016_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heneberg2016-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foraging">Foraging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_bustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Foraging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In winter the feeding intensity increased and then decreased through the morning in both sexes, and was lower in flocks of males than in flocks of females. This sexual difference is greater where legume availability was smaller in central Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-Bautista2017_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bautista2017-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>   Males that foraged slightly less intensively than females could compensate with longer periods of foraging <sup id="cite_ref-Martinez2000_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martinez2000-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> and bigger bite size<sup id="cite_ref-AlonsoMagaña2009_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AlonsoMagaña2009-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>  that would allow them to obtain enough food relative to their absolute daily energy requirements. The size of morning foraging area is smaller in sites with more legume availability, likely because legumes are the most preferred substrate type.<sup id="cite_ref-Bautista2017_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bautista2017-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mortality">Mortality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_bustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mortality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Great bustards typically live for around 10 years, but some have been known to live up to 15 years or more. The maximum known life span for the species was 28 years. Adult males seem to have a higher mortality rate than females due mainly to fierce intraspecies fighting with other males during the breeding season. Many males may perish in their first couple of years of maturity due to this cause.<sup id="cite_ref-gbgroup_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gbgroup-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although little detailed information has been obtained of predators, over 80% of great bustards die in the first year of life and many are victims of predation. Chicks are subject to <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predation</a> by the fact that they are ground-dwelling birds which are reluctant to fly. Predators of <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> and hatchlings include <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corvid" class="mw-redirect" title="Corvid">corvids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hedgehog" title="Hedgehog">hedgehogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">foxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Badger" title="Badger">badgers</a> (<i>Meles</i> ssp.), <a href="/wiki/Marten" title="Marten">martens</a> (<i>Martes</i> ssp.), <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a> (<i>Rattus</i> ssp.) and <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">wild boars</a> (<i>Sus scrofa</i>). The most serious natural predators of nests are perhaps <a href="/wiki/Red_fox" title="Red fox">red foxes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hooded_crow" title="Hooded crow">hooded crows</a> (<i>Corvus cornix</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-grosstrappe_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grosstrappe-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Chicks grow very quickly, by 6 months being nearly two-thirds of their adult size, and are predated by <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">foxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lynx" title="Lynx">lynxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gray_wolf" class="mw-redirect" title="Gray wolf">wolves</a> (<i>Canis lupus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jackal" title="Jackal">jackals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Abdulkarimi_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Abdulkarimi-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Predation on adult male great bustards has been reportedly committed by <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_eagle" title="White-tailed eagle">white-tailed eagles</a> (<i>Haliaeetus albicilla</i>) while <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">golden eagles</a> (<i>Aquila chrysaetos</i>) are potential predators and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_imperial_eagle" title="Eastern imperial eagle">eastern imperial eagles</a> (<i>Aquila heliaca</i>) have been known to prey on great bustards (but not likely to include adult males).<sup id="cite_ref-Love_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Love-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Great bustards of unspecified age and sex have been found amongst <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_eagle-owl" title="Eurasian eagle-owl">Eurasian eagle-owl</a> (<i>Bubo bubo</i>) prey remains in <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mitev2006_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mitev2006-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> A possible act of predation on a great bustard was observed to be committed by a much smaller raptor, the <a href="/wiki/Western_marsh_harrier" title="Western marsh harrier">western marsh harrier</a> (<i>Circus aeruginosus</i>), though it was likely that this bustard was "weak or injured" if it was taken alive.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> The bold, conspicuous behaviour of the breeding adult male bustard may attract the same large mammalian predators that predate chicks, such as wolves and lynx, while the more inconspicuous female may sometimes be attacked by various predators. However, predation is generally fairly rare for adults due to their size, nimbleness and safety in numbers due to their social behaviour.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gbgroup_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gbgroup-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CMS_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CMS-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Magana2010_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Magana2010-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Occasionally, other natural causes may contribute to mortality in the species, especially starvation in harsh winter months.<sup id="cite_ref-CMS_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CMS-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> However, major causes of mortality in recent centuries have been largely linked to human activity, as described below.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_distribution">Population distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_bustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Population distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bet.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Bet.jpg/220px-Bet.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Bet.jpg/330px-Bet.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Bet.jpg/440px-Bet.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="681" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bet.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bustard juvenille</div></div></div>
<p>As of 2008, the global population numbered between 44,000 to 51,000 birds (Palacin &amp; Alonso 2008), about 38,000 to 47,000 in Europe, with 30,000 or more than half in Spain. Hungary had the next largest Great Bustard population with about 1,555 in the year 2012, followed by Ukraine and Austria. Between 4,200 and 4,500 were found in east Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-raab_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-raab-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In recent times, there have been steep declines throughout eastern and central Europe and in Asia, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<p>Sizeable populations exist in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> (23,055 birds), <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> (8,000 birds), <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> (800–3,000 birds), <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> (1,435 birds) and <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> (1,000 birds). In <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> the populations are small (Germany 2016: 232 birds; Austria 2012: 335 birds) but steadily growing for about two decades.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Elsewhere, the populations are declining due to habitat loss throughout its range. A sizeable population also exists in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> (1,100–1,300 birds) where the Eastern European steppe zone ends, near <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9vav%C3%A1nya" title="Dévaványa">Dévaványa</a> town and also in the <a href="/wiki/Hortob%C3%A1gy_National_Park" title="Hortobágy National Park">Hortobágy National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nagykuns%C3%A1g" title="Nagykunság">Nagykunság</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagy-S%C3%A1rr%C3%A9t&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nagy-Sárrét (page does not exist)">Nagy-Sárrét</a> regions. The population is down from a population of 10,000–12,000 before the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>.
</p><p>Agrienvironment schemes as unirrigated legumes fostered a population increase of great bustards in <a href="/wiki/Castilla_y_Leon" class="mw-redirect" title="Castilla y Leon">Castilla y Leon</a>, central Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-Martín2012_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martín2012-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats_and_conservation_status">Threats and conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_bustard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Threats and conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The great bustard is classified as vulnerable at the species level. There are myriad threats faced by great bustards. Increasing human disturbance could lead to habitat loss caused by the ploughing of grasslands, intensive agriculture, <a href="/wiki/Afforestation" title="Afforestation">afforestation</a>, increased development of irrigation schemes, and the construction of roads, power lines, fencing and ditches. Mechanisation, chemical <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, fire and predation by dogs are serious threats for chicks and juveniles, and hunting of adults contributes to high mortality in some of their range countries. Agricultural activity is a major disturbance at nest and, in Hungary, few successful nests are found outside of protected areas.<sup id="cite_ref-CMS_46-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CMS-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Bustards, despite their large size, are able to fly at high speed and are often mutilated or killed by overhead electricity cables, which are placed in the West Pannonia region of Eastern Austria and Western Hungary just at their flying height. The electricity companies affected have buried part of the dangerous cables, and have marked remaining aerial parts with fluorescent markers to warn off the birds. These measures have rapidly reduced bustard mortality.<sup id="cite_ref-CMS_46-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CMS-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RaabSchütz2011_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaabSchütz2011-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Bustards are also occasionally killed by collisions with automobiles or by entanglement in wires.<sup id="cite_ref-CMS_46-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CMS-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Bustard_woodcut_in_Bewick_British_Birds_1797.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Great_Bustard_woodcut_in_Bewick_British_Birds_1797.jpg/220px-Great_Bustard_woodcut_in_Bewick_British_Birds_1797.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="327" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Great_Bustard_woodcut_in_Bewick_British_Birds_1797.jpg/330px-Great_Bustard_woodcut_in_Bewick_British_Birds_1797.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Great_Bustard_woodcut_in_Bewick_British_Birds_1797.jpg/440px-Great_Bustard_woodcut_in_Bewick_British_Birds_1797.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1202" data-file-height="1788" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Bustard_woodcut_in_Bewick_British_Birds_1797.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Wood_engraving" title="Wood engraving">Wood engraving</a> by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bewick" title="Thomas Bewick">Thomas Bewick</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/A_History_of_British_Birds" title="A History of British Birds">A History of British Birds</a></i>, 1797; he was concerned about their probable local extinction. A horseman and greyhound gallop after another bustard in the background.</div></div></div>
<p>The great bustard was formerly native in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and a bustard forms part of the design of the <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a> <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">Coat of Arms</a> and as <a href="/wiki/Supporter" title="Supporter">supporters</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a> arms.<sup id="cite_ref-Cambridgeshire_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridgeshire-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> As early as 1797, the naturalist and wood engraver <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bewick" title="Thomas Bewick">Thomas Bewick</a> commented in his <i><a href="/wiki/A_History_of_British_Birds" title="A History of British Birds">A History of British Birds</a></i> that "Both this [the little bustard] and the Great Bustard are excellent eating, and would well repay the trouble of domestication; indeed, it seems surprising, that we should suffer these fine birds to be in danger of total extinction, although, if properly cultivated, they might afford as excellent a repast as our own domestic poultry, or even as the Turkey, for which we are indebted to distant countries."<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Bewick's prediction was correct; the great bustard was hunted out of existence in Britain by the 1840s.
</p><p>In 2004, a project overseeing the reintroduction to <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Plain" title="Salisbury Plain">Salisbury Plain</a> in Wiltshire using eggs taken from <a href="/wiki/Saratov_Oblast" title="Saratov Oblast">Saratov</a> in Russia was undertaken by The Great Bustard Group, a UK Registered Charity that aims to establish a self-sustaining population of great bustards in the UK. The reintroduced birds have laid eggs and raised chicks in Britain in 2009 and 2010. Although the great bustard was once native to Britain, great bustards are considered an alien species under English law.<sup id="cite_ref-BBCNews_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBCNews-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> The reintroduction of the great bustard to the UK by the Great Bustard Group is being carried out in parallel with researchers from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bath" title="University of Bath">University of Bath</a> who are providing insight into the habitat of native great bustard populations in Russia and Hungary. On January 19, 2011, it was announced that the Great Bustard Project had been awarded EU <a href="/wiki/The_LIFE_Programme" title="The LIFE Programme">LIFE+</a> funding, reportedly to the tune of £1.8 million.<sup id="cite_ref-BBCNews2011_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBCNews2011-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> In Hungary, where the species is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_national_birds" title="List of national birds">national bird</a>, great bustards are actively protected. The Hungarian authorities are seeking to preserve the long-term future of the population by active protection measures; the area affected by the special ecological treatment had grown to 15&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (5.8&#160;sq&#160;mi) by the summer of 2006.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement"><span title="This claim has reliable sources with contradicting facts (November 2017)">disputed</span></a>&#32;<span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Great_bustard#National_bird_status,_preservation" title="Talk:Great bustard">discuss</a></span></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Under the auspices of the <a href="/wiki/Bonn_Convention" class="mw-redirect" title="Bonn Convention">Convention on Migratory Species of Wild Animals (CMS)</a>, also known as the <a href="/wiki/Bonn_Convention" class="mw-redirect" title="Bonn Convention">Bonn Convention</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Bustard_Memorandum_of_Understanding" title="Great Bustard Memorandum of Understanding">the Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) on the Conservation and Management of Middle-European Populations of the Great Bustard</a> was concluded and came into effect on June 1, 2001. The MoU provides a framework for governments, scientists, conservation bodies and others to monitor and coordinate conservation efforts in order to protect the middle-European populations of the great bustard.
</p>
