<p>The <b>Cyprus wheatear</b> or <b>Cyprus pied wheatear</b> (<i>Oenanthe cypriaca</i>) is a small, 14–15&#160;cm long <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> that was formerly classed as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family Turdidae, but is now more generally considered to be an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a>, Muscicapidae. It was formerly treated as a subspecies (race) of <a href="/wiki/Pied_wheatear" title="Pied wheatear">pied wheatear</a> but Sluys and van den Berg (1982)<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> argued that the form deserved full species status, on the basis of differences in biometrics and especially song, and the lack of sexual plumage dimorphism in <i>cypriaca</i>.
</p><p>This <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> insectivorous species <a href="/wiki/Endemic" class="mw-redirect" title="Endemic">breeds only</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, and winters in southern <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It has been recorded as a vagrant on Heligoland, Germany,<sup id="cite_ref-Förschler_et_al_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Förschler_et_al-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species closely resembles pied wheatear, although it has slightly more black on the tail and back, and on the head. The sexes are similar in appearance, a fact first documented by Christensen (1974).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> A 2010 study found that Cyprus wheatear differs from pied wheatear in fourteen external morphometric characters.<sup id="cite_ref-Förschler_et_al_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Förschler_et_al-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The song is distinctive, and very different from that of pied wheatear, resembling an insect. It consists of a series of high-pitched buzzing bursts.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The song-perches utilised by this species are high for a wheatear, typically being 5 to 10 metres above ground. It often breeds in woodland habitats, unlike other wheatears (Oliver 1990<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> suggested that it occupies the ecological niche used elsewhere in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Palearctic">Western Palearctic</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Common_redstart" title="Common redstart">common redstart</a>). It is the most arboreal species of wheatear in the western palearctic and it uses often aerial <a href="/wiki/Hawking_(birds)" title="Hawking (birds)">sallying</a> and perch-pounce-feeding tactics. Recent work suggest an ecological differentiation between Cyprus wheatear and migrating northern wheatears <i>O. oenanthe</i> and black-eared wheatears <i>O. hispanica melanoleuca</i>. Cyprus wheatear uses more aerial sallying and occupies more forested habitats, but needs a minimum amount of open/bare ground, and a minimum of high bush/tree vegetation (Randler et al. 2009<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>).
</p>
