<p>The <b>cinereous vulture</b> (<i>Aegypius monachus</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Raptorial" title="Raptorial">raptorial</a> bird that is distributed through much of temperate <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurasia</a>. It is also known as the <b>black vulture</b>, <b>monk vulture</b>, or <b>Eurasian black vulture</b>. It is a member of the family <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a>, which also includes many other diurnal <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptors</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Kite_(bird)" title="Kite (bird)">kites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buzzard" title="Buzzard">buzzards</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harrier_(bird)" title="Harrier (bird)">harriers</a>. It is one of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_birds" title="List of largest birds">two largest</a> <a href="/wiki/Old_World_vulture" title="Old World vulture">Old World vultures</a>, attaining a maximum size of 14&#160;kg, (roughly 30&#160;lbs), 1.2 m long (almost 4&#160;ft) and 3.1 m (a bit over 10&#160;ft) across the wings.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinereous_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The genus name <i>Aegypius</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> word (αἰγυπιός) for 'vulture', or a bird not unlike one; <a href="/wiki/Claudius_Aelianus" title="Claudius Aelianus">Aelian</a> describes the <i>aegypius</i> as "halfway between a vulture (<i>gyps</i>) and an eagle". Some authorities think this a good description of a <a href="/wiki/Lammergeier" class="mw-redirect" title="Lammergeier">lammergeier</a>; others do not. <a href="/wiki/Aegypius_(mythology)" title="Aegypius (mythology)">Aegypius</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Eponym" title="Eponym">eponym</a> of the species, whatever it was in ancient Greek.<sup id="cite_ref-Celoria_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Celoria-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The English name 'black vulture' refers to the plumage colour, while 'monk vulture', a direct translation of its German name <a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6nchsgeier" class="extiw" title="de:Mönchsgeier">Mönchsgeier</a>, refers to the bald head and ruff of neck feathers like a monk's <a href="/wiki/Cowl" title="Cowl">cowl</a>. '<a href="/wiki/Cinereous" title="Cinereous">Cinereous</a> vulture' (Latin <i>cineraceus</i>, ash-coloured; pale, whitish grey), was a deliberate attempt to rename it with a new name distinct from the American black vulture.<sup id="cite_ref-Sibley_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sibley-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This bird is an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_vulture" title="Old World vulture">Old World vulture</a>, and as such is only distantly related to the <a href="/wiki/New_World_vulture" title="New World vulture">New World vultures</a>, which are in a separate family, <a href="/wiki/Cathartidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Cathartidae">Cathartidae</a>, of the same order. It is, therefore, not closely related to the much smaller <a href="/wiki/Black_vulture" title="Black vulture">American black vulture</a> (<i>Coragyps atratus</i>) despite the similar name and coloration.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinereous_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The cinereous vulture measures 98–120&#160;cm (3&#160;ft 3&#160;in–3&#160;ft 11&#160;in) in total length with a 2.5–3.1&#160;m (8&#160;ft 2&#160;in–10&#160;ft 2&#160;in) wingspan. Males can weigh from 6.3 to 11.5&#160;kg (14 to 25&#160;lb), whereas females can weigh from 7.5 to 14&#160;kg (17 to 31&#160;lb). It is thus one of the world's heaviest flying birds.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Glutz_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glutz-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Average weights are not known to have been published for this species but the median weight figures from two sources were 9.42&#160;kg (20.8&#160;lb) and 9.55&#160;kg (21.1&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Glutz_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glutz-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Unlike most <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">accipitrids</a>, males can broadly overlap in size with the females, although not uncommonly the females may be slightly heavier.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> These are one of the two largest extant <a href="/wiki/Old_World_vulture" title="Old World vulture">Old World vultures</a> and accipitrids, with similar total length and perhaps wingspans recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Himalayan_vulture" title="Himalayan vulture">Himalayan vulture</a> (<i>Gyps himalayensis</i>) , as indicated by broadly similar wing and tail proportions, but the cinereous appears to be slightly heavier as well as slightly larger in tarsus and bill length.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Superficially similar but unrelated New World <a href="/wiki/Condor" title="Condor">condors</a> can either be of <a href="/wiki/California_condor" title="California condor">similar</a> wing area and bulk or <a href="/wiki/Andean_condor" title="Andean condor">slightly larger</a> in these aspects.<sup id="cite_ref-Wood_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  Despite limited genetic variation in the species, body size increases from west to east based on standard measurements, with the birds from southwest Europe (<a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and south <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>) averaging about 10% smaller than the vultures from central Asia (<a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Among standard measurements, the wing chord is 73–89&#160;cm (29–35&#160;in), the tail is 33–41&#160;cm (13–16&#160;in) and the tarsus is 12–14.6&#160;cm (4.7–5.7&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The cinereous vulture is distinctly dark, with the whole body being brown excepting the pale head in adults, which is covered in fine blackish <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">down</a>. This down is absent in the closely related <a href="/wiki/Lappet-faced_vulture" title="Lappet-faced vulture">lappet-faced vulture</a> (<i>Torgos tracheliotos</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The skin of the head and neck is bluish-gray and a paler whitish color above the eye. The adult has brown eyes, a purplish cere, a blue-gray bill and pale blue-gray legs.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The primary quills are often actually black.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> From a distance, flying birds can easily appear all black. The immature plumage is sepia-brown above, with a much paler underside than in adults. Immature cinereous vultures have grey down on the head, a pale mauve cere and grey legs.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The massive bill is one of the largest of any living <a href="/wiki/Accipitriformes" title="Accipitriformes">accipiterid</a>, a feature enhanced by the relatively small skull of the species. The exposed <a href="/wiki/Culmen_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Culmen (bird)">culmen</a> of the cinereous vulture measures 8–9&#160;cm (3.1–3.5&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-IndianBio_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IndianBio-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Only their cousin, the lappet-faced vulture, with a bill length of up to about 10&#160;cm (3.9&#160;in), can rival or outsize the bill of the cinereous.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The wings, with serrated leading edges, are held straight or slightly arched in flight and are broad, sometimes referred to as "barn door wings". Their flight is slow and buoyant, with deep, heavy flaps when necessary. The combination of huge size and dark coloration renders the cinereous vulture relatively distinct, especially against smaller raptors such as <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a> or <a href="/wiki/Buteo" title="Buteo">buzzards</a>. The most similar-shaped species, the <a href="/wiki/Lappet-faced_vulture" title="Lappet-faced vulture">lappet-faced vulture</a> (with which there might be limited range overlap in the southern Middle East), is distinguished by its bare, pinkish head and contrasting plumage. On the lappet-face, the thighs and belly are whitish in adult birds against black to brownish over the remainder of the plumage. All potential <i><a href="/wiki/Gyps" title="Gyps">Gyps</a></i> vultures are distinguished by having paler, often streaky plumage, with bulging wing primaries giving them a less evenly broad-winged form.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Cinereous vultures are generally very silent, with a few querulous mewing, roaring or guttural cries solely between adults and their offspring at the nest site.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinereous_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aegypius_monachus_Mongolia_2010_314.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Aegypius_monachus_Mongolia_2010_314.jpg/220px-Aegypius_monachus_Mongolia_2010_314.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Aegypius_monachus_Mongolia_2010_314.jpg/330px-Aegypius_monachus_Mongolia_2010_314.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Aegypius_monachus_Mongolia_2010_314.jpg/440px-Aegypius_monachus_Mongolia_2010_314.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1920" data-file-height="1224" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aegypius_monachus_Mongolia_2010_314.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Flying over the snowy hillsides of Mongolia</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aegypius_monachus_dis.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Aegypius_monachus_dis.PNG/220px-Aegypius_monachus_dis.PNG" decoding="async" width="220" height="137" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Aegypius_monachus_dis.PNG/330px-Aegypius_monachus_dis.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Aegypius_monachus_dis.PNG/440px-Aegypius_monachus_dis.PNG 2x" data-file-width="633" data-file-height="395" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aegypius_monachus_dis.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><small> The distribution in year 2007 * Green: Current resident breeding range. * Green <b>?</b>: May still breed. * Green <b>R</b>: Re-introduction in progress. * Blue: Winter range; rare where hatched blue. * Dark grey: Former breeding range. * Dark grey <b>?</b>: Uncertain former breeding range.</small></div></div></div>
<p>The cinereous vulture is a Eurasian species. The western limits of its range are in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and inland <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, with a reintroduced population in south <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. They are found discontinuously to <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and throughout the central <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>. Their range continues through <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> eastwards to northern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> to its eastern limits in central <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, where they breed in northern <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>. Their range is fragmented especially throughout their European range. It is generally a permanent resident except in those parts of its range where hard winters cause limited altitudinal movement and for juveniles when they reach breeding maturity. In the eastern limits of its range, birds from the northernmost reaches may migrate down to southern Korea and China. A limited migration has also been reported in the Middle East but is not common.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Snow_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This vulture is a bird of hilly, mountainous areas, especially favoring dry semi-open habitats such as meadows at high altitudes over much of the range. Nesting usually occurs near the <a href="/wiki/Tree_line" title="Tree line">tree line</a> in the mountains.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> They are always associated with undisturbed, remote areas with limited human disturbance. They forage for carcasses over various kinds of terrain, including <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a>, grasslands, open woodlands, along <a href="/wiki/Riparian" class="mw-redirect" title="Riparian">riparian</a> habitats or any kind of mountainous habitat. In their current European range and through the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, cinereous vultures are found from 100 to 2,000&#160;m (330 to 6,560&#160;ft) in elevation, while in their Asian distribution, they are typically found at higher elevations.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Two habitat types were found to be preferred by the species in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>. Some cinereous vultures in these areas live in mountainous forests and shrubland from 800 to 3,800&#160;m (2,600 to 12,500&#160;ft), while the others preferred arid or semi-arid alpine meadows and grasslands at 3,800 to 4,500&#160;m (12,500 to 14,800&#160;ft) in elevation.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili9_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili9-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili6_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili6-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> This species can fly at a very high altitude. One cinereous vulture was observed at an elevation of 6,970&#160;m (22,870&#160;ft) on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It has a specialised <a href="/wiki/Haemoglobin" class="mw-redirect" title="Haemoglobin">haemoglobin</a> alpha<sup>D</sup> subunit of high oxygen affinity which makes it possible to take up oxygen efficiently despite the low <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">partial pressure</a> in the upper <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Juvenile and immature cinereous vultures, especially those in the northern stretches of the species range, may move large distances across undeveloped open-dry habitats in response to snowfall or high summer temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili19_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili19-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinereous_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Black_Vulture_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Black_Vulture_1.jpg/220px-Black_Vulture_1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Black_Vulture_1.jpg/330px-Black_Vulture_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Black_Vulture_1.jpg/440px-Black_Vulture_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2582" data-file-height="1722" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Black_Vulture_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In Spain</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aegypius_monachus_-_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Aegypius_monachus_-_1.jpg/220px-Aegypius_monachus_-_1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Aegypius_monachus_-_1.jpg/330px-Aegypius_monachus_-_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Aegypius_monachus_-_1.jpg/440px-Aegypius_monachus_-_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1920" data-file-height="1440" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aegypius_monachus_-_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In Israel</div></div></div>
<p>The cinereous vulture is a largely solitary bird, being found alone or in pairs much more frequently than most other Old World vultures. At large carcasses or feeding sites, small groups may congregate. Such groups can rarely include up to 12 to 20 vultures, with some older reports of up to 30 or 40.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson_5-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinereous_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vautour_moine_MHNT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Vautour_moine_MHNT.jpg/220px-Vautour_moine_MHNT.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Vautour_moine_MHNT.jpg/330px-Vautour_moine_MHNT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Vautour_moine_MHNT.jpg/440px-Vautour_moine_MHNT.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6172" data-file-height="5286" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vautour_moine_MHNT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An egg</div></div></div>
<p>In Europe, the cinereous vulture return to the nesting ground in January or February.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, they start nesting in February in March, in <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a> in early March, in northwestern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> in February or April, in northeastern India in January, and in <a href="/wiki/Turkestan" title="Turkestan">Turkestan</a> in January.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> They breed in loose colonies, with nests rarely being found in the same tree or rock formation, unlike other Old World vultures which often nest in tight-knit colonies. In Spain, nests have been found from 300&#160;m (980&#160;ft) to 2&#160;km (6,600&#160;ft) apart from each other.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The cinereous vulture breeds in high mountains and large forests, nesting in trees or occasionally on cliff ledges. The breeding season lasts from February until September or October. The most common display consists of synchronous flight movements by pairs. However, flight play between pairs and juveniles is not unusual, with the large birds interlocking talons and spiraling down through the sky. The birds use sticks and twigs as building materials, and males and females cooperate in all matters of rearing the young.<sup id="cite_ref-LPZCinereousVulture_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LPZCinereousVulture-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The huge nest is 1.45–2&#160;m (4.8–6.6&#160;ft) across and 1–3&#160;m (3.3–9.8&#160;ft) deep. The nest increases in size as a pair uses it repeatedly over the years and often comes to be decorated with dung and animal skins.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson_5-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The nests can range up to 1.5 to 12&#160;m (4.9 to 39.4&#160;ft) high in a large tree such as an <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juniper" title="Juniper">juniper</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ABCC_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABCC-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Wild_pear" title="Wild pear">wild pear</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ABCC_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABCC-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Almond" title="Almond">almond</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pine_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Pine tree">pine trees</a>. Most nesting trees are found along cliffs. In a few cases, cinereous vultures have been recorded as nesting directly on cliffs. One cliff nest completely filled a ledge that was 3.63&#160;m (11.9&#160;ft) wide and 2.5&#160;m (8.2&#160;ft) in depth.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The egg clutch typically only a single egg, though two may be exceptionally laid. The eggs have a white or pale buff base color are often overlaid with red, purplish or red-brown marks, being almost as spotted as the egg of a <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon</a>. Eggs measure from 83.4 to 104&#160;mm (3.28 to 4.09&#160;in) in height and 58 to 75&#160;mm (2.3 to 3.0&#160;in) in width, with an average of 90&#160;mm ×&#160;69.7&#160;mm (3.54&#160;in ×&#160;2.74&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The incubation period can range from 50 to 62 days, averaging 50–56 days. Normally hatching occurs in April or May in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The newly hatched young are <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">semi-altricial</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The young are covered in greyish-white to grey-brown colored down which becomes paler with age. The first flight feathers start growing from the same sockets as the down when the nestling is around 30 days old and completely cover the down by 60 days of age.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The parents feed the young by regurgitation and an active nest reportedly becomes very foul and stinking.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Weights of nestlings in Mongolia increased from as little as 2&#160;kg (4.4&#160;lb) when they are around a month old in early June to being slightly more massive than their parents at up to nearly 16&#160;kg (35&#160;lb) shortly before fledging in early autumn.<sup id="cite_ref-Reading_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reading-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Fledging is reported when the nestlings are 104–120 days old, though dependence on parents can continue for another two months. Radio-satellite tracking suggests the age of independence of juveniles from their parents to be 5.7–7 months after hatching (i.e. 2–3 months after fledging).<sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili19_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili19-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The nesting success of cinereous vultures is relatively high, with around 90% of eggs successfully hatching and more than half of yearling birds known to survive to adulthood. They are devoted, active parents, with both members of a breeding pair protecting the nest and feeding the young in shifts via regurgitation.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In Mongolia, <a href="/wiki/Pallas%27s_cat" title="Pallas&#39;s cat">Pallas's cat</a> (<i>Otocolobus manul</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Common_raven" title="Common raven">common raven</a> (<i>Corvus corax</i>) are considered potential predators of eggs in potentially both tree and cliff nests. <a href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf">Gray wolves</a> (<i>Canis lupus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">foxes</a> are also mentioned as potential nest predators, but since neither one can climb trees and there are also no incidents of predation on inaccessible cliff nests, this seems unlikely.<sup id="cite_ref-Reading_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reading-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> There have been witnessed accounts of <a href="/wiki/Bearded_vulture" title="Bearded vulture">bearded vultures</a> (<i>Gypaetus barbatus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_imperial_eagle" title="Spanish imperial eagle">Spanish imperial eagles</a> (<i>Aquila adalberti</i>) attempting to kill nestlings, but in both cases they were chased off by the parents.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> There is a single case of a <a href="/wiki/Spanish_imperial_eagle" title="Spanish imperial eagle">Spanish imperial eagle</a> attacking and killing a cinereous vulture in an act of defense of its own nest in Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">Golden eagles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_eagle-owl" title="Eurasian eagle-owl">Eurasian eagle-owls</a> may rarely attempt to dispatch an older nestling or even adults in an ambush, but the species is not verified prey for either and it would be a rare event in all likelihood if it does occur. This species may live for up to 39 years, though 20 years or less is probably more common, with no regular predators of adults other than man.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinereous_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:6_Aegypius_monachus,_2_Gyps_fulvus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/6_Aegypius_monachus%2C_2_Gyps_fulvus.jpg/220px-6_Aegypius_monachus%2C_2_Gyps_fulvus.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/6_Aegypius_monachus%2C_2_Gyps_fulvus.jpg/330px-6_Aegypius_monachus%2C_2_Gyps_fulvus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/6_Aegypius_monachus%2C_2_Gyps_fulvus.jpg/440px-6_Aegypius_monachus%2C_2_Gyps_fulvus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:6_Aegypius_monachus,_2_Gyps_fulvus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Six cinereous vultures with the smaller Eurasian griffons</div></div></div>
<p>Like all vultures, the cinereous vulture eats mostly carrion. The cinereous vulture feeds on carrion of almost any type, from the largest <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> available to <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-LPZCinereousVulture_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LPZCinereousVulture-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In Tibet, commonly eaten carcasses can include both <a href="/wiki/Wild_yak" title="Wild yak">wild</a> and <a href="/wiki/Domestic_yak" title="Domestic yak">domestic yaks</a> (<i>Bos mutus</i> and <i>Bos grunniens</i>), <a href="/wiki/Bharal" title="Bharal">Bharal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goa_(antelope)" title="Goa (antelope)">Tibetan gazelles</a> (<i>Pseudois nayaur</i>), <a href="/wiki/Kiang" title="Kiang">kiangs</a> (<i>Equus kiang</i>), <a href="/wiki/Woolly_hare" title="Woolly hare">woolly hares</a> (<i>Lepus oiostolus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Himalayan_marmot" title="Himalayan marmot">Himalayan marmots</a> (<i>Marmota himalayana</i>), <a href="/wiki/Domestic_sheep" class="mw-redirect" title="Domestic sheep">domestic sheep</a> (<i>Ovis aries</i>), and even <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>, mainly those at their <a href="/wiki/Sky_burial" title="Sky burial">celestial burial grounds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_16-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Reportedly in <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tarbagan_marmot" title="Tarbagan marmot">Tarbagan marmots</a> (<i>Marmota sibirica</i>) comprised the largest part of the diet, although that species is now endangered as it is preferred in the diet of local people, wild prey ranging from <a href="/wiki/Corsac_fox" title="Corsac fox">corsac fox</a> (<i>Vulpes corsac</i>) to <a href="/wiki/Argali" title="Argali">Argali</a> (<i>Ovis ammon</i>) may be eaten additionally in Mongolia.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_16-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Historically, cinereous vultures in the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> fed mostly on <a href="/wiki/European_rabbit" title="European rabbit">European rabbit</a> (<i>Oryctolagus cuniculus</i>) carcasses, but since <a href="/wiki/Viral_hemorrhagic_fever" title="Viral hemorrhagic fever">viral hemorrhagic pneumonia</a> (VHP) devastated the once abundant rabbit population there, the vultures now rely on the carrion of domestic sheep, supplemented by <a href="/wiki/Pig" title="Pig">pigs</a> (<i>Sus scrofa domesticus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In Turkey, the dietary preferences were <a href="/wiki/Argali" title="Argali">argali</a> (<i>Ovis ammon</i>) (92 carrion items), <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">wild boar</a> (<i>Sus scrofa</i>) (53 items), <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">chickens</a> (<i>Gallus gallus domesticus</i>) (27 items), <a href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf">gray wolves</a> (13 items) and <a href="/wiki/Red_fox" title="Red fox">red foxes</a> (<i>Vulpes vulpes</i>) (13 items). Unusually, a large amount of plant material was found in pellets from Turkey, especially <a href="/wiki/Pine_cone" class="mw-redirect" title="Pine cone">pine cones</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Among the vultures in its range, the cinereous is best equipped to tear open tough carcass skins thanks to its powerful bill. It can even break apart bones, such as ribs, to access the flesh of large animals. It is dominant over other scavengers in its range, even over other large vultures such as <i><a href="/wiki/Gyps" title="Gyps">Gyps</a></i> vultures, <a href="/wiki/Bearded_vulture" title="Bearded vulture">bearded vultures</a> or fierce ground predators such as <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">foxes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> While the noisy <i><a href="/wiki/Gyps" title="Gyps">Gyps</a></i> vultures squawk and fly around, the often silent cinereous vultures will keep them well at bay until they are satisfied and have had their own fill.<sup id="cite_ref-IndianBio_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IndianBio-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili6_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili6-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> A series of photos taken recently show a cinereous vulture attacking a <a href="/wiki/Himalayan_griffon" class="mw-redirect" title="Himalayan griffon">Himalayan griffon</a> in flight for unknown reasons, although the griffon was not seriously injured.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Cinereous vultures frequently bully and dominate <a href="/wiki/Steppe_eagle" title="Steppe eagle">steppe eagles</a> (<i>Aquila nipalensis</i>) when the two species are attracted to the same prey and carrion while wintering in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> A rare successful act of <a href="/wiki/Kleptoparasitism" title="Kleptoparasitism">kleptoparasitism</a> on a cinereous vulture was filmed in <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> when a <a href="/wiki/Steller%27s_sea_eagle" title="Steller&#39;s sea eagle">Steller's sea eagle</a> (<i>Haliaeetus pelagicus</i>) stole food from the vulture.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aegypius_monachus,_Estremadura,_Spain_0002_(15280998997).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Aegypius_monachus%2C_Estremadura%2C_Spain_0002_%2815280998997%29.jpg/220px-Aegypius_monachus%2C_Estremadura%2C_Spain_0002_%2815280998997%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Aegypius_monachus%2C_Estremadura%2C_Spain_0002_%2815280998997%29.jpg/330px-Aegypius_monachus%2C_Estremadura%2C_Spain_0002_%2815280998997%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Aegypius_monachus%2C_Estremadura%2C_Spain_0002_%2815280998997%29.jpg/440px-Aegypius_monachus%2C_Estremadura%2C_Spain_0002_%2815280998997%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="691" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aegypius_monachus,_Estremadura,_Spain_0002_(15280998997).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A cinereous vulture feeding in Spain</div></div></div>
<p>Its closest living relative is probably the lappet-faced vulture, which takes live prey on occasion.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson_5-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Occasionally, the cinereous vulture has been recorded as preying on live prey as well. Live animals reportedly taken by cinereous vultures include calves of <a href="/wiki/Yak" class="mw-redirect" title="Yak">yaks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Domestic_cattle" class="mw-redirect" title="Domestic cattle">domestic cattle</a> (<i>Bos primigenius taurus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Pig" title="Pig">piglets</a>, domestic <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">lambs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">puppies</a> (<i>Canis lupus familiaris</i>), <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">foxes</a>, lambs of <a href="/wiki/Wild_sheep" class="mw-redirect" title="Wild sheep">wild sheep</a>, together with nestling and fledglings of large birds such as <a href="/wiki/Goose" title="Goose">geese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swan" title="Swan">swans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pheasant" title="Pheasant">pheasants</a>, various <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a> and rarely <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> This species has hunted <a href="/wiki/Tortoise" title="Tortoise">tortoises</a> (which the vultures are likely to kill by carrying in flight and dropping on rocks to penetrate the shell; cf. <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus#Death" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus#Death</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_16-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Although rarely observed in the act of killing ungulates, cinereous vultures have been recorded as flying low around herds and feeding on recently killed wild ungulates they are believed to have killed. Mainly neonatal lambs or calves are hunted, especially sickly ones. Although not normally thought to be a threat to healthy domestic lambs, rare predation on apparently healthy lambs has been confirmed.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Species believed to be hunted by cinereous vultures have included <a href="/wiki/Argali" title="Argali">argali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saiga_antelope" title="Saiga antelope">saiga antelope</a> (<i>Saiga tatarica</i>), <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_gazelle" title="Mongolian gazelle">Mongolian gazelle</a> (<i>Procapra gutturosa</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_antelope" title="Tibetan antelope">Tibetan antelope</a> (<i>Pantholops hodgsonii</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_and_conservation">Status and conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinereous_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Status and conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aegypius_monachus_-_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Aegypius_monachus_-_2.jpg/220px-Aegypius_monachus_-_2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Aegypius_monachus_-_2.jpg/330px-Aegypius_monachus_-_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Aegypius_monachus_-_2.jpg/440px-Aegypius_monachus_-_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2560" data-file-height="1440" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aegypius_monachus_-_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A cinereous vulture flying over <a href="/wiki/Mount_Carmel" title="Mount Carmel">Mount Carmel</a> on a rare visit in Israel (2016)</div></div></div>
<p>The cinereous vulture has declined over most of its range in the last 200 years in part due to poisoning by eating poisoned bait put out to kill dogs and other predators, and to higher hygiene standards reducing the amount of available carrion; it is currently listed as Near Threatened. Vultures of all species, although not the target of poisoning operations, may be shot on sight by locals. Trapping and hunting of cinereous vultures is particularly prevalent in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_16-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> although the poaching for trophy hunting are also known for Armenia, and probably other countries in Caucasus.<sup id="cite_ref-ABCC_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABCC-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Perhaps an even greater threat to this desolation-loving species is development and habitat destruction. Nests, often fairly low in the main fork of a tree, are relatively easy to access and thus have been historically compromised by egg and firewood collectors regularly.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_16-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili9_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili9-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The decline has been the greatest in the western half of the range, with extinction in many European countries (<a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moldova" title="Moldova">Moldova</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>) and its entire breeding range in northwest Africa (<a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>). They no longer nest in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>. Turkey holds the second largest population of this species in the Western Palearctic. Despite the recent demographic bottleneck, this population has maintained moderate levels of genetic diversity, with no significant genetic structuring indicating that this is a single meta-population connected by frequent dispersal.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> More recently, protection and deliberate feeding schemes have allowed some local recoveries in numbers, particularly in Spain, where numbers increased to about 1,000 pairs by 1992 after an earlier decline to 200 pairs in 1970. This colony have now spread its breeding grounds to Portugal. Elsewhere in Europe, very small but increasing numbers breed in <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, and a re-introduction scheme is under way in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. Trends in the small populations in Ukraine (Crimea) and European Russia, and in Asian populations, are not well recorded. In the former USSR, it is still threatened by illegal capture for zoos, and in Tibet by rodenticides. It is a regular winter visitor around the coastal areas of <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> in small numbers. As of the turn of the 21st century, the worldwide population of cinereous vultures is estimated at 4,500–5,000 individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson_5-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Snow_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_16-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture_and_mythology">Culture and mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinereous_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Culture and mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Hebrew word for "eagle" is also used for the cinereous vulture.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> As such, <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagles_in_human_culture#Religion" title="Golden eagles in human culture">Biblical passages</a> alluding to eagles might actually be referring to this bird instead.
</p>
