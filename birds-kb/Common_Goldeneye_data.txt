<p>The <b>common goldeneye</b> (<i>Bucephala clangula</i>) is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Sea_duck" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea duck">sea duck</a> of the genus <i>Bucephala</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Goldeneye_(duck)" title="Goldeneye (duck)">goldeneyes</a>. Its closest relative is the similar <a href="/wiki/Barrow%27s_goldeneye" title="Barrow&#39;s goldeneye">Barrow's goldeneye</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The genus name is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>boukephalos</i> ("bullheaded", from <i>bous</i>, "bull " and <i>kephale</i>, "head"), a reference to the bulbous head shape of the <a href="/wiki/Bufflehead" title="Bufflehead">bufflehead</a>. The species name is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>clangere</i> ("to resound").<sup id="cite_ref-job90_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job90-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Common goldeneyes are aggressive and territorial ducks, and have elaborate courtship displays.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_goldeneye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adult males ranges from 45–51&#160;cm (18–20&#160;in) and weigh approximately 1,000&#160;g (2.2&#160;lb), while females range from 40–50&#160;cm (16–20&#160;in) and weigh approximately 800&#160;g (1.8&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The common goldeneye has a wingspan of 30.3-32.7 in (77-83 cm).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The species is named for its golden-yellow eye. Adult males have a dark head with a greenish gloss and a circular white patch below the eye, a dark back and a white neck and belly. Adult females have a brown head and a mostly grey body. Their legs and feet are orange-yellow.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_breeding">Habitat and breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_goldeneye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habitat and breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Their breeding habitat is the <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a>. They are found in the lakes and rivers of boreal forests across <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and the northern United States, <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltic States">Baltic States</a>, and northern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. They are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and most winter in protected coastal waters or open inland waters at more temperate latitudes.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Naturally, they nest in cavities in large trees, where they return year after year,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> though they will readily use <a href="/wiki/Nest_box" title="Nest box">nest boxes</a> as well.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Natural tree cavities chosen for nest sites include those made by broken limbs and those made by large woodpeckers, specifically <a href="/wiki/Pileated_woodpecker" title="Pileated woodpecker">pileated woodpeckers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Black_woodpecker" title="Black woodpecker">black woodpeckers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Average egg size is a breadth of 42.6–44.0&#160;mm (1.68–1.73&#160;in), a length of 58.1–60.6&#160;mm (2.29–2.39&#160;in) and a weight of 61.2–66.6&#160;g (2.16–2.35&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The incubation period ranges from 28 to 32 days. The female does all the incubating and is abandoned by the male about 1 to 2 weeks into incubation. The young remain in the nest for about 24–36 hours. <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasitism" class="mw-redirect" title="Brood parasitism">Brood parasitism</a> is quite common with other common goldeneyes,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and occurs less frequently with other duck species. The broods commonly start to mix with other females' broods as they become more independent or are abandoned by their mothers.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Goldeneye young have been known to be competitively killed by other goldeneye mothers, <a href="/wiki/Common_loon" title="Common loon">common loons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Red-necked_grebe" title="Red-necked grebe">red-necked grebes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The young are capable of flight at 55–65 days of age.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_goldeneye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Common goldeneyes are diving birds that forage underwater. Year-round, about 32% of their prey is <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, 28% is aquatic <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and 10% is <a href="/wiki/Mollusc" class="mw-redirect" title="Mollusc">molluscs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">Insects</a> are the predominant prey while nesting and <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> are the predominant prey during migration and winter. Locally, <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> eggs and aquatic <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> can be important foods. They themselves may fall prey to various <a href="/wiki/Hawk" title="Hawk">hawks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a>, while females and their broods have been preyed upon by <a href="/wiki/Bear" title="Bear">bears</a> (<i>Ursus</i> spp.), various <a href="/wiki/Weasel" title="Weasel">weasels</a> (<i>Mustela</i> spp.), <a href="/wiki/Mink" title="Mink">mink</a> (<i>Mustela vison</i>), <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">raccoons</a> (<i>Procyon lotor</i>) and even <a href="/wiki/Northern_flicker" title="Northern flicker">northern flickers</a> (<i>Colaptes auratus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Red_squirrel" title="Red squirrel">red squirrels</a> (<i>Tamiasciurus husonicus</i>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_goldeneye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common goldeneye is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies. Approximately 188,300 common goldeneyes were killed annually by <a href="/wiki/Duck_hunter" class="mw-redirect" title="Duck hunter">duck hunters</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> during the 1970s, representing slightly less than 4% of the total waterfowl killed in Canada during that period, and less than 1% of the total waterfowl killed in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Both the breeding and winter habitat of these birds has been degraded by clearance and pollution. However, the common goldeneye in North America is known to derive short-term benefits from lake acidification.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
