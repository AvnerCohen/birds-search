<p>The <b>Common Goldeneye</b> (<i>Bucephala clangula</i>) is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Sea_duck" title="Sea duck" class="mw-redirect">sea duck</a> of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Goldeneye_(duck)" title="Goldeneye (duck)">Bucephala</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Goldeneye_(duck)" title="Goldeneye (duck)">goldeneyes</a>. Their closest relative is the similar <a href="/wiki/Barrow%27s_Goldeneye" title="Barrow's Goldeneye">Barrow's Goldeneye</a>.</p>
<p>Adult males ranges from 45–52&#160;cm (18–21&#160;inches) and from 888 to 1400&#160;grams (1.9 to 3.1&#160;lbs), while females range from 40–50&#160;cm (16–20&#160;inches) and from 500 to 1182&#160;grams (1.1 to 2.6&#160;lbs). The species is aptly named for its golden-yellow eye. Adult males have a dark head with a greenish gloss and a circular white patch below the eye, a dark back and a white neck and belly. Adult females have a brown head and a mostly grey body. Their legs and feet are orange-yellow.</p>
<p>Their breeding habitat is the <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a>. They are found in the lakes and rivers of boreal forests across <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and the northern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. They are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and most winter in protected coastal waters or open inland waters at more temperate latitudes. Naturally, they nest in cavities in large trees. They will readily use nestboxes, and this has enabled a healthy breeding population to establish in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> where they are increasing and slowly spreading with the help of nestboxes. They are usually quite common in winter around lakes of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a> and some are being encouraged to nest in nestboxes which are put up to try to have them there all year round.</p>
<p>Often the natural tree cavities are made by broken limbs, unless they are made by <a href="/wiki/Pileated_Woodpecker" title="Pileated Woodpecker">Pileated Woodpeckers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Black_Woodpecker" title="Black Woodpecker">Black Woodpeckers</a>, the only tree-cavity-making animals who make a cavity large enough to normally accommodate a goldeneye. Average egg size is a breadth of 43.3&#160;mm (1.7&#160;inches), a length of 59.3&#160;mm (2.3&#160;inches) and a weight of 64&#160;grams (2.3&#160;oz). The incubation period ranges from 28 to 32 days. The female does all the incubating and is abandoned by the male about 1 to 2 weeks into incubation. The young remain in the nest for about 24–36 hours. <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasitism" title="Brood parasitism" class="mw-redirect">Brood parasitism</a> is quite common both with other Common Goldeneyes as well as other duck species and even <a href="/wiki/Tree_Swallow" title="Tree Swallow">Tree Swallow</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_Starling" title="European Starling" class="mw-redirect">European Starling</a> eggs have been found mixed with goldeneye eggs! The broods commonly start to mix with other females' broods as they become more independent. Goldeneye young have been known to be competitively killed by other goldeneye mothers, <a href="/wiki/Common_Loon" title="Common Loon" class="mw-redirect">Common Loons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Red-necked_Grebe" title="Red-necked Grebe">Red-necked Grebes</a>. The young are capable of flight at 55–65 days of age.</p>

<p>These diving birds forage underwater. Year-round, about 32% of their prey is <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, 28% is aquatic <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and 10% is <a href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc" class="mw-redirect">molluscs</a>. <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">Insects</a> are the predominant prey while nesting and <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> are the predominant prey during migration and winter. Locally, <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> eggs and aquatic <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> can be important foods. They themselves may fall prey to various <a href="/wiki/Hawk" title="Hawk">hawks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a>, while females and their broods have been preyed upon by <a href="/wiki/Bear" title="Bear">bears</a> (<i>Ursus</i> spp.), various <a href="/wiki/Weasel" title="Weasel">weasels</a> (<i>Mustela</i> spp.), <a href="/wiki/Mink" title="Mink">mink</a> (<i>Mustela vison</i>), <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">raccoons</a> (<i>Procyon lotor</i>) and even <a href="/wiki/Northern_Flicker" title="Northern Flicker">Northern Flickers</a> (<i>Colaptes auratus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Red_squirrel" title="Red squirrel">red squirrels</a> (<i>Tamiasciurus husonicus</i>).</p>
<p>The Common Goldeneye is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>
<p>Approximately 188 300 Common Goldeneyes were killed by <a href="/wiki/Duck_hunter" title="Duck hunter" class="mw-redirect">duck hunters</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> during the 1970s, representing about 4% of the total number of ducks killed in the region during that period<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from February 2007">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The rate is probably similar today. Both the breeding and winter habitat of these birds has been degraded by clearance and pollution. However, this is the only duck in North America known to derive short-term benefits from lake acidification.</p>
