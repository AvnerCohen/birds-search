<p><b>Montagu's harrier </b>(<i>Circus pygargus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Harrier_(bird)" title="Harrier (bird)">harrier</a> family. Its common name commemorates the British <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">naturalist</a> <a href="/wiki/George_Montagu_(naturalist)" title="George Montagu (naturalist)">George Montagu</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montagu%27s_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">formal description</a> of Montagu's harrier was by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">tenth edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> under the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Falco pygargus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Circus_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Circus (bird)">Circus</a></i> was introduced by the French naturalist <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Germain_de_Lac%C3%A9p%C3%A8de" title="Bernard Germain de Lacépède">Bernard Germain de Lacépède</a> in 1799.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  The genus name is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>. <i>Circus</i> is from <i>kirkos</i>, referring to a bird of prey named for its circling flight (<i>kirkos</i>, "circle"), probably the <a href="/wiki/Hen_harrier" title="Hen harrier">hen harrier</a>, and <i>pygargus</i> is <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">Modern Latin</a> derived from Greek <i>pugargos</i>, from <i>puge</i>,"rump" and <i>argos</i>, "shining white". The species name was formerly used for the hen harrier before Montagu's was identified as a different species.<sup id="cite_ref-Jobling_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jobling-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identification">Identification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montagu%27s_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Identification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plumage">Plumage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montagu%27s_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Plumage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">Sexual dimorphism</a> is particularly apparent in the <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> of this species. Adult males are characterized by their overall pale grey plumage contrasting with black wingtips. Compared with other harriers this species has characteristic black bands along the secondaries, both above and below the wing and rusty streaks on belly and flanks. Adult females have a broadly similar plumage to that of <a href="/wiki/Pallid_harrier" title="Pallid harrier">pallid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hen_harrier" title="Hen harrier">hen harriers</a>. The underparts are mostly pale yellow-brown, the belly with longitudinal stripes and the wing coverts spotted. The upper parts are uniform dark brown except for the white upper tail coverts ("rump"), and the sightly paler central wing coverts.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Juvenile_(organism)" title="Juvenile (organism)">juvenile</a> plumage resembles that of the female, but differs by the belly and under wing coverts which are not spotted, but uniformly red brown in colour.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Melanism" title="Melanism">melanistic</a> form occurs regularly in this species. In this form the male is much darker than usual, with a black head, brownish black above and grey underparts. The melanistic female is entirely chocolate brown except for grey flight feathers. Partially melanistic morphs can also be found.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight">Flight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montagu%27s_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Flight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Montagu's harrier has a particularly graceful flight, with powerful and elegant wingbeats which give an impression of buoyancy and ease. In true harrier fashion it searches the countryside, flying low, and generally holds its wings with a marked positive <a href="/wiki/Dihedral_(aircraft)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dihedral (aircraft)">dihedral</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Montagu%27s_harrier_(Circus_pygargus)_grounded.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Montagu%27s_harrier_%28Circus_pygargus%29_grounded.jpg/220px-Montagu%27s_harrier_%28Circus_pygargus%29_grounded.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Montagu%27s_harrier_%28Circus_pygargus%29_grounded.jpg/330px-Montagu%27s_harrier_%28Circus_pygargus%29_grounded.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Montagu%27s_harrier_%28Circus_pygargus%29_grounded.jpg/440px-Montagu%27s_harrier_%28Circus_pygargus%29_grounded.jpg 2x" data-file-width="664" data-file-height="634" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Montagu%27s_harrier_(Circus_pygargus)_grounded.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Montagu's harrier (Circus pygargus) from Tal Chappar</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dimensions">Dimensions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montagu%27s_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dimensions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Montagu's harrier is a deceptively small raptor, though it appears larger because of its large wing surface compared to small body weight, which gives it a typically buoyant flight. The female is larger than the male because the female needs to produce eggs, however this is not apparent in the field.
</p>
<ul><li>Wingspan: 97–115&#160;cm (38–45&#160;in)</li>
<li>Length: 43–47&#160;cm (17–19&#160;in) (tail: 16–18&#160;cm (6.3–7.1&#160;in))</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Body_weight" class="mw-redirect" title="Body weight">Weight</a> (average):
<ul><li>Male: 265&#160;g (9.3&#160;oz)</li>
<li>Female: 345&#160;g (12.2&#160;oz)</li></ul></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Risk_of_confusion">Risk of confusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montagu%27s_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Risk of confusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Montagu's harrier can be confused with several species that exist within the same range. The most similar are the <a href="/wiki/Hen_harrier" title="Hen harrier">hen harrier</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pallid_harrier" title="Pallid harrier">pallid harrier</a>. The male is easily distinguished from other species as its plumage is distinctly darker and more mottled than in the males of hen or pallid harriers. However, distinguishing females and juveniles is more difficult. Usually the Montagu's harrier appears more slender in flight than the hen harrier with a longer tail, longer and narrower wings and more pointed "hands". Also its flight is more elegant than the hen harrier, with more elastic, almost tern-like wingbeats. The distinction between female pallid and Montagu's harriers is the most delicate and can only be made in good conditions as the proportions are similar. The best recognition character is the pale collar around the neck of female and juvenile pallid harriers which is not present in the Montagu's.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montagu%27s_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species can be found in a middle-latitude band of predominantly <a href="/wiki/Temperate" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate">temperate climates</a>, but also in <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">Mediterranean</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Boreal_climate" class="mw-redirect" title="Boreal climate">boreal</a> zones. Although it has been found nesting up to 1,500&#160;m (4,900&#160;ft), it is essentially a lowland species, and nests mostly in broad river valleys, plains, and levels bordering lakes and the sea. It can breed in <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a>, though these are often smaller and drier than those used by the <a href="/wiki/Marsh_harrier" title="Marsh harrier">marsh harrier</a>. It also utilizes <a href="/wiki/Heath_(habitat)" class="mw-redirect" title="Heath (habitat)">heaths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dunes" class="mw-redirect" title="Dunes">dunes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moors</a>, and can be found in the <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a>. It adapts to <a href="/wiki/Shrubland" title="Shrubland">shrublands</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gorse" class="mw-redirect" title="Gorse">gorse</a> or <a href="/wiki/Calluna" title="Calluna">heather</a> and to areas planted with young <a href="/wiki/Conifer" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifer">conifers</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Montagu%27s_Harrier,_juvenile,_Bangalore,_India.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Montagu%27s_Harrier%2C_juvenile%2C_Bangalore%2C_India.jpg/220px-Montagu%27s_Harrier%2C_juvenile%2C_Bangalore%2C_India.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Montagu%27s_Harrier%2C_juvenile%2C_Bangalore%2C_India.jpg/330px-Montagu%27s_Harrier%2C_juvenile%2C_Bangalore%2C_India.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Montagu%27s_Harrier%2C_juvenile%2C_Bangalore%2C_India.jpg/440px-Montagu%27s_Harrier%2C_juvenile%2C_Bangalore%2C_India.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Montagu%27s_Harrier,_juvenile,_Bangalore,_India.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult Female</div></div></div>
<p>When no other suitable habitat is available this harrier will nest in agricultural farmlands where it is vulnerable to early harvesting. Amongst these it chooses especially grasslands and <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereal</a> crops such as <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oats" class="mw-redirect" title="Oats">oats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Colza" class="mw-redirect" title="Colza">colza</a>. In western Europe, up to 70% of the population breeds in artificial habitats.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Montagu%27s_Harrier-_Male.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Montagu%27s_Harrier-_Male.JPG/220px-Montagu%27s_Harrier-_Male.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Montagu%27s_Harrier-_Male.JPG/330px-Montagu%27s_Harrier-_Male.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Montagu%27s_Harrier-_Male.JPG/440px-Montagu%27s_Harrier-_Male.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="720" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Montagu%27s_Harrier-_Male.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult Male</div></div></div>
<p>In short, for breeding the Montagu's harrier requires a large open area, with sufficiently tall ground vegetation to afford cover without being overgrown. It favours posts on which both male and female can rest and survey the breeding area: these can be fenceposts, small trees, or rocky outcrops. When hunting, in any season, it prefers areas of low or sparse vegetation where prey is more visible. Densely settled areas are generally avoided and it is highly susceptible to disturbance.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_foraging_methods">Food and foraging methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montagu%27s_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Food and foraging methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The diet consists mainly of small <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, bird eggs, reptiles (including <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a>) and large <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> (mainly Orthoptera, which are numerically the most common).<sup id="cite_ref-Arroyo1997_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arroyo1997-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As this bird has a wide distribution, it will take whatever prey is available in the area where it nests; in the northern range it will mainly take <a href="/wiki/Ground_squirrel" title="Ground squirrel">ground squirrels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a>, whereas in southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, it mostly takes small <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a> and large <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>. In areas where the food supply is composed almost exclusively of <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>, the breeding success depends greatly on the cyclic fluctuations of <a href="/wiki/Vole" title="Vole">vole</a> populations.<sup id="cite_ref-Salamolard2000_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salamolard2000-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> At times they <a href="/wiki/Regurgitation_(digestion)" title="Regurgitation (digestion)">regurgitate</a>.
</p><p>Prey is caught while flying along fixed routes at low heights and constant low speeds (c. 30&#160;km/h (19&#160;mph)), as is typical of harriers. The flight is considered lighter and more dexterous than other harriers enabling it to take more agile prey. When possible it often follows the edges of various vegetation to catch its prey by surprise. This is taken after a short stoop, though fast running animals and flying birds can be chased over a short distance. 
</p><p>During the breeding season, the male will provision the female and later the young with food. The rate of provisioning increases from 5 to 6 times per day during incubation to 7 to 10 times per day when young have hatched, though the male can be handicapped by wet, foggy or windy weather. In a manner typical of harriers, prey is passed between partners in the air: The female flies underneath the male, who drops the prey for her to catch. The male hunts over a large area up to 12&#160;km (7.5&#160;mi) away from the nest. The female hunts closer to the nest, up to 1&#160;km (0.62&#160;mi) away, and only after the young have hatched.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_and_distribution">Status and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montagu%27s_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Status and distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species can still be found throughout most of the Western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>. In most <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> countries there is at least a small population, except in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> where it is not present. The breeding range extends as far east as the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Urals</a>, whereas the most western population is that of <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>. Breeding also occurs in northern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, mostly in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, the species is limited to southern England. In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> the species is rarely seen, and mainly in the South, although there are a number of breeding records, the most recent from 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-irishbirding_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irishbirding-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Despite having a wide distribution, this bird is not common in many areas and has strong populations only in France, Spain, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> where the greater part of the European population can be found. Breeding sites frequently change, with some sporadic nesting occurring outside known breeding areas, however clear signs of reduced range are apparent and are associated with population decline.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_in_Britain">Status in Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montagu%27s_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Status in Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Montagu's harrier is a rare breeding bird in Britain. There are two breeding areas – the area surrounding <a href="/wiki/The_Wash" title="The Wash">The Wash</a>, and downland areas of southern England, from <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a> north to <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">Oxfordshire</a>. Away from these areas it occurs only as a scarce migrant. One site, Estuary Farm, near North Wootton in west Norfolk, a special observation area was negotiated with local landowners, so that pressure could be taken off other nesting pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-Evans1996_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Evans1996-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In 2005, a pair bred on the <a href="/wiki/Holkham" title="Holkham">Holkham</a> estate.<sup id="cite_ref-holkham2006_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holkham2006-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In 2015, it was reported that some Montagu's harrier were nesting at <a href="/wiki/Blacktoft_Sands" class="mw-redirect" title="Blacktoft Sands">Blacktoft Sands</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="East Riding of Yorkshire">East Riding of Yorkshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_trends">Population trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montagu%27s_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Population trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The population for the western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> is estimated at 35,000–50,000 pairs. The global population is unknown and could be anything between 150,000 and 200,000 individuals(Birdlife International, 2004). This uncertainty is due to the fact that most of the world's population is situated in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Former_Soviet_republics" class="mw-redirect" title="Former Soviet republics">former Soviet republics</a> where it is not quantified.
</p><p>The evolution has been paradoxical throughout the 20th century. In the beginning of the century up to the 1940s, during a period when other raptors greatly decreased because of human persecution, the Montagu's harrier actually increased its population and breeding range, breeding for the first time in <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> in the 1900s, and greatly increasing elsewhere. However, from the 1940s onwards it has decreased rapidly. This is due to several negative factors: first, the massive use of agricultural pesticides such as <a href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a> and other environmental poisons was extremely detrimental to the harriers themselves, as well as rarefying their prey, in particular large <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>. The modification of agricultural practises, with an evolution towards more intensive farming also puts pressure on harriers, with faster growing crops preventing those birds that nest in farmlands from finishing their nidification before their clutches are destroyed by harvesting machines. Despite a generally negative trend there are local cases when the population has increased, such as in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> or in Germany<sup id="cite_ref-Belting_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Belting-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> in the 1990s. These local trends show that while they have access to suitable habitats and food supply there can still be positive developments.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_behaviour_and_breeding">Social behaviour and breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montagu%27s_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Social behaviour and breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It can be both solitary and gregarious at times, both during the breeding season and in winter quarters. A breeding pair may associate with others to form loose <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonies</a>, with as many as 30 nests in the same area, sometimes as close as 10&#160;m (33&#160;ft) apart. Semi-colonial nesting is not due to a shortage of nesting sites, but arises rather from the need to provide a better defence against predators.<sup id="cite_ref-Arroyo2001_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arroyo2001-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The actual area defended by both partners covers only 300–400&#160;m (980–1,310&#160;ft) around the nest, and in case of colonial nesting the response to predators may be <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">communal</a>. Other species attacked and mobbed include large <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corvidae" title="Corvidae">corvids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">foxes</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Circus_pygargus_MWNH_0857.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Circus_pygargus_MWNH_0857.JPG/220px-Circus_pygargus_MWNH_0857.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Circus_pygargus_MWNH_0857.JPG/330px-Circus_pygargus_MWNH_0857.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Circus_pygargus_MWNH_0857.JPG/440px-Circus_pygargus_MWNH_0857.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2425" data-file-height="1667" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Circus_pygargus_MWNH_0857.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>Reproduction begins with the return of both partners to the nesting site, at which point both male and female will start displaying. The display consists of various sky-dances and aerobatic figures that vary according to each individual. Both sexes will display, crying loudly, though the males' displays are more frequent and spectacular. Montagu's harriers breed for the first time when two or three years old, but occasionally one-year-old females may attempt to nest. Pairs form on the territory, when returning from migration. As the birds are tied to their former nesting sites, they probably mate with the same partner every year. The nest is built by the female, always in tall vegetation. It is a simple construction made of grass, used only for one season. The female lays 3 to 5 eggs which are incubated for 27–40 days. The young leave the nest after 28–42 days and are independent two weeks later.
</p><p>The males may be <a href="/wiki/Polygamous" class="mw-redirect" title="Polygamous">polygamous</a>, then having to feed two females and later two broods, either simultaneously or consecutively.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration">Migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montagu%27s_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Montagu's harrier is a long distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>. Birds from <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> spend the winter in sub-<a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Saharan</a> <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, while those from the eastern part of the range migrate to the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>. In Europe, the first birds start to move at the beginning of August and most have left by mid-October. They travel over a broad front, crossing the Mediterranean at various points, and only a small number are observed at migration choke points. Western birds don't go further south than the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Guinea" title="Gulf of Guinea">gulf of Guinea</a>, but some eastern birds travel as far as <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>. In Africa, their diet is composed mostly of insects and birds, and it is possible that they follow <a href="/wiki/Locust" title="Locust">locust</a> swarms.<sup id="cite_ref-grauwekiekendief_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grauwekiekendief-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Youngmonties.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Youngmonties.jpg/220px-Youngmonties.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Youngmonties.jpg/330px-Youngmonties.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Youngmonties.jpg/440px-Youngmonties.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="750" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Youngmonties.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Young Montagu's harriers during relocation.</div></div></div>
<p>Spring return peaks in April, and most birds have arrived by May though there is evidence that first-year juveniles spend their first summer in the winter quarters
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_issues">Conservation issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montagu%27s_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Conservation issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In western Europe, an estimated 70% of breeding pairs nest in agricultural farmlands, especially cereal crops. This makes the Montagu's harrier a very vulnerable species, and very dependent on nest protection. Bird protection <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">non-governmental organizations</a> participate in their protection, in collaboration with concerned landowners. Once a nest is spotted in a field, it can be safeguarded either by relocating it to a safer area or by creating a protected space which will not be harvested.<sup id="cite_ref-grauwekiekendief2_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grauwekiekendief2-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In France and in the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>, an average 60% of nestlings are saved by this kind of measures.<sup id="cite_ref-Arroyo2002_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arroyo2002-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
