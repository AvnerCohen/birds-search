<p>The <b>demoiselle crane</b> (<i>Grus virgo</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Crane_(bird)" title="Crane (bird)">crane</a> found in central <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurosiberia</a>, ranging from the Black Sea to Mongolia and North Eastern China. There is also a small breeding population in Turkey. These cranes are migratory birds. Birds from western Eurasia will spend the winter in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> whilst the birds from Asia, Mongolia and China will spend the winter in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>. The bird is symbolically significant in the culture of India and Pakistan, where it is known as <i>Koonj</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ref67xemux_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ref67xemux-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demoiselle_crane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Demoiselle_Crane_Monochrome.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Demoiselle_Crane_Monochrome.jpg/170px-Demoiselle_Crane_Monochrome.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Demoiselle_Crane_Monochrome.jpg/255px-Demoiselle_Crane_Monochrome.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Demoiselle_Crane_Monochrome.jpg/340px-Demoiselle_Crane_Monochrome.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1365" data-file-height="2048" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Demoiselle_Crane_Monochrome.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Individual from <a href="/wiki/Tal_Chhapar_Sanctuary" title="Tal Chhapar Sanctuary">Tal Chhapar Sanctuary</a>, Churu, <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a></div></div></div>
<p>The demoiselle is 85–100&#160;cm (33.5–39.5&#160;in) long, 76&#160;cm (30&#160;in) tall and has a 155–180&#160;cm (61–71&#160;in) wingspan. It weighs 2–3&#160;kg (4.4–6.6&#160;lb). It is the smallest species of crane.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The demoiselle crane is slightly smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Common_crane" title="Common crane">common crane</a> but has similar <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>. It has a long white neck stripe and the black on the foreneck extends down over the chest in a plume.
</p><p>It has a loud <a href="/wiki/Bird_song" class="mw-redirect" title="Bird song">trumpeting call</a>, higher-pitched than the common crane. Like other cranes it has a dancing display, more balletic than the common crane, with less leaping.
</p><p>The demoiselle was so named by Queen Marie Antoinette, for its delicate and maiden-like appearance.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demoiselle_crane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anthropoides_virgo_MWNH_0033.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Anthropoides_virgo_MWNH_0033.JPG/170px-Anthropoides_virgo_MWNH_0033.JPG" decoding="async" width="170" height="243" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Anthropoides_virgo_MWNH_0033.JPG/255px-Anthropoides_virgo_MWNH_0033.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Anthropoides_virgo_MWNH_0033.JPG/340px-Anthropoides_virgo_MWNH_0033.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2848" data-file-height="4070" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anthropoides_virgo_MWNH_0033.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>The demoiselle crane lives in a variety of different environments, including desert areas and numerous types of grasslands (flooded, mountain, temperate and tropical grassland) which are often within a few hundred metres of streams or lakes. However, when nesting, they prefer patchy areas of vegetation which is tall enough to conceal them and their nests, yet short enough to allow them look out for predators whilst incubating their eggs.
</p><p>Demoiselle cranes have to take one of the toughest <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrations</a> in the world. In late August through September, they gather in flocks of up to 400 individuals and prepare for their flight to their winter range. During their migratory flight south, demoiselles fly like all cranes, with their head and neck straight forward and their feet and legs straight behind, reaching altitudes of 16,000–26,000 feet (4,900–7,900 metres). Along their arduous journey they have to cross the <a href="/wiki/Himalayan_mountains" class="mw-redirect" title="Himalayan mountains">Himalayan mountains</a> to get to their over-wintering grounds in India. Many die from fatigue, hunger and <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predation</a> from <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">golden eagles</a>. Simpler, lower routes are possible, such as crossing the range via the <a href="/wiki/Khyber_Pass" title="Khyber Pass">Khyber Pass</a>. However, their presently preferred route has been hard-wired by countless cycles of migration. At their wintering grounds, demoiselles have been observed flocking with common cranes, their combined totals reaching up to 20,000 individuals. Demoiselles maintain separate social groups within the larger flock. In March and April, they begin their long spring journey back to their northern nesting grounds.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Khichan" title="Khichan">Khichan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a> in India, villagers feed the cranes on their migration and these large congregations have become an annual spectacle.
</p><p>The demoiselle crane is evaluated as Least Concern on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> of Threatened Species. It is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demoiselle_crane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Demoiselle_Cranes_(_Grus_virgo).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/The_Demoiselle_Cranes_%28_Grus_virgo%29.jpg/220px-The_Demoiselle_Cranes_%28_Grus_virgo%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/The_Demoiselle_Cranes_%28_Grus_virgo%29.jpg/330px-The_Demoiselle_Cranes_%28_Grus_virgo%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/The_Demoiselle_Cranes_%28_Grus_virgo%29.jpg/440px-The_Demoiselle_Cranes_%28_Grus_virgo%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3508" data-file-height="1973" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Demoiselle_Cranes_(_Grus_virgo).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Demoiselle cranes (<i>Grus virgo</i>) in Jodhpur Bishnoi's Village, Rajasthan, India</div></div></div>
<p>The demoiselle crane is known as the <i>Koonj</i> /Kurjan(<a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">कूंज</a>,कुरजां <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">کونج</a>, <a href="/wiki/Punjabi_language" title="Punjabi language">ਕੂੰਜ</a>) in the languages of <a href="/wiki/North_India" title="North India">North India</a>, and figure prominently in the literature, poetry and idiom of the region. Beautiful women are often compared to the <i>koonj</i> because its long and thin shape is considered graceful. Metaphorical references are also often made to the <i>koonj</i> for people who have ventured far from home or undertaken hazardous journeys.<sup id="cite_ref-ref64fixaz_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ref64fixaz-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BMehta-Demoiselle_Crane.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/BMehta-Demoiselle_Crane.jpg/220px-BMehta-Demoiselle_Crane.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/BMehta-Demoiselle_Crane.jpg/330px-BMehta-Demoiselle_Crane.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/BMehta-Demoiselle_Crane.jpg/440px-BMehta-Demoiselle_Crane.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1616" data-file-height="1080" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BMehta-Demoiselle_Crane.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Demoiselle cranes at Khichan, Rajasthan India</div></div></div>
<p>The name <i>koonj</i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> word <i>kraunch</i>, which is a <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognate</a> <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a> term for <i>crane</i> itself.<sup id="cite_ref-ref67xemux_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ref67xemux-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In the mythology of <a href="/wiki/Valmiki" title="Valmiki">Valmiki</a>, the composer of the <a href="/wiki/Hindu" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> epic <a href="/wiki/Ramayana" title="Ramayana">Ramayana</a>, it is claimed that his first verse was inspired by the sight of a hunter kill the male of a pair of demoiselle<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> cranes that were courting. Observing the lovelorn female circling and crying in grief, he cursed the hunter in verse. Since tradition held that all poetry prior to this moment had been revealed rather than created by man, this verse concerning the demoiselle cranes is regarded as the first human-composed meter.<sup id="cite_ref-ref56hoceg_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ref56hoceg-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (July 2013)">dubious</span></a>&#32;<span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Demoiselle_crane#Dubious" title="Talk:Demoiselle crane">discuss</a></span></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The flying formation of the <i>koonj</i> during migrations also inspired <a href="/wiki/Tactical_formation" title="Tactical formation">infantry formations</a> in ancient India. The <a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a> epic describes both warring sides adopting the <i>koonj</i> formation on the second day of the <a href="/wiki/Kurukshetra_War" title="Kurukshetra War">Kurukshetra War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ref86vicuv_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ref86vicuv-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
