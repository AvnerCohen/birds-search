<p>The <b>lesser black-backed gull</b> (<i>Larus fuscus</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a> that breeds on the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> coasts of Europe. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering from the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> south to West Africa. It is a regular winter visitor to the east coast of North America, probably from the breeding population in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_black-backed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lesser black-backed gull was one of the many species originally described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a>, and it still bears its original name <i>Larus fuscus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Linnaeus1758_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linnaeus1758-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The scientific name is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>. <i>Larus</i> appears to have referred to a gull or other large seabird, and <i>fuscus</i> meant black or brown.<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_black-backed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The five recognized <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are:
</p>
<ul><li><i>L. f. graellsii</i> <small><a href="/wiki/Alfred_Brehm" title="Alfred Brehm">Brehm</a>, 1857</small>: <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, western Europe - mantle dark grey</li>
<li><i>L. f. intermedius</i> <small>Schiøler, 1922</small>: <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, southwest <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> - mantle sooty black</li>
<li><i>L. f. fuscus</i> <small><a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, 1758</small>: northern Norway, Sweden and <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> to the <a href="/wiki/White_Sea" title="White Sea">White Sea</a> - mantle jet black</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Heuglin%27s_gull" title="Heuglin&#39;s gull">L. f. heuglini</a></i> <small>Bree, 1876</small>: northern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> to north-central <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, known as Heuglin's gull, this was previously considered a separate species.</li>
<li><i>L. f. barabensis</i> <small><a href="/wiki/Hans_Johansen" title="Hans Johansen">Johansen</a>, 1960</small>: <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">central Asia</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_black-backed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lesser black-backed gull is smaller than the <a href="/wiki/European_herring_gull" title="European herring gull">European herring gull</a>. The taxonomy of the herring gull / lesser black-backed gull complex is very complicated; different authorities recognise between two and eight species. This group has a <a href="/wiki/Ring_species" title="Ring species">ring species</a> distribution around the Northern Hemisphere. Differences between adjacent forms in this ring are fairly small, but by the time the circuit is completed, the end members, herring gull and lesser black-backed gull, are clearly different species. The lesser black-backed gull measures 51–64&#160;cm (20–25&#160;in), 124–150&#160;cm (49–59&#160;in) across the wings, and weighs 452–1,100&#160;g (0.996–2.425&#160;lb), with the nominate race averaging slightly smaller than the other two subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-AAB_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAB-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Males, at an average weight of 824&#160;g (1.817&#160;lb), are slightly larger than females, at an average of 708&#160;g (1.561&#160;lb). Among standard measurements, the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> is 38 to 45&#160;cm (15 to 18&#160;in), the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> is 4.2 to 5.8&#160;cm (1.7 to 2.3&#160;in), and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> is 5.2 to 6.9&#160;cm (2.0 to 2.7&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen2004_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen2004-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Harrison1991_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison1991-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CRC_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> A confusable species is the <a href="/wiki/Great_black-backed_gull" title="Great black-backed gull">great black-backed gull</a>. The lesser is a much smaller bird, with slimmer build, yellow rather than pinkish legs, and smaller white "mirrors" at the wing tips. The adults have black or dark grey wings (depending on race) and back. The bill is yellow with a red spot at which the young peck, inducing feeding (see <a href="/wiki/Fixed_action_pattern" title="Fixed action pattern">fixed action pattern</a>). The head is greyer in winter, unlike great black-backed gulls. Annual moult for adults begins between May and August and is not complete on some birds until November. Partial prebreeding moult occurs between January and April.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Young birds have scaly black-brown upperparts and a neat wing pattern. They take four years to reach maturity. Identification from juvenile herring gulls is most readily done by the more solidly dark (unbarred) tertial feathers.
</p><p>Their call is a "laughing" cry like that of the herring gull , but with a markedly deeper pitch.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_black-backed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species breeds colonially on coasts and lakes, making a lined nest on the ground or a cliff. Normally, three eggs are laid. In some cities, the species nests within the urban environment, often in association with herring gulls.<sup id="cite_ref-thisbrighton_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thisbrighton-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_black-backed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>They are omnivores like most <i>Larus</i> gulls, and they eat fish, insects, crustaceans, worms, starfish, molluscs, seeds, berries, small mammals, eggs, small birds, chicks, scraps, offal, and carrion.
</p><p><br />
</p>
