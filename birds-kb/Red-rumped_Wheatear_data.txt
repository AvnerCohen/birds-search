<p>The <b>red-rumped wheatear</b> or <b>buff-rumped wheatear</b> (<i>Oenanthe moesta</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Muscicapidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Muscicapidae">Muscicapidae</a>. It is found in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-rumped_wheatear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The red-rumped wheatear is a compact, big-headed wheatear with a rufous rump and all dark tail. the male has a grey crown and nape with a black throat and face and white supercilium. The shoulders and back are black with white fringes to the feathers, the rump and base of the tail are rufous and the distal part of the tail is black. Female is paler with a rufous crown and cheeks. Juveniles are similar to females but are less rufous and do show faint spots and streaks. Length is 16&#160;cm. The fly low to the ground with a loose flapping flight that resembles a skylark.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Beaman_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beaman-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-rumped_wheatear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The territorial song, given by both sexes, seems to vary geographically from a harsher chattering song in the west to a more melodious. pleasant and repetitive "<i>twee-churr-urr-urr</i>" in <a href="/wiki/Cyrenaica" title="Cyrenaica">Cyrenaica</a>. The courtship song sounds like an old fashioned boiling kettle, a long warbling whistle rising in pitch given in duet by both sexes.  The alarm and contact calls are typically chat like harsh clicks.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_subspecies">Distribution and subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-rumped_wheatear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are two recognised subspecies of Red-rumped wheatear, they are:<sup id="cite_ref-ITIS_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITIS-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>Oenanthe moesta brooksbanki</i> <small>Meinertzhagen, 1923</small> - southern <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a> to north-western <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> and south-western <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Avibase_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avibase-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Oenanthe moesta moesta</i> <small>(Lichtenstein, 1823)</small> = North Africa from extreme north of <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a> to coastal north-west <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Avibase_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avibase-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-rumped_wheatear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The red-rumped wheatear avoids  real deserts and is normally found in flat areas, especially near saline or barren areas. In <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> uses more vegetated areas than the <a href="/wiki/Desert_wheatear" title="Desert wheatear">desert wheatear</a> and also found in rocky hills.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habits">Habits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-rumped_wheatear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Habits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The red-rumped wheatear has a prolonged breeding season from late February through to June in North Africa, and it may have up to three broods. The nest is usually placed in a small mammal's burrow, up to 2m underground, or sometimes in a natural hole or a cavity in a wall and sometimes hidden under a bush. The nest is a cup made out of plant material and lined with feathers, woo, and hair, and even shed snakeskin. The clutch is 4-5 but very little is known about the breeding behaviour of this species.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
