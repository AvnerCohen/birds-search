<p>The <b>Eurasian reed warbler</b>, or just <b>reed warbler</b> (<i>Acrocephalus scirpaceus</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">Old World warbler</a> in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Acrocephalus_(bird)" title="Acrocephalus (bird)">Acrocephalus</a></i>. It breeds across <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> into the temperate western <a href="/wiki/Palaearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palaearctic">Palaearctic</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in sub-<a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Saharan</a> <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_reed_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The genus name <i>Acrocephalus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>akros</i>, "highest", and <i>kephale</i>, "head". It is possible that <a href="/wiki/Johann_Andreas_Naumann" title="Johann Andreas Naumann">Naumann</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Naumann" title="Johann Friedrich Naumann">Naumann</a> thought <i>akros</i> meant "sharp-pointed". The specific <i>scirpaceus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means "reed".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An older scientific name for the reed warbler was <i>Acrocephalus streperus</i> (Vieill.).<sup id="cite_ref-streperus_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-streperus-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_population_density">Habitat and population density</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_reed_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habitat and population density">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> is a species found almost exclusively in <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed beds</a>, usually with some bushes.  Direct counts of territorial males in suitable habitat and sampling the population sex-ratio can be a proper alternative to inference-rich predictive modeling based on imperfect habitat-extrapolation of densities of reed warblers at large spatial scales.<sup id="cite_ref-Frías_2018_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frías_2018-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_reed_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is a medium-sized warbler, <span class="nowrap">12.5–14 cm</span> in length. The adult has an unstreaked brown back and buff underparts. The forehead is flattened, and the bill is strong and pointed. The sexes are identical, as with most warblers, but young birds are richer buff below. The Eurasian reed warbler looks similar to the <a href="/wiki/Great_reed_warbler" title="Great reed warbler">great reed warbler</a>, but the great reed warbler is larger in size and has a stronger <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Song">Song</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_reed_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Song">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The song is a slow, chattering <i>jit-jit-jit</i> with typically acrocephaline whistles and mimicry added.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Food">Food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_reed_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like most warblers, it is <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a>, but will take other small food items, including <a href="/wiki/Berry" title="Berry">berries</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nesting">Nesting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_reed_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nesting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 3–5 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> are laid in a basket nest in <a href="/wiki/Phragmites" title="Phragmites">reeds</a>. The chicks fledge after 10 or 11 days. This species is usually <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" class="mw-redirect" title="Monogamous">monogamous</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The Eurasian reed warbler is one of the species that are <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasitism" class="mw-redirect" title="Brood parasitism">brood parasitised</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common cuckoo</a>.
</p>
