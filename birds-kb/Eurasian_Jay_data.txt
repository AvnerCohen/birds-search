<p>The <b>Eurasian jay</b> (<i>Garrulus glandarius</i>) is a species of bird occurring over a vast region from western Europe and north-west Africa to the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> and further to the eastern seaboard of Asia and down into south-east Asia. Across its vast <a href="/wiki/Range_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Range (biology)">range</a>, several very distinct racial forms have evolved to look very different from each other, especially when forms at the extremes of its range are compared.
</p><p>The bird is called <b><a href="/wiki/Jay" title="Jay">jay</a></b>, without any <a href="/wiki/Epithet" title="Epithet">epithets</a>, by English speakers in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_jay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_Jay_Dugalbitta_Chamoli_Uttarakhand_India_13.06.2013.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Eurasian_Jay_Dugalbitta_Chamoli_Uttarakhand_India_13.06.2013.jpg/220px-Eurasian_Jay_Dugalbitta_Chamoli_Uttarakhand_India_13.06.2013.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Eurasian_Jay_Dugalbitta_Chamoli_Uttarakhand_India_13.06.2013.jpg/330px-Eurasian_Jay_Dugalbitta_Chamoli_Uttarakhand_India_13.06.2013.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Eurasian_Jay_Dugalbitta_Chamoli_Uttarakhand_India_13.06.2013.jpg/440px-Eurasian_Jay_Dugalbitta_Chamoli_Uttarakhand_India_13.06.2013.jpg 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_Jay_Dugalbitta_Chamoli_Uttarakhand_India_13.06.2013.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Garrulus glandarius bispecularis</i> from <a href="/wiki/Uttarakhand" title="Uttarakhand">Uttarakhand</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a></div></div></div>
<p>The Eurasian jay was one of the many species originally described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his landmark 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a>. He recognised its affinity with other corvids, naming it <i>Corvus glandarius</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Linnaeus1758_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linnaeus1758-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The current scientific name is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>; <i>garrulus</i> means noisy or chattering, and <i>glandarius</i> is "of acorns", a favoured food.<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_jay_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Eurasian_jay_1.jpg/220px-Eurasian_jay_1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Eurasian_jay_1.jpg/330px-Eurasian_jay_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Eurasian_jay_1.jpg/440px-Eurasian_jay_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3936" data-file-height="2624" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_jay_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A typical example of the <i>atricapillus</i> group in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a></div></div></div>
<p>Eight racial groups (33 <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> in total) are recognised by Madge &amp; Burn (1994):<sup id="cite_ref-Madge1994_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge1994-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jena_Phyletic_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="Jena Phyletic Museum">Jena Phyletic Museum</a>, in Germany, features an excellent display of plumage variations across these races, which is used as a particularly striking example of the variation that can be found within species.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Garrulus_glandarius_brandtii_Stuffed_specimen.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Garrulus_glandarius_brandtii_Stuffed_specimen.jpg/220px-Garrulus_glandarius_brandtii_Stuffed_specimen.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Garrulus_glandarius_brandtii_Stuffed_specimen.jpg/330px-Garrulus_glandarius_brandtii_Stuffed_specimen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Garrulus_glandarius_brandtii_Stuffed_specimen.jpg/440px-Garrulus_glandarius_brandtii_Stuffed_specimen.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3200" data-file-height="2400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Garrulus_glandarius_brandtii_Stuffed_specimen.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Garrulus glandarius brandtii</i></div></div></div>
<ul><li>the nominate group (nine European races), with a streaked <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_(plumage)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Crown (plumage) (page does not exist)">crown</a>.</li>
<li>the <i>cervicalis</i> group (three races in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>), with a rufous <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nape_(plumage)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nape (plumage) (page does not exist)">nape</a>, grey <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mantle_(plumage)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mantle (plumage) (page does not exist)">mantle</a>, very pale head sides, and a streaked or black crown.</li>
<li>the <i>atricapillus</i> group (four races in <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>), with a uniform mantle &amp; nape, black crown and very pale face.</li>
<li>the race <i>hyrcanus</i> (<a href="/wiki/Caspian_Hyrcanian_mixed_forests" title="Caspian Hyrcanian mixed forests">Caspian Hyrcanian mixed forests</a> of <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>), small with black forecrown and broadly streaked hindcrown.</li>
<li>the <i>brandtii</i> group (four races in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>), with a streaked crown, reddish head, dark <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">iris</a> and grey mantle.</li>
<li>the <i>leucotis</i> group (two races in <a href="/wiki/South-east_Asia" class="mw-redirect" title="South-east Asia">south-east Asia</a>), with no white in the wing, a white forecrown, black hindcrown and much white on the sides of the head.</li>
<li>the <i>bispecularis</i> group (six races in the <a href="/wiki/Himalaya" class="mw-redirect" title="Himalaya">Himalayan</a> region), with an unstreaked rufous crown, and no white wing-patch.</li>
<li>the <i>japonicus</i> group (four races in the southern Japanese islands), with a large white wing-patch, blackish face and scaled crown.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_jay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A member of the widespread <a href="/wiki/Jay" title="Jay">jay</a> group, and about the size of the <a href="/wiki/Jackdaw" class="mw-redirect" title="Jackdaw">jackdaw</a>, it inhabits mixed <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodland</a>, particularly with <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oaks</a>, and is a habitual <a href="/wiki/Acorn" title="Acorn">acorn</a> hoarder. In recent years, the bird has begun to migrate into urban areas, possibly as a result of continued erosion of its woodland habitat. Before humans began planting the trees commercially on a wide scale, Eurasian jays were the main source of movement and propagation for the <a href="/wiki/Quercus_robur" title="Quercus robur">European oak</a> (<i>Q. robur</i>), each bird having the ability to spread more than a thousand acorns each year. Eurasian jays will also bury the acorns of other oak species, and have been cited by the <a href="/wiki/National_Trust" class="mw-redirect" title="National Trust">National Trust</a> as a major propagator of the largest population of <a href="/wiki/Holm_oak" class="mw-redirect" title="Holm oak">holm oak</a> (<i>Q. ilex</i>) in Northern Europe, situated in <a href="/wiki/Ventnor" title="Ventnor">Ventnor</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Jays have been recorded carrying single acorns as far as 20&#160;km, and are credited with the rapid northward spread of oaks following the last ice age.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_jay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Its usual call is the alarm call which is a harsh, rasping screech and is used upon sighting various predatory animals, but the jay is well known for its <a href="/wiki/Mimicry" title="Mimicry">mimicry</a>, often sounding so like a different species that it is virtually impossible to distinguish its true identity unless the jay is seen. It will even imitate the sound of the bird it is attacking, such as a <a href="/wiki/Tawny_owl" title="Tawny owl">tawny owl</a>, which it does if attacking during the day. However, the jay is a potential prey item for <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owls</a> at night and other birds of prey such as <a href="/wiki/Northern_goshawk" title="Northern goshawk">goshawks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">peregrines</a> during the day.
</p>

<p>Feeding in both trees and on the ground, it takes a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> including many <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">pest</a> insects, acorns (<a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> seeds, which it buries for use during winter),<sup id="cite_ref-Burton2002_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burton2002-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Beech" title="Beech">beech</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruits</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Blackberry" title="Blackberry">blackberries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sorbus_aucuparia" title="Sorbus aucuparia">rowan berries</a>, young <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> and eggs, bats, and small <a href="/wiki/Rodents" class="mw-redirect" title="Rodents">rodents</a>. Like most species, the jay's diet changes with the seasons but is noteworthy for its prolific caching of food—especially oak acorns and beechnuts—for winter and spring. While caching occurs throughout the year, it is most intense in the autumn.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>It nests in trees or large shrubs laying usually 4–6 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> that hatch after 16–19 days and are fledged generally after 21–23 days. Both sexes typically feed the young.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_jay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>For more information, see <a href="/wiki/Anting_(bird_activity)" title="Anting (bird activity)">Anting (bird activity)</a></i>
</p><p>In order to keep its plumage free from parasites, it lies on top of anthills with spread wings and lets its feathers be sprayed with <a href="/wiki/Formic_acid" title="Formic acid">formic acid</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intelligence">Intelligence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_jay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Intelligence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Similar to other corvids, Eurasian jays have been reported to plan for future needs.<sup id="cite_ref-Cheke2011_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cheke2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Male Eurasian jays also take into account the desires of their partner when sharing food with her as a courtship ritual<sup id="cite_ref-Ostojic_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ostojic-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and when protecting food items from stealing conspecifics.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
