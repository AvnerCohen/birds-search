<p>The <b>bearded vulture</b> (<i>Gypaetus barbatus</i>), also known as the <b>lammergeier</b> and <b>ossifrage</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> and the only member of the genus <i>Gypaetus</i>. This bird is also identified as <a href="/wiki/Huma_bird" title="Huma bird">Huma bird</a> or <a href="/wiki/Huma_bird" title="Huma bird">Homa bird</a> in Iran and north west Asia. Traditionally considered an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_vulture" title="Old World vulture">Old World vulture</a>, it actually forms a minor lineage of <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a> together with the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_vulture" title="Egyptian vulture">Egyptian vulture</a> (<i>Neophron percnopterus</i>), its closest living relative. It is not much more closely related to the Old World vultures proper than to, for example, <a href="/wiki/Hawk" title="Hawk">hawks</a>, and differs from the former by its feathered neck. Although dissimilar, the Egyptian and bearded vulture each have a <a href="/wiki/Lozenge" title="Lozenge">lozenge</a>-shaped tail—unusual among birds of prey.
</p><p>The population of this species continues to decline. In 2004, it was classified by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> as <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>; since 2014, it is listed as <a href="/wiki/Near_threatened" class="mw-redirect" title="Near threatened">near threatened</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
The bearded vulture is the only known vertebrate whose diet consists almost exclusively (70 to 90 percent) of bone.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It lives and breeds on crags in high mountains in southern Europe, the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili5_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili5-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili6_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili6-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili8_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili8-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Africa,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> subcontinent, and <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>, laying one or two <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> in mid-winter that hatch at the beginning of spring. Populations are residents.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lammergeier is sparsely distributed across a vast, considerable range. It can be found in mountainous regions from Europe east to <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>) and Africa. It is found in the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> region, the <a href="/wiki/Zagros_Mountains" title="Zagros Mountains">Zagros Mountains</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Alborz" title="Alborz">Alborz</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Koh-i-Baba" title="Koh-i-Baba">Koh-i-Baba</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bamyan" title="Bamyan">Bamyan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Altai_Mountains" title="Altai Mountains">Altai Mountains</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>, Ladakh in northern India, western and central <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> (Where although extinct as a breeder since 1981, single young birds have been reported in 2000, 2004 and 2016 <sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>), and the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a>. In Africa, it is found in the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas Mountains</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Highlands" title="Ethiopian Highlands">Ethiopian Highlands</a> and down from <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> to northeastern <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a>, central <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>. An isolated population inhabits the <a href="/wiki/Drakensberg" title="Drakensberg">Drakensberg</a> of <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species is almost entirely associated with mountains and <a href="/wiki/Monadnock" class="mw-redirect" title="Monadnock">inselbergs</a> with plentiful cliffs, crags, precipices, canyons and gorges. They are often found near <a href="/wiki/Transhumance_in_the_Alps" class="mw-redirect" title="Transhumance in the Alps">alpine pastures</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alpine_tundra" title="Alpine tundra">meadows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montane_grasslands_and_shrublands" title="Montane grasslands and shrublands">montane grassland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heath" title="Heath">heath</a>, steep-sided, rocky <a href="/wiki/Wadi" title="Wadi">wadis</a>, high <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a> and are occasional around <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a>. They seem to prefer desolate, lightly-populated areas where predators who provide many bones, such as <a href="/wiki/Gray_wolf" class="mw-redirect" title="Gray wolf">wolves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">golden eagles</a>, have healthy populations.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, they are now common at refuse tips on the outskirts of small villages and towns. Although they occasionally descend to 300–600&#160;m (980–1,970&#160;ft), bearded vultures are rare below an elevation of 1,000&#160;m (3,300&#160;ft) and normally reside above 2,000&#160;m (6,600&#160;ft) in some parts of their range. They are typically found around or above the <a href="/wiki/Tree_line" title="Tree line">tree line</a> which are often near the tops of the mountains, at up to 2,000&#160;m (6,600&#160;ft) in Europe, 4,500&#160;m (14,800&#160;ft) in Africa and 5,000&#160;m (16,000&#160;ft) in central Asia. In southern Armenia they have been found to breed below 1,000&#160;m (3,300&#160;ft) if cliff availability permits.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> They even have been observed living at altitudes of 7,500&#160;m (24,600&#160;ft) on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a> and been observed flying at a height of 24,000&#160;ft (7,300&#160;m).<sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili5_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili5-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili6_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili6-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili8_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili8-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During 1970s and 1980s the population of the bearded vulture in southern Africa declined however their distribution remained constant. The bearded vulture population occupies the highlands of <a href="/wiki/Lesotho" title="Lesotho">Lesotho</a>, Free State, Eastern Cape and Maloti-Drakensberg mountains in <a href="/wiki/KwaZulu-Natal" title="KwaZulu-Natal">KwaZulu-Natal</a>. Adult bearded vultures utilise areas with higher altitudes, with steep slopes and sharp points and within areas that are situated closer to their nesting sites. Adult bearded vultures are more likely to fly below 200&#160;m over Lesotho. Along the Drakensberg Escarpment from the area of Golden Gate Highlands National Park south into the northern part of the Eastern Cape there was the greatest densities of bearded vultures.
</p><p>Abundance of bearded vultures is shown for eight regions within the species' range in southern Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Ostrich_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ostrich-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The total population of bearded vultures in southern Africa is calculated as being 408 adult birds and 224 young birds of all age classes therefore giving an estimate of about 632 birds.<sup id="cite_ref-Ostrich_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ostrich-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Though a rare visitor, bearded vultures occasionally travel to parts of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, with the first confirmed sighting taking place in 2016 in Wales and the Westcountry.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> A series of sightings took place in 2020, when an individual bird was sighted separately over the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Island</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alderney" title="Alderney">Alderney</a> after migrating north through France,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> then in the <a href="/wiki/Peak_District" title="Peak District">Peak District</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>. The bird, nicknamed 'Vigo' by Tim Birch of the Derbyshire Wildlife Trust, is believed to have originated from the reintroduced population in the Alps.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bearded_vulture.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Bearded_vulture.jpg/220px-Bearded_vulture.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="196" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Bearded_vulture.jpg/330px-Bearded_vulture.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Bearded_vulture.jpg/440px-Bearded_vulture.jpg 2x" data-file-width="538" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bearded_vulture.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A lammergeier in the Puga valley in <a href="/wiki/Ladakh" title="Ladakh">Ladakh</a> in the Indian <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a></div></div></div>
<p>This bird is 94–125&#160;cm (37–49&#160;in) long with a <a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">wingspan</a> of 2.31–2.83&#160;m (7.6–9.3&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It weighs 4.5–7.8&#160;kg (9.9–17.2&#160;lb), with the nominate race averaging 6.21&#160;kg (13.7&#160;lb) and <i>G. b. meridionalis</i> of Africa averaging 5.7&#160;kg (13&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In Eurasia, vultures found around the Himalayas tend to be slightly larger than those from other mountain ranges.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Females are slightly larger than males.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> It is essentially unmistakable with other vultures or indeed other birds in flight due to its long, narrow wings, with the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> measuring 71.5–91&#160;cm (28.1–35.8&#160;in), and long, wedge-shaped tail, which measures 42.7–52&#160;cm (16.8–20.5&#160;in) in length. The tail is longer than the width of the wing.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The tarsus is relatively small for the bird's size, at 8.8–10&#160;cm (3.5–3.9&#160;in). The proportions of the species have been compared to a <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon</a>, scaled to an enormous size.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Unlike most vultures, the bearded vulture does not have a bald head. This species is relatively small headed, although its neck is powerful and thick. It has a generally elongated, slender shape, sometimes appearing bulkier due to the often hunched back of these birds. The gait on the ground is waddling and the feet are large and powerful. The adult is mostly dark gray, rusty and whitish in color. It is grey-blue to grey-black above. The creamy-coloured forehead contrasts against a black band across the eyes and lores and bristles under the chin, which form a black beard that give the species its English name. Bearded vultures are variably orange or rust of plumage on their head, breast and leg feathers but this is actually cosmetic. This colouration may come from dust-bathing, rubbing mud on its body or from drinking in mineral-rich waters. The tail feathers and wings are gray. The juvenile bird is dark black-brown over most of the body, with a buff-brown breast and takes five years to reach full maturity. The bearded vulture is silent, apart from shrill whistles in their breeding displays and a falcon-like <i>cheek-acheek</i> call made around the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physiology">Physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The acid concentration of the bearded vulture stomach has been estimated to be of pH about 1. Large bones will be digested in about 24 hours, aided by slow mixing/churning of the stomach content. The high fat content of bone marrow makes the net energy value of bone almost as good as that of muscle, even if bone is less completely digested. A skeleton left on a mountain will dehydrate and become protected from bacterial degradation, and the bearded vulture can return to consume the remainder of a carcass even months after the soft parts have been consumed by other animals, larvae and bacteria.<sup id="cite_ref-acid_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acid-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet_and_feeding">Diet and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gipeto_adulto.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Gipeto_adulto.jpg/220px-Gipeto_adulto.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Gipeto_adulto.jpg/330px-Gipeto_adulto.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Gipeto_adulto.jpg/440px-Gipeto_adulto.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2500" data-file-height="1664" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gipeto_adulto.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A flying bearded vulture in <a href="/wiki/Gran_Paradiso_National_Park" title="Gran Paradiso National Park">Gran Paradiso National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gypaetus_Barbatus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Gypaetus_Barbatus.jpg/220px-Gypaetus_Barbatus.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Gypaetus_Barbatus.jpg/330px-Gypaetus_Barbatus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Gypaetus_Barbatus.jpg/440px-Gypaetus_Barbatus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gypaetus_Barbatus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bearded vulture on the rocks in <a href="/wiki/Gran_Paradiso_National_Park" title="Gran Paradiso National Park">Gran Paradiso National Park</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Like other <a href="/wiki/Vulture" title="Vulture">vultures</a>, it is a <a href="/wiki/Scavenger" title="Scavenger">scavenger</a>, feeding mostly on the remains of <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">dead animals</a>. The bearded vulture diet comprises mammals (93%), birds (6%) and reptiles (1%), with medium-sized ungulates forming a large part of the diet.<sup id="cite_ref-Margalida_et_al._2009_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Margalida_et_al._2009-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
Bearded vultures avoid remains of larger species (such as cows and horses) probably because of the variable cost/benefit ratios in handling efficiency, ingestion process and transportation of the remains.<sup id="cite_ref-Margalida_et_al._2009_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Margalida_et_al._2009-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
It usually disdains the actual meat and lives on a diet that is typically 85–90% <a href="/wiki/Bone_marrow" title="Bone marrow">bone marrow</a>. This is the only living bird species that specializes in feeding on marrow.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The bearded vulture can swallow whole or bite through brittle bones up to the size of a lamb's femur<sup id="cite_ref-PBS-Bhutan_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PBS-Bhutan-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and its powerful digestive system quickly dissolves even large pieces. The bearded vulture has learned to crack bones too large to be swallowed by carrying them in flight to a height of 50–150&#160;m (160–490&#160;ft) above the ground and then dropping them onto rocks below, which smashes them into smaller pieces and exposes the nutritious marrow.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> They can fly with bones up to 10&#160;cm (3.9&#160;in) in diameter and weighing over 4&#160;kg (8.8&#160;lb), or nearly equal to their own weight.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After dropping the large bones, the bearded vulture spirals or glides down to inspect them and may repeat the act if the bone is not sufficiently cracked.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This learned skill requires extensive practice by immature birds and takes up to seven years to master.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC-BeardedVulture_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-BeardedVulture-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Its old name of <i>ossifrage</i> ("bone breaker") relates to this habit. Less frequently, these birds have been observed trying to break bones (usually of a medium size) by hammering them with their bill directly into rocks while perched.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> During the breeding season they feed mainly on carrion. They prefer limbs of sheep and other small mammals and they carry the food to the nest, unlike other vultures which feed their young by regurgitation.<sup id="cite_ref-Margalida_et_al._2009_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Margalida_et_al._2009-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Live prey is sometimes attacked by the bearded vulture, with perhaps greater regularity than any other vulture.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Among these, <a href="/wiki/Tortoise" title="Tortoise">tortoises</a> seem to be especially favored depending on their local abundance. Tortoises preyed on may be nearly as heavy as the preying vulture. To kill tortoises, bearded vultures fly with them to some height and drop them to crack open the bulky reptiles' hard shells. Golden eagles have been observed to kill tortoises in the same way.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Other live animals, up to nearly their own size, have been observed to be predaciously seized and dropped in flight. Among these are <a href="/wiki/Rock_hyrax" title="Rock hyrax">rock hyraxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hare" title="Hare">hares</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marmot" title="Marmot">marmots</a> and, in one case, a 62&#160;cm (24&#160;in) long <a href="/wiki/Monitor_lizard" title="Monitor lizard">monitor lizard</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PBS-Bhutan_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PBS-Bhutan-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Larger animals have been known to be attacked by bearded vultures, including <a href="/wiki/Ibex" title="Ibex">ibex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capra_(genus)" title="Capra (genus)"><i>Capra</i> goats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chamois" title="Chamois">chamois</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steenbok" title="Steenbok">steenbok</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> These animals have been killed by being surprised by the large birds and battered with wings until they fall off precipitous rocky edges to their deaths; although in some cases these may be accidental killings when both the vulture and the mammal surprise each other.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Many large animals killed by bearded vultures are unsteady young, or have appeared sickly or obviously injured.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Humans have been anecdotally reported to have been killed in the same way. This is unconfirmed, however, and if it does happen, most biologists who have studied the birds generally agree it would be accidental on the part of the vulture.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Occasionally smaller ground-dwelling birds, such as <a href="/wiki/Partridge" title="Partridge">partridges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pigeon" class="mw-redirect" title="Pigeon">pigeons</a>, have been reported eaten, possibly either as fresh carrion (which is usually ignored by these birds) or killed with beating wings by the vulture.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> While foraging for bones or live prey while in flight, bearded vultures fly fairly low over the rocky ground, staying around 2 to 4&#160;m (6.6 to 13.1&#160;ft) high.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Occasionally, breeding pairs may forage and hunt together.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Highlands" title="Ethiopian Highlands">Ethiopian Highlands</a>, bearded vultures have adapted to living largely off human refuse.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bearded vulture occupies an enormous territory year-round. It may forage over two square kilometers each day. The breeding period is variable, being December through September in <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>, November to June in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>, October to May in <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, throughout the year in eastern Africa and May to January in southern Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Although generally solitary, the bond between a breeding pair is often considerably close. Biparental monogamous care occurs in the bearded vulture.<sup id="cite_ref-Margalida_et_al._2000_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Margalida_et_al._2000-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In a few cases, <a href="/wiki/Polyandry" title="Polyandry">polyandry</a> has been recorded in the species.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The territorial and breeding display between bearded vultures is often spectacular, involving the showing of talons, tumbling and spiralling while in solo flight. The large birds also regularly lock feet with each other and fall some distance through the sky with each other.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In Europe the breeding pairs of bearded vultures are estimated to be 120.<sup id="cite_ref-Donazar_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donazar-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The mean productivity of the bearded vulture is 0.43±0.28 fledgings/breeding pair/year and the breeding success averaged 0.56±0.30 fledgings/pair with clutches/year.<sup id="cite_ref-Margalida_et_al._2003_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Margalida_et_al._2003-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The nest is a massive pile of sticks, that goes from around 1&#160;m (3.3&#160;ft) across and 69&#160;cm (27&#160;in) deep when first constructed up to 2.5&#160;m (8.2&#160;ft) across and 1&#160;m (3.3&#160;ft) deep, with a covering of various animal matter from food, after repeated uses. The female usually lays a clutch of 1 to 2 eggs, though 3 have been recorded on rare occasions.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> which are incubated for 53 to 60 days. After hatching, the young spend 100 to 130 days in the nest before <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledging</a>. The young may be dependent on the parents for up to 2 years, forcing the parents to nest in alternate years on a regular basis.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Typically, the bearded vulture nests in caves and on ledges and rock outcrops or caves on steep rock walls, so are very difficult for nest-predating mammals to access.<sup id="cite_ref-PBS-Bhutan_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PBS-Bhutan-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Wild bearded vultures have a mean lifespan of 21.4 years,<sup id="cite_ref-CJBrown1997_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CJBrown1997-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> but have been observed to live for up to at least 45 years in captivity.<sup id="cite_ref-RJAntor2007_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RJAntor2007-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reintroduction_in_the_Alps">Reintroduction in the Alps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reintroduction in the Alps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The bearded vulture had a very poor reputation in early modern Europe, due in large part to tales of the birds stealing babies and livestock. The growing availability of firearms, combined with bounties offered for dead vultures, caused a sharp decline in the bearded vulture population around the Alps. By the beginning of the 20th century, they had completely disappeared from the Alpine regions.
</p><p>Efforts to reintroduce the bearded vulture began in earnest in the 1970s, in the <a href="/wiki/French_Alps" title="French Alps">French Alps</a>. Zoologists Paul Geroudet and Gilbert Amigues attempted to release vultures that had been captured in Afghanistan, but this approach proved unsuccessful: it was too difficult to capture the vultures in the first place, and too many died in transport on their way to France. A second attempt was made in 1987, using a technique called "hacking," by which young individuals (from 90–100 days) from zoological parks would be taken from the nest and placed in a protected area in the Alps. As they were still unable to fly at that age, the chicks were hand-fed by humans until the birds learned to fly and were able to reach food without human assistance. This method has proven more successful, with over 200 birds released in the Alps from 1987 to 2015, and a bearded vulture population has reestablished itself in the Alps.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats_and_conservation_status">Threats and conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Threats and conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lammergeier_with_boy,_Kabul,_1973.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Lammergeier_with_boy%2C_Kabul%2C_1973.JPG/170px-Lammergeier_with_boy%2C_Kabul%2C_1973.JPG" decoding="async" width="170" height="237" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Lammergeier_with_boy%2C_Kabul%2C_1973.JPG/255px-Lammergeier_with_boy%2C_Kabul%2C_1973.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Lammergeier_with_boy%2C_Kabul%2C_1973.JPG/340px-Lammergeier_with_boy%2C_Kabul%2C_1973.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1356" data-file-height="1888" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lammergeier_with_boy,_Kabul,_1973.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Boy with live bearded vulture, <a href="/wiki/Kabul" title="Kabul">Kabul</a></div></div></div>
<p>The bearded vulture is one of the most endangered European bird species as over the last century its abundance and breeding range have drastically declined.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> It naturally occurs at low densities, with anywhere from a dozen to 500 pairs now being found in each mountain range in Eurasia where the species breeds. The species is most common in <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, where an estimated 1,400 to 2,200 are believed to breed.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Relatively large, healthy numbers seem to occur in some parts of the Himalayas as well. It was largely wiped out in Europe, and by the beginning of the 20th century the only substantial population was in the Spanish and French Pyrenees. Since then, it has been successfully reintroduced to the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Alps" title="Swiss Alps">Swiss</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italian_Alps" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian Alps">Italian</a> Alps, from where they have spread over into France.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> They have also declined somewhat in parts of Asia and Africa, though less severely than in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_8-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many raptor species were shielded from anthropogenic influences in previously underdeveloped areas therefore they are greatly impacted as the human population rises and infrastructure increases in underdeveloped areas. The increase in human population and infrastructure results in the declines of the bearded vulture populations today. The increase of infrastructure includes the building of houses, roads and power lines and a major issue with infrastructure and bird species populations is the collision with power lines.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The declines of the bearded vulture populations have been documented throughout their range resulting from a decrease in habitat space, fatal collisions with energy infrastructure, reduced food availability, poisons left out for carnivores and direct persecution in the form of Trophy Hunting.<sup id="cite_ref-howstuffworks_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-howstuffworks-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species is currently listed as near threatened by the IUCN Red List last accessed on 1 October 2016, the population continues to decline as the distribution ranges of this species continues to decline due to human development.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_action">Conservation action</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Conservation action">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There have been mitigation plans that have been established to reduce the population declines in bearded vulture populations. One of these plans includes the South African Biodiversity Management Plan that has been ratified by the government to stop the population decline in the short term. Actions that have been implemented include the mitigation of existing and proposed energy structures to prevent collision risks, the improved management of supplementary feeding sites as well to reduce the populations from being exposed to human persecution and poisoning accidents and to also have outreach programmes that are aimed as reducing poisoning incidents.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a> as <i>Vultur barbatus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Linnaeus1758_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linnaeus1758-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The present scientific name means "bearded vulture-eagle".
</p><p>The name <i>lammergeyer</i> originates from <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i><i lang="de" title="German language text">Lämmergeier</i></i>, which means "lamb-vulture". The name stems from the belief that it attacked <a href="/wiki/Domestic_sheep" class="mw-redirect" title="Domestic sheep">lambs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The bearded vulture is considered a threatened species in <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. Iranian mythology considers the rare bearded vulture (<a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: هما, 'Homa') the symbol of luck and happiness. It was believed that if the shadow of a <i><a href="/wiki/Huma_bird" title="Huma bird">Homa</a></i> fell on one, he would rise to sovereignty<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> and anyone shooting the bird would die in forty days. The habit of eating bones and apparently not killing living animals was noted by <a href="/wiki/Saadi_Shirazi" title="Saadi Shirazi">Sa'di</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gulistan_(book)" title="Gulistan (book)"><i>Gulistan</i></a>, written in 1258, and Emperor Jahangir had a bird's <a href="/wiki/Crop_(anatomy)" title="Crop (anatomy)">crop</a> examined in 1625 to find that it was filled with bones.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The ancient <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a> used <a href="/wiki/Ornithomancy" title="Ornithomancy">ornithomancers</a> to guide their political decisions: bearded vultures, or ossifragae were one of the few species of birds that could yield valid signs to these soothsayers.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greek</a> playwright <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus</a> was said to have been killed in 456 or 455 BC by a <a href="/wiki/Tortoise" title="Tortoise">tortoise</a> dropped by an eagle who mistook his bald head for a stone – if this incident did occur, the bearded vulture is a likely candidate for the "eagle".
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>/<a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a>, the bearded vulture, as the ossifrage, is among the birds forbidden to be eaten (<a href="/wiki/Leviticus" class="mw-redirect" title="Leviticus">Leviticus</a> 11:13).
</p><p>More recently, in 1944, <a href="/wiki/Shimon_Peres" title="Shimon Peres">Shimon Peres</a> (called Shimon Persky at the time) and <a href="/wiki/David_Ben-Gurion" title="David Ben-Gurion">David Ben-Gurion</a> found a nest of bearded vultures in the <a href="/wiki/Negev" title="Negev">Negev</a> desert. The bird is called <i><i lang="he-Latn" title="Hebrew language text">peres</i></i> in <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>, and Shimon Persky liked it so much he adopted it as his <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Robot bearded vultures appear in some science fiction literature, including the first volume of the <a href="/wiki/Viriconium" title="Viriconium">Viriconium</a> series by M. John Harrison and <i><a href="/wiki/Feersum_Endjinn" title="Feersum Endjinn">Feersum Endjinn</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Iain_Banks" title="Iain Banks">Iain M. Banks</a>.
</p>
