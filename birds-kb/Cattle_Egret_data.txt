<p>The <b>Cattle Egret</b> (<i>Bubulcus ibis</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Cosmopolitan_distribution" title="Cosmopolitan distribution">cosmopolitan</a> species of <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">heron</a> (<a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">Ardeidae</a>) found in the tropics, subtropics and warm temperate zones. It is the only member of the <a href="/wiki/Monotype_(biology)" title="Monotype (biology)" class="mw-redirect">monotypic</a> genus <i><b>Bubulcus</b></i>, although some authorities regard its two subspecies as full species, the <b>Western Cattle Egret</b> and the <b>Eastern Cattle Egret</b>. Despite the similarities in plumage to the egrets of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Egretta" title="Egretta">Egretta</a></i>, it is more closely related to the herons of <i><a href="/wiki/Ardea_(genus)" title="Ardea (genus)">Ardea</a></i>. Originally native to parts of Asia, Africa and Europe, it has undergone a rapid expansion in its <a href="/wiki/Range_(biology)" title="Range (biology)">distribution</a> and successfully <a href="/wiki/Colonisation_(biology)" title="Colonisation (biology)">colonised</a> much of the rest of the world.</p>
<p>It is a white bird adorned with buff <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">plumes</a> in the breeding season. It nests in <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonies</a>, usually near bodies of water and often with other <a href="/wiki/Wading_bird" title="Wading bird" class="mw-redirect">wading birds</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a> is a platform of sticks in trees or shrubs. Cattle Egrets exploit drier and open habitats more than other heron species. Their feeding habitats include seasonally inundated grasslands, pastures, farmlands, wetlands and rice paddies. They often <a href="/wiki/Commensalism" title="Commensalism">accompany</a> cattle or other large <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, catching insect and small <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrate</a> prey disturbed by these animals. Some populations of the Cattle Egret are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and others show post-breeding <a href="/wiki/Biological_dispersal" title="Biological dispersal">dispersal</a>.</p>
<p>The adult Cattle Egret has few <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predators</a>, but birds or mammals may raid its nests, and chicks may be lost to starvation, calcium deficiency or disturbance from other large birds. This species maintains a special relationship with cattle, which extends to other large grazing mammals. The cattle egret removes ticks and flies from cattle and consumes them. This benefits both species, but it has been implicated in the spread of tick-borne animal diseases.</p>

<p>The Cattle Egret was first described in 1758 by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_naturae" title="Systema naturae" class="mw-redirect">Systema naturae</a></i> as <i>Ardea ibis</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> but was moved to its current genus by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lucien_Bonaparte" title="Charles Lucien Bonaparte">Charles Lucien Bonaparte</a> in 1855.<sup id="cite_ref-Bonaparte_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bonaparte-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Its genus name <i>Bubulcus</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for herdsman, referring, like the English name, to this species' association with cattle.<sup id="cite_ref-Valpy_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Valpy-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Ibis</i> is a Latin and <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> word which originally referred to another white wading bird, the <a href="/wiki/Sacred_Ibis" title="Sacred Ibis" class="mw-redirect">Sacred Ibis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Webster_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Webster-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Cattle Egret has two geographical races which are sometimes classified as full species, the Western Cattle Egret, <i>B. ibis</i>, and Eastern Cattle Egret, <i>B. coromandus</i>. The two forms were split by McAllan and Bruce,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> but were regarded as <a href="/wiki/Conspecificity" title="Conspecificity">conspecific</a> by almost all other recent authors until the publication of the influential <i><a href="/wiki/Birds_of_South_Asia._The_Ripley_Guide" title="Birds of South Asia. The Ripley Guide">Birds of South Asia</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rasmussen_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rasmussen-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The eastern subspecies <i>B. (i.) coromandus</i>, described by <a href="/wiki/Pieter_Boddaert" title="Pieter Boddaert">Pieter Boddaert</a> in 1783, breeds in Asia and Australasia, and the western nominate form occupies the rest of the species range, including the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Krebs_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krebs-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Some authorities recognise a third <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">Seychelles</a> subspecies, <i>B. i. seychellarum</i>, which was first described by <a href="/wiki/Finn_Salomonsen" title="Finn Salomonsen">Finn Salomonsen</a> in 1934.<sup id="cite_ref-Drury_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Drury-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Despite superficial similarities in appearance, the Cattle Egret is more closely related to the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Ardea_(genus)" title="Ardea (genus)">Ardea</a></i>, which comprises the great or typical herons and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Egret" title="Great Egret">Great Egret</a> (<i>A. alba</i>), than to the majority of species termed egrets in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Egretta" title="Egretta">Egretta</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sheldon_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sheldon-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Rare cases of <a href="/wiki/Hybridisation_in_birds" title="Hybridisation in birds" class="mw-redirect">hybridization</a> with <a href="/wiki/Little_Blue_Heron" title="Little Blue Heron">Little Blue Heron</a> <i>Egretta caerulea</i>, <a href="/wiki/Little_Egret" title="Little Egret">Little Egret</a> <i>Egretta garzetta</i> and <a href="/wiki/Snowy_Egret" title="Snowy Egret">Snowy Egret</a> <i>Egretta thula</i> have been recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Cattle_Egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cattle_Egret2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Cattle_Egret2.jpg/170px-Cattle_Egret2.jpg" width="170" height="254" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cattle_Egret2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A Cattle Egret hitching a ride on a Buffalo in <a href="/wiki/Asola_Bhatti_Wildlife_Sanctuary" title="Asola Bhatti Wildlife Sanctuary">Asola Bhatti Wildlife Sanctuary</a> in <a href="/wiki/National_Capital_Region" title="National Capital Region" class="mw-redirect">National Capital Region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Delhi" title="Delhi">Delhi</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Cattle Egret is a stocky <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">heron</a> with a 88–96&#160;cm (35–38&#160;in) wingspan; it is 46–56&#160;centimetres (18–22&#160;in) long and weighs 270–512&#160;grams (9.5–18.1&#160;<a href="/wiki/Ounce" title="Ounce">oz</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> It has a relatively short thick neck, sturdy <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a>, and a hunched posture. The non-breeding adult has mainly white plumage, a yellow bill and greyish-yellow legs. During the breeding season, adults of the nominate western subspecies develop orange-buff <a href="/wiki/Plume_(feather)" title="Plume (feather)">plumes</a> on the back, breast and <a href="/wiki/Crown_(anatomy)" title="Crown (anatomy)">crown</a>, and the bill, legs and <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">irises</a> become bright red for a brief period prior to pairing.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The sexes are similar, but the male is marginally larger and has slightly longer breeding plumes than the female; juvenile birds lack coloured plumes and have a black bill.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NMcKilligan_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NMcKilligan-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>B. i. coromandus</i> differs from the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" title="Nominate subspecies" class="mw-redirect">nominate subspecies</a> in breeding plumage, when the buff colour on its head extends to the cheeks and throat, and the plumes are more golden in colour. This subspecies' bill and <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> are longer on average than in <i>B. i. ibis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-CITES_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CITES-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>B. i. seychellarum</i> is smaller and shorter-winged than the other forms. It has white cheeks and throat, like <i>B. i. ibis</i>, but the nuptial plumes are golden, as with <i>B. i. coromandus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Drury_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Drury-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The positioning of the egret's eyes allows for <a href="/wiki/Binocular_vision" title="Binocular vision">binocular vision</a> during feeding,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> and physiological studies suggest that the species may be capable of <a href="/wiki/Crepuscular" title="Crepuscular">crepuscular</a> or nocturnal activity.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Adapted to foraging on land, they have lost the ability possessed by their wetland relatives to accurately correct for light <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refraction</a> by water.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species gives a quiet, throaty "rick-rack" call at the breeding colony, but is otherwise largely silent.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Cattle_Egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<div style="float:right;clear:right;">
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cattle_Egret_I2m_IMG_5478.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Cattle_Egret_I2m_IMG_5478.jpg/220px-Cattle_Egret_I2m_IMG_5478.jpg" width="220" height="92" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cattle_Egret_I2m_IMG_5478.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Flock over typical grassland habitat in <a href="/wiki/Kolkata" title="Kolkata">Kolkata</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/w/index.php?title=File:Cattle_egret_spread.svg&amp;page=1" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Cattle_egret_spread.svg/220px-Cattle_egret_spread.svg.png" width="220" height="207" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cattle_egret_spread.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Range expansion in the Americas (click to magnify)</div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Cattle Egret has undergone one of the most rapid and wide reaching natural expansions of any bird species.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> It was originally native to parts of Southern <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, tropical and subtropical <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and humid tropical and subtropical <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. In the end of the 19th century it began expanding its range into southern Africa, first breeding in the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Province" title="Cape Province">Cape Province</a> in 1908.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Cattle Egrets were first sighted in the Americas on the boundary of <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">Guiana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> in 1877, having apparently flown across the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Krebs_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krebs-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> It was not until the 1930s that the species is thought to have become established in that area.<sup id="cite_ref-Crosby_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crosby-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The species first arrived in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> in 1941 (these early sightings were originally dismissed as escapees), bred in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> in 1953, and spread rapidly, breeding for the first time in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> in 1962.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> It is now commonly seen as far west as <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. It was first recorded breeding in <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> in 1957, in <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a> in 1958, and in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> in 1963, although it was probably established before that.<sup id="cite_ref-Crosby_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crosby-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> In Europe the species had historically declined in Spain and Portugal, but in the latter part of the 20th century it expanded back through the Iberian Peninsula, and then began to colonise other parts of Europe; southern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> in 1958, northern France in 1981 and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> in 1985.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Breeding in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> was recorded for the first time in 2008 only a year after an influx seen in the previous year.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2008 cattle egrets were also reported as having moved into <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In Australia the colonisation began in the 1940s, with the species establishing itself in the north and east of the continent.<sup id="cite_ref-Maddock_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maddock-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> It began to regularly visit <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> in the 1960s. Since 1948 the Cattle Egret has been permanently resident in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>. Prior to 1948 it was only a winter visitor. <sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The massive and rapid expansion of the Cattle Egret's <a href="/wiki/Range_(biology)" title="Range (biology)">range</a> is due to its relationship with humans and their <a href="/wiki/Domesticated_animals" title="Domesticated animals" class="mw-redirect">domesticated animals</a>. Originally adapted to a <a href="/wiki/Commensalism" title="Commensalism">commensal</a> relationship with large browsing animals, it was easily able to switch to domesticated cattle and horses. As the keeping of livestock spread throughout the world, the Cattle Egret was able to occupy otherwise empty <a href="/wiki/Ecological_niche" title="Ecological niche">niches</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Many populations of Cattle Egrets are highly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and dispersive,<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> and this has helped the species' range expansion. The species has been seen as a vagrant in various sub-Antarctic islands, including <a href="/wiki/South_Georgia" title="South Georgia" class="mw-redirect">South Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marion_Island" title="Marion Island" class="mw-redirect">Marion Island</a>, the <a href="/wiki/South_Sandwich_Islands" title="South Sandwich Islands" class="mw-redirect">South Sandwich Islands</a> and the <a href="/wiki/South_Orkney_Islands" title="South Orkney Islands">South Orkney Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> A small flock of eight birds was also seen in <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a> in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the natural expansion of its range, Cattle Egrets have been <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced</a> into a few areas. The species was introduced to <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> in 1959, and to the <a href="/wiki/Chagos_Archipelago" title="Chagos Archipelago">Chagos Archipelago</a> in 1955. Successful releases were also made in the <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">Seychelles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rodrigues_(island)" title="Rodrigues (island)" class="mw-redirect">Rodrigues</a>, but attempts to introduce the species to <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a> failed. Numerous birds were also released by <a href="/wiki/Whipsnade_Zoo" title="Whipsnade Zoo">Whipsnade Zoo</a> in England, but the species was never established.<sup id="cite_ref-Lever_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lever-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Although the Cattle Egret sometimes feeds in shallow water, unlike most herons it is typically found in fields and dry grassy habitats, reflecting its greater dietary reliance on terrestrial insects rather than aquatic prey.<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Cattle_Egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Migration and movements">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Migration_and_movements">Migration and movements</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bubulcus_ibis_-Dallas,_Texas,_USA_-flying-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Bubulcus_ibis_-Dallas%2C_Texas%2C_USA_-flying-8.jpg/220px-Bubulcus_ibis_-Dallas%2C_Texas%2C_USA_-flying-8.jpg" width="220" height="138" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bubulcus_ibis_-Dallas,_Texas,_USA_-flying-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Flying in Dallas, Texas, USA. It is carrying a twig in its beak for its nest</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Some populations of Cattle Egrets are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, others are dispersive, and distinguishing between the two can be difficult for this species.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> In many areas populations can be both <a href="/wiki/Sedentism" title="Sedentism">sedentary</a> and migratory. In the northern hemisphere migration is from cooler climes to warmer areas, but Cattle Egrets nesting in Australia migrate to cooler <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a> and New Zealand in the winter and return in the spring.<sup id="cite_ref-Maddock_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maddock-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Migration in western Africa is in response to rainfall, and in South America migrating birds travel south of their breeding range in the non breeding season.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Populations in southern India appear to show local migrations in response to the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoons</a>. They move north from <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a> after September.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> During winter, many birds have been seen flying at night with flocks of <a href="/wiki/Indian_Pond_Heron" title="Indian Pond Heron">Indian Pond Herons</a> (<i>Ardeola grayii</i>) on the southeastern coast of India<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> and a winter influx has also been noted in <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rasmussen_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rasmussen-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Young birds are known to disperse up to 5,000&#160;km (3,100&#160;mi) from their breeding area. Flocks may fly vast distances and have been seen over seas and oceans including in the middle of the Atlantic.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Cattle_Egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h3>
<p>This species has a large range, with an estimated global extent of occurrence of 10&#160;million square kilometres (3.8&#160;million square miles). Its global population estimated to be 3.8–6.7&#160;million individuals. For these reasons, the species is evaluated as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> On the other hand the expansion and establishment of the species over large ranges has led it to be classed as an <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> (although little, if any impact has been noted yet).<sup id="cite_ref-GISS_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GISS-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The Cattle Egret nests in <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonies</a>, which are often, but not always, found around bodies of water.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_18-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The colonies are usually found in woodlands near lakes or rivers, in swamps, or on small inland or coastal islands, and are sometimes shared with other wetland birds, such as <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">herons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egret" title="Egret">egrets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ibis" title="Ibis">ibises</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cormorant" title="Cormorant">cormorants</a>. The breeding season varies within <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rasmussen_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rasmussen-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Nesting in northern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> begins with the onset of monsoons in May.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> The breeding season in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> is November to early January, with one <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">brood</a> laid per season.<sup id="cite_ref-Ber93_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ber93-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> breeding season lasts from April to October.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_18-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">Seychelles</a>, the breeding season of the subspecies <i>B.i. seychellarum</i> is April to October.<sup id="cite_ref-Seychelles_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seychelles-38"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The male displays in a tree in the colony, using a range of <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritualised</a> behaviours such as shaking a twig and sky-pointing (raising bill vertically upwards),<sup id="cite_ref-Hanzab_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hanzab-39"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> and the pair forms over three or four days. A new mate is chosen in each season and when re-nesting following nest failure.<sup id="cite_ref-kushlan_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kushlan-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> The nest is a small untidy platform of sticks in a tree or shrub constructed by both parents. Sticks are collected by the male and arranged by the female, and stick-stealing is rife.<sup id="cite_ref-NMcKilligan_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NMcKilligan-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> size can be anywhere from one to five eggs, although three or four is most common. The pale bluish-white eggs are oval-shaped and measure 45&#160;mm × 53&#160;mm. (1.8–2.1&#160;in)<sup id="cite_ref-Ber93_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ber93-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" title="Avian incubation" class="mw-redirect">Incubation</a> lasts around 23&#160;days, with both sexes sharing incubation duties.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_18-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The chicks are partly covered with <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">down</a> at hatching, but are <a href="/wiki/Nidicolous" title="Nidicolous">not capable of fending for themselves</a>; they become capable of <a href="/wiki/Thermoregulation" title="Thermoregulation">regulating their temperature</a> at 9–12&#160;days and are fully feathered in 13–21&#160;days.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> They begin to leave the nest and climb around at 2&#160;weeks, <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> at 30&#160;days and become independent at around the 45th day.<sup id="cite_ref-kushlan_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kushlan-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Cattle Egret engages in low levels of <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasitism" title="Brood parasitism" class="mw-redirect">brood parasitism</a>, and there are a few instances of Cattle Egret eggs being laid in the nests of <a href="/wiki/Snowy_Egret" title="Snowy Egret">Snowy Egrets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Little_Blue_Heron" title="Little Blue Heron">Little Blue Herons</a>, although these eggs seldom hatch.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_18-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> There is also evidence of low levels of intraspecific brood parasitism, with females laying eggs in the nests of other Cattle Egrets. As much as 30% <a href="/wiki/Extra-pair_copulation" title="Extra-pair copulation" class="mw-redirect">extra-pair copulations</a> have been noted.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The dominant factor in nesting mortality is starvation. Sibling rivalry can be intense, and in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> third and fourth chicks inevitably starve.<sup id="cite_ref-kushlan_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kushlan-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> In the dryer habitats with fewer amphibians the diet may lack sufficient vertebrate content and may cause bone abnormalities in growing chicks due to calcium deficiency.<sup id="cite_ref-Phalen_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phalen-44"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>, nests were sometimes raided by <a href="/wiki/Grivet" title="Grivet">vervet monkeys</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Krebs_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krebs-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> and a study in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> reported the <a href="/wiki/Fish_Crow" title="Fish Crow">Fish Crow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black_rat" title="Black rat">black rat</a> as other possible nest raiders. The same study attributed some nestling mortality to <a href="/wiki/Brown_Pelican" title="Brown Pelican">Brown Pelicans</a> nesting in the vicinity, which accidentally, but frequently, dislodged nests or caused nestlings to fall.<sup id="cite_ref-Maxwell_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maxwell-45"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> In Australia, <a href="/wiki/Torresian_Crow" title="Torresian Crow">Torresian Crows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wedge-tailed_Eagle" title="Wedge-tailed Eagle">Wedge-tailed Eagles</a> and <a href="/wiki/White-bellied_Sea_Eagle" title="White-bellied Sea Eagle">White-bellied Sea Eagles</a> take eggs or young, and <a href="/wiki/Tick" title="Tick">tick</a> infestation and <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viral</a> infections may also be causes of mortality.<sup id="cite_ref-NMcKilligan_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NMcKilligan-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Cattle_Egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cattle_Egret_with_mouse_Feb_2009.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Cattle_Egret_with_mouse_Feb_2009.jpg/220px-Cattle_Egret_with_mouse_Feb_2009.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cattle_Egret_with_mouse_Feb_2009.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
With a mouse in its beak</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Cattle Egret feeds on a wide range of prey, particularly <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cricket_(insect)" title="Cricket (insect)">crickets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a> (adults and maggots <sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup>), and <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moths</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> In a rare instance they have been observed foraging along the branches of a <a href="/wiki/Banyan" title="Banyan">Banyan</a> tree for ripe figs.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> The species is usually found with <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a> and other large grazing and browsing animals, and catches small creatures disturbed by the mammals. Studies have shown that Cattle Egret foraging success is much higher when foraging near a large animal than when feeding singly.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> When foraging with cattle, it has been shown to be 3.6&#160;times more successful in capturing prey than when foraging alone. Its performance is similar when it follows <a href="/wiki/Farm_machinery" title="Farm machinery" class="mw-redirect">farm machinery</a>, but it is forced to move more.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup>In urban situations cattle egrets have also been observed foraging in peculiar situations like railway lines <sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A Cattle Egret will weakly defend the area around a grazing animal against others of the same species, but if the area is swamped by egrets it will give up and continue foraging elsewhere. Where numerous large animals are present, Cattle Egrets selectively forage around species that move at around 5–15&#160;steps per minute, avoiding faster and slower moving herds; in Africa, Cattle Egrets selectively forage behind <a href="/wiki/Plains_Zebra" title="Plains Zebra" class="mw-redirect">Plains Zebras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waterbuck" title="Waterbuck">Waterbuck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blue_Wildebeest" title="Blue Wildebeest" class="mw-redirect">Blue Wildebeest</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Buffalo" title="Cape Buffalo" class="mw-redirect">Cape Buffalo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dominance_(ethology)" title="Dominance (ethology)">Dominant</a> birds feed nearest to the host, and obtain more food.<sup id="cite_ref-NMcKilligan_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NMcKilligan-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Cattle Egret may also show versatility in its diet. On islands with <a href="/wiki/Seabird_colony" title="Seabird colony" class="mw-redirect">seabird colonies</a>, it will prey on the eggs and chicks of <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">terns</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lever_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lever-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> During <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migration</a> it has also been reported to eat exhausted migrating landbirds.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> Birds of the Seychelles race also indulge in some <a href="/wiki/Kleptoparasitism" title="Kleptoparasitism">kleptoparasitism</a>, chasing the chicks of <a href="/wiki/Sooty_Tern" title="Sooty Tern">Sooty Terns</a> and forcing them to disgorge food.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Cattle_Egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bubulcus_ibis_Victoria_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Bubulcus_ibis_Victoria_2.jpg/220px-Bubulcus_ibis_Victoria_2.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bubulcus_ibis_Victoria_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>B. i. seychellarum</i> waiting for scraps at the fish market of <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_Seychelles" title="Victoria, Seychelles">Victoria, Seychelles</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A conspicuous species, the Cattle Egret has attracted many <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">common names</a>. These mostly relate to its habit of following cattle and other large animals, and it is known variously as cow crane, cow bird or cow heron, or even elephant bird, rhinoceros egret.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_18-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Its <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> name, <i>abu qerdan</i>, means "father of ticks", a name derived from the huge number of parasites such as avian ticks found in its breeding colonies.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_18-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WLM_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WLM-56"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Cattle Egret is a popular bird with cattle <a href="/wiki/Rancher" title="Rancher" class="mw-redirect">ranchers</a> for its perceived role as a <a href="/wiki/Biocontrol" title="Biocontrol" class="mw-redirect">biocontrol</a> of cattle <a href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite" class="mw-redirect">parasites</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Tick" title="Tick">ticks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_18-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> A study in Australia found that Cattle Egrets reduced the number of flies that bothered cattle by pecking them directly off the skin.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> It was the benefit to stock that prompted ranchers and the Hawaiian Board of Agriculture and Forestry to release the species in Hawaii.<sup id="cite_ref-Lever_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lever-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Not all interactions between humans and Cattle Egrets are beneficial. The Cattle Egret can be a safety hazard to aircraft due to its habit of feeding in large groups in the grassy verges of airports,<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> and it has been implicated in the spread of animal infections such as <a href="/wiki/Heartwater" title="Heartwater" class="mw-redirect">heartwater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infectious_bursal_disease" title="Infectious bursal disease">infectious bursal disease</a><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> and possibly <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_disease" title="Newcastle disease">Newcastle disease</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-APHIS_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-APHIS-61"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fagbohun1_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fagbohun1-62"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup><br style="clear:both;" /></p>
