<p>The <b>grey-headed gull</b> (<i>Chroicocephalus cirrocephalus</i>), also known as the <b>grey-hooded gull</b>, is a small <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a> which breeds patchily in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> south of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>. It is not truly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, but is more widespread in winter. This species has occurred as a rare vagrant to <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>. As is the case with many gulls, it has traditionally been placed in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Larus" title="Larus">Larus</a></i>.
</p><p>This locally abundant gull breeds in large colonies in reedbeds and marshes, and lays two or three eggs in a nest, which can be on the ground or floating. Like most gulls, it is highly gregarious in winter, both when feeding and in evening roosts. Although it is predominantly coastal or estuarine, it is not a pelagic species, and is rarely seen at sea far from land.
</p><p>Flocks numbering hundreds or thousands of these gulls can form when the feeding conditions are appropriate.
</p><p>The grey-headed gull is slightly larger than the <a href="/wiki/Black-headed_gull" title="Black-headed gull">black-headed gull</a> at 42&#160;cm length. The summer adult has a pale grey head, a grey body, darker in tone than the black-headed, and red bill and legs. The black tips to the primary wing feathers have conspicuous white "mirrors". The underwing is dark grey with black wingtips. The grey hood is lost in winter, leaving just dark streaks.
</p><p>Sexes are similar. The South American <a href="/wiki/Race_(biology)" title="Race (biology)">race</a> is slightly larger and paler-backed than the African <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>.
</p>
<ul><li><i>C. c. cirrocephalus</i> <small>(<a href="/wiki/Louis_Jean_Pierre_Vieillot" title="Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot">Vieillot</a>, 1818)</small> â <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a></li>
<li><i>C. c. poliocephalus</i> <small>(<a href="/wiki/William_John_Swainson" title="William John Swainson">William John Swainson</a>, 1837)</small> â <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a></li></ul>
<p>This gull takes two years to reach maturity. First year birds have a black terminal tail band, and more dark areas in the wings.
</p><p>In flight, the wings are broader and held flatter than those of black-headed gull.
</p><p>This is a noisy species, especially at colonies. The call is a raucous  <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crow</a>-like <i>caw, caw</i>.
</p>
