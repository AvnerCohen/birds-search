<p>The <b>taiga bean goose</b> (<i>Anser fabalis</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Goose" title="Goose">goose</a> that breeds in northern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. This and the <a href="/wiki/Tundra_bean_goose" title="Tundra bean goose">tundra bean goose</a> are recognised as separate species by the <a href="/wiki/American_Ornithological_Society" title="American Ornithological Society">American Ornithological Society</a> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Ornithologists%27_Union" title="International Ornithologists&#39; Union">International Ornithologists' Union</a>, but are considered a single species by other authorities (collectively called bean goose). It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and winters further south in Europe and Asia. The taiga and tundra bean goose diverged about 2.5 million years ago and established secondary contact ca. 60,000 years ago, resulting in extensive gene flow.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Taiga_bean_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The length ranges from 68&#160;to 90&#160;cm (27–35&#160;in), wingspan from 140&#160;to 174&#160;cm (55–69&#160;in) and weight from 1.7–4&#160;kg (3.7–8.8&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate subspecies</a>, males average 3.2&#160;kg (7.1&#160;lb) and females average 2.84&#160;kg (6.3&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The bill is black at the base and tip, with an orange band across the middle; the legs and feet are also bright orange.
</p><p>The upper wing-coverts are dark brown, as in the <a href="/wiki/White-fronted_goose" class="mw-redirect" title="White-fronted goose">white-fronted goose</a> (<i>Anser albifrons</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_white-fronted_goose" title="Lesser white-fronted goose">lesser white-fronted goose</a> (<i>A. erythropus</i>), but differing from these in having narrow white fringes to the feathers.
</p><p>The voice is a loud honking, higher pitched in the smaller subspecies.
</p><p>The closely related <a href="/wiki/Pink-footed_goose" title="Pink-footed goose">pink-footed goose</a> (<i>A. brachyrhynchus</i>) has the bill short, bright pink in the middle, and the feet also pink, the upper wing-coverts being nearly of the same bluish-grey as in the <a href="/wiki/Greylag_goose" title="Greylag goose">greylag goose</a>. In size and bill structure, it is very similar to <i>Anser fabalis rossicus</i>, and in the past was often treated as a sixth subspecies of bean goose.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Taiga_bean_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">scientific names</a> of the bean goose come from its habit in the past of grazing in <a href="/wiki/Bean" title="Bean">bean</a> field stubbles in winter. <i>Anser</i> is the Latin for "goose", and <i>fabalis</i> is derived from the Latin 
<i>faba</i>, a broad bean.<sup id="cite_ref-job90_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job90-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anser_fabalis_Arnoud_B_van_den_Berg.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Anser_fabalis_Arnoud_B_van_den_Berg.jpg/220px-Anser_fabalis_Arnoud_B_van_den_Berg.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Anser_fabalis_Arnoud_B_van_den_Berg.jpg/330px-Anser_fabalis_Arnoud_B_van_den_Berg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Anser_fabalis_Arnoud_B_van_den_Berg.jpg/440px-Anser_fabalis_Arnoud_B_van_den_Berg.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="518" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anser_fabalis_Arnoud_B_van_den_Berg.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Taiga bean goose (<i>Anser fabalis</i> sensu stricto) in the background, tundra bean goose (<i>Anser serrirostris</i>) in the foreground and <a href="/wiki/Greylag_goose" title="Greylag goose">greylag goose</a> (<i>Anser anser</i>) on the right, at Spaarndam, Noord-Holland, the Netherlands</div></div></div>
<p>There are three <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>, with complex variation in body size and bill size and pattern; generally, size increases from north to south and from west to east. 
</p>
<dl><dt>Taiga bean goose (<i>Anser fabalis</i> sensu stricto) (<a href="/wiki/John_Latham_(ornithologist)" title="John Latham (ornithologist)">Latham</a>, 1787)</dt></dl>
<ul><li><i>A. f. fabalis</i> (Latham, 1787). Scandinavia east to the Urals. Large; bill long and narrow, with broad orange band. <i>Anser fabalis fabalis</i> is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.</li>
<li><i>A. f. johanseni</i> (<a href="/wiki/Jean_Th%C3%A9odore_Delacour" title="Jean Théodore Delacour">Delacour</a>, 1951). West Siberian taiga. Large; bill long and narrow, with narrow orange band.</li>
<li><i>A. f. middendorffii</i> (<a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Severtzov" title="Nikolai Severtzov">Severtzov</a>, 1873). East Siberian taiga. Very large; bill long and stout, with narrow orange band.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Taiga_bean_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The taiga bean goose is a rare winter visitor to <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a>. There are two regular wintering flocks of taiga bean goose, in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yare_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Yare Valley (page does not exist)">Yare Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a> and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Avon_Valley,_Scotland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Avon Valley, Scotland (page does not exist)">Avon Valley</a>, Scotland. A formerly regular flock in <a href="/wiki/Dumfries_and_Galloway" title="Dumfries and Galloway">Dumfries and Galloway</a> no longer occurs there.
</p><p>The taiga bean geese <i>Anser fabalis fabalis</i> wintering in Europe are considered to migrate across three different flyways: Western, Central and Eastern; which has been confirmed by stable isotope analysis of their flight feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
