<p>The <b>great reed warbler</b> (<i>Acrocephalus arundinaceus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurasian</a> <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Acrocephalus_(bird)" title="Acrocephalus (bird)">Acrocephalus</a></i>.
</p><p>The genus name <i>Acrocephalus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>akros</i>, "highest", and <i>kephale</i>, "head". It is possible that <a href="/wiki/Johann_Andreas_Naumann" title="Johann Andreas Naumann">Naumann</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Naumann" title="Johann Friedrich Naumann">Naumann</a> thought <i>akros</i> meant "sharp-pointed". The specific <i>arundinaceus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means "like a reed", from <i>arundo, arundinis</i>, "reed".<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It used to be placed in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">Old World warbler</a> assemblage, but is now recognized as part of the marsh and tree-warbler <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> (<a href="/wiki/Acrocephalidae" title="Acrocephalidae">Acrocephalidae</a>). Great reed warblers are medium-sized birds and are the largest of the European warblers. They breed throughout mainland <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and the west <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> and migrate to <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a> in the winter. Great reed warblers favour reed beds as their habitat during breeding months, while living in reed beds, bush thickets, rice fields, and forest clearings during the winter. Great reed warblers exhibit relatively low <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a>, and both genders of the species are similar in appearance. This species mates both <a href="/wiki/Polygynous" class="mw-redirect" title="Polygynous">polygynously</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" class="mw-redirect" title="Monogamous">monogamously</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_reed_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a>-sized <a href="/wiki/Warbler" title="Warbler">warbler</a> is one of the largest species of <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">Old World warbler</a>. It measures 16–21&#160;cm (6.3–8.3&#160;in) in length, 25 to 30&#160;cm (9.8 to 11.8&#160;in) in wingspan and weighs 22 to 38&#160;g (0.78 to 1.34&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-birdskz_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdskz-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HES_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HES-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-stevenson_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stevenson-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The adult has unstreaked brown upperparts and dull buffish-white chin and underparts. The forehead is flattened, and the bill is strong and pointed. It looks very much like a giant <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_reed_warbler" title="Eurasian reed warbler">Eurasian reed warbler</a> (<i>A. scirpaceus</i>), but with a stronger <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a>.
</p><p>The sexes are identical, as with most old world warblers, but young birds are richer buff below.
</p><p>The warbler's song is very loud and far-carrying. The song's main phrase is a chattering and creaking <i>carr-carr-cree-cree-cree-jet-jet</i>, to which the whistles and <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">vocal mimicry</a> typical of <a href="/wiki/Marsh_warblers" class="mw-redirect" title="Marsh warblers">marsh warblers</a> are added.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_ecology">Distribution and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_reed_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_nest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_nest.jpg/220px-Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_nest.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_nest.jpg/330px-Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_nest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_nest.jpg/440px-Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_nest.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1545" data-file-height="1403" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_nest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nest with <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> of eggs</div></div></div>
<p>The great reed warbler breeds in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and the west <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>. It does not breed in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, but is an irregular visitor. Its population has in recent decades increased around the eastern <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>, while it has become rarer at the western end of its range. It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> bird, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Tropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical">tropical</a> <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. This bird migrates north at a rather late date, and some birds remain in their winter quarters until the end of April.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-traylor_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-traylor-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bensch1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bensch1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While there are no <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of this bird, <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" class="mw-redirect" title="MtDNA">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Haplotype" title="Haplotype">haplotype</a> data indicate that during the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a> there were two <a href="/wiki/Allopatric" class="mw-redirect" title="Allopatric">allopatric</a> populations of great reed warbler. The great reed warblers in southwestern and southeastern Europe were at that time apparently separated by the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period#Vistulian_glaciation,_in_Scandinavia_and_northern_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="Last glacial period">Vistulian</a>-<a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period#Würm_glaciation,_in_the_Alps" class="mw-redirect" title="Last glacial period">Würm</a> <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a> and the surrounding barren lands. Though the data are insufficient to robustly <a href="/wiki/Inference" title="Inference">infer</a> a date for this separation, it suggests the populations became separated around 80,000 years ago – coincident with the first major advance of the ice sheets. The populations must have expanded their range again at the start of the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> about 13,000 years ago, but even today the western birds winter in the west and the eastern birds in the east of tropical Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-bensch1_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bensch1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><br />
</p><p>This <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> is found in large <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed beds</a>, often with some bushes. On their breeding grounds, they are <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territorial</a>. In their winter quarters, they are frequently found in large groups, and may occupy a reed bed to the exclusion of other birds.<sup id="cite_ref-traylor_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-traylor-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Like most warblers, it is <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a>, but it will take other prey items of small size, including <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Tadpole" title="Tadpole">tadpoles</a>.
</p><p>The great reed warbler undergoes marked long-term population fluctuations, and it is able to expand its range quickly when new <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> becomes available. This common and widespread bird is considered a species of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bensch1_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bensch1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Population size can be calculated with a <a href="/wiki/Suitability_model" title="Suitability model">suitability model</a>, but direct counts of territorial males in suitable habitat and sampling the population sex-ratio can be a proper alternative to inference-rich predictive modeling based on imperfect habitat-extrapolation of densities of reed warblers at large spatial scales.<sup id="cite_ref-Frías_2018_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frías_2018-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_reed_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_reed_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>A. arundinacius</i> has a primarily <a href="/wiki/Carnivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Carnivorous">carnivorous</a> diet. Observation of prey collection specifically during breeding season has shown the retrieval of insect larvae, moths, dragonflies, damselflies, beetles, spiders, small fish, and frogs.<sup id="cite_ref-ezaki1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ezaki1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <i>A. arundinacius</i> has also been reported to eat fruit during non-breeding seasons.<sup id="cite_ref-grzimek1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grzimek1-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Nestlings typically feed on <a href="/wiki/True_flies" class="mw-redirect" title="True flies">diptera</a> and arachnids, though this may not be their preferred food.<sup id="cite_ref-dyrcz1_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dyrcz1-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Male great reed warblers have been observed to communicate via two basic song types: short songs about one second in length with few syllables, and long songs of about four seconds that have more syllables and are louder than the short variety. It has been observed that long songs are primarily used by males to attract females; long songs are only given spontaneously by unpaired males, and cease with the arrival of a female. Short songs, however, are primarily used in territorial encounters with rival males.<sup id="cite_ref-catchpole1_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-catchpole1-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During experimental observation, male great reed warblers showed reluctance to approach recordings of short songs, and when lured in by long songs, would retreat when playback was switched to short songs.<sup id="cite_ref-catchpole1_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-catchpole1-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Traditionally, monogamous species of genus <i>Acrocephalus</i> use long, variable, and complex songs to attract mates, whereas polygynous varieties use short, simple, stereotypical songs for territorial defence. There is evidence that long songs have been evolved through intersexual selection, whereas short songs have been evolved through intrasexual selection. The great reed warbler is a notable example of these selective pressures, as it is a partial polygynist and has evolved variable song structure (both long and short) through evolutionary compromise.<sup id="cite_ref-catchpole1_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-catchpole1-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to communication, the great reed warbler's song size has been implicated in organism fitness and reproductive success. Though no direct relationship has been found between song size and either territory size or beneficial male qualities, such as wing length, weight, or age, strong correlation has been observed between repertoire size and territory quality. Furthermore, partial correlation analysis has shown that territory quality has significant effect on number of females obtained, while repertoire length is linked to the number of young produced.<sup id="cite_ref-catchpole2_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-catchpole2-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mating_system_and_sexual_behavior">Mating system and sexual behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_reed_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mating system and sexual behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_MHNT_323.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_MHNT_323.jpg/220px-Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_MHNT_323.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="205" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_MHNT_323.jpg/330px-Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_MHNT_323.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_MHNT_323.jpg/440px-Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_MHNT_323.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4530" data-file-height="4214" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_MHNT_323.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Great reed warbler eggs</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_reed_warbler_nest_with_eggs.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Great_reed_warbler_nest_with_eggs.jpg/220px-Great_reed_warbler_nest_with_eggs.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Great_reed_warbler_nest_with_eggs.jpg/330px-Great_reed_warbler_nest_with_eggs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Great_reed_warbler_nest_with_eggs.jpg/440px-Great_reed_warbler_nest_with_eggs.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_reed_warbler_nest_with_eggs.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Great reed warbler nest containing a slightly larger common cuckoo egg and four warbler eggs (Apaj, Hungary).</div></div></div>
<p>Great reed warbler females lay 3–6 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> in an open cup-nest in reeds. Some pairs of warblers are <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" class="mw-redirect" title="Monogamous">monogamous</a>, but others are not, and unpaired, so-called "satellite" males still father some young.<sup id="cite_ref-Leisler_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leisler-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Great reed warblers defend their nests using graded alarm calls, directed towards a wide range of enemies<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>, although these alarm calls might reveal the whereabouts of the nest to brood parasites.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A long-term study of the factors that contribute to male <a href="/wiki/Fitness_(biology)" title="Fitness (biology)">fitness</a> examined the characteristics of males and territories in relation to annual and lifetime breeding success. It showed that the arrival order of the male was the most significant factor for predicting pairing success, fledgling success, and number of offspring that survive. It also found that arrival order was closely correlated with territory attractiveness rank. Females seem to prefer early arriving males that occupy more attractive territories. These females also tend to gain direct benefits through the increased production of fledglings and <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">offspring</a> that become adults. In addition, male song repertoire length is positively correlated to annual harem size and overall lifetime production of offspring that survive. Song repertoire size alone is able to predict male lifetime number of surviving offspring. Females tend to be attracted to males with longer song repertoires since they tend to sire offspring with improved viability. In doing so, they gain indirect benefits for their own young.<sup id="cite_ref-catchpole3_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-catchpole3-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hquist1_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hquist1-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Great reed warblers have a short, <a href="/wiki/Polygynous" class="mw-redirect" title="Polygynous">polygynous</a> breeding cycle in which the male contributes little to parental care. They defend large territories in reed beds where there is reduced visibility, which may allow males to practice deception by moving and attracting a second female. This second female may not realize that the male has already mated. Polygyny of the great reed warbler was assessed in another study that showed the importance of <a href="/wiki/Mate_selection" class="mw-redirect" title="Mate selection">female choice</a>. The differences in territory characteristics seemed to be more important. However, there is also a strong correlation between male and their territory characteristics. Models based on the polygyny threshold and sexy son hypotheses predict that females should gain evolutionary advantage in either short-term or long-term in this mating system, yet the study did not support this. The data showed that secondary females had greatly reduced breeding success.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
