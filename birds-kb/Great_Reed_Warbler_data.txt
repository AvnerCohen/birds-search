<p>The <b>Great Reed Warbler</b> (<i>Acrocephalus arundinaceus</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasian</a> <a href="/wiki/Songbird" title="Songbird">songbird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Acrocephalus" title="Acrocephalus">Acrocephalus</a></i>. It used to be placed in the "<a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">Old World warbler</a>" assemblage, but nowadays is recognized to be part of the marsh- and tree-warbler <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> (<a href="/wiki/Acrocephalidae" title="Acrocephalidae">Acrocephalidae</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Reed_Warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
<div id="ogg_player_1">

</div>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>This is <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a>-sized <a href="/wiki/Warbler" title="Warbler">warbler</a> is one of the largest species of <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">Old World warbler</a>. It measures 16–21 cm (6.3–8.3 in) in length, 25 to 30 cm (9.8 to 12 in) in wingspan and weighs 22 to 38 g (0.78 to 1.3 oz).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Stevenson_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stevenson-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The adult has an unstreaked brown upperparts and dull buffish-white chin and underparts. The forehead is flattened, and the bill is strong and pointed. It looks very much like a giant <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Reed_Warbler" title="Eurasian Reed Warbler">Eurasian Reed Warbler</a> (<i>A. scirpaceus</i>), but with a stronger <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a>.</p>
<p>The sexes are identical, as with most old world warblers, but young birds are richer buff below.</p>
<p>The song is very loud and far-carrying. Its main phrase is a chattering and creaking <i>carr-carr-cree-cree-cree-jet-jet</i>, to which the whistles and <a href="/wiki/Vocal_mimicry" title="Vocal mimicry" class="mw-redirect">vocal mimicry</a> typical of marsh-warblers are added.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Reed_Warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_ecology">Distribution and ecology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_nest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_nest.jpg/220px-Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_nest.jpg" width="220" height="200" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Acrocephalus_arundinaceus_nest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Nest with <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> of eggs</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>A. arundinaceus</i> breeds in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and westernmost <a href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate" class="mw-redirect">temperate</a> <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. It does not breed in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, but is a regular visitor. Its population has in recent decades increased around the eastern <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic</a>, while it has become rarer at the western end of its range. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical" class="mw-redirect">tropical</a> <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. This bird migrates north at a rather late date, with some birds still in winter quarters at the end of April.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Traylor_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Traylor-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bensch_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bensch-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>While no <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are diagnosable in this bird, <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA" class="mw-redirect">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Haplotype" title="Haplotype">haplotype</a> data indicates that during the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a> there were two <a href="/wiki/Allopatric" title="Allopatric" class="mw-redirect">allopatric</a> populations of this species. The Great Reed Warblers in southwestern and southeastern Europe were at that time apparently separated by the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period#Vistulian_glaciation.2C_in_Scandinavia_and_northern_Europe" title="Last glacial period">Vistulian</a>-<a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period#W.C3.BCrm_glaciation.2C_in_the_Alps" title="Last glacial period">Würm</a> <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a> and the barren land around these. Though the data is insufficient to robustly <a href="/wiki/Inference" title="Inference">infer</a> a date for this separation, it suggests the populations became separated around 80,000 years ago – coincident with the first major advance of the ice sheets. The populations must have expanded their range again at the start of the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> about 13,000 years ago, but even today the western birds winter in the west and the eastern birds in the east of tropical Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Bensch_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bensch-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Flickr_-_coniferconifer_-_Great_Reed_Warbler.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Flickr_-_coniferconifer_-_Great_Reed_Warbler.jpg/170px-Flickr_-_coniferconifer_-_Great_Reed_Warbler.jpg" width="170" height="170" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flickr_-_coniferconifer_-_Great_Reed_Warbler.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Male warbler declaring his territory</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> is a species found in large <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed beds</a>, often with some bushes. On their breeding grounds, they are <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territorial</a>. In their winter quarters, they are frequently found in large groups, and may occupy a reed bed to the exclusion of almost all other birds<sup id="cite_ref-Traylor_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Traylor-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup>. Like most warblers, it is <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" title="Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect">insectivorous</a>, but it will take other prey items of sufficiently small size, even including <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Tadpole" title="Tadpole">tadpoles</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Reed_Warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h2>
<p>3–6 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> are laid in a basket nest in reeds. Some pairs are <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" title="Monogamous" class="mw-redirect">monogamous</a>, but others are not, and unpaired males without territory usually father some young also<sup id="cite_ref-Leisler_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leisler-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup>.</p>
<p>The Great Reed Warbler apparently undergoes marked long-term population fluctuations. Able to expand its range again quickly when new <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> becomes available, this common and widespread bird is considered a Species of <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN" class="mw-redirect">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bensch_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bensch-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
