<p>The <b>Red-backed Shrike</b> (<i>Lanius collurio</i>) is a carnivorous <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> and member of the <a href="/wiki/Shrike" title="Shrike">shrike</a> family <a href="/wiki/Laniidae" title="Laniidae" class="mw-redirect">Laniidae</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-backed_Shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>

<p>This 16–18&#160;cm (approx. 6.3–7.1&#160;inches) long <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> bird eats large insects, small birds, frogs, rodents and lizards. Like other shrikes it hunts from prominent perches, and impales corpses on thorns or barbed wire as a "<a href="/wiki/Larder" title="Larder">larder</a>." This practice has earned it the nickname of "butcher bird."</p>
<p>The general colour of the male’s upper parts is reddish. It has a grey head and a typical shrike black stripe through the eye. Underparts are tinged pink, and the tail has a black and white pattern similar to that of a <a href="/wiki/Wheatear" title="Wheatear">wheatear</a>. In the female and young birds the upperparts are brown and vermiculated. Underparts are buff and also vermiculated.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-backed_Shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Range">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Range">Range</span></h2>
<p>This bird breeds in most of Europe and western Asia and winters in tropical Africa. Once a common migratory visitor to Great Britain, numbers declined sharply during the 20th century. The bird's last stronghold was in <a href="/wiki/Breckland" title="Breckland">Breckland</a> but by 1988 just a single pair remained, successfully raising young at <a href="/wiki/Santon_Downham" title="Santon Downham">Santon Downham</a>. The following year for the first time no nests were recorded in the UK. But since then sporadic breeding has taken place, mostly in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>. In September 2010 the <a href="/wiki/RSPB" title="RSPB" class="mw-redirect">RSPB</a> announced that a pair had raised chicks at a secret location on <a href="/wiki/Dartmoor" title="Dartmoor">Dartmoor</a> where the bird last bred in 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-rspb_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2011, two pairs nested in the same locality, fledging seven young.<sup id="cite_ref-birdwatch_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdwatch-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> This return to south western England has been an unexpected development and has raised speculation that a warming climate could assist the bird in re-colonising some of its traditional sites, if only in small numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
