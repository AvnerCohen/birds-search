<p>The <b>red-backed shrike</b> (<i>Lanius collurio</i>) is a carnivorous <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird and member of the <a href="/wiki/Shrike" title="Shrike">shrike</a> family <a href="/wiki/Laniidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Laniidae">Laniidae</a>. Its breeding range stretches from Western Europe east to central Russia. It is migratory and winters in the eastern areas of tropical Africa and southern Africa.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-backed_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The red-backed shrike was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">formally described</a> by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">tenth edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> under its current <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Lanius collurio</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  The genus name, <i><a href="/wiki/Lanius" title="Lanius">Lanius</a></i>, is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word for "<a href="/wiki/Butcher" title="Butcher">butcher</a>", and some shrikes are also known as "butcher birds" because of their feeding habits. The specific <i>collurio</i> is from  <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>kollurion</i>, a bird mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The common English name "shrike" is from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>scríc</i>, "shriek", referring to the shrill call.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-backed_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This 16–18&#160;cm (approx. 6.3–7.1&#160;inches) long <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> bird eats large <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a>. Like other shrikes it hunts from prominent perches, and impales corpses on thorns or barbed wire as a "<a href="/wiki/Larder" title="Larder">larder</a>." This practice has earned it the nickname of "butcher bird."
</p><p>The general colour of the male's upper parts is reddish. It has a grey head and a typical shrike black stripe through the eye. Underparts are tinged pink, and the tail has a black and white pattern similar to that of a <a href="/wiki/Wheatear" title="Wheatear">wheatear</a>. In the female and young birds the upperparts are brown and vermiculated. Underparts are buff and also vermiculated.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-backed_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This bird breeds in most of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Asia" title="Western Asia">western Asia</a> and winters in tropical <a href="/wiki/Afrotropical_realm" title="Afrotropical realm">Africa</a>. The bird is listed as a "least concern" (LC) species on a global scale, but some parts of its range have seen a steep decline in numbers, so locally its status can be less secure.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Britain">Great Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-backed_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Great Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Once a common migratory visitor to Great Britain, numbers declined sharply during the 20th century. The bird's last stronghold was in <a href="/wiki/Breckland" title="Breckland">Breckland</a> but by 1988 just a single pair remained, successfully raising young at <a href="/wiki/Santon_Downham" title="Santon Downham">Santon Downham</a>. The following year for the first time no nests were recorded in the UK. But since then sporadic breeding has taken place, mostly in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>.  In September 2010 the <a href="/wiki/RSPB" class="mw-redirect" title="RSPB">RSPB</a> announced that a pair had raised chicks at a secret location on <a href="/wiki/Dartmoor" title="Dartmoor">Dartmoor</a> where the bird last bred in 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-rspb_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 2011, two pairs nested in the same locality, fledging seven young.<sup id="cite_ref-birdwatch_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdwatch-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In 2012 there was another breeding attempt, this time unsuccessful, probably due to a prolonged spell of wet weather.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Harrier_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Harrier-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In 2013 breeding was again confirmed in Devon, with two young fledged at a new site.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  This return to south western England has been an unexpected development and has raised speculation that a warming climate could assist the bird in re-colonising some of its former haunts, if only in small numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
