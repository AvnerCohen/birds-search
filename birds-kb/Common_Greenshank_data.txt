<p>The <b>common greenshank</b> (<i>Tringa nebularia</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the large family Scolopacidae, the typical waders. The genus name <i>Tringa</i> is the <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">New Latin</a> name given to the <a href="/wiki/Green_sandpiper" title="Green sandpiper">green sandpiper</a> by <a href="/wiki/Ulisse_Aldrovandi" title="Ulisse Aldrovandi">Aldrovandus</a> in 1599 based on <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>trungas</i>, a thrush-sized, white-rumped, tail-bobbing wading bird mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>. The specific <i>nebularia</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>nebula</i> "mist". Like the Norwegian <i>Skoddefoll</i>, this refers to the greenshank's damp marshy habitat.<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relatives">Relatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_greenshank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Relatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Its closest relative is the <a href="/wiki/Greater_yellowlegs" title="Greater yellowlegs">greater yellowlegs</a>, which together with the <a href="/wiki/Spotted_redshank" title="Spotted redshank">spotted redshank</a> form a close-knit group. Among them, these three species show all the basic leg and foot colours found in the <a href="/wiki/Tringa" title="Tringa">shanks</a>, demonstrating that this character is <a href="/wiki/Paraphyletic" class="mw-redirect" title="Paraphyletic">paraphyletic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pereira2005_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pereira2005-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> They are also the largest shanks apart from the <a href="/wiki/Willet" title="Willet">willet</a>, which is altogether more robustly built. The greater yellowlegs and the common greenshank share a coarse, dark, and fairly crisp breast pattern as well as much black on the shoulders and back in breeding plumage.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_greenshank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is a subarctic bird, breeding from northern Scotland eastwards across northern Europe and east across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> species, wintering in Africa, the Indian subcontinent, and Australasia, usually on fresh water. It breeds on dry ground near marshy areas, laying about four eggs in a ground scrape.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_greenshank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Common greenshanks are brown in breeding plumage, and grey-brown in winter. When in water, they can appear very similar to <a href="/wiki/Marsh_sandpiper" title="Marsh sandpiper">marsh sandpipers</a> but are distinguished by the shape of the lower bill which gives it an upturned appearance to the bill. They have long greenish legs and a long bill with a grey base. They show a white wedge on the back in flight. They are somewhat larger than the related <a href="/wiki/Common_redshank" title="Common redshank">common redshank</a>. The usual call is a rapid series of three short fluty notes syllabilized as <i>teu-teu-teu</i>.
</p><p>Like most waders, they feed on small <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>, but will also take small fish and amphibians.
</p><p>The common greenshank is one of the species to which the <a href="/wiki/Agreement_on_the_Conservation_of_African-Eurasian_Migratory_Waterbirds" title="Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a> (AEWA) applies.
</p>
