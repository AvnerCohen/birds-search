<p>The <b>Northern Goshawk</b> <span class="nowrap"><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/</a></span><span class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ˈ</span></a></span><span class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="'g' in 'guy'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ɡ</span></a></span><span class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ɒ</span></a></span><span class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="'s' in 'sigh'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">s</span></a></span><span class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="/./ syllable break" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">.</span></a></span><span class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="'h' in 'hi'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">h</span></a></span><span class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="/ɔː/ 'au' in 'fraud'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ɔː</span></a></span><span class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="'k' in 'kind'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">k</span></a></span><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/</a></span></span> (<a href="/wiki/Old_English_language" title="Old English language" class="mw-redirect">Old English</a>: <i>gōsheafoc</i>, "goose-hawk"), <i><b>Accipiter gentilis</b></i>, is a medium-large <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a>, which also includes other diurnal raptors, such as <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buzzard" title="Buzzard">buzzards</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harrier_(bird)" title="Harrier (bird)">harriers</a>.</p>
<p>It is a widespread species that inhabits the temperate parts of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere" class="mw-redirect">northern hemisphere</a>. It is the only species in the <i><a href="/wiki/Accipiter" title="Accipiter">Accipiter</a></i> genus found in both Eurasia and North America. With the exception of Asia, it is the only species of "goshawk" in its range and it is thus often referred to (officially and unofficially, respectively) as simply the "Goshawk". It is mainly <a href="/wiki/Resident_bird" title="Resident bird" class="mw-redirect">resident</a>, but birds from colder regions <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> south for the winter. In North America, migratory goshawks are often seen migrating south along mountain ridge tops in September and October.</p>
<p>This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in his <i>Systema naturae</i> in 1758 under its current scientific name.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Northern Goshawk appears on the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_Azores" title="Flag of the Azores">flag of the Azores</a>. The archipelago of the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, takes its name from the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese language</a> word for goshawk, (<i>açor</i>), because the explorers who discovered the archipelago thought the birds of prey they saw there were goshawks; later it was found that these birds were <a href="/wiki/Milvus" title="Milvus">kites</a> or <a href="/wiki/Common_Buzzard" title="Common Buzzard">Common Buzzards</a> (<i>Buteo buteo rothschildi</i>).</p>

<p>The Northern Goshawk is the largest member of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Accipiter" title="Accipiter">Accipiter</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> It is a raptor with short, broad wings and a long tail, both adaptations to manoeuvring through trees in the forests it lives and nests in. Across most of the species's range, it is blue-grey above and barred grey or white below, but Asian subspecies in particular range from nearly white overall to nearly black above. The juvenile is brown above and barred brown below. Juveniles and adults have a barred tail, with dark brown or black barring. Adults always have a white eye stripe. In North America, juveniles have pale-yellow eyes, and adults develop dark red eyes usually after their second year, although nutrition and genetics may affect eye color as well. In Europe and Asia, juveniles also have pale-yellow eyes, however adults develop orange-colored eyes.</p>
<p>The Northern Goshawk, like all <a href="/wiki/Accipiter" title="Accipiter">accipiters</a>, exhibits <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a>, where females are significantly larger than males. Males, being the smaller sex by around 10–25%, are 46–57 cm (18–22 in) long and have a 89–105 cm (35–41 in) wingspan.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The female is much larger, 58–69 cm (23–27 in) long with a 108–127 cm (43–50 in) wingspan.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Males average around 780&#160;g (1.7&#160;lb), with a range of 500 to 1,200 g (1.1 to 2.6 lb).<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The female can be more than twice as heavy, averaging 1,220&#160;g (2.7&#160;lb) with a range of 820 to 2,200 g (1.8 to 4.9 lb).<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Among standard measurements, the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> is 28.6–39 cm (11.3–15 in), the tail is 20–28 cm (7.9–11 in), the <a href="/wiki/Culmen_(bird)" title="Culmen (bird)" class="mw-redirect">culmen</a> is 2–2.6 cm (0.79–1.0 in) and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> is 6.8–9 cm (2.7–3.5 in).<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>, the species follows <a href="/wiki/Bergmann%27s_rule" title="Bergmann's rule">Bergmann's rule</a>, specimens from the northern races generally are larger-bodied than goshawks near the southern reaches of the species range.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Going on wing chord length, <i>A. g. apache</i>, found in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> to <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a> is the largest subspecies at an average of 36.8&#160;cm (14.5&#160;in) and is larger than more Northern subspecies in that continent, thus running contrary to Bergmann's rule. <i>A. g. fujiyamae</i> of Japan is the smallest race, at 30.9&#160;cm (12.2&#160;in) in wing chord length.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The flight is a characteristic "flap flap, glide", but is sometimes seen soaring in migration, and is capable of considerable, sustained, horizontal speed in pursuit of prey with speeds of 38&#160;mph (61&#160;km/h) reported.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Goshawks are sometimes confused with <a href="/wiki/Gyrfalcon" title="Gyrfalcon">gyrfalcons</a> especially when observed in high speed pursuit, with their wingtips drawn backward in a falcon-like profile.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>, the male is sometimes confused with a female <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian Sparrowhawk">Sparrowhawk</a>, but is larger, much bulkier and has relatively longer wings. In <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, juveniles are sometimes confused with the smaller <a href="/wiki/Cooper%27s_Hawk" title="Cooper's Hawk">Cooper's Hawk</a>, however the juvenile goshawk displays a heavier, vertical streaking pattern on their chest and abdomen and sometimes appears to have a shorter tail due to its much larger and broader body. Although there appears to be a size overlap between small male goshawks and large female Cooper's Hawks, <a href="/wiki/Morphometric" title="Morphometric" class="mw-redirect">morphometric</a> measurements (wing and tail length) of both species demonstrate no such overlap, although weight overlap can occur due to variation in seasonal condition and food intake at time of weighing. In North America, the <a href="/wiki/Sharp-shinned_Hawk" title="Sharp-shinned Hawk">Sharp-shinned Hawk</a> is markedly smaller.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Goshawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span></h2>
<p>Northern Goshawks can be found in both deciduous and coniferous forests. They seem to only thrive in areas with mature, old-growth woods and are typically found where human activity is relatively low. During nesting season, they favor tall trees with intermediate canopy coverage and small openings below for hunting. They can be found at almost any altitude, but are typically found at high elevations recently due to a paucity of extensive forests remaining in lowlands across much of its range. In winter months, the Northernmost populations move down to warmer forests with lower elevations, continuing to avoid detection except while migrating. A majority of goshawks around the world remain sedentary throughout the year.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Goshawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Food and hunting">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_hunting">Food and hunting</span></h2>
<p>This species hunts birds and mammals in a variety of woodland habitats, often utilizing a combination of speed and obstructing cover to ambush birds and mammals. Goshawks are often seen flying along adjoining habitat types, such as the edge of a forest and meadow; flying low and fast hoping to surprise unsuspecting prey. They are usually opportunistic predators, as are most <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">birds of prey</a>. The most important prey species are small mammals and birds found in forest habitats, in North America, this is compromised largely by <a href="/wiki/Grouse" title="Grouse">grouse</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Crow" title="American Crow">American Crow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snowshoe_hare" title="Snowshoe hare">snowshoe hare</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Red_squirrel" title="Red squirrel">red squirrel</a>. Compared to many smaller <i><a href="/wiki/Accipiter" title="Accipiter">Accipiter</a></i> species, Northern Goshawks are less specialized as predators of birds, with up to 69% of their diet comprised by either birds or mammals depending on location.<sup id="cite_ref-Sora_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sora-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Prey species may be quite diverse, including <a href="/wiki/Columbiformes" title="Columbiformes" class="mw-redirect">pigeons and doves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pheasant" title="Pheasant">pheasants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Partridge" title="Partridge">partridges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grouse" title="Grouse">grouse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, assorted <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">waders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Woodpecker" title="Woodpecker">woodpeckers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corvid" title="Corvid" class="mw-redirect">corvids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waterfowl" title="Waterfowl">waterfowl</a> (mostly tree-nesting varieties such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Aythya" title="Aythya">Aythya</a></i> genus<sup id="cite_ref-Sora_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sora-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup>) and various <a href="/wiki/Passerines" title="Passerines" class="mw-redirect">passerines</a> depending on the region. Mammal prey may include <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hare" title="Hare">hares</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tree_squirrel" title="Tree squirrel">tree squirrels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ground_squirrel" title="Ground squirrel">ground squirrels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chipmunk" title="Chipmunk">chipmunks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vole" title="Vole">voles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mice" title="Mice" class="mw-redirect">mice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weasel" title="Weasel">weasels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shrew" title="Shrew">shrews</a>. Prey is often smaller than the hunting hawk, with an average prey mass of 275&#160;g (9.7&#160;oz) in one study of nesting birds in <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sora_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sora-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, male prey averaged 277&#160;g (9.8&#160;oz) whereas female prey averaged 505&#160;g (17.8&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> However, Northern Goshawks will also occasionally kill much larger animals, up to the size of <a href="/wiki/Geese" title="Geese" class="mw-redirect">geese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">raccoons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">foxes</a> and large <a href="/wiki/Hare" title="Hare">hares</a>, any of which can be more than twice their own weight.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The Goshawk is likely a significant predator of other <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptors</a>, known prey including <a href="/wiki/European_Honey_Buzzard" title="European Honey Buzzard">European Honey Buzzards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owls</a>, smaller <i><a href="/wiki/Accipiter" title="Accipiter">Accipiter</a></i>s and the <a href="/wiki/American_Kestrel" title="American Kestrel">American Kestrel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Northern goshawks sometimes cache prey on tree branches or wedged in a crotch between branches for up to 32 hours. This is done primarily during the nestling stage.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Goshawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Rabbit_(1898)_%27Just_missed_him%27.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/The_Rabbit_%281898%29_%27Just_missed_him%27.png/220px-The_Rabbit_%281898%29_%27Just_missed_him%27.png" width="220" height="131" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/The_Rabbit_%281898%29_%27Just_missed_him%27.png/330px-The_Rabbit_%281898%29_%27Just_missed_him%27.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/The_Rabbit_%281898%29_%27Just_missed_him%27.png/440px-The_Rabbit_%281898%29_%27Just_missed_him%27.png 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Rabbit_(1898)_%27Just_missed_him%27.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>In the spring <a href="/wiki/Breeding_season" title="Breeding season">breeding season</a>, Northern Goshawks perform a spectacular "undulating flight display", and this is one of the best times to see this secretive forest bird. At this time, the surprisingly <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a>-like call of this bird is sometimes heard. Adults defend their territories fiercely from intruders, including passing humans. It is presumed that their unusually aggressive nest defense is an adaptation to tree-climbing bears species, such as the <a href="/wiki/American_Black_Bear" title="American Black Bear" class="mw-redirect">black bear</a> in North America. Additional predators at the nest may include formidable species such as <a href="/wiki/Fisher" title="Fisher">fishers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wolves" title="Wolves" class="mw-redirect">wolves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Horned_Owl" title="Great Horned Owl">Great Horned</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Eagle_Owl" title="Eurasian Eagle Owl" class="mw-redirect">Eurasian Eagle Owls</a>. Goshawks are most under threat from hatching until their fledgling stage, and are rarely threatened outside their own species once they attain adulthood. Other raptor species are attacked at nest sites, over competition for food and in territorial behavior and many are regularly displaced or even killed by the aggressive goshawk.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_3-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The Northern Goshawk is considered a secretive raptor, and is rarely observed even in areas where nesting sites are common.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Goshawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h2>
<p>Adults return to their nesting territories by March or April and begin laying eggs in April or May. Territories often encompass a variety of habitats, however the immediate nest area is often found in a mature or old-growth forest. The clutch size is usually 2 to 4, but anywhere from 1 to 5 eggs may be laid. The female is the primary incubator, although the male will sometimes take a shift to give the female a chance to eat. The male does most of the hunting for both the female and the young at the nest. The eggs average 59&#160;mm ×&#160;45&#160;mm (2.3&#160;in ×&#160;1.8&#160;in) and weigh about 60&#160;g (2.1&#160;oz). The incubation period can range from 28 to 38 days. The young leave the nest after about 35 days and start trying to fly another 10 days later. The young may remain in their parents' territory for up to a year of age, at which point sexual maturity is reached.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Goshawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, the Northern Goshawk was <a href="/wiki/Extirpated" title="Extirpated" class="mw-redirect">extirpated</a> in the 19th century because of specimen collectors and persecution by <a href="/wiki/Gamekeeper" title="Gamekeeper">gamekeepers</a>, but in recent years it has come back by immigration from <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, escaped <a href="/wiki/Falconry" title="Falconry">falconry</a> birds<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from March 2009">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, and deliberate releases<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from March 2009">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The Goshawk is now found in considerable numbers in <a href="/wiki/Kielder_Forest" title="Kielder Forest">Kielder Forest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>, which is the largest forest in Britain. The main threat to Northern Goshawks internationally today is the clearing of <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a> habitat on which both they and their prey depend, and in Britain illegal shooting and poisoning for <a href="/wiki/Game_preservation" title="Game preservation">game preservation</a> when young dispersing move into farming areas.</p>
<p>In North America, several non-governmental conservation organizations petitioned the Department of Interior, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Fish_%26_Wildlife_Service" title="United States Fish &amp; Wildlife Service" class="mw-redirect">United States Fish &amp; Wildlife Service</a> (1991 &amp; 1997) to list the Goshawk as "threatened" or "endangered" under the authority of the <a href="/wiki/Endangered_Species_Act" title="Endangered Species Act">Endangered Species Act</a>. Both petitions argued for listing primarily on the basis of historic and ongoing nesting habitat loss, specifically the loss of old-growth and mature forest stands throughout the goshawk's known range. In both petitions, the U.S. Fish &amp; Wildlife Service concluded that listing was not warranted, but state and federal natural resource agencies responded during the petition process with standardized and long-term goshawk inventory and monitoring efforts, especially throughout U.S. Forest Service lands in the Western U.S. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Forest_Service" title="United States Forest Service">United States Forest Service</a> (US Dept of Agriculture) has listed the goshawk as a "sensitive species", while it also benefits from various protection at the state level. In North America, the goshawk is federally protected under the <a href="/wiki/Migratory_Bird_Treaty_Act_of_1918" title="Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918">Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918</a> by an amendment incorporating native birds of prey into the Act in 1972. The Northern Goshawk is also listed in Appendix II of the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species (<a href="/wiki/CITES" title="CITES">CITES</a>) .<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Goshawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In falconry">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="In_falconry">In falconry</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Accipiter_gentilis_-owned_by_a_falconer_in_Scotland_-upper_body-8a.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Accipiter_gentilis_-owned_by_a_falconer_in_Scotland_-upper_body-8a.jpg/220px-Accipiter_gentilis_-owned_by_a_falconer_in_Scotland_-upper_body-8a.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Accipiter_gentilis_-owned_by_a_falconer_in_Scotland_-upper_body-8a.jpg/330px-Accipiter_gentilis_-owned_by_a_falconer_in_Scotland_-upper_body-8a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Accipiter_gentilis_-owned_by_a_falconer_in_Scotland_-upper_body-8a.jpg/440px-Accipiter_gentilis_-owned_by_a_falconer_in_Scotland_-upper_body-8a.jpg 2x" /></a>

<p>The name "goshawk" is a traditional name from <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_language" title="Anglo-Saxon language" class="mw-redirect">Anglo-Saxon</a> <i>gōshafoc</i>, literally "goose hawk".<sup id="cite_ref-ODBBN_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODBBN-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The name implies prowess against larger quarry such as geese, but were also flown against crane species and other large waterbirds. The name "goose hawk" is somewhat of a misnomer however, as the traditional quarry for goshawks in ancient and contemporary <a href="/wiki/Falconry" title="Falconry">falconry</a> has been rabbits, pheasants, partridge, and medium sized waterfowl. A notable exception is in records of traditional Japanese falconry, where goshawks were used more regularly on goose and crane species.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> In ancient European falconry literature, goshawks were often referred to as a yeoman's bird or the "cook's bird" due to their utility as a hunting partner as opposed to the <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon" class="mw-redirect">peregrine falcon</a>, also a prized falconry bird, but more associated with nobleman and less adapted to a variety of hunting techniques and prey types found in wooded areas. The Northern Goshawk has remained equal to the <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon" class="mw-redirect">peregrine falcon</a> in its stature and popularity in modern <a href="/wiki/Falconry" title="Falconry">falconry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-F.L._Beebe.2C_H.M._Webster_2000_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-F.L._Beebe.2C_H.M._Webster_2000-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Goshawk hunting flights in falconry typically begin from the falconer's gloved hand, where the fleeing bird or rabbit is pursued in a horizontal chase. The goshawk's flight in pursuit of prey is characterized by an intense burst of speed often followed by a binding maneuver, where the goshawk seizes its prey in an inverted position below its winged quarry. The goshawk, like other <a href="/wiki/Accipiter" title="Accipiter">accipiters</a>, shows a marked willingness to follow prey into thick vegetation, even pursuing prey on foot through brush.<sup id="cite_ref-F.L._Beebe.2C_H.M._Webster_2000_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-F.L._Beebe.2C_H.M._Webster_2000-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
