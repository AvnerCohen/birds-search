<p>The <b>Yellow-legged Gull</b> (<i>Larus michahellis</i>), sometimes referred to as Western Yellow-legged Gull (to distinguish it from eastern populations of yellow-legged <a href="/wiki/Large_white-headed_gull" title="Large white-headed gull" class="mw-redirect">large white-headed gulls</a>), is a large <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a> of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, which has only recently achieved wide recognition as a distinct species. It was formerly treated as a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of either the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Gull" title="Caspian Gull">Caspian Gull</a> <i>L. cachinnans</i>, or more broadly as a subspecies of the <a href="/wiki/European_Herring_Gull" title="European Herring Gull">Herring Gull</a> <i>L. argentatus</i>. It is named after the German zoologist <a href="/wiki/Karl_Michahelles" title="Karl Michahelles">Karl Michahelles</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-legged_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span></h2>
<p>It is now generally accepted that the Yellow-legged Gull is a full species, but until recently there was much disagreement. For example, <i><a href="/wiki/British_Birds_(magazine)" title="British Birds (magazine)">British Birds</a></i> magazine split Yellow-legged Gull from Herring Gull in 1993 but included the Caspian Gull in the former,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> but the <a href="/wiki/British_Ornithologists%27_Union" title="British Ornithologists' Union">BOU</a> in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> retained the Yellow-legged Gull as a subspecies of the Herring Gull until 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> research however suggests that Yellow-legged Gull is actually closest to <a href="/wiki/Great_Black-backed_Gull" title="Great Black-backed Gull">Great Black-backed Gull</a> <i>L. marinus</i> and <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Gull" title="Armenian Gull">Armenian Gull</a> <i>L. armenicus</i>, while Caspian Gull is closer to Herring Gull and <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Black-backed_Gull" title="Lesser Black-backed Gull">Lesser Black-backed Gull</a> <i>L. fuscus</i>, rather than being each other's closest relatives.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-collinson_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collinson-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>There are two subspecies of the Yellow-legged Gull:<sup id="cite_ref-collinson_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collinson-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>Larus michahellis michahellis</i> Naumann, 1840. Mediterranean.</li>
<li><i>Larus michahellis atlantis</i> (Dwight, 1922), syn. <i>Larus fuscus atlantis</i> Dwight, 1922. <a href="/wiki/Macaronesia" title="Macaronesia">Macaronesia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>, <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>). Birds breeding on the Atlantic coasts of <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a> (and spreading north from there) are usually also included here, but are sometimes considered to be a third subspecies <i>L. m. lusitanius</i>. Atlantic Ocean birds have darker wings and back by comparison with Mediterranean birds, creating a more pronounced contrast to the white parts.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-legged_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2010.04.11_yellow-legged_gulls,_Constanta,_Romania_096cc.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/2010.04.11_yellow-legged_gulls%2C_Constanta%2C_Romania_096cc.jpg/220px-2010.04.11_yellow-legged_gulls%2C_Constanta%2C_Romania_096cc.jpg" width="220" height="182" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2010.04.11_yellow-legged_gulls,_Constanta,_Romania_096cc.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Mating on roof-top, Constanta, Romania</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The breeding range is centred around the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. In North Africa it is common in Morocco, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> and increasing in places. Recent breeding has occurred in <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>. In the Middle East a few breed in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> with larger numbers in <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>. In Europe there are colonies all along the Mediterranean coast, and also on the Atlantic islands and coasts north to <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> and west to the Azores. It also breeds on the west side of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>; here it overlaps with the Caspian Gull but there is a difference in habitat, with the Yellow-legged Gull preferring sea cliffs and Caspian Gull on flatter shores. In recent decades birds have spread north into central and western Europe. One to four pairs have attempted to breed in southern <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> since 1995 (sometimes hybrid pairs with Lesser Black-backed Gulls), though colonisation has been very slow.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Many birds remain in the same area all year round but others migrate to spend the winter in mild areas of western Europe or head south as far as <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Gambia" title="Gambia" class="mw-redirect">Gambia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>. There is also extensive northward post-breeding dispersal in the late summer, with numbers in southern England high from July to October.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> It is reported as a vagrant to northeastern North America<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from November 2009">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-legged_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Larus_portrait.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Larus_portrait.jpg/220px-Larus_portrait.jpg" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Larus_portrait.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Nominate <i>L. m. michahellis</i>, <a href="/wiki/Elba" title="Elba">Elba</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Yellow-legged Gull is a large gull, though the size does vary, with the smallest females being scarcely larger than a <a href="/wiki/Common_Gull" title="Common Gull">Common Gull</a> and the largest males being roughly the size of a <a href="/wiki/Great_Black-backed_Gull" title="Great Black-backed Gull">Great Black-backed Gull</a>. They range in length from 52 to 68 cm (20 to 27 in) in total length, from 120 to 155 cm (47 to 61 in) in wingspan and from 550 to 1,600 g (1.2 to 3.5 lb) in weight.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Among standard measurements, the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> is 40.8 to 47.2 cm (16.1 to 18.6 in), the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> is 4.6 to 6 cm (1.8 to 2.4 in) and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> is 5.6 to 7.5 cm (2.2 to 3.0 in).<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Adults are externally similar to <a href="/wiki/Herring_Gull" title="Herring Gull">Herring Gulls</a> but have yellow legs. They have a grey back, slightly darker than Herring Gulls but lighter than <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Black-backed_Gull" title="Lesser Black-backed Gull">Lesser Black-backed Gulls</a>. They are much whiter-headed in autumn, and have more extensively black wing tips with few white spots, just as Lesser Black-backed. They have a red spot on the bill as adults, like the entire complex. There is a red ring around the eye like in the Lesser Black-backed Gull but unlike in the Herring Gull which has a dark yellow ring.</p>
<p>First-year birds have a paler head, rump and underparts than those of the Herring Gull, more closely resembling first-year Great Black-backed Gulls in plumage. They have a dark bill and eyes, pinkish grey legs, dark <a href="/wiki/Remiges" title="Remiges" class="mw-redirect">flight feathers</a> and a well-defined black band on the tail. They become lighter in the underparts and lose the upperpart pattern subsequently. By their second winter, birds are essentially feathered like adults, save for the patterned feathers remaining on the <a href="/wiki/Wing_covert" title="Wing covert" class="mw-redirect">wing coverts</a>. However, their bill tips are black, their eyes still dark, and the legs are a light yellow flesh colour.</p>
<p>The call is a loud laugh which is deeper and more nasal than the call of the Herring Gull.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-legged_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Seagull_eating_a_pigeon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Seagull_eating_a_pigeon.jpg/220px-Seagull_eating_a_pigeon.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Seagull_eating_a_pigeon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Yellow-legged Gull eating a pigeon. Barcelona.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>They are omnivores like most <i>Larus</i> gulls, and they will scavenge on rubbish tips and elsewhere, as well as seeking suitable prey in fields or on the coast, or robbing smaller gulls and other seabirds of their catches.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-legged_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span></h2>
<p>Yellow-legged Gulls usually breed in colonies. Eggs, usually three, are laid from mid March to early May and are defended vigorously by this large gull. The nest is a sometimes sparse mound of vegetation built on the ground or on cliff ledges. In some places such as <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a> they have started nesting on buildings. The eggs are incubated for 27–31 days and the young birds <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> after 35–40 days.</p>
