<p>The <b>yellow-legged gull</b> (<i>Larus michahellis</i>), sometimes referred to as western yellow-legged gull (to distinguish it from eastern populations of yellow-legged <a href="/wiki/Large_white-headed_gull" class="mw-redirect" title="Large white-headed gull">large white-headed gulls</a>), is a large <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a> of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, which has only recently achieved wide recognition as a distinct species. It was formerly treated as a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of either the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_gull" title="Caspian gull">Caspian gull</a> <i>L. cachinnans</i>, or more broadly as a subspecies of the <a href="/wiki/European_herring_gull" title="European herring gull">herring gull</a> <i>L. argentatus</i>.
The genus name is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>Larus</i> which appears to have referred to a gull or other large seabird, and the species name honours the German zoologist <a href="/wiki/Karl_Michahelles" title="Karl Michahelles">Karl Michahelles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-legged_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is now generally accepted that the yellow-legged gull is a full species, but until recently there was much disagreement. For example, <i><a href="/wiki/British_Birds_(magazine)" title="British Birds (magazine)">British Birds</a></i> magazine split the yellow-legged gull from the herring gull in 1993 but included the Caspian gull in the former,<sup id="cite_ref-Sharrock1993_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sharrock1993-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> but the <a href="/wiki/British_Ornithologists%27_Union" title="British Ornithologists&#39; Union">BOU</a> in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> retained the yellow-legged gull as a subspecies of the herring gull until 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-Sangster2007_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sangster2007-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> research, however, suggests that the yellow-legged gull is actually closest to the <a href="/wiki/Great_black-backed_gull" title="Great black-backed gull">great black-backed gull</a> <i>L. marinus</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_gull" title="Armenian gull">Armenian gull</a> <i>L. armenicus</i>, while the Caspian gull is closer to the herring gull and the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_black-backed_gull" title="Lesser black-backed gull">lesser black-backed gull</a> <i>L. fuscus</i>, rather than being each other's closest relatives.<sup id="cite_ref-Pons2005_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pons2005-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-collinson_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collinson-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are two subspecies of the yellow-legged gull:<sup id="cite_ref-collinson_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collinson-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>L. m. michahellis</i> <small><a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Naumann" title="Johann Friedrich Naumann">Naumann</a>, 1840</small>: <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies#Nominotypical_subspecies_and_subspecies_autonyms" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate</a>, found in western and southern Europe, northwestern Africa, and the Mediterranean.</li>
<li><i>L. m. atlantis</i> <small>(Dwight, 1922), syn. <i>Larus fuscus atlantis</i>, Dwight, 1922</small>: found in <a href="/wiki/Macaronesia" title="Macaronesia">Macaronesia</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>). This subspecies is also sometimes known as the <b>Atlantic gull</b> and is potentially a full species in its own right.<sup id="cite_ref-Callahan_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Callahan-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Birds breeding on the Atlantic coasts of <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a> (and spreading north from there) are usually also included here, but are sometimes considered to be a third subspecies, <i>L. m. lusitanius</i>. Atlantic Ocean birds have darker wings and back in comparison with Mediterranean birds, creating a more pronounced contrast to the white parts.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-legged_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2010.04.11_yellow-legged_gulls,_Constanta,_Romania_096cc.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/2010.04.11_yellow-legged_gulls%2C_Constanta%2C_Romania_096cc.jpg/220px-2010.04.11_yellow-legged_gulls%2C_Constanta%2C_Romania_096cc.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="182" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/2010.04.11_yellow-legged_gulls%2C_Constanta%2C_Romania_096cc.jpg/330px-2010.04.11_yellow-legged_gulls%2C_Constanta%2C_Romania_096cc.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/2010.04.11_yellow-legged_gulls%2C_Constanta%2C_Romania_096cc.jpg/440px-2010.04.11_yellow-legged_gulls%2C_Constanta%2C_Romania_096cc.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1758" data-file-height="1457" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2010.04.11_yellow-legged_gulls,_Constanta,_Romania_096cc.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mating on roof-top, Constanta, Romania</div></div></div>
<p>The breeding range is centred on the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. In North Africa it is common in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> and increasing in places. Recent breeding has occurred in <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>. In the Middle East, a few breed in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> with larger numbers in <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>. In Europe there are colonies all along the Mediterranean coast, and also on the Atlantic islands and coasts north to <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> and west to the Azores. It also breeds on the western side of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>; here it overlaps with the Caspian gull but there is a difference in habitat, with the yellow-legged gull preferring sea cliffs and the Caspian gull flatter shores. In recent decades birds have spread north into central and western Europe. One to four pairs have attempted to breed in southern <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> since 1995 (sometimes hybrid pairs with lesser black-backed gulls), though colonisation has been very slow.<sup id="cite_ref-Holling_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holling-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many birds remain in the same area all year round, but others migrate to spend the winter in mild areas of western Europe or head south as far as <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gambia" class="mw-redirect" title="Gambia">Gambia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>. There is also extensive northward post-breeding dispersal in the late summer, with numbers in southern England high from July to October.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow1998_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow1998-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> It is reported as a vagrant to northeastern North America<sup id="cite_ref-NG_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NG-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-legged_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Larus_portrait.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Larus_portrait.jpg/220px-Larus_portrait.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Larus_portrait.jpg/330px-Larus_portrait.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Larus_portrait.jpg/440px-Larus_portrait.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="732" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Larus_portrait.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nominate <i>L. m. michahellis</i>, <a href="/wiki/Elba" title="Elba">Elba</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kopf.einer.zweijaehrigen.Mittelmeermoewe.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Kopf.einer.zweijaehrigen.Mittelmeermoewe.jpg/220px-Kopf.einer.zweijaehrigen.Mittelmeermoewe.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Kopf.einer.zweijaehrigen.Mittelmeermoewe.jpg/330px-Kopf.einer.zweijaehrigen.Mittelmeermoewe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Kopf.einer.zweijaehrigen.Mittelmeermoewe.jpg/440px-Kopf.einer.zweijaehrigen.Mittelmeermoewe.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5184" data-file-height="3888" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kopf.einer.zweijaehrigen.Mittelmeermoewe.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Head of a two-year old yellow-legged gull taken at the <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Breton</a> coast</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Venice_seagull_mouth.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Venice_seagull_mouth.jpg/220px-Venice_seagull_mouth.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Venice_seagull_mouth.jpg/330px-Venice_seagull_mouth.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Venice_seagull_mouth.jpg/440px-Venice_seagull_mouth.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2161" data-file-height="2162" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Venice_seagull_mouth.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Juvenile with open <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">beak</a></div></div></div>
<p>The yellow-legged gull is a large gull, though the size does vary, with the smallest females being scarcely larger than a <a href="/wiki/Common_gull" title="Common gull">common gull</a> and the largest males being roughly the size of a <a href="/wiki/Great_black-backed_gull" title="Great black-backed gull">great black-backed gull</a>. They range in length from 52 to 68&#160;cm (20 to 27&#160;in) in total length, from 120 to 155&#160;cm (47 to 61&#160;in) in wingspan and from 550 to 1,600&#160;g (1.21 to 3.53&#160;lb) in weight.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Among standard measurements, the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> is 40.8 to 47.2&#160;cm (16.1 to 18.6&#160;in), the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> is 4.6 to 6&#160;cm (1.8 to 2.4&#160;in) and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> is 5.6 to 7.5&#160;cm (2.2 to 3.0&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Adults are externally similar to <a href="/wiki/Herring_gull" title="Herring gull">herring gulls</a> but have yellow legs. They have a grey back, slightly darker than herring gulls but lighter than <a href="/wiki/Lesser_black-backed_gull" title="Lesser black-backed gull">lesser black-backed gulls</a>. They are much whiter-headed in autumn, and have more extensively black wing tips with few white spots, just as lesser black-backed. They have a red spot on the bill as adults, like the entire complex. There is a red ring around the eye like in the lesser black-backed gull but unlike in the herring gull which has a dark yellow ring.
</p><p>First-year birds have a paler head, rump and underparts than those of the herring gull, more closely resembling first-year great black-backed gulls in plumage. They have a dark bill and eyes, pinkish grey legs, dark <a href="/wiki/Remiges" class="mw-redirect" title="Remiges">flight feathers</a> and a well-defined black band on the tail. They become lighter in the underparts and lose the upperpart pattern subsequently. By their second winter, birds are essentially feathered like adults, save for the patterned feathers remaining on the <a href="/wiki/Wing_covert" class="mw-redirect" title="Wing covert">wing coverts</a>. However, their bill tips are black, their eyes still dark, and the legs are a light yellow flesh colour.
</p><p>The call is a loud laugh which is deeper and more nasal than the call of the herring gull.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-legged_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Seagull_eating_a_pigeon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Seagull_eating_a_pigeon.jpg/220px-Seagull_eating_a_pigeon.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Seagull_eating_a_pigeon.jpg/330px-Seagull_eating_a_pigeon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Seagull_eating_a_pigeon.jpg/440px-Seagull_eating_a_pigeon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Seagull_eating_a_pigeon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Yellow-legged gull eating a <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_collared_dove" title="Eurasian collared dove">Eurasian collared dove</a> in Barcelona</div></div></div>
<p>Like most <i>Larus</i> gulls, they are omnivores and opportunistic foragers.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> They will scavenge on rubbish tips and elsewhere, as well as seeking suitable prey in fields or on the coast, or robbing smaller gulls and other seabirds of their catches. Although urban populations are generally opportunistic <a href="/wiki/Scavenger" title="Scavenger">scavengers</a>, they can shift to a <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predatory</a> diet if necessary; this was observed during the <a href="/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic_lockdown_in_Italy" title="COVID-19 pandemic lockdown in Italy">lockdown of Italy</a> in 2020, when the lack of food scraps led the yellow-legged gulls of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> to take prey as large as <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rock_dove" title="Rock dove">rock doves</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-legged_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Larus_michahellis_atlantis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.122.7.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Larus_michahellis_atlantis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.122.7.jpg/220px-Larus_michahellis_atlantis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.122.7.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Larus_michahellis_atlantis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.122.7.jpg/330px-Larus_michahellis_atlantis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.122.7.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Larus_michahellis_atlantis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.122.7.jpg/440px-Larus_michahellis_atlantis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.122.7.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3307" data-file-height="3307" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Larus_michahellis_atlantis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.122.7.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Larus michahellis atlantis</i> - <a href="/wiki/MHNT" class="mw-redirect" title="MHNT">MHNT</a></div></div></div>
<p>Yellow-legged gulls usually breed in colonies. Eggs, usually three, are laid from mid March to early May and are defended vigorously by this large gull. The nest is a sometimes sparse mound of vegetation built on the ground or on cliff ledges. In some places such as <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a> they have started nesting on buildings and even on trees. The eggs are incubated for 27–31 days and the young birds <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> after 35–40 days.
</p>
