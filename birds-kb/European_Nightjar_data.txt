<p>The <b>European nightjar</b> (<i>Caprimulgus europaeus</i>), <b>common goatsucker</b>, <b>Eurasian nightjar</b> or just <b>nightjar</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Crepuscular" class="mw-redirect" title="Crepuscular">crepuscular</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nocturnal" class="mw-redirect" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a> bird in the <a href="/wiki/Nightjar" title="Nightjar">nightjar</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> that breeds across most of Europe and the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> to Mongolia and Northwestern China. The Latin <a href="/wiki/Genus#Generic_name" title="Genus">generic name</a> refers to the old myth that the nocturnal nightjar suckled goats, causing them to cease to give milk. The six <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> differ <a href="/wiki/Cline_(biology)" title="Cline (biology)">clinally</a>, the birds becoming smaller and paler towards the east of the range. All populations are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a>. Their densely patterned grey and brown plumage makes individuals difficult to see in the daytime when they rest on the ground or perch motionless along a branch, although the male shows white patches in the wings and tail as he flies at night.
</p><p>The preferred habitat is dry, open country with some trees and small bushes, such as <a href="/wiki/Heath" title="Heath">heaths</a>, forest clearings or newly planted woodland. The male European nightjar occupies a <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territory</a> in spring and advertises his presence with a distinctive sustained churring trill from a perch. He patrols his territory with wings held in a V and tail fanned, chasing intruders while wing-clapping and calling. Wing clapping also occurs when the male chases the female in a spiralling display flight. The European nightjar does not build a nest, and its two grey and brown blotched eggs are laid directly on the ground; they hatch after about 17–21 days and the downy chicks <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> in another 16–17 days.
</p><p>The European nightjar feeds on a wide variety of flying insects, which it seizes in flight, often fly-catching from a perch. It hunts by sight, silhouetting its prey against the night sky. Its eyes are relatively large, each with a <a href="/wiki/Tapetum_lucidum" title="Tapetum lucidum">reflective layer</a>, which improves night vision. It appears not to rely on its hearing to find insects and does not <a href="/wiki/Animal_echolocation" title="Animal echolocation">echolocate</a>. Drinking and bathing take place during flight. Although it suffers a degree of predation and parasitism, the main threats to the species are habitat loss, disturbance and a reduction of its insect prey through <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticide</a> use. Despite population decreases, its large numbers and huge breeding range mean that it is classified by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> as being of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_nightjar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nightjar" title="Nightjar">nightjars</a>, Caprimulgidae, are a large <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> of mostly <a href="/wiki/Nocturnality" title="Nocturnality">nocturnal</a> <a href="/wiki/Insectivore" title="Insectivore">insect-eating</a> birds. The largest and most widespread <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> is <i><a href="/wiki/Caprimulgus" title="Caprimulgus">Caprimulgus</a></i>, characterised by stiff bristles around the mouth, long pointed wings, a comb-like middle claw and patterned plumage. The males, and sometimes females, often have white markings in the wing or tail. Within the genus, the European nightjar forms a superspecies with the <a href="/wiki/Rufous-cheeked_nightjar" title="Rufous-cheeked nightjar">rufous-cheeked nightjar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sombre_nightjar" title="Sombre nightjar">sombre nightjar</a>, African species with similar songs.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cleere_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleere-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is replaced further east in Asia by the <a href="/wiki/Jungle_nightjar" title="Jungle nightjar">jungle nightjar</a> which occupies similar habitat.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The European nightjar was described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his landmark 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a> under its current scientific name.<sup id="cite_ref-Linn_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linn-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <i>Caprimulgus</i> is derived from the Latin <i>capra</i>, "nanny goat", and <i>mulgere</i>, "to milk", referring to an old myth that nightjars suck milk from goats,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJobling201090_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJobling201090-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and the species name, <i>europaeus</i> is Latin for "European".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJobling2010153_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJobling2010153-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The common name "nightjar", first recorded in 1630, refers to the nocturnal habits of the bird, the second part of the name deriving from the distinctive churring song.<sup id="cite_ref-OEDnight_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OEDnight-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Old or local names refer to the song, "churn owl", habitat, "fern owl",<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELockwood198442,_61_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELockwood198442,_61-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> diet, "dor hawk" and "moth hawk".<sup id="cite_ref-OEDdor_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OEDdor-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_nightjar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The fossil record is deficient, but it is likely that these poorly defined subspecies diverged as global temperature rose over the last 10,000 years or so. Only one record of the species possibly dates back to the late <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHolyoakWoodcock200137_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHolyoakWoodcock200137-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_nightjar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Caprimulgus_europaeus_-_Mior_Rino_001.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Caprimulgus_europaeus_-_Mior_Rino_001.jpg/220px-Caprimulgus_europaeus_-_Mior_Rino_001.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="222" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Caprimulgus_europaeus_-_Mior_Rino_001.jpg/330px-Caprimulgus_europaeus_-_Mior_Rino_001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Caprimulgus_europaeus_-_Mior_Rino_001.jpg/440px-Caprimulgus_europaeus_-_Mior_Rino_001.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="1034" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Caprimulgus_europaeus_-_Mior_Rino_001.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Young bird hissing with mouth open in threat display<sup id="cite_ref-vanGrouw_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vanGrouw-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The European nightjar is 24.5–28&#160;cm (9.6–11.0&#160;in) long, with a 52–59&#160;cm (20–23&#160;in) wingspan. The male weighs 51–101&#160;g (1.8–3.6&#160;oz) and the female 67–95&#160;g (2.4–3.4&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The adult of the nominate subspecies has greyish-brown upperparts with dark streaking, a pale buff hindneck collar and a white moustachial line. The closed wing is grey with buff spotting, and the underparts are greyish-brown, with brown barring and buff spots. The bill is blackish, the <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">iris</a> is dark brown and the legs and feet are brown.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The flight on long pointed wings is noiseless, due to their soft plumage, and very buoyant.<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Flying birds can be sexed since the male has a white wing patch across three <a href="/wiki/Primary_feather" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary feather">primary feathers</a> and white tips to the two outer tail feathers, whereas females do not show any white in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Chicks have downy brown and buff plumage, and the fledged young are similar in appearance to the adult female. Adults <a href="/wiki/Moulting" title="Moulting">moult</a> their body feathers from June onwards after breeding, suspend the process while migrating, and replace the tail and <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">flight feathers</a> on the wintering grounds. Moult is completed between January and March. Immature birds follow a similar moult strategy to the adults unless they are from late broods, in which case the entire moult may take place in Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Cleere_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleere-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other nightjar species occur in parts of the breeding and wintering ranges. The <a href="/wiki/Red-necked_nightjar" title="Red-necked nightjar">red-necked nightjar</a> breeds in <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberia</a> and northwest Africa; it is larger, greyer and longer winged than the European nightjar, and has a broad buff collar and more conspicuous white markings on the wings and tail.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECleereNurney1998227–229_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECleereNurney1998227–229-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Wintering European nightjars in Africa may overlap with the related rufous-cheeked and sombre nightjars. Both have a more prominent buff hind-neck collar and more spotting on the wing <a href="/wiki/Covert_feather" title="Covert feather">coverts</a>. The sombre nightjar is also much darker than its European cousin.<sup id="cite_ref-Cleere_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleere-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Given their nocturnal habits, cryptic plumage and difficulty of observation, nightjar observation "is as much a matter of fortune as effort or knowledge".<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_nightjar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The male European nightjar's song is a sustained churring trill, given continuously for up to 10&#160;minutes with occasional shifts of speed or pitch. It is delivered from a perch, and the male may move around its <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territory</a> using different song posts. Singing is more frequent at dawn and dusk than during the night, and is reduced in poor weather. The song may end with a bubbling trill and wing-clapping, perhaps indicating the approach of a female. Migrating or wintering birds sometimes sing.<sup id="cite_ref-Cleere_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleere-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Individual male nightjars can be identified by analysing the rate and length of the pulses in their songs.<sup id="cite_ref-Rebbeck_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rebbeck-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Even a singing male may be hard to locate; the perched bird is difficult to spot in low light conditions, and the song has a <a href="/wiki/Ventriloquism" title="Ventriloquism">ventriloquial</a> quality as the singer turns his head.<sup id="cite_ref-cocker_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cocker-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The song is easily audible at 200&#160;m (660&#160;ft), and can be heard at 600&#160;m (2,000&#160;ft) in good conditions; it can be confused with the very similar sound of the <a href="/wiki/Gryllotalpa_gryllotalpa" title="Gryllotalpa gryllotalpa">European mole cricket</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The female does not sing, but when on the wing, both sexes give a short <i>cuick, cuick</i> call, also used when chasing predators. Other calls include variations on a sharp <i>chuck</i> when alarmed, hisses given by adults when handled or chicks when disturbed, and an assortment of <i>wuk, wuk, wuk</i>, muffled <i>oak, oak</i> and murmurs given at the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-Cleere_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleere-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Large young have a threat display with the mouth opened wide while hissing loudly.<sup id="cite_ref-vanGrouw_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vanGrouw-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_nightjar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Heathland,_near_Bere_Regis_-_geograph.org.uk_-_587734.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Heathland%2C_near_Bere_Regis_-_geograph.org.uk_-_587734.jpg/220px-Heathland%2C_near_Bere_Regis_-_geograph.org.uk_-_587734.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Heathland%2C_near_Bere_Regis_-_geograph.org.uk_-_587734.jpg/330px-Heathland%2C_near_Bere_Regis_-_geograph.org.uk_-_587734.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Heathland%2C_near_Bere_Regis_-_geograph.org.uk_-_587734.jpg/440px-Heathland%2C_near_Bere_Regis_-_geograph.org.uk_-_587734.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Heathland,_near_Bere_Regis_-_geograph.org.uk_-_587734.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Heath" title="Heath">Heathland</a> is a favoured habitat</div></div></div>
<p>The breeding range of the European nightjar comprises Europe north to around latitude 64°N and Asia north to about 60°N and east to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a> and eastern Mongolia. The southern limits are northwestern Africa, Iraq, Iran and the northwestern <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cleere_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleere-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This nightjar formerly bred in Syria and Lebanon.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>All populations are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, and most birds winter in Africa south of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>, with just a few records from Pakistan, Morocco and Israel. Migration is mainly at night, singly or in loose groups of up to twenty birds. European breeders cross the Mediterranean and North Africa, whereas eastern populations move through the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and East Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Cleere_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleere-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BWP_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Some Asian birds may therefore cross 100° of <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> on their travels.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Most birds start their migration at the time of a full moon.<sup id="cite_ref-moon_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moon-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
Most birds winter in eastern or southeastern Africa,<sup id="cite_ref-Cleere_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleere-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> although individuals of the nominate race have been recently discovered wintering in the Democratic Republic of the Congo; records elsewhere in West Africa may be wintering birds of this subspecies or <i>C. e. meridionalis</i>. Most autumn migration takes place from August to September, and the birds return to the breeding grounds by May.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Recent tracking data has revealed that European nightjars have a loop migration from Western Europe to sub-equatorial Africa where they have to cross several ecological barriers (Mediterranean Sea, Sahara and the Central African Tropical Rainforest). Individuals use similar stop-over sites as do other European migrants.<sup id="cite_ref-evans_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-evans-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Vagrants have occurred in Iceland, the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, the Seychelles,<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cleere_3-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleere-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
The European nightjar is a bird of dry, open country with some trees and small bushes, such as <a href="/wiki/Heath" title="Heath">heaths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_land" title="Common land">commons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moorland</a>, forest clearings or felled or newly planted woodland. When breeding, it avoids treeless or heavily wooded areas, cities, mountains, and farmland, but it often feeds over wetlands, cultivation or gardens. In winter it uses a wider range of open habitats including <a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">acacia</a> <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a>, sandy country and highlands. It has been recorded at altitudes of 2,800&#160;m (9,200&#160;ft) on the breeding grounds and 5,000&#160;m (16,000&#160;ft) in the wintering areas.<sup id="cite_ref-Cleere_3-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleere-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_nightjar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Caprimulgus_europaeus_1200x855.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Caprimulgus_europaeus_1200x855.jpg/220px-Caprimulgus_europaeus_1200x855.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Caprimulgus_europaeus_1200x855.jpg/330px-Caprimulgus_europaeus_1200x855.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Caprimulgus_europaeus_1200x855.jpg/440px-Caprimulgus_europaeus_1200x855.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="855" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Caprimulgus_europaeus_1200x855.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Roosting lengthwise on a branch to enhance camouflage</div></div></div>
<p>The European nightjar is <a href="/wiki/Crepuscular" class="mw-redirect" title="Crepuscular">crepuscular</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nocturnal" class="mw-redirect" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a>. During the day it rests on the ground, often in a partly shaded location, or perches motionless lengthwise along an open branch or a similar low perch. The <a href="/wiki/Crypsis" title="Crypsis">cryptic</a> plumage makes it difficult to see in the daytime, and birds on the ground, if they are not already in shade, will turn occasionally to face the sun thereby minimising their shadow.<sup id="cite_ref-Cleere_3-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleere-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarthelDougalis2008108_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarthelDougalis2008108-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> If it feels threatened, the nightjar flattens itself to the ground with eyes almost closed, flying only when the intruder is 2–5&#160;m (7–16&#160;ft) away. It may call or wing clap as it goes, and land as far as 40&#160;m (130&#160;ft) from where it was flushed. In the wintering area it often roosts on the ground but also uses tree branches up to 20&#160;m (66&#160;ft) high. Roost sites at both the breeding and wintering grounds are used regularly if they are undisturbed, sometimes for weeks at a time.<sup id="cite_ref-Holyoak496_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holyoak496-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Like other nightjars, it will sit on roads or paths during the night and hover to investigate large intruders such as deer or humans. It may be mobbed by birds while there is still light, and by bats, other nightjar species or <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_woodcock" title="Eurasian woodcock">Eurasian woodcocks</a> during the night. Owls and other predators such as <a href="/wiki/Red_fox" title="Red fox">red foxes</a> will be mobbed by both male and female European nightjars.<sup id="cite_ref-Cleere_3-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleere-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Like other aerial birds, such as <a href="/wiki/Swift" title="Swift">swifts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swallow" title="Swallow">swallows</a>, nightjars make a quick plunge into water to wash.<sup id="cite_ref-Burton_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burton-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> They have a unique serrated comb-like structure on the middle claw, which is used to <a href="/wiki/Preening_(bird)" title="Preening (bird)">preen</a> and perhaps to remove parasites.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In cold or inclement weather, several nightjar species can slow their <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> and go into <a href="/wiki/Torpor" title="Torpor">torpor</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Fletcher_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fletcher-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> notably the <a href="/wiki/Common_poorwill" title="Common poorwill">common poorwill</a>, which will maintain that state for weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECleereNurney1998187–189_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECleereNurney1998187–189-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The European nightjar has been observed in captivity to be able to maintain a state of torpor for at least eight days without harm, but the relevance of this to wild birds is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-Holyoak496_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holyoak496-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_nightjar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Caprimulgus_europaeus_-Beldibi,_Mugla,_Turkey_-two_eggs_in_a_nest-8_(3).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Caprimulgus_europaeus_-Beldibi%2C_Mugla%2C_Turkey_-two_eggs_in_a_nest-8_%283%29.jpg/220px-Caprimulgus_europaeus_-Beldibi%2C_Mugla%2C_Turkey_-two_eggs_in_a_nest-8_%283%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Caprimulgus_europaeus_-Beldibi%2C_Mugla%2C_Turkey_-two_eggs_in_a_nest-8_%283%29.jpg/330px-Caprimulgus_europaeus_-Beldibi%2C_Mugla%2C_Turkey_-two_eggs_in_a_nest-8_%283%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Caprimulgus_europaeus_-Beldibi%2C_Mugla%2C_Turkey_-two_eggs_in_a_nest-8_%283%29.jpg/440px-Caprimulgus_europaeus_-Beldibi%2C_Mugla%2C_Turkey_-two_eggs_in_a_nest-8_%283%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Caprimulgus_europaeus_-Beldibi,_Mugla,_Turkey_-two_eggs_in_a_nest-8_(3).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A clutch of two eggs on the ground in Turkey</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Caprimulgus_europaeus_MWNH_1236.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Caprimulgus_europaeus_MWNH_1236.JPG/220px-Caprimulgus_europaeus_MWNH_1236.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Caprimulgus_europaeus_MWNH_1236.JPG/330px-Caprimulgus_europaeus_MWNH_1236.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Caprimulgus_europaeus_MWNH_1236.JPG/440px-Caprimulgus_europaeus_MWNH_1236.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1810" data-file-height="1215" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Caprimulgus_europaeus_MWNH_1236.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>Breeding is normally from late May to August, but may be significantly earlier in northwest Africa or western Pakistan. Returning males arrive about two weeks before the females and establish territories which they patrol with wings held in a V-shape and tail fanned, chasing intruders while wing-clapping and calling. Fights may take place in flight or on the ground. The male's display flight involves a similar wing and tail position with frequent wing clapping as he follows the female in a rising spiral. If she lands, he continues to display with bobbing and fluttering until the female spreads her wings and tail for copulation. Mating occasionally takes place on a raised perch instead of the ground. In good habitat, there may be 20&#160;pairs per square kilometre (50&#160;per square mile).<sup id="cite_ref-Cleere_3-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleere-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The European nightjar is normally <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a>. There is no nest, and the eggs are laid on the ground among plants or tree roots, or beneath a bush or tree. The site may be bare ground, leaf litter or pine needles, and is used for a number of years. The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is usually one or two whitish eggs, rarely unmarked, but normally blotched with browns and greys.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The eggs average 32&#160;mm ×&#160;22&#160;mm (1.26&#160;in ×&#160;0.87&#160;in) and weigh 8.4&#160;g (0.30&#160;oz), of which 6% is shell.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several nightjar species are known to be more likely to lay in the two weeks before the full moon than the during the waning moon, possibly because insect food may be easier to catch as the moon waxes.<sup id="cite_ref-mills_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mills-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> A study specifically looking at the European nightjar showed that the phase of the moon is a factor for birds laying in June, but not for earlier breeders.<sup id="cite_ref-crick_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crick-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> This strategy means that a second brood in July would also have a favourable lunar aspect.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHolyoakWoodcock2001499_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHolyoakWoodcock2001499-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Eggs are laid 36–48 hours apart, and incubation, mainly by the female, starts with the first egg. The male may incubate for short periods, especially around dawn or dusk, but spends the day roosting, sometimes outside his territory or close to other males. If the female is disturbed while breeding, she runs or flutters along the ground feigning injury until she has drawn the intruder away. She may also move the eggs a short distance with her bill. Each egg hatches after about 17–21 days. The <a href="/wiki/Precocial" class="mw-redirect" title="Precocial">semi-precocial</a> downy chicks are mobile when hatched, but are brooded to keep them warm. They <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> in 16–17 days and become independent of the adults around 32&#160;days after hatching. A second brood may be raised by early nesting pairs, in which case the female leaves the first brood a few days before they fledge; the male then cares for the first brood and assists with the second. Both adults feed the young with balls of insects which are either regurgitated into the chick's mouth or pecked by the chick from the adult's open bill.<sup id="cite_ref-Cleere_3-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleere-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Broods that fail tend to do so during incubation. One English study showed that only 14.5% of eggs survived to hatching, but once that stage was reached the chances of fledging successfully were high.<sup id="cite_ref-Murison_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Murison-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> European nightjars breed when aged one year, and typically live four years. The adult annual survival rate is 70%, but that for juveniles is unknown. The maximum known age in the wild is just over 12&#160;years.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_nightjar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Caprimulgus_europaeus_-France_-night-6_(1).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Caprimulgus_europaeus_-France_-night-6_%281%29.jpg/220px-Caprimulgus_europaeus_-France_-night-6_%281%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Caprimulgus_europaeus_-France_-night-6_%281%29.jpg/330px-Caprimulgus_europaeus_-France_-night-6_%281%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Caprimulgus_europaeus_-France_-night-6_%281%29.jpg/440px-Caprimulgus_europaeus_-France_-night-6_%281%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1816" data-file-height="1270" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Caprimulgus_europaeus_-France_-night-6_(1).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Perching in a tree at night in France. The <a href="/wiki/Eyeshine" class="mw-redirect" title="Eyeshine">eyeshine</a> is due to reflected light.</div></div></div>
<p>The European nightjar feeds on a wide variety of flying insects, including <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mantis" title="Mantis">mantises</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dragonfly" title="Dragonfly">dragonflies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cockroach" title="Cockroach">cockroaches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>. It will pick <a href="/wiki/Glowworm" title="Glowworm">glowworms</a> off vegetation. It consumes grit to aid with digesting its prey, but any plant material and non-flying invertebrates consumed are taken inadvertently while hunting other food items. Young chicks have been known to eat their own <a href="/wiki/Faeces" class="mw-redirect" title="Faeces">faeces</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cleere_3-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleere-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Birds hunt over open habitats and woodland clearings and edges, and may be attracted to insects concentrating around artificial lights, near farm animals or over stagnant ponds. They usually feed at night, but occasionally venture out on overcast days. Nightjars pursue insects with a light twisting flight, or flycatch from a perch; they may rarely take prey off the ground. They drink by dipping to the water surface as they fly.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Breeding European nightjars travel on average 3.1&#160;km (3,400&#160;yd) from their nests to feed.<sup id="cite_ref-alex_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alex-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Migrating birds live off their fat reserves.<sup id="cite_ref-Cleere_3-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleere-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Zata.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Zata.jpg/220px-Zata.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Zata.jpg/330px-Zata.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Zata.jpg/440px-Zata.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1680" data-file-height="1120" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Zata.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bristles around the mouth may help in feeding</div></div></div>
<p>European nightjars hunt by sight, silhouetting their prey against the night sky. They tend to flycatch from a perch on moonlit nights, but fly continuously on darker nights when prey is harder to see;<sup id="cite_ref-crick_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crick-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Tracking experiments show that feeding activity more than doubles on moonlit nights.<sup id="cite_ref-moon_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moon-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Hunting frequency reduces in the middle of the night.<sup id="cite_ref-Holyoak496_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holyoak496-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Although they have very small bills, the mouth can be opened very wide as they catch insects.<sup id="cite_ref-Holyoak59_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holyoak59-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> They have long sensitive bristles around the mouth, which may help to locate or funnel prey into the mouth.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Indigestible parts of insects, such as the <a href="/wiki/Chitin" title="Chitin">chitin</a> <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeleton</a>, are regurgitated as <a href="/wiki/Pellet_(ornithology)" title="Pellet (ornithology)">pellets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Holyoak496_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holyoak496-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Nightjars have relatively large eyes, each with a <a href="/wiki/Tapetum_lucidum" title="Tapetum lucidum">tapetum lucidum</a> (reflective layer behind the <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">retina</a>) that makes the eyes shine in torchlight and improves light detection at dusk, dawn and in moonlight.<sup id="cite_ref-Holyoak11_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holyoak11-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The retinas of nocturnal birds, including nightjars, are adapted for sight in low-light levels and have a higher density of <a href="/wiki/Rod_cell" title="Rod cell">rod cells</a> and far fewer <a href="/wiki/Cone_cell" title="Cone cell">cone cells</a> compared to those of most diurnal birds.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESinclair198596_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESinclair198596-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> These adaptations favour good night vision at the expense of colour discrimination<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoots20064_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoots20064-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In many day-flying species, light passes through coloured oil droplets within the cone cells to improve colour vision.<sup id="cite_ref-Burton_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burton-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In contrast, nightjars have a limited number of cone cells, either lacking or having only a few oil droplets.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHolyoakWoodcock200167_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHolyoakWoodcock200167-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The nocturnal eyesight of nightjars is probably equivalent to that of owls. Although they have good hearing, European nightjars appear not to rely on sound to find insects, and nightjars do not <a href="/wiki/Animal_echolocation" title="Animal echolocation">echolocate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Holyoak11_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holyoak11-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators_and_parasites">Predators and parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_nightjar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Predators and parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The eggs and chicks of this ground-nesting bird are vulnerable to predation by red foxes, <a href="/wiki/Pine_marten" class="mw-redirect" title="Pine marten">pine martens</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Holyoak12_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holyoak12-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/European_hedgehog" title="European hedgehog">European hedgehogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Least_weasel" title="Least weasel">least weasels</a> and domestic dogs, and by birds including <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_magpie" title="Eurasian magpie">Eurasian magpies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_jay" title="Eurasian jay">Eurasian jays</a> and owls.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Snakes, such as <a href="/wiki/Common_adder" class="mw-redirect" title="Common adder">common adders</a>, may also rob the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-Holyoak12_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holyoak12-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Adults may be caught by birds of prey including <a href="/wiki/Northern_goshawk" title="Northern goshawk">northern goshawks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hen_harrier" title="Hen harrier">hen harriers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">Eurasian sparrowhawks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_buzzard" title="Common buzzard">common buzzards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">peregrine</a><sup id="cite_ref-Holyoak12_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holyoak12-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Sooty_falcon" title="Sooty falcon">sooty falcons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_4-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Parasites recorded on the European nightjar include a single species of <a href="/wiki/Mallophaga" title="Mallophaga">biting louse</a> found on the wings,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERothschildClay1957222_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERothschildClay1957222-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> and a <a href="/wiki/Analgoidea" title="Analgoidea">feather mite</a> that occurs only on the white wing markings.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERothschildClay1957225_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERothschildClay1957225-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Avian_malaria" title="Avian malaria">Avian malaria</a> has also been recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERothschildClay1957150_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERothschildClay1957150-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Leucocytozoon" title="Leucocytozoon">leucocytozoon</a> blood parasite <i><a href="/wiki/Leucocytozoon_caprimulgi" title="Leucocytozoon caprimulgi">L. caprimulgi</a></i> is rare in the European nightjar. Its scarcity and the fact that it is the only one of its genus found in nightjars support the suggestion that it has crossed over from close relatives that normally infect owls.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEValkiunas2004809_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEValkiunas2004809-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_nightjar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Estimates of the European population of the European nightjar range from 470,000 to more than 1&#160;million birds, suggesting a total global population of 2–6&#160;million individuals. Although there appears to be a fall in numbers, it is not rapid enough to trigger the vulnerability criteria. The huge breeding range and population mean that this species is classified by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> as being of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The largest breeding populations are in Russia (up to 500,000&#160;pairs), Spain (112,000 pairs) and Belarus (60,000 pairs). There have been declines in much of the range, but especially in northwestern Europe. The loss of insect prey through <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticide</a> use, coupled with disturbance, collision with vehicles and habitat loss have contributed to the falling population.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_4-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> As ground-nesting birds, they are adversely affected by disturbance, especially by domestic dogs, which may destroy the nest or advertise its presence to crows or predatory mammals. Breeding success is higher in areas with no public access; where access is permitted, and particularly where dog owners allow their pets to run loose, successful nests tend to be far from footpaths or human habitation.<sup id="cite_ref-Murison_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Murison-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ibis_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibis-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Britain and elsewhere, commercial forestry has created new habitat which has increased numbers, but these gains are likely to be temporary as the woodland develops and becomes unsuitable for nightjars.<sup id="cite_ref-ibis_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibis-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> In the United Kingdom, it is red-listed as a cause for concern,<sup id="cite_ref-bto_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and in Ireland it is close to extinction.<sup id="cite_ref-perry_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-perry-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_nightjar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Caprimulgus_europaeus_-Coloured_figures_of_the_birds_of_the_British_Islands_(1885-1897).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Caprimulgus_europaeus_-Coloured_figures_of_the_birds_of_the_British_Islands_%281885-1897%29.jpg/220px-Caprimulgus_europaeus_-Coloured_figures_of_the_birds_of_the_British_Islands_%281885-1897%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Caprimulgus_europaeus_-Coloured_figures_of_the_birds_of_the_British_Islands_%281885-1897%29.jpg/330px-Caprimulgus_europaeus_-Coloured_figures_of_the_birds_of_the_British_Islands_%281885-1897%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Caprimulgus_europaeus_-Coloured_figures_of_the_birds_of_the_British_Islands_%281885-1897%29.jpg/440px-Caprimulgus_europaeus_-Coloured_figures_of_the_birds_of_the_British_Islands_%281885-1897%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3904" data-file-height="2518" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Caprimulgus_europaeus_-Coloured_figures_of_the_birds_of_the_British_Islands_(1885-1897).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A nineteenth-century lithograph of a painting by <a href="/wiki/J.G._Keulemans" class="mw-redirect" title="J.G. Keulemans">J.G. Keulemans</a>. It is captioned "Nightjar, goatsucker, or fern-owl", alternative old names for the European nightjar</div></div></div>
<p>Poets sometimes use the nightjar as an indicator of warm summer nights, as in <a href="/wiki/George_Meredith" title="George Meredith">George Meredith's</a> "Love in the Valley"
<i>Lone on the fir-branch, his rattle-notes unvaried/Brooding o'er the gloom, spins the brown eve-jar</i>, <a href="/wiki/Dylan_Thomas" title="Dylan Thomas">Dylan Thomas's</a>
"<a href="/wiki/Fern_Hill" title="Fern Hill">Fern Hill</a>" <i>and all the night long I heard, blessed among stables, the nightjars/flying with the ricks</i>, or <a href="/wiki/William_Wordsworth" title="William Wordsworth">Wordsworth's</a> "Calm is the fragrant air", <i>The busy dor-hawk chases the white moth/With burring note</i>. Nightjars sing only when perched, and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hardy" title="Thomas Hardy">Thomas Hardy</a> referenced the eerie silence of a hunting bird in "Afterwards": <i>If it be in the dusk when, like an eyelid's soundless blink/The dewfall-hawk comes crossing the shades to alight/Upon the wind-warped upland thorn</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-cocker_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cocker-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Caprimulgus</i> and the old name "goatsucker" both refer to the myth, old even in the time of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, that nightjars suckled from nanny goats, which subsequently ceased to give milk or went blind.<sup id="cite_ref-cocker_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cocker-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> This ancient belief is reflected in nightjar names in other European languages, such as German <i>Ziegenmelker</i>, Polish <i>kozodój</i> and Italian <i>succiacapre</i>, which also mean goatsucker, but despite its antiquity, it has no equivalents in Arab, Chinese or Hindu traditions.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECockerTipling2013285–291_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECockerTipling2013285–291-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> It is likely that the birds were attracted by insects around domestic animals, and, as strange nocturnal creatures, were then blamed for any misfortune that befell the beast.<sup id="cite_ref-cocker_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cocker-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Another old name, "puckeridge", was used to refer to both the bird and a disease of farm animals,<sup id="cite_ref-oedpucker_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oedpucker-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> the latter actually caused by <a href="/wiki/Botfly" title="Botfly">botfly</a> <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> under the skin.<sup id="cite_ref-Blaine_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blaine-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> "Lich fowl" (corpse bird) is an old name which reflects the superstitions that surrounded this strange nocturnal bird.<sup id="cite_ref-oedlich_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oedlich-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Like "gabble ratchet" (corpse hound), it may refer to the belief that the souls of unbaptised children were doomed to wander in nightjar form until <a href="/wiki/Last_Judgment" title="Last Judgment">Judgement Day</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-OConnor_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OConnor-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
