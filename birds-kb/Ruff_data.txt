<p>The <b>Ruff</b> (<i>Philomachus pugnax</i>) is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wading bird</a> that breeds in <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a> and wet <a href="/wiki/Meadow" title="Meadow">meadows</a> across northern <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>. This highly <a href="/wiki/Gregarious" title="Gregarious" class="mw-redirect">gregarious</a> <a href="/wiki/Scolopacidae" title="Scolopacidae" class="mw-redirect">sandpiper</a> is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and sometimes forms huge flocks in its winter grounds, which include southern and western Europe, Africa, southern Asia and Australia. It is usually considered to be the <a href="/wiki/Monotypic_taxon" title="Monotypic taxon">only member</a> of its <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Broad-billed_Sandpiper" title="Broad-billed Sandpiper">Broad-billed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sharp-tailed_Sandpiper" title="Sharp-tailed Sandpiper">Sharp-tailed Sandpipers</a> are its closest relatives.</p>
<p>The Ruff is a long-necked, pot-bellied bird. This species shows marked <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism#Birds" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a>; the male is much larger than the female (the reeve), and has a breeding <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> that includes brightly coloured head tufts, bare orange facial skin, extensive black on the breast, and the large collar of ornamental feathers that inspired this bird's English name. The female and the non-breeding male have grey-brown upperparts and mainly white underparts. Three differently plumaged types of male, including a rare form that mimics the female, use a variety of strategies to obtain mating opportunities at a <a href="/wiki/Lek_(mating_arena)" title="Lek (mating arena)" class="mw-redirect">lek</a>, and the colourful head and neck feathers are erected as part of the elaborate main courting display. The female has one brood per year and lays four <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> in a well-hidden ground <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a>, incubating the eggs and rearing the chicks, which are mobile soon after hatching, on her own. Predators of wader chicks and eggs include mammals such as <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">foxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feral_cat" title="Feral cat">feral cats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stoat" title="Stoat">stoats</a>, and birds such as large <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corvidae" title="Corvidae">corvids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skua" title="Skua">skuas</a>.</p>
<p>The Ruff <a href="/wiki/Forage" title="Forage">forages</a> in wet grassland and soft mud, probing or searching by sight for edible items. It primarily feeds on insects, especially in the breeding season, but it will consume plant material, including <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> and maize, on migration and in winter. Classified as "<a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">least concern</a>" on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> criteria, the global <a href="/wiki/Bird_conservation" title="Bird conservation">conservation</a> concerns are relatively low because of the large numbers that breed in Scandinavia and the Arctic. However, the range in much of Europe is contracting because of land drainage, increased fertiliser use, the loss of mown or grazed breeding sites, and over-hunting. This decline has seen it listed in the <i><a href="/wiki/Agreement_on_the_Conservation_of_African-Eurasian_Migratory_Waterbirds" title="Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a></i> (AEWA).</p>

<p>The Ruff is a <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the large family <a href="/wiki/Scolopacidae" title="Scolopacidae" class="mw-redirect">Scolopacidae</a>, the typical shorebirds. It is the sole member of the genus <i>Philomachus</i>, and recent research suggests that its closest relatives are the Broad-billed Sandpiper <i>Limicola falcinellus</i> and the Sharp-tailed Sandpiper <i>Calidris acuminata</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-thomas_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thomas-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> It has no recognised subspecies or geographical variants.<sup id="cite_ref-hayman_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayman-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> in 1758 as <i>Tringa pugnax</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-linn_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linn-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> It was moved into its current genus by <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">naturalist</a> <a href="/wiki/Blasius_Merrem" title="Blasius Merrem">Blasius Merrem</a> in 1804.<sup id="cite_ref-merrem_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-merrem-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Both parts of the binomial name refer to the aggressive behaviour of the bird at its <a href="/wiki/Lek_(mating_arena)" title="Lek (mating arena)" class="mw-redirect">mating arenas</a>; <i>Philomachus</i> is derived from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>philo-</i> "loving" and <i>machē</i> "battle",<sup id="cite_ref-Liddell1980_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Liddell1980-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> and <i>pugnax</i> from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> term for "combative".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The original English name for this bird, dating back to at least 1465, is the <i>ree</i>, perhaps derived from a dialectical term meaning "frenzied";<sup id="cite_ref-lockwood_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lockwood-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> a later name <i>reeve</i>, which is still used for the female, is of unknown origin, but may be derived from the <a href="/wiki/Sheriff" title="Sheriff">shire-reeve</a>, a feudal officer, likening the male's flamboyant plumage to the official's robes. The current name was first recorded in 1634, and is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Ruff_(clothing)" title="Ruff (clothing)">ruff</a>, an exaggerated collar fashionable from the mid-sixteenth century to the mid-seventeenth century, since the male bird's neck ornamental feathers resemble the neck-wear.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The Ruff has a distinctive <a href="/wiki/Sauce_boat" title="Sauce boat">gravy boat</a> appearance, with a small head, medium-length bill, longish neck and pot-bellied body. It has long legs that are variable in colour but usually yellow or orange. In flight, it has a deeper, slower wing stroke than other waders of a similar size, and displays a thin, indistinct white bar on the wing, and white ovals on the sides of the tail .<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> This species shows <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a>. Although a small percentage of males resemble females,<sup id="cite_ref-jukema_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jukema-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> the typical male is much larger than the female and has an elaborate breeding plumage. He is 29–32&#160;cm (11.4–12.6&#160;in) long with a 54–60&#160;cm (21.25–23.6&#160;in) wingspan,<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> and weighs about 180&#160;g (6.4&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-bto_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> In the May-to-June breeding season, the typical male's legs, bill and warty bare facial skin are orange, and he has distinctive head tufts and a neck ruff. These ornaments vary on individual birds, being black, chestnut or white, with the colouring solid, barred or irregular.<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The grey-brown back has a scale-like pattern, often with black or chestnut feathers, and the underparts are white with extensive black on the breast.<sup id="cite_ref-hayman_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayman-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The extreme variability of the main breeding plumage is thought to have developed to aid individual recognition in a species that has communal breeding displays, but is usually mute.<sup id="cite_ref-lank7_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lank7-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Outside the breeding season, the typical male's head and neck decorations and the bare facial skin are lost and the legs and bill become duller. The upperparts are grey-brown, and the underparts are white with grey mottling on the breast and flanks.<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The female, or "reeve", is 22–26&#160;cm (8.7–10.2&#160;in) long with a 46–49&#160;cm (18.1–19.32&#160;in) wingspan,<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> and weighs about 110&#160;g (3.9&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-bto_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> In breeding plumage, she has grey-brown upperparts with white-fringed, dark-centred feathers. The breast and flanks are variably blotched with black. In winter, her plumage is similar to that of the male, but the sexes are distinguishable on size.<sup id="cite_ref-hayman_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayman-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The plumage of the juvenile Ruff resembles the non-breeding adult, but has upperparts with a neat, scale-like pattern with dark feather centres, and a strong buff tinge to the underparts.<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Typical adult male Ruffs start to <a href="/wiki/Moult" title="Moult" class="mw-redirect">moult</a> into the main display plumage before their return to the breeding areas, and the proportion of birds with head and neck decorations gradually increases through the spring. Second-year birds lag behind full adults in developing breeding plumage. They have a lower body mass and a slower weight increase than full adults, and perhaps the demands made on their energy reserves during the migration flight are the main reason of the delayed moult.<sup id="cite_ref-Karlionova1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Karlionova1-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Ruffs of both sexes have an additional moult stage between the winter and final summer plumages, a phenomenon also seen in the <a href="/wiki/Bar-tailed_Godwit" title="Bar-tailed Godwit">Bar-tailed Godwit</a>. Before developing the full display finery with coloured ruff and tufts, the males replace part of their winter plumage with striped feathers. Females also develop a mix of winter and striped feathers before reaching their summer appearance. The final male breeding plumage results from the replacement of both winter and striped feathers, but the female retains the striped feathers and replaces only the winter feathers to reach her summer plumage. The striped prenuptial plumages may represent the original breeding appearance of this species, the male's showy nuptial feathers evolving later under strong <a href="/wiki/Sexual_selection" title="Sexual selection">sexual selection</a> pressures.<sup id="cite_ref-jukema2_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jukema2-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Adult males and most adult females start their pre-winter moult before returning south, but complete most feather replacement on the wintering grounds. In <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, males moult 3–4 weeks ahead of the females, finishing before December, whereas females typically complete feather replacement during December and early January. Juveniles moult from their first summer body plumage into winter plumage during late September to November, and later undergo a pre-breeding moult similar in timing and duration to that of the adults, and often producing as brightly coloured an appearance.<sup id="cite_ref-pearson_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pearson-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Two other waders can be confused with the Ruff. The juvenile <a href="/wiki/Sharp-tailed_Sandpiper" title="Sharp-tailed Sandpiper">Sharp-tailed Sandpiper</a> is a little smaller than a juvenile female Ruff and has a similar rich orange-buff breast, but the Ruff is slimmer with a longer neck and legs, a rounder head, and a much plainer face.<sup id="cite_ref-BB1980_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB1980-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Buff-breasted_Sandpiper" title="Buff-breasted Sandpiper">Buff-breasted Sandpiper</a> also resembles a small juvenile Ruff, but even the female Ruff is noticeably larger than the sandpiper, with a longer bill, more rotund body and scaly-patterned upperparts.<sup id="cite_ref-BB76_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB76-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Philomachus_pugnax_naumann.jpg" class="image"><img alt="  A white-collared satellite male and a brown-collared territorial male are displaying to each other. <div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Philomachus_pugnax_naumann.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

</div>

<p>The Ruff is a migratory species, breeding in wetlands in colder regions of northern Eurasia, and spends the northern winter in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>, mainly in Africa. Some Siberian breeders undertake an annual round trip of up to 30,000&#160;km (18,500&#160;mi) to the West African wintering grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-Vaillancourt_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vaillancourt-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> There is a limited overlap of the summer and winter ranges in western Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-hayman_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayman-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The Ruff breeds in extensive lowland freshwater marshes and damp grasslands.<sup id="cite_ref-hayman_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayman-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> It avoids barren <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> and areas badly affected by severe weather, preferring hummocky marshes and deltas with shallow water. The wetter areas provide a source of food, the mounds and slopes may be used for <a href="/wiki/Lek_(mating_arena)" title="Lek (mating arena)" class="mw-redirect">leks</a>, and dry areas with <a href="/wiki/Cyperaceae" title="Cyperaceae">sedge</a> or low <a href="/wiki/Scrubland" title="Scrubland" class="mw-redirect">scrub</a> offer nesting sites.<sup id="cite_ref-snow_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> A Hungarian study showed that moderately intensive grazing of grassland, with more than one cow per hectare (2.5&#160;acres), was found to attract more nesting pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> When not breeding, the birds use a wider range of shallow wetlands, such as <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigated fields</a>, lake margins, and mining <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a> and other floodlands. Dry grassland, tidal mudflats and the seashore are less frequently used.<sup id="cite_ref-snow_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The density can reach 129 individuals per square kilometre (334 per square mile), but is usually much lower.<sup id="cite_ref-Blokhin_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blokhin-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Ruff breeds in Europe and Asia from Scandinavia and Great Britain almost to the Pacific. In Europe it is found in cool temperate areas, but over its Russian range it is an Arctic species, occurring mainly north of about 65°N. The largest numbers breed in Russia (more than 1 million pairs), Sweden (61,000 pairs), Finland (39,000 pairs) and Norway (14,000 pairs). Although it also breeds from Britain east through the Low Countries to Poland, Germany and Denmark, there are fewer than 2,000 pairs in these more southerly areas.<sup id="cite_ref-jncc_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jncc-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>It is highly gregarious on migration, travelling in large flocks that can contain hundreds or thousands of individuals. Huge dense groups form on the wintering grounds;<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> one flock in <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> contained a million birds.<sup id="cite_ref-hayman_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayman-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> A minority winter further east to Burma, south China,<sup id="cite_ref-hayman_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayman-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> New Guinea and scattered parts of southern Australia,<sup id="cite_ref-Slater70_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Slater70-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> or on the Atlantic and Mediterranean coasts of Europe. In Great Britain and parts of coastal western Europe, where the breeding and wintering ranges overlap, birds may be present throughout the year.<sup id="cite_ref-snow_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Non-breeding birds may also remain year round in the tropical wintering quarters. The Ruff is an uncommon visitor to Alaska (where it has occasionally bred), Canada and the contiguous states of the US, and has wandered to Iceland, Middle America, northern South America, Madagascar and New Zealand.<sup id="cite_ref-hayman_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayman-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-johnson_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-johnson-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> It has been recorded as breeding well south of its main range in northern <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>, a major migration stopover area.<sup id="cite_ref-unep_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unep-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Khrokov_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Khrokov-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The male, which plays no part in nesting or chick care, leaves the breeding grounds in late June or early July, followed later in July by the female and juveniles.<sup id="cite_ref-Baccetti_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baccetti-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Males typically make shorter flights and winter further north than females; for example, virtually all wintering Ruffs in Britain are males,<sup id="cite_ref-berthold_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-berthold-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> whereas in Kenya most are females.<sup id="cite_ref-pearson_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pearson-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Many migratory species use this differential wintering strategy, since it reduces feeding competition between the sexes and enables territorial males to reach the breeding grounds as early as possible, improving their chances of successful mating. Male Ruffs may also be able to tolerate colder winter conditions because they are larger than females.<sup id="cite_ref-berthold_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-berthold-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Birds returning north in spring across the central Mediterranean appear to follow a well-defined route. Large concentrations of Ruffs form every year at particular <a href="/wiki/Layover" title="Layover">stopover</a> sites to feed, and individuals marked with <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">rings</a> or dye reappear in subsequent years. The refuelling sites are closer together than the theoretical maximum travel distance calculated from the mean body mass, and provide evidence of a migration strategy using favoured intermediate sites.<sup id="cite_ref-Baccetti_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baccetti-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> The Ruff stores fat as a fuel, but unlike mammals, uses <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a> as the main energy source for exercise (including migration) and, when necessary, keeps warm by shivering; however, little research has been conducted on the mechanisms by which they <a href="/wiki/Oxidation#Redox_reactions_in_biology" title="Oxidation" class="mw-redirect">oxidise</a> lipids.<sup id="cite_ref-Vaillancourt_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vaillancourt-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mating">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Mating">Mating</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Philomachus_pugnax.jpg" class="image"><img alt=" A displaying male is leaping with wings extended, showing its white underwings." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Philomachus_pugnax.jpg/170px-Philomachus_pugnax.jpg" width="170" height="243" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Philomachus_pugnax.jpg/255px-Philomachus_pugnax.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Philomachus_pugnax.jpg/340px-Philomachus_pugnax.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Philomachus_pugnax.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Displaying male</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Males display during the breeding season at a <a href="/wiki/Lek_(mating_arena)" title="Lek (mating arena)" class="mw-redirect">lek</a> in a traditional open grassy arena. The Ruff is one of the few lekking species in which the display is primarily directed at other males rather than to the females, and it is among the small percentage of birds in which the males have well-marked and <a href="/wiki/Mendelian_inheritance" title="Mendelian inheritance">inherited</a> variations in plumage and mating behaviour.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Payne_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Payne-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> There are three male forms: the typical territorial males, satellite males which have a white neck ruff, and a very rare, recently discovered variant with female-like plumage. The territorial males, about 84% of the total, have strongly coloured black or chestnut ruffs and stake out and occupy small mating territories in the lek. They actively court females and display a high degree of aggression towards other resident males;<sup id="cite_ref-snow_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> 5–20 territorial males each hold an area of the lek about 1&#160;m (1&#160;yd) across, usually with bare soil in the centre. They perform an elaborate display that includes wing fluttering, jumping, standing upright, crouching with ruff erect, or lunging at rivals.<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> They are typically silent even when displaying, although a soft <i>gue-gue-gue</i> may occasionally be given.<sup id="cite_ref-snow_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">Territorial</a> males are very site-faithful; 90% return to the same lekking sites in subsequent seasons, the most dominant males being the most likely to reappear. Site-faithful males can acquire accurate information about the competitive abilities of other males, leading to well-developed dominance relationships. Such stable relationships reduce conflict and the risk of injury, and the consequent lower levels of male aggression are less likely to scare off females. Lower-ranked territorial males also benefit from site fidelity since they can remain on the leks while waiting for the top males eventually to drop out.<sup id="cite_ref-widemo_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-widemo-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Satellite males, about 16% of the total number, have white or mottled ruffs and do not occupy territories; they enter leks and attempt to mate with the females visiting the territories occupied by the resident males.<sup id="cite_ref-lank_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lank-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> Resident males tolerate the satellite birds because, although they are competitors for mating with the females, the presence of both types of male on a territory attracts additional females.<sup id="cite_ref-hill_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hill-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hugie_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hugie-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> Females also prefer larger leks,<sup id="cite_ref-lank3_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lank3-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> and leks surrounded by taller plants, which give better nesting habitat.<sup id="cite_ref-hoglund_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hoglund-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The behaviour and appearance for an individual male remain constant through its adult life, and are determined by a simple genetic <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(biology)" title="Polymorphism (biology)">polymorphism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-lank_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lank-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> It was originally thought that the difference between the two types of males was due to a sex-linked genetic factor, but in fact the genetic <a href="/wiki/Locus_(genetics)" title="Locus (genetics)">locus</a> relevant for the mating strategy is located on an <a href="/wiki/Autosome" title="Autosome">autosome</a>, or non-sex <a href="/wiki/Chromosome" title="Chromosome">chromosome</a>. That means that both sexes can carry the two different forms of the gene, not just males. The non-territorial female does not normally show evidence of its genetic type, but when females are given <a href="/wiki/Testosterone" title="Testosterone">testosterone</a> implants, they display the male territorial behaviour corresponding to their <a href="/wiki/Genotype" title="Genotype">genotype</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-lankcoupe_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lankcoupe-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> Territorial plumage and behaviour are inherited as <a href="/wiki/Recessive_characteristic" title="Recessive characteristic" class="mw-redirect">recessive characteristics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-lanksmith_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lanksmith-38"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kampfl%C3%A4ufer_wei%C3%9F_070608.jpg" class="image"><img alt=" A satellite male seen from the front, showing white underparts and a white ruff" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Kampfl%C3%A4ufer_wei%C3%9F_070608.jpg/170px-Kampfl%C3%A4ufer_wei%C3%9F_070608.jpg" width="170" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Kampfl%C3%A4ufer_wei%C3%9F_070608.jpg/255px-Kampfl%C3%A4ufer_wei%C3%9F_070608.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Kampfl%C3%A4ufer_wei%C3%9F_070608.jpg/340px-Kampfl%C3%A4ufer_wei%C3%9F_070608.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kampfl%C3%A4ufer_wei%C3%9F_070608.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Satellite male with white ruff</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Although satellite males are on average slightly smaller and lighter than residents, the nutrition of the chicks does not, as previously thought, influence mating strategy; rather, the inherited mating strategy influences body size. Resident-type chicks will, if provided with the same amount of food, grow heavier than satellite-type chicks. Satellite males do not have to expend energy to defend a territory, and can spend more time foraging, so they do not need to be as bulky as the residents; indeed, since they fly more, there would be a physiological cost to additional weight.<sup id="cite_ref-lank_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lank-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A third type of male was first described in 2006; this is a permanent female mimic, the first such reported for a bird. About 1% of males are small, intermediate in size between males and females, and do not grow the elaborate breeding plumage of the territorial and satellite males, although they have much larger internal <a href="/wiki/Testicle" title="Testicle">testes</a> than the ruffed males. This cryptic male, or "faeder" (<a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> "father") obtains access to mating territories together with the females, and "steals" matings when the females crouch to solicit copulation.<sup id="cite_ref-jukema_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jukema-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The faeder moults into the prenuptial male plumage with striped feathers, but does not go on to develop the ornamental feathers of the normal male. As described above, this stage is thought to show the original male breeding plumage, before other male types evolved. A faeder can be distinguished in the hand by its wing length, which is intermediate between those of displaying males and females.<sup id="cite_ref-Karlionova2_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Karlionova2-39"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> Despite their feminine appearance, the faeders migrate with the larger 'normal' lekking males and spend the winter with them.<sup id="cite_ref-VerkuilY_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VerkuilY-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The faeders are sometimes mounted by independent or satellite males, but are as often "on top" in homosexual mountings as the ruffed males, suggesting that their true identity is known by the other males. Females never mount males.<sup id="cite_ref-jukema_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jukema-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Preliminary research results suggest that the faeder characteristics are genetically controlled by a single dominant gene.<sup id="cite_ref-lankproject_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lankproject-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> Females often seem to prefer mating with faeders to copulation with normal males, and normal males also copulate with faeders (and <i>vice versa</i>) relatively more often than with females. The homosexual copulations may attract females to the lek, like the presence of satellite males.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Not all mating takes place at the lek, since only a minority of the males attend an active lek. As alternative strategies, males can also directly pursue females ("following") or wait for them as they approached good feeding sites ("intercepting"). Males switched between the three tactics, being more likely to attend a lek when the copulation rate the previous day was high or when fewer females were available after nesting had started. Lekking rates were low in cold weather early in the season when off-lek males spent most of their time feeding.<sup id="cite_ref-lank1087_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lank1087-43"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The level of <a href="/wiki/Polyandry" title="Polyandry">polyandry</a> in the Ruff is the highest known for any avian lekking species and for any shorebird. More than half of female Ruffs mate with, and have clutches fertilised by, more than one male, and individual females mate with males of both main behavioural morphs more often than expected by chance. In lekking species, females can choose mates without risking the loss of support from males in nesting and rearing chicks, since the males take no part in raising the brood anyway. In the absence of this cost, if polyandry is advantageous, it would be expected to occur at a higher rate in lekking than among pair-bonded species.<sup id="cite_ref-lanksmith_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lanksmith-38"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nesting and survival">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Nesting_and_survival">Nesting and survival</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Corvus_corone_Rabenkr%C3%A4he_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt=" A Carrion Crow picking a small food item from short grass. This species will raid the nests of wetland waders for eggs and chicks." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Corvus_corone_Rabenkr%C3%A4he_1.jpg/220px-Corvus_corone_Rabenkr%C3%A4he_1.jpg" width="220" height="123" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Corvus_corone_Rabenkr%C3%A4he_1.jpg/330px-Corvus_corone_Rabenkr%C3%A4he_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Corvus_corone_Rabenkr%C3%A4he_1.jpg/440px-Corvus_corone_Rabenkr%C3%A4he_1.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Corvus_corone_Rabenkr%C3%A4he_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Carrion_Crow" title="Carrion Crow">Carrion Crow</a> will raid the nests of wetland waders for eggs and young.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The nest is a shallow ground scrape lined with grass leaves and stems, and concealed in marsh plants or tall grass up to 400&#160;m (400&#160;yd) from the lek. Nesting is solitary, although several females may lay in the general vicinity of a lek.<sup id="cite_ref-hayman_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayman-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-snow_19-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The eggs are slightly glossy, green or olive, and marked with dark blotches; they are laid from mid-March to early June depending on latitude.<sup id="cite_ref-snow_19-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The typical clutch is four eggs, each egg measuring 44&#160;x&#160;31&#160;mm (1.7&#160;x&#160;1.2&#160;in) in size and weighing 21.0&#160;g (0.75&#160;oz) of which 5% is shell. Incubation is by the female alone, and the time to hatching is 20–23 days, with a further 25–28 days to fledging.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Precocial" title="Precocial">precocial</a> chicks have buff and chestnut down, streaked and barred with black, and frosted with white;<sup id="cite_ref-Coward_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-44"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> they feed themselves on a variety of small invertebrates, but are brooded by the female.<sup id="cite_ref-eaza_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eaza-45"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> One brood is raised each year.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Predators of waders breeding in wet grasslands include birds such as large gulls, <a href="/wiki/Common_Raven" title="Common Raven">Common Raven</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carrion_Crow" title="Carrion Crow">Carrion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hooded_Crow" title="Hooded Crow">Hooded Crows</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Great_Skua" title="Great Skua">Great</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Skua" title="Arctic Skua" class="mw-redirect">Arctic Skuas</a>; foxes occasionally take waders, and the impact of <a href="/wiki/Feral_cats" title="Feral cats" class="mw-redirect">feral cats</a> and stoats is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-defra_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-defra-46"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bolton_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bolton-47"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wal_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wal-48"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> Overgrazing can increase predation by making nests easier to find.<sup id="cite_ref-wal_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wal-48"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> In captivity, the main causes of chick mortality were stress-related sudden death and <a href="/wiki/Torticollis" title="Torticollis">twisted neck syndrome</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-eaza_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eaza-45"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> Adults seem to show little evidence of <a href="/wiki/Parasitism" title="Parasitism">external parasites</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Threlfall_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Threlfall-49"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> but may have significant levels of disease on their tropical wintering grounds,<sup id="cite_ref-mendes_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mendes-50"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Avian_malaria" title="Avian malaria">avian malaria</a> in their inland freshwater habitats,<sup id="cite_ref-mendes_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mendes-50"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> and so they might be expected to invest strongly in their <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune systems</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-piersmat1997_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-piersmat1997-51"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> however, a 2006 study that analysed the blood of migrating Ruffs intercepted in <a href="/wiki/Friesland" title="Friesland">Friesland</a> showed that this bird actually has unexplained low levels of immune responses on at least one measure of resistance.<sup id="cite_ref-mendes2_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mendes2-52"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> The Ruff can breed from its second year, and the <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">average lifespan</a> for birds that have passed the chick stage is about 4.4 years,<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> although a Finnish bird lived to a record 13 years and 11 months.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<p>The Ruff normally feeds using a steady walk and pecking action, selecting food items by sight, but it will also wade deeply and submerge its head. On saline lakes in East Africa it often swims like a <a href="/wiki/Phalarope" title="Phalarope">phalarope</a>, picking items off the surface.<sup id="cite_ref-hayman_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayman-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> It will feed at night as well as during the day.<sup id="cite_ref-delhoyo_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-delhoyo-54"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> When feeding, the Ruff frequently raises its back feathers, producing a loose pointed peak on the back; this habit is shared only by the Black-tailed Godwit.<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tamil_Nadu_countryside.jpg" class="image"><img alt=" A flooded rice field in Tamil Nadu. Rice paddies are a favoured winter feeding ground for Ruff" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Tamil_Nadu_countryside.jpg/220px-Tamil_Nadu_countryside.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Tamil_Nadu_countryside.jpg/330px-Tamil_Nadu_countryside.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Tamil_Nadu_countryside.jpg/440px-Tamil_Nadu_countryside.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tamil_Nadu_countryside.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Rice paddies are a favoured winter feeding ground</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During the breeding season, the Ruff’s diet consists almost exclusively of the adults and larva of terrestrial and aquatic insects such as beetles and flies. On migration and during the winter, the Ruff eats insects (including <a href="/wiki/Trichoptera" title="Trichoptera" class="mw-redirect">caddis flies</a>, water-beetles, <a href="/wiki/Mayfly" title="Mayfly">mayflies</a> and grasshoppers), crustaceans, spiders, molluscs, worms, frogs, small fish, and also the seeds of rice and other cereals, sedges, grasses and aquatic plants.<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Migrating birds in Italy varied their diet according to what was available at each stopover site. Green aquatic plant material, spilt rice and maize, flies and beetles were found, along with varying amounts of grit.<sup id="cite_ref-BacettiN_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BacettiN-55"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> On the main wintering grounds in West Africa, rice is a favoured food during the later part of the season as the ricefields dry out.<sup id="cite_ref-Trolliet_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trolliet-56"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Just before migration, the Ruff increases its body mass at a rate of about 1% a day, much slower than the Bar-tailed Godwits breeding in Alaska, which fatten at four times that rate. This is thought to be because the godwit cannot use refuelling areas to feed on its trans-Pacific flight, whereas the Ruff is able to make regular stops and take in food during overland migration. For the same reason, the Ruff does not physiologically shrink its digestive organs to reduce bodyweight before migrating, unlike the godwit.<sup id="cite_ref-Piersma_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Piersma-57"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup><br style="clear:both;" /></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RuffNetting.jpg" class="image"><img alt=" A 1897 black-and-white drawing of a group of Ruffs moving towards an area baited with food. Some are flying towards the food. A net lies on the ground beside the feeding area." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/RuffNetting.jpg/220px-RuffNetting.jpg" width="220" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/RuffNetting.jpg/330px-RuffNetting.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/RuffNetting.jpg/440px-RuffNetting.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RuffNetting.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
1897 illustration of Ruffs being trapped for food with a net</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Ruffs were formerly trapped for food in England in large numbers; on one occasion, 2,400 were served at <a href="/wiki/George_Neville_(archbishop)" title="George Neville (archbishop)" class="mw-redirect">Archbishop Neville's</a> enthronement banquet in 1465. The birds were netted while lekking, sometimes being fattened with bread, milk and seed in holding pens before preparation for the table.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pennant_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pennant-58"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>...if expedition is required, sugar is added, which will make them in a fortnight's time a lump of fat: they then sell for two Shillings or half-a-crown a piece… The method of killing them is by cutting off their head with a pair of scissars [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>], the quantity of blood that issues is very great, considering the size of the bird. They are dressed like the Woodcock, with their intestines; and, when killed at the critical time, say the Epicures, are reckoned the most delicious of all morsels.<sup id="cite_ref-Pennant_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pennant-58"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The heavy toll on breeding birds, together with loss of habitat through drainage and collection by nineteenth-century trophy hunters and egg collectors, meant that the species became almost extinct in England by the 1880s, although recolonisation in small numbers has occurred since 1963.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bto_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Coward_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-44"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> The draining of wetlands from the 1800s onwards in southern Sweden has resulted in the Ruff's disappearance from many areas there, although it remains common in the north of the country.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> The use of <a href="/wiki/Insecticide" title="Insecticide">insecticides</a> and draining of wetlands has led to a decrease in the number of Ruff in Denmark since the early 1900s.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> There are still areas where the Ruff and other wetland birds are hunted legally or otherwise for food.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hayman29_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayman29-62"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-balmaki_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-balmaki-63"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> A large-scale example is the capture of more than one million waterbirds (including Ruffs) in a single year from <a href="/wiki/Lake_Chilwa" title="Lake Chilwa">Lake Chilwa</a> in <a href="/wiki/Malawi" title="Malawi">Malawi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kanstrup_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kanstrup-64"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Although this bird eats rice on the wintering grounds, where it can make up nearly 40% of its diet, it takes mainly waste and residues from cropping and threshing, not harvestable grain. It has sometimes been viewed as a pest, but the deeper water and presence of invertebrate prey in the economically important early winter period means that the wader has little effect on crop yield.<sup id="cite_ref-Trolliet2_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trolliet2-65"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span></h2>
<p>The Ruff has a large range, estimated at 1–10&#160;million&#160;square kilometres (0.38–3.8&#160;million square miles) and a population of at least 2,000,000 birds. The European population of 200,000–510,000 pairs, occupying more than half of the total breeding range, seems to have declined by up to 30% over ten years, but this may reflect geographical changes in breeding populations. Numbers in Asia do not appear to be declining, and more Ruffs are wintering in Africa. The species as a whole is therefore not believed to approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> (that is, declining more than 30 percent in ten years or three generations). For these reasons, the Ruff is classified as "<a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">least concern</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>The most important breeding populations in Europe, in Russia and Sweden are stable, and the breeding range in Norway has expanded to the south, but populations have more than halved in Finland, Poland, Latvia and The Netherlands. Although the small populations in these countries are of limited overall significance, the decline is a continuation of trend towards range contraction that has occurred over the last two centuries. The drop in numbers in Europe has been attributed to drainage, increased fertiliser use, the loss of formerly mown or grazed breeding sites and over-hunting.<sup id="cite_ref-jncc_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jncc-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Fossils from the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> suggest that this species bred further south in Europe in the cool periods between glaciations than it does now.<sup id="cite_ref-bochenski_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bochenski-66"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> Its sensitivity to changing climate as well as to water table levels and the speed of vegetation growth has led to suggestions that its range is affected by global warming, and the Ruff might act as an indicator species for monitoring climate change.<sup id="cite_ref-Zockler_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zockler-67"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> Potential threats to this species may also include outbreaks of diseases to which it is susceptible such as <a href="/wiki/Avian_influenza" title="Avian influenza">influenza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Botulism" title="Botulism">botulism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avian_malaria" title="Avian malaria">avian malaria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Ruff is one of the species to which the <i><a href="/wiki/Agreement_on_the_Conservation_of_African-Eurasian_Migratory_Waterbirds" title="Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a></i> (AEWA) applies, where it is allocated to category 2c; that is, the populations in need of special attention as they are showing "significant long-term decline" in much of its range.<sup id="cite_ref-AEWA_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AEWA-68"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> This commits signatories to regulate the taking of listed species or their eggs, to establish protected areas to conserve habitats for the listed species, to regulate hunting and to monitor the populations of the birds concerned.<sup id="cite_ref-AEWA2_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AEWA2-69"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
