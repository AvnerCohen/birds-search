<p>The <b>pomarine jaeger</b> (<i>Stercorarius pomarinus</i>), <b>pomarine skua</b>, or <b>pomatorhine skua</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-birdsbritannica_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdsbritannica-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Skua" title="Skua">skua</a> family Stercorariidae. It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>, wintering at sea in the tropical oceans.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pomarine_jaeger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Its relationships are not fully resolved; its <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> is most similar to the <a href="/wiki/Great_skua" title="Great skua">great skua</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Blechschmidt1993_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blechschmidt1993-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> but from morphology and behavior, it is closer to the lesser skuas (such as the <a href="/wiki/Parasitic_jaeger" title="Parasitic jaeger">parasitic jaeger</a>). The most likely explanation is extensive <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridization</a> between the great and one species of lesser skuas, which resulted in a hybrid population that eventually evolved into a distinct species, the pomarine jaeger; or alternatively between the pomarine and a species of Southern Hemisphere skua, with the great skua being the hybrid offspring, perhaps appearing as recently as the 15th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Blechschmidt1993_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blechschmidt1993-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Furness2003_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Furness2003-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Judging from characteristics of the <a href="/wiki/Skeleton" title="Skeleton">skeleton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Behavior" title="Behavior">behavior</a>, the former seems more likely, as the pomarine jaeger shares several similarities with the "Catharacta" skuas, while the great skua does not seem much different from its Southern Hemisphere relatives.
</p><p>The mtDNA difference between the pomarine jaeger and the great skua is one of the smallest between any two vertebrate species yet analyzed, being less than the variation found between different individuals of widespread species. The apparent capability for hybridization has led to the abolition of the separate genus <i>Catharacta</i> for the Southern Hemisphere and great skuas.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pomarine_jaeger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "jaeger" is derived from the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> word <i>Jäger</i>, meaning "hunter".<sup id="cite_ref-OEDjaeger_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OEDjaeger-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OEDskua_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OEDskua-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The genus name <i>Stercorarius</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means "of dung"; the food disgorged by other birds when pursued by skuas was once thought to be excrement. The specific <i>Pomatorhinus</i> is from  <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>  <i>poma, pomatos</i>, "lid" and <i>rhis, rhinos</i>,  "nostrils".<sup id="cite_ref-job_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This refers to the <a href="/wiki/Cere" class="mw-redirect" title="Cere">cere</a>, which the pomarine jaeger shares with the other skuas. Although it is sometimes erroneously referred to as the <b>Pomeranian skua</b>, the name of this species is unrelated to the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Pomerania" title="Pomerania">Pomerania</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-birdsbritannica_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdsbritannica-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pomarine_jaeger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stercorarius_pomarinusDUCA.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Stercorarius_pomarinusDUCA.jpg/220px-Stercorarius_pomarinusDUCA.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="264" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Stercorarius_pomarinusDUCA.jpg/330px-Stercorarius_pomarinusDUCA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Stercorarius_pomarinusDUCA.jpg/440px-Stercorarius_pomarinusDUCA.jpg 2x" data-file-width="853" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stercorarius_pomarinusDUCA.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This is a large skua at about 45cm length, excluding the central tail feathers of the summer adult, which can add another 10cm or so.</div></div></div> 
<p>This species ranges from 46 to 67&#160;cm (18 to 26&#160;in) in length, 110 to 138&#160;cm (43 to 54&#160;in) in wingspan and 540–920&#160;g (1.19–2.03&#160;lb) in weight.<sup id="cite_ref-arkive_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arkive-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-enature_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enature-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CRC_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The upper limit of the length includes the elongated tail streamer of breeding adults, which is about 10&#160;cm (3.9&#160;in) in length. Identification of this jaeger is complicated by its similarities to <a href="/wiki/Parasitic_jaeger" title="Parasitic jaeger">parasitic jaeger</a> and the existence of three <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(biology)" title="Polymorphism (biology)">morphs</a>. Pomarine jaegers are larger than <a href="/wiki/Common_gull" title="Common gull">common gulls</a>. They are much bulkier, broader-winged and less <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon</a>-like than the parasitic jaeger, but show the same wide range of plumage variation. The flight is more measured than that of the smaller species. It has many harsh chattering calls and others which sounds like <i>which-yew</i>.
</p><p>Light-morph adult pomarine jaegers have a brown back, mainly white underparts and dark primary wing feathers with a white "flash". The head and neck are yellowish-white with a black cap. Dark morph adults are dark brown, and intermediate morph birds are dark with somewhat paler underparts, head and neck. All morphs have the white wing flash, which appears as a diagnostic double flash on the underwing. In breeding adults of all morphs, the two central tail feathers are much longer than the others, spoon-shaped, and twisted from the horizontal. Juveniles are even more problematic to identify, and are difficult to separate from parasitic jaegers at a distance on plumage alone.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pomarine_jaeger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pomarine_jaeger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This species breeds in the far north of <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurasia</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. It nests on Arctic <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> and islands, laying 2–3 olive-brown eggs in grass lined depressions. Like other skuas, it will fly at the head of a human or other intruder approaching its nest. Although it cannot inflict serious damage, the experience is frightening and painful.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pomarine_jaeger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> feeds on <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">carrion</a>, scraps, smaller <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> up to the size of <a href="/wiki/Common_gull" title="Common gull">common gull</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Lemming" title="Lemming">lemmings</a>. It <a href="/wiki/Kleptoparasitism" title="Kleptoparasitism">robs</a> <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">terns</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Northern_gannet" title="Northern gannet">gannets</a> of their catches. Like most other skua <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, it continues this piratical behaviour throughout the year, showing great agility as it harasses its victims. Only the Great Black Backed Gull, White-Tailed Eagle and Golden Eagle are known to take adult, healthy pomarine skuas.
</p>
