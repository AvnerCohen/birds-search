<p>The <b>Hen Harrier</b> (<i>Circus cyaneus</i>) or <b>Northern Harrier</b> (in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>) is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a>. It breeds throughout the northern parts of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere" class="mw-redirect">northern hemisphere</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and the northernmost <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">USA</a>, and in northern <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>. This species is <a href="/wiki/Polytypic" title="Polytypic">polytypic</a>, with two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>. <b>Marsh Hawk</b> is a historical name for the American form.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrates</a> to more southerly areas in winter. Eurasian birds move to southern Europe and southern temperate Asia, and American breeders to the southernmost USA, Mexico, and <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>. In the mildest regions, such as France, <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, and the southern US, Hen Harriers may be present all year, but the higher ground is largely deserted in winter.</p>

<p>The Hen Harrier is 41–52 cm (16–20 in)<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> long with a 97–122 cm (38–48 in) wingspan.<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> It resembles other <a href="/wiki/Harrier_(bird)" title="Harrier (bird)">harriers</a> in having distinct male and female plumages. The sexes also differ in weight, with males weighing 290 to 400 g (10 to 14 oz), with an average of 350&#160;g (12&#160;oz), and females weighing 390 to 750 g (14 to 26 oz), with an average of 530&#160;g (19&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Among standard measurements, the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> is 32.8 to 40.6 cm (12.9 to 16.0 in), the <a href="/wiki/Tail" title="Tail">tail</a> is 19.3 to 25.8 cm (7.6 to 10.2 in) and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> is 7.1 to 8.9 cm (2.8 to 3.5 in).<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> It is relatively long winged and long tailed, having the longest wing and tail relative to its body size of any raptor occurring in North America.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The male of the nominate race, <i>C. c. cyaneus</i> (Linnaeus, 1766), which breeds in Europe and Asia, is mainly grey above and white below except for the upper breast, which is grey like the upperparts, and the rump, which is white; the wings are grey with black wingtips. The female is brown above with white upper tail coverts, hence females, and the similar juveniles, are often called "ringtails". Their underparts are buff streaked with brown.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The race <i>C. c. hudsonius</i> (Linnaeus, 1766) breeds in North America and is sometimes considered a distinct species, <i>C. hudsonius</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-lees_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lees-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The male's plumage is darker grey than that of <i>C. c. cyaneus</i> and the female is also darker and more rufous.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> In both North American and Eurasia, the adult male is sometimes nicknamed the "Grey Ghost", because of his striking plumage and spectral aura.</p>
<p>The female gives a whistled <i>piih-eh</i> when receiving food from the male, and her alarm call is <i>chit-it-it-it-it-et-it</i>. The male calls <i>chek-chek-chek</i>, with a more bouncing <i>chuk-uk-uk-uk</i> during his display flight<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hen_Harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<p>This medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptor</a> breeds on <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moorland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prairie" title="Prairie">prairies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farmland</a> <a href="/wiki/Western_Gulf_coastal_grasslands" title="Western Gulf coastal grasslands">coastal prairies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grasslands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a> and other assorted open areas.<sup id="cite_ref-TexasPWD2012_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TexasPWD2012-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The nest is built on the ground or on a mound of dirt or vegetation. Nests are made of sticks and are lined inside with grass and leaves. Four to eight (exceptionally 2 to 10) whitish <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> are laid.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TexasPWD2012_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TexasPWD2012-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The eggs measure approximately 47&#160;mm ×&#160;36&#160;mm (1.9&#160;in ×&#160;1.4&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> These are the only hawk-like bird known to practice polygyny – one male mates with several females. When incubating eggs, the female sits on the nest while the male hunts and brings food to her and the chicks.<sup id="cite_ref-TexasPWD2012_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TexasPWD2012-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Up to five females have been known to mate with one male in a season.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> A male will maintain a territory averaging 2.6&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (1.0&#160;sq&#160;mi), though male territories have ranged from 1.7 to 150 km<sup>2</sup> (0.66 to 58 sq&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-Macwhirter_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macwhirter-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The eggs are incubated mostly by the female for 31 to 32 days. The male will help feed chicks after they hatch, but doesn't usually watch them for a greater period of time than around 5 minutes.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The male usually passes off food to the female, which she then feeds to the young, although later the female will capture food and simply drop into the nest for her nestlings to eat.<sup id="cite_ref-Macwhirter_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macwhirter-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The chicks fledge at around 36 days old, though breeding maturity isn't reached until 2 years in females and 3 years in males.</p>
<p>In winter, the Hen Harrier is a bird of open country, and will then roost communally, often with <a href="/wiki/Merlin_(bird)" title="Merlin (bird)">Merlins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marsh_Harrier" title="Marsh Harrier">Marsh Harriers</a>. There is now an accepted record of <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">transatlantic vagrancy</a> by the American subspecies, with a juvenile being recorded in <a href="/wiki/Scilly" title="Scilly" class="mw-redirect">Scilly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> from October 1982 to June 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-BB2007_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB2007-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hen_Harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hunting behavior">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Hunting_behavior">Hunting behavior</span></h3>
<p>This is a typical harrier, which hunts long wings held in a shallow V in its low flight during which the bird closely hugs the contours of the land below them. Northern or Hen Harriers hunt primarily small <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, as do most harriers. Preferred prey species can include <a href="/wiki/Vole" title="Vole">voles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cotton_rat" title="Cotton rat">cotton rats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ground_squirrel" title="Ground squirrel">ground squirrels</a>. Up to 95% of the diet is comprised by small mammals.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> However <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> are hunted with some regularity as well, especially by males. Preferred avian prey include <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerines</a> of open country (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Sparrow" title="Sparrow">sparrows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lark" title="Lark">larks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pipit" title="Pipit">pipits</a>), small <a href="/wiki/Shorebird" title="Shorebird" class="mw-redirect">shorebirds</a> and the young of <a href="/wiki/Waterfowl" title="Waterfowl">waterfowl</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galliformes" title="Galliformes">galliforms</a>. Supplementing the diet occasionally are <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a>), <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Orthoptera" title="Orthoptera">orthopterans</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Larger prey, such as <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a> and adult <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">ducks</a> are taken sometimes and harriers have been known to subdue these by drowning them underwater.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Harriers hunt by surprising prey while flying low to the ground in open areas, as they drift low over fields and moors.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TexasPWD2012_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TexasPWD2012-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The Harriers circle an area several times listening and looking for prey.<sup id="cite_ref-TexasPWD2012_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TexasPWD2012-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Harriers use hearing regularly to find prey, as they have exceptionally good hearing for diurnal raptors, this being the function of their <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owl</a>-like facial disc.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> This harrier tends to be a very vocal bird while it glides over its hunting ground.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hen_Harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mortality and competition">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Mortality_and_competition">Mortality and competition</span></h3>
<p>Little information is available on longevity in Hen or Northern Harriers. It is known that the longest lived known bird was 16 years and 5 months. However, even adults rarely live more than 8 years. Mortality in the early stages of life is often related to predation. Predators of eggs and nestlings include <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">raccoons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skunk" title="Skunk">skunks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Badger" title="Badger">badgers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">foxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corvus" title="Corvus">crows and ravens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owls</a>. Fledgings are also predated regularly, especially by <a href="/wiki/Great_Horned_Owl" title="Great Horned Owl">Great Horned Owls</a> in North America.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> If a potential predator visits, both parents respond aggressively, issuing alarm calls and striking with talons. <a href="/wiki/Short-eared_Owl" title="Short-eared Owl">Short-eared Owls</a> are natural enemies of this species that favor the same prey and habitat, as well as having a similarly broad distribution. Occasionally, both harriers and Short-eared Owls will harrass each other until the victim it drops its prey and it can be stolen, a practice known as <a href="/wiki/Kleptoparasitism" title="Kleptoparasitism">kleptoparasitism</a>. Most commonly, the harriers are the aggressors whom pirate prey away from the owls than vica versa.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hen_Harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h2>
<p>This species has a large range, with an estimated global extent 1–20 million km², and a population estimated at 1.3 million individuals. There is evidence of a population decline, but the species is not believed to approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> (i.e., declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations). It is therefore classified as "least concern".<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hen_Harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span></h2>
<p>In some parts of Europe people believed that seeing a harrier perched on a house was a sign that three people would die; on a happier note, some Native American tribes believe that seeing a hawk on your wedding day is a sign of a long, happy marriage. Unlike many raptors, Hen or Northern Harriers have historically been favorably regarded by farmers because they eat predators of quail eggs and mice that damage crops. Harriers are sometimes called "good hawks" because they pose no threat to poultry as some hawks do. Heavy pesticide use in the 1970s and 1980s caused a decline in harrier populations.<sup id="cite_ref-TexasPWD2012_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TexasPWD2012-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hen_Harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Problems in the United Kingdom">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Problems_in_the_United_Kingdom">Problems in the United Kingdom</span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>, the Hen Harrier suffers illegal persecution by <a href="/wiki/Gamekeeper" title="Gamekeeper">gamekeepers</a> and their employers on shooting estates, particularly those managed for <a href="/wiki/Red_Grouse" title="Red Grouse">Red Grouse</a> shooting, resulting in local and regional extinction in many areas, particularly in England where only 4 breeding pairs survive despite abundant suitable habitat capable of holding several hundred pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-birdguides_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdguides-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nwcu_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nwcu-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Because of this they are now very rare in many parts of the UK, and under threat in many more areas.<sup id="cite_ref-rspb1_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb1-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rspb2_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb2-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rspb3_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb3-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This problem received a high profile in October 2007 when police investigating the killing of two Hen Harriers on the <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Elizabeth II of the United Kingdom" class="mw-redirect">Queen</a>'s estate at <a href="/wiki/Sandringham_House" title="Sandringham House">Sandringham</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a> interviewed <a href="/wiki/Prince_Henry_of_Wales" title="Prince Henry of Wales" class="mw-redirect">Prince Harry</a> and a friend during their investigation.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-times_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> No charges were brought as police were unable to obtain sufficient evidence to prosecute.<sup id="cite_ref-birdguides_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdguides-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Since the assumed threat to Red Grouse is the main reason for the persecution of this species in the UK, a project funded by <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Natural_Heritage" title="Scottish Natural Heritage">Scottish Natural Heritage</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Game_%26_Wildlife_Conservation_Trust" title="Game &amp; Wildlife Conservation Trust">Game &amp; Wildlife Conservation Trust</a>, the <a href="/wiki/RSPB" title="RSPB" class="mw-redirect">RSPB</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a> was launched at Langholm Moor in Scotland from 2007. The Langholm Moor Demonstration Project (LMDP), a 10-year investigation, costing £3 million, is intended to see whether grouse and raptors can live side-by-side harmoniously.</p>
<p>A similar project, the Joint Raptor Study (also referred to as the 'JRS' or 'the Langholm Study') was run on Langholm from 1992 to 1997. The study made many findings and a host of peer reviewed papers were published on the work, in addition to the final report. Among the most often quoted findings were that long term declines in red grouse populations were "extremely unlikely" to be due to raptor predation and were attributed to habitat degradation/loss, and that raptor predation was the most likely explanation for the failure of grouse stocks to recover at Langholm once the population had fallen to a low level.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> The project ended in 1997, although a follow up supplementary feeding trial was run by the same team in 1998 and 1999. Grouse shooting on the moor was abandoned for the 1998 season onwards.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hen_Harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Forestry and Hen Harriers">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Forestry_and_Hen_Harriers">Forestry and Hen Harriers</span></h2>
<p>The Hen Harrier is a bird of open habitats such as heather moorland and extensive agriculture. However, much of its range, particularly in Ireland and parts of western Britain, has been (and continues to be) afforested, predominantly with non-native conifers such as Sitka Spruce (Picea sitchensis) from North America (Barton et al. 2006,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Fielding et al. 2010<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup>). Hen Harriers nest and forage in commercial forestry when it is young, before the canopy closes (typically at between 9–12 and years old), but do not make much use of thicket and subsequent growth stages (Madders 2000,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> O'Donoghue 2004<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup>), which typically comprise between two thirds and three quarters of the commercial growth cycle. Where forests replace habitats that were used by Hen Harriers they will therefore tend to reduce overall habitat availability (O'Flynn, 1983<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup>). However, where afforestation takes place in areas that were previously underutilised by Hen Harriers, it may increase the value of such areas to this species in the long-term (Wilson et al. 2009,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> Haworth &amp; Fielding 2009<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup>). Areas dominated by forestry may remain suitable to Hen Harriers provided that a mosaic of age classes is maintained within the forest, such that areas of young, pre-thicket forest are always available.</p>
