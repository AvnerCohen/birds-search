<p>The <b>hen harrier</b> (<i>Circus cyaneus</i>)  is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a>. The genus name <i>Circus</i> is derived from&#32;<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a>&#32;<i> kirkos</i>&#160;'circle', referring to a bird of prey named for its circling flight. The specific <i>cyaneus</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, meaning "dark-blue".<sup id="cite_ref-Jobling_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jobling-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While many taxonomic authorities split the <a href="/wiki/Northern_harrier" title="Northern harrier">northern harrier</a> and the hen harrier into distinct species, others consider them conspecific.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It breeds in <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurasia</a>. The term "hen harrier" refers to its former habit of preying on free-ranging fowl.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPBHH_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPBHH-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrates</a> to more southerly areas in winter. Eurasian birds move to southern Europe and southern temperate Asia. In the mildest regions, such as France and <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, hen harriers may be present all year, but the higher ground is largely deserted in winter.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hen_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hen_Harrier_Lungthu_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_08.11.2015.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Hen_Harrier_Lungthu_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_08.11.2015.jpg/220px-Hen_Harrier_Lungthu_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_08.11.2015.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Hen_Harrier_Lungthu_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_08.11.2015.jpg/330px-Hen_Harrier_Lungthu_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_08.11.2015.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Hen_Harrier_Lungthu_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_08.11.2015.jpg/440px-Hen_Harrier_Lungthu_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_08.11.2015.jpg 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hen_Harrier_Lungthu_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_08.11.2015.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bird in flight at an altitude over 12,500 ft in <a href="/wiki/Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary" title="Pangolakha Wildlife Sanctuary">Pangolakha Wildlife Sanctuary</a> in <a href="/wiki/East_Sikkim" class="mw-redirect" title="East Sikkim">East Sikkim</a> district, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> in the month of November</div></div></div>
<p>The hen harrier is 41–52&#160;cm (16–20&#160;in)<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> long with a 97–122&#160;cm (38–48&#160;in) wingspan.<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It resembles other <a href="/wiki/Harrier_(bird)" title="Harrier (bird)">harriers</a> in having distinct male and female plumages. The sexes also differ in weight, with males weighing 290 to 400&#160;g (10 to 14&#160;oz), with an average of 350&#160;g (12&#160;oz), and females weighing 390 to 750&#160;g (14 to 26&#160;oz), with an average of 530&#160;g (19&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Among standard measurements, the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> is 32.8 to 40.6&#160;cm (12.9 to 16.0&#160;in), the <a href="/wiki/Tail" title="Tail">tail</a> is 19.3 to 25.8&#160;cm (7.6 to 10.2&#160;in) and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> is 7.1 to 8.9&#160;cm (2.8 to 3.5&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It is relatively long winged and long tailed.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The male is mainly grey above and white below except for the upper breast, which is grey like the upperparts, and the rump, which is white; the wings are grey with black wingtips. The female is brown above with white upper tail coverts, hence females, and the similar juveniles, are often called "ringtails". Their underparts are buff streaked with brown.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Immatures look like females but with less distinct barring, dark brown secondaries dark brown and less-streaked belly.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The female gives a whistled <i>piih-eh</i> when receiving food from the male, and her alarm call is <i>chit-it-it-it-it-et-it</i>. The male calls <i>chek-chek-chek</i>, with a more bouncing <i>chuk-uk-uk-uk</i> during his display flight.<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hen_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptor</a> breeds on <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moorland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prairie" title="Prairie">prairies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farmland</a> <a href="/wiki/Western_Gulf_coastal_grasslands" title="Western Gulf coastal grasslands">coastal prairies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grasslands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a> and other assorted open areas. A male will maintain a territory averaging 2.6&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (1.0&#160;sq&#160;mi), though male territories have ranged from 1.7 to 150&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (0.66 to 57.92&#160;sq&#160;mi).
</p><p>These, are the one of the few raptorial birds known to practice <a href="/wiki/Polygyny#Polygyny_in_animals" title="Polygyny">polygyny</a> – one male mates with several females. Up to five females have been known to mate with one male in a season. A supplementary feeding experiment on the Orkney islands showed that rates of polygyny were influenced by food levels; males provided with extra food had more breeding females than 'control' males that received no extra food.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The nest is built on the ground or on a mound of dirt or vegetation. Nests are made of sticks and are lined inside with grass and leaves. Four to eight (exceptionally 2 to 10) whitish <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> are laid. The eggs measure approximately 47&#160;mm ×&#160;36&#160;mm (1.9&#160;in ×&#160;1.4&#160;in). The eggs are incubated mostly by the female for 31 to 32 days. When incubating eggs, the female sits on the nest while the male hunts and brings food to her and the chicks. The male will help feed chicks after they hatch, but does not usually watch them for a greater period of time than around 5 minutes.<sup id="cite_ref-Weidensaul1996_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weidensaul1996-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The male usually passes off food to the female, which she then feeds to the young, although later the female will capture food and simply drop into the nest for her nestlings to eat. The chicks fledge at around 36 days old, though breeding maturity is not reached until 2 years in females and 3 years in males.
</p><p>In winter, the hen harrier is a bird of open country, and will then roost communally, often with <a href="/wiki/Merlin_(bird)" title="Merlin (bird)">merlins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marsh_harrier" title="Marsh harrier">marsh harriers</a>. There is now an accepted record of <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">transatlantic vagrancy</a> by the northern harrier, with a juvenile being recorded in <a href="/wiki/Scilly" class="mw-redirect" title="Scilly">Scilly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> from October 1982 to June 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-BB2007_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB2007-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hunting_behavior">Hunting behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hen_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hunting behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This is a typical harrier, which hunts on long wings held in a shallow V in its low flight during which the bird closely hugs the contours of the land below it. Northern or hen harriers hunt primarily small <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, as do most harriers. Up to 95% of the diet comprises small mammals.However, <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> are hunted with some regularity as well, especially by males. Preferred avian prey include <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerines</a> of open country (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Old_World_sparrow" title="Old World sparrow">sparrows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lark" title="Lark">larks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pipit" title="Pipit">pipits</a>), small <a href="/wiki/Shorebird" class="mw-redirect" title="Shorebird">shorebirds</a> and the young of <a href="/wiki/Waterfowl" class="mw-redirect" title="Waterfowl">waterfowl</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galliformes" title="Galliformes">galliforms</a>. Supplementing the diet occasionally are <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a>), <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Orthoptera" title="Orthoptera">orthopterans</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The species has been observed to hunt <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a> if these are available.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Larger prey, such as <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a> and adult <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">ducks</a> are taken sometimes and harriers have been known to subdue these by drowning them in water.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Harriers hunt by surprising prey while flying low to the ground in open areas, as they drift low over fields and moors.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The harriers circle an area several times listening and looking for prey. Harriers use hearing regularly to find prey, as they have exceptionally good hearing for diurnal raptors, this being the function of their <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owl</a>-like facial disc.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This harrier tends to be a very vocal bird while it glides over its hunting ground.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mortality_and_competition">Mortality and competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hen_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mortality and competition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Little information is available on longevity in hen harriers. The longest-lived known bird is 16 years and 5 months. However, adults rarely live more than 8 years. Early mortality mainly results from predation. Predators of eggs and nestlings include <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">raccoons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skunk" title="Skunk">skunks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Badger" title="Badger">badgers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">foxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corvus_(genus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Corvus (genus)">crows and ravens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owls</a>. Both parents attack potential predators with alarm calls and striking with talons. <a href="/wiki/Short-eared_owl" title="Short-eared owl">Short-eared owls</a> are natural competitors of this species that favor the same prey and habitat, as well as having a similarly broad distribution. Occasionally, both harriers and short-eared owls will harass each other until the victim drops its prey and it can be stolen, a practice known as <a href="/wiki/Kleptoparasitism" title="Kleptoparasitism">kleptoparasitism</a>. Most commonly, the harriers are the aggressors pirating prey from owls.<sup id="cite_ref-owlpages_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-owlpages-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hen_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species has a large range. There is evidence of a population decline, but the species is not believed to approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> (i.e., declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations). It is therefore classified as "least concern".<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In the United Kingdom, however, hen harrier populations are in a critical condition, due to habitat loss and illegal killing on <a href="/wiki/Grouse_moor" class="mw-redirect" title="Grouse moor">grouse moors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPBHHL_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPBHHL-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In 2012 only 617 pairs remained, representing a fall of 20% from 2004.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hen_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In some parts of Europe people believed that seeing a harrier perched on a house was a sign that three people would die. Unlike many raptors, hen harriers have historically been favorably regarded by farmers because they eat predators of quail eggs and mice that damage crops. Harriers are sometimes called "good hawks" because they pose no threat to poultry as some hawks do.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forestry_and_hen_harriers">Forestry and hen harriers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hen_harrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Forestry and hen harriers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Circus_cyaneus_MWNH_0850.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Circus_cyaneus_MWNH_0850.JPG/220px-Circus_cyaneus_MWNH_0850.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="191" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Circus_cyaneus_MWNH_0850.JPG/330px-Circus_cyaneus_MWNH_0850.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Circus_cyaneus_MWNH_0850.JPG/440px-Circus_cyaneus_MWNH_0850.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3135" data-file-height="2717" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Circus_cyaneus_MWNH_0850.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>The hen harrier is a bird of open habitats such as heather moorland and extensive agriculture. However, much of its range, particularly in Ireland and parts of western Britain, has been (and continues to be) afforested, predominantly with non-native conifers such as <a href="/wiki/Picea_sitchensis" title="Picea sitchensis">Sitka spruce</a> (<i>Picea sitchensis</i>) from North America.<sup id="cite_ref-Barton2006_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barton2006-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fielding2010_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fielding2010-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Hen harriers nest and forage in commercial forestry when it is young, before the canopy closes (typically at between 9–12 and years old), but do not make much use of thicket and subsequent growth stages,<sup id="cite_ref-Madders2000_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madders2000-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ODonoghue2004_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODonoghue2004-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> which typically comprise between &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>2</sup>&#8260;<sub>3</sub></span> and &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span> of the commercial growth cycle. Where forests replace habitats that were used by hen harriers they will therefore tend to reduce overall habitat availability.<sup id="cite_ref-OFlynn1983_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OFlynn1983-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> However, where afforestation takes place in areas that were previously underutilised by hen harriers, it may increase the value of such areas to this species in the long-term.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilson2009_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson2009-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Haworth2009_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haworth2009-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Areas dominated by forestry may remain suitable to hen harriers provided that a mosaic of age classes is maintained within the forest, such that areas of young, pre-thicket forest are always available.
</p>
