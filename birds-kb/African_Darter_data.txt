<p>The <b> African darter </b>(<i>Anhinga rufa</i>), sometimes called the <b>snakebird</b>, is a water bird of <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African_darter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The African darter is a member of the darter family, <a href="/wiki/Anhingidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Anhingidae">Anhingidae</a>, and is closely related to <a href="/wiki/Anhinga" title="Anhinga">American</a> (<i>Anhinga anhinga</i>), <a href="/wiki/Oriental_darter" title="Oriental darter">Oriental</a> (<i>Anhinga melanogaster</i>), and <a href="/wiki/Australasian_darter" title="Australasian darter">Australasian</a> (<i>Anhinga novaehollandiae</i>) darters.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African_darter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The male is mainly glossy black with white streaking, but females and immature birds are browner. The African darter differs in appearance from the American darter most recognisably by its thin white lateral neck stripe against a rufous background colour. The pointed bill should prevent confusion with cormorants.
</p><p>It is an 80&#160;cm long cormorant-like fish-eating species with a very long neck, like other anhingas.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African_darter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The African darter is found throughout <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a> wherever large bodies of water occur; overall the species remains widespread and common.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>, the Levant darter (<i>Anhinga rufa chantrei</i>), occurred at <a href="/wiki/Lake_Amik" title="Lake Amik">Lake Amik</a> (Amik Gölü) in south-central Turkey, in <a href="/wiki/Hula_valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Hula valley">Hula valley</a> lake and marshes in northern <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamian_Marshes" title="Mesopotamian Marshes">Mesopotamian Marshes</a> of the lower <a href="/wiki/Euphrat" class="mw-redirect" title="Euphrat">Euphrat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tigris" title="Tigris">Tigris</a> rivers in southern <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>. The Turkish population disappeared during the 1930s and the Israeli population during Hula drainage in the 1950s. It was feared that it also had disappeared from Iraq, but a small and threatened population remains at least in the <a href="/wiki/Hawizeh_Marshes" title="Hawizeh Marshes">Hawizeh Marshes</a> (part of the Mesopotamian Marshes), which are also home to numerous other waters birds such as <a href="/wiki/Little_grebe" title="Little grebe">little grebe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pygmy_cormorant" title="Pygmy cormorant">pygmy cormorant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marbled_teal" class="mw-redirect" title="Marbled teal">marbled teal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sacred_ibis" class="mw-redirect" title="Sacred ibis">sacred ibis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African_darter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pato_aguja_africano_(Anhinga_rufa),_parque_nacional_de_Chobe,_Botsuana,_2018-07-28,_DD_47.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Pato_aguja_africano_%28Anhinga_rufa%29%2C_parque_nacional_de_Chobe%2C_Botsuana%2C_2018-07-28%2C_DD_47.jpg/220px-Pato_aguja_africano_%28Anhinga_rufa%29%2C_parque_nacional_de_Chobe%2C_Botsuana%2C_2018-07-28%2C_DD_47.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="317" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Pato_aguja_africano_%28Anhinga_rufa%29%2C_parque_nacional_de_Chobe%2C_Botsuana%2C_2018-07-28%2C_DD_47.jpg/330px-Pato_aguja_africano_%28Anhinga_rufa%29%2C_parque_nacional_de_Chobe%2C_Botsuana%2C_2018-07-28%2C_DD_47.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Pato_aguja_africano_%28Anhinga_rufa%29%2C_parque_nacional_de_Chobe%2C_Botsuana%2C_2018-07-28%2C_DD_47.jpg/440px-Pato_aguja_africano_%28Anhinga_rufa%29%2C_parque_nacional_de_Chobe%2C_Botsuana%2C_2018-07-28%2C_DD_47.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2205" data-file-height="3177" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pato_aguja_africano_(Anhinga_rufa),_parque_nacional_de_Chobe,_Botsuana,_2018-07-28,_DD_47.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>African darter with prey, a <a href="/wiki/Cichlid" title="Cichlid">cichlid</a> fish</div></div></div>
<p>This species builds a stick nest in a tree and lays 3–6 eggs. It often nests with <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">herons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egret" title="Egret">egrets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cormorant" title="Cormorant">cormorants</a>.
</p><p>It often swims with only the neck above water, hence the common name <i>snakebird</i>. This, too, is a habit shared with the other anhingas.
</p><p>Unlike many other waterbirds the feathers of the African darter do not contain any oil and are therefore not waterproof. Because of this, the bird is less positively buoyant and its diving capabilities are enhanced. After diving for fish, the feathers can become waterlogged. In order to be able to fly and maintain heat insulation, it needs to dry its feathers. Thus the African darter is often seen sitting along the waterside spreading its wings and drying its feathers in the wind and the sun along with cormorants which may share its habitat.
</p>
