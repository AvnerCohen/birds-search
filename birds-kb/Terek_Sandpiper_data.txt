<p>The <b>Terek Sandpiper</b> (<i>Xenus cinereus</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" title="Palearctic" class="mw-redirect">Palearctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Monotypic_taxon" title="Monotypic taxon">only member</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><b>Xenus</b></i>. It is named after the <a href="/wiki/Terek_River" title="Terek River">Terek River</a> which flows into the west of the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>, as it was first observed around this area.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Terek_Sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description and systematics">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_systematics">Description and systematics</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Terek_Sandpiper_(Xenus_cinereus)_W_IMG_6894.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Terek_Sandpiper_%28Xenus_cinereus%29_W_IMG_6894.jpg/250px-Terek_Sandpiper_%28Xenus_cinereus%29_W_IMG_6894.jpg" width="250" height="196" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Terek_Sandpiper_(Xenus_cinereus)_W_IMG_6894.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
in <a href="/wiki/Krishna_Wildlife_Sanctuary" title="Krishna Wildlife Sanctuary">Krishna Wildlife Sanctuary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andhra_Pradesh" title="Andhra Pradesh">Andhra Pradesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Slightly larger than the <a href="/wiki/Common_Sandpiper" title="Common Sandpiper">Common Sandpiper</a> at 22–25&#160;cm length, its long upcurved bill – somewhat reminiscent of an <a href="/wiki/Avocet" title="Avocet">avocet</a>'s, but not as strongly curved – makes it very distinctive. As the scientific specific name implies, this wader has a grey back, face and breast in all <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumages</a>; a white <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a> may appear more or less distinct. The belly is whitish and the feet yellow; the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> has a yellowish base, with the rest being black.</p>
<p>The call is a high whistle.</p>
<p>Among the <a href="/wiki/Scolopacidae" title="Scolopacidae" class="mw-redirect">Scolopacidae</a>, <i>Xenus</i> is part of the <a href="/wiki/Tringa" title="Tringa">shank</a>-<a href="/wiki/Tattler_(bird)" title="Tattler (bird)">tattler</a>-<a href="/wiki/Phalarope" title="Phalarope">phalarope</a> <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> and less closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Calidrid" title="Calidrid">calidrid</a> sandpipers. Based on the degree of <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">DNA sequence</a> divergence and putative shank and phalarope <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> from around the <a href="/wiki/Oligocene" title="Oligocene">Oligocene</a>/<a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> boundary some 23-22 <a href="/wiki/Million_years_ago" title="Million years ago" class="mw-redirect">million years ago</a>, the Terek Sandpiper presumably diverged from their relatives in the <a href="/wiki/Late_Oligocene" title="Late Oligocene" class="mw-redirect">Late Oligocene</a>. Given the numerous <a href="/wiki/Basal_(evolution)" title="Basal (evolution)" class="mw-redirect">basal</a> fossils of the group found in <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> it is likely that the Terek Sandpiper lineage originated there, possibly by being isolated as the remains of the <a href="/wiki/Turgai_Sea" title="Turgai Sea">Turgai Sea</a> dried up, which happened just around this time.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Terek_Sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_ecology">Distribution and ecology</span></h2>
<p>This bird breeds near water in the <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a> from <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> through northern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Kolyma_River" title="Kolyma River">Kolyma River</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> south in winter to tropical coasts in east Africa, south Asia and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, usually preferring muddy areas. It is a rare vagrant in western Europe, and particularly in autumn it is sometimes seen passing through the <a href="/wiki/Marianas" title="Marianas" class="mw-redirect">Marianas</a> on migration; on <a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">Palau</a>, further off its usual migration route, it is decidedly uncommon on the other hand. Almost annually and apparently more and more often in recent times, a few birds stray to <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Islands" title="Aleutian Islands">Aleutian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pribilof_Islands" title="Pribilof Islands">Pribilof Islands</a>. Every few years, individual vagrants are recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Neotropics" title="Neotropics" class="mw-redirect">Neotropics</a>, where they arrive either as migrating birds from Africa, or as North American strays accompanying local waders south for winter. Such vagrants have been recorded as far south as <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It feeds in a distinctive and very active way, chasing insects and other mobile prey, and sometimes then running to the water's edge to wash its catch. It lays three or four eggs in a lined ground scrape.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Terek_Sandpiper_%26_Little_Stint_(Calidris_minuta)_W3_IMG_6891.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Terek_Sandpiper_%26_Little_Stint_%28Calidris_minuta%29_W3_IMG_6891.jpg/250px-Terek_Sandpiper_%26_Little_Stint_%28Calidris_minuta%29_W3_IMG_6891.jpg" width="250" height="162" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Terek_Sandpiper_%26_Little_Stint_(Calidris_minuta)_W3_IMG_6891.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Terek Sandpiper <i><a href="/wiki/Xenus_cinereus" title="Xenus cinereus" class="mw-redirect">Xenus cinereus</a></i> &amp; Little Stint <i><a href="/wiki/Calidris_minuta" title="Calidris minuta" class="mw-redirect">Calidris minuta</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Krishna_Wildlife_Sanctuary" title="Krishna Wildlife Sanctuary">Krishna Wildlife Sanctuary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andhra_Pradesh" title="Andhra Pradesh">Andhra Pradesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Terek Sandpiper likes to associate with <a href="/wiki/Ruddy_Turnstone" title="Ruddy Turnstone">Ruddy Turnstones</a> (<i>Arenaria interpres</i>), smallish <a href="/wiki/Calidrid" title="Calidrid">calidrids</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Charadrius" title="Charadrius">Charadrius</a></i> (but maybe not <i><a href="/wiki/Pluvialis" title="Pluvialis">Pluvialis</a></i>) <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">plovers</a>; a vagrant bird at <a href="/wiki/Paraty" title="Paraty">Paraty</a> (<a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro_state" title="Rio de Janeiro state" class="mw-redirect">Rio de Janeiro state</a>) was noted to pair up with a <a href="/wiki/Spotted_Sandpiper" title="Spotted Sandpiper">Spotted Sandpiper</a> (<i>Actitis macularius</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Thus it may be that the Terek Sandpiper under natural conditions may mate with <a href="/wiki/Common_Sandpiper" title="Common Sandpiper">Common Sandpiper</a> (<i>A. hypoleucos</i>), the Old World <a href="/wiki/Sister_species" title="Sister species" class="mw-redirect">sister species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Spotted_Sandpiper" title="Spotted Sandpiper">Spotted Sandpiper</a> (<i>A. macularius</i>). As <a href="/wiki/Hybridisation_in_shorebirds" title="Hybridisation in shorebirds">hybridisation in shorebirds</a> is extremely commonplace and <i>Actitis</i> is among the closer relatives of the Terek Sandpiper, such pairings (should they indeed occur) may produce <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrid</a> offspring.</p>
<p>This is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies. Widespread and often quite commonly seen, the Terek Sandpiper is not considered a threatened species by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN" class="mw-redirect">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
