<p>The <b>Terek sandpiper</b> (<i>Xenus cinereus</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> and is the <a href="/wiki/Monotypic_taxon" title="Monotypic taxon">only member</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><b>Xenus</b></i>. It is named after the <a href="/wiki/Terek_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Terek River">Terek River</a> which flows into the west of the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>, as it was first observed around this area.<sup id="cite_ref-Carnaby2009_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carnaby2009-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The genus name <i>Xenus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>xenos</i> stranger, and <i>cinereus</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "ash-grey" from <i>cinis, cineris</i>, "ashes".<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_systematics">Description and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terek_sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Xenus_cinereus_Lapland.JPG" class="image"><img alt="Xenus cinereus Lapland.JPG" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Xenus_cinereus_Lapland.JPG/220px-Xenus_cinereus_Lapland.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Xenus_cinereus_Lapland.JPG/330px-Xenus_cinereus_Lapland.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Xenus_cinereus_Lapland.JPG/440px-Xenus_cinereus_Lapland.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1305" data-file-height="870" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Xenus_cinereus_Lapland.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>
<p>Slightly larger than the <a href="/wiki/Common_sandpiper" title="Common sandpiper">common sandpiper</a> at 22–25&#160;cm (8.7–9.8&#160;in) length, its long upcurved bill – somewhat reminiscent of an <a href="/wiki/Avocet" title="Avocet">avocet</a>'s, but not as strongly curved – makes it very distinctive. As the scientific specific name implies, this wader has a grey back, face and breast in all <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumages</a>; a white <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a> may appear more or less distinct. The belly is whitish and the feet yellow; the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> has a yellowish base, with the rest being black.
</p><p>The call is a high whistle.
</p><p>Among the <a href="/wiki/Scolopacidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Scolopacidae">Scolopacidae</a>, <i>Xenus</i> is part of the <a href="/wiki/Tringa" title="Tringa">shank</a>-<a href="/wiki/Tattler_(bird)" title="Tattler (bird)">tattler</a>-<a href="/wiki/Phalarope" title="Phalarope">phalarope</a> <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> and less closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Calidrid" class="mw-redirect" title="Calidrid">calidrid</a> sandpipers. Based on the degree of <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" class="mw-redirect" title="DNA sequence">DNA sequence</a> divergence and putative shank and phalarope <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> from around the <a href="/wiki/Oligocene" title="Oligocene">Oligocene</a>/<a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> boundary some 23–22 <a href="/wiki/Million_years_ago" class="mw-redirect" title="Million years ago">million years ago</a>, the Terek sandpiper presumably diverged from their relatives in the <a href="/wiki/Late_Oligocene" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Oligocene">Late Oligocene</a>. Given the numerous <a href="/wiki/Basal_(evolution)" class="mw-redirect" title="Basal (evolution)">basal</a> fossils of the group found in <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> it is likely that the Terek sandpiper lineage originated there, possibly by being isolated as the remains of the <a href="/wiki/Turgai_Sea" title="Turgai Sea">Turgai Sea</a> dried up, which happened just around this time.<sup id="cite_ref-Mlikovsky2002_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mlikovsky2002-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Paton2003_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Paton2003-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Thomas2004_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas2004-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_ecology">Distribution and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terek_sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This bird breeds near water in the <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a> from <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> through northern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Kolyma_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Kolyma River">Kolyma River</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> south in winter to tropical coasts in east Africa, south Asia and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, usually preferring muddy areas. It is a rare vagrant in western Europe, and particularly in autumn it is sometimes seen passing through the <a href="/wiki/Marianas" class="mw-redirect" title="Marianas">Marianas</a> on migration; on <a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">Palau</a>, further off its usual migration route, it is decidedly uncommon on the other hand. Almost annually and apparently more and more often in recent times, a few birds stray to <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Islands" title="Aleutian Islands">Aleutian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pribilof_Islands" title="Pribilof Islands">Pribilof Islands</a>. Every few years, individual vagrants are recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Neotropics" class="mw-redirect" title="Neotropics">Neotropics</a>, where they arrive either as migrating birds from Africa, or as North American strays accompanying local waders south for winter. Such vagrants have been recorded as far south as <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiles2004_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiles2004-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-VanderWerf2006_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VanderWerf2006-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-White2006_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White2006-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The overall genetic variation in Terek sandpipers across their range is low, with some evidence of contractions followed by expansion. Although the geographically isolated Dniephe River population in Eastern Europe does show significant genetic differentiation.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It feeds in a distinctive and very active way, chasing insects and other mobile prey, and sometimes then running to the water's edge to wash its catch.
</p><p>It lays three or four eggs in a lined ground scrape.
</p><p>The Terek sandpiper likes to associate with <a href="/wiki/Ruddy_turnstone" title="Ruddy turnstone">ruddy turnstones</a> (<i>Arenaria interpres</i>), smallish <a href="/wiki/Calidrid" class="mw-redirect" title="Calidrid">calidrids</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Charadrius" title="Charadrius">Charadrius</a></i> (but maybe not <i><a href="/wiki/Pluvialis" title="Pluvialis">Pluvialis</a></i>) <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">plovers</a>; a vagrant bird at <a href="/wiki/Paraty" title="Paraty">Paraty</a> (<a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro_state" class="mw-redirect" title="Rio de Janeiro state">Rio de Janeiro state</a>) was noted to pair up with a <a href="/wiki/Spotted_sandpiper" title="Spotted sandpiper">spotted sandpiper</a> (<i>Actitis macularius</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-White2006_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White2006-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Thus it may be that the Terek sandpiper under natural conditions may mate with <a href="/wiki/Common_sandpiper" title="Common sandpiper">common sandpiper</a> (<i>A. hypoleucos</i>), the Old World <a href="/wiki/Sister_species" class="mw-redirect" title="Sister species">sister species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Spotted_sandpiper" title="Spotted sandpiper">spotted sandpiper</a> (<i>A. macularius</i>). As <a href="/wiki/Hybridisation_in_shorebirds" title="Hybridisation in shorebirds">hybridisation in shorebirds</a> is extremely commonplace and <i>Actitis</i> is among the closer relatives of the Terek sandpiper, such pairings (should they indeed occur) may produce <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrid</a> offspring.
</p><p>This is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies. Widespread and often quite commonly seen, the Terek sandpiper is not considered a threatened species by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BLI_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BLI-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
