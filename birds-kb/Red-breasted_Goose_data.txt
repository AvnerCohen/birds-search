<p>The <b>red-breasted goose</b> (<i>Branta ruficollis</i>) is a brightly marked species of <a href="/wiki/Goose" title="Goose">goose</a> in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Branta" title="Branta">Branta</a></i> from <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>. It is currently classified as <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_etymology">Taxonomy and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-breasted_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The red-breasted goose is sometimes placed in its own genus <i>Rufibrenta</i> but appears close enough to the <a href="/wiki/Brant_goose" class="mw-redirect" title="Brant goose">brant goose</a> (<i>Branta bernicla</i>) to make this unnecessary, despite its distinct appearance. Today all major authorities include the red-breasted goose in <i><a href="/wiki/Branta" title="Branta">Branta</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">Genetic</a> studies indicate that the red-breasted goose probably is one the few known examples of <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_speciation" title="Hybrid speciation">hybrid speciation</a> in birds, being the result of ancient interbreeding between the ancestral brant goose and white-cheeked goose (the latter being the ancestral species that later split into all today's <i>Branta</i> species, except the brant and red-breasted geese). This interbreeding appears to have happened at least 3.5 million years ago, with the <a href="/wiki/Lineage_(evolution)" title="Lineage (evolution)">lineage</a> of the red-breasted goose following its own <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolutionary</a> path since then.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Branta</i> is a Latinised form of <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> <i>Brandgás</i>, "burnt (black) goose and <i>ruficollis</i> is from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>rufus</i> "red" and <i>collis</i> "necked".<sup id="cite_ref-job90_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job90-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-breasted_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Red-breasted_Goose_(Branta_ruficollis)_RWD2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Red-breasted_Goose_%28Branta_ruficollis%29_RWD2.jpg/220px-Red-breasted_Goose_%28Branta_ruficollis%29_RWD2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Red-breasted_Goose_%28Branta_ruficollis%29_RWD2.jpg/330px-Red-breasted_Goose_%28Branta_ruficollis%29_RWD2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Red-breasted_Goose_%28Branta_ruficollis%29_RWD2.jpg/440px-Red-breasted_Goose_%28Branta_ruficollis%29_RWD2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2102" data-file-height="1576" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Red-breasted_Goose_(Branta_ruficollis)_RWD2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Red-breasted goose swimming</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Branta_ruficollis_portrait.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Branta ruficollis portrait.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Branta_ruficollis_portrait.jpg/220px-Branta_ruficollis_portrait.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Branta_ruficollis_portrait.jpg/330px-Branta_ruficollis_portrait.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Branta_ruficollis_portrait.jpg/440px-Branta_ruficollis_portrait.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3665" data-file-height="2749" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Branta_ruficollis_portrait.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>
<p>All the <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of the genus <i>Branta</i> are distinguished by their dark sooty colour, relieved by white, and as a distinction from the grey geese of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Anser_(genus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Anser (genus)">Anser</a></i>. Among the species from these two genera, the red-breasted goose is the smallest at 53–56&#160;cm (21–22&#160;in) in length.<sup id="cite_ref-Ogilvie_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ogilvie-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This brightly marked species is unmistakable, but can be surprisingly difficult to find amongst brant geese. At long distances, the red of the breast tends to look dark.<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-breasted_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The red-breasted goose breeds in Arctic <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, mainly on the <a href="/wiki/Taymyr_Peninsula" title="Taymyr Peninsula">Taymyr Peninsula</a>, with smaller populations in the <a href="/wiki/Gyda_Peninsula" title="Gyda Peninsula">Gyda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yamal_Peninsula" title="Yamal Peninsula">Yamal peninsulas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BLIfactsheet_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BLIfactsheet-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Most winter along the northwestern shores of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> (occasionally moving further southwest to <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>), but some winter in <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BLIfactsheet_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BLIfactsheet-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It is a rare vagrant to <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and other western European areas, where it is sometimes found with flocks of Brent or <a href="/wiki/Barnacle_goose" title="Barnacle goose">barnacle geese</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> However, since it is common in captive wildfowl collections, escapees outside its usual range are fairly frequent.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A large part of the population traditionally wintered in <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0stisu,_Lankaran" title="İstisu, Lankaran">Kirov</a> Bay in the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>, but in the 1960s the area became unsuitable for the geese due to the agricultural change. Vineyards and cotton replaced the cereal crops used by the wintering geese. However, catastrophic population decline was avoided because they were able to alter their migration strategy and now winter in suitable habitats in Bulgaria and the <a href="/wiki/Dobruja" title="Dobruja">Dobrogea</a> region of Eastern Romania.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Late <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> remains of the species have been found in Bulgaria,<sup id="cite_ref-Boev1998_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boev1998-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and it is featured on <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egyptian</a> paintings, including the famous <a href="/wiki/Meidum_Geese" class="mw-redirect" title="Meidum Geese">Meidum Geese</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-breasted_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rufibrenta_ruficollis_Rothalsgans_4.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Rufibrenta_ruficollis_Rothalsgans_4.jpg/220px-Rufibrenta_ruficollis_Rothalsgans_4.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="125" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Rufibrenta_ruficollis_Rothalsgans_4.jpg/330px-Rufibrenta_ruficollis_Rothalsgans_4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Rufibrenta_ruficollis_Rothalsgans_4.jpg/440px-Rufibrenta_ruficollis_Rothalsgans_4.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1661" data-file-height="945" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rufibrenta_ruficollis_Rothalsgans_4.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Front view</div></div></div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Branta_ruficollis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.14.2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Branta_ruficollis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.14.2.jpg/220px-Branta_ruficollis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.14.2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="139" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Branta_ruficollis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.14.2.jpg/330px-Branta_ruficollis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.14.2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Branta_ruficollis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.14.2.jpg/440px-Branta_ruficollis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.14.2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3556" data-file-height="2242" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Branta_ruficollis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.14.2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs of red-breasted goose</div></div></div>
<p>Calls of red-breasted goose – <i>ki-kui</i> or <i>ki-yik</i>, shrill and <a href="/wiki/Staccato" title="Staccato">staccato</a>
</p><p>The red-breasted goose often nests close to nests of <a href="/wiki/Birds_of_prey" class="mw-redirect" title="Birds of prey">birds of prey</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Snowy_owl" title="Snowy owl">snowy owls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">peregrine falcons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rough-legged_buzzard" title="Rough-legged buzzard">rough-legged buzzards</a>, which helps to protect this small goose from <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammalian</a> predators such as the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_fox" title="Arctic fox">Arctic fox</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ogilvie_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ogilvie-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Collins_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The closer the goose's nest to the eyrie (bird of prey nest), the safer it is from predation.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Based on the size and how timid the geese are, they rely on the bird of prey for defense. It is extremely unusual for the bird of prey to attack, but is still possible. Additionally, the larger the colony the safer it is. This in turn positively influences the survival and reproductive success of the red-breasted goose. Aside from nesting close to birds of prey, red-breasted geese nest on islands on rivers which also protects them from land-based predators.<sup id="cite_ref-Prop_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prop-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> However, there is evidence that the red-breasted goose will prefer to nest near birds of prey over the choice of a river island.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In contrast to the birds of prey that offer the red-breasted goose protection from predators, which choose to place their nests in the open cliff side, this goose will create a cryptic nest hidden much better than the birds of prey nests.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> A typical red-breasted goose colony is around 4 pairs depending on nest location, food abundance, and bird of prey density.<sup id="cite_ref-Prop_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prop-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> While incubating, the red-breasted goose stays within a 1.5&#160;km (0.9&#160;mi) range of its nest.<sup id="cite_ref-Prop_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prop-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  Male red-breasted goose tend to guard the nest of their young from a distance while the females generally hide on the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The main predator for red-breasted geese eggs and goslings is the <a href="/wiki/Taimyr_Gull" class="mw-redirect" title="Taimyr Gull">Taimyr Gull</a> which have access to nests located on river islands.<sup id="cite_ref-Prop_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prop-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While wintering, the red-breasted goose feeds on grasses, leaves and seeds.<sup id="cite_ref-BLIfactsheet_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BLIfactsheet-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Since owl and buzzard populations fluctuate every few years, depending on <a href="/wiki/Lemming" title="Lemming">lemming</a> abundance, the only consistent protection from predators are river islands and the peregrine falcon.<sup id="cite_ref-Prop_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prop-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-breasted_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Branta_ruficollis.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Branta ruficollis.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Branta_ruficollis.jpg/220px-Branta_ruficollis.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Branta_ruficollis.jpg/330px-Branta_ruficollis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Branta_ruficollis.jpg/440px-Branta_ruficollis.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Branta_ruficollis.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>
<p>In autumn of 1997 the population was estimated to be around 88,000 individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In the winter these numbers can shrink to around 70,000. The red-breasted goose is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies. The red-breasted goose is legally protected in many states, however hunting is still continued. The AEWA is monitoring the species and providing up to date information on the status of the species, its habitat, migrations, ecology, and conservation needs.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It was considered a <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">Vulnerable species</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>. Over 80% of the population roost during the winter at just five sites, with nearby feeding areas threatened by changes in land use. In addition, there has been a strong decline in numbers in the last decades. However, it is possible that this decline may have been exaggerated, as it is possible that some birds may winter at unknown sites. The species' winter distribution has already changed significantly since the 1960s when much of the population occurred along the western coast of the Caspian Sea, in Iran, and in Iraq. Some birds may now be wintering farther west as indicated by recorded counts of 2,000 birds in Hungary as of the winter of 2014, whereas counts previously only accounted for a few hundred. As it is not clear to what extent the known population fluctuates in this species—as in other Arctic geese—and given the worsening outlook for the species as a whole, the red-breasted goose was uplisted from a species of <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> to <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">Endangered</a> status in the 2007 <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BLI_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BLI-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In 2015 it was relisted as Vulnerable.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> There have been attempts to conserve the species, such as in 2005–2008, a <a href="/wiki/The_LIFE_Programme" title="The LIFE Programme">Life</a>-funded project in Romania was implemented so that the habitat quality of an important salt water lake used by the species was increased.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The Life Programme and AEWA hosted a workshop in February 2009 for the species that aimed to draft a new International Species Action Plan and report the results of the Life project.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, Ornithologists from the Bulgarian Society for the Protection of Birds (BSPB/BirdLife in Bulgaria) and the <a href="/wiki/Wildfowl_%26_Wetlands_Trust" title="Wildfowl &amp; Wetlands Trust">Wildfowl &amp; Wetlands Trust</a> enacted a project that would lead to a greater understanding of the red-breasted geese's migration patterns. Six red-breasted geese were branded and given miniature GPS transmitters. The red-breasted geese will now be tracked via satellite. Scientist and conservationists alike hope that learning about the geese's migration patterns will lead to proper conservation and more aggressive policies in the geese's territory. Some prior counts have revealed a more positive discovery, noting an increase in the geese's population. This led to a prediction which states there may be over a fifty percent increase in their population.
</p><p>In February 2013, the Bulgarian court had overturned a decision which prohibited an energy wind project from continuing their work in an important winter site for the threatened red-breasted geese. The construction of wind turbines in the area not only threaten the geese by being an obstacle that they fly into. The area they are being constructed in will also deprive red-breasted geese of a critical feeding ground. As of now, an appeal had been sent to court to convince the court otherwise.
</p>
