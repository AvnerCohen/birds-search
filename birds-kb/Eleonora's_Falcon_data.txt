<p><b>Eleonora's Falcon</b> (<i>Falco eleonorae</i>) is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon</a>. It belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Hobby_(bird)" title="Hobby (bird)">hobby</a> group, a rather close-knit number of similar falcons often considered a <a href="/wiki/Subgenus" title="Subgenus">subgenus</a> <i>Hypotriorchis</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Sooty_Falcon" title="Sooty Falcon">Sooty Falcon</a> is sometimes considered its closest relative, but while they certainly belong to the same lineage, they do not seem to be close <a href="/wiki/Sister_species" title="Sister species" class="mw-redirect">sister species</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Eleonora's Falcon is named after <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_of_Arborea" title="Eleanor of Arborea">Eleonor of Arborea</a>, national heroine of <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eleonora%27s_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eleonora%27s_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habitat and distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_distribution">Habitat and distribution</span></h3>
<p>This species breeds on <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">islands</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a> particularly off <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> (where two-thirds of the world's population breeds), but also in the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ibiza" title="Ibiza">Ibiza</a> and off <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tilos_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tilos Park (page does not exist)">Tilos Park</a> is the breeding area for ten percent of the world population of Eleonora Falcons. Six hundred and fifty pairs of this species <a href="/wiki/Breeding_in_the_wild" title="Breeding in the wild">breed</a> on this island according to research conducted by the Hellenic Ornithological Society and the European Union LIFE-Nature program of Tilos. It is rare as a vagrant north of its range.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eleonora%27s_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Morphology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span></h3>
<p>Eleonora's Falcon is an elegant <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a>, 36–42&#160;cm long with an 87–104&#160;cm wingspan. It is shaped like a large <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Hobby" title="Eurasian Hobby">Eurasian Hobby</a> or a small slender <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_Falcon" title="Peregrine Falcon">Peregrine Falcon</a>, with its long pointed wings, long tail and slim body. There are two colour <a href="/wiki/Morph_(zoology)" title="Morph (zoology)" class="mw-redirect">morphs</a>: The adult dark morph is all sooty brown, with black <a href="/wiki/Underwing" title="Underwing" class="mw-redirect">underwing</a> <a href="/wiki/Covert_(feather)" title="Covert (feather)" class="mw-redirect">coverts</a>. The light morph is more like a juvenile Eurasian Hobby, but has buff underparts, and also shows the contrast between the black underwing coverts and paler base to the flight feathers. Young birds are also like a large juvenile Hobby, but the pale underparts contrast with darker wingtips and wing coverts. The <a href="/wiki/Bird_song" title="Bird song" class="mw-redirect">call</a> is a typical falcon <i>kek-kek-kek</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eleonora%27s_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Migration route">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Migration_route">Migration route</span></h3>
<p>This is a long-distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrator</a>, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>. The migration route has been recently discovered and contrary to previous suggestions it has been demonstrated by satellite telemetry to be inland through the African continent. Traditionally it has been suggested to be coastal, with birds from the western end of the Mediterranean flying to <a href="/wiki/Suez" title="Suez">Suez</a> before flying south down the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>, and across the Horn of Africa. However, recent satellite tracked animals by Spanish and German researchers have demonstrated an inland route through the <a href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" title="Sahara Desert" class="mw-redirect">Sahara Desert</a>, the equatorial rainforests until reaching Kenya and Mozambique. The total distance covered during the flight has reached up to 9000&#160;km for a single one-way trip.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eleonora%27s_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Feeding habits">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding_habits">Feeding habits</span></h3>
<p>It will take large <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Dragonfly" title="Dragonfly">dragonflies</a>, which are transferred from talons to beak and eaten in flight. It nests colonially on coastal cliffs, laying up to four <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>. This species has a delayed breeding season, in late summer, because it is a specialist hunter of <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrating birds</a> which pass through the Mediterranean islands at this time of year. It captures small birds in flight, using its speed and aerobatic skills. Birds spend much time cruising along coastal cliffs with steady wingbeats watching for tired incoming migrants.(Walter 1979). This falcon is unique in that it is one of the few species that breeds during early autumn, feeding its chicks with other migratory birds that are in abundance that period. It is also one of the few falcon species that creates breeding colonies.</p>
