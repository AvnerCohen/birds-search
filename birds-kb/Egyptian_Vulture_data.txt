<p>The <b>Egyptian Vulture</b> (<i>Neophron percnopterus</i>), also called the <b>White Scavenger Vulture</b> or <b>Pharaoh's Chicken</b>, is a small <a href="/wiki/Old_World_vulture" title="Old World vulture">Old World vulture</a> and the only member of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i>Neophron</i>. It is widely distributed; the Egyptian Vulture is found from southwestern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> to <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. The contrasting underwing pattern and wedge-shaped tail make it distinctive in flight as it soars in thermals during the warmer parts of the day. Egyptian Vultures feed mainly on carrion but are opportunistic and will prey on small mammals, birds, and reptiles. They also feed on the eggs of other birds, breaking larger ones by tossing a large pebble onto them. The use of tools is rare in birds and apart from the use of a pebble as a hammer, Egyptian Vultures also use twigs to roll up wool for use in their nest. Egyptian Vultures that breed in the temperate regions migrate south in winter while tropical populations are relatively sedentary. Populations of this species have declined in the 20th century and some island populations are endangered by hunting, accidental poisoning, and collision with power lines.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Egyptian_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span></h2>
<p>The genus <i>Neophron</i> contains only a single species. A few prehistoric species from the <a href="/wiki/Neogene" title="Neogene">Neogene</a> period in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> placed in the genus <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neophrontops&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Neophrontops (page does not exist)">Neophrontops</a></i> (the name meaning "looks like <i>Neophron</i>") are believed to have been very similar to these vultures in lifestyle, but the genetic relationships are unclear.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFeduccia1974_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFeduccia1974-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHertel1995_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHertel1995-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The genus <i>Neophron</i> is considered to represent the oldest branch within the evolutionary tree of vultures.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWinkHeidrichFentzloff1996_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWinkHeidrichFentzloff1996-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Along with its nearest evolutionary relative, the <a href="/wiki/Lammergeier" title="Lammergeier" class="mw-redirect">Lammergeier</a> (<i>Gypaetus barbatus</i>), they are sometimes placed in a separate subfamily, the Gypaetinae.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWink1995_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWink1995-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESeiboldHelbig1995_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeiboldHelbig1995-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> There are three widely-recognised subspecies of the Egyptian Vulture, although there is considerable gradation due to movement and intermixing of the populations.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zar_et_al._2002b_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zar_et_al._2002b-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The nominate subspecies, <i>N. p. percnopterus</i>, has the largest range, occurring in southern Europe, northern Africa, the Middle East, Central Asia, and the north-west of India. Populations breeding in the temperate zone migrate south during winter. It has a dark grey bill. The Indian Subcontinent is the range of subspecies <i>N. p. ginginianus</i>, the smallest of the three subspecies, which is identifiable by a pale yellow bill.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeters1979304_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeters1979304-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERasmussenAnderton2005_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERasmussenAnderton2005-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The subspecies name is derived from <a href="/wiki/Gingee" title="Gingee">Gingee</a> in southern India.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPittie2004_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPittie2004-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> A small population that is found only in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> was found to be genetically distinct and identified as a new subspecies, <i>N. p. majorensis</i> in 2002. Known locally as the <i>guirre</i> they are genetically more distant from <i>N. p. percnopterus</i>, significantly greater even than <i>N. p. ginginianus</i> is from <i>N. p. percnopterus</i>. Unlike neighbouring populations in Africa and southern Europe, it is non-migratory and consistently larger in size. The subspecies name <i>majorensis</i> is derived from "Majorata", the ancient name for the island of <a href="/wiki/Fuerteventura" title="Fuerteventura">Fuerteventura</a>. The island was named by Spanish conquerors in the 15th century after the "Majos", the main native <a href="/wiki/Guanches" title="Guanches">Guanche tribe</a> there.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zar_et_al._2002b_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zar_et_al._2002b-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKretzmannCapoteGautschiGodoy2003_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKretzmannCapoteGautschiGodoy2003-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> One study in 2010 suggested that the species established on the island about 2,500 years ago when the island was first colonized by humans.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAgudoRicoVil.C3.A0Hiraldo2010_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAgudoRicoVil.C3.A0Hiraldo2010-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Zarudny" title="Nikolai Zarudny">Nikolai Zarudny</a> and Härms described a subspecies, <i>rubripersonatus</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Baluchistan" title="Baluchistan" class="mw-redirect">Baluchistan</a> in 1902. This was described as having a deeper reddish orange skin<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHartert1920_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHartert1920-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> on the head and a yellow-tipped dark bill. This has rarely been considered a valid subspecies but the intermediate pattern of bill colouration suggests intermixing of subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZarudnyH.C3.A4rms1902_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZarudnyH.C3.A4rms1902-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERasmussenAnderton2005_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERasmussenAnderton2005-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The genus name is derived from <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>. <a href="/wiki/Timandra_(mythology)" title="Timandra (mythology)">Timandra</a> was the mother of <a href="/wiki/Neophron_(mythology)" title="Neophron (mythology)" class="mw-redirect">Neophron</a>. <a href="/wiki/Aegypius_(mythology)" title="Aegypius (mythology)">Aegypius</a> was a friend of Neophron and about the same age. It upset Neophron to know that his mother Timandra was having a love affair with Aegypius. Seeking revenge, Neophron made advances towards Aegypius' mother, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bulis_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bulis (mythology) (page does not exist)">Bulis</a>. Neophron succeeded and enticed Bulis into entering the dark chamber where his mother and Aegypius were to meet soon. Neophron then distracted his mother, tricking Aegypius into entering the chamber and sleeping with his own mother Bulis. When Bulis discovered the deception she gouged out the eyes of her son Aegypius before killing herself. Aegypius prayed for revenge and <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> on hearing the prayer changed Aegypius and Neophron into vultures.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrimal1996_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrimal1996-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> "Percnopterus" is derived from Greek for "black wings": "περκνóς" (<i>perknos</i>, meaning "blue-black") and πτερόν (<i>pteron</i>, meaning wing).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKoenig1907_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKoenig1907-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1895146_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1895146-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Egyptian_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Neophron_percnopterus.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Neophron_percnopterus.JPG/220px-Neophron_percnopterus.JPG" width="220" height="187" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Neophron_percnopterus.JPG/330px-Neophron_percnopterus.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Neophron_percnopterus.JPG/440px-Neophron_percnopterus.JPG 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Neophron_percnopterus.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Adult <i>N. p. percnopterus</i> in captivity showing white plumage</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The adult's <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> is white, with black flight feathers in the wings. Wild birds usually appear soiled with a rusty or brown shade to the white plumage, derived from mud or iron-rich soil. Captive specimens without access to soil have clean white plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClarkSchmitt1998_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClarkSchmitt1998-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zar_et_al._2002a_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zar_et_al._2002a-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The bill is slender and long, and the tip of the upper mandible is hooked. The nostril is an elongated horizontal slit. The neck feathers are long and form a <a href="/wiki/Hackle" title="Hackle">hackle</a>. The wings are pointed, with the third primary being the longest; the tail is wedge shaped. The legs are pink in adults and grey in juveniles.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFerguson-LeesChristie2001_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFerguson-LeesChristie2001-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The claws are long and straight, and the third and fourth toes are slightly webbed at the base. The bill is black in the nominate subspecies but pale or yellowish in adults of the smaller Indian <i>ginginianus</i>. <a href="/wiki/Pamela_C._Rasmussen" title="Pamela C. Rasmussen">Rasmussen</a> and Anderton (2005) suggest that this variation may need further study, particularly due to the intermediate black-tipped bill described in <i>rubripersonatus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERasmussenAnderton2005_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERasmussenAnderton2005-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhistler1922_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhistler1922-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The facial skin is yellow and unfeathered down to the throat. The sexes are indistinguishable in plumage but breeding males have a deeper orange facial skin colour than females.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClarkSchmitt1998_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClarkSchmitt1998-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Females average slightly larger and are about 10-15% heavier than males.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFerguson-LeesChristie2001_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFerguson-LeesChristie2001-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Young birds are blackish or chocolate brown with black and white patches.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAliRipley1978_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAliRipley1978-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> The adult plumage is attained only after about five years.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClarkSchmitt1998_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClarkSchmitt1998-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The adult Egyptian Vulture measures 47–65 centimetres (19–26 in) from the point of the beak to the extremity of the tail feathers. In the smaller <i>N. p. ginginianus</i> males are about 47–52 centimetres (19–20 in) long while females are 52–55.5 centimetres (20–21.9 in) long.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERasmussenAnderton2005_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERasmussenAnderton2005-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The wingspan is about 2.7 times the body length.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFerguson-LeesChristie2001_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFerguson-LeesChristie2001-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Birds from Spain weigh about 1.9 kilograms (4.2&#160;lb) while birds of the Canary Island subspecies <i>majorensis</i>, representing a case of <a href="/wiki/Island_gigantism" title="Island gigantism">island gigantism</a>, are heavier with an average weight of 2.4 kilograms (5.3&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zar_et_al._2002a_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zar_et_al._2002a-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Egyptian_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and movements">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_movements">Distribution and movements</span></h2>
<p>Egyptian Vultures are widely distributed across the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> with their breeding range from southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> to northern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> east to <a href="/wiki/Western_Asia" title="Western Asia">western</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">southern Asia</a>. They are rare vagrants in <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAliRipley1978_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAliRipley1978-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> They occur mainly on the dry plains and lower hills. In the Himalayas, they go up to about 2,000 metres (6,600&#160;ft) in summer. European populations migrate south to Africa in winter. Vagrants may occur as far south as in South Africa although they bred in the <a href="/wiki/Transkei" title="Transkei">Transkei</a> region prior to 1923.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMundy1978_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMundy1978-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> They nest mainly on rocky cliffs, sometimes adopting ledges on tall buildings in cities and on large trees.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAliRipley1978_21-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAliRipley1978-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Most Egyptian Vultures in the temperate zone migrate south to Africa in winter. Like many other large soaring migrants, they avoid making long crossings over water.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYosefAlon1997_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYosefAlon1997-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpaar1997_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpaar1997-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Italian birds cross over through Sicily and into Tunisia making short sea crossings by passing through the islands of <a href="/wiki/Marettimo" title="Marettimo">Marettimo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pantelleria" title="Pantelleria">Pantelleria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAgostiniPremudaMellonePanuccio2004_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAgostiniPremudaMellonePanuccio2004-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Those that migrate through the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> cross into Africa over the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Gibraltar" title="Strait of Gibraltar">Strait of Gibraltar</a> while others cross further east through the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGarc.C3.ADa-Ripoll.C3.A9sL.C3.B3pez-L.C3.B3pezUrios2010_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGarc.C3.ADa-Ripoll.C3.A9sL.C3.B3pez-L.C3.B3pezUrios2010-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeyburgGallardoMeyburgDimitrova2004_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeyburgGallardoMeyburgDimitrova2004-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFerguson-LeesChristie2001_19-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFerguson-LeesChristie2001-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Migrating birds can sometimes cover 500 kilometres (310&#160;mi) in a single day until they reach the southern edge of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>, 3,500 to 5,500 kilometres (2,200 to 3,400 mi) from their summer home. Young birds that have not reached breeding age may overwinter in the grassland and semi-desert regions of the <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeyburgGallardoMeyburgDimitrova2004_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeyburgGallardoMeyburgDimitrova2004-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Egyptian_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Egyptian_vulture_in_flight.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Egyptian_vulture_in_flight.jpg/220px-Egyptian_vulture_in_flight.jpg" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Egyptian_vulture_in_flight.jpg/330px-Egyptian_vulture_in_flight.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Egyptian_vulture_in_flight.jpg/440px-Egyptian_vulture_in_flight.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Egyptian_vulture_in_flight.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>N. p. ginginianus</i> in flight</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Egypian Vulture is usually seen singly or in pairs, soaring in thermals along with other scavengers and birds of prey, or perched on the ground or atop a building. On the ground, they walk with a waddling gait.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAliRipley1978_21-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAliRipley1978-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> They feed on a range of food, including mammal faeces (including those of humans<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhistler1949_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhistler1949-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup>), insects in dung, carrion, vegetable matter, and sometimes small animals.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPrakashNanjappa1988_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPrakashNanjappa1988-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> When it joins other vulture species at a dead animal, it tends to stay on the periphery and waits until the larger species to leave.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFerguson-LeesChristie2001_19-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFerguson-LeesChristie2001-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Wild rabbits (<i><a href="/wiki/Oryctolagus_cuniculus" title="Oryctolagus cuniculus" class="mw-redirect">Oryctolagus cuniculus</a></i>) form a significant part of the diet of Spanish vultures.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMargalidaBen.C3.ADtezS.C3.A1nchez-Zapata.C3.81vila2012_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMargalidaBen.C3.ADtezS.C3.A1nchez-Zapata.C3.81vila2012-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Studies suggest that they feed on <a href="/wiki/Ungulate" title="Ungulate">ungulate</a> faeces to obtain <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">carotenoid</a> pigments responsible for their bright yellow and orange facial skin. The ability to assimilate carotenoid pigments may serve as a <a href="/wiki/Signalling_theory" title="Signalling theory">reliable signal</a> of fitness.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENegroGrandeTellaGarrido2002_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENegroGrandeTellaGarrido2002-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Egyptian Vultures are mostly silent but make high-pitched mewing or hissing notes at the nest and screeching noises when squabbling at a <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">carcass</a>. Young birds have been heard making a hissing croak in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERasmussenAnderton2005_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERasmussenAnderton2005-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> They also hiss or growl when threatened or angry.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaker1928_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaker1928-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Egyptian Vultures roost communally on large trees, buildings or on cliffs.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERasmussenAnderton2005_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERasmussenAnderton2005-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Roost sites are usually chosen close to a dump site or other suitable foraging area. In Spain, summer roosts are formed mainly by immature birds. The favourite roost trees tended to be large dead <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pines</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zarCeballosTella1996_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zarCeballosTella1996-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECeballosDon.C3.A1zar1990_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECeballosDon.C3.A1zar1990-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> The number of adults at the roost increases towards June. It is thought that breeding adults may be able to forage more efficiently by joining the roost and following others to the best feeding areas. Breeding birds that failed to raise young may also join the non-breeding birds at the roost during June.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMargalidaBoudet2003_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMargalidaBoudet2003-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The breeding season is in spring.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAliRipley1978_21-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAliRipley1978-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> During the beginning of the breeding season, courting pairs soar high together and one or both may make steep spiralling or swooping dives.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFerguson-LeesChristie2001_19-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFerguson-LeesChristie2001-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The birds are monogamous and pair bonds may be maintained for more than one breeding season and the same nest sites may be reused each year. The nest is an untidy platform of twigs lined with rags and placed on a cliff ledge,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECeballosDon.C3.A1zar1989_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECeballosDon.C3.A1zar1989-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> building, or the fork of a large tree. Old nest platforms of eagles may also be taken over.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAliRipley1978_21-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAliRipley1978-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERasmussenAnderton2005_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERasmussenAnderton2005-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Nests placed on the ground are rare but have been recorded in subspecies <i>N. p. ginginianus</i> and <i>N. p. majorensis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBiddulph1937_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiddulph1937-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPaynter1924_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPaynter1924-38"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGangoso2005_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGangoso2005-39"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> Extra-pair copulation with neighbouring birds has been recorded and may be a reason for adult males to stay close to the female before and during the egg laying period.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zarCeballosTella1994_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zarCeballosTella1994-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> Females may sometimes associate with two males and all three help in raising the brood.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETella1993_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETella1993-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> The typical clutch consists of two eggs which are incubated in turns by both parents. The eggs are brick red with the broad end covered more densely with blotches of red, brown, and black.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaker1928_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaker1928-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> The parents begin incubating soon after the first egg is laid leading to asynchronous hatching. The first egg hatches after about 42 days.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAliRipley1978_21-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAliRipley1978-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> The second chick may hatch three to five days later and a longer delay increases the likelihood that it will die of starvation.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zar_.26_Ceballos_1989a_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zar_.26_Ceballos_1989a-42"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> In cliffs where the nests are located close to each other, young birds have been known to clamber over to neighbouring nests to obtain food.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zarCeballos1990_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zarCeballos1990-43"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> In the Spanish population, young fledge and leave the nest after 90 to 110 days.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zar_.26_Ceballos_1989b_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zar_.26_Ceballos_1989b-44"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> Fledged birds continue to remain dependent on their parents for at least a month.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFerguson-LeesChristie2001_19-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFerguson-LeesChristie2001-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Once the birds begin to forage on their own, they move away from their parents' territory; young birds have been found nearly 500&#160;km away from their nest site.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEElorriagaZuberogoitiaCastilloAzkona2009_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEElorriagaZuberogoitiaCastilloAzkona2009-45"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECeballosDon.C3.A1zar1990_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECeballosDon.C3.A1zar1990-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> One year old European birds migrate to Africa and stay there for at least one year. A vulture that fledged in France stayed in Africa for three years before migrating north in spring.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeyburgGallardoMeyburgDimitrova2004_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeyburgGallardoMeyburgDimitrova2004-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGarc.C3.ADa-Ripoll.C3.A9sL.C3.B3pez-L.C3.B3pezUrios2010_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGarc.C3.ADa-Ripoll.C3.A9sL.C3.B3pez-L.C3.B3pezUrios2010-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> After migrating back to their breeding areas, young birds move widely in search of good feeding territories and mates. The full adult plumage is attained in the fourth or fifth year. Egyptian Vultures have been known to live for up to 37 years in captivity and at least 21 years in the wild. The probability of survival in the wild varies with age, increasing till the age of 2 and then falling at the age of 5. Older birds have an annual survival probability varying from 0.75 for non-breeders to 0.83 for breeding birds.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrandeSerranoTavecchiaCarrete2009_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrandeSerranoTavecchiaCarrete2009-46"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Healthy adults do not have many predators, but human activities pose many threats. Collisions with power lines, hunting, intentional poisoning, lead accumulation from ingesting gunshot in carcasses, and pesticide accumulation take a toll on populations. Young birds at the nest are sometimes taken by <a href="/wiki/Golden_Eagle" title="Golden Eagle">Golden Eagles</a>, eagle owls,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETellaMa.C3.B1osa1993_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETellaMa.C3.B1osa1993-47"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> and red foxes.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zarCeballos1988_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zarCeballos1988-48"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> Only rarely do adult birds attempt to drive away predators.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMateoOlea2007_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMateoOlea2007-49"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> Young birds that fall off of cliff ledges may be preyed on by mammalian predators such as jackals, foxes and wolves.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStoyanovaStefanov1993_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStoyanovaStefanov1993-50"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The nominate population, especially in Africa, is known for its use of stones as tools. When a large egg, such as that of an ostrich or bustard, is located, the bird walks up to it with a large pebble held in its bill and tosses the pebble by swinging the neck down over the egg. The operation is repeated until the egg cracks from the blows.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Lawick-Goodallvan_Lawick1966_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Lawick-Goodallvan_Lawick1966-51"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> They prefer using rounded pebbles to jagged rocks. This behaviour, first reported by <a href="/wiki/Jane_Goodall" title="Jane Goodall">Jane Goodall</a> in 1966, has however not been recorded in <i>N. p. ginginianus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERasmussenAnderton2005_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERasmussenAnderton2005-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Tests with both hand-reared and wild birds suggests that the behaviour is innate, not learnt by observing other birds, and displayed once they associate eggs with food and have access to pebbles.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThoulessFanshaweBertram1989_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThoulessFanshaweBertram1989-52"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> Another case of tool-use described from Bulgaria involves the use of a twig as a tool to roll up and gather strands of wool to use for lining the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStoyanovaStefanovSchmutz2010_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStoyanovaStefanovSchmutz2010-53"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Egyptian_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NeophronGould.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/NeophronGould.jpg/170px-NeophronGould.jpg" width="170" height="266" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/NeophronGould.jpg/255px-NeophronGould.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/NeophronGould.jpg/340px-NeophronGould.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NeophronGould.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Immature (behind) and adult (from <a href="/wiki/John_Gould" title="John Gould">John Gould</a>'s <i>Birds of Europe</i>)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Egyptian Vulture populations have declined in most parts of its range. In Europe and most of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, populations in 2001 were half of those from 1980. In India, the decline has been rapid with a 35% decrease each year since 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECuthbertGreenRanadeSaravanan2006_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECuthbertGreenRanadeSaravanan2006-54"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1967–70, the area around <a href="/wiki/Delhi" title="Delhi">Delhi</a> was estimated to have 12,000-15,000 of these vultures, with an average density of about 5 pairs per 10&#160;km<sup>2</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGalushin2001_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGalushin2001-55"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGalushin1975_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGalushin1975-56"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> The exact cause of the decline is not known, but has been linked with the use of the <a href="/wiki/Non-steroidal_anti-inflammatory_drug" title="Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drug">NSAID</a> <a href="/wiki/Diclofenac" title="Diclofenac">Diclofenac</a>, which has been known to cause death in <i>Gyps</i> vultures.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECuthbertGreenRanadeSaravanan2006_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECuthbertGreenRanadeSaravanan2006-54"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> In Italy, the number of breeding pairs declined from 30 in 1970 to 9 in the 1990s. Nearly all breeding failures were due to human activities.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELiberatoriPenteriani2001_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELiberatoriPenteriani2001-57"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> In Spain, which holds about 50% of the European population suggested causes of decline include poisoning by accumulation of lead,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECort.C3.A9s-AvizandaCeballosDon.C3.A1zar2009_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECort.C3.A9s-AvizandaCeballosDon.C3.A1zar2009-58"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> pesticides (especially due to large-scale use in the control of <i><a href="/wiki/Schistocerca_gregaria" title="Schistocerca gregaria" class="mw-redirect">Schistocerca gregaria</a></i> locust swarms), and electrocution.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zar_et_al._2002a_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zar_et_al._2002a-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHern.C3.A1ndezMargalida2009_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHern.C3.A1ndezMargalida2009-59"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGarc.C3.ADa-Ripoll.C3.A9sL.C3.B3pez-L.C3.B3pez2006_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGarc.C3.ADa-Ripoll.C3.A9sL.C3.B3pez-L.C3.B3pez2006-60"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> Windfarms may also pose a threat.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarreteS.C3.A1nchez-ZapataBen.C3.ADtezLob.C3.B3n2009_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarreteS.C3.A1nchez-ZapataBen.C3.ADtezLob.C3.B3n2009-61"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMargalidaBen.C3.ADtezS.C3.A1nchez-Zapata.C3.81vila2012_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMargalidaBen.C3.ADtezS.C3.A1nchez-Zapata.C3.81vila2012-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Poorly designed power transmission lines in east Africa electrocute many wintering vultures.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAngelovHashimOppel2012_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAngelovHashimOppel2012-62"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> Furthermore, studies in Spain suggest that the absorption of veterinary antibiotics suppresses the vultures' innate immunity, making them more prone to infection.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELemusBlanco2009_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELemusBlanco2009-63"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> A shortage of carrion resulting from new rules for disposal of dead animals following the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/Bovine_spongiform_encephalopathy" title="Bovine spongiform encephalopathy">Bovine Spongiform Encephalitis</a> in parts of Europe during 2000 may have also had an effect on some populations.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHidalgo_et_al._2005_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHidalgo_et_al._2005-64"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMargalidaBen.C3.ADtezS.C3.A1nchez-Zapata.C3.81vila2012_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMargalidaBen.C3.ADtezS.C3.A1nchez-Zapata.C3.81vila2012-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The population of Egyptian Vultures in the Canary Islands has been isolated from those in Europe and Africa for a significant period of time leading to genetic differentiation. The vulture population there declined by 30% in the ten years between 1987 and 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPalacios2000_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPalacios2000-65"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> The Canarian Egyptian Vulture was historically common, occurring on the islands of <a href="/wiki/La_Gomera" title="La Gomera">La Gomera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tenerife" title="Tenerife">Tenerife</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gran_Canaria" title="Gran Canaria">Gran Canaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fuerteventura" title="Fuerteventura">Fuerteventura</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lanzarote" title="Lanzarote">Lanzarote</a>. It is now restricted to Fuerteventura and Lanzarote, the two easternmost islands. The total population in 2000 was estimated at about 130 individuals, including 25–30 breeding pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zar_et_al._2002a_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDon.C3.A1zar_et_al._2002a-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPalacios2004_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPalacios2004-66"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> The island birds, owing to reduced exposure to infections and low genetic diversity, appear to be more susceptible to infections due to a weaker immune response.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGangoso_et_al._2009b_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGangoso_et_al._2009b-67"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> Island birds also appear to accumulate significant amounts of lead from scavenging on hunted animal carcasses. The long-term effect of this poison at a sublethal level is not known, though it is known to alter the mineralization of their bones.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGangoso_et_al._2009a_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGangoso_et_al._2009a-68"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> In order to provide safe and uncontaminated food for nesting birds, attempts have been made to create "vulture restaurants" where carcasses are made available. However, these interventions may also encourage other opportunist predators and scavengers to concentrate at the site and pose a threat to vultures nesting in the vicinity.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECort.C3.A9s-AvizandaCarreteSerranoDon.C3.A1zar2009_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECort.C3.A9s-AvizandaCarreteSerranoDon.C3.A1zar2009-69"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:EgyptianVultureThurston.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/EgyptianVultureThurston.jpg/220px-EgyptianVultureThurston.jpg" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/EgyptianVultureThurston.jpg/330px-EgyptianVultureThurston.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/EgyptianVultureThurston.jpg/440px-EgyptianVultureThurston.jpg 2x" /></a>

<th style="background: navajowhite; border-bottom: 1px solid peru; padding: 0.5em">Egyptian Vulture<br />

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a>, the vulture <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs" title="Egyptian hieroglyphs">hieroglyph</a> was the <a href="/wiki/Transliteration_of_Ancient_Egyptian" title="Transliteration of Ancient Egyptian">uniliteral sign</a> used for the glottal sound (/ɑː/). The Bible makes a reference to the Egyptian Vulture under the Hebrew name of <i>rachamah</i>/<i>racham</i> which has been translated into English as "gier-eagle".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKoenig1907_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKoenig1907-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECoultas1876138_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoultas1876138-70"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> The bird was held sacred to <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a> in the ancient Egyptian religion. The use of the vulture as a <a href="/wiki/Nekhbet" title="Nekhbet">symbol of royalty</a> in Egyptian culture and their protection by Pharaonic law made the species common on the streets of Egypt and gave rise to the name "Pharaoh's Chicken".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIngerson192334_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIngerson192334-71"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson189548_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson189548-72"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStratton-Porter1909182_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStratton-Porter1909182-73"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnonymous185480_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnonymous185480-74"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A southern Indian temple at <a href="/wiki/Thirukazhukundram" title="Thirukazhukundram" class="mw-redirect">Thirukalukundram</a> near <a href="/wiki/Chengalpattu" title="Chengalpattu">Chengalpattu</a> was famed for a pair of birds that reputedly visited the temple for "centuries". These birds were ceremonially fed by the temple priests and arrived before noon to feed on offerings made from rice, wheat, <a href="/wiki/Ghee" title="Ghee">ghee</a>, and sugar. Although normally punctual, the failure of the birds to turn up was attributed to the presence of "sinners" among the onlookers.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAliRipley1978_21-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAliRipley1978-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENeelakantan1977_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENeelakantan1977-75"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESiromoney1977_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESiromoney1977-76"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup> Legend has it the vultures (or "eagles") represented eight sages who were punished by <a href="/wiki/Shiva" title="Shiva">Shiva</a>, with two of them leaving in each of a series of epochs.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPope1900260_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPope1900260-77"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThurston1906252_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThurston1906252-78"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The habit of <a href="/wiki/Coprophagy" title="Coprophagy" class="mw-redirect">coprophagy</a> in Egyptian Vultures gives them the Spanish names of "churretero" and "moñiguero", which mean "dung-eater".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENegroGrandeTellaGarrido2002_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENegroGrandeTellaGarrido2002-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> British naturalists in colonial India considered them to be among the ugliest birds, and their habit of feeding on faeces was particularly despised.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDewar1906_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDewar1906-79"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
