<p>The <b>Sooty Tern</b> (<i>Onychoprion fuscatus</i>) (formerly <i>Sterna fuscata</i><sup id="cite_ref-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup>), is a <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">tern</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> (Sternidae). It is a bird of the tropical oceans, breeding on islands throughout the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equatorial</a> zone. Colloquially, it is known as the <b>Wideawake Tern</b> or just <b>wideawake</b>. This refers to the incessant calls produced by a colony of these birds, as does the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_(language)" title="Hawaiian (language)" class="mw-redirect">Hawaiian</a> name <i><b>ʻewa ʻewa</b></i> which roughly means "<a href="/wiki/Cacophony" title="Cacophony" class="mw-redirect">cacophony</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> In most of <a href="/wiki/Polynesia" title="Polynesia">Polynesia</a> its name is <i><b>manutara</b></i> or similar however – literally "tern-bird",<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> though it might be better rendered in English as "the tern" or "common tern". This refers to the fact that wherever <a href="/wiki/Polynesian_seafarer" title="Polynesian seafarer" class="mw-redirect">Polynesian seafarers</a> went on their long voyages, they would find these birds, and usually in astounding numbers.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sooty_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>This is a large tern, similar in size to the <a href="/wiki/Sandwich_Tern" title="Sandwich Tern">Sandwich Tern</a> (<i>Thalasseus sandvicensis</i>) at 33–36&#160;cm (13–14&#160;in) long with a 82–94&#160;cm (34–37&#160;in) wingspan. The wings and deeply forked tail are long, and it has dark black upperparts and white underparts. It has black legs and bill. The average life span is 32 years.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Juvenile Sooty Terns are scaly grey above and below. The Sooty Tern is unlikely to be confused with any tern apart from the similarly dark-backed but smaller <a href="/wiki/Bridled_Tern" title="Bridled Tern">Bridled Tern</a> (<i>O. anaethetus</i>). It is darker-backed than that species, and has a broader white forehead and no pale neck collar.</p>
<p>The call is a loud piercing <i>ker-wack-a-wack</i> or <i>kvaark</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sooty_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h3>
<p>The Sooty Tern has little <a href="/wiki/Interspecific" title="Interspecific">interspecific</a> variation, but it can be divided into at least two <a href="/wiki/Allopatric" title="Allopatric" class="mw-redirect">allopatric</a> <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>. Some recent authors further subdivide the <a href="/wiki/Indopacific" title="Indopacific" class="mw-redirect">Indopacific</a> population into up to 8 subspecies altogether, but much of the variation is really <a href="/wiki/Cline_(population_genetics)" title="Cline (population genetics)" class="mw-redirect">clinal</a>. The affinities of the eastern Pacific birds (including the famous <i>manutara</i> of <a href="/wiki/Easter_Island" title="Easter Island">Easter Island</a>) are most strongly contested.</p>
<p><i><b>Onychoprion fuscatus fuscatus</b></i> <small>(Linnaeus, 1766)</small> – <b>Atlantic Sooty Tern</b><br />
Underparts white. Breeds <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic" class="mw-redirect">Atlantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>.</p>
<p><i><b>Onychoprion fuscatus nubilosus</b></i> <small>(Sparrman, 1788)</small> – <b>Indopacific Sooty Tern</b><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><br />
Underparts light grey in fresh plumage, dull white in worn plumage. Breeds from <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> across <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> to at least central <a href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific" class="mw-redirect">Pacific</a>. Some authors restrict this <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxon</a> to the Indian Ocean population and use the following subspecies for the birds from <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Onychoprion fuscatus infuscatus</i> – Sunda Sooty Tern <small>(Lichtenstein, 1823)</small> – <a href="/wiki/Sunda_Islands" title="Sunda Islands">Sunda Islands</a> and vicinity</li>
<li><i>Onychoprion fuscatus oahuensis</i> – Central Pacific Sooty Tern <small>(Bloxam, 1826)</small> – <a href="/wiki/Bonin_Islands" title="Bonin Islands">Bonin Islands</a> through <a href="/wiki/Micronesia" title="Micronesia">Micronesia</a> to southern <a href="/wiki/Polynesia" title="Polynesia">Polynesia</a></li>
<li><i>Onychoprion fuscatus serrata</i> – Melanesian Sooty Tern <small>(Wagler, 1830)</small> – <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a></li>
<li><i>Onychoprion fuscatus luctuosa</i> – Juan Fernández Sooty Tern <small>(Philippi &amp; Landbeck, 1866)</small> – <a href="/wiki/Juan_Fern%C3%A1ndez_Islands" title="Juan Fernández Islands">Juan Fernández Islands</a></li>
<li><i>Onychoprion fuscatus crissalis</i> – East Pacific Sooty Tern <small>Lawrence, 1872</small><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material in the vicinity of this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s) from March 2008">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> – Eastern Pacific from <a href="/wiki/Guadalupe_Island" title="Guadalupe Island">Guadalupe Island</a> to <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_Islands" title="Galápagos Islands">Galápagos Islands</a></li>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sooty_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span></h2>

<p>Sooty Terns breed in colonies on rocky or <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">coral</a> islands.<sup id="cite_ref-streets1877_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-streets1877-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> It nests in a ground scrape or hole and lays one to three eggs. It feeds by picking <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> from the surface in marine environments, often in large flocks, and rarely comes to land except to breed, and can stay out to sea (either soaring or floating on the water) for 3 to 10 years.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This bird is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and dispersive, wintering more widely through the tropical oceans. It has very marine habits compared to most terns; Sooty Terns are generally found inland only after severe <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storms</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Field_Museum" title="Field Museum" class="mw-redirect">Field Museum</a>, for example, has a male specimen which was found exhausted on August 2, 1933 on the slopes of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Cameroon" title="Mount Cameroon">Mount Cameroon</a> above <a href="/wiki/Buea" title="Buea">Buea</a>, about 1000 m (3,500&#160;ft) <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" title="Above mean sea level">ASL</a>, after foul weather had hit the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Guinea" title="Gulf of Guinea">Gulf of Guinea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> This species is a rare vagrant to western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, although a bird was present at <a href="/wiki/Cemlyn_Bay_and_lagoon" title="Cemlyn Bay and lagoon">Cemlyn Bay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> for 11 days in July 2005.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from September 2007">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>It is also not normally found on the <a href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific" class="mw-redirect">Pacific</a> coasts of the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> due to its <a href="/wiki/Pelagic" title="Pelagic" class="mw-redirect">pelagic</a> habits. At <a href="/wiki/Baja_California" title="Baja California">Baja California</a>, where several nesting locations are offshore, it can be seen more frequently, whereas for example only two individuals have ever been recorded on the coast of <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a> - one <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">ring</a> recovered in 1972, and a bird photographed on October 10, 2001 at <a href="/wiki/Lake_Olomega" title="Lake Olomega">Lake Olomega</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material in the vicinity of this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s) from September 2007">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> which was probably blown there by a storm&#160;<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup>. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane" class="mw-redirect">Hurricanes</a> can also devastate small breeding colonies, as has been surmised for example for the Sooty Tern nesting sites on <a href="/wiki/Cay" title="Cay">cays</a> off the <a href="/wiki/San_Andr%C3%A9s_and_Providencia#Island_of_San_Andr.C3.A9s.2C_Colombia" title="San Andrés and Providencia" class="mw-redirect">San Andrés Islands</a> of <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>An exceptionally common bird, the Sooty Tern is not considered threatened by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN" class="mw-redirect">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sooty_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Role in Easter Island culture">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_Easter_Island_culture">Role in Easter Island culture</span></h2>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/Easter_Island" title="Easter Island">Easter Island</a>, this species and the <a href="/wiki/Spectacled_Tern" title="Spectacled Tern">Spectacled Tern</a> (<i>O. lunatus</i>) are collectively known as <i>manutara</i>. The <i>manutara</i> played an important role in the <i><a href="/wiki/Tangata_manu" title="Tangata manu">tangata manu</a></i> ("birdman") ritual: whichever <i>hopu</i> (champion) could retrieve the first <i>manutara</i> egg from <a href="/wiki/Motu_Nui" title="Motu Nui">Motu Nui</a> islet would become that year's <i>tangata manu</i>; his <a href="/wiki/Clan" title="Clan">clan</a> would receive prime access to resources, especially seabird eggs.</p>
