<p>The <b>sooty tern</b> (<i>Onychoprion fuscatus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Laridae" title="Laridae">Laridae</a>. It is a bird of the tropical oceans which sleeps on the wing, returning to land only for breeding- on islands throughout the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equatorial</a> zone.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sooty_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The sooty tern was described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1766 as <i>Sterna fuscata</i>, bearing this name for many years until the genus <i>Sterna</i> was split up. It is now known as <i>Onychoprion fuscatus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The genus name is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">ancient Greek</a> <i>onux</i>, "claw" or "nail", and <i>prion</i>, "saw". The specific <i>fuscatus</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "dark".<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Colloquially, it is known as the wideawake tern or just wideawake. This refers to the incessant calls produced by a colony of these birds, as does the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_(language)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hawaiian (language)">Hawaiian</a> name <i>ʻewa ʻewa</i> which roughly means "<a href="/wiki/Cacophony" class="mw-redirect" title="Cacophony">cacophony</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In most of <a href="/wiki/Polynesia" title="Polynesia">Polynesia</a> its name is <i>manutara</i> or similar &#8211; literally "tern-bird",<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> though it might be better rendered in English as "the tern" or "common tern". This refers to the fact that wherever <a href="/wiki/Polynesian_seafarer" class="mw-redirect" title="Polynesian seafarer">Polynesian seafarers</a> went on their long voyages, they usually would find these birds in astounding numbers. It is also known as <i>kaveka</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Marquesas_Islands" title="Marquesas Islands">Marquesas Islands</a>, where dishes using its eggs are a delicacy.<sup id="cite_ref-BlondBrash2006_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BlondBrash2006-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The sooty tern has little <a href="/wiki/Interspecific" class="mw-redirect" title="Interspecific">interspecific</a> variation, but it can be divided into at least two <a href="/wiki/Allopatric" class="mw-redirect" title="Allopatric">allopatric</a> <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>. Some recent authors further subdivide the <a href="/wiki/Indopacific" class="mw-redirect" title="Indopacific">Indopacific</a> population into up to 8 subspecies altogether, but much of the variation is really <a href="/wiki/Cline_(population_genetics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cline (population genetics)">clinal</a>. The affinities of eastern Pacific birds (including the famous <i>manutara</i> of <a href="/wiki/Easter_Island" title="Easter Island">Easter Island</a>) are most strongly contested.
</p><p><i>Onychoprion fuscatus fuscatus</i> <small>(Linnaeus, 1766)</small> &#8211; Atlantic sooty tern<br />
Underparts white. Breeds <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>.
</p><p><i>Onychoprion fuscatus nubilosus</i> <small>(Sparrman, 1788)</small> &#8211; Indopacific sooty tern<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><br />
Underparts light grey in fresh plumage, dull white in worn plumage. Breeds from <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> across <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> to at least central <a href="/wiki/Pacific" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific">Pacific</a>. Some authors restrict this <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxon</a> to the Indian Ocean population and use the following subspecies for the birds from <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Onychoprion fuscatus infuscatus</i> &#8211; Sunda sooty tern <small>(Lichtenstein, 1823)</small> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Sunda_Islands" title="Sunda Islands">Sunda Islands</a> and vicinity</li>
<li><i>Onychoprion fuscatus oahuensis</i> &#8211; Central Pacific sooty tern <small>(Bloxam, 1826)</small> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Bonin_Islands" title="Bonin Islands">Bonin Islands</a> through <a href="/wiki/Micronesia" title="Micronesia">Micronesia</a> to southern <a href="/wiki/Polynesia" title="Polynesia">Polynesia</a></li>
<li><i>Onychoprion fuscatus serrata</i> &#8211; Melanesian sooty tern <small>(Wagler, 1830)</small> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a></li>
<li><i>Onychoprion fuscatus luctuosa</i> &#8211; Juan Fernández sooty tern <small>(Philippi &amp; Landbeck, 1866)</small> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Juan_Fern%C3%A1ndez_Islands" title="Juan Fernández Islands">Juan Fernández Islands</a></li>
<li><i>Onychoprion fuscatus crissalis</i> &#8211; East Pacific sooty tern <small>Lawrence, 1872.</small> <sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> &#8211; Eastern Pacific from <a href="/wiki/Guadalupe_Island" title="Guadalupe Island">Guadalupe Island</a> to <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_Islands" title="Galápagos Islands">Galápagos Islands</a></li>
<li><i>Onychoprion fuscatus kermadeci</i> &#8211; Kermadec sooty tern <small>Mathews, 1916.</small> <sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Kermadec_Islands" title="Kermadec Islands">Kermadec Islands</a></li>
<li><i>Onychoprion fuscatus somaliensis</i> &#8211; Somali sooty tern &#8211; Maydh Island (<a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Aden" title="Gulf of Aden">Gulf of Aden</a>)</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sooty_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>This is a large tern, similar in size to the <a href="/wiki/Sandwich_tern" title="Sandwich tern">Sandwich tern</a> (<i>Thalasseus sandvicensis</i>) at 33–36&#160;cm (13–14&#160;in) long with an 82–94&#160;cm (32.5–37&#160;in) wingspan. The wings and deeply forked tail are long, and it has dark black upperparts and white underparts. It has black legs and bill. The average life span is 32 years.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Juvenile sooty terns are scaly grey above and below. The sooty tern is unlikely to be confused with any tern apart from the similarly dark-backed but smaller <a href="/wiki/Bridled_tern" title="Bridled tern">bridled tern</a> (<i>O. anaethetus</i>). It is darker-backed than that species, and has a broader white forehead and no pale neck collar.
</p><p>The call is a loud piercing <i>ker-wack-a-wack</i> or <i>kvaark</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sooty_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sooty_tern_on_nest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Sooty_tern_on_nest.jpg/220px-Sooty_tern_on_nest.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="224" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Sooty_tern_on_nest.jpg/330px-Sooty_tern_on_nest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Sooty_tern_on_nest.jpg/440px-Sooty_tern_on_nest.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1127" data-file-height="1147" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sooty_tern_on_nest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult <i>O. f. nubilosus</i> with egg in "nest", <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">Seychelles</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bird_flocks_Bird_Island_Seychelles.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Bird_flocks_Bird_Island_Seychelles.jpg/220px-Bird_flocks_Bird_Island_Seychelles.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="282" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Bird_flocks_Bird_Island_Seychelles.jpg/330px-Bird_flocks_Bird_Island_Seychelles.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Bird_flocks_Bird_Island_Seychelles.jpg/440px-Bird_flocks_Bird_Island_Seychelles.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2179" data-file-height="2792" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bird_flocks_Bird_Island_Seychelles.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>O. f. nubilosus</i> at <a href="/wiki/Bird_Island,_Seychelles" title="Bird Island, Seychelles">Bird Island, Seychelles</a>, home to more than a million of sooty terns at its peak</div></div></div>
<p>Sooty terns breed in colonies on rocky or <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">coral</a> islands.<sup id="cite_ref-streets1877_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-streets1877-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It nests in a ground scrape or hole and lays one to three eggs. It feeds by picking <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> from the surface in marine environments, often in large flocks, and rarely comes to land except to breed, and can stay out to sea for 3 to 10 years.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Due to the lack oil in its feathers, it cannot float, and spends that entire time on the wing.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This bird is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biological_dispersal" title="Biological dispersal">dispersive</a>, wintering more widely through the tropical oceans. It has very marine habits compared to most terns; sooty terns are generally found inland only after severe <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storms</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Field_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="Field Museum">Field Museum</a>, for example, has a male specimen which was found exhausted on August 2, 1933 on the slopes of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Cameroon" title="Mount Cameroon">Mount Cameroon</a> above <a href="/wiki/Buea" title="Buea">Buea</a>, about 1,000&#160;m (3,300&#160;ft) <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Above mean sea level">ASL</a>, after foul weather had hit the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Guinea" title="Gulf of Guinea">Gulf of Guinea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> This species is a rare vagrant to western Europe, although a bird was present at <a href="/wiki/Cemlyn_Bay_and_lagoon" title="Cemlyn Bay and lagoon">Cemlyn Bay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> for 11 days in July 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is also not normally found on the <a href="/wiki/Pacific" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific">Pacific</a> coasts of the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> due to its <a href="/wiki/Pelagic" class="mw-redirect" title="Pelagic">pelagic</a> habits. At <a href="/wiki/Baja_California" title="Baja California">Baja California</a>, where several nesting locations are offshore, it can be seen more frequently, whereas for example only two individuals have ever been recorded on the coast of <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a> - one <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">ring</a> recovered in 1972, and a bird photographed on October 10, 2001 at <a href="/wiki/Lake_Olomega" title="Lake Olomega">Lake Olomega</a><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> which was probably blown there by a storm&#160;.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane">Hurricanes</a> can also devastate small breeding colonies, as has been surmised for example for the sooty tern nesting sites on <a href="/wiki/Cay" title="Cay">cays</a> off the <a href="/wiki/San_Andr%C3%A9s_and_Providencia#Island_of_San_Andr.C3.A9s.2C_Colombia" class="mw-redirect" title="San Andrés and Providencia">San Andrés Islands</a> of <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An exceptionally common bird, the sooty tern is not considered threatened by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_Easter_Island_culture">Role in Easter Island culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sooty_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Role in Easter Island culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/Easter_Island" title="Easter Island">Easter Island</a>, this species and the <a href="/wiki/Spectacled_tern" title="Spectacled tern">spectacled tern</a> (<i>O. lunatus</i>) are collectively known as <i>manutara</i>. The <i>manutara</i> played an important role in the <i><a href="/wiki/Tangata_manu" title="Tangata manu">tangata manu</a></i> ("birdman") ritual: whichever <i>hopu</i> (champion) could retrieve the first <i>manutara</i> egg from <a href="/wiki/Motu_Nui" title="Motu Nui">Motu Nui</a> islet would become that year's <i>tangata manu</i>; his <a href="/wiki/Clan" title="Clan">clan</a> would receive prime access to resources, especially seabird eggs.
</p>
