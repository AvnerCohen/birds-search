<p>The <b>band-rumped storm petrel</b>, <b>Madeiran storm petrel</b>, or <b>Harcourt's storm petrel</b> (<i>Oceanodroma castro</i>) is of the <a href="/wiki/Storm_petrel" title="Storm petrel">storm petrel</a> family <a href="/wiki/Hydrobatidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrobatidae">Hydrobatidae</a>.
</p><p>The band-rumped storm petrel is 19–21&#160;cm in length with a 43–46&#160;cm wingspan, and weighs 44–49&#160;g. It is mainly brownish black with an extensive white rump. Similar to <a href="/wiki/Leach%27s_storm_petrel" title="Leach&#39;s storm petrel">Leach's storm petrel</a> with the forked tail, long wings, but Leach's has a more deeply forked tail, a differently shaped (V-shaped or triangular) white rump, and a '<a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">tern</a>-like' flight, whereas the band-rumped storm-petrel has a more '<a href="/wiki/Shearwater" title="Shearwater">shearwater</a>-like' flight.
</p><p>The species breeds on islands in the warmer parts of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Oceans</a>. These include <a href="/w/index.php?title=Farilh%C3%A3o_Grande&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Farilhão Grande (page does not exist)">Farilhão Grande</a> (a few tens of kilometres off mainland Portugal), the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> in the Atlantic, and the <a href="/wiki/Galapagos_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Galapagos Islands">Galapagos Islands</a> in the Pacific. In 2018, the species was reported to have also started breeding on the <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Loa" title="Mauna Loa">Mauna Loa</a> volcano on the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Birds nest in colonies close to the sea in rock crevices and females lay a single white egg per breeding attempt.  The band-rumped storm petrel spends the non-breeding period at sea. Individuals feed by picking up prey items (<a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a> and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">carrion</a>) from the water surface. A study aiming to determine the diving abilities of this species <sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> was actually conducted on the 'warm season' population from the Azores, which was later recognized as a distinct species (see below).
</p><p>The band-rumped storm petrel is strictly nocturnal at its breeding sites to avoid predation by <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a> and diurnal raptors such as peregrines, and will even avoid coming to land on clear moonlit nights. Like most petrels, its walking ability is limited to a short shuffle from/to the burrow.
</p><p>Recent discoveries of 'cold season' and 'warm season' populations, which use the same nest sites at different times of year, and also differ in terms of <a href="/wiki/Animal_communication" title="Animal communication">vocalisations</a> and moulting period, may hint at the existence of two 'cryptic species' within the currently understood limits of the species. After population genetics analyses of mtDNA the warm season population in the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> was recognized as a separate species, <a href="/wiki/Monteiro%27s_storm_petrel" title="Monteiro&#39;s storm petrel">Monteiro's storm petrel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IBIS_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IBIS-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
