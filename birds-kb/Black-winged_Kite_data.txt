<p>The <b>Black-winged Kite</b> (<i>Elanus caeruleus</i>) is a small diurnal <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a> best known for its habit of hovering over open grasslands in the manner of the much-smaller <a href="/wiki/Kestrel" title="Kestrel">kestrels</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasian</a> and <a href="/wiki/African" title="African" class="mw-redirect">African</a> species was sometimes combined with the <a href="/wiki/Australian" title="Australian" class="mw-redirect">Australian</a> <a href="/wiki/Black-shouldered_Kite" title="Black-shouldered Kite">Black-shouldered Kite</a> (<i>Elanus axillaris</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_Kite" title="White-tailed Kite">White-tailed Kite</a> (<i>Elanus leucurus</i>) of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> which together form a <a href="/wiki/Superspecies" title="Superspecies">superspecies</a>. This kite is distinctive, with long-wings, white, grey and black plumage and owl like forward-facing eyes with red irises. Although mainly seen on the plains, they are sometimes seen on grassy slopes of hills in the higher elevation regions of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. They are not migratory, but make short-distance movements in response to weather.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-winged_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>This long-winged raptor is predominantly grey or white with black shoulder patches, wing tips and eye stripe. The long <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon-like</a> wings extend beyond the tail when the bird is perched. In flight, the short and square tail is visible and it is not forked as in the typical kites of the genus <i>Milvus</i>. When perched, often on roadside wires, it often adjusts its wings and jerks its tail up and down as if to balance itself. The sexes are alike in plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Their large forward-facing eyes and velvety plumage are characters that are shared with owls and the genus itself has been considered as a <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogeny)" title="Basal (phylogeny)" class="mw-redirect">basal</a> group within the Accipitridae.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-winged_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Black-shouldered_Kite_(Elanus_caeruleus)_in_Hyderabad_W_IMG_4418.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Black-shouldered_Kite_%28Elanus_caeruleus%29_in_Hyderabad_W_IMG_4418.jpg/250px-Black-shouldered_Kite_%28Elanus_caeruleus%29_in_Hyderabad_W_IMG_4418.jpg" width="250" height="187" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Black-shouldered_Kite_%28Elanus_caeruleus%29_in_Hyderabad_W_IMG_4418.jpg/375px-Black-shouldered_Kite_%28Elanus_caeruleus%29_in_Hyderabad_W_IMG_4418.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Black-shouldered_Kite_%28Elanus_caeruleus%29_in_Hyderabad_W_IMG_4418.jpg/500px-Black-shouldered_Kite_%28Elanus_caeruleus%29_in_Hyderabad_W_IMG_4418.jpg 2x" /></a>

<p>The Black-winged Kite is a species primarily of open land and semi-deserts in sub-<a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Saharan</a> <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and tropical <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, but it has a foothold within <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>. The species range appears to be expanding in southern Europe and possible in West Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Several geographic populations have been named as subspecies and these include the nominate subspecies which occurs in Spain, Africa and <a href="/wiki/Arabia" title="Arabia" class="mw-redirect">Arabia</a>. The subspecies <i>vociferus</i> is found east of this range across South Asia and into Southeast Asia. Along <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a>, <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> subspecies <i>hypoleucus</i> (sometimes considered a full species) is found while <i>wahgiensis</i> is restricted to New Guinea. Subspecies <i>sumatranus</i> is not always recognized. The <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_Kite" title="White-tailed Kite">White-tailed Kite</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Black-shouldered_Kite" title="Black-shouldered Kite">Black-shouldered Kite</a> were formerly included with this species but have since been treated as separate species.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Although found mainly on the plains they have been seen at higher altitudes in Sikkim (3650m),<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> the Nilgiris (Doddabetta, 2670m)<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> and Nagaland (2020m).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>They are said to be winter visitors in some parts of their range such as the <a href="/wiki/Western_Ghats" title="Western Ghats">Western Ghats</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-winged_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Elanus_caeruleus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Elanus_caeruleus.jpg/220px-Elanus_caeruleus.jpg" width="220" height="229" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Elanus_caeruleus.jpg/330px-Elanus_caeruleus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Elanus_caeruleus.jpg/440px-Elanus_caeruleus.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Elanus_caeruleus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Immature from <a href="/wiki/Etosha_National_Park" title="Etosha National Park">Etosha National Park</a>, Namibia</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Black-winged Kite breeds at different times of the year across its range. Although nesting has been noted throughout the year in India, they appear not to breed in April and May. Courtship is noisy and involves chases and once the pair is formed they copulate frequently.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a> is a loose platform of twigs in which 3 or 4 eggs are laid. The female spends more effort in the construction of the nest than the male. The eggs are pale creamy with spots of deep red. Both parents incubate but when the chicks hatch, the male spends more time on foraging for food.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Females initially feed the young, sometimes hunting close to the nest but will also receive food from the male. After fledging the young birds continue to be dependent for food on the male parent for about 80 days, initially transferring food at perch and later in the air.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The prey include grasshoppers, crickets and other large insects, lizards and rodents. Injured birds, small snakes and frogs have also been recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The slow hunting flight is like a harrier, but it will hover like a <a href="/wiki/Kestrel_(bird)" title="Kestrel (bird)" class="mw-redirect">Kestrel</a>. It has on rare occasions been known to hunt prey in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Favourite perches are used for hunting and for feeding but large prey may sometimes be handled on the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> In southern Africa, they appear to favour roadside verges for foraging and are sometimes killed by collisions with <a href="/wiki/Roadkill" title="Roadkill">vehicles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>These birds roost communally with groups of 15 to 35 (larger numbers in Europe<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup>) converging at a large leafy tree.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> They are extremely silent and the calls recorded include a high-pitched squeal or a soft whistle.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> They call a lot mainly during the breeding season.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A species of <a href="/wiki/Nematode" title="Nematode">nematode</a>, <i>Physaloptera acuticauda</i>, has been recorded as a parasite of the species in South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
