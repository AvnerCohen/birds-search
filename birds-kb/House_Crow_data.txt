<p>The <b>House Crow</b> (<i>Corvus splendens</i>), also known as the <b>Colombo Crow</b> is a common <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Corvidae" title="Corvidae">Crow family</a> that is of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> origin but now found in many parts of the world, where they arrived assisted by shipping. It is between the <a href="/wiki/Jackdaw" title="Jackdaw" class="mw-redirect">Jackdaw</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Carrion_Crow" title="Carrion Crow">Carrion Crow</a> in size (40&#160;cm in length) but is relatively slimmer than either. The forehead, crown, throat and upper breast are a richly glossed black, whilst the neck and breast are a lighter grey-brown in colour. The wings, tail and legs are black. There are regional variations in the thickness of the bill and the depth of colour in areas of the plumage.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=House_Crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>In the dry parts of South Asia and Iran the subspecies <i>C. s. zugmayeri</i> is found and this has a very pale neck collar. The nominate race is found in India, Nepal and Bangladesh and has a grey neck collar. In southern India, the Maldives (sometimes separated as <i>maledivicus</i>) and Sri Lanka, the subspecies <i>C. s. protegatus</i> is darker grey. The darkest form however is the Myanmar form <i>C. s. insolens</i> and lacks the grey collar.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=House_Crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>It has a widespread distribution in southern Asia, being native to <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laccadive_Islands" title="Laccadive Islands" class="mw-redirect">Laccadive Islands</a>, South West <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> and coastal southern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. It has been introduced to East <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> around <a href="/wiki/Zanzibar" title="Zanzibar">Zanzibar</a> (around 1897<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup>) and <a href="/wiki/Port_Sudan" title="Port Sudan">Port Sudan</a>, and arrived in Australia via ship but has up to now been exterminated. Recently it has made its arrival in Europe and has been breeding in the <a href="/wiki/Hook_of_Holland" title="Hook of Holland">Hook of Holland</a> since 1998. An individual of this species has been present in Cork Harbour on the south coast of Ireland since early September 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the New World, a small population of House Crows is established in the area around <a href="/wiki/St._Petersburg,_Florida" title="St. Petersburg, Florida">St. Petersburg, Florida</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is associated with human settlements in all of its range, from small villages to large cities. In Singapore there was a density of 190 birds/km<sup>2</sup> in 2001 with efforts to suppress the population in planning.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Due to a human population explosion in the areas it inhabits, this species has also proportionately multiplied. Being an omnivorous scavenger has enabled it to thrive in such circumstances.</p>
<p>The invasive potential for the species is great all over the tropics. This species is able to make use of resources with great flexibility and appears to be associated with humans, and no populations are known to exist independently of humans.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=House_Crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span></h3>
<p>It feeds largely on refuse around human habitations, small <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a> and other animals such as <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and other small invertebrates, eggs, nestlings, grain and fruits. Crows have also been observed swooping down from the air and snatching baby squirrels. Most food is taken from the ground, but also from trees as opportunity arises. It is a highly opportunistic bird and given its omnivorous diet, it can survive on nearly anything that is edible. These birds can be seen near marketplaces and garbage dumps, foraging for scraps.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=House_Crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nesting">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Nesting">Nesting</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:House_Crow_feeding_chicks.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/House_Crow_feeding_chicks.jpg/220px-House_Crow_feeding_chicks.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/House_Crow_feeding_chicks.jpg/330px-House_Crow_feeding_chicks.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/House_Crow_feeding_chicks.jpg/440px-House_Crow_feeding_chicks.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:House_Crow_feeding_chicks.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
House Crow feeding chicks in Chennai, India</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At least some trees in the local environment seem to be necessary for its successful breeding although they occasionally nest on telephone towers.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> It lays 3-6 eggs in a typical stick nest, and occasionally there are several nests in the same tree. In <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> they are <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasitism" title="Brood parasitism" class="mw-redirect">parasitized</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Asian_Koel" title="Asian Koel">Asian Koel</a>. Peak breeding in India as well as Peninsular Malaysia was from April to July. Large trees with big crowns are preferred for nesting.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=House_Crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Roosting">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Roosting">Roosting</span></h3>
<p>House Crows roost communally near human habitations and often over busy streets. A study in Singapore found that the preferred roost sites were in well-lit areas with a lot of human activity, close to food sources and in tall trees with dense crowns that were separated from other trees. The roost sites were often enclosed by tall buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=House_Crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span></h3>
<p>The voice is a harsh <i>kaaa-kaaa</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=House_Crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Relationship to humans">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_humans">Relationship to humans</span></h2>
<p>It is suspected that paramyxoviruses, such as PMV 1 that causes of Newcastle disease<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> may be spread by <i>Corvus splendens</i>. Outbreaks in India of Newcastle disease were often preceded by mortality in crows.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> They have also been found to carry <i>Cryptococcus neoformans</i> which can cause <a href="/wiki/Cryptococcosis" title="Cryptococcosis">cryptococcosis</a> in humans.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Crows in Tanzania curiously showed an absence of <a href="/wiki/Haematozoa" title="Haematozoa">blood parasites</a> although some species such as <i>Trypanosoma corvi</i> have been first described from this species.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
