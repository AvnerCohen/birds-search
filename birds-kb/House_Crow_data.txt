<p>The <b>house crow</b> (<i>Corvus splendens</i>), also known as the <b>Indian, greynecked, Ceylon</b> or <b>Colombo crow</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> is a common <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Corvidae" title="Corvidae">crow family</a> that is of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> origin but now found in many parts of the world, where they arrived assisted by shipping. It is between the <a href="/wiki/Jackdaw" class="mw-redirect" title="Jackdaw">jackdaw</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Carrion_crow" title="Carrion crow">carrion crow</a> in size (40&#160;cm (16&#160;in) in length) but is slimmer than either. The forehead, crown, throat and upper breast are a richly glossed black, whilst the neck and breast are a lighter grey-brown in colour. The wings, tail and legs are black. There are regional variations in the thickness of the bill and the depth of colour in areas of the plumage.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The nominate race <i>C. s. splendens</i> is found in Pakistan, India, Nepal and Bangladesh and has a grey neck collar. The subspecies <i>C. s. zugmayeri</i> is found in the dry parts of South Asia and Iran and has a very pale neck collar. The subspecies <i>C. s. protegatus</i> is found in southern India, the Maldives (sometimes separated as <i>maledivicus</i>) and Sri Lanka and is darker grey. <i>C. s. insolens</i>, found in <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>, is the darkest form and lacks the grey collar.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It has a widespread distribution in southern Asia, being native to <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laccadive_Islands" title="Laccadive Islands">Laccadive Islands</a>, South West <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> and coastal southern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. It was introduced to <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a> around <a href="/wiki/Zanzibar" title="Zanzibar">Zanzibar</a> (about 1897)<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Port_Sudan" title="Port Sudan">Port Sudan</a>. It arrived in Australia via ship but has up to now been exterminated. Recently, it has made its arrival in Europe and has been breeding in the Dutch harbour town <a href="/wiki/Hook_of_Holland" title="Hook of Holland">Hook of Holland</a> since 1998.
</p><p>A population between 200 and 400 birds has been present in Sham Shui Po, <a href="/wiki/New_Kowloon" title="New Kowloon">New Kowloon</a>, Hong Kong, in particular Lai Kok Estate and <a href="/wiki/Sham_Shui_Po_Park" title="Sham Shui Po Park">Sham Shui Po Park</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Kowloon_Tsai_Park" title="Kowloon Tsai Park">Kowloon Tsai Park</a> in Kowloon Tsai.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> An individual has been present in Cork Harbour on the south coast of Ireland since early September 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the New World, a small population of house crows is established in the area around <a href="/wiki/St._Petersburg,_Florida" title="St. Petersburg, Florida">St. Petersburg, Florida</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is associated with human settlements throughout its range, from small villages to large cities. In Singapore, there was a density of 190 birds/km<sup>2</sup> in 2001 with efforts to suppress the population in planning.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to a human population explosion in the areas it inhabits, this species has also proportionately multiplied. Being an omnivorous scavenger has enabled it to thrive in such circumstances.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Invasive_species">Invasive species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Invasive species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The invasive potential for the species is great all over the tropics. This species is able to make use of resources with great flexibility and appears to be associated with humans, and no populations are known to exist independently of humans. Based on <a href="/wiki/Species_distribution_modelling" title="Species distribution modelling">niche-modelling</a> it is estimated that it can also persist in some coastal areas in southern and northwestern Europe <sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>. In 2016, house crow was added to the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://ec.europa.eu/environment/nature/invasivealien/list/index_en.htm">List of Invasive Alien Species of Union concern</a>. Breeding population of this species is reported from Netherlands (Hoek van Holland), while individuals were seen in Belgium, Hungary, Poland and Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>House crows feed largely on refuse around human habitations, small <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and other animals such as <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and other small invertebrates, eggs, nestlings, grain and fruits. House crows have also been observed swooping down from the air and snatching baby squirrels. Most food is taken from the ground, but also from trees as opportunity arises. They are highly opportunistic birds and given their omnivorous diet, they can survive on nearly anything that is edible. These birds can be seen near marketplaces and garbage dumps, foraging for scraps. They have also been observed to eat sand after feeding on carcasses.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nesting">Nesting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nesting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At least some trees in the local environment seem to be necessary for successful breeding although house crows occasionally nest on telephone towers.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It lays 3–5 eggs in a typical stick nest, and occasionally there are several nests in the same tree. In <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> they are <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasitism" class="mw-redirect" title="Brood parasitism">parasitized</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Asian_koel" title="Asian koel">Asian koel</a>. Peak breeding in India as well as Peninsular Malaysia is from April to July. Large trees with big crowns are preferred for nesting.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roosting">Roosting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Roosting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>House crows roost communally near human habitations and often over busy streets. A study in Singapore found that the preferred roost sites were in well-lit areas with a lot of human activity, close to food sources and in tall trees with dense crowns that were separated from other trees. The roost sites were often enclosed by tall buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Before flying into roost trees, crows make pre-roosting aggregations perched on TV antennas, roof tops, wayside trees, open fields, and feed or preen during this time.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The voice is a harsh <i>kaaw-kaaw</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_humans">Relationship to humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Relationship to humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is suspected that <a href="/wiki/Paramyxoviridae" title="Paramyxoviridae">paramyxoviruses</a>, such as PMV 1 that causes of <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Newcastle disease">Newcastle disease</a><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> may be spread by <i>Corvus splendens</i>. Outbreaks of Newcastle disease in India were often preceded by mortality in crows.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> They have also been found to carry <i>Cryptococcus neoformans</i>, which can cause <a href="/wiki/Cryptococcosis" title="Cryptococcosis">cryptococcosis</a> in humans.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>House crows in <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a> curiously showed an absence of <a href="/wiki/Haematozoa" title="Haematozoa">blood parasites</a>, although some species such as <i>Trypanosoma corvi</i> have been first described from this species.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Pathologist <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Richards_Lewis" title="Timothy Richards Lewis">T.R. Lewis</a> expressed surprise at the numbers of haematozoa present in the blood of house crows from Calcutta.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
