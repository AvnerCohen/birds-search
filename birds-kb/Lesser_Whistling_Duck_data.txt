<p>The <b> lesser whistling duck</b> (<i>Dendrocygna javanica</i>), also known as <b>Indian whistling duck</b> or <b>lesser whistling teal</b>, is a species of <a href="/wiki/Whistling_duck" title="Whistling duck">whistling duck</a> that breeds in the Indian subcontinent and Southeast Asia. They are nocturnal feeders that during the day may be found in flocks around lakes and wet <a href="/wiki/Paddy_field" title="Paddy field">paddy fields</a>. They can perch on trees and sometimes build their nest in the hollow of a tree. This brown and long-necked duck has broad wings that are visible in flight and produces a loud two-note wheezy call. It has a chestnut rump, differentiating it from its larger relative, the <a href="/wiki/Fulvous_whistling_duck" title="Fulvous whistling duck">fulvous whistling duck</a>, which has a creamy white rump.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_whistling_duck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Migratory_Birds_at_Sukhna_Lake,Chandigarh,India.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Migratory_Birds_at_Sukhna_Lake%2CChandigarh%2CIndia.JPG/220px-Migratory_Birds_at_Sukhna_Lake%2CChandigarh%2CIndia.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Migratory_Birds_at_Sukhna_Lake%2CChandigarh%2CIndia.JPG/330px-Migratory_Birds_at_Sukhna_Lake%2CChandigarh%2CIndia.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Migratory_Birds_at_Sukhna_Lake%2CChandigarh%2CIndia.JPG/440px-Migratory_Birds_at_Sukhna_Lake%2CChandigarh%2CIndia.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4608" data-file-height="3456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Migratory_Birds_at_Sukhna_Lake,Chandigarh,India.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The chicks are patterned in black and white</div></div></div>
<p>This chestnut brown duck is confusable only with the <a href="/wiki/Fulvous_whistling_duck" title="Fulvous whistling duck">fulvous whistling duck</a> (<i>D. bicolor</i>) but has chestnut upper-tail <a href="/wiki/Covert_feather" title="Covert feather">coverts</a> unlike the creamy white in the latter. The ring around the eye is orange to yellow.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> When flying straight, their head is held below the level of the body as in other <i>Dendrocygna</i> species. The crown appears dark and the sexes are alike in <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>. They fly slowly but with rapid wing-flapping and usually produce a repetitive wheezy <i>seasick</i> call as they circle overhead. They are very <a href="/wiki/Nocturnal" class="mw-redirect" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a> and often rest during the day. The outermost primary feather has the inner vane modified. They produce very prominent whistling sound while flying.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_whistling_duck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DendrocygnaFeather.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/DendrocygnaFeather.svg/220px-DendrocygnaFeather.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="49" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/DendrocygnaFeather.svg/330px-DendrocygnaFeather.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/DendrocygnaFeather.svg/440px-DendrocygnaFeather.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="112" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DendrocygnaFeather.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Shape of the outermost primary, claimed to produce a whistle in flight</div></div></div>
<p>This is a largely resident species distributed widely across lowland <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> and Southeast Asia. The species also occur on islands in the region including the <a href="/wiki/Andaman_Islands" title="Andaman Islands">Andamans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicobar_Islands" title="Nicobar Islands">Nicobars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> They sometimes make local movements in response to weather and changes in water availability and the more northern birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">winter</a> further south. They are found in freshwater wetlands with good vegetation cover and often rest during the day on the banks or even on the open sea in coastal areas.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Downy chicks are black with a white eyebrow and white patches on the back of the head, the wing, lower back and rump.<sup id="cite_ref-fbi2_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fbi2-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Albinism_in_biology" class="mw-redirect" title="Albinism in biology">Albino</a> individuals have been seen in the wild.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:White-bellied_Sea_eagle_captured_Lesser_Whistling_Duck_in_Sri_Lanka.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/White-bellied_Sea_eagle_captured_Lesser_Whistling_Duck_in_Sri_Lanka.jpg/220px-White-bellied_Sea_eagle_captured_Lesser_Whistling_Duck_in_Sri_Lanka.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/White-bellied_Sea_eagle_captured_Lesser_Whistling_Duck_in_Sri_Lanka.jpg/330px-White-bellied_Sea_eagle_captured_Lesser_Whistling_Duck_in_Sri_Lanka.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/White-bellied_Sea_eagle_captured_Lesser_Whistling_Duck_in_Sri_Lanka.jpg/440px-White-bellied_Sea_eagle_captured_Lesser_Whistling_Duck_in_Sri_Lanka.jpg 2x" data-file-width="698" data-file-height="472" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:White-bellied_Sea_eagle_captured_Lesser_Whistling_Duck_in_Sri_Lanka.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/White-bellied_sea_eagle" title="White-bellied sea eagle">White-bellied sea eagle</a> (<i>Haliaeetus leucogaster</i>) with captured duck</div></div></div>
<p>Large numbers are sometimes found in urban wetlands such as in <a href="/wiki/Kolkata" title="Kolkata">Kolkata</a> and <a href="/wiki/Goa" title="Goa">Goa</a>, particularly during winter.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Alipore_Zoological_Gardens" class="mw-redirect" title="Alipore Zoological Gardens">Alipore Zoological Gardens</a>, captive individuals were introduced in the 1930s and wild birds joined this nucleus subsequently.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>With a wide distribution range between 1 and 10 million km², they are considered to have a secure global population of between two and twenty million individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> They are not threatened by hunting as they are not considered good to eat.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Hunters in <a href="/wiki/Assam" title="Assam">Assam</a> however have been known to raise the ducklings to serve as live decoy.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_whistling_duck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dendrocygna_flight.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Dendrocygna_flight.jpg/220px-Dendrocygna_flight.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Dendrocygna_flight.jpg/330px-Dendrocygna_flight.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Dendrocygna_flight.jpg/440px-Dendrocygna_flight.jpg 2x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="537" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dendrocygna_flight.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In flight the broad wings are distinctive</div></div></div><p>Lesser whistling duck are usually <a href="/wiki/Gregarious" class="mw-redirect" title="Gregarious">gregarious</a>. They feed mainly on plants taken from the water as well as grains from cultivated rice apart from small fish, <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a> and invertebrates such as <a href="/wiki/Mollusc" class="mw-redirect" title="Mollusc">molluscs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Worm" title="Worm">worms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> They dabble as well as dive in water. They will often waddle on the land and <a href="/wiki/Common_myna" title="Common myna">Common mynas</a> have been noted to follow them on grass.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Courtship involves the male facing the female and dipping and raising its bill in the water and swimming around the female.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> They breed during the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> or rainy season and may vary locally in relation to the food availability. The nest site may be a tree hole lined with twigs and grass or built in the fork of a large tree, sometimes reusing an old nest of a <a href="/wiki/Kite_(bird)" title="Kite (bird)">kite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">heron</a> or even on the ground. The clutch varies from 7 to 12 white eggs that are incubated by both the parents. Large clutches of up to 17 have been noted<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> although these may be indications of intraspecific <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasitism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The eggs hatch after about 22–24 days. More than one brood may be raised in a single season.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Young birds may sometimes be carried on the back of the parents.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Local names like <i>sili</i> and <i>silhahi</i> in India are based on their wheezy two-note calls. They become very tame in captivity, walking about and responding to whistles. Individuals in captivity in the USA have lived for up to 9 years.<sup id="cite_ref-nhd_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhd-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several endoparasitic <a href="/wiki/Cestode" class="mw-redirect" title="Cestode">cestodes</a> including <i>Hymenolepis javanensis</i> and <i>Cittotaenia sandgroundi</i> have been described from lesser whistling duck hosts apart from ectoparasitic <a href="/wiki/Bird_lice" class="mw-redirect" title="Bird lice">bird lice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mite" title="Mite">mites</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
