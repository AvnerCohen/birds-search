<p>The <b>Lesser Whistling Duck</b> (<i>Dendrocygna javanica</i>), also known as <b>Indian Whistling Duck</b> or <b>Lesser Whistling Teal</b>, is a species of <a href="/wiki/Whistling_duck" title="Whistling duck">whistling duck</a> that breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Subcontinent" title="Indian Subcontinent" class="mw-redirect">Indian Subcontinent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>. They are nocturnal feeders and during the day may be found in flocks around lakes and wet paddy fields. They can perch on trees and sometimes build their nest in the hollow of a tree. This brown and long-necked duck has broad wings that are visible in flight and produces a loud two-note wheezy call. It has a chestnut rump, differentiating it from its larger relative, the <a href="/wiki/Fulvous_Whistling_Duck" title="Fulvous Whistling Duck">Fulvous Whistling Duck</a>, which has creamy white.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_Whistling_Duck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lesser_Whistling_Duck_RWD1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Lesser_Whistling_Duck_RWD1.jpg/220px-Lesser_Whistling_Duck_RWD1.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lesser_Whistling_Duck_RWD1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Swimming at Sylvan Heights Waterfowl Park, North Carolina</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This chestnut brown duck is confusable only with the <a href="/wiki/Fulvous_Whistling_Duck" title="Fulvous Whistling Duck">Fulvous Whistling Duck</a> (<i>D. bicolor</i>) but has chestnut upper-tail coverts unlike the creamy white in the latter. When flying straight, their head is held below the level of the body as in other <i>Dendrocygna</i> species. The crown appears dark and the sexes are alike in plumage. They fly slowly but with rapid wing-flapping and usually produce a repetitive wheezy <i>seasick</i> call as they circle overhead. They are very nocturnal and often rest during the day. The outermost primary feather has the inner vane modified. This has been said to aid in producing a whistling sound in flight, although this has not been supported by field studies.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbk_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pcr_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nhd_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhd-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_Whistling_Duck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/w/index.php?title=File:DendrocygnaFeather.svg&amp;page=1" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/DendrocygnaFeather.svg/220px-DendrocygnaFeather.svg.png" width="220" height="49" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DendrocygnaFeather.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Shape of the outermost primary, claimed to produce a whistle in flight</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dendrocygna_flight.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Dendrocygna_flight.jpg/220px-Dendrocygna_flight.jpg" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dendrocygna_flight.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
In flight the broad wings are distinctive</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This is a largely resident species distributed widely across lowland wetlands of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Subcontinent" title="Indian Subcontinent" class="mw-redirect">Indian Subcontinent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>. The species ranges across <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar" class="mw-redirect">Myanmar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>. They also occur on islands in the region including the Andamans, Nicobars and Maldives.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> They sometimes make local movements in response to weather and changes in water availability and the more northern birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">winter</a> further south. They are found in freshwater wetlands with good vegetation cover and often rest during the day on the banks or even on the open sea in coastal areas.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Downy chicks are black with a white eyebrow and white patches on the back of the head, the wing, lower back and rump.<sup id="cite_ref-fbi2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fbi2-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Albino individuals have been seen in the wild.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Large numbers are sometimes found in urban wetlands such as in Kolkata and Goa, particularly during winter.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> In the Alipore Zoological gardens, captive individuals were introduced in the 1930s and wild birds joined this nucleus subsequently.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>With a wide distribution range between 1 to 10 million km², they are considered to have a secure global population of between two and twenty million individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> They are not threatened by hunting as they are not considered good to eat.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Hunters in <a href="/wiki/Assam" title="Assam">Assam</a> however have been known to raise the chicks to serve as live decoy.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_Whistling_Duck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lesser_Whistling-ducks-_Landing_together_I2_IMG_1067.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Lesser_Whistling-ducks-_Landing_together_I2_IMG_1067.jpg/220px-Lesser_Whistling-ducks-_Landing_together_I2_IMG_1067.jpg" width="220" height="114" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lesser_Whistling-ducks-_Landing_together_I2_IMG_1067.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A flock landing (<a href="/wiki/Kolkata" title="Kolkata">Kolkata</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Lesser Whistling Duck are usually gregarious. They feed mainly on plants taken from the water as well as grains from cultivated rice apart from small fish, frogs and invertebrates such as molluscs and worms.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> They dabble as well as dive in water. They will often waddle on the land and Common Mynas have been noted to follow them on grass.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Courtship involves the male facing the female and dipping and raising its bill in the water and swimming around the female.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> They breed during the monsoon or rainy season and may vary locally in relation to the food availability. The nest site may be a tree hole lined with twigs and grass or built in the fork of large tree, sometimes reusing and old nest of a kite or heron or even on the ground. The clutch varies from 7 to 12 white eggs that are incubated by both the parents. Large clutches of up to 17 have been noted<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> although these may be indications of intraspecific brood parasitism.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The eggs hatch after about 22-24 days. More than one brood may be raised in a single season.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbk_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Young birds may sometimes be carried on the back of the parents.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Local names like <i>sili</i> and <i>silhahi</i> in India are based on their wheezy two-note calls. They become very tame in captivity, walking about and responding to whistles. Individuals in captivity in the USA have lived for up to 9 years.<sup id="cite_ref-nhd_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhd-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
