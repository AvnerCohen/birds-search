<p>The <b>Brown Booby</b> (<i>Sula leucogaster</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Booby" title="Booby">booby</a> family, <i>Sulidae</i>. They present sexual dimorphism. The female reaches about 80 centimetres (31&#160;in) in length, its wingspan measures up to 150&#160;cm (4.9&#160;ft), and they can weigh up to 1,300&#160;g (2.9&#160;lb). The male reaches about 75 centimetres (30&#160;in) in length, its wingspan measures up to 140&#160;cm (4.6&#160;ft), and they can weigh up to 1,000&#160;g (2.2&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-ospina-alvarez_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ospina-alvarez-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Its head and upper body are covered in dark brown, with the remainder being a contrasting white. The juvenile form is gray-brown with darkening on the head, wings and tail. While these birds are typically silent, bird watchers have reported occasional sounds similar to grunting or quacking.</p>
<p>Their heads and backs are black, and their bellies are white. Their beaks are quite sharp and contain many jagged edges. They have short wings and long, tapered tails.</p>
<p>This species breeds on islands and coasts in the pantropical areas of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic" class="mw-redirect">Atlantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific" class="mw-redirect">Pacific</a> oceans. They frequent the breeding grounds of the islands in the Gulf of Mexico and the Caribbean Sea. This bird nests in large colonies, laying two chalky blue eggs on the ground in a mound of broken shells and vegetation. It <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">winters</a> at sea over a wider area.</p>
<p>Brown Booby pairs may remain together over several seasons. They perform elaborate greeting rituals.</p>
<p>Brown Boobies are spectacular divers, plunging into the ocean at high speed. They mainly eat small <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> or <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a> which gather in groups near the surface and may catch leaping fish while skimming the surface. Although they are powerful and agile fliers, they are particularly clumsy in takeoffs and landings; they use strong winds and high perches to assist their takeoffs.</p>
