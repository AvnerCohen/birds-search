<p>The <b>brown booby</b> (<i>Sula leucogaster</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Booby" title="Booby">booby</a> family, Sulidae, of which it is perhaps the most common and widespread species.<sup id="cite_ref-ph_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ph-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It has a pantropical range, which overlaps with that of other booby species. The gregarious brown booby commutes and forages at low height over inshore waters. Flocks plunge-dive to take small fish, especially when these are driven near the surface by their predators. They only nest on the ground, and roost on solid objects rather than the water surface.<sup id="cite_ref-ph_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ph-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown_booby&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The brown booby was described by the French polymath <a href="/wiki/Georges-Louis_Leclerc,_Comte_de_Buffon" title="Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon">Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon</a> in his <i>Histoire Naturelle des Oiseaux</i> in 1781.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The bird was also illustrated in a hand-coloured plate engraved by <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Nicolas_Martinet" title="François-Nicolas Martinet">François-Nicolas Martinet</a> in the <i>Planches Enluminées D'Histoire Naturelle</i> which was produced under the supervision of <a href="/wiki/Edme-Louis_Daubenton" title="Edme-Louis Daubenton">Edme-Louis Daubenton</a> to accompany Buffon's text.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Buffon did not include a scientific name with his description but in 1783 the Dutch naturalist <a href="/wiki/Pieter_Boddaert" title="Pieter Boddaert">Pieter Boddaert</a> coined the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> <i>Pelecanus leucogaster</i> in his catalogue of the <i>Planches Enluminées</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Type_locality_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Type locality (biology)">type locality</a> is <a href="/wiki/Cayenne" title="Cayenne">Cayenne</a> in <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The current <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Sula_(genus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sula (genus)">Sula</a></i> was introduced by the French zoologist <a href="/wiki/Mathurin_Jacques_Brisson" title="Mathurin Jacques Brisson">Mathurin Jacques Brisson</a> in 1760.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The word <i>Sula</i> is Norwegian for a <a href="/wiki/Gannet" title="Gannet">gannet</a>; the specific <i>leucogaster</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>leuko</i> for "white" and <i>gastēr</i> for "belly".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are four recognised <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>S. l. leucogaster</i> <small>(Boddaert, 1783)</small> – Caribbean and Atlantic Islands</li>
<li><i>S. l. brewsteri</i> <small>Nathaniel Stickney Goss, 1888</small> – Pacific coasts of USA and Mexico</li>
<li><i>S. l. etesiaca</i> <small><a href="/wiki/John_Thayer_(ornithologist)" title="John Thayer (ornithologist)">Thayer</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Outram_Bangs" title="Outram Bangs">Bangs</a>, 1905</small> – Pacific coasts of Central America and Colombia</li>
<li><i>S. l. plotus</i> <small>(<a href="/wiki/Johann_Reinhold_Forster" title="Johann Reinhold Forster">Forster, JR</a>, 1844)</small> – Red Sea through the Indian Ocean to the west and central Pacific<sup id="cite_ref-Redman_Stevenson_Fanshawe_2016_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Redman_Stevenson_Fanshawe_2016-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown_booby&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The booby's head and upper body (back) is covered in dark brown to black plumage, with the remainder (belly) being a contrasting white. The bare part colours vary geographically, but not seasonally.<sup id="cite_ref-ph_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ph-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The species also displays <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a> of the bare part colours, the males having a blue <a href="/wiki/Eye-ring" title="Eye-ring">orbital ring</a>, as opposed to the yellow orbital ring of the female. In addition the male of subspecies <i>S. l. brewsteri</i> is distinctly plumaged in having the forehead, forecrown and chin white, merging to a greyish brown neck and breast.<sup id="cite_ref-ph_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ph-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The female booby reaches about 80 centimetres (31&#160;in) in length, her wingspan measures up to 150&#160;cm (4.9&#160;ft), and she can weigh up to 1,300&#160;g (2.9&#160;lb). The male booby reaches about 75 centimetres (30&#160;in) in length, his wingspan measures up to 140&#160;cm (4.6&#160;ft), and he can weigh up to 1,000&#160;g (2.2&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-ospina-alvarez_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ospina-alvarez-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Unlike other species of sulid the juvenile plumage already resembles that of the adult.<sup id="cite_ref-ph_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ph-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> They are gray-brown with darkening on the head, upper surfaces of the wings and tail, while the lower breast and underpart plumages are heavily flecked brown on white. Juveniles of subspecies <i>S. l. brewsteri</i> are once again distinct in having the underpart plumage more evenly mouse brown.<sup id="cite_ref-ph_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ph-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Their beaks are quite sharp and contain many jagged edges. They have fairly short wings resulting in a fast flap rate, but long, tapered tails. While these birds are typically silent, bird watchers have reported occasional sounds similar to grunting or quacking.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown_booby&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Atob%C3%A1_com_filho_no_ninho_no_Arquip%C3%A9lago_de_S%C3%A3o_Pedro_e_S%C3%A3o_Paulo.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Atob%C3%A1_com_filho_no_ninho_no_Arquip%C3%A9lago_de_S%C3%A3o_Pedro_e_S%C3%A3o_Paulo.jpg/220px-Atob%C3%A1_com_filho_no_ninho_no_Arquip%C3%A9lago_de_S%C3%A3o_Pedro_e_S%C3%A3o_Paulo.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Atob%C3%A1_com_filho_no_ninho_no_Arquip%C3%A9lago_de_S%C3%A3o_Pedro_e_S%C3%A3o_Paulo.jpg/330px-Atob%C3%A1_com_filho_no_ninho_no_Arquip%C3%A9lago_de_S%C3%A3o_Pedro_e_S%C3%A3o_Paulo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Atob%C3%A1_com_filho_no_ninho_no_Arquip%C3%A9lago_de_S%C3%A3o_Pedro_e_S%C3%A3o_Paulo.jpg/440px-Atob%C3%A1_com_filho_no_ninho_no_Arquip%C3%A9lago_de_S%C3%A3o_Pedro_e_S%C3%A3o_Paulo.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5472" data-file-height="3648" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Atob%C3%A1_com_filho_no_ninho_no_Arquip%C3%A9lago_de_S%C3%A3o_Pedro_e_S%C3%A3o_Paulo.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Male bird with chick in <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Pedro_and_S%C3%A3o_Paulo_Archipelago" class="mw-redirect" title="São Pedro and São Paulo Archipelago">São Pedro and São Paulo Archipelago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pernambuco" title="Pernambuco">Pernambuco</a>, Brazil</div></div></div>
<p>This species breeds on islands and coasts in the pantropical areas of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pacific" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific">Pacific</a> oceans.  They frequent the breeding grounds of the islands in the Gulf of Mexico and the Caribbean Sea. With the rise in pollution in the world, brown boobies have been using <a href="/wiki/Marine_debris" title="Marine debris">marine debris</a> to make their nests. 90.1 percent of these nest were consisted of plastic, while nests near shipwreck have a high percentage of the wreckage debris. <sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>This bird nests in large colonies, laying two chalky blue eggs on the ground in a mound of broken shells and vegetation, but usually raises just one chick, the second one to hatch being unable to compete for food with its older sibling, or even ejected from the nest by it.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">winters</a> at sea over a wider area.
</p><p>Brown booby pairs may remain together over several seasons. They perform elaborate greeting rituals, and are also spectacular divers, plunging into the ocean at high speed. They mainly eat small <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> or <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a> which gather in groups near the surface and may catch leaping fish while skimming the surface. Although they are powerful and agile fliers, they are particularly clumsy in takeoffs and landings; they use strong winds and high perches to assist their takeoffs.
</p>
