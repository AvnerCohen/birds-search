<p>The <b>Ruddy Turnstone</b> (<i>Arenaria interpres</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wading</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a>, one of two species of <a href="/wiki/Turnstone" title="Turnstone">turnstone</a> in the genus <i>Arenaria</i>. It is now classified in the sandpiper family <a href="/wiki/Scolopacidae" title="Scolopacidae" class="mw-redirect">Scolopacidae</a> but was formerly sometimes placed in the plover family <a href="/wiki/Charadriidae" title="Charadriidae">Charadriidae</a>. It is a highly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> bird, breeding in northern parts of <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and flying south to winter on coastlines almost worldwide. It is the only species of turnstone in much of its range and is often known simply as <b>Turnstone</b>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruddy_Turnstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Arenaria_interpres_SK.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Arenaria_interpres_SK.jpg/220px-Arenaria_interpres_SK.jpg" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Arenaria_interpres_SK.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Non-breeding plumage</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It is a fairly small and stocky bird, 22–24 centimetres (8.7–9.4 in) long with a wingspan of 50–57 centimetres (20–22 in) and a weight of 85–150 g (3.0–5.3 oz). The dark, wedge-shaped <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> is 2–2.5 centimetres (0.79–0.98 in) long and slightly upturned. The legs are fairly short at 3.5 centimetres (1.4&#160;in) and are bright orange.</p>
<p>At all seasons, the <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> is dominated by a harlequin-like pattern of black and white. Breeding birds have reddish-brown upper parts with black markings. The head is mainly white with black streaks on the crown and a black pattern on the face. The breast is mainly black apart from a white patch on the sides. The rest of the underparts are white. In flight it reveals a white wingbar, white patch near the base of the wing and white lower back, rump and tail with dark bands on the uppertail-coverts and near the tip of the tail. The female is slightly duller than the male and has a browner head with more streaking.</p>
<p>Non-breeding adults are duller than breeding birds and have dark grey-brown upperparts with black mottling and a dark head with little white. <a href="/wiki/Juvenile_(organism)" title="Juvenile (organism)">Juvenile</a> birds have a pale brown head and pale fringes to the upperpart feathers creating a scaly impression.</p>
<p><br /></p>
<p>Birds of the <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> <i>morinella</i> are smaller with darker upperparts and less streaking on the crown.</p>
<p>The Ruddy Turnstone has a staccato, rattling call and also a chattering alarm-call which is mainly given during the breeding season.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruddy_Turnstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ruddy-Turnstone-Floreana.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Ruddy-Turnstone-Floreana.jpg/220px-Ruddy-Turnstone-Floreana.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ruddy-Turnstone-Floreana.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Floreana Island, <a href="/wiki/Galapagos_Islands" title="Galapagos Islands" class="mw-redirect">Galapagos Islands</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It breeds in northern latitudes, usually no more than a few kilometres from the sea. The subspecies <i>A. i. morinella</i> occurs in northern <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and in Arctic <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> as far east as <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Island" title="Baffin Island">Baffin Island</a>. <i>A. i. interpres</i> breeds in western <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ellesmere_Island" title="Ellesmere Island">Ellesmere Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. It formerly bred on the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic</a> coast of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and has possibly bred in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>, the species winters on coastlines from <a href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)" class="mw-redirect">Washington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> southwards to the southern tip of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> although it is scarce in southern parts of <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> and is only an unconfirmed <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_in_birds" title="Vagrancy in birds" class="mw-redirect">vagrant</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> it winters in western regions from <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, Norway and Denmark southwards. Only small numbers are found on <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a> coasts. In <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> it is common all the way down to <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> with good numbers on many offshore islands. In <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> it is widespread in the south with birds wintering as far north as southern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> (mainly in the <a href="/wiki/Ryukyu_Islands" title="Ryukyu Islands">Ryukyu Islands</a>). It occurs south to <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and is present on many <a href="/wiki/Pacific_islands" title="Pacific islands" class="mw-redirect">Pacific islands</a>. Some non-breeding birds remain year round in many parts of the wintering range, with some of those birds still taking on breeding plumage in the spring and summer.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruddy_Turnstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruddy_Turnstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Feeding and diet">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding_and_diet">Feeding and diet</span></h3>
<p>Ruddy Turnstones typically feed on insects in the summer, though their diet is extended to other invertebrates such as crustaceans, mollusks, and worms in other seasons.</p>
<p>They have also been observed preying on the eggs of other bird species such as <a href="/wiki/Gulls" title="Gulls" class="mw-redirect">gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terns" title="Terns" class="mw-redirect">terns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ducks" title="Ducks" class="mw-redirect">ducks</a>, and even other turnstones, though this behavior is uncommon. In the majority of observed cases, Turnstones typically go after undefended or unattended nests, puncturing the shells with their beaks to get at the contents within.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Ruddy Turnstones engage in a variety of behaviors to locate and capture prey. These behaviors can be placed into six general categories:<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Routing — The Turnstone manipulates piles of seaweed through flicking, bulldozing, and pecking to expose small <a href="/wiki/Crustaceans" title="Crustaceans" class="mw-redirect">crustaceans</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gastropod" title="Gastropod" class="mw-redirect">gastropod</a> <a href="/wiki/Molluscs" title="Molluscs" class="mw-redirect">molluscs</a> hidden underneath.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Turning stones — As per its name, the Turnstone flicks stones with its bill to uncover hidden <a href="/wiki/Littoraria" title="Littoraria">littorinids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gammaridae" title="Gammaridae">gammarid amphipods</a>.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Digging — With small flicks of its bill, the Turnstone creates holes in the ground substrate (usually sand or mud) and then pecks at the exposed prey - often <a href="/wiki/Talitridae" title="Talitridae">sandhoppers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Coelopidae" title="Coelopidae">seaweed flies</a>.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Probing — The Turnstone inserts its bill more than a quarter-length into the ground to get at littorinids and other gastropods.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Hammer–probing — The Turnstone cracks open its prey's shell by using it's bill as a hammer, and then extracts the animal inside through pecking and probing.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Surface pecking — The Turnstone uses short, shallow pecks (less than a quarter bill-length) to get at prey at or just below the ground's surface.</li>
</ul>
<p>There is evidence that Turnstones vary between these feeding behaviors based on individual preference, sex, and even social status with respect to other Turnstones. In one studied population, dominant individuals tended to engage in routing while preventing subordinates from doing the same. When these dominant individuals were temporarily removed, some of the subordinates started to rout, while others enacted no change in foraging strategy.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruddy_Turnstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aggression and territory defense">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Aggression_and_territory_defense">Aggression and territory defense</span></h3>
<p>When foraging, Turnstones adopt different postures indicative of their level of dominance. A lowered tail and a hunched stance is associated with chasing and aggression, and thus a dominant individual. Dominance in aggression is age-related, with juveniles assuming the subordinate role a disproportionate amount of the time.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> patterns of Ruddy Turnstones exhibit an unusual amount of variation in comparison with other shorebirds. Turnstones use these unique plumage patterns to recognize individuals and discriminate intruders in their territory from neighbors occupying an adjacent territory. When a fake fiberglass Turnstone model is placed in a Turnstone's territory, the occupant is less likely to respond aggressively if the model is painted to have the plumage pattern of a neighboring Turnstone.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruddy_Turnstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Arenaria_interpres_plumage.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Arenaria_interpres_plumage.jpg/200px-Arenaria_interpres_plumage.jpg" width="200" height="148" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Arenaria_interpres_plumage.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Flock of birds in varying stages of moult amongst pebbles and seaweed.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It can survive in a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climatic</a> conditions from Arctic to tropical. The typical breeding habitat is open <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> with water nearby. Outside the breeding season, it is found along coasts, particularly on rocky or stony shores. It is often found on man-made structures such as <a href="/wiki/Breakwater_(structure)" title="Breakwater (structure)">breakwaters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jetty" title="Jetty">jetties</a>. It may venture onto open grassy areas near the coast. Small numbers sometimes turn up on inland wetlands, especially during the spring and autumn migrations.</p>
<p>In terms of wintering sites, Ruddy Turnstones are particularly faithful to specific locations. A study published in 2009 examined Turnstones wintering along a stretch of coastline in the Firth of Clyde. It found that 95% of birds resident to the area at the end of winter returned the following autumn. The same study also confirmed Ruddy Turnstones as one of the longest lived wader species, with annual adult mortality rates of under 15%.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Their average lifespan is 9 years with 19 years and 2 months being the longest recorded.</p>
<p>The Ruddy Turnstone has a varied diet including <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">carrion</a>, eggs and plant material but it feeds mainly on <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>. <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">Insects</a> are particularly important in the breeding season. At other times it also takes <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc" class="mw-redirect">molluscs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Worm" title="Worm">worms</a>. It often flips over stones and other objects to get at prey items hiding underneath; this behaviour is the origin of the name "turnstone". It usually forages in flocks.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruddy_Turnstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>It is a <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" title="Monogamous" class="mw-redirect">monogamous</a> bird and pairs may remain together for more than one breeding season. The <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a> is a shallow scrape, often with a lining of leaves. It is about 11 centimetres across and 3 centimetres deep. It may be built amongst vegetation or on bare stony or rocky ground. Several pairs may nest close together.</p>
<p>A single clutch of two to five <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> is laid with four being most common. The eggs measure about 41 millimetres by 29 and weigh around 17.9&#160;grams. They are smooth, slightly glossy and oval to pear-shaped. They are variable in colour but are commonly pale green-brown with dark brown markings, densest at the larger end. <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" title="Avian incubation" class="mw-redirect">Incubation</a> begins when the first egg is laid and lasts for about 22–24 days. The female is mainly responsible for incubating the eggs but the male may help towards the end.</p>
<p>The young birds are <a href="/wiki/Precocial" title="Precocial">precocial</a> and are able to leave the nest soon after hatching. They are buff above with dark grey markings and are white below. They are able to feed themselves but are protected by the parents, particularly the male. They <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> after 19–21 days.</p>
