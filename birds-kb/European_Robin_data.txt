<p>The <b>European Robin</b> (<i>Erithacus rubecula</i>), most commonly known in <a href="/wiki/English-speaking_world" title="English-speaking world">Anglophone</a> <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> simply as the <b>Robin</b>, is a small <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" title="Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect">insectivorous</a> <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> that was formerly classed as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family (Turdidae), but is now considered to be a <a href="/wiki/Chat_(bird)" title="Chat (bird)">chat</a>. Around 12.5–14.0&#160;cm (5.0–5.5&#160;in) in length, the male and female are similar in colouration, with an orange breast and face lined with grey, brown upperparts and a whitish belly. It is found across Europe, east to Western Siberia and south to North Africa; it is sedentary in most of its range except the far north.</p>
<p>The term <i>Robin</i> is also applied to some birds in other families with red or orange breasts. These include the <a href="/wiki/American_Robin" title="American Robin">American Robin</a> (<i>Turdus migratorius</i>), which is a <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a>, and the Australian red robins of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Petroica" title="Petroica">Petroica</a></i>, members of a family whose relationships are unclear.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Robin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The European Robin was one of the many species originally described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in his 18th century work, <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>, under the name of <i>Motacilla rubecula</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Its specific epithet <i>rubecula</i> is a diminutive derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>ruber</i> 'red'.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The genus <i><a href="/wiki/Erithacus" title="Erithacus">Erithacus</a></i> was created by French naturalist <a href="/wiki/Georges_Cuvier" title="Georges Cuvier">Georges Cuvier</a> in 1800, giving the bird its current <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> of <i>E. rubecula</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cuvier_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cuvier-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The distinctive orange breast of both sexes contributed to the European Robin's original name of <i>redbreast</i> (<i>orange</i> as the name of a colour was unknown in English until the sixteenth century, by which time the <a href="/wiki/Orange_(fruit)" title="Orange (fruit)">fruit of that name</a> had been introduced). In the fifteenth century, when it became popular to give human names to familiar species, the bird came to be known as <i>Robin redbreast</i>, which was eventually shortened to <i>Robin</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Other older English names for the bird include <i>Ruddock</i> and <i>Robinet</i>. In American literature of the late 19th century, this robin was frequently called the <i>English Robin</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/West_Frisian_language" title="West Frisian language">Frisian</a> <i>robyntsje</i> or <i>robynderke</i> is similar to the English name,<sup id="cite_ref-OED_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> while Dutch <i>Roodborstje</i> and French <i>Rouge-gorge</i> both refer to the distinctively coloured front.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Robin belongs to a group of mainly <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" title="Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect">insectivorous</a> birds that have been variously assigned to the thrushes or "<a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">flycatchers</a>", depending on how these groups were perceived <a href="/wiki/Taxonomically" title="Taxonomically" class="mw-redirect">taxonomically</a>. Eventually, the flycatcher-thrush assemblage was re-analysed and the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Erithacus" title="Erithacus">Erithacus</a></i> assigned to a group of thrush-like true flycatchers, the tribe Saxicolini, that also includes the <a href="/wiki/Common_Nightingale" title="Common Nightingale">Common Nightingale</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chat_(bird)" title="Chat (bird)">Old World chats</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Two Eastern <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" title="Palearctic" class="mw-redirect">Palearctic</a> species are usually placed in the genus <i>Erithacus</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Robin" title="Japanese Robin">Japanese Robin</a> (<i>E. akahige</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Ry%C5%ABky%C5%AB_Robin" title="Ryūkyū Robin" class="mw-redirect">Ryūkyū Robin</a> (<i>E. komadori</i>), the latter being a restricted-range island species. <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">Biogeography</a> and <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA" class="mw-redirect">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome <i>b</i></a> <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">sequence</a> data indicate that these might better be classified with some <a href="/wiki/Far_East" title="Far East">Far Eastern</a> "nightingales", leaving only the European species in <i>Erithacus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Seki_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seki-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Robin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Erithacus-rubecula-melophilus_Dublin-Ireland.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Erithacus-rubecula-melophilus_Dublin-Ireland.jpg/150px-Erithacus-rubecula-melophilus_Dublin-Ireland.jpg" width="150" height="242" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Erithacus-rubecula-melophilus_Dublin-Ireland.jpg/225px-Erithacus-rubecula-melophilus_Dublin-Ireland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Erithacus-rubecula-melophilus_Dublin-Ireland.jpg/300px-Erithacus-rubecula-melophilus_Dublin-Ireland.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Erithacus-rubecula-melophilus_Dublin-Ireland.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
The British subspecies, <i>Erithacus rubecula melophilus</i>, differs only slightly from Continental birds.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In its large continental Eurasian range, Robins vary somewhat, but do not form discrete populations that might be considered <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dietzen_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dietzen-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Patzold95_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Patzold95-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Thus, Robin subspecies are mainly distinguished by forming resident populations on islands and in mountainous areas. The Robin found in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> and most of western Europe, <i>Erithacus rubecula melophilus</i>, also occurs as a vagrant in adjacent regions. <i>E. r. witherbyi</i> from Northwestern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a> closely resembles <i>melophilus</i> but for a shorter wing length.<sup id="cite_ref-Lack46_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lack46-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The northeasternmost birds, large and fairly washed-out in colour are <i>E. r. tataricus</i>. In the southeast of its range, <i>E. r. valens</i> of the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_Peninsula" title="Crimean Peninsula" class="mw-redirect">Crimean Peninsula</a>, <i>E. r. caucasicus</i> of the Caucasus and N <a href="/wiki/Transcaucasia" title="Transcaucasia" class="mw-redirect">Transcaucasia</a>, and <i>E. r. hyrcanus</i> southeastwards into <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> are generally accepted as significantly distinct.<sup id="cite_ref-Lack46_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lack46-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, the local population has been described as <i>E. r. microrhynchos</i>, and although not distinct in <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>, its isolation seems to suggests the subspecies is valid (but see below).</p>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Robin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Canary Islands robin">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Canary_Islands_robin">Canary Islands robin</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Erithacus_rubecula_-Canary_Islands,_Spain_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Erithacus_rubecula_-Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg/220px-Erithacus_rubecula_-Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Erithacus_rubecula_-Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg/330px-Erithacus_rubecula_-Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Erithacus_rubecula_-Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg/440px-Erithacus_rubecula_-Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Erithacus_rubecula_-Canary_Islands,_Spain_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Adult and juvenile exemplaries of Gran Canaria's Robin.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The most distinct birds are those of <a href="/wiki/Gran_Canaria" title="Gran Canaria">Gran Canaria</a> (<i>E. r. marionae</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Tenerife" title="Tenerife">Tenerife</a> (<i>E. r. superbus</i>), which may be considered two distinct species or at least two different subspecies. It is readily distinguished by a white eye-ring, an intensely coloured breast, and a grey line that separates the orange-red from the brown colouration. Its belly is entirely white.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>While cytochrome <i>b</i> sequence data and vocalisations<sup id="cite_ref-Bergmann_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bergmann-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> indicate that the Gran Canaria/Tenerife Robins are indeed very distinct and probably derived from colonisation by mainland birds some 2 <a href="/wiki/Mya_(unit)" title="Mya (unit)" class="mw-redirect">million years ago</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition, Gran Canaria and Tenerife birds are well distinct genetically between each other.</p>
<p>Christian Dietzen, Hans-Hinrich Witt and Michael Wink published in 2003 in Avian Science a study called "The phylogeographic differentiation of the European robin Erithacus rubecula on the Canary Islands revealed by mitochondrial DNA sequence data and morphometrics: evidence for a new robin taxon on Gran Canaria?".<sup id="cite_ref-Dietzen_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dietzen-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> In it they concluded that Gran Canaria's Robin diverged genetically from their European relatives as far back as 2.3 million years, while the Tenerife ones took half a million years to make this leap, 1.8 million years ago. The reason would be a different colonization of the Canaries by this bird, which arrived to the oldest island first (Gran Canaria) and subsequently passed to the neighboring island (Tenerife).<sup id="cite_ref-canarias7.es_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-canarias7.es-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A thorough comparison between <i>marionae</i> and <i>superbus</i> is pending to confirm that the first one is effectively a different subspecies. Initial results suggest that birds from Gran Canaria have wings about 10% shorter than those on Tenerife.<sup id="cite_ref-Dietzen_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dietzen-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The west Canary Islands populations are younger (<a href="/wiki/Middle_Pleistocene" title="Middle Pleistocene">Middle Pleistocene</a>) and only beginning to diverge genetically. Robins from the western <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>: <a href="/wiki/El_Hierro" title="El Hierro">El Hierro</a>, <a href="/wiki/La_Palma" title="La Palma">La Palma</a> and <a href="/wiki/La_Gomera" title="La Gomera">La Gomera</a> (<i>E. (r.) microrhynchus</i>) are similar to the European (<i>Erithacus rubecula</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Lack46_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lack46-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Finally, the Robins which can be found in <a href="/wiki/Fuerteventura" title="Fuerteventura">Fuerteventura</a> are the European ones, which is not surprising as the species does not breed either in this island or in the nearby <a href="/wiki/Lanzarote" title="Lanzarote">Lanzarote</a>, they are wintering exemplaries or just passing through during their long migrations between <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-canarias7.es_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-canarias7.es-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Robin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other robins">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_robins">Other robins</span></h3>
<p>The larger <a href="/wiki/American_Robin" title="American Robin">American Robin</a> (<i>Turdus migratorius</i>) is named for its similarity to the European Robin, but the two birds are not closely related. The similarity lies largely in the <a href="/wiki/Orange_(colour)" title="Orange (colour)">orange</a> chest patch in both species. This American species was incorrectly shown "feathering its nest" in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Mary_Poppins_(film)" title="Mary Poppins (film)">Mary Poppins</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-imdb_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-imdb-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> but it only occurs in the UK as a very rare vagrant.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Some South and <a href="/wiki/Middle_America_(Americas)" title="Middle America (Americas)" class="mw-redirect">Middle American</a> <i>Turdus</i> thrushes are also named as robins such as the <a href="/wiki/Rufous-collared_Robin" title="Rufous-collared Robin" class="mw-redirect">Rufous-collared Robin</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> "robin redbreast", more correctly the <a href="/wiki/Scarlet_Robin" title="Scarlet Robin">Scarlet Robin</a> (<i>Petroica multicolor</i>), is more closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jay" title="Jay">jays</a> than it is to the European Robin. It belongs to the family <a href="/wiki/Petroicidae" title="Petroicidae">Petroicidae</a>, commonly called "Australasian robins". The <a href="/wiki/Red-billed_Leiothrix" title="Red-billed Leiothrix">Red-billed Leiothrix</a> (<i>Leiothrix lutea</i>) is sometimes named "Pekin Robin" by <a href="/wiki/Aviculture" title="Aviculture">aviculturalists</a>. Yet another group of Old World Flycatchers, this time from Africa and Asia is the genus <i>Copsychus</i>; its members are known as <a href="/wiki/Magpie-robin" title="Magpie-robin">Magpie-robins</a>, one of which, the <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Magpie_Robin" title="Oriental Magpie Robin" class="mw-redirect">Oriental Magpie Robin</a> <i>(C. saularis)</i>, is the national bird of Bangladesh.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Robin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Young-robin-lg-wikipedia.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Young-robin-lg-wikipedia.jpg/170px-Young-robin-lg-wikipedia.jpg" width="170" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Young-robin-lg-wikipedia.jpg/255px-Young-robin-lg-wikipedia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Young-robin-lg-wikipedia.jpg/340px-Young-robin-lg-wikipedia.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Young-robin-lg-wikipedia.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>The adult European Robin is 12.5–14.0&#160;cm (5.0–5.5&#160;in) long and weighs 16–22&#160;<a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">g</a> (9/16–13/16&#160;<a href="/wiki/Ounce" title="Ounce">oz</a>), with a wingspan of 20–22&#160;cm (8–9&#160;in). The male and female bear similar plumage; an orange breast and face (more strongly coloured in the otherwise similar British subspecies <i>E. r. mesophilus</i>), lined by a bluish grey on the sides of the neck and chest. The upperparts are brownish, or olive-tinged in British birds, and the belly whitish, while the legs and feet are brown. The bill and eyes are black. Juveniles are a spotted brown and white in colouration, with patches of orange gradually appearing.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The Robin has a fluting, warbling <span class="unicode"><a href="/wiki/File:120401-132827_Erithacus_rubecula.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" /></a>&#160;<a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/29/120401-132827_Erithacus_rubecula.ogg" class="internal" title="120401-132827 Erithacus rubecula.ogg">song</a><small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">&#160;(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:120401-132827_Erithacus_rubecula.ogg" title="File:120401-132827 Erithacus rubecula.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span> in the breeding season, when they often sing into the evening, and sometimes into the night, leading some to confuse them with the <a href="/wiki/Common_Nightingale" title="Common Nightingale">Common Nightingale</a> (<i>Luscinia megarhynchos</i>). Nocturnal singing in urban Robins occurs in places that are noisy during the day, suggesting that they sing at night because it is quieter, and their message can propagate through the environment more clearly. Daytime noise outperformed night-time light pollution as a predictor of nocturnal singing activity in urban robins in <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a>, England.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Both the male and female sing during the winter, when they hold separate territories, the song then sounding more plaintive than the summer version.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The female Robin moves a short distance from the summer nesting territory to a nearby area that is more suitable for winter feeding. The male Robin keeps the same territory throughout the year.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Robin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>The Robin occurs in Eurasia east to Western <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, south to <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> and on the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> islands as far west as the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>. It is not found in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> In the south east, it reaches the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> range. <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">British</a> Robins are largely resident but a small minority, usually female, migrate to southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> during winter, a few as far as Spain. <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> Robins migrate to Britain and western Europe to escape the harsher winters. These migrants can be recognised by the greyer tone of the upper parts of their bodies and duller orange breast. The European Robin prefers spruce woods in northern Europe, contrasting with its preference for parks and gardens in the British Isles.<sup id="cite_ref-Jon78_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jon78-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Attempts to introduce the European Robin into Australia and New Zealand in the latter part of the 19th century were unsuccessful. Birds were released around <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Auckland" title="Auckland">Auckland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christchurch" title="Christchurch">Christchurch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">Wellington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dunedin" title="Dunedin">Dunedin</a> by various local <a href="/wiki/Acclimatisation_society" title="Acclimatisation society">Acclimatisation societies</a>, with none becoming established. There was a similar outcome in North America as birds failed to establish after being released in <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>, New York in 1852, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> in 1889-92, and the <a href="/wiki/Saanich_Peninsula" title="Saanich Peninsula">Saanich Peninsula</a> in <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> in 1908-10.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

</div>
</div>
<p>The Robin is <a href="/wiki/Diurnality" title="Diurnality">diurnal</a>, although has been reported to be active hunting insects on moonlit nights or near artificial light at night.<sup id="cite_ref-Patzold95_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Patzold95-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Well known to British and Irish gardeners, it is relatively unafraid of people and likes to come close when anyone is digging the soil, in order to look out for <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a> and other food freshly turned up. Indeed, the robin is considered to be a gardener's friend and for various <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a> reasons the robin would never be harmed. In continental Europe on the other hand, robins were hunted and killed as with most other small birds, and are more wary.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Robins also approach large wild animals, such as <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">wild boar</a> and other animals which disturb the ground, to look for any food that might be brought to the surface. In autumn and winter, robins will supplement their usual diet of terrestrial invertebrates, such as spiders, worms and insects, with berries and fruit.<sup id="cite_ref-Jon78_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jon78-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> They will also eat seed mixtures placed on bird-tables.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_20-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Male Robins are noted for their highly aggressive territorial behaviour. They will attack other males that stray into their territories, and have been observed attacking other small birds without apparent provocation. Such attacks sometimes lead to fatalities, accounting for up to 10% of adult Robin deaths in some areas.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPBterr_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPBterr-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Because of high mortality in the first year of life, a Robin has an average <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">life expectancy</a> of 1.1&#160;years; however, once past its first year it can expect to live longer and one Robin has been recorded as reaching 12&#160;years of age.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> A spell of very low temperatures in winter may also result in significant mortality.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPBterr_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPBterr-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> This species is parasitised by the <a href="/wiki/Moorhen_flea" title="Moorhen flea">moorhen flea</a>, <i>Dasypsyllus gallinulae</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rothschild_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rothschild-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Robin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft" style="width: 379px;">
<div class="thumbinner">
<div style="float: left; margin: 1px; width: 171px">

<p>Robins may choose a wide variety of sites for building a nest, in fact anything which can offer some form of depression or hole may be considered. As well as the usual crevices, or sheltered banks, odder objects include pieces of machinery, barbecues, bicycle handlebars, bristles on upturned brooms, discarded kettles, watering cans, flower pots and even hats. The nest is composed of moss, leaves and grass, with finer grass, hair and feathers for lining. Two or three clutches of five or six eggs are laid throughout the breeding season, which commences in March in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. The eggs are a cream, buff or white speckled or blotched with reddish-brown colour, often more heavily so at the larger end.<sup id="cite_ref-Observerbirdseggs_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Observerbirdseggs-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> When juvenile birds fly from the nests they are mottled brown in colour all over. After two to three months out of the nest, the juvenile bird grows some orange feathers under its chin and over a similar period this patch gradually extends to complete the adult appearance.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Robin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Magnetoreception">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetoreception">Magnetoreception</span></h2>
<p>The avian magnetic compass of the Robin has been extensively researched and uses Vision-Based <a href="/wiki/Magnetoreception" title="Magnetoreception" class="mw-redirect">Magnetoreception</a>, in which the Robin's ability to sense the magnetic field of the earth for navigation is affected by the light entering the bird's eye. The physical mechanism of the Robin's magnetic sense is not fully understood, but may involve <a href="/wiki/Quantum_entanglement" title="Quantum entanglement">quantum entanglement</a> of electron spins.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Robin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cultural depictions">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_depictions">Cultural depictions</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RedbreastDE.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/RedbreastDE.jpg/170px-RedbreastDE.jpg" width="170" height="263" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/RedbreastDE.jpg/255px-RedbreastDE.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/28/RedbreastDE.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RedbreastDE.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
1880 Engraving</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The robin features prominently in British folklore, and that of northwestern France, but much less so in other parts of Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-ingersoll167_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ingersoll167-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> It was held to be a storm-cloud bird and sacred to <a href="/wiki/Thor" title="Thor">Thor</a>, the god of thunder, in <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse mythology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cooper92_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper92-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Robins also feature in the traditional children's tale, <i><a href="/wiki/Babes_in_the_Wood" title="Babes in the Wood">Babes in the Wood</a></i>; the birds cover the dead bodies of the children.<sup id="cite_ref-deVries76_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deVries76-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> More recently, the robin has become strongly associated with <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a>, taking a starring role on many <a href="/wiki/Christmas_card" title="Christmas card">Christmas cards</a> since the mid 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-deVries76_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deVries76-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> The Robin has also appeared on many Christmas postage stamps. An old <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Folk_tale" title="Folk tale" class="mw-redirect">folk tale</a> seeks to explain the Robin's distinctive breast. Legend has it that when <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> was dying on the cross, the Robin, then simply brown in colour, flew to his side and sang into his ear in order to comfort him in his pain. The blood from his wounds stained the Robin's breast, and thereafter all Robins got the mark of Christ's blood upon them.<sup id="cite_ref-Cooper92_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper92-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> An alternative legend has it that its breast was scorched fetching water for souls in Purgatory.<sup id="cite_ref-deVries76_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deVries76-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> The association with Christmas, however, more probably arises from the fact that postmen in <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Victorian</a> Britain wore red uniforms and were nicknamed "Robin"; the Robin featured on the Christmas card is an emblem of the postman delivering the card.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1960s, in a vote publicised by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> newspaper, the Robin was adopted as the unofficial national bird of the <a href="/wiki/UK" title="UK" class="mw-redirect">UK</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> The Robin was then used as a symbol of a Bird Protection Society.<sup id="cite_ref-Scot_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scot-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Several <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Welsh</a> sports organisations are nicknamed "The Robins". These include the professional <a href="/wiki/Football_(soccer)" title="Football (soccer)" class="mw-redirect">football (soccer)</a> clubs <a href="/wiki/Bristol_City" title="Bristol City" class="mw-redirect">Bristol City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swindon_Town" title="Swindon Town" class="mw-redirect">Swindon Town</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham_Town" title="Cheltenham Town" class="mw-redirect">Cheltenham Town</a> (whose home colours are red) and, traditionally, <a href="/wiki/Wrexham_FC" title="Wrexham FC" class="mw-redirect">Wrexham FC</a>, as well as the English Rugby League team <a href="/wiki/Hull_Kingston_Rovers" title="Hull Kingston Rovers">Hull Kingston Rovers</a> (whose home colours are white with a red band).<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> A small bird is an unusual choice, though it is thought to symbolise agility in darting around the field.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Moreover, the <a href="/wiki/Swindon_Robins" title="Swindon Robins">Swindon Robins</a> is the full name of the local <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_speedway" title="Motorcycle speedway">Speedway</a> promotion.</p>
