<p>The <b>European robin</b> (<i>Erithacus rubecula</i>), known simply as the <b>robin</b> or <b>robin redbreast</b> in the British Isles, is a small <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a> <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> that belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Chat_(bird)" title="Chat (bird)">chat</a> subfamily of the <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a> family. About 12.5–14.0&#160;cm (5.0–5.5&#160;inches) in length, the male and female are similar in colouration, with an orange breast and face lined with grey, brown upper-parts and a whitish belly. It is found across Europe, east to Western Siberia and south to North Africa; it is <a href="/wiki/Sedentism" title="Sedentism">sedentary</a> in most of its range except the far north.
</p><p>The term <i>robin</i> is also applied to some birds in other families with red or orange breasts. These include the <a href="/wiki/American_robin" title="American robin">American robin</a> (<i>Turdus migratorius</i>), a <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a>, and the Australasian robins of the family <a href="/wiki/Petroicidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Petroicidae">Petroicidae</a>, the relationships of which are unclear.
</p>

<h2><span id="Name.2C_taxonomy_and_systematics"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Name,_taxonomy_and_systematics">Name, taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_robin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name, taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:European_robin_(Erithacus_rubecula),_Bristol,_UK.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/European_robin_%28Erithacus_rubecula%29%2C_Bristol%2C_UK.jpg/220px-European_robin_%28Erithacus_rubecula%29%2C_Bristol%2C_UK.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/European_robin_%28Erithacus_rubecula%29%2C_Bristol%2C_UK.jpg/330px-European_robin_%28Erithacus_rubecula%29%2C_Bristol%2C_UK.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/European_robin_%28Erithacus_rubecula%29%2C_Bristol%2C_UK.jpg/440px-European_robin_%28Erithacus_rubecula%29%2C_Bristol%2C_UK.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:European_robin_(Erithacus_rubecula),_Bristol,_UK.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Robin standing on a fence in Bristol, UK</div></div></div>
<p>The European robin was described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> under the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Motacilla rubecula</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Its <a href="/wiki/Specific_epithet_(zoology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Specific epithet (zoology)">specific epithet</a> <i>rubecula</i> is a diminutive derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">ruber</i> 'red'.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The genus <i><a href="/wiki/Erithacus" class="mw-redirect" title="Erithacus">Erithacus</a></i> was introduced by French naturalist <a href="/wiki/Georges_Cuvier" title="Georges Cuvier">Georges Cuvier</a> in 1800, giving the bird its current binomial name <i>E. rubecula</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> name <i>Erithacus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and refers to an unknown bird, now usually identified as robin.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The distinctive orange breast of both sexes contributed to the European robin's original name of "redbreast", <i>orange</i> as a colour name being unknown in English until the 16th century, by which time the fruit had been introduced. In the 15th century, when it became popular to give human names to familiar species, the bird came to be known as <b>robin redbreast</b>, which was eventually shortened to <i>robin</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> As a <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a>, Robin is originally a diminutive of Robert. Other older English names for the bird include <i>ruddock</i> and <i>robinet</i>. In American literature of the late 19th century, this robin was frequently called the <i>English robin</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Dutch <i lang="nl" title="Dutch language text">roodborstje</i>, French <i lang="fr" title="French language text">rouge-gorge</i>, German <i lang="de" title="German language text">Rotkehlchen</i>, Italian <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">pettirosso</i>, Spanish <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">petirrojo</i> and Portuguese <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">pisco-de-peito-ruivo</i> all refer to the distinctively coloured front.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The genus <i>Erithacus</i> previously included the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_robin" title="Japanese robin">Japanese robin</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ryukyu_robin" title="Ryukyu robin">Ryukyu robin</a>. These east Asian species were shown in <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular phylogenetic">molecular phylogenetic</a> studies to be more similar to a group of other Asian species than to the European robin.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sangster2010_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sangster2010-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In a reorganisation of the genera, the Japanese and the Ryukyu robins were moved to the resurrected genus <i><a href="/wiki/Larvivora" title="Larvivora">Larvivora</a></i> leaving the European robin as the sole member of <i>Erithacus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The phylogenetic analysis placed <i>Erithacus</i> in the subfamily Erithacinae, which otherwise contained only African species, but its exact position with respect to the other genera was not resolved.<sup id="cite_ref-sangster2010_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sangster2010-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The genus <i>Erithacus</i> was formerly classified as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family (Turdidae) but is now considered to belong to the <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a> family (Muscicapidae), specifically to the <a href="/wiki/Chat_(bird)" title="Chat (bird)">chats</a> (subfamily Saxicolinae) which also include the <a href="/wiki/Common_nightingale" title="Common nightingale">common nightingale</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_robin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In its large continental Eurasian range, robins vary somewhat, but do not form discrete populations that might be considered <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dietzen_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dietzen-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Patzold95_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Patzold95-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Robin subspecies are mainly distinguished by forming resident populations on islands and in mountainous areas. The robin found in the British Isles and much of western Europe, <i>Erithacus rubecula melophilus</i>, occurs as a vagrant in adjacent regions. <i>E. r. witherbyi</i> from northwest Africa, Corsica, and Sardinia closely resembles <i>melophilus</i> but has shorter wings.<sup id="cite_ref-Lack46_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lack46-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The northeasternmost birds, large and fairly washed-out in colour, are <i>E. r. tataricus</i>. In the southeast of its range, <i>E. r. valens</i> of the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_Peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Crimean Peninsula">Crimean Peninsula</a>, <i>E. r. caucasicus</i> of the Caucasus and northern <a href="/wiki/Transcaucasia" title="Transcaucasia">Transcaucasia</a>, and <i>E. r. hyrcanus</i> southeastwards into Iran are generally accepted as significantly distinct.<sup id="cite_ref-Lack46_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lack46-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, the local population has been described as <i>E. r. microrhynchos</i>, and although not distinct in <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>, its isolation seems to suggest the subspecies is valid (but see below).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Canary_Islands_robin">Canary Islands robin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_robin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Canary Islands robin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><div class="mediaContainer" style="width:200px"><audio id="mwe_player_0" controls="" preload="none" style="width:200px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="91.8" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="Pechuguero_de_Gran_Canaria_(Erithacus_Rubecula_Marionae).ogg" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/74/Pechuguero_de_Gran_Canaria_%28Erithacus_Rubecula_Marionae%29.ogg" type="audio/ogg; codecs=&quot;vorbis&quot;" data-title="Original Ogg file (73 kbps)" data-shorttitle="Ogg source" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="73182" /><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/7/74/Pechuguero_de_Gran_Canaria_%28Erithacus_Rubecula_Marionae%29.ogg/Pechuguero_de_Gran_Canaria_%28Erithacus_Rubecula_Marionae%29.ogg.mp3" type="audio/mpeg" data-title="MP3" data-shorttitle="MP3" data-transcodekey="mp3" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="125104" /></audio></div>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pechuguero_de_Gran_Canaria_(Erithacus_Rubecula_Marionae).ogg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gran Canaria robin call</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Erithacus_rubecula_-Canary_Islands,_Spain_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Erithacus_rubecula_-Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg/220px-Erithacus_rubecula_-Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Erithacus_rubecula_-Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg/330px-Erithacus_rubecula_-Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Erithacus_rubecula_-Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg/440px-Erithacus_rubecula_-Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="560" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Erithacus_rubecula_-Canary_Islands,_Spain_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult and juvenile Gran Canaria robins</div></div></div>
<p>The most distinct birds are those of <a href="/wiki/Gran_Canaria" title="Gran Canaria">Gran Canaria</a>  (<i>E. r. marionae</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Tenerife" title="Tenerife">Tenerife</a> (<i>E. r. superbus</i>), which may be considered two distinct species or at least two different subspecies. It is readily distinguished by a white eye-ring, an intensely coloured breast, and a grey line that separates the orange-red from the brown colouration. Its belly is entirely white.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2020)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cytochrome <i>b</i> sequence data and vocalisations<sup id="cite_ref-Bergmann_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bergmann-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> indicate that the Gran Canaria/Tenerife robins are indeed very distinct and probably derived from colonisation by mainland birds some 2 million years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Christian Dietzen, Hans-Hinrich Witt and Michael Wink published in 2003 in Avian Science a study called "The phylogeographic differentiation of the European robin <i>Erithacus rubecula</i> on the Canary Islands revealed by mitochondrial DNA sequence data and morphometrics: evidence for a new robin taxon on Gran Canaria?".<sup id="cite_ref-Dietzen_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dietzen-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In it they concluded that Gran Canaria's robin diverged genetically from their European relatives as far back as 2.3 million years, while the Tenerife ones took another half a million years to make this leap, 1.8 million years ago. The most likely reason would be a different colonization of the Canaries by this bird, which arrived at the oldest island first (Gran Canaria) and subsequently passed to the neighboring island (Tenerife).<sup id="cite_ref-canarias7.es_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-canarias7.es-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A thorough comparison between <i>marionae</i> and <i>superbus</i> is pending to confirm that the first one is effectively a different subspecies. Initial results suggest that birds from Gran Canaria have wings about 10% shorter than those on Tenerife.<sup id="cite_ref-Dietzen_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dietzen-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The west Canary Islands populations are younger (<a href="/wiki/Middle_Pleistocene" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle Pleistocene">Middle Pleistocene</a>) and only beginning to diverge genetically. Robins from the western <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>: El Hierro, La Palma and La Gomera (<i>E. r. microrhynchus</i>) are similar to the European type subspecies (<i>E. r. rubecula</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Lack46_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lack46-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Finally, the robins which can be found in <a href="/wiki/Fuerteventura" title="Fuerteventura">Fuerteventura</a> are the European ones, which is not surprising as the species does not breed either in this island or in the nearby <a href="/wiki/Lanzarote" title="Lanzarote">Lanzarote</a>; they are wintering birds or just passing through during their long migration between Africa and Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-canarias7.es_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-canarias7.es-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_robins">Other robins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_robin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other robins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The larger American robin (<i>T. migratorius</i>) is named for its similarity to the European robin, but the two birds are not closely related. The similarity lies largely in the <a href="/wiki/Orange_(colour)" title="Orange (colour)">orange</a> chest patch in both species. This American species was incorrectly shown "feathering its nest" in London in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Mary_Poppins_(film)" title="Mary Poppins (film)">Mary Poppins</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-imdb_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-imdb-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> but it only occurs in the U.K. as a very rare vagrant.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some South and Central American <i>Turdus</i> thrushes are also called robins, such as the <a href="/wiki/Rufous-collared_thrush" title="Rufous-collared thrush">rufous-collared thrush</a>. The Australian "robin redbreast", more correctly the <a href="/wiki/Scarlet_robin" title="Scarlet robin">scarlet robin</a> (<i>Petroica multicolor</i>), is more closely related to crows and jays than it is to the European robin. It belongs to the family <a href="/wiki/Petroicidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Petroicidae">Petroicidae</a>, whose members are commonly called "Australasian robins". The <a href="/wiki/Red-billed_leiothrix" title="Red-billed leiothrix">red-billed leiothrix</a> (<i>Leiothrix lutea</i>) is sometimes named the "Pekin robin" by <a href="/wiki/Aviculture" title="Aviculture">aviculturalists</a>. Another group of Old World flycatchers, this time from Africa and Asia, is the genus <i>Copsychus</i>; its members are known as <a href="/wiki/Magpie-robin" title="Magpie-robin">magpie-robins</a>, one of which, the <a href="/wiki/Oriental_magpie_robin" class="mw-redirect" title="Oriental magpie robin">Oriental magpie robin</a> (<i>C. saularis</i>), is the national bird of Bangladesh.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_robin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:European_robin_in_Villa_Ada.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/European_robin_in_Villa_Ada.JPG/220px-European_robin_in_Villa_Ada.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/European_robin_in_Villa_Ada.JPG/330px-European_robin_in_Villa_Ada.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/European_robin_in_Villa_Ada.JPG/440px-European_robin_in_Villa_Ada.JPG 2x" data-file-width="5184" data-file-height="3456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:European_robin_in_Villa_Ada.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>European robin in its natural habitat in Villa Ada, Rome</div></div></div>
<p>The adult European robin is 12.5–14.0&#160;cm (5.0–5.5&#160;in) long and weighs 16–22&#160;<a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">g</a> (9/16–13/16&#160;<a href="/wiki/Ounce" title="Ounce">oz</a>), with a wingspan of 20–22&#160;cm (8–9&#160;in). The male and female bear similar plumage; an orange breast and face (more strongly coloured in the otherwise similar British subspecies <i>E. r. melophilus</i>), lined by a bluish grey on the sides of the neck and chest. The upperparts are brownish, or olive-tinged in British birds, and the belly whitish, while the legs and feet are brown. The bill and eyes are black. Juveniles are a spotted brown and white in colouration, with patches of orange gradually appearing.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_robin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:European_Robin_looks.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/European_Robin_looks.jpg/220px-European_Robin_looks.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/European_Robin_looks.jpg/330px-European_Robin_looks.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/European_Robin_looks.jpg/440px-European_Robin_looks.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3847" data-file-height="2548" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:European_Robin_looks.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>European robin in  <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a>, Germany</div></div></div>
<p>The robin occurs in Eurasia east to Western Siberia, south to Algeria and on the Atlantic islands as far west as the Azores and Madeira. It is a vagrant in Iceland. In the southeast, it reaches the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> range. Irish and British robins are largely resident but a small minority, usually female, migrate to southern Europe during winter, a few as far as Spain. Scandinavian and Russian robins migrate to Britain and western Europe to escape the harsher winters. These migrants can be recognised by the greyer tone of the upper parts of their bodies and duller orange breast. The European robin prefers spruce woods in northern Europe, contrasting with its preference for parks and gardens in Ireland and Great Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-Jon78_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jon78-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In southern Iberia, habitat segregation of resident and migrant robins occurs, with resident robins remaining in the same woodlands where they bred.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Attempts to introduce the European robin into Australia and New Zealand in the latter part of the 19th century were unsuccessful. Birds were released around Melbourne, Auckland, Christchurch, Wellington and <a href="/wiki/Dunedin" title="Dunedin">Dunedin</a> by various local <a href="/wiki/Acclimatisation_society" title="Acclimatisation society">acclimatisation societies</a>, with none becoming established. There was a similar outcome in North America, as birds failed to become established after being released in <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>, New York in 1852, Oregon in 1889–1892, and the <a href="/wiki/Saanich_Peninsula" title="Saanich Peninsula">Saanich Peninsula</a> in British Columbia in 1908–1910.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_robin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:European_Robin_(Erithacus_rubecula)_with_mealworm.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/European_Robin_%28Erithacus_rubecula%29_with_mealworm.jpeg/220px-European_Robin_%28Erithacus_rubecula%29_with_mealworm.jpeg" decoding="async" width="220" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/European_Robin_%28Erithacus_rubecula%29_with_mealworm.jpeg/330px-European_Robin_%28Erithacus_rubecula%29_with_mealworm.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/European_Robin_%28Erithacus_rubecula%29_with_mealworm.jpeg/440px-European_Robin_%28Erithacus_rubecula%29_with_mealworm.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="1406" data-file-height="1414" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:European_Robin_(Erithacus_rubecula)_with_mealworm.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Robin with prey</div></div></div>
<p>The robin is <a href="/wiki/Diurnality" title="Diurnality">diurnal</a>, although it has been reported to be active hunting insects on moonlit nights or near artificial light at night.<sup id="cite_ref-Patzold95_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Patzold95-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Well known to British and Irish gardeners, it is relatively unafraid of people and drawn to human activities involving the digging of soil, in order to look out for <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a> and other food freshly turned up. Indeed, the robin is considered to be a gardener's friend and for various folklore reasons the robin would never be harmed. In continental Europe on the other hand, robins were hunted and killed as with most other small birds, and are more wary.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Robins also approach large wild animals, such as wild boar and other animals which disturb the ground, to look for any food that might be brought to the surface. In autumn and winter, robins will supplement their usual diet of terrestrial invertebrates, such as spiders, worms and insects, with berries and fruit.<sup id="cite_ref-Jon78_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jon78-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> They will also eat seed mixtures placed on bird-tables.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Male robins are noted for their highly aggressive territorial behaviour. They will fiercely attack other males and competitors that stray into their territories and have been observed attacking other small birds without apparent provocation. There are instances of robins attacking their own reflection.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Territorial disputes sometimes lead to fatalities, accounting for up to 10% of adult robin deaths in some areas.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Because of high mortality in the first year of life, a robin has an average life expectancy of 1.1&#160;years; however, once past its first year it can expect to live longer and one robin has been recorded as reaching 19&#160;years of age.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>  A spell of very low temperatures in winter may also result in significant mortality.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> This species is parasitised by the <a href="/wiki/Moorhen_flea" title="Moorhen flea">moorhen flea</a> (<i>Dasypsyllus gallinulae</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-Rothschild_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rothschild-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Acanthocephala" title="Acanthocephala">acanthocephalan</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Apororhynchus_silesiacus" class="mw-redirect" title="Apororhynchus silesiacus">Apororhynchus silesiacus</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dimitrova_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dimitrova-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Robins may choose a wide variety of sites for building a nest. In fact, anything which can offer some shelter, like a depression or hole may be considered. As well as the usual crevices, or sheltered banks, other objects include pieces of machinery, barbecues, bicycle handlebars, bristles on upturned brooms, discarded kettles, watering cans, flower pots and hats. The nest is composed of moss, leaves and grass, with fine grass, hair and feathers for lining. Two or three clutches of five or six eggs are laid throughout the breeding season, which commences in March in Britain and Ireland. The eggs are a cream, buff or white speckled or blotched with reddish-brown colour, often more heavily so at the larger end.<sup id="cite_ref-Observerbirdseggs_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Observerbirdseggs-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> When juvenile birds fly from the nests they are mottled brown in colour all over. After two to three months out of the nest, the juvenile bird grows some orange feathers under its chin and over a similar period this patch gradually extends to complete the adult appearance.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocalisations">Vocalisations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_robin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Vocalisations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The robin produces a fluting, warbling <span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:120401-132827_Erithacus_rubecula.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" decoding="async" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a></span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/29/120401-132827_Erithacus_rubecula.ogg" class="internal" title="120401-132827 Erithacus rubecula.ogg">song</a></span>&#160;<small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:120401-132827_Erithacus_rubecula.ogg" title="File:120401-132827 Erithacus rubecula.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span> during the breeding season. Both the male and female sing during the winter, when they hold separate territories, the song then sounding more plaintive than the summer version.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_26-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The female robin moves a short distance from the summer nesting territory to a nearby area that is more suitable for winter feeding.  The male robin keeps the same territory throughout the year. During the breeding season, male robins usually initiate their morning song an hour before civil sunrise, and usually terminate their daily singing around thirty minutes after sunset.<sup id="cite_ref-Behavioral_Ecology_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Behavioral_Ecology-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Nocturnal singing can also occur, especially in urban areas that are artificially lit during the night.<sup id="cite_ref-Behavioral_Ecology_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Behavioral_Ecology-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Under artificial light, nocturnal singing can be used by urban robins to actively shunt daytime anthropogenic noise.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> A variety of calls is also made at any time of year, including a ticking note indicating anxiety or mild alarm.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetoreception">Magnetoreception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_robin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Magnetoreception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The avian magnetic compass of the robin has been extensively researched and uses vision-based <a href="/wiki/Magnetoreception" title="Magnetoreception">magnetoreception</a>, in which the robin's ability to sense the magnetic field of the earth for navigation is affected by the light entering the bird's eye. The physical mechanism of the robin's magnetic sense is not fully understood, but may involve <a href="/wiki/Quantum_entanglement" title="Quantum entanglement">quantum entanglement</a> of electron spins.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The robin features prominently in British folklore and that of northwestern France, but much less so in other parts of Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-ingersoll167_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ingersoll167-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> It was held to be a storm-cloud bird and sacred to <a href="/wiki/Thor" title="Thor">Thor</a>, the god of thunder, in <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse mythology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cooper92_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper92-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Robins feature in the traditional children's tale <i><a href="/wiki/Babes_in_the_Wood" title="Babes in the Wood">Babes in the Wood</a></i>, the birds cover the dead bodies of the children.<sup id="cite_ref-deVries76_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deVries76-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>More recently, the robin has become strongly associated with <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a>, taking a starring role on many <a href="/wiki/Christmas_card" title="Christmas card">Christmas cards</a> since the mid-19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-deVries76_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deVries76-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The robin has appeared on many <a href="/wiki/Christmas_stamp" title="Christmas stamp">Christmas postage stamps</a>. An old British <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folk tale</a> seeks to explain the robin's distinctive breast. Legend has it that when <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> was dying on the cross, the robin, then simply brown in colour, flew to his side and sang into his ear in order to comfort him in his pain. The blood from his wounds stained the robin's breast, and thereafter all robins got the mark of Christ's blood upon them.<sup id="cite_ref-Cooper92_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper92-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An alternative legend has it that its breast was scorched fetching water for souls in Purgatory.<sup id="cite_ref-deVries76_42-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deVries76-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The association with Christmas more probably arises from the fact that postmen in <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Victorian</a> Britain wore red jackets and were nicknamed "Robins"; the robin featured on the Christmas card is an emblem of the postman delivering the card.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1960s, in a vote publicised by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i>, the robin was adopted as the unofficial national bird of the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> In 2015, the robin was again voted Britain's national bird in a poll organised by birdwatcher David Lindo, taking 34% of the final vote.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several English and Welsh sports organisations are nicknamed "the Robins". The nickname is typically used for teams whose home colours predominantly use red. These include the professional football clubs <a href="/wiki/Bristol_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Bristol City">Bristol City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crewe_Alexandra" class="mw-redirect" title="Crewe Alexandra">Crewe Alexandra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swindon_Town" class="mw-redirect" title="Swindon Town">Swindon Town</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham_Town" class="mw-redirect" title="Cheltenham Town">Cheltenham Town</a> (with <a href="/wiki/Bristol_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Bristol City">Bristol City</a> (as of 2019),<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Swindon Town, and Cheltenham Town also incorporating a robin image in their current badge designs), and, traditionally, <a href="/wiki/Wrexham_FC" class="mw-redirect" title="Wrexham FC">Wrexham FC</a>, as well as the English rugby league team the <a href="/wiki/Hull_Kingston_Rovers" title="Hull Kingston Rovers">Hull Kingston Rovers</a> (whose home colours are white with a red band).<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> A small bird is an unusual choice, although it is thought to symbolise agility in darting around the field.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<li id="cite_note-21"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-21">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Although Dietzen <i>et al.</i> (2003) conclude that both the Tenerife and Gran Canaria populations are independently derived from mainland populations and should constitute two species or both be placed in <i>E. rubecula</i> as subspecies, their data does not allow for a definite conclusion. The alternative explanation – that Tenerife was colonised by already-distinct Gran Canaria robins – has not been explored and the proposed model relies only on probabilistic inference. Likewise, the seemingly exact molecular dating is doubtful as it assumes a <a href="/wiki/Molecular_clock" title="Molecular clock">molecular clock</a> that may or may not be correct, and of course the assumption that the ancestor of all robins was similar in colouration to <i>superbus</i> and not the continental birds is, being inferred from their model of colonisation, entirely conjectural.</span>
