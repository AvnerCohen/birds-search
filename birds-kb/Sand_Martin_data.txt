<p>The <b>sand martin</b> (<i>Riparia riparia</i>) or <b>European sand martin</b>, <b>bank swallow</b>, and <b>collared sand martin</b> in India, is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Swallow" title="Swallow">swallow</a> family. It has a wide <a href="/wiki/Range_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Range (biology)">range</a> in summer, embracing practically the whole of Europe and the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a> countries and across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> to the Pacific Ocean. It is a <a href="/wiki/Holarctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Holarctic">Holarctic</a> species also found in North America. It winters in eastern and southern Africa, South America, and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Subcontinent" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian Subcontinent">Indian Subcontinent</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sand_martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a>, and originally named <i>Hirundo riparia</i>; the description consisted of the simple "<i>H[irundo] cinerea, gula abdomineque albis</i>" &#8211; "an ash-grey swallow, with white throat and belly" &#8211; and the <a href="/wiki/Type_locality_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Type locality (biology)">type locality</a> was simply given as "Europa".<sup id="cite_ref-Linnaeus1758_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linnaeus1758-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific name</a> means "of the riverbank"; it is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>ripa</i> "riverbank".<sup id="cite_ref-job_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Pale_martin" title="Pale martin">pale martin</a> of northern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and southeastern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> is now usually split as a separate species <i><b>Riparia diluta</b></i>. It has paler grey-brown upperparts and a less distinct breast band. It winters in <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and southern India.<sup id="cite_ref-Turner1989_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Turner1989-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sand_martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Digesvale.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Digesvale.jpg/220px-Digesvale.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Digesvale.jpg/330px-Digesvale.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Digesvale.jpg/440px-Digesvale.jpg 2x" data-file-width="697" data-file-height="503" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Digesvale.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Showing dark breast band</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Riparia_Riparia-2006-Ejdzej-1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Riparia_Riparia-2006-Ejdzej-1.jpg/220px-Riparia_Riparia-2006-Ejdzej-1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Riparia_Riparia-2006-Ejdzej-1.jpg/330px-Riparia_Riparia-2006-Ejdzej-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Riparia_Riparia-2006-Ejdzej-1.jpg/440px-Riparia_Riparia-2006-Ejdzej-1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1365" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Riparia_Riparia-2006-Ejdzej-1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult at nest site, <a href="/wiki/Dziwn%C3%B3wek" title="Dziwnówek">Dziwnówek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a></div></div></div>
<p>The sand martin is brown above, white below with a narrow brown band on the breast; the bill is black, the legs brown. The young have <a href="/wiki/Rufous" title="Rufous">rufous</a> tips to the <a href="/wiki/Coverts" class="mw-redirect" title="Coverts">coverts</a> and margins to the secondaries.
</p><p>Its brown back, white throat, small size and quick jerky flight separate it at once from similar swallows, such as the <a href="/wiki/Common_house_martin" title="Common house martin">common house martin</a> (<i>Delichon urbicum</i>), the <a href="/wiki/American_cliff_swallow" class="mw-redirect" title="American cliff swallow">American cliff swallow</a> (<i>Petrochelidon pyrrhonota</i>) or other species of <i><a href="/wiki/Riparia" title="Riparia">Riparia</a></i>. Only the <a href="/wiki/Banded_martin" title="Banded martin">banded martin</a> (<i>R. cincta</i>) of <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a> is similar, but the sand martin only occurs there in (the northern) winter.
</p><p><b>Measurements</b><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Length</b>: 4.7-5.5 in (12-14 cm)</li>
<li><b>Weight</b>: 0.4-0.7 oz (10.2-18.8 g)</li>
<li><b>Wingspan</b>: 9.8-13.0 in (25-33 cm)</li></ul>
<p>The sand martin's twittering <a href="/wiki/Birdsong" class="mw-redirect" title="Birdsong">song</a> is continuous when the birds are on the wing and becomes a conversational undertone after they have settled in the roost. The harsh alarm is heard when a passing <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crow</a> or other suspected <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predator</a> requires combined action to drive it away.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sand_martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sand-Martin.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Sand-Martin.jpg/220px-Sand-Martin.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="177" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Sand-Martin.jpg/330px-Sand-Martin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Sand-Martin.jpg/440px-Sand-Martin.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2276" data-file-height="1836" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sand-Martin.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Active breeding colony</div></div></div>
<p>Linnaeus already remarked on this species' breeding habits: <i>Habitat in Europae collibus arenosis abruptis, foramine serpentino</i>&#8212;"it lives in Europe, in winding holes in sheer sandy hills". It has been observed that sand martins favour <a href="/wiki/Loess" title="Loess">loess</a> as a particular type of ground to nest in.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Sand martins are generally found near larger bodies of water, such as <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a> or even the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>, throughout the year.<sup id="cite_ref-Linnaeus1758_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linnaeus1758-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Accordi2006_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Accordi2006-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Britain, the sand martin appears on its breeding grounds as the first of its family, starting towards the end of March, just in advance of the <a href="/wiki/Barn_swallow" title="Barn swallow">barn swallow</a>. In northern <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, they arrive in numbers by mid-April, about 10 days earlier than they did 100 years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-Henninger1906_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Henninger1906-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OOS2004_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OOS2004-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> At first, they flit over the larger bodies of water alone, in search of early flies. Later parties accompany other swallow species, but for a time, varying according to weather, the birds remain at these large waters and do not visit their nesting haunts. The sand martin departs early, at any rate from its more northerly haunts. In August, the gatherings at the nightly roost increase enormously, though the advent and departure of passage birds causes great irregularity in numbers. They are essentially gone from their breeding range by the end of September.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Riparia_riparia-nest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Riparia_riparia-nest.jpg/220px-Riparia_riparia-nest.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Riparia_riparia-nest.jpg/330px-Riparia_riparia-nest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Riparia_riparia-nest.jpg/440px-Riparia_riparia-nest.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2540" data-file-height="1428" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Riparia_riparia-nest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nest with egg</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Riparia_riparia_MWNH_2218.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Riparia_riparia_MWNH_2218.JPG/220px-Riparia_riparia_MWNH_2218.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="191" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Riparia_riparia_MWNH_2218.JPG/330px-Riparia_riparia_MWNH_2218.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Riparia_riparia_MWNH_2218.JPG/440px-Riparia_riparia_MWNH_2218.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2944" data-file-height="2556" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Riparia_riparia_MWNH_2218.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden,_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Museum Wiesbaden, Germany">Museum Wiesbaden, Germany</a></div></div></div>
<p>The food consists of small insects, mostly <a href="/wiki/Gnat" title="Gnat">gnats</a> and other flies whose early stages are <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_animal" title="Aquatic animal">aquatic</a>.
</p><p>The sand martin is <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">sociable in its nesting habits</a>; from a dozen to many hundred pairs will nest close together, according to available space. The <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nests</a> are at the end of tunnels of from a few inches to three or four feet in length, bored in sand or <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a>. The actual nest is a litter of straw and feathers in a chamber at the end of the burrow; it soon becomes a hotbed of parasites. Four or five white <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> are laid about mid-late May, and a second brood is usual in all but the most northernly breeding sites.
</p><p>This is not a rare bird, and it is classified as a species of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a> by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It does have some local protections, as certain populations have declined or face threats from habitat loss and fragmentation. They are considered threatened in California, where populations exist in the <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_Valley" title="Sacramento Valley">Sacramento Valley</a><sup id="cite_ref-Garrison1998_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Garrison1998-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and at two coastal sites, <a href="/wiki/Ano_Nuevo_State_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="Ano Nuevo State Park">Año Nuevo State Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fort_Funston" title="Fort Funston">Fort Funston</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-parks_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-parks-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
