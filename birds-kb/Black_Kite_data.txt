<p>The <b>Black Kite</b> (<i>Milvus migrans</i>) is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> in the family Accipitridae, which also includes many other diurnal <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptors</a>. Unlike others of the group, Black Kites are opportunistic hunters and are more likely to scavenge. They spend a lot of time soaring and gliding in thermals in search of food. Their angled wing and distinctive forked tail make them easy to identify. This <a href="/wiki/Kite_(bird)" title="Kite (bird)">kite</a> is widely distributed through the temperate and tropical parts of <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Oceania</a>, with the temperate region populations tending to be migratory. Several subspecies are recognized and formerly had their own English names. The European populations are small, but the South Asian population is very large.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>Black Kites can be distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Red_Kite" title="Red Kite">Red Kites</a> by the slightly smaller size, less forked tail (visible in flight), and generally dark <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> without any rufous. The sexes are alike. The upper plumage is brown but the head and neck tend to be paler. The patch behind the eye appears darker. The outer flight feathers are black and the feathers have dark cross bars and are mottled at the base. The lower parts of the body are pale brown, becoming lighter towards the chin. The body feathers have dark shafts giving it a streaked appearance. The cere and gape are yellow, but the bill is black (unlike in the <a href="/wiki/Yellow-billed_Kite" title="Yellow-billed Kite">Yellow-billed Kite</a>). The legs are yellow and the claws are black. They have a distinctive shrill whistle followed by a rapid whinnying call.<sup id="cite_ref-whistler_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whistler-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Systematics and taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Systematics_and_taxonomy">Systematics and taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Red_Kite" title="Red Kite">Red Kite</a> has been known to <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridize</a> with the Black Kite (in captivity where both species were kept together, and in the wild on the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_Islands" title="Cape Verde Islands" class="mw-redirect">Cape Verde Islands</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Recent DNA studies suggest that the yellow-billed, African races, <i>parasitus</i> and <i>aegyptius</i>, differ significantly from Black Kites in the Eurasian <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a>, and should be considered as a separate, <a href="/wiki/Allopatric" title="Allopatric" class="mw-redirect">allopatric</a> species <a href="/wiki/Yellow-billed_Kite" title="Yellow-billed Kite">Yellow-billed Kite</a>, <i>M. aegyptius</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> They occur throughout <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> except for the <a href="/wiki/Congo_River" title="Congo River">Congo</a> basin and the <a href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" title="Sahara Desert" class="mw-redirect">Sahara Desert</a>. There have been some suggestions that the Black-eared Kite (<i>M. m. lineatus</i>) should be elevated to full species status as <i>M. lineatus</i>, but this is not well supported.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i>Milvus migrans migrans</i> <small>(<a href="/wiki/Pieter_Boddaert" title="Pieter Boddaert">Boddaert</a>, 1783)</small>: <b>European Black Kite</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Breeds central, southern and eastern Europe to <a href="/wiki/Tien_Shan" title="Tien Shan" class="mw-redirect">Tien Shan</a> and south to northwest <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>. Winters in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>. The head is whitish.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>Milvus migrans lineatus</i> <small>(<a href="/wiki/John_Edward_Gray" title="John Edward Gray">J. E. Gray</a>, 1831)</small>: <b>Black-eared Kite</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Amurland" title="Amurland" class="mw-redirect">Amurland</a> S around <a href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya" class="mw-redirect">Himalaya</a> to N <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, N <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">Indochina</a> and S <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>; <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. Northern inland birds migrate to E <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> coast and S Asia in winter. This has a larger pale carpal patch.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>Milvus migrans govinda</i> <small>(<a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Sykes" title="William Henry Sykes">Sykes</a>, 1832)</small>: <b>Small Indian Kite</b> (formerly <b>Pariah Kite</b>)</li>
</ul>

<dd>Eastern <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> east through tropical <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> to Indochina and <a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">Malay Peninsula</a>. The name <i>Pariah</i> originates from the Indian caste system and usage of this name is deprecated.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></dd>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Black_Kite_June09.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Black_Kite_June09.jpg/220px-Black_Kite_June09.jpg" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Black_Kite_June09.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Black Kite in flight, <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a> <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Milvus_migrans_head.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Milvus_migrans_head.jpg/220px-Milvus_migrans_head.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Milvus_migrans_head.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>Milvus migrans'</i> head</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The species is found in Europe, Asia, Africa and Australia. The temperate populations of this kite tend to be migratory while the tropical ones are resident. <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> and central <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> birds (<a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> <i>M. m. milvus</i> and <b>Black-eared Kite</b> <i>M. m. lineatus</i>, respectively) are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, moving to the tropics in winter, but races in warmer regions such as the <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> <i>M. m. govinda</i> (<b>Pariah Kite</b>), or the Australasian <i>M. m. affinis</i> (<b>Fork-tailed Kite</b>), are resident. In some areas such as in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the Black Kite occurs only as a wanderer on migration. These birds are usually of the nominate race, but in November 2006 a juvenile of the eastern <i>lineatus</i>, not previously recorded in western Europe, was found in <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The species is not found in the <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesian</a> <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> between the <a href="/wiki/South_East_Asia" title="South East Asia" class="mw-redirect">South East Asian</a> mainland and the <a href="/wiki/Wallace_Line" title="Wallace Line">Wallace Line</a>. Vagrants, most likely of the Black-eared Kite, on occasion range far into the <a href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific" class="mw-redirect">Pacific</a>, out to the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_islands" title="Hawaiian islands" class="mw-redirect">Hawaiian islands</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from July 2010">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Milvus_migrans_front(ThKraft).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Milvus_migrans_front%28ThKraft%29.jpg/220px-Milvus_migrans_front%28ThKraft%29.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Milvus_migrans_front(ThKraft).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>Milvus migrans migrans</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In India, the population of <i>M. m. govinda</i> is particularly large especially in areas of high human population. Here the birds avoid heavily forested regions. A survey in 1967 in the 150 square kilometres of the city of New Delhi produced an estimate of about 2200 pairs or roughly 15 per square kilometer.<sup id="cite_ref-whistler_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whistler-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Food and foraging">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_foraging">Food and foraging</span></h3>
<p>Black Kites are most often seen gliding and soaring on thermals as they search for food. The flight is buoyant and the bird glides with ease, changing directions easily. They will swoop down with their legs lowered to snatch small live prey, <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, household refuse and <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">carrion</a>, for which behaviour they are known in British <a href="/wiki/Military_slang" title="Military slang">military slang</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Shite-hawk" title="Shite-hawk">shite-hawk</a>. They are opportunist hunters and have been known to take birds, bats and rodents.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> They are attracted to smoke and fires, where they seek escaping prey.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> This behaviour has led to Australian native beliefs that kites spread fires by picking up burning twigs and dropping them on dry grass.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The Indian populations are well adapted to living in cities and are found in densely populated areas. Large numbers may be seen soaring in <a href="/wiki/Thermal" title="Thermal">thermals</a> over cities. In some places they will readily swoop and snatch food held by humans.<sup id="cite_ref-whistler_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whistler-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbk_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Black Kites in Spain prey on nestling waterfowl especially during summer to feed their young. Predation of nests of other pairs of Black Kites has also been noted.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Kites have also been seen to tear and carry away the nests of <a href="/wiki/Baya_Weaver" title="Baya Weaver">Baya Weavers</a> in an attempt to obtain eggs or chicks.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>In winter, kites form large communal roosts. Flocks may fly about before settling at the roost.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> When migrating, the Black Kite has a greater propensity to form large flocks than other migratory raptors, particularly prior to making a crossing across water.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> In India, the subspecies <i>govinda</i> shows large seasonal fluctuations with the highest numbers seen from July to October, after the Monsoons, and it has been suggested that they make local movements in response to high rainfall.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Milvus_migrans_MHNT.ZOO.2006.11.86.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Milvus_migrans_MHNT.ZOO.2006.11.86.jpg/220px-Milvus_migrans_MHNT.ZOO.2006.11.86.jpg" width="220" height="189" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bird_of_prey_in_Hong_Kong.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Bird_of_prey_in_Hong_Kong.jpg/220px-Bird_of_prey_in_Hong_Kong.jpg" width="220" height="185" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bird_of_prey_in_Hong_Kong.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

</div>
</div>
<p>The breeding season of Black Kites in India begins in winter, the young birds fledging before the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoons</a>. The nest is a rough platform of twigs and rags placed in a tree. Nest sites may be reused in subsequent years. European birds breed in summer. Birds in the Italian Alps tended to build their nest close to water in steep cliffs or tall trees.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Nest orientation may be related to wind and rainfall.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The nests may sometimes be decorated with bright materials such as white plastic and a study in Spain suggests that they may have a role in signalling to keep away other kites.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> After pairing, the male frequently copulates with the female. Unguarded females may be approached by other males, and extra pair copulations are frequent. Males returning from a foraging trip will frequently copulate on return, as this increases the chances of his sperm fertilizing the eggs rather than a different male.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Both the male and female take part in nest building, incubation and care of chicks. The typical clutch size is 2 or sometimes 3 eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> The incubation period varies from 30–34 days. Chicks of the Indian population stayed at the nest for nearly two months.<sup id="cite_ref-delhi_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-delhi-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Chicks hatched later in European populations appeared to fledge faster. The care of young by the parents also rapidly decreased with the need for adults to migrate.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Siblings show aggression to each other and often the weaker chick may be killed, but parent birds were found to preferentially feed the smaller chicks in experimentally altered nests.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Newly hatched young have down (prepennae) which are sepia on the back and black around the eye and buff on the head, neck and underparts. This is replaced by brownish gray second down (preplumulae). After 9 to 12 days the second down appears on the whole body except the top of the head. Body feathers begin to appear after 18 to 22 days. The feathers on the head become noticeable from the 24th to 29th day. The nestlings initially feed on food fallen at the bottom of the nest and begin to tear flesh after 33–39 days. They are able to stand on their legs after 17–19 days and begin flapping their wings after 27–31 days. After 50 days they begin to move to branches next to the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Birds are able to breed after their second year.<sup id="cite_ref-delhi_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-delhi-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Parent birds guard their nest and will dive aggressively at intruders. Humans who intrude the nest appear to be recognized by birds and singled out for dive attacks.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mortality factors">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Mortality_factors">Mortality factors</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Blac_Kite_I4-_Kolkata_IMG_1159.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Blac_Kite_I4-_Kolkata_IMG_1159.jpg/220px-Blac_Kite_I4-_Kolkata_IMG_1159.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Blac_Kite_I4-_Kolkata_IMG_1159.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>Milvus migrans govinda</i> calling</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Black-eared Kites in Japan were found to accumulate nearly 70% of mercury accumulated from polluted food in the feathers, thus excreting it in the moult process.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Black Kites often perch on electric wires and are frequent victims of electrocution.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> Their habit of swooping to pick up dead <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Roadkill" title="Roadkill">roadkill</a> leads to collisions with vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> Instances of mass poisoning as a result of feeding on poisoned voles in agricultural fields have been noted.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> They are also a major nuisance at some <a href="/wiki/Airport" title="Airport">airports</a>, where their size makes them a significant <a href="/wiki/Birdstrike" title="Birdstrike" class="mw-redirect">birdstrike</a> hazard.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Like most bird species, they have parasites, several species of endoparasitic <a href="/wiki/Trematode" title="Trematode" class="mw-redirect">trematodes</a> are known<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> and some <a href="/wiki/Digenea" title="Digenea">Digenea</a> species that are transmitted via fishes.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Birds with abnormal development of a secondary upper mandible have been recorded in <i>govinda</i><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> and <i>lineatus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
