<p>The <b>Semipalmated Sandpiper</b> (<i>Calidris pusilla</i>) is a very small <a href="/wiki/Shorebird" title="Shorebird" class="mw-redirect">shorebird</a>. It is sometimes separated with other "<a href="/wiki/Stint" title="Stint">stints</a>" in <i>Erolia</i> but although these apparently form a <a href="/wiki/Monophyletic" title="Monophyletic" class="mw-redirect">monophyletic</a> group, the present species' old <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i>Ereunetes</i> had been proposed before <i>Erolia</i>.</p>
<p>Adults have black legs and a short stout straight dark bill. The body is dark grey-brown on top and white underneath. The head and neck are tinged light grey-brown. This bird can be difficult to distinguish from other similar tiny shorebirds, in particular the <a href="/wiki/Western_Sandpiper" title="Western Sandpiper">Western Sandpiper</a>; these are known collectively as "peeps" or "<a href="/wiki/Stint" title="Stint">stints</a>".</p>
<p>Their breeding habitat is the southern tundra in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> near water. They nest on the ground. The male makes several shallow scrapes, the female chooses one and adds grass and other material to line the nest. The female lays 4 eggs; the male assists in <a href="/wiki/Incubate_(bird)" title="Incubate (bird)" class="mw-redirect">incubation</a>. After a few days, the female leaves the young with the male; the young feed themselves.</p>
<p>They are long distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrants</a> and winter in coastal <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> with some going to the southern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. They migrate in flocks which can number in the hundreds of thousands, particularly in favoured feeding locations such as the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Fundy" title="Bay of Fundy">Bay of Fundy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Bay" title="Delaware Bay">Delaware Bay</a>. This species is a rare but regular vagrant to western Europe.</p>
<p>These birds forage on mudflats, picking up food by sight and feel (bill). They mainly eat aquatic insects and crustaceans.</p>
<p>Although very numerous, these birds are highly dependent on a few key stopover habitats during their migration, notably, <a href="/wiki/Shepody_Bay" title="Shepody Bay">Shepody Bay</a>, an arm of the Bay of Fundy <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.atl.ec.gc.ca/wildlife/ramsar/shepody.html">[1]</a>.</p>
<p>During the months of July and August you can go to one of two information centers run by the Nature Conservancy of Canada about the shorebirds in either Johnson's Mills or Mary's point.</p>
