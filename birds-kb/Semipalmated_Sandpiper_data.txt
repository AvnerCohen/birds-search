<p>The <b>semipalmated sandpiper</b> (<i>Calidris pusilla</i>) is a very small <a href="/wiki/Shorebird" class="mw-redirect" title="Shorebird">shorebird</a>. The genus name is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>kalidris</i> or <i>skalidris</i>, a term used by <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> for some grey-coloured waterside birds. The specific <i>pusilla</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "very small".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is sometimes separated with other "<a href="/wiki/Stint" title="Stint">stints</a>" in <i>Erolia,</i> but, although these apparently form a <a href="/wiki/Monophyletic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monophyletic">monophyletic</a> group, the present species' old <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i>Ereunetes</i> had been proposed before <i>Erolia</i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semipalmated_sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is a small sandpiper, 13–15&#160;cm (5.1-5.9&#160;in) long and weighing around 20-32&#160;<a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">g</a> (0.7-1.1&#160;<a href="/wiki/Ounce" title="Ounce">oz</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Wingspan ranges from 13.8-14.6 in (35-37 cm)<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>. Adults have black legs and a short, stout, straight dark bill. The body is dark grey-brown on top and white underneath. The head and neck are tinged light grey-brown. This bird can be difficult to distinguish from other similar tiny shorebirds, in particular the <a href="/wiki/Western_sandpiper" title="Western sandpiper">western sandpiper</a>; these are known collectively as "peeps" or "<a href="/wiki/Stint" title="Stint">stints</a>".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding_and_habitat">Breeding and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semipalmated_sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Breeding and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Their breeding habitat is the southern tundra in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> near water. They nest on the ground. The male makes several shallow scrapes; the female chooses one and adds grass and other material to line the nest. The female lays 4 eggs; the male assists in <a href="/wiki/Incubate_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Incubate (bird)">incubation</a>. After a few days, the female leaves the young with the male; the young feed themselves.
</p><p>These birds forage on mudflats, picking up food by sight and feel (bill). They mainly eat aquatic insects and crustaceans.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_and_migration">Status and migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semipalmated_sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Status and migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>They are long distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrants</a> and winter in coastal <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, with some going to the southern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>. They migrate in flocks which can number in the hundreds of thousands, particularly in favoured feeding locations such as the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Fundy" title="Bay of Fundy">Bay of Fundy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Bay" title="Delaware Bay">Delaware Bay</a>. This species is a rare but regular vagrant to western Europe.
</p><p>Although very numerous, these birds are highly dependent on a few key stopover habitats during their migration, notably <a href="/wiki/Mary%27s_Point" title="Mary&#39;s Point">Mary's Point</a> and Johnson's Mills along <a href="/wiki/Shepody_Bay" title="Shepody Bay">Shepody Bay</a>, an arm of the Bay of Fundy.<sup id="cite_ref-envCan_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-envCan-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> During the months of July and August, the Nature Conservancy of Canada runs an information center about these shorebirds in Johnson's Mills, New Brunswick.<sup id="cite_ref-natureCon_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-natureCon-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
