<p>The <b>northern shoveler</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ʃ/: &#39;sh&#39; in &#39;shy&#39;">ʃ</span><span title="/ʌ/: &#39;u&#39; in &#39;cut&#39;">ʌ</span><span title="&#39;v&#39; in &#39;vie&#39;">v</span><span title="/əl/: &#39;le&#39; in &#39;bottle&#39;">əl</span><span title="/ər/: &#39;er&#39; in &#39;letter&#39;">ər</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <i>Spatula clypeata</i>), known simply in Britain as the <b>shoveler</b>, is a common and widespread <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">duck</a>. It breeds in northern areas of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> and across most of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Clements_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clements-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> wintering in <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">southern Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, and northern <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. It is a rare vagrant to <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. In <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, it breeds along the southern edge of <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Bay" title="Hudson Bay">Hudson Bay</a> and west of this body of water, and as far south as the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> west to <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Floyd_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Floyd-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dunn_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunn-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Northern shoveler is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.<sup id="cite_ref-AEWA_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AEWA-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The conservation status of this bird is <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_shoveler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The northern shoveler was first <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">formally described</a> by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">tenth edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>. He introduced the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Anas clypeata</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogentic" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular phylogentic">molecular phylogentic</a> study comparing <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> sequences published in 2009 found that the genus <i>Anas</i>, as then defined, was <a href="/wiki/Monophyletic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monophyletic">non-monophyletic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  The genus was subsequently split into four monophyletic genera with ten species including the northern shoveler moved into the resurrected genus <i><a href="/wiki/Spatula_(genus)" title="Spatula (genus)">Spatula</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This genus had been originally proposed by the German zoologist <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Boie" title="Friedrich Boie">Friedrich Boie</a> in 1822.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The name <i>Spatula</i> is the Latin for a "spoon" or "spatula". The specific epithet is derived from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>clypeata</i>, "shield-bearing" (from <i>clypeus</i>, "shield").<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>No living <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are accepted today. <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">Fossil</a> bones of a very similar duck have been found in <a href="/wiki/Early_Pleistocene" title="Early Pleistocene">Early Pleistocene</a> deposits at Dursunlu, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>. It is unresolved, however, how these birds were related to the northern shoveler of today; i.e., whether the differences noted were due to being a related <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> or <a href="/wiki/Paleosubspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleosubspecies">paleosubspecies</a>, or attributable to individual variation.<sup id="cite_ref-Louchart_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Louchart-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_shoveler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species is unmistakable in the northern hemisphere due to its large spatulate bill. The breeding <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/drake" class="extiw" title="wikt:drake">drake</a> has an iridescent dark green head,<sup id="cite_ref-du_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-du-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> white breast and chestnut belly and flanks. In flight, pale blue forewing feathers are revealed, separated from the green <a href="/wiki/Speculum_feathers" title="Speculum feathers">speculum</a> by a white border. In early fall the male will have a white crescent on each side of the face.<sup id="cite_ref-Dunn_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunn-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In non-breeding (eclipse) plumage, the drake resembles the female.
</p><p>The female is a drab mottled brown like other dabblers,<sup id="cite_ref-du_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-du-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> with plumage much like a female <a href="/wiki/Mallard" title="Mallard">mallard</a>, but easily distinguished by the long broad bill, which is gray tinged with orange on cutting edge and lower <a href="/wiki/Mandible" title="Mandible">mandible</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dunn_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunn-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The female's forewing is gray.
</p><p>They are 48&#160;cm (19&#160;in) long and have a wingspan of 76&#160;cm (30&#160;in) with a weight of 600&#160;g (1.3&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-Floyd_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Floyd-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_shoveler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anas_clypeata_3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Anas_clypeata_3.jpg/220px-Anas_clypeata_3.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Anas_clypeata_3.jpg/330px-Anas_clypeata_3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Anas_clypeata_3.jpg/440px-Anas_clypeata_3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anas_clypeata_3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In flight</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Spinus-northern-shoveler-2015-01-n025006-w.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Spinus-northern-shoveler-2015-01-n025006-w.jpg/220px-Spinus-northern-shoveler-2015-01-n025006-w.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="122" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Spinus-northern-shoveler-2015-01-n025006-w.jpg/330px-Spinus-northern-shoveler-2015-01-n025006-w.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Spinus-northern-shoveler-2015-01-n025006-w.jpg/440px-Spinus-northern-shoveler-2015-01-n025006-w.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1888" data-file-height="1048" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Spinus-northern-shoveler-2015-01-n025006-w.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Large groups of northern shovelers swim rapidly in circles to collect food from the surface by creating a funnel effect.</div></div></div>
<p>Northern shovelers feed by dabbling for plant food, often by swinging its bill from side to side and using the bill to strain food from the water. They use their highly specialized bill (from which their name is derived) to forage for aquatic invertebrates – a carnivorous diet. Their wide-flat bill is equipped with well-developed lamellae – small, comb-like structures on the edge of the bill that act like sieves, allowing the birds to skim crustaceans and plankton from the water's surface. This adaptation, more specialized in shovelers, gives them an advantage over other puddle ducks, with which they do not have to compete for food resources during most of the year. Thus, mud-bottomed marshes rich in invertebrate life are their habitat of choices.<sup id="cite_ref-du_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-du-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The shoveler prefers to nest in grassy areas away from open water. Their nest is a shallow depression on the ground, lined with plant material and down. Hens typically lay about nine eggs. The drakes are very territorial during breeding season and will defend their territory and partners from competing males. Drakes also engage in elaborate courtship behaviors, both on the water and in the air; it is not uncommon for a dozen or more males to pursue a single hen. Despite their stout appearance, shovelers are nimble fliers.<sup id="cite_ref-du_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-du-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This is a fairly quiet species. The male has a clunking call, whereas the female has a Mallard-like quack.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_range">Habitat and range</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_shoveler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Habitat and range">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anas_clypeata_MWNH_1990.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Anas_clypeata_MWNH_1990.JPG/220px-Anas_clypeata_MWNH_1990.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="170" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Anas_clypeata_MWNH_1990.JPG/330px-Anas_clypeata_MWNH_1990.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Anas_clypeata_MWNH_1990.JPG/440px-Anas_clypeata_MWNH_1990.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3696" data-file-height="2848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anas_clypeata_MWNH_1990.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>This is a bird of open wetlands, such as wet grassland or marshes with some <a href="/wiki/Emergent_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Emergent plant">emergent vegetation</a>. It breeds in wide areas across Eurasia, western North America and the Great Lakes region of the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This bird winters in southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Subcontinent" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian Subcontinent">Indian Subcontinent</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malay_Archipelago" title="Malay Archipelago">Malay Archipelago</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Clements_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clements-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and other areas. Those wintering in the Indian Subcontinent make the taxing journey over the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>, often taking a break in wetlands just south of the Himalaya before continuing further south to warmer regions. In <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> it winters south of a line from <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a> to <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a> and from <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a> east to <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>, also along the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">Eastern Seaboard</a> as far north as <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Floyd_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Floyd-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dunn_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunn-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, home to more than 20% of the North Western European population, it is best known as a winter visitor, although it is more frequently seen in southern and eastern England, especially around the <a href="/wiki/Ouse_Washes" title="Ouse Washes">Ouse Washes</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Humber" title="Humber">Humber</a> and the North <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a> Marshes, and in much smaller numbers in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and western parts of England. In winter, breeding birds move south, and are replaced by an influx of continental birds from further north. It breeds across most of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, but the population there is very difficult to assess. Surveys in 2017 and 2018 suggest that it is more common and widespread  in Ireland 
than previously thought.
</p><p>It is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and winters further south than its breeding range. It has occasionally been reported as a vagrant as far south as Australia, New Zealand and South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> It is not as gregarious as some dabbling ducks outside the breeding season and tends to form only small flocks. Among North America's duck species, northern shovelers trail only mallards and blue-winged teal in overall abundance. Their populations have been healthy since the 1960s, and have soared in recent years to more than 5 million birds (2015), most likely because of favorable breeding, migration, and wintering habitat conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-du_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-du-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
