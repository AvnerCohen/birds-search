<p>The <b>Wallcreeper</b> (<i>Tichodroma muraria</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> found throughout the high mountains of <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>. It is the <a href="/wiki/Monotypic_taxon" title="Monotypic taxon">only member</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><b>Tichodroma</b></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Wallcreeper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and etymology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_etymology">Taxonomy and etymology</span></h2>
<p>There is some disagreement among <a href="/wiki/Ornithologist" title="Ornithologist" class="mw-redirect">ornithologists</a> as to where the Wallcreeper belongs in the taxonomic order. Initially, <a href="/wiki/Linnaeus" title="Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> put it in the family Certhiidae, with the <a href="/wiki/Treecreeper" title="Treecreeper">treecreepers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lack_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lack-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The Wallcreeper is placed in a monotypic family Tichodromadidae by, amongst others, Clements 2007, while other authorities such as Dickinson 2003 put it in the monotypic Tichodromadinae, a subfamily of the <a href="/wiki/Nuthatch" title="Nuthatch">nuthatch</a> family Sittidae. In either case, it is very closely related to the nuthatches.</p>
<p>There are two subspecies of Wallcreeper:<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>T. m. muraria</i></li>
<li><i>T. m. nipalensis</i> is found from <a href="/wiki/Turkmenistan" title="Turkmenistan">Turkmenistan</a> east. It is slightly darker than the nominate race.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Wallcreeper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The Wallcreeper is a 15.5–17 centimetres (6.1–6.7 in) long bird, with a mass of 17–19 grams (0.60–0.67 oz). Its <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> is primarily blue-grey, with darker flight and tail feathers. Its most striking plumage feature, though, are its extraordinary crimson wings. Largely hidden when the wings are folded, this bright coloring covers most of the covert feathers, and the basal half of the primaries and secondaries.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Wallcreeper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>A bird of the high <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountains</a>, the Wallcreeper breeds at elevations ranging between 1,000–3,000 metres (3,300–9,800 ft).<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> It is largely resident across its <a href="/wiki/Range_(biology)" title="Range (biology)">range</a>, but is known to move to lower elevations in winter, when it is occasionally found on buildings and in quarries. Birds have wintered as far afield as England and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, where one spent two consecutive winters between 1989 and 1991 at the <a href="/wiki/Vrije_Universiteit" title="Vrije Universiteit">Vrije Universiteit</a> in <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The species is resident across much of the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>, ranging across <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Wallcreeper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span></h2>
<p>This species can be quite tame, but is often surprisingly difficult to see on mountain faces. While it may be confiding in the breeding and non-breeding seasons, and vagrant birds especially are extremely tame, they will still hide when they are aware of being watched, and will hesitate before entering the nest and even take roundabout routes towards the nest during prolonged observations.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Wallcreepers are territorial, and pairs vigorously defend their breeding territory during the summer. During the winter the Wallcreeper is solitary, with males and females defending individual feeding territories. The size of these feeding territories is hard to estimate but may comprise a single large quarry or rock massif; or, alternatively, a series of smaller quarries and rock faces. Wallcreepers may travel some distances from roosting sites to feeding territories. They have also been demonstrated showing site fidelity to winter feeding territories in consecutive years.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Wallcreeper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<p>The Wallcreeper is an <a href="/wiki/Insectivore" title="Insectivore">insectivore</a>, feeding on terrestrial invertebrates—primarily insects and <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a>—gleaned from rock faces.<sup id="cite_ref-Handbook_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Handbook-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> It sometimes also chases flying insects in short <a href="/wiki/Sallying" title="Sallying" class="mw-redirect">sallies</a> from a rock wall perch. Feeding birds move across a cliff face in short flights and quick hops, often with their wings partially spread.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Wallcreeper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<p>The female Wallcreeper builds a <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">cup nest</a> of <a href="/wiki/Grass" title="Grass">grass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">moss</a>, sheltered deep in a rock crevice, hole or cave.<sup id="cite_ref-Handbook_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Handbook-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The nest is lined with softer materials, often including <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Lack_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lack-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> and typically has two entrances. The female usually lays 4–5 <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a>, though <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutches</a> as small as three have been found. The white eggs measure 21&#160;mm long, and are marked with a small number of black or reddish-brown speckles. Once her entire clutch has been laid, the female <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" title="Avian incubation" class="mw-redirect">incubates</a> the eggs for 19–20 days, until they hatch. During incubation, she is regularly fed by her mate.<sup id="cite_ref-Lack_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lack-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Young are <a href="/wiki/Altricial" title="Altricial">altricial</a>, which means they are blind, featherless and helpless at birth. Both parents feed the nestlings for a period of 28–30 days, until the young birds fledge. Each pair raises a single brood a year.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Wallcreeper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span></h3>
<p>Though largely silent, both male and female Wallcreepers <a href="/wiki/Bird_call" title="Bird call" class="mw-redirect">sing</a>, the females generally only while defending feeding territories in the winter.<sup id="cite_ref-Handbook_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Handbook-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The song is a high-pitched, drawn-out whistle, with notes that alternately rise and fall.<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> During the breeding season, the male sings while perched or climbing.</p>
