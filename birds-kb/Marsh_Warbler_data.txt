<p>The <b>marsh warbler</b> (<i>Acrocephalus palustris</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">Old World warbler</a> currently classified in the family <a href="/wiki/Acrocephalidae" title="Acrocephalidae">Acrocephalidae</a>. It breeds in temperate Europe and the western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> and winters mainly in south east Africa. It is notable for incorporating striking imitations of a wide variety of other birds into its song.
</p><p>The marsh warbler breeds in a variety of mostly damp habitats, but in Africa winters mainly in dry, well-vegetated areas.  It is common over much of its breeding range and expanding its distribution in some areas.  However, in Britain it is now virtually extinct as a breeding bird.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a> warbler can be easily confused with several close relatives, but the imitative song of the male is highly distinctive.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_taxonomy">Description and taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marsh_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description and taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is a medium-sized warbler. It is very similar in appearance to several other <a href="/wiki/Acrocephalus_(bird)" title="Acrocephalus (bird)">acrocephaline</a> warblers, such as the <a href="/wiki/Reed_warbler" class="mw-redirect" title="Reed warbler">reed warbler</a> which also occurs in wetlands and has a similar breeding range. The male's distinctive song is useful for identification, as no other member of the genus mimics other birds to any significant extent.  The marsh warbler also tends to avoid the stands of pure <a href="/wiki/Phragmites" title="Phragmites">reed</a> which are the reed warbler's favoured habitat.<sup id="cite_ref-BWPi_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWPi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The species is <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monotypic">monotypic</a>, and there is no significant geographical variation. The sexes are alike in appearance.  <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">Hybridisation</a> with both reed warbler and <a href="/wiki/Blyth%27s_reed_warbler" title="Blyth&#39;s reed warbler">Blyth's reed warbler</a> has been occasionally recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-BWPi_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWPi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The genus name <i>Acrocephalus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>akros</i>, "highest", and <i>kephale</i>, "head". It is possible that <a href="/wiki/Johann_Andreas_Naumann" title="Johann Andreas Naumann">Naumann</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Naumann" title="Johann Friedrich Naumann">Naumann</a> thought <i>akros</i> meant "sharp-pointed". The specific <i>palustris</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means "marshy".<sup id="cite_ref-job_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marsh_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The marsh warbler breeds in the middle latitudes of Europe and western Asia, from the English Channel to about 70 degrees east.  It mainly occupies areas with a <a href="/wiki/Continental_climate" title="Continental climate">continental climate</a>, but breeds, or has bred, in Britain and northern France as well. it is principally a bird of the lowlands, but occurs at altitudes of up to 3000m in Georgia.  In recent decades it has expanded its range to the north, with increasing numbers of birds breeding in Scandinavia and north-west Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-BWPi_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWPi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Singing male are occasionally heard in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, most recently  in  2017.
</p><p>In western Europe the marsh warbler breeds mainly in rank vegetation on damp or seasonally flooded soils, and is particularly attracted to tall herbaceous vegetation such as <a href="/wiki/Stinging_nettle" class="mw-redirect" title="Stinging nettle">nettles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Filipendula_ulmaria" title="Filipendula ulmaria">meadowsweet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epilobium" title="Epilobium">willowherbs</a> and to young <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">osiers</a> and other low woody plants. It may breed in urban <a href="/wiki/Brownfield" class="mw-redirect" title="Brownfield">brownfield</a> sites with suitable vegetation, for instance in Berlin, and also occasionally in arable crops.  In the eastern part of its range, it breeds on dry hillsides with shrubs and in open woodland, as well as the kind of damper habitats it frequents in the west.<sup id="cite_ref-BWPi_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWPi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Marsh Warbler winters mainly in south-east Africa, from Cape Province north to Zambia and Malawi. It makes use of a range of well-vegetated habitats, from moist scrub to dense thickets and <a href="/wiki/Woodland_edge" title="Woodland edge">woodland edge</a>, at altitudes of up to 2400m.<sup id="cite_ref-BWPi_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWPi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Marsh warblers tend to <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> from Europe to Africa via the Middle East, with many crossing Arabia and arriving in Africa on Sudan's <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> coast. Adults usually leave their breeding grounds soon after their young are independent, with their offspring following about two weeks later. On the Red Sea coast most birds arrive from mid-August to mid-September, with numbers of adults peaking in August and of young birds in September. Birds tend to spend much of the autumn somewhere in north-east or east Africa, before continuing south to arrive on their wintering grounds in December or January.<sup id="cite_ref-BWPi_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWPi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In spring, marsh warblers leave their wintering grounds in March or April. They are thought to follow broadly similar routes to their autumn migration. Birds breeding in south-east Europe, for instance on the Black Sea coast, may arrive there by late April.  In other parts of their range, the majority of birds do not arrive until mid-May. On the western and northern edge of their range, for instance in England, birds do not tend to arrive until the end of May or early June.<sup id="cite_ref-BWPi_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWPi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As a <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrant</a>, the species has been recorded as far away as Iceland and Madeira.<sup id="cite_ref-BWPi_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWPi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_in_Britain">Status in Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marsh_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Status in Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Britain the species was never widespread, and disappeared from many areas from the 1930s onwards.<sup id="cite_ref-BWPi_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWPi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> By the 1970s marsh warblers bred in significant numbers only in Worcestershire, where 40-70 pairs were recorded each year during that decade.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This population was effectively extinct by the end of the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> From the 1970s and 1980s onwards, a very small population slowly developed in south-east England.  However, this population is also now close to extinction.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marsh_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_behaviour_and_breeding">Social behaviour and breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marsh_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Social behaviour and breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Marsh_Warbler_pho_0069.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Marsh_Warbler_pho_0069.jpg/250px-Marsh_Warbler_pho_0069.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Marsh_Warbler_pho_0069.jpg/375px-Marsh_Warbler_pho_0069.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Marsh_Warbler_pho_0069.jpg/500px-Marsh_Warbler_pho_0069.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="651" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Marsh_Warbler_pho_0069.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Marsh warbler with a cuckoo nestling.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Acrocephalus_palustris_MWNH_1715.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Acrocephalus_palustris_MWNH_1715.JPG/220px-Acrocephalus_palustris_MWNH_1715.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Acrocephalus_palustris_MWNH_1715.JPG/330px-Acrocephalus_palustris_MWNH_1715.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Acrocephalus_palustris_MWNH_1715.JPG/440px-Acrocephalus_palustris_MWNH_1715.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3464" data-file-height="2216" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Acrocephalus_palustris_MWNH_1715.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>The species is usually <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" class="mw-redirect" title="Monogamous">monogamous</a>. Marsh warblers tend to choose new mates each year and do not necessarily return to breed in the same area as previous years. On their breeding grounds they are <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territorial</a>, with territories often grouped into loose colonies. In Africa, they are essentially solitary, and may defend territories to some extent.<sup id="cite_ref-BWPi_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWPi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The nest is a cup, made mostly from leaves and plant stems, and is usually in dense vegetation, at varying heights. Three to six eggs are laid. Both sexes bring food to the nestlings.<sup id="cite_ref-BWPi_3-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWPi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In continental Europe at least, the species has a short breeding season, of 52–55 days.<sup id="cite_ref-Leisler_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leisler-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In some areas, such as Bulgaria, marsh warblers suffer significant levels of <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasitism" class="mw-redirect" title="Brood parasitism">parasitism</a> by <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common cuckoos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Song">Song</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marsh_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Song">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bosrietzanger_Acrocephalus_palustris_Jos_Zwarts_3.tiff" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Bosrietzanger_Acrocephalus_palustris_Jos_Zwarts_3.tiff/lossy-page1-220px-Bosrietzanger_Acrocephalus_palustris_Jos_Zwarts_3.tiff.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="298" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Bosrietzanger_Acrocephalus_palustris_Jos_Zwarts_3.tiff/lossy-page1-330px-Bosrietzanger_Acrocephalus_palustris_Jos_Zwarts_3.tiff.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Bosrietzanger_Acrocephalus_palustris_Jos_Zwarts_3.tiff/lossy-page1-440px-Bosrietzanger_Acrocephalus_palustris_Jos_Zwarts_3.tiff.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1900" data-file-height="2575" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bosrietzanger_Acrocephalus_palustris_Jos_Zwarts_3.tiff" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Drawing by Jos Zwarts</div></div></div>
<p>The marsh warbler is best known for the highly imitative song uttered by males, and very occasionally by females. Each male marsh warbler incorporates imitations of a wide range of other birds into its song. Other <a href="/wiki/Passerines" class="mw-redirect" title="Passerines">passerines</a> are most commonly imitated, but the calls of other kinds of bird such as <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">waders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hornbill" title="Hornbill">hornbills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Columbidae" title="Columbidae">pigeons</a> have been noted too. On average, each male bird incorporates imitations of 75 other species into its song, with rather more African than northern species mimicked. All learning seems to take place in the summer the bird is hatched in Europe or Asia, and in its first winter in Africa. The calls of birds heard in subsequent years are not added to the warbler's repertoire.<sup id="cite_ref-BWPi_3-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWPi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Females may utter a simple, non-imitative song, and a range of other calls are also known.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet_and_feeding_behaviour">Diet and feeding behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marsh_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diet and feeding behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The marsh warbler is mostly insectivorous, also taking some spiders and small numbers of snails. It generally gleans insects from vegetation but sometimes catches them on the ground or in mid-air. In autumn small numbers of berries may be eaten.  There have been no detailed studies of the bird's diet in Africa, though foraging techniques during winter are known to be very similar.<sup id="cite_ref-BWPi_3-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWPi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marsh_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Globally, the population is believed to be increasing, and the <a href="/wiki/World_Conservation_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="World Conservation Union">IUCN</a> categorises the species as of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>. It is estimated to have a total population of 10 to 27 million individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The reasons for the population decline in Britain are not completely understood, and there appears to be much suitable habitat.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_Action_Plan" class="mw-redirect" title="Biodiversity Action Plan">Biodiversity Action Plan</a> for the species further comments that it is not clear what can be done to conserve the species apart from protecting habitat at known breeding sites and protecting birds from egg collectors and from disturbance.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
