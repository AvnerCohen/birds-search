<p>The <b>Redwing</b> (<i>Turdus iliacus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family Turdidae, native to <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, slightly smaller than the related <a href="/wiki/Song_Thrush" title="Song Thrush">Song Thrush</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Redwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in his <i>Systema naturae</i> in 1758 under its current scientific name.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The English name derives from the bird's red underwing. It is not closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Red-winged_Blackbird" title="Red-winged Blackbird">Red-winged Blackbird</a>, a <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> species sometimes <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nicknamed</a> "redwing", which is an <a href="/wiki/Icterid" title="Icterid">icterid</a>, not a thrush.<sup id="cite_ref-Jaramillo_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jaramillo-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The binomial name derives from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> words <i>Turdus</i>, "thrush", and <i>ile</i> "flank".<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Redwing has two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbw_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thrushes_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thrushes-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>T. i. iliacus</i>, the nominate subspecies described by Linnaeus, which breeds in mainland Eurasia.</li>
<li><i>T. i. coburni</i> described by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bowdler_Sharpe" title="Richard Bowdler Sharpe">Richard Bowdler Sharpe</a> in 1901, which breeds in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a> and winters from western Scotland and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> south to northern <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>. It is darker overall, and marginally larger than the nominate form.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Redwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>It is 20–24&#160;cm long with a wingspan of 33–34.5&#160;cm and a weight of 50–75 g. The sexes are similar, with plain brown backs and with dark brown spots on the white underparts. The most striking identification features are the red flanks and underwing, and the creamy white stripe above the eye.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbw_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thrushes_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thrushes-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The male has a varied short song, and a whistling flight call.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Redwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Redwing.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Redwing.jpg/220px-Redwing.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" /></a>

</div>

<p>It breeds in northern regions of Europe and Asia, from <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> south to northernmost <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, and east through <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_States" title="Baltic States" class="mw-redirect">Baltic States</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>, and through most of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> to about 165°E in <a href="/wiki/Chukotka_Autonomous_Okrug" title="Chukotka Autonomous Okrug">Chukotka Autonomous Okrug</a>. In recent years it has expanded its range slightly, both in eastern Europe where it now breeds south into northern <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, and in southern <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Qaqortoq" title="Qaqortoq">Qaqortoq</a> area was colonised in 1990–1991.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbw_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thrushes_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thrushes-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is often replaced by the related Ring Ouzel in areas of higher altitude.<sup id="cite_ref-Observerbirdseggs_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Observerbirdseggs-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in western, central and southern Europe, northwest <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, and southwest Asia east to northern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. Birds in some parts of the west of the breeding range (particularly southwestern <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>) may be resident, not migrating at all, while those in the far east of the range migrate at least 6,500–7,000&#160;km to reach their wintering grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbw_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thrushes_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thrushes-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>There are multiple records of vagrants from the northeast coast of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, as well as two records on the Northwest coast (one in Washington in 2005, and one in Seward, Alaska in November 2011).<sup id="cite_ref-thrushes_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thrushes-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

</div>
</div>
<p>Migrating and wintering birds often form loose flocks of ten to 200 or more birds, often feeding together with <a href="/wiki/Fieldfare" title="Fieldfare">Fieldfares</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_Blackbird" title="Common Blackbird">Common Blackbirds</a>, and <a href="/wiki/European_Starling" title="European Starling" class="mw-redirect">Starlings</a>, sometimes also with <a href="/wiki/Mistle_Thrush" title="Mistle Thrush">Mistle Thrushes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Song_Thrush" title="Song Thrush">Song Thrushes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ring_Ouzel" title="Ring Ouzel">Ring Ouzels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbw_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thrushes_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thrushes-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Unlike the Song Thrush, the more nomadic Redwing does not tend to return regularly to the same wintering areas.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Redwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<p>It breeds in <a href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer" class="mw-redirect">conifer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a> forest and <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>. Redwings nest in shrubs or on the ground, laying four to six <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> in a neat nest. The eggs are typically 2.6&#160;x&#160;1.9 centimetres in size and weigh 4.6&#160;grammes, of which 5% is shell,<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> and hatch after 12–13 days. The chicks fledge at 12–15 days, but the young remain dependent on their parents for a further 14 days.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbw_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thrushes_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thrushes-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A Russian study of blood parasites showed that all the Fieldfares, Redwings and Song Thrushes sampled carried <a href="/wiki/Haematozoa" title="Haematozoa">haematozoans</a>, particularly <i><a href="/wiki/Haemoproteus" title="Haemoproteus">Haemoproteus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Trypanosoma" title="Trypanosoma">Trypanosoma</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Markovets_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Markovets-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Redwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<p>It is omnivorous, eating a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a> all year, supplemented by <a href="/wiki/Berry" title="Berry">berries</a> in autumn and winter, particularly of <a href="/wiki/Sorbus_aucuparia" title="Sorbus aucuparia">rowan</a> <i>Sorbus aucuparia</i> and <a href="/wiki/Crataegus_monogyna" title="Crataegus monogyna">hawthorn</a> <i>Crataegus monogyna</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbw_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thrushes_6-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thrushes-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Redwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h2>
<p>The Redwing has an extensive range, estimated at 10&#160;million&#160;square kilometres (3.8&#160;million square miles), and a large population, including an estimated 31 to 42 million individuals in Europe alone. The species is not believed to approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the IUCN Red List (i.e., declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations), and is therefore evaluated as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Numbers can be adversely affected by severe winters, which may cause heavy mortality, and cold wet summers, which reduce breeding success.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
