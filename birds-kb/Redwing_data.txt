<p>The <b>redwing</b> (<i>Turdus iliacus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family, Turdidae, native to <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>, slightly smaller than the related <a href="/wiki/Song_thrush" title="Song thrush">song thrush</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a> under its current scientific name.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The English name derives from the bird's red underwing. It is not closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Red-winged_blackbird" title="Red-winged blackbird">red-winged blackbird</a>, a North American species sometimes <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nicknamed</a> "redwing", which is an <a href="/wiki/Icterid" title="Icterid">icterid</a>, not a thrush.<sup id="cite_ref-Jaramillo_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jaramillo-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The binomial name derives from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> words <i>turdus</i>, "thrush", and <i>ile</i> "flank".<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>About 65 species of medium to large thrushes are in the genus <i>Turdus</i>, characterised by rounded heads, longish, pointed wings, and usually melodious songs. Although two European thrushes, the <a href="/wiki/Song_thrush" title="Song thrush">song thrush</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mistle_thrush" title="Mistle thrush">mistle thrush</a>, are early offshoots from the Eurasian lineage of <i>Turdus</i> thrushes after they spread north from Africa, the redwing is descended from ancestors that had colonised the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> islands from Africa and subsequently reached Europe from there.<sup id="cite_ref-reilly_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reilly-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The redwing has two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbw_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thrushes_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thrushes-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>T. i. iliacus</i>, the nominate subspecies described by Linnaeus, which breeds in mainland Eurasia.</li>
<li><i>T. i. coburni</i> described by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bowdler_Sharpe" title="Richard Bowdler Sharpe">Richard Bowdler Sharpe</a> in 1901, which breeds in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a> and winters from western Scotland and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> south to northern <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>. It is darker overall, and marginally larger than the nominate form.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is 20–24&#160;cm long with a wingspan of 33–34.5&#160;cm and a weight of 50–75&#160;g. The sexes are similar, with plain brown backs and with dark brown spots on the white underparts. The most striking identification features are the red flanks and underwing, and the creamy white stripe above the eye.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbw_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thrushes_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thrushes-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Adults moult between June and September, which means that some start to replace their flight feathers while still feeding young.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The male has a varied short song, and a whistling flight call.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Redwing.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Redwing.jpg/220px-Redwing.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Redwing.jpg/330px-Redwing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Redwing.jpg/440px-Redwing.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="533" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Redwing.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Head of <i>T. i. coburni</i> in Iceland</div></div></div>
<p>It breeds in northern regions of Europe and the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> south to northernmost <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, and east through <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltic States">Baltic States</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>, and through most of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> to about <a href="/wiki/165th_meridian_east" title="165th meridian east">165°E</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chukotka_Autonomous_Okrug" title="Chukotka Autonomous Okrug">Chukotka Autonomous Okrug</a>. In recent years it has expanded its range slightly, both in eastern Europe where it now breeds south into northern <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, and in southern <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Qaqortoq" title="Qaqortoq">Qaqortoq</a> area was colonised in 1990–1991.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbw_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thrushes_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thrushes-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is often replaced by the related <a href="/wiki/Ring_ouzel" title="Ring ouzel">ring ouzel</a> in areas of higher altitude.<sup id="cite_ref-Observerbirdseggs_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Observerbirdseggs-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in western, central and southern Europe, north-west Africa, and south-west Asia east to northern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. Birds in some parts of the west of the breeding range (particularly south-western <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>) may be resident, not migrating at all, while those in the far east of the range migrate at least 6,500–7,000&#160;km to reach their wintering grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbw_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thrushes_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thrushes-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are multiple records of vagrants from the north-east coast of North America, as well as two sightings on the north-west coast (one in Washington in 2005, and one in Seward, Alaska in November 2011).<sup id="cite_ref-thrushes_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thrushes-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Migrating and wintering birds often form loose flocks of 10 to 200 or more birds, often feeding together with <a href="/wiki/Fieldfare" title="Fieldfare">fieldfares</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_blackbird" title="Common blackbird">common blackbirds</a>, and <a href="/wiki/European_starling" class="mw-redirect" title="European starling">starlings</a>, sometimes also with <a href="/wiki/Mistle_thrush" title="Mistle thrush">mistle thrushes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Song_thrush" title="Song thrush">song thrushes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ring_ouzel" title="Ring ouzel">ring ouzels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbw_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thrushes_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thrushes-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Unlike the song thrush, the more nomadic redwing does not tend to return regularly to the same wintering areas.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Turdus_iliacus_MWNH_2239.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Turdus_iliacus_MWNH_2239.JPG/220px-Turdus_iliacus_MWNH_2239.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Turdus_iliacus_MWNH_2239.JPG/330px-Turdus_iliacus_MWNH_2239.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Turdus_iliacus_MWNH_2239.JPG/440px-Turdus_iliacus_MWNH_2239.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3564" data-file-height="2808" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Turdus_iliacus_MWNH_2239.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Redwing_nest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Redwing_nest.jpg/220px-Redwing_nest.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Redwing_nest.jpg/330px-Redwing_nest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Redwing_nest.jpg/440px-Redwing_nest.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1257" data-file-height="855" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Redwing_nest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nests are often constructed on the ground.</div></div></div>
<p>It breeds in <a href="/wiki/Conifer" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifer">conifer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a> forest and <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>. Redwings nest in shrubs or on the ground, laying four to six <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> in a neat nest. The eggs are typically 2.6&#160;x&#160;1.9 centimetres in size and weigh 4.6&#160;grammes, of which 5% is shell,<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and which hatch after 12–13 days. The chicks fledge at 12–15 days, but the young remain dependent on their parents for a further 14 days.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbw_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thrushes_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thrushes-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is omnivorous, eating a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a> all year, supplemented by <a href="/wiki/Berry" title="Berry">berries</a> in autumn and winter, particularly of <a href="/wiki/Sorbus_aucuparia" title="Sorbus aucuparia">rowan</a> <i>Sorbus aucuparia</i> and <a href="/wiki/Crataegus_monogyna" title="Crataegus monogyna">hawthorn</a> <i>Crataegus monogyna</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbw_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thrushes_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thrushes-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_threats">Natural threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Natural threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A Russian study of blood parasites showed that all the fieldfares, redwings and song thrushes sampled carried <a href="/wiki/Haematozoa" title="Haematozoa">haematozoans</a>, particularly <i><a href="/wiki/Haemoproteus" title="Haemoproteus">Haemoproteus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Trypanosoma" title="Trypanosoma">Trypanosoma</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Markovets_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Markovets-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_and_conservation">Status and conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Status and conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The redwing has an extensive range, estimated at 10&#160;million&#160;square kilometres (3.8&#160;million square miles), and an estimated population of 26 to 40 million individuals in Europe alone. The European population forms approximately 40% of the global population, thus the very preliminary estimate of the global population is 98 to 151 million individuals. The species is believed to approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> (i.e., declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations), and is therefore precautionarily uplisted to <a href="/wiki/Near_threatened" class="mw-redirect" title="Near threatened">near threatened</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Numbers can be adversely affected by severe winters, which may cause heavy mortality, and cold wet summers, which reduce breeding success.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_7-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
