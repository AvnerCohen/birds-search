<p>The <b>red-breasted flycatcher</b> (<i>Ficedula parva</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a> family. It breeds in eastern Europe and across <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> and is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in south Asia. It is a regular passage migrant in western Europe, whereas the <a href="/wiki/Collared_flycatcher" title="Collared flycatcher">collared flycatcher</a> which breeds further east is rare. This is because of the different migration direction.
</p>

<p>The Asian species <i>Ficedula albicilla</i>, previously considered a subspecies of the red-breasted flycatcher, has the red throat surrounded by grey and a different song. It is usually now separated as the <a href="/wiki/Taiga_flycatcher" title="Taiga flycatcher">taiga flycatcher</a> (<a href="/wiki/Peter_Simon_Pallas" title="Peter Simon Pallas">Pallas</a>, 1811).
</p><p>The breeding male of this small 11–12&#160;cm long flycatcher is mainly brown above and white below, with a grey head and orange throat. The bill is black and has the broad but pointed shape typical of aerial insectivores. As well as taking insects in flight, this species hunts <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a> amongst the oak foliage, and will take berries. The base of the outertail feather is white and the tail is often flicked upwards as they perch looking out for insect prey which are caught on the wing or sometimes from the ground. In winter they are mostly silent but have a typical <i>chip-chip-chr-rrr</i> flycatcher call. In their breeding season, the song consists of melodious whistles, like that of the <a href="/wiki/European_pied_flycatcher" title="European pied flycatcher">European pied flycatcher</a>.
</p><p>Non-breeding males, females and juveniles have brown heads and lack the throat collar, but are easily distinguished from other <i>Ficedula</i> flycatchers on size and the <a href="/wiki/Wheatear" title="Wheatear">wheatear</a>-like tail pattern, with an inverted dark T against the white tail sides.
</p><p>They are found mainly deciduous woodlands, especially near water. They build an open nest in a tree hole or similar recess. 4–7 eggs are laid.
</p><p>Studies on their spring arrivals to the breeding quarters in Poland from 1973–2002 show that males are returning earlier with increasing temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The genus name is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and refers to a small fig-eating bird (<i>ficus</i>, "fig") supposed to change into the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_blackcap" title="Eurasian blackcap">blackcap</a> in winter. The specific <i>parva</i> is Latin for "small".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
