<p>The <b>Bar-tailed Godwit</b> (<i>Limosa lapponica</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Scolopacidae" title="Scolopacidae" class="mw-redirect">Scolopacidae</a>, which breeds on <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> coasts and tundra mainly in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a>, and winters on coasts in temperate and tropical regions of the Old World.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> It makes the longest known non-stop flight of any <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> and also the longest journey without pausing to feed by any <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a>, 11,680 kilometres (7,258&#160;mi) along a route from <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> to <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gill_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gill-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bar-tailed_Godwit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bar-tailed_Godwit_Cairns_RWD.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Bar-tailed_Godwit_Cairns_RWD.jpg/220px-Bar-tailed_Godwit_Cairns_RWD.jpg" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" /></a>

<p>The Bar-tailed Godwit is a relatively short-legged species of <a href="/wiki/Godwit" title="Godwit">godwit</a>. The bill-to-tail length is 37–41&#160;cm, with a wingspan of 70–80&#160;cm. Males average smaller than females but with much overlap; males weigh 190–400&#160;<a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">g</a>, while females weigh 260–630&#160;g; there is also some regional variation in size (see subspecies, below). The adult has blue-grey legs and a very long dark bill with a slight upward curve and pink at the tip. The neck, breast and belly are unbroken brick red in breeding plumage, off white in winter. The back is mottled grey.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is distinguished from the <a href="/wiki/Black-tailed_Godwit" title="Black-tailed Godwit">Black-tailed Godwit</a> (<i>Limosa limosa</i>) by its barred, rather than wholly black, tail and a lack of white wing bars. The most similar species is the <a href="/wiki/Asiatic_Dowitcher" title="Asiatic Dowitcher" class="mw-redirect">Asiatic Dowitcher</a>.</p>
<p><br />
There are three <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>, listed from west to east:<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>Limosa lapponica lapponica</i> (Linnaeus, 1758). Breeds from northern <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> east to the <a href="/wiki/Taymyr_Peninsula" title="Taymyr Peninsula">Taymyr Peninsula</a>; winters western coasts of Europe and Africa from the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> south to <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, and also around the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>. Smallest subspecies, males up to 360 g, females to 450 g.</li>
<li><i>Limosa lapponica menzbieri</i> Portenko, 1936. Breeds northeastern Asia from the Taymyr Peninsula east to the <a href="/wiki/Kolyma_River" title="Kolyma River">Kolyma River</a> delta; winters southeastern Asia and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. Intermediate between the other two subspecies.</li>
<li><i>Limosa lapponica baueri</i> Naumann, 1836. Breeds far northeastern Asia east of the Kolyma River, and western <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>; winters in Australia and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. Largest subspecies.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bar-tailed_Godwit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span></h2>
<p>It forages by probing in mudflats or marshes. It may find insects by sight in short vegetation. It eats mainly insects and crustaceans, but also parts of aquatic plants.</p>

</div>
<p>The Bar-tailed Godwit is a non-breeding migrant in Australia. Breeding take place each year in Scandinavia, northern Asia, and Alaska. The nest is a shallow cup in moss sometimes lined with vegetation. Both sexes share incubation of the eggs and care for the young.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bar-tailed_Godwit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Migrations">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Migrations">Migrations</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bar-tailed_Godwit_migration.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Bar-tailed_Godwit_migration.jpg/300px-Bar-tailed_Godwit_migration.jpg" width="300" height="294" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bar-tailed_Godwit_migration.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
The routes of satellite tagged Bar-tailed Godwits migrating north from New Zealand to Korea and China</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Bar-tailed Godwit <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrates</a> in flocks to coastal <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, northwestern Europe and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, where the sub-species <i>Limosa lapponica baueri</i> is called <b>Kūaka</b> in <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="Māori language">Māori</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It was shown in 2007 to undertake the longest non-stop flight of any bird. Birds in New Zealand were tagged and tracked by satellite to the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Sea" title="Yellow Sea">Yellow Sea</a> in China. According to Dr. Clive Minton (Australasian Wader Studies Group) "The distance between these two locations is 9,575 kilometres (5,950&#160;mi), but the actual track flown by the bird was 11,026 kilometres (6,851&#160;mi). This was the longest known non-stop flight of any bird. The flight took approximately nine days. At least three other Bar-tailed Godwits also appear to have reached the Yellow Sea after non-stop flights from New Zealand."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>One specific female of the flock, nicknamed "E7", flew onward from China to Alaska and stayed there for the breeding season. Then on 29 August 2007 she departed on a non-stop flight from the Avinof Peninsula in western Alaska to the <a href="/wiki/Piako_River" title="Piako River">Piako River</a> near Thames New Zealand, setting a new known flight record of 11,680 kilometres (7,258&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-gill_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gill-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Stray birds from Europe and Asia occasionally appear on both <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> coasts.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from October 2008">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bar-tailed_Godwit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Protection">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Protection">Protection</span></h2>
<p>The Bar-tailed Godwit is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>
