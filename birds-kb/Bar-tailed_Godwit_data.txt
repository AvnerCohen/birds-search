<p>The <b>bar-tailed godwit</b> (<i>Limosa lapponica</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Scolopacidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Scolopacidae">Scolopacidae</a>, which feeds on <a href="/wiki/Polychaete" title="Polychaete">bristle-worms</a> and shellfish on coastal mudflats and <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuaries</a>. It has distinctive red breeding plumage, long legs, and a long upturned bill. Bar-tailed godwits breed on <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> coasts and tundra from <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, and overwinter on coasts in temperate and tropical regions of the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a>, Australia and New Zealand. The <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migration</a> of the subspecies <i>Limosa lapponica baueri</i> across the Pacific Ocean from Alaska to New Zealand is the longest known non-stop flight of any bird, and also the longest journey without pausing to feed by any animal. The round-trip migration for this subspecies is over 29,000&#160;km (18,020&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-gill_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gill-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bar-tailed_godwit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The genus name <i>Limosa</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means "muddy", from <i>limus</i>, "mud", referring to its preferred habitat. The specific name<i> lapponica</i> refers to <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A1pmi" title="Sápmi">Lapland</a>: it was originally named <i>Scolopax lapponica</i> by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linneaus</a> from a northern Swedish specimen.<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The English term "godwit" was first recorded in about 1416–17 and may be an imitation of the bird's call, or be derived from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> "god whit", meaning "good creature", perhaps referring to its eating qualities.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Its English name is taken from the black-and-white barred tail and upper tail coverts in this species.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> it is known as <i>barge rousse</i>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> <i>maliy veretennik</i>, <a href="/wiki/Inuit_languages" title="Inuit languages">Inuit</a> <i>chiuchiuchiak</i>, <a href="/wiki/Yup%27ik" title="Yup&#39;ik">Yup'ik</a> <i>tevatevaaq</i>, and <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="Māori language">Māori</a> <i>kūaka</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Stap2009_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stap2009-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bar-tailed_godwit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Speed_(21327488468)_(cropped).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Speed_%2821327488468%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/220px-Speed_%2821327488468%29_%28cropped%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="232" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Speed_%2821327488468%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/330px-Speed_%2821327488468%29_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Speed_%2821327488468%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/440px-Speed_%2821327488468%29_%28cropped%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="786" data-file-height="829" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Speed_(21327488468)_(cropped).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In flight, showing tail barring</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><div class="mediaContainer" style="width:220px"><audio id="mwe_player_0" controls="" preload="none" style="width:220px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="120.34612244898" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="XC234849-Default_Project.ogg" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/04/XC234849-Default_Project.ogg" type="audio/ogg; codecs=&quot;vorbis&quot;" data-title="Original Ogg file (79 kbps)" data-shorttitle="Ogg source" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="79217" /><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/0/04/XC234849-Default_Project.ogg/XC234849-Default_Project.ogg.mp3" type="audio/mpeg" data-title="MP3" data-shorttitle="MP3" data-transcodekey="mp3" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="140848" /></audio></div>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:XC234849-Default_Project.ogg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Limosa lapponica</i>, Finland</div></div></div>
<p>The bar-tailed godwit is a relatively short-legged species of <a href="/wiki/Godwit" title="Godwit">godwit</a>. The bill-to-tail length is 37–41&#160;cm (15–16&#160;in), with a wingspan of 70–80&#160;cm (28–31&#160;in). Males average smaller than females but with much overlap; males weigh 190–400&#160;g (6.7–14.1&#160;oz), while females weigh 260–630&#160;g (9.2–22.2&#160;oz); there is also some regional variation in size (see subspecies, below). The adult has blue-grey legs and a long, tapering, slightly upturned bi-colored bill: pink at the base and black towards the tip. The neck, breast and belly are unbroken brick red in breeding plumage, and dark brown above.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Females breeding plumage is much duller than males, with a chestnut to cinnamon belly.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Breeding plumage is not fully apparent until the third year, and there are three distinguishable age classes; during their first migration north immature males are noticeably paler in colour than more mature males.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Non-breeding birds seen in the Southern Hemisphere are plain grey-brown with darker feather centres, giving them a striped look, and are whitish underneath. Juveniles are similar to non-breeding adults but more buff overall with streaked plumages on flanks and breast.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Alaska-breeding bar-tailed godwits show an increase in body size from north to south, but this trend is not apparent in their non-breeding grounds in New Zealand; birds of different sizes mix freely.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Limosa lapponica</i> is distinguished from the <a href="/wiki/Black-tailed_godwit" title="Black-tailed godwit">black-tailed godwit</a> (<i>Limosa limosa</i>) by its black-and-white horizontally-barred (rather than wholly black) tail, and lack of white wing bars. The most similar species is the <a href="/wiki/Asiatic_dowitcher" class="mw-redirect" title="Asiatic dowitcher">Asiatic dowitcher</a> (<i>Limnodromus semipalmatus</i>).
</p><p>There are now five generally accepted <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of bar-tailed godwit, listed from west to east:<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>L. l. lapponica</i> - <small>(<a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, 1758)</small>: Breeds from northern <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> east to the <a href="/wiki/Yamal_Peninsula" title="Yamal Peninsula">Yamal Peninsula</a>; winters western coasts of Europe and Africa from the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> south to <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, and also around the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>. Smallest subspecies, males up to 360&#160;g (13&#160;oz), females to 450&#160;g (16&#160;oz).</li>
<li><i>L. l. taymyrensis</i>: <small>Engelmoer &amp; Roselaar, 1998</small>: Breeds in central Siberia from the Yamal Peninsula east to the <a href="/wiki/Anabar_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Anabar River">Anabar River</a> delta.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>L. l. menzbieri</i> - <small><a href="/wiki/Leonid_Portenko" title="Leonid Portenko">Portenko</a>, 1936</small>: Breeds northeastern Asia from the <a href="/wiki/Anabar_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Anabar River">Anabar River</a> east to the <a href="/wiki/Kolyma_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Kolyma River">Kolyma River</a> delta; winters in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>.</li>
<li><i>L. l. anadyrensis</i>: <small>Engelmoer &amp; Roselaar, 1998</small>: from the <a href="/wiki/Anadyrsky_District" title="Anadyrsky District">Anadyr</a> lowlands in far northeastern Asia east of the Kolyma River; the second largest subspecies, smaller than <i>L. l. baueri</i><sup id="cite_ref-:2_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>L. l. baueri</i> - <small><a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Naumann" title="Johann Friedrich Naumann">Naumann</a>, 1836</small>: (called ) Breeds in western <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>; winters in Australia and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. Largest subspecies.</li></ul>
<p><br />
</p>

<p>The birds' main source of food in wetlands is <a href="/wiki/Polychaete" title="Polychaete">bristle-worms</a> (up to 70%), supplemented by small <a href="/wiki/Bivalvia" title="Bivalvia">bivalves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>. In wet pastures, bar-tailed godwits eat invertebrates.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In a major staging site in the northern <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Sea" title="Yellow Sea">Yellow Sea</a>, they continue to hunt <a href="/wiki/Polychaete" title="Polychaete">polychaetes</a>, but most of their food intake is the bivalve mollusc <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potamocorbula_laevis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Potamocorbula laevis (page does not exist)">Potamocorbula laevis</a></i>, which they generally swallow whole.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Male bar-tailed godwits are smaller than females and have shorter bills. In a study at the Manawatū Estuary, shorter-billed birds (males) fed mostly on small surface prey like <i><a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_mud_snail" title="New Zealand mud snail">Potamopyrgus</a></i> snails, half being snail specialists, whereas females consumed more deeply-buried prey such as worms; the birds also displayed some individual food preferences.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bar-tailed_godwit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The bar-tailed godwit is a non-breeding migrant in Australia and New Zealand. Birds first depart for their northern hemisphere breeding sites at age 2–4.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Breeding take place each year in Scandinavia, northern Asia, and Alaska. The nest is a shallow cup in moss sometimes lined with vegetation. Clutch size is from 2 to 5, averaging four.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Both sexes share incubation of the eggs for 20 to 21 days, the female during the day and the male at night.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Migrations">Migrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bar-tailed_godwit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Migrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bar-tailed_Godwit_migration.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Bar-tailed_Godwit_migration.jpg/220px-Bar-tailed_Godwit_migration.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="216" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Bar-tailed_Godwit_migration.jpg/330px-Bar-tailed_Godwit_migration.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fe/Bar-tailed_Godwit_migration.jpg 2x" data-file-width="416" data-file-height="408" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bar-tailed_Godwit_migration.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Routes of satellite tagged bar-tailed godwits migrating north from New Zealand to Korea and China</div></div></div>
<p>All bar-tailed godwits spend the Northern Hemisphere summer in the Arctic, where they breed, and make a long-distance migration south in winter to more temperate areas. <i>L. l. lapponica</i> make the shortest migration, some only as far as the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>, while others travel as far as India.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Bar-tailed godwits nesting in Alaska (<i>L. l. baueri</i>) travel all the way to Australia and New Zealand. They undertake the longest non-stop migrations of any bird, and to fuel this carry the greatest fat loads of any migratory bird so far studied, reducing the size of their digestive organs to do so.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gill_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gill-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p><i>L. l. bauri</i> breeds in Alaska and spends the non-breeding season in eastern Australia and New Zealand. <i>L. l. menzbieri</i> breeds in Siberia and migrates to northern and western Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Birds breeding in Siberia follow the coast of Asia northwards and southwards, but those breeding in Alaska migrate directly across the Pacific to Australasia 11,000&#160;km (6,835&#160;mi) away.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> To track the return journey, seven birds in New Zealand were tagged with surgically-implanted transmitters and tracked by satellite to the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Sea" title="Yellow Sea">Yellow Sea</a> in China, a distance of 9,575&#160;km (5,950&#160;mi); the actual track flown by one bird was 11,026&#160;km (6,851&#160;mi), taking nine days. At least three other bar-tailed godwits also appear to have reached the Yellow Sea after non-stop flights from New Zealand.
</p><p>One specific female of the flock, nicknamed "E7", flew onward from China to Alaska and stayed there for the breeding season. Then in August 2007 she departed on an eight-day non-stop flight from western Alaska to the <a href="/wiki/Piako_River" title="Piako River">Piako River</a> near <a href="/wiki/Thames,_New_Zealand" title="Thames, New Zealand">Thames</a>, New Zealand, setting a new known flight record of 11,680&#160;km (7,258&#160;mi). This <i>L. l. bauri</i> female made a 174 day round-trip journey of 29,280&#160;km (18,194&#160;mi) with 20 days of flying.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In September 2020, a tagged male bird flew from Alaska to New Zealand in 11 days, a journey length estimated at 12,200&#160;km (7,581&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-Boffey_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boffey-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MirandaNzBirdRinging01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/MirandaNzBirdRinging01.jpg/220px-MirandaNzBirdRinging01.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/MirandaNzBirdRinging01.jpg/330px-MirandaNzBirdRinging01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/MirandaNzBirdRinging01.jpg/440px-MirandaNzBirdRinging01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MirandaNzBirdRinging01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Banding <i>L. l. baueri</i> at <a href="/wiki/Miranda_Naturalists%27_Trust" class="mw-redirect" title="Miranda Naturalists&#39; Trust">Miranda Shorebird Centre</a>, New Zealand</div></div></div>
<p>To fuel such long journeys, <i>L. l. baueri</i> birds in New Zealand deposit much more fat for their body size than other subspecies, allowing them to fly 6,000&#160;km (3,728&#160;mi) to 8,600&#160;km (5,344&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Both Australasian subspecies head north to their breeding grounds along the coast of Asia to the <a href="/wiki/Yalu_River" title="Yalu River">Yalu Jiang</a> coastal wetland in the north Yellow Sea, the most important staging grounds for godwits and <a href="/wiki/Great_knot" title="Great knot">great knots</a> (<i>Calidris tenuirostris)</i> in their northern migration.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> <i>Baueri</i> birds rested for about 41 days before continuing approximately 7,000&#160;km (4,350&#160;mi) on to Alaska. <i>Menzbieri</i> spent on average 38 days in the Yellow Sea region and flew an additional 4,100&#160;km (2,548&#160;mi) to high Arctic Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Birds will often depart early from New Zealand if there are favourable winds; they seem to be able to predict weather patterns that will assist them on the entire migration route.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Birds that had nested in southern Alaska were larger and departed New Zealand earliest; this pattern was repeated six months later, with birds departing Alaska in the same order they arrived, and over the same span of days.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Birds in southern New Zealand departed on average 9–11 days earlier than birds in more northern sites.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Godwits arrive at the <a href="/wiki/Yukon%E2%80%93Kuskokwim_Delta" title="Yukon–Kuskokwim Delta">Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta</a> in Alaska in two waves; local breeders in early May, and larger flocks in the third week of May en route to breeding grounds further north.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protection">Protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bar-tailed_godwit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The status of the bar-tailed godwit is Near Threatened, and the population is declining.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn2017_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn2017-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Fewer birds have been using East African estuaries since 1979, and there has been a steady decline in numbers around the Kola Peninsula, Siberia, since 1930.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The global population is estimated to number 1,099,000&#8211;1,149,000 individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn2017_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn2017-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Both <i>L. l. bauri</i>  and <i>L. l. menzbieri</i> adult survival rates decreased between 2005 and 2012, probably because of the loss of intertidal staging areas in the Yellow Sea.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The construction of seawalls and the reclamation of mudflats have led to a critical reduction in food supplies for migrating birds, particularly subspecies like <i>L. l. menzbieri</i> that rely on the Yalu Jiang estuary on both their northward and southward migrations.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Numbers of <i>L. l. baueri</i> have declined in New Zealand from over 100,000 in the late 1980s to 67,500 in 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The bar-tailed godwit is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.<sup id="cite_ref-aewa_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aewa-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In New Zealand the species is protected under the 1953 Wildlife Act.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
