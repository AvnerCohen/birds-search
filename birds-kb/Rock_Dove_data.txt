<p>The <b>Rock Dove</b><sup id="cite_ref-worldbirdnames_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-worldbirdnames-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> (<i>Columba livia</i>) or <b>Rock Pigeon</b>, is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> family <a href="/wiki/Columbidae" title="Columbidae">Columbidae</a> (doves and pigeons).<sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> In common usage, this bird is often simply referred to as the "pigeon".</p>
<p>The species includes the <a href="/wiki/Domestic_Pigeon" title="Domestic Pigeon">domestic pigeon</a> (including the <a href="/wiki/Fancy_pigeon" title="Fancy pigeon">fancy pigeon</a>), and escaped domestic pigeons have given rise to <a href="/wiki/Feral_Pigeon" title="Feral Pigeon">feral populations</a> around the world.<sup id="cite_ref-Blechman_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blechman-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Wild Rock Doves are pale grey with two black bars on each wing, although domestic and <a href="/wiki/Feral" title="Feral">feral</a> pigeons are very variable in colour and pattern. There are few visible differences between males and females.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The species is generally <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a>, with two squeakers (young) per <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">brood</a>. Both parents care for the young for a time.<sup id="cite_ref-Levi_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levi-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">Habitats</a> include various open and semi-open environments. <a href="/wiki/Cliff" title="Cliff">Cliffs</a> and rock ledges are used for roosting and breeding in the wild. Originally found wild in Europe, North Africa, and western Asia, feral Pigeon have become established in cities around the world. The species is abundant, with an estimated population of 17 to 28 million feral and wild birds in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_Dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and naming">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_naming">Taxonomy and naming</span></h2>
<p>The Rock Dove was first described by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Gmelin" title="Johann Friedrich Gmelin">Gmelin</a> in 1789.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The genus name <i>Columba</i> is the Latinized form of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> κόλυμβος (<i>kolumbos</i>), "a diver", from <i>κολυμβάω</i> (kolumbao), "dive, plunge headlong, swim".<sup id="cite_ref-Liddell_1980_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Liddell_1980-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes" title="Aristophanes">Aristophanes</a> (Birds, 304) and others use the word κολυμβίς (<i>kolumbis</i>), "diver", for the name of the bird, because of its swimming motion in the air. The specific epithet is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>livor</i>, "bluish".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Its closest relative in the <i>Columba</i> genus is the <a href="/wiki/Hill_Pigeon" title="Hill Pigeon">Hill Pigeon</a>, followed by the other rock pigeons: the <a href="/wiki/Snow_Pigeon" title="Snow Pigeon">Snow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Speckled_Pigeon" title="Speckled Pigeon">Speckled</a> and <a href="/wiki/White-collared_Pigeon" title="White-collared Pigeon">White-collared Pigeons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The species is also known as the <b>Rock Pigeon</b> or <b>Blue Rock Dove</b>, the former being the official name from 2004 to 2011, at which point the <a href="/wiki/International_Ornithological_Congress" title="International Ornithological Congress">IOC</a> changed their official listing to its original British name of Rock Dove.<sup id="cite_ref-worldbirdnames_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-worldbirdnames-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-White_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> In common usage, this bird is still often simply referred to as the "pigeon". Baby pigeons are called <i>squabs</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Levi_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levi-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_Dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span></h3>
<p>There are 12 subspecies recognised by Gibbs (2000); some of these may be derived from feral stock.<sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>C. l. livia</i>, the nominate subspecies, occurs in western and southern Europe, northern Africa, and Asia to western Kazakhstan, the northern <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>.</li>
<li><i>C. l. atlantis</i> (<a href="/wiki/David_Armitage_Bannerman" title="David Armitage Bannerman">Bannerman</a>, 1931) of <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>, is a very variable population with chequered upperparts obscuring the black wingbars, and is almost certainly derived from feral pigeons.</li>
<li><i>C. l. canariensis</i> (Bannerman, 1914) of the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, is smaller and averages darker than the nominate subspecies.</li>
<li><i>C. l. gymnocyclus</i> (Gray, 1856) from <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guinea" title="Guinea">Guinea</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a> is smaller and very much darker than nominate <i>C. l. livia</i>. It is almost blackish on the head, rump and underparts with a white back and the iridescence of the nape extending onto the head.</li>
<li><i>C. l. targia</i> (Geyr von Schweppenburg, 1916) breeds in the mountains of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> east to <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>. It is slightly smaller than the nominate form, with similar plumage, but the back is concolorous with the mantle instead of white.</li>
<li><i>C. l. dakhlae</i> (<a href="/wiki/Richard_Meinertzhagen" title="Richard Meinertzhagen">Richard Meinertzhagen</a>, 1928) is confined to the two oases in central Egypt. It is smaller and much paler than the nominate subspecies.</li>
<li><i>C. l. schimperi</i> (<a href="/wiki/Charles_Lucien_Bonaparte" title="Charles Lucien Bonaparte">Bonaparte</a>, 1854) is found in the Nile Delta south to northern Sudan. It closely resembles <i>C. l. targia</i>, but has a distinctly paler mantle.</li>
<li><i>C. l. palaestinae</i> (Zedlitz, 1912) occurs from <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sinai" title="Sinai" class="mw-redirect">Sinai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabia" title="Arabia" class="mw-redirect">Arabia</a>. It is slightly larger than <i>C. l. schimperi</i> and has darker plumage.</li>
<li><i>C. l. gaddi</i> (Zarodney &amp; Looudoni, 1906), breeds from <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> east to <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a> is larger and paler than <i>C. l. palaestinae</i> with which it intergrades in the west. It also intergrades with the next subspecies to the east.</li>
<li><i>C. l. neglecta</i> (Hume, 1873), is found in the mountains of eastern Central Asia. It is similar to the nominate subspecies in size, but is darker with a stronger and more extensive iridescent sheen on the neck. It intergrades with the next race in the south.</li>
<li><i>C. l. intermedia</i> (<a href="/wiki/Hugh_Edwin_Strickland" title="Hugh Edwin Strickland">Strickland</a>, 1844) occurs in <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> and in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> south of the <a href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya" class="mw-redirect">Himalayan</a> range of <i>C. l. neglecta</i>. It is similar to that subspecies, but darker with a less contrasting back.</li>
<li><i>C. l. nigricans</i> (<a href="/wiki/Sergei_Aleksandrovich_Buturlin" title="Sergei Aleksandrovich Buturlin">Buturlin</a>, 1908) in <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> and north <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> is variable and probably derived from feral stock.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_Dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pigeon_portrait_4861.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Pigeon_portrait_4861.jpg/170px-Pigeon_portrait_4861.jpg" width="170" height="191" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Pigeon_portrait_4861.jpg/255px-Pigeon_portrait_4861.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Pigeon_portrait_4861.jpg/340px-Pigeon_portrait_4861.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pigeon_portrait_4861.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A distinctive <a href="/wiki/Operculum_(bird)" title="Operculum (bird)" class="mw-redirect">operculum</a> is located on top of the beak</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The adult of the nominate subspecies of the Rock Dove is 29 to 37 cm (11 to 15 in) long with a 62 to 72 cm (24 to 28 in) wingspan.<sup id="cite_ref-Jahan_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jahan-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Weight for wild or feral Rock Doves ranges from 238 to 380&#160;grams (8.4-13.4&#160;oz), though overfed domestic and semi-domestic individuals can exceed normal weights.<sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> It has a dark bluish-gray head, neck, and chest with glossy yellowish, greenish, and reddish-purple iridescence along its neck and wing feathers. The iris is orange, red or golden with a paler inner ring, and the bare skin round the eye is bluish-grey. The bill is grey-black with a conspicuous off-white cere, and the feet are purplish-red. Among standard measurements, the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> is typically around 22.3&#160;cm (8.8&#160;in), the <a href="/wiki/Tail" title="Tail">tail</a> is 9.5 to 11 cm (3.7 to 4.3 in), the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> is around 1.8&#160;cm (0.71&#160;in) and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> is 2.6 to 3.5 cm (1.0 to 1.4 in).<sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The adult female is almost identical to the male, but the iridescence on the neck is less intense and more restricted to the rear and sides, while that on the breast is often very obscure.<sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The white lower back of the pure Rock Dove is its best identification character, the two black bars on its pale grey wings are also distinctive. The tail has a black band on the end and the outer web of the tail feathers are margined with white. It is strong and quick on the wing, dashing out from sea caves, flying low over the water, its lighter grey rump showing well from above.<sup id="cite_ref-White_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Young birds show little lustre and are duller. Eye colour of the pigeon is generally orange but a few pigeons may have white-grey eyes. The eyelids are orange in colour and are encapsulated in a grey-white eye ring. The feet are red to pink.<sup id="cite_ref-Levi_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levi-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rock_dove_-_natures_pics.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Rock_dove_-_natures_pics.jpg/220px-Rock_dove_-_natures_pics.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Rock_dove_-_natures_pics.jpg/330px-Rock_dove_-_natures_pics.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Rock_dove_-_natures_pics.jpg/440px-Rock_dove_-_natures_pics.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rock_dove_-_natures_pics.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
In flight</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>When circling overhead, the white underwing of the bird becomes conspicuous. In its flight, behaviour, and voice, which is more of a dovecot <i>coo</i> than the phrase of the <a href="/wiki/Wood_Pigeon" title="Wood Pigeon">Wood Pigeon</a>, it is a typical pigeon. Although it is a relatively strong flier, it also glides frequently, holding its wings in a very pronounced V shape as it does. Though fields are visited for grain and green food, it is nowhere so plentiful as to be a pest.<sup id="cite_ref-Wright_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wright-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Pigeons feed on the ground in flocks or individually. They roost together in buildings or on walls or statues. When drinking, most birds take small sips and tilt their heads backwards to swallow the water. Pigeons are able to dip their bills into the water and drink continuously without having to tilt their heads back. When disturbed, a pigeon in a group will take off with a noisy clapping sound.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Pigeons, especially <a href="/wiki/Homing_pigeon" title="Homing pigeon">homing</a> or <a href="/wiki/Carrier_pigeon" title="Carrier pigeon">carrier</a> breeds, are well known for their ability to find their way home from long distances. Despite these demonstrated abilities, wild Rock Doves are sedentary and rarely leave their local areas.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_Dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rock_pigeons_on_cliffs.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Rock_pigeons_on_cliffs.jpg/220px-Rock_pigeons_on_cliffs.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Rock_pigeons_on_cliffs.jpg/330px-Rock_pigeons_on_cliffs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Rock_pigeons_on_cliffs.jpg/440px-Rock_pigeons_on_cliffs.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rock_pigeons_on_cliffs.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Feral Rock Doves in semi-natural <a href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" title="Habitat (ecology)" class="mw-redirect">habitat</a> perched on sea <a href="/wiki/Cliff" title="Cliff">cliffs</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Rock Dove has a restricted natural resident range in western and southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, and into <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>. The Rock Dove is often found in pairs in the breeding season but is usually gregarious.<sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The species (including ferals) has a large range, with an estimated global extent of occurrence of 10 million km². It has a large global population, including an estimated 17–28 million individuals in Europe<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">Fossil</a> evidence suggests the Rock Dove originated in southern Asia and skeletal remains unearthed in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> confirm their existence there for at least three hundred thousand years.<sup id="cite_ref-Blechman_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blechman-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> However, this species has such a long history with humans that it's impossible to tell exactly where the species' original range was.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Its habitat is natural cliffs, usually on coasts. Its domesticated form, the <a href="/wiki/Feral" title="Feral">feral</a> pigeon, has been widely introduced elsewhere, and is common, especially in cities, over much of the world. A Rock Pigeon's life span is anywhere from 3–5 years in the wild to 15 years in captivity, though longer-lived specimens have been reported.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The species was first introduced to <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> in 1606 at <a href="/wiki/Habitation_at_Port-Royal" title="Habitation at Port-Royal">Port Royal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-White_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The Rock Dove breeds at any time of the year, but peak times are spring and summer. Nesting sites are situated along coastal cliff faces, as well as the artificial cliff faces created by apartment buildings with accessible ledges or roof spaces.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The type of nest constructed is a flimsy platform of straw and sticks, put on ledge, under cover. Often window ledges of buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Two white eggs are laid with incubation that is shared by both parents lasting from seventeen to nineteen days.<sup id="cite_ref-Levi_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levi-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nestling" title="Nestling" class="mw-redirect">nestling</a> has pale yellow down and a flesh-coloured bill with a dark band. It is tended and fed on "<a href="/wiki/Crop_milk" title="Crop milk">crop milk</a>" like other doves. The fledging period is 30 days.<sup id="cite_ref-Jahan_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jahan-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_Dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Predators">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Predators">Predators</span></h2>
<p>With only its flying abilities protecting it from predation, rock pigeons are a favorite almost around the world for a wide range of raptorial birds. In fact, with feral pigeons existing in most every city in the world, they may form the majority of prey for several raptor species who live in urban areas. <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_Falcon" title="Peregrine Falcon">Peregrine Falcons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian Sparrowhawk">Eurasian Sparrowhawks</a> are natural predators of pigeons that are quite adept at catching and feeding upon this species, as shown by the high losses of racing pigeons to these predators.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Up to 80% of the diet of Peregrine Falcons in several cities that have breeding falcons is compromised by feral pigeons.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Some common predators of feral pigeons in the North America are <a href="/wiki/Opossum" title="Opossum">Opossums</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">Raccoons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red-tailed_Hawk" title="Red-tailed Hawk">Red-tailed Hawks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Horned_Owl" title="Great Horned Owl">Great Horned Owls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Screech_Owl" title="Eastern Screech Owl">Eastern Screech Owls</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Accipiter" title="Accipiter">Accipiter</a></i>s. The birds that predate pigeons in North America can range in size from <a href="/wiki/American_Kestrel" title="American Kestrel">American Kestrels</a> to <a href="/wiki/Golden_Eagle" title="Golden Eagle">Golden Eagles</a> and can even include <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crows</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Raven" title="Raven">ravens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Roof_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roof-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> On the ground the adults, their young and their eggs are at risk from feral and domestic <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Levi_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levi-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Doves and pigeons are considered to be <a href="/wiki/Game_(food)" title="Game (food)">game birds</a> as many species have been hunted and used for food in many of the countries in which they are native.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Pigeons may harbour a diverse parasite fauna.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> They often host the intestinal helminths <i>Capillaria columbae</i> and <i>Ascaridia columbae</i>. Their ectoparasites include the <a href="/wiki/Ischnocera" title="Ischnocera">Ischnoceran</a> lice <i>Columbicola columbae</i>, <i>Campanulotes bidentatus compar</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Amblycera" title="Amblycera">Amblyceran</a> lice <i>Bonomiella columbae</i>, <i>Hohorstiella lata</i>, <i>Colpocephalum turbinatum</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Mites" title="Mites" class="mw-redirect">mites</a> <i>Tinaminyssus melloi</i>, <i>Dermanyssus gallinae</i>, <i>Dermoglyphus columbae</i>, <i>Falculifer rostratus</i>, and <i>Diplaegidia columbae</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Hippoboscidae" title="Hippoboscidae">hippoboscid</a> fly <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudolynchia_canariensis" title="Pseudolynchia canariensis">Pseudolynchia canariensis</a></i> is a typical blood-sucking ectoparasite of pigeons, found only in tropical and sub-tropical regions.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_Dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Human health">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Human_health">Human health</span></h2>
<p>Pigeons have been falsely associated with the spread of human diseases.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material in the vicinity of this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s) from July 2011">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Contact with pigeon droppings poses a minor risk of contracting <a href="/wiki/Histoplasmosis" title="Histoplasmosis">histoplasmosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cryptococcosis" title="Cryptococcosis">cryptococcosis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Psittacosis" title="Psittacosis">psittacosis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Pigeons are not a major concern in the spread of <a href="/wiki/West_Nile_virus" title="West Nile virus">West Nile virus</a>; though they can contract it, they do not appear to be able to transmit it.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Pigeons are, however, at potential risk for carrying and spreading <a href="/wiki/Avian_influenza" title="Avian influenza">avian influenza</a>. Although one study has shown that adult pigeons are not clinically susceptible to the most dangerous strain of avian influenza, the <a href="/wiki/H5N1" title="H5N1" class="mw-redirect">H5N1</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-pmid17994324_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17994324-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> other studies have presented definitive evidence of clinical signs and neurological lesions resulting from infection.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid16846988_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16846988-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pmid17947534_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17947534-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Furthermore, it has been shown that pigeons are susceptible to other strains of avian influenza, such as the <a href="/wiki/H7N7" title="H7N7" class="mw-redirect">H7N7</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-pmid8883790_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid8883790-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> from which at least one human fatality has been recorded.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_Dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Domestication">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Domestication">Domestication</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pigeon_Revivim_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Pigeon_Revivim_2.jpg/220px-Pigeon_Revivim_2.jpg" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Pigeon_Revivim_2.jpg/330px-Pigeon_Revivim_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Pigeon_Revivim_2.jpg/440px-Pigeon_Revivim_2.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pigeon_Revivim_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Domestic pigeons</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Domestic_pigeon" title="Domestic pigeon" class="mw-redirect">Domestic pigeon</a></div>
<p>Rock Doves have been domesticated for several thousand years, giving rise to the <a href="/wiki/Domestic_Pigeon" title="Domestic Pigeon">domestic pigeon</a> (<i>Columba livia domestica</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Levi_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levi-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> As well as pets, domesticated pigeons are utilised as <a href="/wiki/Homing_pigeons" title="Homing pigeons" class="mw-redirect">homing pigeons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carrier_pigeons" title="Carrier pigeons" class="mw-redirect">carrier pigeons</a>, and so-called <a href="/wiki/War_pigeons" title="War pigeons" class="mw-redirect">war pigeons</a> have served and played important roles during wartime, with many pigeons having received bravery awards and medals for their services in saving hundreds of human lives: including, notably, the British pigeon <a href="/wiki/Cher_Ami" title="Cher Ami">Cher Ami</a> who received the <a href="/wiki/Croix_de_guerre_1914%E2%80%931918_(France)" title="Croix de guerre 1914–1918 (France)">Croix de Guerre</a> for his heroic actions during World War I, and the Irish <a href="/wiki/Paddy_(pigeon)" title="Paddy (pigeon)">Paddy</a> and the American <a href="/wiki/G.I._Joe_(pigeon)" title="G.I. Joe (pigeon)">G.I. Joe</a>, who both received the <a href="/wiki/Dickin_Medal" title="Dickin Medal">Dickin Medal</a>, amongst 32 pigeons to receive this medallion, for their gallant and brave actions during World War II.<sup id="cite_ref-Levi_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levi-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> There are numerous <a href="/wiki/Breed" title="Breed">breeds</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fancy_pigeon" title="Fancy pigeon">fancy pigeons</a> of all sizes, colours and types.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_Dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Feral pigeon">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feral_pigeon">Feral pigeon</span></h2>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Feral_Pigeon" title="Feral Pigeon">Feral Pigeon</a></div>
<p>Many domestic birds have escaped or been released over the years, and have given rise to the <a href="/wiki/Feral" title="Feral">feral</a> pigeon. These show a variety of plumages, although some have the blue barred pattern as does the pure Rock Dove. Feral pigeons are found in large numbers in cities and towns all over the world.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> The scarcity of the pure wild species is partly due to interbreeding with feral birds.<sup id="cite_ref-Wright_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wright-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
