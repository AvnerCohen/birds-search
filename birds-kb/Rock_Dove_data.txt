<p>The <b>rock dove</b>, <b>rock pigeon</b>, or <b>common pigeon</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;p&#39; in &#39;pie&#39;">p</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="/dʒ/: &#39;j&#39; in &#39;jam&#39;">dʒ</span><span title="/./: syllable break">.</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>/</a></span></span> also <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;p&#39; in &#39;pie&#39;">p</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="/dʒ/: &#39;j&#39; in &#39;jam&#39;">dʒ</span><span title="/./: syllable break">.</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <i>Columba livia</i>) is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> family <a href="/wiki/Columbidae" title="Columbidae">Columbidae</a> (doves and pigeons).<sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>624</span></sup> In common usage, this bird is often simply referred to as the "pigeon".
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Domestic_pigeon" title="Domestic pigeon">domestic pigeon</a> (<i>Columba livia domestica</i>, which includes about 1,000 different <a href="/wiki/Breed" title="Breed">breeds</a>) descended from this species. Escaped domestic pigeons have increased the populations of <a href="/wiki/Feral_pigeon" title="Feral pigeon">feral pigeons</a> around the world.<sup id="cite_ref-Blechman_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blechman-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Wild rock doves are pale grey with two black bars on each wing, whereas domestic and <a href="/wiki/Feral" title="Feral">feral</a> pigeons vary in colour and pattern. Few differences are seen between males and females.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The species is generally <a href="/wiki/Monogamous_pairing_in_animals" class="mw-redirect" title="Monogamous pairing in animals">monogamous</a>, with two squabs (young) per <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">brood</a>. Both parents care for the young for a time.<sup id="cite_ref-Levi_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levi-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">Habitats</a> include various open and semi-open environments. <a href="/wiki/Cliff" title="Cliff">Cliffs</a> and rock ledges are used for roosting and breeding in the wild. Originally found wild in Europe, North Africa, and western Asia, pigeons have become established in cities around the world. The species is abundant, with an estimated population of 17 to 28 million feral and wild birds in Europe alone and up to 120 million worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rock_Pigeon_Columba_livia.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Rock_Pigeon_Columba_livia.jpg/220px-Rock_Pigeon_Columba_livia.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Rock_Pigeon_Columba_livia.jpg/330px-Rock_Pigeon_Columba_livia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Rock_Pigeon_Columba_livia.jpg/440px-Rock_Pigeon_Columba_livia.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2881" data-file-height="1921" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rock_Pigeon_Columba_livia.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Columba livia</i> in India</div></div></div>
<p>The rock dove was first described by German naturalist <a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Gmelin" title="Johann Friedrich Gmelin">Johann Gmelin</a> in 1789.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The genus name <i>Columba</i> is the Latin word meaning "pigeon, dove",<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> whose older etymology comes from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> κόλυμβος (<i>kolumbos</i>), "a diver", from <i>κολυμβάω</i> (kolumbao), "dive, plunge headlong, swim".<sup id="cite_ref-Liddell_1980_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Liddell_1980-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes" title="Aristophanes">Aristophanes</a> (Birds, 304) and others use the word κολυμβίς (<i>kolumbis</i>), "diver", for the name of the bird, because of its swimming motion in the air. The specific epithet is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>livor</i>, "bluish".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Its closest relative in the genus <i>Columba</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Hill_pigeon" title="Hill pigeon">hill pigeon</a>, followed by the other rock pigeons: the <a href="/wiki/Snow_pigeon" title="Snow pigeon">snow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Speckled_pigeon" title="Speckled pigeon">speckled</a>, and <a href="/wiki/White-collared_pigeon" title="White-collared pigeon">white-collared pigeons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The official common name is <b>rock dove</b>, as given by the <a href="/wiki/International_Ornithological_Congress" class="mw-redirect" title="International Ornithological Congress">International Ornithological Congress</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  Pigeon chicks are called squabs.<sup id="cite_ref-Levi_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levi-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The rock dove was central to <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwins</a> discovery of <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>, and featured in four of his works from 1859 to 1872. Darwin posited, that despite wide-ranging morphological differences, the many hundreds of breeds of domestic pigeons could all be traced back to the wild rock dove; in essence human selection of pigeon breeds was analogous to <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Twelve subspecies are recognised by Gibbs (2000); some of these may be derived from feral stock.<sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>176–179</span></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The <b>European rock dove</b> (<i>C. l. livia</i>) – The <a href="/wiki/Nominate_race" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate race">nominate subspecies</a>; it occurs in western and southern Europe, northern Africa, and Asia to western <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>, the northern <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>.</li>
<li>The <b>Cape Verde rock dove</b> (<i>C. l. atlantis</i>) <small>(<a href="/wiki/David_Armitage_Bannerman" title="David Armitage Bannerman">Bannerman</a>, 1931)</small> – Found in <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>, it is a very variable population with chequered upperparts obscuring the black wingbars, and is almost certainly derived from feral pigeons.</li>
<li>The <b>Canary Islands rock dove</b> (<i>C. l. canariensis</i>) <small>(Bannerman, 1914)</small> – Found in the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, it is smaller and is usually darker than the nominate subspecies.</li>
<li>The <b>Senegal rock dove</b> (<i>C. l. gymnocyclus</i>) <small>(Gray, 1856)</small> – Found from <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guinea" title="Guinea">Guinea</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benin" title="Benin">Benin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, it is smaller and very much darker than the nominate. It is almost blackish on the head, rump, and underparts with a white back and the iridescence of the nape extending onto the head.</li>
<li>The <b>Saharan rock dove</b> (<i>C. l. targia</i>) <small>(Geyr von Schweppenburg, 1916)</small> – It breeds in the mountains of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> east to <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>. It is slightly smaller than the nominate form, with similar plumage, but the back is concolorous with the mantle instead of white.</li>
<li>The <b>oasis rock dove</b> (<i>C. l. dakhlae</i>) <small>(<a href="/wiki/Richard_Meinertzhagen" title="Richard Meinertzhagen">Richard Meinertzhagen</a>, 1928)</small> – Found only in two oases in central <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, it is smaller and much paler than the nominate subspecies.</li>
<li>The <b>Egyptian rock dove</b> (<i>C. l. schimperi</i>) <small>(<a href="/wiki/Charles_Lucien_Bonaparte" title="Charles Lucien Bonaparte">Bonaparte</a>, 1854)</small> – Found in the <a href="/wiki/Nile_Delta" title="Nile Delta">Nile Delta</a> south to northern Sudan, it closely resembles <i>C. l. targia</i>, but has a distinctly paler mantle.</li>
<li>The <b>Arabian rock dove</b> (<i>C. l. palaestinae</i>) <small>(Zedlitz, 1912)</small> – Ranging from <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sinai_Peninsula" title="Sinai Peninsula">Sinai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabia">Arabia</a>, it is slightly larger than <i>C. l. schimperi</i> and has darker plumage.</li>
<li>The <b>Iranian rock dove</b> (<i>C. l. gaddi</i>) <small>(Zarodney &amp; Looudoni, 1906)</small> – Breeds from <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> east to <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a> and is larger and paler than <i>C. l. palaestinae,</i> with which it <a href="/wiki/Intergrade" class="mw-redirect" title="Intergrade">intergrades</a> in the west. It also intergrades with the next subspecies to the east.</li>
<li><b>Hume's rock dove</b> (<i>C. l. neglecta</i>) <small>(Hume, 1873)</small> – Found in the mountains of eastern Central Asia, it is similar to the nominate subspecies in size, but is darker with a stronger and more extensive iridescent sheen on the neck. It intergrades with the next race in the south.</li>
<li>The <b>Indian rock dove</b> (<i>C. l. intermedia</i>) <small>(<a href="/wiki/Hugh_Edwin_Strickland" title="Hugh Edwin Strickland">Strickland</a>, 1844)</small> – Occurs in <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> and in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> south of the <a href="/wiki/Himalaya" class="mw-redirect" title="Himalaya">Himalayan</a> range of <i>C. l. neglecta</i>. It is similar to that subspecies, but darker with a less contrasting back.</li>
<li>The <b>Mongolian rock dove</b> (<i>C. l. nigricans</i>) <small>(<a href="/wiki/Sergei_Aleksandrovich_Buturlin" class="mw-redirect" title="Sergei Aleksandrovich Buturlin">Buturlin</a>, 1908)</small> – Found in <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, it is variable and probably derived from feral pigeons.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pigeon_portrait_4861.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Pigeon_portrait_4861.jpg/170px-Pigeon_portrait_4861.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="191" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Pigeon_portrait_4861.jpg/255px-Pigeon_portrait_4861.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Pigeon_portrait_4861.jpg/340px-Pigeon_portrait_4861.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pigeon_portrait_4861.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A distinctive <a href="/wiki/Operculum_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Operculum (bird)">operculum</a> is located on top of the beak.</div></div></div>
<p>The adult of the nominate subspecies of the rock dove is 29 to 37&#160;cm (11 to 15&#160;in) long with a 62 to 72&#160;cm (24 to 28&#160;in) wingspan.<sup id="cite_ref-Jahan_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jahan-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Weight for wild or feral rock doves ranges from 238–380&#160;g (8.4–13.4&#160;oz), though overfed domestic and semidomestic individuals can exceed normal weights.<sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It has a dark bluish-grey head, neck, and chest with glossy yellowish, greenish, and reddish-purple iridescence along its neck and wing feathers. The iris is orange, red, or golden with a paler inner ring, and the bare skin round the eye is bluish-grey. The bill is grey-black with a conspicuous off-white cere, and the feet are purplish-red. Among standard measurements, the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> is typically around 22.3&#160;cm (8.8&#160;in), the <a href="/wiki/Tail" title="Tail">tail</a> is 9.5 to 11&#160;cm (3.7 to 4.3&#160;in), the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> is around 1.8&#160;cm (0.71&#160;in), and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> is 2.6 to 3.5&#160;cm (1.0 to 1.4&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The adult female is almost identical in outward appearance to the male, but the iridescence on her neck is less intense and more restricted to the rear and sides, whereas that on the breast is often very obscure.<sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The white lower back of the pure rock dove is its best identification characteristic; the two black bars on its pale grey wings are also distinctive. The tail has a black band on the end, and the outer web of the tail feathers are margined with white. It is strong and quick on the wing, dashing out from sea caves, flying low over the water, its lighter grey rump showing well from above.<sup id="cite_ref-White_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Self-published_sources" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on a self-published source. (July 2019)">self-published source?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Young birds show little lustre and are duller. Eye colour of the pigeon is generally orange, but a few pigeons may have white-grey eyes. The eyelids are orange and encapsulated in a grey-white eye ring. The feet are red to pink.<sup id="cite_ref-Levi_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levi-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>When circling overhead, the white underwing of the bird becomes conspicuous. In its flight, behaviour, and voice, which is more of a dovecot <i>coo</i> than the phrase of the <a href="/wiki/Common_wood_pigeon" title="Common wood pigeon">wood pigeon</a>, it is a typical pigeon. Although it is a relatively strong flier, it also glides frequently, holding its wings in a very pronounced V shape as it does.<sup id="cite_ref-Wright_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wright-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> As prey birds, they must keep their vigilance, and when disturbed a pigeon within a flock will take off with a noisy clapping sound that cues for other pigeons to take to flight. The noise of the take-off increases the faster a pigeon beats its wings, thus advertising the magnitude of a perceived threat to its flockmates.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Pigeons feed on the ground in flocks or individually. Pigeons are naturally granivorous, eating seeds that fit down their gullet. They may sometimes consume small invertebrates such as worms or insect larvae as a protein supplement. As they do not possess an enlarged cecum as in European wood pigeons, they cannot digest adult plant tissue; the various seeds they eat containing the appropriate nutrients they require.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-audubon.org_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-audubon.org-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> While most birds take small sips and tilt their heads backwards when drinking, pigeons are able to dip their bills into the water and drink continuously, without having to tilt their heads back. In cities they typically resort to scavenging human garbage, as unprocessed grain may be impossible to find. Pigeon groups typically consist of producers, which locate and obtain food, and scroungers, which feed on food obtained by the producers. Generally, groups of pigeons contain a greater proportion of scroungers than producers.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Pigeons, especially <a href="/wiki/Homing_pigeon" title="Homing pigeon">homing</a> or <a href="/wiki/Carrier_pigeon" class="mw-redirect" title="Carrier pigeon">carrier</a> breeds, are well known for their ability to find their way home from long distances. Despite these demonstrated abilities, wild rock doves are sedentary and rarely leave their local areas. It is hypothesized that in their natural, arid habitat, they rely on this sense to navigate back home after foraging as deserts rarely possess navigational landmarks that may be used.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A rock pigeon's lifespan ranges from 3–5 years in the wild to 15 years in captivity, though longer-lived specimens have been reported.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The main causes of mortality in the wild are predators and persecution by humans.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The species was first introduced to <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> in 1606 at <a href="/wiki/Habitation_at_Port-Royal" class="mw-redirect" title="Habitation at Port-Royal">Port Royal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-White_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Osmoregulation">Osmoregulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Osmoregulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Osmoregulation_in_rock_doves" title="Osmoregulation in rock doves">Osmoregulation in rock doves</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rock_pigeons_on_cliffs.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Rock_pigeons_on_cliffs.jpg/220px-Rock_pigeons_on_cliffs.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Rock_pigeons_on_cliffs.jpg/330px-Rock_pigeons_on_cliffs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Rock_pigeons_on_cliffs.jpg/440px-Rock_pigeons_on_cliffs.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rock_pigeons_on_cliffs.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rock doves perched on sea <a href="/wiki/Cliff" title="Cliff">cliffs</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a>, England</div></div></div>
<p>The rock dove has a restricted natural resident range in western and southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, and extending into <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>. It is often found in pairs in the breeding season, but is usually gregarious.<sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The species (including ferals) has a large range, with an estimated global extent of occurrence of 10,000,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (3,900,000&#160;sq&#160;mi). It has a large global population, including an estimated 17 to 28 million individuals in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">Fossil</a> evidence suggests the rock dove originated in southern Asia, and skeletal remains, unearthed in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, confirm its existence there for at least three-hundred thousand years.<sup id="cite_ref-Blechman_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blechman-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> However, this species has such a long history with humans that it is impossible to identify the species' original range exactly.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Pigeons in the wild reside in rock formations, settling in crevices to nest. They nest communally, often forming large colonies of many hundreds of individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-audubon.org_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-audubon.org-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Feral pigeons are usually unable to find these accommodations, so they must nest on building ledges, walls or statues. They may damage these structures via their feces; starving birds can only excrete urates, which over time corrodes masonry and metal. In contrast, a well-fed bird passes mostly solid feces, containing only small amounts of uric acid.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior_and_ecology">Behavior and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behavior and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The rock dove breeds at any time of the year, but peak times are spring and summer. Nesting sites are along coastal cliff faces, as well as the artificial cliff faces created by apartment buildings with accessible ledges or roof spaces.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The nest is a flimsy platform of straw and sticks, laid on a ledge, under cover, often on the window ledges of buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Two white eggs are laid; incubation, shared by both parents, lasts 17 to 19 days.<sup id="cite_ref-Levi_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levi-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The newly hatched <a href="/wiki/Domestic_pigeon" title="Domestic pigeon">squab</a> (<a href="/wiki/Nestling" class="mw-redirect" title="Nestling">nestling</a>) has pale yellow down and a flesh-coloured bill with a dark band. For the first few days, the baby squabs are tended and fed (through regurgitation) exclusively on "<a href="/wiki/Crop_milk" title="Crop milk">crop milk</a>" (also called "pigeon milk" or "pigeon's milk"). The pigeon milk is produced in the crops of both parents in all species of pigeons and doves. The fledging period is about 30 days.<sup id="cite_ref-Jahan_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jahan-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rock doves are omnivorous, but prefer plant matter: chiefly fruits and grains.<sup id="cite_ref-:BOTW_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:BOTW-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preening">Preening</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Preening">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pigeons primarily use powder down feathers for preening, which gives a soft and silky feel to their plumage. They have no preen gland or at times have very rudimentary preen glands, so oil is not used for preening. Rather, powder down feathers are spread across the body. These have a tendency to disintegrate, and the powder, akin to talcum powder, helps maintain the plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-Grzimek&#39;s_Animal_Life_Encyclopedia_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grzimek&#39;s_Animal_Life_Encyclopedia-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Some varieties of domestic pigeons have modified feathers called "fat quills". These feathers contain yellow, oil-like fat that derives from the same cells as powder down. This is used while preening and helps reduce bacterial degradation of feathers by feather bacilli.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Survival">Survival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Survival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators">Predators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Predators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>With only their flying abilities protecting them from predation, rock pigeons are a favourite almost around the world for a wide range of raptors. In fact, with feral pigeons existing in almost every city in the world, they may form the majority of prey for several raptor species that live in urban areas. <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">Peregrine falcons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">Eurasian sparrowhawks</a> are natural predators of pigeons that are quite adept at catching and feeding upon this species. Up to 80% of the diet of peregrine falcons in several cities that have breeding falcons is composed of feral pigeons.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Some common predators of feral pigeons in North America are <a href="/wiki/Opossum" title="Opossum">opossums</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">raccoons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red-tailed_hawk" title="Red-tailed hawk">red-tailed hawks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_horned_owl" title="Great horned owl">great horned owls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_screech_owl" title="Eastern screech owl">eastern screech owls</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Accipiter" title="Accipiter">accipiters</a>. The birds that prey on pigeons in North America can range in size from <a href="/wiki/American_kestrel" title="American kestrel">American kestrels</a> to <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">golden eagles</a><sup id="cite_ref-Roof_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roof-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> and can even include <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crows</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Raven" title="Raven">ravens</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> On the ground, the adults, their young, and their eggs are at risk from feral and domestic <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Levi_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levi-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Doves and pigeons are considered to be <a href="/wiki/Game_(food)" class="mw-redirect" title="Game (food)">game birds</a>, as many species have been hunted and used for food in many of the countries in which they are native.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The body feathers have dense, fluffy bases and are loosely attached to the skin, hence they drop out easily. When a predator catches it, large numbers of feathers come out in the attacker's mouth, and the pigeon may use this temporary distraction to make an escape.<sup id="cite_ref-Grzimek&#39;s_Animal_Life_Encyclopedia_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grzimek&#39;s_Animal_Life_Encyclopedia-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> It also tends to drop the tail feathers when preyed upon or under traumatic conditions, probably as a distraction mechanism.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Pigeons may harbour a diverse parasite fauna.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
They often host the intestinal helminths <i>Capillaria columbae</i> and <i>Ascaridia columbae</i>. Their ectoparasites include the <a href="/wiki/Ischnocera" title="Ischnocera">ischnoceran</a> lice <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Columbicola_columbae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Columbicola columbae (page does not exist)">Columbicola columbae</a></i>, <i>Campanulotes bidentatus compar</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Amblycera" title="Amblycera">amblyceran</a> lice <i>Bonomiella columbae</i>, <i>Hohorstiella lata</i>, <i>Colpocephalum turbinatum</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Mites" class="mw-redirect" title="Mites">mites</a> <i>Tinaminyssus melloi</i>, <i>Dermanyssus gallinae</i>, <i>Dermoglyphus columbae</i>, <i>Falculifer rostratus</i>, and <i>Diplaegidia columbae</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Hippoboscidae" title="Hippoboscidae">hippoboscid</a> fly <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudolynchia_canariensis" title="Pseudolynchia canariensis">Pseudolynchia canariensis</a></i> is a typical blood-sucking ectoparasite of pigeons, found only in tropical and subtropical regions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_humans">Relationship to humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Relationship to humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestication">Domestication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Domestication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pigeon_Revivim_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Pigeon_Revivim_2.jpg/220px-Pigeon_Revivim_2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Pigeon_Revivim_2.jpg/330px-Pigeon_Revivim_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Pigeon_Revivim_2.jpg/440px-Pigeon_Revivim_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1125" data-file-height="735" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pigeon_Revivim_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Domestic pigeons</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Domestic_pigeon" title="Domestic pigeon">Domestic pigeon</a></div>
<p>Rock doves have been domesticated for several thousand years, giving rise to the <a href="/wiki/Domestic_pigeon" title="Domestic pigeon">domestic pigeon</a> (<i>Columba livia domestica</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Levi_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levi-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> They may have been domesticated as many as 5,000 years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Numerous <a href="/wiki/Breed" title="Breed">breeds</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fancy_pigeon" title="Fancy pigeon">fancy pigeons</a> of all sizes, colours, and types have been bred.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Domesticated pigeons are used as <a href="/wiki/Homing_pigeons" class="mw-redirect" title="Homing pigeons">homing pigeons</a> as well as food and pets. They were in the past also used as <a href="/wiki/Carrier_pigeons" class="mw-redirect" title="Carrier pigeons">carrier pigeons</a>, and so-called <a href="/wiki/War_pigeons" class="mw-redirect" title="War pigeons">war pigeons</a> have played significant roles during wartime, including <a href="/wiki/Julius_Neubronner" title="Julius Neubronner">delivering urgent medicines</a>, with many pigeons having received bravery awards and medals for their services in saving hundreds of human lives, including, notably, the British pigeon <a href="/wiki/Cher_Ami" title="Cher Ami">Cher Ami</a>, which received the <a href="/wiki/Croix_de_guerre_1914%E2%80%931918_(France)" title="Croix de guerre 1914–1918 (France)"><i>Croix de Guerre</i></a> for actions during World War I, and the Irish <a href="/wiki/Paddy_(pigeon)" title="Paddy (pigeon)">Paddy</a> and the American <a href="/wiki/G.I._Joe_(pigeon)" title="G.I. Joe (pigeon)">G.I. Joe</a>, which both received the <a href="/wiki/Dickin_Medal" title="Dickin Medal">Dickin Medal</a>, amongst 32 pigeons to receive this award, for their actions during World War II, more than any other species of animal <sup id="cite_ref-Levi_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levi-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Feral_pigeon">Feral pigeon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Feral pigeon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Feral_pigeon" title="Feral pigeon">Feral pigeon</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Feralpigeon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/0e/Feralpigeon.jpg/220px-Feralpigeon.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/0e/Feralpigeon.jpg/330px-Feralpigeon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/0e/Feralpigeon.jpg/440px-Feralpigeon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4608" data-file-height="3072" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Feralpigeon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Feral pigeon at Nanjing, China</div></div></div>
<p>Many domestic birds have got lost, escaped or been released over the years, and have given rise to feral pigeons. These show a variety of plumages, although many have the blue-barred pattern as does the pure rock dove. Feral pigeons are found in cities and towns all over the world.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The scarcity of the pure wild species is partly due to interbreeding with feral birds.<sup id="cite_ref-Wright_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wright-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_health">Human health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Human health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Contact with pigeon droppings poses a minor risk of contracting <a href="/wiki/Histoplasmosis" title="Histoplasmosis">histoplasmosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cryptococcosis" title="Cryptococcosis">cryptococcosis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Psittacosis" title="Psittacosis">psittacosis</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> and long-term exposure to both droppings and feathers can induce an allergy known as <a href="/wiki/Bird_fancier%27s_lung" title="Bird fancier&#39;s lung">bird fancier's lung</a>.
Pigeons are not a major concern in the spread of <a href="/wiki/West_Nile_virus" title="West Nile virus">West Nile virus</a>; though they can contract it, they do not appear to be able to transmit it.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Pigeons are, however, at potential risk for carrying and spreading <a href="/wiki/Avian_influenza" title="Avian influenza">avian influenza</a>. One study has shown that adult pigeons are not clinically susceptible to the most dangerous strain of avian influenza,  <a href="/wiki/H5N1" class="mw-redirect" title="H5N1">H5N1</a>, and that they did not transmit the virus to chickens.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid17994324_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17994324-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Other studies have presented evidence of clinical signs and neurological lesions resulting from infection, but found that the pigeons did not transmit the disease to chickens reared in direct contact with them.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid16846988_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16846988-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pmid17947534_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17947534-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Pigeons were found to be "resistant or minimally susceptible" to other strains of avian influenza, such as the <a href="/wiki/H7N7" class="mw-redirect" title="H7N7">H7N7</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid8883790_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid8883790-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
