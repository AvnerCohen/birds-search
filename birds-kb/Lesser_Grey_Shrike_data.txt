<p>The <b>lesser grey shrike</b> (<i>Lanius minor</i>) is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Shrike" title="Shrike">shrike</a> family <i>Laniidae</i>. It breeds in South and Central Europe and <a href="/wiki/Western_Asia" title="Western Asia">western Asia</a> in the summer and <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrates</a> to winter quarters in southern Africa in the early autumn, returning in spring. It is a scarce vagrant to western Europe, including <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, usually as a spring or autumn erratic.
</p><p>It is similar in appearance to the <a href="/wiki/Great_grey_shrike" title="Great grey shrike">great grey shrike</a> <i>Lanius excubitor</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_grey_shrike" title="Iberian grey shrike">Iberian grey shrike</a> <i>Lanius meridionalis</i> being predominantly black, white and grey, with the males having pink-flushed underparts. It is slightly smaller than the great grey shrike, and has a black forehead and relatively longer wings. This species prefers dry open lowlands, and is often seen on telephone wires.
</p><p>This medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Perching_bird" class="mw-redirect" title="Perching bird">passerine</a> eats large <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, butterflies, moths and grasshoppers. Like other shrikes it hunts from prominent perches and sometimes impales corpses on thorns or barbed wire as a "larder".
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_grey_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lesser grey shrike was first described by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Gmelin" title="Johann Friedrich Gmelin">Johann Friedrich Gmelin</a> in 1788. There are two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>, <i>Lanius minor minor</i> and <i>Lanius minor turanicus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The genus name, <i><a href="/wiki/Lanius" title="Lanius">Lanius</a></i>, is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word for "<a href="/wiki/Butcher" title="Butcher">butcher</a>", and some shrikes are also known as "butcher birds" because of their feeding habits. The specific <i>minor</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "smaller".<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The common English name "shrike" is from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>scríc</i>, "shriek", referring to the shrill call.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_grey_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The adult male lesser grey shrike has its nape, cheeks, ear and eye <a href="/wiki/Covert_feather" title="Covert feather">coverts</a> and front part of the crown black. The hind part of the crown and the back is a pale bluish-grey and the rump is a similar but rather paler colour. The underparts are white with the lower breast and belly suffused with pink. The axillaries are greyish-white and the underwing coverts are brownish-black. The two central tail feathers are black with a white tip and base. The other pairs have increasing areas of white and less black. The <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">primaries</a> are black with a buff tip and white base. The <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">secondaries</a> are black with broader, paler tips but no white bases. The wing coverts are black with the lesser coverts being fringed with grey. The female has similar plumage but the head is dark grey rather than black, the ear coverts brownish-black, the upperparts a brownish-grey and the underparts less pink than the male. The juvenile is similar to the adults but is altogether more brown. It lacks the grey back and rump which are instead pale brown and faintly barred, and the underparts are white and cream without any pink. All birds have a brownish-black beak with a paler base to the lower mandible, brown irises and black legs and feet.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Adult length is around 20&#160;cm (7.9&#160;in) with a wing length of 13&#160;cm (5.1&#160;in) and a <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> length of 2.5&#160;cm (1&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Coward_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_grey_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lesser grey shrike spends the summer in South and Central Europe and western Asia. It breeds in southern France, Switzerland, Austria, Czech Republic, Italy, the former Yugoslavia, Albania, Greece, Romania, Bulgaria and southern Russia. In Asia it breeds in the Middle East, its range extending as far as eastern Turkey and Iran. It is a vagrant to more northerly parts of Europe, usually in spring or autumn. Countries where it has been seen include Sweden, Finland, Denmark, United Kingdom, Netherlands, Belgium and northern France. It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> species and winters in a broad belt across tropical southern Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Coward_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the summer the lesser grey shrike inhabits open countryside, the edges of cultivated areas, <a href="/wiki/Heathland" class="mw-redirect" title="Heathland">heathland</a> with scattered bushes and trees, gardens, coppices, woodland and roadside trees. In its winter quarters it is usually found in <a href="/wiki/Scrubland" class="mw-redirect" title="Scrubland">scrubland</a> and among <a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">thorn trees</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The lesser grey shrike hunts from a strategic post, wire or branch and primarily feeds on insects which it catches in the air or on the ground. The diet includes beetles, moths and butterflies, large flies, grasshoppers, crickets and millipedes. Some fruits such as cherries and figs are eaten to a limited extent.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The bird occasionally impales freshly caught prey on thorns for use later, but this is done to a much lesser extent than by some other shrike species. It has been shown experimentally that this species only creates a larder when it is satiated and that prey items are seldom plentiful enough for this to be the case. The male has been observed to feed the female before starting to create a food store. Repeated experimental exposure of birds to a food surplus significantly increases the rate at which they impale freshly caught prey. Other causal factors in the failure of this species to hoard may be a shortage of suitable caching sites and the fact that the bird lacks practice in storing food and this constrains its learning ability.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The flight of the lesser grey shrike is low and somewhat undulating and it occasionally glides with extended wings. At the end of the flight it swoops upward to land on a new hunting perch. It then turns its head from side to side searching for prey. When on the ground it hops, but it normally only stays there for long enough to pick up an item of food. Like other shrikes, when excited it fans its tail and moves it up and down or from sided to side. It is pugnacious and will defend its nest with vigour and drive away larger birds.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_grey_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lanius_minor_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.214._Szigetesch_Hongrie_Balsac.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Lanius_minor_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.214._Szigetesch_Hongrie_Balsac.jpg/220px-Lanius_minor_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.214._Szigetesch_Hongrie_Balsac.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="191" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Lanius_minor_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.214._Szigetesch_Hongrie_Balsac.jpg/330px-Lanius_minor_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.214._Szigetesch_Hongrie_Balsac.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Lanius_minor_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.214._Szigetesch_Hongrie_Balsac.jpg/440px-Lanius_minor_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.214._Szigetesch_Hongrie_Balsac.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3536" data-file-height="3069" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lanius_minor_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.214._Szigetesch_Hongrie_Balsac.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Lanius minor</i> - <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9um_de_Toulouse" title="Muséum de Toulouse">Muséum de Toulouse</a></div></div></div>
<p>The nest is often built in a roadside tree with good all-round visibility some 4 to 10 metres (13 to 33&#160;ft) off the ground. It is built by both birds out of the stems of various flowering plants such as cudweed  (<i><a href="/wiki/Gnaphalium" title="Gnaphalium">Gnaphalium</a></i>) and (<i><a href="/wiki/Filago_(plant)" title="Filago (plant)">Filago</a></i>), and thyme (<i><a href="/wiki/Thymus_(plant)" title="Thymus (plant)">Thymus</a></i>), and lined with whisps of wool, hairs, roots and feathers. There are five to seven eggs in the clutch, usually bluish green blotched with greenish-brown. Sometimes the background colour is cream or buff with pinkish-brown blotches. Their average size is 26.1 by 18.2 millimetres (1.03&#160;in ×&#160;0.72&#160;in). Incubation lasts about fifteen days and is chiefly done by the female though both parents take part. They both feed the young which are ready to leave the nest after about fourteen days. There is usually a single brood.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_grey_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lesser grey shrike has a world population estimated as being between two and a half and nine million individuals. These are spread out over a wide range and the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>, in its <a href="/wiki/Red_List_of_Threatened_Species" class="mw-redirect" title="Red List of Threatened Species">Red List of Threatened Species</a>, lists the bird as being of "<a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>". Though the number of birds may be decreasing slightly, this is not thought to be sufficient to raise the species to the more threatened "<a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">Vulnerable</a>" category.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Europe the numbers of lesser grey shrikes has been dwindling and there are now thought to be three to five thousand breeding pairs. The diminution in numbers may be due to a series of wet summers leading to a dearth of insects. Another factor may be changes in agricultural practices with small enclosures with varying crops and patches of woodland being replaced by large fields. As a result, this bird has been listed as a threatened species in Annex I of the European Commission <a href="/wiki/Birds_Directive" title="Birds Directive">Birds Directive</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
