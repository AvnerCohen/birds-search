<p>The <b>Hooded Crow</b> (<i>Corvus cornix</i>) (sometimes called <b>Hoodiecrow</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasian</a> bird species in the <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crow</a> <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a>. Widely distributed, it is also known locally as <b>Scotch Crow</b>, <b>Danish Crow</b>, and <b>Corbie</b> or <b>Grey Crow</b> in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>; Grey Crow is also what its Welsh name, <b>Brân Lwyd</b>, translates as. Found across <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_Europe" title="Southeastern Europe" class="mw-redirect">Southeastern Europe</a>, as well as parts of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, it is an ashy grey bird with black head, throat, wings, tail and thigh feathers, as well as a black bill, eyes and feet. Like other corvids it is an omnivorous and opportunistic forager and feeder.</p>
<p>It is so similar in morphology and habits to the <a href="/wiki/Carrion_Crow" title="Carrion Crow">Carrion Crow</a> (<i>Corvus corone</i>) that for many years they were considered by most authorities to be merely geographical races of one species. The fact that hybridization was observed where their ranges overlapped added weight to this view. However, since 2002, the Hooded Crow has been elevated to full species status after closer observation; the hybridisation was less than expected and hybrids had decreased vigour. Within the Hooded Crow species, four subspecies are recognized, with one, the <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamian_Crow" title="Mesopotamian Crow">Mesopotamian Crow</a>, possibly distinct enough to warrant species status itself.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hooded_Crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The Hooded Crow was one of the many species originally described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in his 18th-century work <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> and it once again bears its original name of <i>Corvus cornix</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The binomial name is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> words <i>Corvus</i>, "Raven",<sup id="cite_ref-M-W_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M-W-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> and <i>cornix</i>, "crow".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> It was subsequently considered a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of the Carrion Crow for many years,<sup id="cite_ref-observerbook_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-observerbook-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> and hence known as <i>Corvus corone cornix</i>, due to similarities in structure and habits. Since 2002, it has been re-elevated to full species status<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material in the vicinity of this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s) from July 2010">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-Parkin03_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parkin03-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is locally known as a <i>Hoodie</i> in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hooded_Crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span></h3>
<p>Four subspecies of the Hooded Crow are now recognised; previously all were considered subspecies of <i>Corvus corone</i>. A fifth, <i>Corvus cornix sardonius</i> (Trischitta, 1939) has been listed though it has been alternately partitioned between <i>C. c. sharpii</i> (most populations), <i>C. c. cornix</i> (Corsican population) and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle Eastern</a> <i>C. c. pallescens</i>.</p>
<ul>
<li><i><b>C. c. cornix</b></i>, the nominate race, occurs in the British Isles (principally Scotland and Ireland) and Europe, south to <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>.</li>
<li><i><b>C. c. pallescens</b></i> (Madarász, 1904) is found in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, and is a paler form as its name suggests.</li>
<li><i><b>C. c. sharpii</b></i> (Oates, 1889) is named for <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bowdler_Sharpe" title="Richard Bowdler Sharpe">Richard Bowdler Sharpe</a>. This is a paler grey form found from western <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> through to the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> region and <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CotW83_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CotW83-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i><b>C. c. capellanus</b></i> (P.L. Sclater, 1877) is known as the <b><a href="/wiki/Mesopotamian_Crow" title="Mesopotamian Crow">Mesopotamian Crow</a></b> or <b>Iraqi Pied Crow</b>. This distinctive form occurs in <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> and southwestern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. It has very pale grey plumage which looks almost white from a distance.<sup id="cite_ref-CotW83_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CotW83-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> It is possibly distinct enough to be considered a separate species.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>

<p>Except for the head, throat, wings, tail and thigh feathers, which are black and mostly glossy, the plumage is ash-grey, the dark shafts giving it a streaky appearance. The bill and legs are black; the iris dark brown. There is only one moult, in autumn, as in other crow species. The male is the larger bird, otherwise the sexes are alike. The flight is slow and heavy and usually straight. The length varies from 48 to 52&#160;cm (19 to 20&#160;in). When first hatched the young are much blacker than the parents. Juveniles have duller plumage with bluish or greyish eyes and initially a red mouth. Wingspan is 98&#160;cm (39&#160;in) and weight is on average 510&#160;grammes.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Hooded Crow, with its contrasted greys and blacks, cannot be confused with either the Carrion Crow or <a href="/wiki/Rook_(bird)" title="Rook (bird)">Rook</a>, but the <span class="unicode"><a href="/wiki/File:Corvus_cornix.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" /></a>&#160;<a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/91/Corvus_cornix.ogg" class="internal" title="Corvus cornix.ogg"><i>kraa</i></a><small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">&#160;(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:Corvus_cornix.ogg" title="File:Corvus cornix.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span> call notes of the two are almost indistinguishable.<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The Hooded Crow breeds in northern and eastern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, and closely allied forms inhabit southern Europe and western <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. Where its range overlaps with Carrion Crow, as in northern <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, their hybrids are fertile. However, the hybrids are less well-adapted than pure-bred birds, and this is one of the reasons that this species was split from the Carrion Crow.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> There are some areas, such as Iran and central Russia, where little or no interbreeding occurs.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, the Hooded Crow breeds regularly in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, and in the Scottish Islands. It also breeds widely in Ireland. In autumn some <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> arrive on the east coast of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a>. In the past, this was a more common visitor, and in <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire" title="Hertfordshire">Hertfordshire</a> was known as the <b>Royston Crow</b> after the town of <a href="/wiki/Royston,_Hertfordshire" title="Royston, Hertfordshire">Royston</a>. The 150-year old local newspaper is still titled <i><a href="/wiki/Royston_Crow_(newspaper)" title="Royston Crow (newspaper)">Royston Crow</a></i>, and depicts the bird’s head on its masthead.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hooded_Crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hooded_Crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CrowWastebag.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/CrowWastebag.jpg/220px-CrowWastebag.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CrowWastebag.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Searching for food from punctured wastebag</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Hooded Crow is omnivorous, with a diet similar to that of the Carrion Crow, and is a constant scavenger. It drops <a href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc" class="mw-redirect">molluscs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a> to break them after the manner of the Carrion Crow, and an old <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> name for empty <a href="/wiki/Sea_urchin" title="Sea urchin">sea urchin</a> shells was "crow's cups".<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> On coastal cliffs the eggs of <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Cormorant" title="Great Cormorant">cormorants</a> and other birds are stolen when their owners are absent, and it will enter the burrow of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Puffin" title="Atlantic Puffin">Puffin</a> to steal eggs. It will also feed on small mammals, scraps, smaller birds and carrion.</p>

<p>Nesting occurs later in colder regions: mid-May to mid-June in northwest <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shetland_Islands" title="Shetland Islands" class="mw-redirect">Shetland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, and late February in the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> region.<sup id="cite_ref-CotW83_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CotW83-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> In warmer parts of the British Isles, the clutch is laid in April.<sup id="cite_ref-Observerbirdseggs_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Observerbirdseggs-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The bulky stick nest is normally placed in a tall tree, but cliff ledges, old buildings and pylons may be used. Nests are occasionally placed on or near the ground. The nest resembles that of the Carrion Crow, but on the coast seaweed is often interwoven in the structure, and animal bones and wire are also frequently incorporated.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BWP_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The four to six brown-speckled blue eggs are 4.3 x 3.0&#160;centimetres (1.7 x 1.2&#160;in) in size and weigh 19.8&#160;grammes (0.71&#160;oz), of which 6% is shell.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Altricial" title="Altricial">altricial</a> young are incubated for 17–19 days&#160;by the female alone, who is fed by the male. They fledge after 32 to 36&#160;days. Incubating females have been reported to obtain most of their own food and later that for their young.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The typical lifespan is unknown, but that of the Carrion Crow is four years.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO2_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO2-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The maximum recorded age for a Hooded Crow is 16 years, and 9 months old.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species is a secondary host of the <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">parasitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Great_Spotted_Cuckoo" title="Great Spotted Cuckoo">Great Spotted Cuckoo</a>, the <a href="/wiki/European_Magpie" title="European Magpie" class="mw-redirect">European Magpie</a> being the preferred host. However, in areas where the latter species is absent, such as <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, the Hooded Crow becomes the normal corvid host.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP2_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP2-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species, like its relative, is seen regularly killed by farmers and on grouse estates. In <a href="/wiki/County_Cork" title="County Cork">County Cork</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> the county's gun clubs shot over 23,000 Hooded Crows in two years in the early 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hooded_Crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> does not distinguish the Hooded Crow from the Carrion Crow, but the two species together have an extensive range, estimated at 10&#160;million&#160;square kilometres (3.8&#160;million square miles), and a large population, including an estimated 14 to 34 million individuals in Europe alone. They are not believed to approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the IUCN Red List (i.e., declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations), and are therefore evaluated as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> The Carrion Crow/Hooded Crow hybrid zone is slowly spreading northwest, but the Hooded Crow has of the order of three million territories in just Europe (excluding Russia).<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hooded_Crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cultural significance">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_significance">Cultural significance</span></h2>
<p>In Celtic folklore, the bird appears on the shoulder of the dying <a href="/wiki/C%C3%BA_Chulainn" title="Cú Chulainn">Cú Chulainn</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-folkloreofbirds2_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-folkloreofbirds2-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> and could also be a manifestation of the <a href="/wiki/Morr%C3%ADgan" title="Morrígan" class="mw-redirect">Morrígan</a>, the wife of <a href="/wiki/Tethra" title="Tethra">Tethra</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Cailleach" title="Cailleach">Cailleach</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> This idea has persisted, and the Hooded Crow is associated with fairies in the Scottish highlands and Ireland; in the 18th century, Scottish shepherds would make offerings to them to keep them from attacking sheep.<sup id="cite_ref-ingersoll23_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ingersoll23-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroese</a> folklore, a maiden would go out on <a href="/wiki/Candlemas" title="Candlemas" class="mw-redirect">Candlemas</a> morn and throw a stone, then a bone, then a clump of turf at a Hooded Crow – if it flew over the sea, her husband would be a foreigner; if it landed on a farm or house, she would marry a man from there; but if it stayed put, she would remain unmarried.<sup id="cite_ref-folkloreofbirds_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-folkloreofbirds-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Hooded Crow is featured on the crest of the North Hertfordshire District Council.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> it is also one of the 37 Norwegian birds depicted in the Bird Room of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Palace,_Oslo" title="Royal Palace, Oslo">Royal Palace</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jethro_Tull_(band)" title="Jethro Tull (band)">Jethro Tull</a> mentions the Hooded Crow on the song "Jack Frost and the Hooded Crow" as a bonus track on the digitally remastered version of <i><a href="/wiki/Broadsword_and_the_Beast" title="Broadsword and the Beast" class="mw-redirect">Broadsword and the Beast</a></i> and on their <i>The Christmas Album</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
