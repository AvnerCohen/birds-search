<p>The <b>hooded crow</b> (<i>Corvus cornix</i>) (also called <b>hoodie</b>)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasian</a> bird species in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Corvus" title="Corvus">Corvus</a></i>. Widely distributed, it is also known locally as  <b>Scotch crow</b> and <b>Danish crow</b>. In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, it is called <i>carÃ³g liath</i> or <b>grey crow</b>, as it is in the <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic languages</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a>. In German, it is called "mist crow" ("NebelkrÃ¤he"). Found across <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="Southeastern Europe">Southeastern Europe</a>, as well as parts of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, it is an ashy grey bird with black head, throat, wings, tail, and thigh feathers, as well as a black bill, eyes, and feet. Like other corvids, it is an omnivorous and opportunistic forager and feeder.
</p><p>It is so similar in morphology and habits to the <a href="/wiki/Carrion_crow" title="Carrion crow">carrion crow</a> (<i>Corvus corone</i>), for many years they were considered by most authorities to be geographical races of one species. Hybridization observed where their ranges overlapped added weight to this view. However, since 2002, the hooded crow has been elevated to full species status after closer observation; the hybridisation was less than expected and hybrids had decreased vigour.<sup id="cite_ref-Parkin03_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parkin03-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Poelstra_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Poelstra-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Within the hooded crow species, four subspecies are recognized, with one, the <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamian_crow" title="Mesopotamian crow">Mesopotamian crow</a>, possibly distinct enough to warrant species status itself.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hooded_crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The hooded crow was one of the many species originally described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his landmark 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a> and it bears its original name <i>Corvus cornix</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The binomial name is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> words <i>corvus</i>, "raven",<sup id="cite_ref-M-W_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M-W-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and <i>cornix</i>, "crow".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It was subsequently considered a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of the carrion crow for many years,<sup id="cite_ref-observerbook_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-observerbook-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> hence known as <i>Corvus corone cornix</i>, due to similarities in structure and habits.
</p><p>It is locally known as a hoodie in Scotland and as a grey crow in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hooded_crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Four subspecies of the hooded crow are now recognised; previously, all were considered subspecies of <i>Corvus corone</i>. A fifth, the <b>Sardinian hooded crow</b> (<i>C. c. sardonius</i>) (Trischitta, 1939) has been listed, although it has been alternately partitioned between <i>C. c. sharpii</i> (most populations), <i>C. c. cornix</i> (Corsican population), and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle Eastern</a> <i>C. c. pallescens</i>.
</p>
<ul><li>The <b>northern European hooded crow</b> (<i>C. c. cornix</i>), the nominate race, occurs in Britain, Ireland and the rest of Europe south to <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>.</li>
<li>The <b>eastern Mediterranean hooded crow</b> (<i>C. c. pallescens</i>) (MadarÃ¡sz, 1904) is found in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, and is a paler form as its name suggests.</li>
<li>The <b>southern European hooded crow</b> (<i>C. c. sharpii</i>) (Oates, 1889) is named for <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bowdler_Sharpe" title="Richard Bowdler Sharpe">Richard Bowdler Sharpe</a>. This is a paler grey form found from western <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> through to the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> region and <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CotW83_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CotW83-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <b>Mesopotamian hooded crow</b> (<i>C. c. capellanus</i>) (P. L. Sclater, 1877) is sometimes considered a separate species called the <b><a href="/wiki/Mesopotamian_crow" title="Mesopotamian crow">Mesopotamian crow</a></b> or <b>Iraqi pied crow</b>. This distinctive form occurs in <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> and southwestern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. It has very pale grey plumage, which looks almost white from a distance.<sup id="cite_ref-CotW83_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CotW83-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> It is possibly distinct enough to be considered a separate species.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetic_difference_from_carrion_crows">Genetic difference from carrion crows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hooded_crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Genetic difference from carrion crows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Distribution_of_carrion_and_hooded_crows_across_Europe.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Distribution_of_carrion_and_hooded_crows_across_Europe.jpg/250px-Distribution_of_carrion_and_hooded_crows_across_Europe.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Distribution_of_carrion_and_hooded_crows_across_Europe.jpg/375px-Distribution_of_carrion_and_hooded_crows_across_Europe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Distribution_of_carrion_and_hooded_crows_across_Europe.jpg/500px-Distribution_of_carrion_and_hooded_crows_across_Europe.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5000" data-file-height="4412" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Distribution_of_carrion_and_hooded_crows_across_Europe.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A map of Europe indicating the distribution of the <a href="/wiki/Carrion_crow" title="Carrion crow">carrion</a> and hooded crows on either side of a contact zone (white line) separating the two species.</div></div></div><p> The hooded crow (<i>Corvus cornix</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Carrion_crow" title="Carrion crow">carrion crow</a> (<i>Corvus corone</i>) are two closely related species whose geographical distribution across Europe is illustrated in the accompanying diagram. It is believed that this distribution might have resulted from the glaciation cycles during the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>, which caused the parent population to split into isolates which subsequently re-expanded their ranges when the climate warmed causing secondary contact.<sup id="cite_ref-Poelstra_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Poelstra-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Knijf_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knijf-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Poelstra and coworkers sequenced almost the entire genomes of both species in populations at varying distances from the contact zone to find that the two species were genetically identical, both in their DNA and in its expression (in the form of <i>m</i>RNA), except for the <i>lack of expression</i> of a small portion (&lt;0.28%) of the genome (situated on avian chromosome 18) in the hooded crow, which imparts the lighter plumage colouration on its torso.<sup id="cite_ref-Poelstra_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Poelstra-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Thus the two species can viably hybridize, and occasionally do so at the contact zone, but the all-black carrion crows on the one side of the contact zone mate almost exclusively with other all-black carrion crows, while the same occurs among the hooded crows on the other side of the contact zone. It is therefore clear that it is only the outward appearance of the two species that inhibits hybridization.<sup id="cite_ref-Poelstra_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Poelstra-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Knijf_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knijf-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The authors attribute this to <a href="/wiki/Assortative_mating" title="Assortative mating">assortative mating</a> (rather than to ecological selection), the advantage of which is not clear, and it would lead to the rapid appearance of streams of new lineages, and possibly even species, through mutual attraction between mutants. Unnikrishnan and Akhila<sup id="cite_ref-Unnikrishnan2_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Unnikrishnan2-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> propose, instead, that <a href="/wiki/Koinophilia" title="Koinophilia">koinophilia</a> is a more parsimonious explanation for the resistance to hybridization across the contact zone, despite the absence of physiological, anatomical or genetic barriers to such hybridization.
</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hooded_crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Corvus_cornix_-Berlin,_Germany-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Corvus_cornix_-Berlin%2C_Germany-8.jpg/220px-Corvus_cornix_-Berlin%2C_Germany-8.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Corvus_cornix_-Berlin%2C_Germany-8.jpg/330px-Corvus_cornix_-Berlin%2C_Germany-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Corvus_cornix_-Berlin%2C_Germany-8.jpg/440px-Corvus_cornix_-Berlin%2C_Germany-8.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3008" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Corvus_cornix_-Berlin,_Germany-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, Germany</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Corvus_cornix_-Egypt-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Corvus_cornix_-Egypt-8.jpg/220px-Corvus_cornix_-Egypt-8.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Corvus_cornix_-Egypt-8.jpg/330px-Corvus_cornix_-Egypt-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Corvus_cornix_-Egypt-8.jpg/440px-Corvus_cornix_-Egypt-8.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1068" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Corvus_cornix_-Egypt-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In Egypt</div></div></div>
<p>Except for the head, throat, wings, tail, and thigh feathers, which are black and mostly glossy, the plumage is ash-grey, the dark shafts giving it a streaky appearance. The bill and legs are black; the iris dark brown. Only one moult occurs, in autumn, as in other crow species. The male is the larger bird, otherwise the sexes are alike. Their flight is slow and heavy and usually straight. Their length varies from 48 to 52&#160;cm (19 to 20&#160;in). When first hatched, the young are much blacker than the parents. Juveniles have duller plumage with bluish or greyish eyes and initially a red mouth. Wingspan is 105&#160;cm (41&#160;in) and weight is on average 510&#160;g.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The hooded crow, with its contrasted greys and blacks, cannot be confused with either the carrion crow or <a href="/wiki/Rook_(bird)" title="Rook (bird)">rook</a>, but the <span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Corvus_cornix.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" decoding="async" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a></span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/91/Corvus_cornix.ogg" class="internal" title="Corvus cornix.ogg"><i>kraa</i></a></span>&#160;<small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>Â·<a href="/wiki/File:Corvus_cornix.ogg" title="File:Corvus cornix.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span> call notes of the two are almost indistinguishable.<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hooded_crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Flying_Crow.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Flying_Crow.jpg/220px-Flying_Crow.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Flying_Crow.jpg/330px-Flying_Crow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Flying_Crow.jpg/440px-Flying_Crow.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flying_Crow.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In flight at <a href="/wiki/Isfahan" title="Isfahan">Isfahan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a></div></div></div>
<p>The hooded crow breeds in northern and eastern Europe, and closely allied forms inhabit southern Europe and western Asia. Where <a href="/wiki/Koinophilia#Rapid_emergence_of_reproductive_isolation_on_exposure_of_populations_to_different_diets_and_environmental_conditions" title="Koinophilia">its range overlaps with carrion crow</a>, as in northern <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, their hybrids are fertile. However, the hybrids are less well-adapted than purebred birds, and this is one of the reasons this species was split from the carrion crow.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Little or no interbreeding occurs in some areas, such as Iran and central Russia.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, the hooded crow breeds regularly in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, and the Scottish Islands; it also breeds widely in Ireland. In autumn, some <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> arrive on the east coast of Britain. In the past, this was a more common visitor.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The hooded crow is omnivorous, with a diet similar to that of the carrion crow, and is a constant scavenger. It drops <a href="/wiki/Mollusc" class="mw-redirect" title="Mollusc">molluscs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a> to break them after the manner of the carrion crow, and an old <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> name for empty <a href="/wiki/Sea_urchin" title="Sea urchin">sea urchin</a> shells was "crow's cups".<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> On coastal cliffs, the eggs of <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_cormorant" title="Great cormorant">cormorants</a>, and other birds are stolen when their owners are absent, and this crow will enter the burrow of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_puffin" title="Atlantic puffin">puffin</a> to steal eggs. It will also feed on small mammals, scraps, smaller birds, and carrion. The crow has the habit of hiding food, especially meat or nuts, in places such as rain gutters, flower pots, or in the earth under bushes, to feed on it later, sometimes on the insects that have meanwhile developed on it. Other crows often watch if another one hides food and then search this place later when the other crow has left.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nesting">Nesting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hooded_crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nesting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Nesting occurs later in colder regions: mid-May to mid-June in northwest <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shetland_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Shetland Islands">Shetland</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, and late February in the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> region.<sup id="cite_ref-CotW83_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CotW83-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In warmer parts of the European Archipelago, the clutch is laid in April.<sup id="cite_ref-Observerbirdseggs_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Observerbirdseggs-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The bulky stick nest is normally placed in a tall tree, but cliff ledges, old buildings, and pylons may be used. Nests are occasionally placed on or near the ground. The nest resembles that of the carrion crow, but on the coast, seaweed is often interwoven in the structure, and animal bones and wire are also frequently incorporated.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BWP_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The four to six brown-speckled blue eggs are 4.3&#160;cm Ã&#160;3.0&#160;cm (1.7&#160;in Ã&#160;1.2&#160;in) in size and weigh 19.8&#160;g (0.70&#160;oz), of which 6% is shell.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a> young are incubated for 17â19 days&#160;by the female alone, that is fed by the male. They fledge after 32 to 36&#160;days. Incubating females have been reported to obtain most of their own food and later that for their young.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The typical lifespan is unknown, but that of the carrion crow is four years.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO2_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO2-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The maximum recorded age for a hooded crow is 16 years, and 9 months.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species is a secondary host of the <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">parasitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Great_spotted_cuckoo" title="Great spotted cuckoo">great spotted cuckoo</a>, the <a href="/wiki/European_magpie" class="mw-redirect" title="European magpie">European magpie</a> being the preferred host. However, in areas where the latter species is absent, such as <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, the hooded crow becomes the normal corvid host.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP2_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP2-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species, like its relative, is seen regularly killed by farmers and on grouse estates. In <a href="/wiki/County_Cork" title="County Cork">County Cork</a>, Ireland, the county's gun clubs shot over 23,000 hooded crows in two years in the early 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hooded_crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> does not distinguish the hooded crow from the carrion crow, but the two species together have an extensive range, estimated at 10&#160;million&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (3.8&#160;million mi<sup>2</sup>), and a large population, including an estimated 14 to 34 million individuals in Europe alone. They are not believed to approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the IUCN Red List (i.e., declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations), so are evaluated as <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The carrion crow and hooded crow hybrid zone is slowly spreading northwest, but the hooded crow has on the order of three million territories in just Europe (excluding Russia).<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> This movement is also attested to by the fact that in April 2020 the hooded crow was redlisted in Sweden, where the Species Information Centre does distinguish between hooded and carrion crow.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_significance">Cultural significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hooded_crow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cultural significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CrowWastebag.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/CrowWastebag.jpg/220px-CrowWastebag.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/CrowWastebag.jpg/330px-CrowWastebag.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/CrowWastebag.jpg/440px-CrowWastebag.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3007" data-file-height="2004" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CrowWastebag.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Searching for food from punctured wastebag</div></div></div>
<p>In Celtic folklore, the bird appears on the shoulder of the dying <a href="/wiki/C%C3%BA_Chulainn" title="CÃº Chulainn">CÃº Chulainn</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-folkloreofbirds2_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-folkloreofbirds2-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> and could also be a manifestation of the <a href="/wiki/Morr%C3%ADgan" class="mw-redirect" title="MorrÃ­gan">MorrÃ­gan</a>, the wife of <a href="/wiki/Tethra" title="Tethra">Tethra</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Cailleach" title="Cailleach">Cailleach</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> This idea has persisted, and the hooded crow is associated with <a href="/wiki/Fairy" title="Fairy">fairies</a> in the Scottish highlands and Ireland; in the 18th century, Scottish shepherds would make offerings to them to keep them from attacking sheep.<sup id="cite_ref-ingersoll23_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ingersoll23-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroese</a> folklore, a maiden would go out on <a href="/wiki/Candlemas" title="Candlemas">Candlemas</a> morn and throw a stone, then a bone, then a clump of turf at a hooded crow â if it flew over the sea, her husband would be a foreigner; if it landed on a farm or house, she would marry a man from there, but if it stayed put, she would remain unmarried.<sup id="cite_ref-folkloreofbirds_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-folkloreofbirds-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The old name of <b>Royston crow</b> originates from the days when this bird was a common winter visitor to southern England, the sheep fields around <a href="/wiki/Royston,_Hertfordshire" title="Royston, Hertfordshire">Royston, Hertfordshire</a>, providing carcasses on which the birds could feed. The local newspaper, founded in 1855, is called <i><a href="/wiki/Royston_Crow_(newspaper)" title="Royston Crow (newspaper)">The Royston Crow</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and the hooded crow also features on the town's coat of arms.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The hooded crow is one of the 37 Norwegian birds depicted in the Bird Room of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Palace,_Oslo" title="Royal Palace, Oslo">Royal Palace</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jethro_Tull_(band)" title="Jethro Tull (band)">Jethro Tull</a> mentions the hooded crow on the song "Jack Frost and the hooded crow" as a bonus track on the digitally remastered version of <i><a href="/wiki/Broadsword_and_the_Beast" class="mw-redirect" title="Broadsword and the Beast">Broadsword and the Beast</a></i> and on their <i>The Christmas Album</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2014, a hooded crow along with <a href="/wiki/Yellow-legged_gull" title="Yellow-legged gull">yellow-legged gull</a> each attacked one of two peace doves which <a href="/wiki/Pope_Francis" title="Pope Francis">Pope Francis</a> allowed children to release in <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">Vatican City</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
