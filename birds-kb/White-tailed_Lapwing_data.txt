<p>The <b>white-tailed lapwing</b> or <b>white-tailed plover</b> (<i>Vanellus leucurus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Lapwing" title="Lapwing">lapwing</a> genus. The genus name <i>Vanellus</i> is <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Northern_lapwing" title="Northern lapwing">lapwing</a> and derives from <i>vannus</i> a <a href="/wiki/Winnowing#In_Greek_culture" title="Winnowing">winnowing fan</a>. The specific <i>leucurus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>leukouros</i>, "white-tailed".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This medium-sized lapwing is long-legged and fairly long-billed. It is the only lapwing likely to be seen in other than very shallow water, where it picks <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and other small prey mainly from the surface.
</p><p>Adults are slim erect birds with a brown back and foreneck, paler face and grey breast. Its long yellow legs, pure white tail and distinctive brown, white and black wings make this species unmistakable. Young birds have a scaly back, and may show some brown in the tail.
</p><p>The breeding season call is a <i>peewit</i>, similar to <a href="/wiki/Northern_lapwing" title="Northern lapwing">northern lapwing</a>.
</p><p>It breeds semi-colonially on inland marshes in <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. Four eggs are laid in a ground nest. The Iraqi and Iranian breeders are mainly residents, but Russian birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> south in winter to the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Subcontinent" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian Subcontinent">Indian Subcontinent</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and north east <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It is a very rare vagrant in western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the first example in Britain being found at <a href="/wiki/Packington_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Packington (disambiguation)">Packington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warwickshire" title="Warwickshire">Warwickshire</a> on 12 July 1975.
</p><p>In some parts of its distribution range the species faces threats related to <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a> and unintentional <a href="/wiki/Poaching" title="Poaching">poaching</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ABCC_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABCC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The white-tailed lapwing is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>
