<p>The <b>Lesser Sand Plover</b> (<i>Charadrius mongolus</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">plover</a> family of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>. The spelling is commonly given as Lesser Sandplover, but the official <a href="/wiki/British_Ornithologists%27_Union" title="British Ornithologists' Union">British Ornithologists' Union</a> spelling is Lesser Sand Plover.</p>
<p>There are five races, and the large east Asian forms, <i>C. m. mongolus</i> and <i>C. m. stegmanni</i>, are sometimes<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> given specific status as <b>Mongolian Plover</b>, <i>Charadrius mongolus</i>. If the taxonomic split is accepted, Lesser Sandplover as then defined becomes <i>Charadrius atrifrons</i>, including the three races <i>atrifrons, pamirensis</i> and <i>schaeferi</i>.</p>
<p>It breeds above the tree line in the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> and discontinuously across to bare coastal plains in north-eastern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, with the Mongolian Plover in the eastern part of the range; it has also bred in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. It nests in a <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest#Scrape" title="Bird nest">bare ground scrape</a>, laying three <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a>. This species is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering on sandy beaches in east <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, south Asia and <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a>. It is a very rare vagrant in western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, but, surprisingly, of the three individuals recorded in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> up to 2003, one was a Mongolian Plover.</p>

<p>This chunky plover is long-legged and long-billed. Breeding males have grey backs and white underparts. The breast, forehead and nape are chestnut, and there is a black eye mask. The <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">female is duller</a>, and winter and juvenile birds lack the chestnut, apart from a hint of <a href="/wiki/Rufous" title="Rufous">rufous</a> on the head. Legs are dark and the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> black.</p>
<p>In all <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumages</a>, this species is very similar to <a href="/wiki/Greater_Sand_Plover" title="Greater Sand Plover">Greater Sand Plover</a>, <i>Charadrius leschenaultii</i>. Separating the species may be straightforward in mixed wintering flocks on an <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> beach, where the difference in size and structure is obvious; it is another thing altogether to identify a lone vagrant to western Europe, where both species are very rare. The problem is compounded in that the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle Eastern</a> race of Greater Sandplover is the most similar to Lesser. Lesser usually has darker legs, a white forehead, and a more even white wing bar than greater.</p>
<p>The Lesser Sand Plover's food is <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Annelid" title="Annelid">annelid</a> worms, which are obtained by a run-and-pause technique, rather than the steady probing of some other wader groups. This species takes fewer steps and shorter pauses than the Greater Sand Plover when feeding.</p>
<p>The flight call is a hard trill.</p>
<p>The Lesser Sand Plover is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_Sand_Plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Identification">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Identification">Identification</span></h2>
<p><b>Differences from Greater Sand Plover</b></p>
<p>Size is one of the factors distinguishing a Lesser Sand Plover from a Greater of the species with the Lesser Sand Plover being slightly smaller. Having said that, it is not easy to rely on size alone especially when seen individually. The length of the bill is another distinguishing feature with the Lesser Sand Plover generally having a shorter bill compared to a Greater Sand Plover. The colour of the legs in a Lesser Sand Plover is generally darker ranging from black to grey while it is much paler ranging from grey to yellowish for Greater Sand Plovers.</p>
<p>Garner, Martin, Ian Lewington and Russell Slack (2003) Mongolian and Lesser Sand Plovers: an identification overview <i><a href="/wiki/Birding_World" title="Birding World">Birding World</a></i> 16(9): 377-85</p>
