<p>The <b>lesser sand plover</b> (<i>Charadrius mongolus</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">plover</a> family of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>. The spelling is commonly given as lesser sand-plover, but the official <a href="/wiki/British_Ornithologists%27_Union" title="British Ornithologists&#39; Union">British Ornithologists' Union</a> spelling is "lesser sand plover". The genus name <i>Charadrius</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">Late Latin</a> word for a yellowish bird mentioned in the fourth-century <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a>. It derives from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>kharadrios</i> a bird found in ravines and river valleys (<i>kharadra</i>, "ravine"). The specific <i>mongolus</i> is  <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and refers to <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> which at the time of naming referred to a larger area than the present country.<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_sand_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are five races, and the large east Asian forms, <i>C. m. mongolus</i> and <i>C. m. stegmanni</i>, are sometimes given specific status as <b>Mongolian plovers</b>, <i>Charadrius mongolus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> If the taxonomic split is accepted, lesser sand plover as then defined becomes <i>Charadrius atrifrons</i>, including the three races <i>atrifrons, pamirensis</i> and <i>schaeferi</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_sand_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It breeds above the tree line in the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> and discontinuously across to bare coastal plains in north-eastern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, with the Mongolian plover in the eastern part of the range; it has also bred in Alaska. It nests in a <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest#Scrape" title="Bird nest">bare ground scrape</a>, laying three <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a>. This species is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering on sandy beaches in east Africa, south Asia and Australasia. It is a very rare vagrant in western Europe, but of the three individuals recorded in Great Britain up to 2003, one was a Mongolian plover.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_sand_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This chunky plover is long-legged and long-billed. Breeding males have grey backs and white underparts. The breast, forehead and nape are chestnut, and there is a black eye mask. The <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">female is duller</a>, and winter and juvenile birds lack the chestnut, apart from a hint of <a href="/wiki/Rufous" title="Rufous">rufous</a> on the head. Legs are dark and the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> black.
</p><p>In all <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumages</a>, this species is very similar to the <a href="/wiki/Greater_sand_plover" title="Greater sand plover">greater sand plover</a>, <i>Charadrius leschenaultii</i>. Separating the species may be straightforward in mixed wintering flocks on an Indian beach, where the difference in size and structure is obvious; it is more difficult to identify a lone vagrant to western Europe, where both species are very rare. The problem is compounded in that the Middle Eastern race of greater sand plover is the most similar to the lesser. The lesser usually has darker legs, a white forehead, and a more even white wing bar than the greater.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_sand_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lesser sand plover's feeds on <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Annelid" title="Annelid">annelid</a> worms, which are obtained by a run-and-pause technique, rather than the steady probing of some other wader groups. This species takes fewer steps and shorter pauses than the greater sand plover when feeding.
</p><p>The flight call is a hard trill.
</p><p>The lesser sand plover is one of the species to which the <a href="/wiki/Agreement_on_the_Conservation_of_African-Eurasian_Migratory_Waterbirds" title="Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a> (AEWA) applies.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identification">Identification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_sand_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Identification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Size is one of the factors distinguishing a lesser sand plover from a <a href="/wiki/Greater_sand_plover" title="Greater sand plover">greater sand plover</a>, with the lesser being slightly smaller. However, it is not easy to rely on size alone especially when seen individually. The length of the bill is another distinguishing feature, with the lesser generally having a shorter bill compared to a greater. The colour of the legs in a lesser sand plover is generally darker, ranging from black to grey, while in a greater sand plover it is much paler, ranging from grey to yellowish.
</p>
