<p>The <b>red-necked stint</b> (<i>Calidris ruficollis</i>) is a small migratory <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a>. The genus name is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>kalidris</i> or <i>skalidris</i>, a term used by <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> for some grey-coloured waterside birds. The specific <i>ruficollis</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>rufus</i>, "red" and <i>collum</i>, "neck".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-necked_stint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>These birds are among the smallest of waders, very similar to the <a href="/wiki/Little_stint" title="Little stint">little stint</a>, <i>Calidris minuta</i>, with which they were once considered conspecific. The red-necked stint's small size, fine dark bill, dark legs and quicker movements distinguish this species from all waders except the other dark-legged stints. It measures 13–17&#160;cm (5.1–6.7&#160;in) in length, 28–37&#160;cm (11–15&#160;in) in wingspan and 21–51&#160;g (0.74–1.80&#160;oz) in body mass.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It can be distinguished from the <a href="/wiki/Western_sandpiper" title="Western sandpiper">western sandpiper</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Semipalmated_sandpiper" title="Semipalmated sandpiper">semipalmated sandpiper</a> in all plumages by its combination of a fine bill tip, unwebbed toes, and longer primary projection.
</p><p>The breeding adult has an unstreaked orange breast, bordered with dark markings below, and a white V on its back. In winter plumage identification is difficult, although it is shorter legged and longer winged than the <a href="/wiki/Little_stint" title="Little stint">little stint</a>. Juveniles have more contrasting mantle plumage and weaker white lines down the back than their relative. The call is a hoarse "stit".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-necked_stint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Red-necked stints are strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, breeding along the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Littoral" class="mw-redirect" title="Littoral">littoral</a> of eastern <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurasia</a> and spending the non-breeding season in <a href="/wiki/South_East_Asia" class="mw-redirect" title="South East Asia">South East Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a> as far south as <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. They are rare vagrants to western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, with most records from <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. They are often seen in western <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and occasionally elsewhere in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-necked_stint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Red-necked stints are highly gregarious and will form flocks with other small <i>Calidris</i> waders, such as <a href="/wiki/Sharp-tailed_sandpiper" title="Sharp-tailed sandpiper">sharp-tailed sandpipers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Curlew_sandpiper" title="Curlew sandpiper">curlew sandpipers</a> in their non-breeding areas.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-necked_stint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Their breeding habitat is <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>. They nest on the ground and breed from spring to summer.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-necked_stint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>They forage in wet grassland and soft mud, mainly picking up food by sight. In their non-breeding habitat they feed on intertidal mudflats and along the muddy margins of freshwater lakes. They mainly eat insects and other small invertebrates.
</p>
