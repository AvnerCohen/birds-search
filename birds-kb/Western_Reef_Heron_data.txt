<p>The <b>western reef heron</b> (<i>Egretta gularis</i>) also called the <b>western reef egret</b>, is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">heron</a> found in southern Europe, Africa and parts of Asia. It has a mainly coastal distribution and occurs in several plumage forms: a <a href="/wiki/Slaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Slaty">slaty</a>-grey plumage in which it can only be confused with the rather uncommon dark <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(biology)" title="Polymorphism (biology)">morph</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Little_Egret" class="mw-redirect" title="Little Egret">Little Egret</a> (<i>Egretta garzetta</i>); a white form which can look very similar to the little egret although the bill tends to be paler and larger and the black form with white throat <i>E. g. gularis</i> of West Africa. There are also differences in size, structure and foraging behaviour. There have been suggestions that the species hybridizes with the Little Egret, and based on this, some authors treat <i>schistacea</i> and <i>gularis</i> as subspecies of <i>Egretta garzetta</i>. Works that consider the Western Reef Heron as a valid species include the nominate <i>gularis</i> and <i>schistacea</i> as <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_reef_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> has two plumage colour forms. There is an all-white morph and a dark grey morph; morphs can also occur with intermediate shades of grey which may be related to age<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> or particoloured in grey and white. The white morph is similar in general appearance to the <a href="/wiki/Little_egret" title="Little egret">little egret</a>, but has a larger yellower bill, extended yellow on thicker legs, and when foraging tends to be very active, sometimes also moving its wing or using it to shade the water surface. The grey morph has a whitish throat and is unlikely to be confused with any other species within the range of this <a href="/wiki/Egret" title="Egret">egret</a> with beak and legs similar to that of the white morph. During the breeding season the legs and facial skin are reddish.<sup id="cite_ref-softparts_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-softparts-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Breeding birds have two long feathers on the sides of the nape. The nominate subspecies <i>gularis</i> has a range from West Africa to Gabon, with some birds breeding in southern Europe. Subspecies <i>schistacea</i> (Hemprich &amp; Ehrenberg, 1828) breeds from the Persian Gulf along the coast of India to the east of the India Peninsula. The bill of <i>gularis</i> is more pointed while <i>schistacea</i> has the larger bill especially towards the base.  The form on the eastern coast of South Africa is usually separated as the <a href="/wiki/Dimorphic_egret" title="Dimorphic egret">dimorphic egret</a> <i>Egretta dimorpha</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-britbirds_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britbirds-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pcr_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The dark and white morph is thought to be controlled by a single allele with the dark character being incompletely dominant over the gene for white.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_reef_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The species was first described as <i>Ardea gularis</i> from a specimen obtained in Senegal by the French naturalist <a href="/wiki/Louis_Augustin_Guillaume_Bosc" title="Louis Augustin Guillaume Bosc">Louis Augustin Guillaume Bosc</a> in 1792. The distinctive white throat (or gular region) in the dark morph gives it the species name. Later authors have treated it the genera <i>Demiegretta</i> (in which dimorphic egrets were once included<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>), <i>Herodias</i> and <i>Lepterodius</i> until its current stable position in the genus <i>Egretta</i>. The distinctness of this species from <i>Egretta garzetta</i> has however been highly debated with some authors treating <i>garzetta</i> as a polytypic <a href="/wiki/Superspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Superspecies">superspecies</a>. There has been confusion related to what were thought to be dark little egrets and the possibility of hybrids. Dark morphs in little egrets are extremely rare and nearly all specimens of what were thought to be dark morphs have turned out to be western reef herons.<sup id="cite_ref-aquila_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aquila-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In India some research claimed circumstantial evidence of interbreeding with little egrets, but the same researchers noted that the breeding seasons of the two species were different.<sup id="cite_ref-timing_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timing-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Hybridization has also been claimed to occur in Morocco<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and Kenya (but this might refer to <i>Egretta dimorpha</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Christidis and Boles quote a report by McCracken and Sheldon (2002) that the nucleotide sequences of the <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome b</a> genes from a sampled little egret and a western reef heron were identical and use this as evidence for demotion. The sequences and the origins of the samples are not publicly available or verifiable.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In the past the Indian form which was also described as <i>Ardea asha</i> by <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Sykes" title="William Henry Sykes">William Henry Sykes</a> has been treated as a subspecies of the little egret as <i>Egretta garzetta schistacea</i> on the basis of presumed hybridization with <i>Egretta gularis</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Lore_(anatomy)" title="Lore (anatomy)">lores</a> of breeding little egrets are blue while those of the reef heron are reddish although some individuals of <i>schistacea</i> show blue and this is thought to be due to hybridization.<sup id="cite_ref-softparts_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-softparts-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The dark coastal form of Madagascar, Aldabra, Comoro Islands, Seychelles and parts of East Africa (southern Kenya to Tanzania) was earlier treated as a subspecies (the mainland African form has sometimes been considered as <i>schistacea</i>) but is now raised to full species as the dimorphic egret (<i>Egretta dimorpha</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-britbirds_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britbirds-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_status">Distribution and status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_reef_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Western_Reef_Egret_(Egretta_gularis)_W_IMG_6913.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Western_Reef_Egret_%28Egretta_gularis%29_W_IMG_6913.jpg/170px-Western_Reef_Egret_%28Egretta_gularis%29_W_IMG_6913.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="93" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Western_Reef_Egret_%28Egretta_gularis%29_W_IMG_6913.jpg/255px-Western_Reef_Egret_%28Egretta_gularis%29_W_IMG_6913.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Western_Reef_Egret_%28Egretta_gularis%29_W_IMG_6913.jpg/340px-Western_Reef_Egret_%28Egretta_gularis%29_W_IMG_6913.jpg 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="493" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Western_Reef_Egret_(Egretta_gularis)_W_IMG_6913.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dark morph <i>schistacea</i> (<a href="/wiki/Krishna_Wildlife_Sanctuary" title="Krishna Wildlife Sanctuary">Krishna Wildlife Sanctuary</a>) showing extent of yellow on the feet</div></div></div>
<p>It occurs mainly on the coasts in tropical west <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> (Iran)<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> extending east to <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. It also occurs in the Lakshadweep Islands and <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> where breeding was once recorded at Chilaw.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The nominate subspecies breeds in west Africa from Mauritania to Gabon. Birds may also be found off the mainland such as in the Canary Islands. Small numbers breed in Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Subspecies <i>schistacea</i> is found from the Red Sea coast east round the Indian coast.<sup id="cite_ref-britbirds_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britbirds-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Breeding colonies are known from the east coast of India around <a href="/wiki/Pulicat_Lake" title="Pulicat Lake">Pulicat Lake</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> They occasionally occur further inland.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The western reef heron (nominate subspecies) occurs as a vagrant in North America, South America and the Caribbean islands.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Based on the growing number of records it is suspected that they may establish breeding colonies in Brazil.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Several records around 1980–90 in Germany, Austria and France have been attributed to birds that escaped from an animal dealer in Mittelfranken.<sup id="cite_ref-aquila_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aquila-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_reef_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>These birds stalk their prey in shallow water, often running or stirring the water with their feet or flicking their wings to disturb prey; they may also stand still and wait to ambush prey. They eat <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mollusc" class="mw-redirect" title="Mollusc">molluscs</a>. In coastal areas, they regularly feed on <a href="/wiki/Mudskipper" title="Mudskipper">mudskippers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Laboratory studies show them to be capable of making corrections for refractions but the probability of missing increases when they are forced to strike at prey at very acute angles to the water surface.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Like other herons and egrets they have few vocalizations, making a low <i>kwok</i> or grating sounds when disturbed or near the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The western reef heron's breeding habitat is coastal wetlands. In the Red Sea region the breeding season is from June to August. The nominate subspecies breeds in West Africa from late April to September. In India the breeding season is during the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> rains from April to August and end of May in the former breeding colony at Chilaw in Sri Lanka. Most of the breeding colonies in Gujarat in western India were on mangroves.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> They nest in colonies usually of their own species but sometimes with other egrets and smaller herons, usually on platforms of sticks placed in trees or shrubs. The male fetches sticks of the nest while the female places them to form the platform. The nest height varies from about 5 to 15 meters, but very low nesting (as low as 0.6 meters) has been observed in mangrove trees in salt pans. The usual clutch is three to four eggs which are light blue as in the little egret. Incubation begins when the first egg is laid resulting in chicks with size disparities. Both parents take turns to incubate and the eggs hatch after about 23 to 24 days. The chicks are white with grey dapples. Young birds sometimes die after falling off from the nest platform. Adults will shade the chicks during the hotter parts of the day. Predators of eggs include rats and young chicks may be taken by cats and crows. Adults usually guard the nest when eggs or young chicks are present. Adults feed the young by regurgitating semi-digested food at the nest. This diet includes mainly small fishes of the families <a href="/wiki/Clupeidae" title="Clupeidae">Clupeidae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gobiidae" title="Gobiidae">Gobiidae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Engraulidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Engraulidae">Engraulidae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The young birds leave the nest after about a month.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
