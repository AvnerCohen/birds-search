<p>The <b>Western Reef Heron</b>, <i>Egretta gularis</i>, also known as the <b>Western Reef Egret</b>, is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">heron</a>. It occurs mainly on the coasts in tropical west <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> and east to <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. It has been recorded as a vagrant in the <a href="/wiki/Cocos_(Keeling)_Islands" title="Cocos (Keeling) Islands">Cocos (Keeling) Islands</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> territory in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from January 2008">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Western Reef Heron has occurred as a vagrant twice in Canada and four times in the United States of America, first on <a href="/wiki/Nantucket" title="Nantucket">Nantucket</a> in April, 1983<sup id="cite_ref-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> and several times between 2005 and 2007.</p>
<p>The Western Reef Heron's breeding habitat is coastal wetlands. They nest in colonies, often with other wading birds, usually on platforms of sticks in trees or shrubs. The normal clutch is two or three eggs (although Ahmed Al Ali from UAE recorded a 4 eggs).</p>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> has two plumage colour forms. There is an all-white morph and a dark grey morph; intermediate morphs also occur. The white morph is similar in general appearance to the <a href="/wiki/Little_Egret" title="Little Egret">Little Egret</a>, but has a thicker bill, duller legs, and a less elegant appearance. The grey morph is unlikely to be confused with any other species within the range of this <a href="/wiki/Egret" title="Egret">egret</a>.</p>
<p>These birds stalk their prey in shallow water, often running or shuffling their feet; they may also stand still and wait to ambush prey. They eat <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc" class="mw-redirect">molluscs</a>.</p>
<p>The taxonomy of this species is being seriously questioned. There are three subspecies; <i>E. g. gularis</i>, <i>schistacea</i>, and <i>dimorpha</i>; all of which seem to be less related than formerly believed.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
