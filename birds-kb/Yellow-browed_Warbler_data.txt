<p>The <b>yellow-browed warbler</b> (<i>Phylloscopus inornatus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Leaf_warbler" title="Leaf warbler">leaf warbler</a> (<a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> Phylloscopidae) which breeds in the east <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Warbler" title="Warbler">warbler</a> is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and winters mainly in <a href="/wiki/Tropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical">tropical</a> <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/South-east_Asia" class="mw-redirect" title="South-east Asia">South-east Asia</a>, but also in small numbers in western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. Like the rest of Phylloscopidae, it was formerly included in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">Old World warbler</a> assemblage.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was formerly considered to comprise three <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>, but <i>P. i. humei</i> and <i>P. i. mandellii</i> are now split as a separate species, <a href="/wiki/Hume%27s_leaf_warbler" title="Hume&#39;s leaf warbler">Hume's leaf warbler</a> <i>P. humei</i>, leaving <i>P. inornatus</i> <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monotypic">monotypic</a>. The two <a href="/wiki/Sister_species" class="mw-redirect" title="Sister species">sister species</a> differ slightly but consistently in <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bioacoustics" title="Bioacoustics">bioacoustics</a>, and molecular characters.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Svensson_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Svensson-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Before the species was split, the names yellow-browed willow warbler<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and inornate warbler<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> were used by a few authors.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-browed_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The yellow-browed warbler was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">first described</a>  by the English zoologist <a href="/wiki/Edward_Blyth" title="Edward Blyth">Edward Blyth</a> in 1842 and given the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> <i>Regulus inornatus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The current genus name <i>Phylloscopus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>phullon</i>, "leaf", and <i>skopos</i>, "seeker" (from <i>skopeo</i>, "to watch"). The specific <i>inornatus</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "plain".<sup id="cite_ref-job_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-browed_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is one of the smaller Old World warblers, at 9.5–11&#160;cm long and weighing 4–9 g distinctly smaller than a <a href="/wiki/Common_chiffchaff" title="Common chiffchaff">chiffchaff</a> but slightly larger than <a href="/wiki/Pallas%27s_leaf_warbler" title="Pallas&#39;s leaf warbler">Pallas's leaf warbler</a>. Like many other <a href="/wiki/Leaf_warbler" title="Leaf warbler">leaf warblers</a>, it has overall greenish upperparts and white underparts. It also has prominent double wing bars formed by yellowish-white tips to the wing covert feathers (a long bar on the greater coverts and a short bar on the median coverts), yellow-margined tertial feathers, and long yellow <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a>. Some individuals also have a faint paler green central crown stripe though many do not show this.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Svensson_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Svensson-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Snow_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is not shy, but its arboreal life style makes it difficult to observe. It is almost constantly in motion. Its song is a high pitched medley of whistles; the call is piercing, often disyllabic "tseeweest", strikingly loud for the bird's small size. The only real possibility of confusion is with the similar-looking <a href="/wiki/Hume%27s_leaf_warbler" title="Hume&#39;s leaf warbler">Hume's leaf warbler</a> (<i>P. humei</i>), which in the limited area of overlap has duller colours, a faint second wing bar and dark legs and lower mandible. Their songs and calls are clearly distinct, with Hume's having a more chirping "chwee" call. It can easily be distinguished from Pallas's warbler as it does not have the a conspicuous yellow central crown stripe and rump patch shown by that species.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Svensson_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Svensson-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology_and_distribution">Ecology and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-browed_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology and distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like most warblers, it is <a href="/wiki/Insectivore" title="Insectivore">insectivorous</a>. The nest is built in dense vegetation often at the base of a tree or old stump; two to four (occasionally more) eggs are laid, hatching after 11–14 days, with the chicks fledging when 12–13 days old.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This is an abundant bird of lowland and <a href="/wiki/Montane_forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Montane forest">montane forests</a> and woodlands; particularly in winter it may also be found in more open woods. Its breeding range extends from just west of the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural Mountains</a> eastwards to eastern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northeast_China" title="Northeast China">Northeast China</a>. Its winter <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> is lowland <a href="/wiki/Broadleaf_forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Broadleaf forest">broadleaf</a> or <a href="/wiki/Conifer" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifer">coniferous</a> forest, from <a href="/wiki/West_Bengal" title="West Bengal">West Bengal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Assam" title="Assam">Assam</a> in northeastern India  east through southern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> to <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, and through <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> south to the <a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">Malay Peninsula</a>. In summer, it occurs at altitudes of up to 2,440 m, and in winter, up to 1,525 m.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The European breeding population west of the Urals has increased westwards in recent decades; in 1950 it was described as 'fairly scarce', but 'locally abundant' with 45,000–46,000 pairs in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Small numbers, most likely from the western end of the breeding range, regularly winter in western Europe. These arrive in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> in late September and October after a 3,000–3,500&#160;km migration from the Urals, a markedly shorter distance than the 5,500–6,000&#160;km they would need to fly to reach the normal wintering areas in southeastern Asia. Exact numbers in this population are unknown, but typically several hundred are found arriving in Great Britain each autumn; given their unobtrusive behaviour, this is probably only a fraction of the total. In the past widely considered to be <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrants</a>, these birds are now thought to be undertaking a normal regular migration, able to take advantage of the mild <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a> winters on the western fringes of Europe for wintering.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A common species in most of its wide range, the yellow-browed warbler is not considered <a href="/wiki/Threatened" class="mw-redirect" title="Threatened">threatened</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hume's leaf warbler overlaps its breeding range with yellow-browed warbler in the western <a href="/wiki/Sayan_Mountains" title="Sayan Mountains">Sayan Mountains</a>, but the species apparently do not <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridise</a>. Their lineages diverged roughly 2.5 <a href="/wiki/Million_years_ago" class="mw-redirect" title="Million years ago">million years ago</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
