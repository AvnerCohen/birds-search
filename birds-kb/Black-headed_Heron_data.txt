<p>The <b>Black-headed Heron</b> (<i>Ardea melanocephala</i>) is a wading bird of the <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">heron</a> family <a href="/wiki/Ardeidae" title="Ardeidae" class="mw-redirect">Ardeidae</a>, common throughout much of <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>. It is mainly resident but some west African birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">move</a> further north in the rainy season.</p>
<p>This species usually breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">wet season</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonies</a> in trees, reedbeds or cliffs. It builds a bulky stick nest and lays 2–4 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>.</p>
<p>It often feeds in shallow water, spearing <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a> with its long, sharp bill. It will also hunt well away from water, taking large insects, small <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, and birds. It will wait motionless for prey, or slowly stalk its victim.</p>
<p>The Black-headed Heron is a large bird, standing 85&#160;cm tall, and it has a 150&#160;cm wingspan. It is nearly as large as the <a href="/wiki/Grey_Heron" title="Grey Heron">Grey Heron</a>, which it resembles in appearance, although it is generally darker. Its <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">plumage</a> is largely grey above, and paler grey below. It has a powerful dusky bill.</p>
<p>The flight is slow, with its neck retracted. This is characteristic of herons and <a href="/wiki/Bittern" title="Bittern">bitterns</a>, and distinguishes them from <a href="/wiki/Stork" title="Stork">storks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crane_(bird)" title="Crane (bird)">cranes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Spoonbill" title="Spoonbill">spoonbills</a>, which extend their necks. The white underwing coverts are striking in flight.</p>
<p>The call is a loud croaking.</p>
