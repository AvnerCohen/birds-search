<p>The <b>Long-tailed Shrike</b> or <b>Rufous-backed Shrike</b> (<i>Lanius schach</i>) is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> family Laniidae, the <a href="/wiki/Shrike" title="Shrike">shrikes</a>. They are found widely distributed across Asia and there are variations in plumage across the range. The species ranges across much of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, both on the mainland and the eastern archipelagos. The eastern or <a href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya" class="mw-redirect">Himalayan</a> subspecies, <i>L. s. tricolor</i>, is sometimes called the <b>Black-headed Shrike</b>. Although there are considerable differences in plumage among the subspecies, they all have a long and narrow black tail, have a black mask and forehead, rufous rump and flanks and a small white patch on the shoulder. It is considered to form a <a href="/wiki/Superspecies" title="Superspecies">superspecies</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Grey-backed_Shrike" title="Grey-backed Shrike">Grey-backed Shrike</a> (<i>Lanius tephronotus</i>) which breeds on the Tibetan Plateau.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-tailed_Shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Long-tailed_Shrike_(Lanius_schach-_race_tricolor)_in_Kolkata_W_IMG_3434.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Long-tailed_Shrike_%28Lanius_schach-_race_tricolor%29_in_Kolkata_W_IMG_3434.jpg/220px-Long-tailed_Shrike_%28Lanius_schach-_race_tricolor%29_in_Kolkata_W_IMG_3434.jpg" width="220" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Long-tailed_Shrike_%28Lanius_schach-_race_tricolor%29_in_Kolkata_W_IMG_3434.jpg/330px-Long-tailed_Shrike_%28Lanius_schach-_race_tricolor%29_in_Kolkata_W_IMG_3434.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Long-tailed_Shrike_%28Lanius_schach-_race_tricolor%29_in_Kolkata_W_IMG_3434.jpg/440px-Long-tailed_Shrike_%28Lanius_schach-_race_tricolor%29_in_Kolkata_W_IMG_3434.jpg 2x" /></a>

<p>The Long-tailed Shrike is a typical shrike, favouring dry open habitats and found perched prominently atop a bush or on a wire. The dark mask through the eye is broad and covers the forehead in most subspecies and the whole head is black in subspecies <i>tricolor</i> and <i>nasutus</i>. The tail is narrow and graduated with pale rufous on the outer feathers. Subspecies <i>erythronotus</i> has the grey of the mantle and upper back suffused with rufous while the southern Indian <i>caniceps</i> has pure grey.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> A small amount of white is present at the base of the primaries. The <a href="/wiki/Bay-backed_Shrike" title="Bay-backed Shrike">Bay-backed Shrike</a> is smaller and more contrastingly patterned and has a more prominent white patch on the wing. The sexes are alike in plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-tailed_Shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>A number of subspecies are noted within the widely distributed range of this species. <a href="/wiki/Edward_Charles_Stuart_Baker" title="Edward Charles Stuart Baker" class="mw-redirect">Stuart Baker</a> in the second edition of <a href="/wiki/The_Fauna_of_British_India" title="The Fauna of British India" class="mw-redirect">The Fauna of British India</a> considered <i>Lanius schach</i>, <i>Lanius tephronotus</i> and <i>Lanius tricolor</i> as three species. He considered <i>nigriceps</i> as synonymous with <i>tricolor</i> and included <i>erythronotus</i> as a race of <i>schach</i>. Other treatments were proposed by <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Whistler" title="Hugh Whistler">Hugh Whistler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norman_Boyd_Kinnear" title="Norman Boyd Kinnear">N B Kinnear</a> where <i>tephronotus</i> was considered a subspecies of <i>schach</i> and <i>nigriceps</i> and <i>nasutus</i> grouped together. Another treatment considered <i>tricolor</i> as a subspecies of <i>L. tephronotus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> It was subsequently however noted that <i>tephronotus</i> and <i>schach</i> co-occurred in the Kumaon region and so the two were confirmed as distinct species. Molecular distances also indicate that they are distant enough.<sup id="cite_ref-acta2007_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acta2007-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The <i>erythronotus</i> group have a grey head which continues into the back with a gradual suffusion of rufous. The westernmost population from Transcaspia named by <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Aleksandrovich_Buturlin" title="Sergei Aleksandrovich Buturlin">Sergei Buturlin</a> as <i>jaxartensis</i> and said to be larger, is not considered valid.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> A very light grey form from western dry region of India named by Walter Koelz as <i>kathiawarensis</i> is also considered merely as a variant.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> In southern India and Sri Lanka, subspecies <i>caniceps</i>, is marked by the rufous restricted to the rump, light crown and the pure grey on the back. <a href="/wiki/Biswamoy_Biswas" title="Biswamoy Biswas">Biswamoy Biswas</a> supported the view that <i>nigriceps</i> (having upper mantle grey and lower mantle rufous) was a hybrid of <i>tricolor</i> and <i>erythronotus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Subspecies <i>longicaudatus</i> has a greyer crown and is found in Thailand and Burma. The nominate subspecies is found in China from the Yangtze valley south to Hainan and Taiwan. Some individuals of the nominate form show melanism and were once described as a species <i>fuscatus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-acta2007_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acta2007-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Island forms include <i>nasutus</i> (Philippine lslands from Mindanao to Luzon and north Borneo), <i>suluensis</i> (Sulu Island), <i>bentet</i> (Sunda Islands and Sumatra other than Borneo) and <i>stresemanni</i> of New Guinea.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-tailed_Shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>The species is found across Asia from Kazhakstan to New Guinea. It is found mainly in scrub and open habitats. Many of the temperate zone populations are migratory, moving south in winter while those in the tropics tend to be sedentary although they may make short distance movements. Subspecies <i>caniceps</i> of southern India is found in winter in the dry coastal zone of southern India.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Subspecies <i>tricolor</i> migrates south to Bengal in India.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> They are found in scrub, grassland and open land under cultivation. A survey in southern India found them to be among the commonest wintering shrikes and found at a linear density along roadsides at about 0.58 per kilometer, often choosing wires to perch.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species is a rare <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrant</a> to western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> on the strength of two accepted records in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> on <a href="/wiki/South_Uist" title="South Uist">South Uist</a> in November 2000<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> near <a href="/wiki/Den_Helder" title="Den Helder">Den Helder</a> in October 2011. A bird matching the features of <i>caniceps</i> was seen on the island of Maldives.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> It has also occurred as a vagrant to <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from May 2012">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-tailed_Shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Long-tailed_Shrike_(Lanius_schach)-_erythronotus_race_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5401.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Long-tailed_Shrike_%28Lanius_schach%29-_erythronotus_race_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5401.jpg/170px-Long-tailed_Shrike_%28Lanius_schach%29-_erythronotus_race_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5401.jpg" width="170" height="184" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Long-tailed_Shrike_%28Lanius_schach%29-_erythronotus_race_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5401.jpg/255px-Long-tailed_Shrike_%28Lanius_schach%29-_erythronotus_race_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5401.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Long-tailed_Shrike_%28Lanius_schach%29-_erythronotus_race_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5401.jpg/340px-Long-tailed_Shrike_%28Lanius_schach%29-_erythronotus_race_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5401.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Long-tailed_Shrike_(Lanius_schach)-_erythronotus_race_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5401.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Upright posture (ssp. <i>erythronotus</i> (<a href="/wiki/Keoladeo_National_Park" title="Keoladeo National Park">Keoladeo National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>)</div>

</div>
<p>This bird has a characteristic upright "shrike" attitude when perched on a bush, from which it glides down at an angle to take <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a>, large <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, small <a href="/wiki/List_of_birds" title="List of birds">birds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>. They maintain feeding territories and are usually found single or in pairs that are well spaced out. Several members have been observed indulging in play behaviour fighting over perches.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The usual calls are harsh grating and scolding calls, likened to the squealing of a frog caught by a snake. They are capable of vocal mimicry and include the calls of many species including lapwings, cuckoos, puppies and squirrels in their song.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> This singing ability makes it a popular pet in parts of southeast Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Long-tailed Shrikes take a wide variety of animal prey. On occasion, they have been noted capturing fish from a stream.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> They also take small snakes.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> It sometimes indulges in kleptoparasitism and takes prey from other birds. It also captures flying insects in the air. They sometimes impale prey on a thorny bush after feeding just on the head or brain. They have been reported to feed on the fruits of the <a href="/wiki/Neem" title="Neem" class="mw-redirect">neem</a> in Kerala, even attempting to impale them on a twig.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The breeding season is in summer in the temperate ranges. The nest is a deep and loose cup made up of thorny twigs, rags and hair. This is placed in a thorny bush, trees such as <i><a href="/wiki/Flacourtia" title="Flacourtia">Flacourtia</a></i> and wild date palms (<i><a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(plant)" title="Phoenix (plant)">Phoenix</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> The usual clutch is about 3 to 6 eggs which are incubated by both sexes. The eggs hatch after about 13 to 16 days. Young chicks are often fed with pieces of small birds captured by the parents.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> A second brood may be raised in the same nest. They are sometimes parasitized by cuckoos such as the <a href="/wiki/Common_Cuckoo" title="Common Cuckoo">Common Cuckoo</a> (Dehra Dun), <a href="/wiki/Common_Hawk-Cuckoo" title="Common Hawk-Cuckoo">Common Hawk-Cuckoo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacobin_Cuckoo" title="Jacobin Cuckoo">Jacobin Cuckoo</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Asian_Koel" title="Asian Koel">Asian Koel</a> in Bangladesh.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
