<p>The <b>Eurasian blackcap</b> (<i>Sylvia atricapilla</i>) usually known simply as the <b>blackcap</b>, is a common and widespread <a href="/wiki/Typical_warbler" title="Typical warbler">typical warbler</a>. It has mainly olive-grey upperparts and pale grey underparts, and differences between the five <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are small. Both sexes have a neat coloured cap to the head, black in the male and reddish-brown in the female. The male's typical song is a rich musical warbling, often ending in a loud high-pitched crescendo, but a simpler song is given in some isolated areas, such as valleys in the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>. The blackcap's closest relative is the <a href="/wiki/Garden_warbler" title="Garden warbler">garden warbler</a>, which looks quite different but has a similar song.
</p><p>The blackcap breeds in much of Europe, western Asia and northwestern Africa, and its preferred habitat is mature <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> woodland. The male holds a territory when breeding, which is defended against garden warblers as well as other blackcaps. The nest is a neat cup, built low in <a href="/wiki/Bramble" title="Bramble">brambles</a> or scrub, and the <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is typically 4–6 mainly buff eggs, which hatch in about 11&#160;days. The chicks <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> in 11–12&#160;days, but are cared for by both adults for some time after leaving the nest. The blackcap is a partial <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>; birds from the colder areas of its range winter in scrub or trees in northwestern Europe, around the Mediterranean and in tropical Africa. Some German birds have adapted to spending the winter in gardens in Great Britain and Ireland. Insects are the main food in the breeding season, but, for the rest of the year, blackcaps survive primarily on small fruit. Garden birds also eat bread, fat and <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanuts</a> in winter.
</p><p>Despite extensive hunting in Mediterranean countries and the natural hazards of predation and disease, the blackcap has been extending its range for several decades, and is classified by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> as <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>. Its rich and varied song has led to it being described as the "mock nightingale" and it has featured in literature, films and music. In <a href="/wiki/Olivier_Messiaen" title="Olivier Messiaen">Messiaen's</a> opera <i><a href="/wiki/Saint_Fran%C3%A7ois_d%27Assise" title="Saint François d&#39;Assise">Saint François d'Assise</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Francis_of_Assisi" title="Francis of Assisi">saint</a> is represented by themes based on the blackcap's song.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_blackcap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The genus <i>Sylvia</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Typical_warbler" title="Typical warbler">typical warblers</a>, forms part of a large family of Old World warblers, the <a href="/wiki/Sylviidae" title="Sylviidae">Sylviidae</a>. The blackcap and its nearest relative, the <a href="/wiki/Garden_warbler" title="Garden warbler">garden warbler</a>, are an ancient <a href="/wiki/Superspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Superspecies">species pair</a> which diverged very early from the rest of the genus at between 12 and 16&#160;million years ago. In the course of time, these two species have become sufficiently distinctive that they have been placed in separate subgenera, with the blackcap in subgenus <i>Sylvia</i> and the garden warbler in <i>Epilais</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-shir_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> These <a href="/wiki/Sister_group" title="Sister group">sister species</a> have a breeding range which extends farther northeast than all other <i>Sylvia</i> species except the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_whitethroat" title="Lesser whitethroat">lesser whitethroat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_whitethroat" title="Common whitethroat">common whitethroat</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-zoolscripta_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zoolscripta-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The nearest relatives of the garden warbler outside the sister group are believed to be the <a href="/wiki/African_hill_babbler" title="African hill babbler">African hill babbler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dohrn%27s_thrush-babbler" class="mw-redirect" title="Dohrn&#39;s thrush-babbler">Dohrn's thrush-babbler</a>, both of which should probably be placed in <i>Sylvia</i> rather than their current genera, <i>Pseudoalcippe</i> and <i>Horizorhinus</i> respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-malaria_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-malaria-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BMC_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMC-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The blackcap was one of the many <a href="/wiki/Aves_in_the_10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="Aves in the 10th edition of Systema Naturae">bird species originally described</a> by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his landmark 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>, as <i>Motacilla atricapilla</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-linn_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linn-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The current genus name is from <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">Modern Latin</a> <i>silvia</i>, a woodland sprite, related to <i>silva</i>, a wood.<sup id="cite_ref-job376_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job376-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The species name, like the English name, refers to the male's black cap. <i>Atricapilla</i> is from the Latin <i>ater</i>, "black", and <i>capillus</i>, "hair (of the head)".<sup id="cite_ref-job59_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job59-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">Fossils</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subfossil" title="Subfossil">subfossils</a> of the blackcap have been found in a number of European countries; the oldest, dated to 1.2–1.0&#160;million years ago, are from the <a href="/wiki/Early_Pleistocene" title="Early Pleistocene">Early Pleistocene</a> of Bulgaria.<sup id="cite_ref-boev1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boev1-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Boev2_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boev2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Fossils from France show that the genus <i>Sylvia</i> dates back at least 20&#160;million years.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason11_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason11-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The differences between subspecies are small, making subspecific boundaries hard to define, and the exact distribution of <i>S. a. heineken</i> is unclear, since birds from northwest Africa may be of this form.<sup id="cite_ref-mason15_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mason15-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> About 2% of male blackcaps on <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> are <a href="/wiki/Melanism" title="Melanism">melanistic</a>, with black plumage on the whole head and upper breast.<sup id="cite_ref-veil_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-veil-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Melanistic females are rarer, and are characterised by grey-brown underparts. This dark <a href="/wiki/Morph_(zoology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Morph (zoology)">morph</a> has also been recorded from the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, but not from <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>. The melanistic birds were formerly sometimes considered to be a distinct subspecies, <i>S. a. obscura</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-shir54_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir54-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_blackcap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Curruca_Capirotada.(_Sylvia_atricapilla_heineken)(%E2%99%82)_(5215354550).jpg" class="image"><img alt="A male blackcap perched in a tree" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Curruca_Capirotada.%28_Sylvia_atricapilla_heineken%29%28%E2%99%82%29_%285215354550%29.jpg/220px-Curruca_Capirotada.%28_Sylvia_atricapilla_heineken%29%28%E2%99%82%29_%285215354550%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Curruca_Capirotada.%28_Sylvia_atricapilla_heineken%29%28%E2%99%82%29_%285215354550%29.jpg/330px-Curruca_Capirotada.%28_Sylvia_atricapilla_heineken%29%28%E2%99%82%29_%285215354550%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Curruca_Capirotada.%28_Sylvia_atricapilla_heineken%29%28%E2%99%82%29_%285215354550%29.jpg/440px-Curruca_Capirotada.%28_Sylvia_atricapilla_heineken%29%28%E2%99%82%29_%285215354550%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2143" data-file-height="1646" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Curruca_Capirotada.(_Sylvia_atricapilla_heineken)(%E2%99%82)_(5215354550).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Male <i>S. a. heineken</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, Spain</div></div></div>
<p>The blackcap is a mainly grey warbler with distinct male and female <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumages</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Subspecies#Nominotypical_subspecies_and_subspecies_autonyms" title="Subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> is about 13&#160;cm (5.1&#160;in) long with a 7–8&#160;cm (2.8–3.1&#160;in) wing length.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The weight is typically 16–25&#160;g (0.56–0.88&#160;oz), but can be up to 31&#160;g (1.1&#160;oz) for birds preparing to migrate.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The adult male has olive-grey upperparts, other than a paler grey nape and a neat black cap on the head. The underparts are light grey, becoming silvery white on the chin, throat and upper breast. The tail is dark grey, with an olive tint to the outer edge of each feather. The bill and long legs are grey, and the <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">iris</a> is reddish brown. The female resembles the male, but has a reddish-brown cap and a slightly browner tone to the grey of the upperparts. Juveniles are similar to the female, but their upperparts have a slight rufous tinge, and the breast and flanks have a more olive tone; young males have a darker brown cap than their female counterparts.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> This species is unmistakable; other dark-headed <i>Sylvia</i> species, such <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_warbler" title="Sardinian warbler">Sardinian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orphean_warbler" title="Orphean warbler">Orphean warblers</a> have extensive black on the head instead of a small cap. They are also larger and have white edges on the tail.<sup id="cite_ref-Simms_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simms-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Blackcaps have a complete <a href="/wiki/Moulting" title="Moulting">moult</a> in their breeding areas in August and September prior to migration. Some birds, typically those migrating the greatest distances, have a further partial moult between December and March. Juveniles replace their loosely structured body feathers with adult plumage, starting earlier, but taking longer to complete, than the adults. Blackcaps breeding in the north of the range have an earlier and shorter post-juvenile moult than those further south, and <a href="/wiki/Crossbreed" title="Crossbreed">cross-breeding</a> of captive birds shows that the timing is <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genetically</a> controlled.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason92_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason92-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The male's song is a rich musical warbling, often ending in a loud high-pitched crescendo, which is given in bursts of up to 30&#160;seconds. The song is repeated for about two-and-a-half minutes, with a short pause before each repetition. In some geographically isolated areas, such as islands, <a href="/wiki/Peninsula" title="Peninsula">peninsulas</a> and valleys in the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>, a simplified fluting song occurs, named the <i>Leiern</i> (drawling) song by the German <a href="/wiki/Ornithology" title="Ornithology">ornithologists</a> who first described it. The song's introduction is like that of other blackcaps, but the final warbling part is a simple alternation between two notes, as in a <a href="/wiki/Great_tit" title="Great tit">great tit</a>'s call but more fluting.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mason85_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mason85-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The main song is confusable with that of the garden warbler, but it is slightly higher pitched than in that species, more broken into discrete song segments, and less mellow. Both species have a quiet subsong, a muted version of the full song, which is even harder to separate. The blackcap occasionally mimics the song of other birds,<sup id="cite_ref-simms56_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-simms56-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> the most frequently copied including the garden warbler and the <a href="/wiki/Common_nightingale" title="Common nightingale">common nightingale</a>. The main call is a hard <i>tac-tac</i>, like stones knocking together,<sup id="cite_ref-Simms_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simms-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and other vocalisations include a squeaking <i>sweet</i> alarm, and a low-pitched trill similar to that of a garden warbler.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Male blackcaps will sometimes sing even when incubating, especially with the second brood. This appears to be intended to maintain the bond with the female.<sup id="cite_ref-bb106_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb106-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Wintering birds in Africa are quiet initially, but start singing in January or February prior to their return north.<sup id="cite_ref-Barlow_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barlow-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ash_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ash-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_blackcap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_blackcap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sylvia_atricapilla_-Slottsskogen,_Gothenburg,_Vastra_Gotaland,_Sweden_-female-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A grey bird with a brown cap" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Sylvia_atricapilla_-Slottsskogen%2C_Gothenburg%2C_Vastra_Gotaland%2C_Sweden_-female-8.jpg/220px-Sylvia_atricapilla_-Slottsskogen%2C_Gothenburg%2C_Vastra_Gotaland%2C_Sweden_-female-8.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Sylvia_atricapilla_-Slottsskogen%2C_Gothenburg%2C_Vastra_Gotaland%2C_Sweden_-female-8.jpg/330px-Sylvia_atricapilla_-Slottsskogen%2C_Gothenburg%2C_Vastra_Gotaland%2C_Sweden_-female-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Sylvia_atricapilla_-Slottsskogen%2C_Gothenburg%2C_Vastra_Gotaland%2C_Sweden_-female-8.jpg/440px-Sylvia_atricapilla_-Slottsskogen%2C_Gothenburg%2C_Vastra_Gotaland%2C_Sweden_-female-8.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="932" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sylvia_atricapilla_-Slottsskogen,_Gothenburg,_Vastra_Gotaland,_Sweden_-female-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult female in Sweden showing reddish brown cap</div></div></div>
<p>The continental breeding range of the blackcap lies between the 14–30° July <a href="/wiki/Isotherm_(contour_line)" class="mw-redirect" title="Isotherm (contour line)">isotherms</a>, and is occupied by the nominate subspecies, the other forms being restricted to islands or fringe areas in the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> and eastern Iberia.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Birds on the Mediterranean and Atlantic islands and in the milder west and south of the main Eurasian distribution often winter within the nesting range, but populations elsewhere are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>. The blackcap is a leap-frog migrant; birds from the north of the breeding range travel furthest south, whereas Mediterranean breeders move much shorter distances. The wintering areas overlap with the breeding range, but also include extensive areas in <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a> south to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Malawi" title="Lake Malawi">Lake Malawi</a>, and further north in Ethiopia, South Sudan and Eritrea.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The large majority of birds wintering in eastern Africa are of the southwest Asian race, <i>S. a. dammholzi</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ash_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ash-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ZTP0_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZTP0-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is a migratory divide in Europe at <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> 10–11°E. Birds to the west of this line head southwest towards Iberia or West Africa, whereas populations to the east migrate to the eastern Mediterranean and on to East Africa. Cross-breeding in captivity of birds from the resident population on the Canary Islands with migratory blackcaps from Germany showed the urge to migrate is genetically controlled, the offspring showing intermediate behaviour in terms of restlessness at migration time.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason107_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason107-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Similar experiments using birds from southern Germany and eastern Austria, on opposite sides of the migratory divide, demonstrated that the direction of migration is also genetically determined.<sup id="cite_ref-Newton320_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newton320-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Climate change appears to be affecting the migration pattern of the garden warbler and blackcap. Both are arriving in Europe earlier than previously, and blackcaps and juvenile (but not adult) garden warblers are departing nearly two weeks later than in the 1980s. Birds of both species are longer-winged and lighter than in the past, suggesting a longer migration as the breeding range expands northwards.<sup id="cite_ref-kovacs_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kovacs-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In recent decades, substantial numbers of central European birds have taken to wintering in gardens in Great Britain, and, to a lesser extent, Ireland,<sup id="cite_ref-bto2_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto2-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mokwa_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mokwa-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> where the blackcap was formerly just a summer visitor. Although the British climate is sub-optimal, compensatory factors include the ready availability of food, (particularly from <a href="/wiki/Bird_feeder" title="Bird feeder">bird tables</a>), a shorter migration distance, and the avoidance of the Alps and the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara Desert</a>. These wintering birds come from Germany, and <a href="/wiki/Isotope_analysis" title="Isotope analysis">isotope analysis</a> (which enables the wintering location to be determined) showed that the German birds wintering in Britain tend to mate only among themselves, and do not usually interbreed with those wintering in the Mediterranean or western Africa. This is because the British migrants arrive back on the breeding grounds earlier than blackcaps wintering around the Mediterranean, and form pairs before the southern birds arrive. Mixed pairings are also selected against because the hybrid young would migrate in an intermediate direction, which would take them into the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">Bay of Biscay</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bearhop_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bearhop-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the Iberian Peninsula, migratory blackcaps (and <a href="/wiki/European_robin" title="European robin">European robins</a>) track similar climatic conditions over the season, which sedentary individuals must cope with great variation in climate over the year. This suggests a trade-off between the cost of travelling long distances of migrants, and the flexibility required by sedentary individuals to tolerate a wide variety of environmental conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_blackcap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Holies_-_geograph.org.uk_-_915202.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A woodland glade" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/The_Holies_-_geograph.org.uk_-_915202.jpg/220px-The_Holies_-_geograph.org.uk_-_915202.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/The_Holies_-_geograph.org.uk_-_915202.jpg/330px-The_Holies_-_geograph.org.uk_-_915202.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/The_Holies_-_geograph.org.uk_-_915202.jpg/440px-The_Holies_-_geograph.org.uk_-_915202.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Holies_-_geograph.org.uk_-_915202.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Deciduous woodland is the preferred breeding habitat.</div></div></div>
<p>The blackcap's main breeding habitat is mature deciduous woodland, with good scrub cover below the trees. Other habitats, such as parks, large gardens and overgrown hedges, are used as long as they meet the essential requirements of tall trees for songposts and an established <a href="/wiki/Understory" title="Understory">understory</a>. Where other <i>Sylvia</i> warblers also breed, blackcaps tend to use taller trees than their relatives, preferably those with a good canopy, such as <a href="/wiki/Quercus_robur" title="Quercus robur">pedunculate oak</a>. In prime habitat, breeding densities reach 100–200&#160;pairs per square kilometre (250–500&#160;pairs per square mile) in northern Europe, and 500–900&#160;pairs per square kilometre (1,250–2,250&#160;pairs per square mile) in Italy. Densities are much lower in poorer habitats such as conifer forests.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason20_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason20-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Breeding occurs in Europe at altitudes up to 2,200&#160;m (7,200&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The preferred winter habitat around the Mediterranean is scrub and olive orchards, where densities approach the levels found in the best breeding areas. The British wintering population is atypical, with 95% found in gardens, mostly in towns at altitudes below 100&#160;m (330&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In Africa, habitats include cultivated land, <a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">acacia</a> scrub, <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangroves</a> and forest, and these warblers are found at altitudes up to 3,600&#160;m (11,800&#160;ft) in the east of the continent.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mason36_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason36-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Wintering birds wander in search of good fruit supplies, but often stay in good feeding areas, and return in subsequent winters. Migrants may occur in a wide variety of habitats, such as <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed bed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fen" title="Fen">fen</a>, but show a preference for shrubland.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason36_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason36-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_blackcap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Territory">Territory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_blackcap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Territory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sylvia_atricapilla_MWNH_1719.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Sylvia_atricapilla_MWNH_1719.JPG/220px-Sylvia_atricapilla_MWNH_1719.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="336" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Sylvia_atricapilla_MWNH_1719.JPG/330px-Sylvia_atricapilla_MWNH_1719.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Sylvia_atricapilla_MWNH_1719.JPG/440px-Sylvia_atricapilla_MWNH_1719.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2277" data-file-height="3481" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sylvia_atricapilla_MWNH_1719.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden,_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Museum Wiesbaden, Germany">Museum Wiesbaden, Germany</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wwalas_Sylvia_atricapilla_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Two eggs in a cup-shaped nest" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Wwalas_Sylvia_atricapilla_1.jpg/220px-Wwalas_Sylvia_atricapilla_1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Wwalas_Sylvia_atricapilla_1.jpg/330px-Wwalas_Sylvia_atricapilla_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Wwalas_Sylvia_atricapilla_1.jpg/440px-Wwalas_Sylvia_atricapilla_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="683" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wwalas_Sylvia_atricapilla_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The eggs normally take about 11 days to hatch</div></div></div>
<p>When male blackcaps return to their breeding areas, they establish a <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territory</a>. Adults that have previously bred return to the site they have used in previous summers, whereas inexperienced birds either wander until they find a suitable area, or establish a very large initial territory which contracts under pressure from neighbours. Territorial boundaries are established initially by loud singing, performed while the male displays with his crown raised, tail fanned and slow wingbeats. This display is followed, if necessary, by a chase, often leading to a fight. The typical territory size in a French study was 1.12 hectares (2.8 acres), but in insect-rich tall <a href="/wiki/Maquis_shrubland" title="Maquis shrubland">maquis</a> in Gibraltar, the average was only 0.16 hectares (0.40 acres). Females feed within a home range which may overlap other blackcap territories, and covers up to six times the area of the defended zone.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason38_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason38-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Sylvia</i> warblers are unusual in that they vigorously defend their territories against other members of their genus as well as <a href="/wiki/Conspecificity" class="mw-redirect" title="Conspecificity">conspecifics</a>. Blackcaps and garden warblers use identical habits in the same wood, yet aggressive interactions mean that their territories never overlap.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason42_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason42-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-garcia_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-garcia-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Similar songs are a feature of the <i>Sylvia</i> warblers as a group, and it has been suggested that this promotes <a href="/wiki/Interspecific_competition" title="Interspecific competition">interspecific competition</a> and helps to segregate territories between related species.<sup id="cite_ref-mason85_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mason85-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cody_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cody-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> It appears more likely from later studies that segregation of <a href="/wiki/Sympatry" title="Sympatry">sympatric</a> species, other than the blackcap and garden warbler, is due to subtle habitat preferences rather than interspecies aggression.<sup id="cite_ref-martin_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-martin-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_blackcap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wwalas_Sylvia_atricapilla_4.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Three small chicks with open red mouths in a nest" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Wwalas_Sylvia_atricapilla_4.jpg/220px-Wwalas_Sylvia_atricapilla_4.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Wwalas_Sylvia_atricapilla_4.jpg/330px-Wwalas_Sylvia_atricapilla_4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Wwalas_Sylvia_atricapilla_4.jpg/440px-Wwalas_Sylvia_atricapilla_4.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="771" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wwalas_Sylvia_atricapilla_4.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Young chicks begging for food. These are still largely unfeathered.</div></div></div>
<p>Blackcaps first breed when they are one year old, and are mainly <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a>, although both sexes may sometimes deviate from this. A male attracts a female to his territory through song<sup id="cite_ref-Mason57_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason57-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> and a display involving raising the black crown feathers, fluffing the tail, slow wingbeats, and a short flapping flight. He also builds one or more simple nests (cock nests), usually near his songpost.<sup id="cite_ref-Simms_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simms-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The final nest, which may be one of the cock nests or built from scratch, is a neat cup of roots, stems and grasses lined with fine material such as hair. The nest is typically 5.5&#160;cm (2.2&#160;in) deep and 10&#160;cm (3.9&#160;in) across,<sup id="cite_ref-Mason57_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason57-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> and is built in the cover of <a href="/wiki/Bramble" title="Bramble">bramble</a>, scrubs or trees.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_17-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> It is constructed mainly by the female, and may be up to 4.5&#160;m (15&#160;ft) above the ground, although lower than 1&#160;m (3.3&#160;ft) is more typical.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason57_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason57-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is typically 4–6 eggs (range 2–7), which are usually buff with grey and brown blotches and a few dark brown spots. The average size of the egg is 19.7&#160;mm ×&#160;14.7&#160;mm (0.78&#160;in ×&#160;0.58&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_17-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mason63_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason63-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The eggs are <a href="/wiki/Incubate_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Incubate (bird)">incubated</a> for an average of 11&#160;days (range 10–16). Both adults incubate, although only the female stays on the nest at night. The chicks are <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a>, hatching naked and with closed eyes, and are fed by both parents. They <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> about 11–12&#160;days after hatching, leaving the nest shortly before they are able to fly. They are assisted with feeding for a further two or three weeks. If the nest is threatened, the non-incubating bird gives an alarm call so that the sitting parent and chicks stay still and quiet. A male blackcap may <a href="/wiki/Mobbing_(animal_behavior)" title="Mobbing (animal behavior)">mob</a> a potential predator, or try to lure it away with disjointed runs and flaps on the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason65_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason65-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The blackcap normally raises just one brood, but second nestings are sometimes recorded, particularly in the milder climate of the Mediterranean and the Atlantic islands; triple brooding has been observed once, the female laying a total of 23 eggs in the season.<sup id="cite_ref-weidinger_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weidinger-43">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Of eggs laid, 65–93% hatch successfully, and 75–92% of the chicks go on to fledge.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason69_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason69-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;b&#93;</a></sup> The productivity (young fledged per nest) varies with location, level of predation and quality of habitat, but the national figure for the UK was 2.5.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason69_44-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason69-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Weidinger2_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weidinger2-46">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-weid3_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weid3-47">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> The adult annual survival rate is 43% (males 46%, females 29%), and 36% of juveniles live through their first year.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-48">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The typical life expectancy is two years,<sup id="cite_ref-Mason69_44-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason69-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> but the record is 13&#160;years and 10&#160;months for a bird in the Czech Republic.<sup id="cite_ref-euring_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euring-49">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_blackcap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sylvia_atricapilla_Nice_2008.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A male blackcap eating a berry from a tree" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Sylvia_atricapilla_Nice_2008.jpg/220px-Sylvia_atricapilla_Nice_2008.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Sylvia_atricapilla_Nice_2008.jpg/330px-Sylvia_atricapilla_Nice_2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Sylvia_atricapilla_Nice_2008.jpg/440px-Sylvia_atricapilla_Nice_2008.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1571" data-file-height="1098" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sylvia_atricapilla_Nice_2008.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Male eating an olive from a tree in France in December</div></div></div>
<p>The blackcap feeds mainly on insects during the breeding season, then switches to fruit in late summer, the change being triggered by an internal <a href="/wiki/Chronobiology" title="Chronobiology">biological rhythm</a>. When migrants arrive on their territories they initially take berries, <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nectar" title="Nectar">nectar</a> if there are insufficient insects available, then soon switch to their preferred diet. They mainly pick prey off <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">foliage</a> and twigs, but may occasionally hover, flycatch or feed on the ground. Blackcaps eat a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrate</a> prey, although <a href="/wiki/Aphids" class="mw-redirect" title="Aphids">aphids</a> are particularly important early in the season, and <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a> are also taken in large numbers. Small snails are swallowed whole, since the shell is a source of calcium for the bird's eggs. Chicks are mainly fed soft-bodied insects, fruit only if invertebrates are scarce.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason72_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason72-50">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In July, the diet switches increasingly to fruit. The <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> needed for egg-laying and for the chicks to grow is replaced by fruit sugar which helps the birds to fatten for migration. Aphids are still taken while they are available, since they often contain sugars from the plant sap on which they feed. Blackcaps eat a wide range of small fruit, and squeeze out any seeds on a branch before consuming the pulp. This technique makes them an important propagator of <a href="/wiki/Mistletoe" title="Mistletoe">mistletoe</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Mistle_thrush" title="Mistle thrush">mistle thrush</a>, which also favours that plant, is less beneficial since it tends to crush the seeds. Although any suitable fruit may be eaten, some have seasonal or local importance; <a href="/wiki/Sambucus_nigra" title="Sambucus nigra">elder</a> makes up a large proportion of the diet of northern birds preparing for migration, and energy-rich <a href="/wiki/Olive" title="Olive">olives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pistacia_lentiscus" title="Pistacia lentiscus">lentisc</a> are favoured by blackcaps wintering in the Mediterranean.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason72_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason72-50">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The German birds wintering in British gardens rely on provided food, and the major items are bread and fat, each making up around 20% of the diet; one bird survived the whole winter eating only <a href="/wiki/Christmas_cake" title="Christmas cake">Christmas cake</a>. Fruit is also eaten, notably <a href="/wiki/Cotoneaster" title="Cotoneaster">cotoneaster</a> (41% of the fruit consumed), <a href="/wiki/Ivy" class="mw-redirect" title="Ivy">ivy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Honeysuckle" title="Honeysuckle">honeysuckle</a>, and apple if available. Some birds have learned to take <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanuts</a> from feeders.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason80_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason80-51">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Blackcaps defend good winter food sources in the wild,<sup id="cite_ref-Mason38_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason38-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> and at garden feeding stations they repel competitors as large as <a href="/wiki/Common_starling" title="Common starling">starlings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_blackbird" title="Common blackbird">blackbirds</a>. Birds occasionally become tame enough to feed from the hand.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason80_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason80-51">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators_and_parasites">Predators and parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_blackcap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Predators and parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo_chris_romeiks_CHR0431.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A bird with a grey back, pale underparts and along tail perched on a post" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo_chris_romeiks_CHR0431.jpg/220px-Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo_chris_romeiks_CHR0431.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="162" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo_chris_romeiks_CHR0431.jpg/330px-Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo_chris_romeiks_CHR0431.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo_chris_romeiks_CHR0431.jpg/440px-Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo_chris_romeiks_CHR0431.jpg 2x" data-file-width="903" data-file-height="666" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo_chris_romeiks_CHR0431.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common cuckoo</a> is an occasional <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasite</a> of the blackcap.</div></div></div>
<p>Blackcaps are caught by <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">Eurasian sparrowhawks</a> in the breeding range, and by <a href="/wiki/Eleonora%27s_falcon" title="Eleonora&#39;s falcon">Eleonora's falcons</a> on migration. <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_jay" title="Eurasian jay">Eurasian jays</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_magpie" title="Eurasian magpie">Eurasian magpies</a> take eggs and young, as do mammals such as <a href="/wiki/Stoat" title="Stoat">stoats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Least_weasel" title="Least weasel">weasels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Squirrel" title="Squirrel">squirrels</a>. Domestic cats are the most important predator, possibly killing up to 10% of blackcaps.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason49_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason49-52">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Blackcaps are occasionally hosts of the <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common cuckoo</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasite</a>. The level of parasitism is low because the cuckoo's eggs are often rejected. Blackcaps have evolved adaptations which make it difficult for the parasitic species to succeed, despite the cuckoo's tendency to lay eggs which resemble those of their host. Blackcaps are good at spotting alien eggs, and their own eggs are very alike within a clutch, making it easier to spot the intruder. There is, however, considerable variation between different clutches, making it harder for the cuckoo to convincingly mimic a blackcap egg. The open habitat and cup nest of the warbler make it a potential target for the cuckoo; it may have experienced much higher levels of parasitism in the past, and countermeasures would have spread rapidly once they evolved.<sup id="cite_ref-honza_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-honza-53">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The only blood parasites found in a study of blackcaps trapped on migration were <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoans</a> from the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Haemoproteus" title="Haemoproteus">Haemoproteus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Plasmodium" title="Plasmodium">Plasmodium</a></i>. Of those affected, 45.5% were males and 22.7% were females, but the number of parasites was small, and the ability to store fat for the migration flight was unimpaired.<sup id="cite_ref-arizaga_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arizaga-54">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Seventeen strains of <i><a href="/wiki/Haemoproteus_parabelopolskyi" title="Haemoproteus parabelopolskyi">H. parabelopolskyi</a></i> are found only in the blackcap, and form a <a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">monophyletic</a> group; three further members of that group are found only in the garden warbler, and another three occur in the African hill babbler, supporting the shared ancestry of the three bird species.<sup id="cite_ref-malaria_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-malaria-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The protozoan <i><a href="/wiki/Isospora_ashmoonensis" title="Isospora ashmoonensis">Isospora ashmoonensis</a></i> was first identified in a blackcap in Egypt.<sup id="cite_ref-egypt_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-egypt-55">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Blackcaps may carry <a href="/wiki/Parasitic_worm" title="Parasitic worm">parasitic worms</a> that sometimes kill their hosts.<sup id="cite_ref-helminth_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-helminth-56">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> External parasites include <a href="/wiki/Mallophaga" title="Mallophaga">chewing lice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feather_mite" title="Feather mite">feather mites</a>. The latter do little damage, although heavy infestations cause individual tail feathers to develop asymmetrically.<sup id="cite_ref-tail_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tail-57">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-french_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-french-58">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_blackcap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Clare.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A painting of a seated man in a brown jacket and buff waistcoat" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/John_Clare.jpg/170px-John_Clare.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="208" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/John_Clare.jpg/255px-John_Clare.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/John_Clare.jpg/340px-John_Clare.jpg 2x" data-file-width="654" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Clare.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The English poet <a href="/wiki/John_Clare" title="John Clare">John Clare</a> described the blackcap as the "March Nightingale"</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, in his <i><a href="/wiki/History_of_Animals" title="History of Animals">History of Animals</a></i>, considered that the <a href="/wiki/Garden_warbler" title="Garden warbler">garden warbler</a> eventually <a href="/wiki/Metamorphosis" title="Metamorphosis">metamorphosed</a> into a blackcap.<sup id="cite_ref-arnason_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arnason-59">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> The blackcap's song has led to it being described as the "mock nightingale" or "country nightingale", and <a href="/wiki/John_Clare" title="John Clare">John Clare</a>, in "The March Nightingale" describes the listener as believing that the rarer species has arrived prematurely. "He stops his own and thinks the nightingale/Hath of her monthly reckoning counted wrong".<sup id="cite_ref-cocker_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cocker-60">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> The song is also the topic of Italian poet <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Pascoli" title="Giovanni Pascoli">Giovanni Pascoli</a>'s "La Capinera" [The Blackcap].<sup id="cite_ref-pasco_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pasco-61">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Verga" title="Giovanni Verga">Giovanni Verga</a>'s 1871 novel <i>Storia di una capinera</i>, according to its author, was inspired by a story of a blackcap trapped and caged by children. The bird, silent and pining for its lost freedom, eventually dies. In the book, a nun evacuated from her convent by <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a> falls in love with a family friend, only to have to return to her confinement when the disease wanes. The novel was adapted as films of the same name in 1917, 1943 and 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-Cardillo_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cardillo-62">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-chiti351_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiti351-63">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> The last version was directed by <a href="/wiki/Franco_Zeffirelli" title="Franco Zeffirelli">Franco Zeffirelli</a>, and its English-language version was retitled as <i><a href="/wiki/Sparrow_(1993_film)" title="Sparrow (1993 film)">Sparrow</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-chiti231_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiti231-64">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/Saint_Fran%C3%A7ois_d%27Assise" title="Saint François d&#39;Assise">Saint François d'Assise</a></i>, an opera by <a href="/wiki/Olivier_Messiaen" title="Olivier Messiaen">Messiaen</a>, the orchestration is based on bird song. <a href="/wiki/Francis_of_Assisi" title="Francis of Assisi">St Francis</a> himself is represented by the blackcap.<sup id="cite_ref-hill_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hill-65">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Folk names for the blackcap often refer to its most obvious plumage feature (black-headed peggy, King Harry black cap and coal hoodie) or to its song, as in the "nightingale" names above. Other old names are based on its choice of nesting material (Jack Straw, hay bird, hay chat and hay Jack).<sup id="cite_ref-Mason13_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason13-66">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> There is a tradition of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Air_Arm" title="Fleet Air Arm">Fleet Air Arm</a> bases being named for birds. A former base near <a href="/wiki/Stretton,_Warrington" title="Stretton, Warrington">Stretton</a> in Cheshire was called <a href="/wiki/RNAS_Stretton_(HMS_Blackcap)" title="RNAS Stretton (HMS Blackcap)">HMS <i>Blackcap</i></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-67">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_blackcap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The blackcap has a very large range, and its population in Europe is estimated at 25–49&#160;million breeding pairs. Allowing for birds breeding in Africa and Asia, the total population is between 80–196&#160;million individuals. It is therefore classified by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> as being of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Blackcaps and other small birds are illegally trapped and hunted in large numbers in Mediterranean countries, particularly in Lebanon, Syria, Palestine, Malta, Libya, Egypt and Cyprus, where they are considered as a <a href="/wiki/Delicacy" title="Delicacy">delicacy</a>. Despite hunting and natural hazards, the European population of the blackcap has been rising for several decades as the range extends northwards,<sup id="cite_ref-Mason49_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason49-52">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cocker_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cocker-60">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> for example to Scotland and Denmark. There are occasional nesting records from outside the main range, such as in northern Israel and the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroes</a>, and wandering birds may appear further afield in Iceland or on the islands of Arctic Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_17-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In the Baltic, the spread of the blackcap appears to have been helped by the availability of territories formerly occupied by the declining <a href="/wiki/Barred_warbler" title="Barred warbler">barred warbler</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-vlad_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vlad-68">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
