<p>The <b>Green Bee-eater</b> (<i>Merops orientalis</i>) (sometimes <b>Little Green Bee-eater</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Near_passerine" title="Near passerine">near passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bee-eater" title="Bee-eater">bee-eater</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a>. It is resident but prone to seasonal movements and is found widely distributed across sub-Saharan <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> from <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Gambia" title="The Gambia">The Gambia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> valley, western Arabia and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> through <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> to <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-fry_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fry-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> They are mainly insect eaters and they are found in grassland, thin scrub and forest often quite far from water. Several regional plumage variations are known and several subspecies have been named.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Bee-eater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Little_Green_Bee-eater.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Little_Green_Bee-eater.jpg/170px-Little_Green_Bee-eater.jpg" width="170" height="231" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Little_Green_Bee-eater.jpg/255px-Little_Green_Bee-eater.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Little_Green_Bee-eater.jpg/340px-Little_Green_Bee-eater.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Little_Green_Bee-eater.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Subspecies <i>muscatensis</i> from the UAE</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Like other bee-eaters, this species is a richly coloured, slender bird. It is about 9&#160;inches (16–18&#160;cm) long with about 2&#160;inches made up by the elongated central tail-feathers. The sexes are not visually distinguishable. The entire plumage is bright green and tinged with blue especially on the chin and throat. The crown and upper back are tinged with golden rufous. The flight feathers are rufous washed with green and tipped with blackish. A fine black line runs in front of and behind the eye. The iris is crimson and the bill is black while the legs are dark grey. The feet are weak with the three toes joined at the base.<sup id="cite_ref-whistler_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whistler-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Southeast Asian birds have rufous crown and face, and green underparts, whereas Arabian <i>beludschicus</i> has a green crown, blue face and bluish underparts. The wings are green and the beak is black. The elongated tail feathers are absent in juveniles. Sexes are alike.<sup id="cite_ref-fry_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fry-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The calls is a nasal trill <i>tree-tree-tree-tree</i>, usually given in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-whistler_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whistler-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Bee-eater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Little_green_bee_eater_(cropped).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Little_green_bee_eater_%28cropped%29.jpg/170px-Little_green_bee_eater_%28cropped%29.jpg" width="170" height="294" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Little_green_bee_eater_%28cropped%29.jpg/255px-Little_green_bee_eater_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/97/Little_green_bee_eater_%28cropped%29.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Little_green_bee_eater_(cropped).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Race <i>cyanophrys</i> from Israel</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Several populations have been designated as subspecies:<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>viridissimus</i> is found from Senegal to northern Ethiopia (has more green on the throat, crown and nape with long streamers)</li>
<li><i>cleopatra</i> from the Nile Valley to northern Sudan</li>
<li><i>flavoviridis</i> from northern Chad to Sudan</li>
<li><i>muscatensis</i> on the Arabian plateau (more yellowish green with narrow gorget on throat)</li>
<li><i>cyanophrys</i> found in Israel and the Arabian region (includes <i>meccanus</i>)</li>
<li><i>beludschicus</i>(=<i>biludschicus</i><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup>) Iran to Pakistan (paler colours with a blue throat)<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>orientalis</i> in India and Sri Lanka (has head and neck tinged with rufous)</li>
<li><i>ferrugeiceps</i> (=<i>birmanus</i>) in northeastern India, Myanmar, Thailand and Vietnam (has rufous crown, name and mantle)</li>
<li><i>ceylonicus</i> in Sri Lanka has the nape and hindneck with more pronounced golden brown sheen<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> often included within the nominate race</li>
</ul>
<p>A study of species within the genus <i>Merops</i> based on plumage characteristics found that most of the subspecies of <i>orientalis</i> grouping together with the most similar species being <i><a href="/wiki/Merops_leschenaulti" title="Merops leschenaulti" class="mw-redirect">Merops leschenaulti</a></i> and subspecies <i>ferrugeiceps</i> appeared closer to that group.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Leucism" title="Leucism">Leucistic</a> individuals have been noted.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Bee-eater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>This is an abundant and fairly tame bird, familiar throughout its range. It is a bird which breeds in open country with bushes. In Africa and Arabia it is found in arid areas, but is more diverse in its habitats further east. This species often hunts from low perches, maybe only a metre or less high. It readily makes use of fence wires and electric wires. Unlike some other bee-eaters, they can be found well away from water.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>They are mostly see in the plains but can sometimes be found up to 5000 or 6000 feet in the Himalayas. They are resident in the lowlands of South Asia but some populations move seasonally but the patterns are unclear<sup id="cite_ref-whistler_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whistler-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> moving away to drier regions in the rainy season and to warmer regions in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> In parts of Pakistan, they are summer visitors.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Bee-eater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Like other species in the genus, bee-eaters predominantly eat <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">wasps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ants</a>, which are caught in the air by sorties from an open perch. Before swallowing prey, a bee-eater removes stings and breaks the exoskeleton of the prey by repeatedly thrashing it on the perch. <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">Migration</a> is not known but they make seasonal movements in response to rainfall.<sup id="cite_ref-fry_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fry-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> These birds are somewhat sluggish in the mornings and may be found huddled next to each other on wires sometimes with their bills tucked in their backs well after sunrise. They sand-bathe more frequently than other bee-eater species and will sometimes bathe in water by dipping into water in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> They are usually seen in small groups and often roost communally in large numbers (200-300). The birds move excitedly at the roost site and call loudly, often explosively dispersing before settling back to the roost tree.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The Little Green Bee-Eater is also becoming common in urban and sub-urban neighborhoods, and has been observed perching on television antennae, only to launch into a brief, zig-zag flight formation to catch an insect, then return to the same perch and consume the meal. This behaviour is generally observed between the hours of 7:00 and 8:00am, and after 4:00pm.</p>

<p>The breeding season is from March to June. Unlike many bee-eaters, these are often solitary nesters, making a tunnel in a sandy bank. The breeding pairs are often joined by <a href="/wiki/Helpers_at_the_nest" title="Helpers at the nest">helpers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> They nest in hollows in vertical mud banks. The nest tunnel that they construct can run as much as 5 feet long and the 3-5 eggs are laid on the bare ground in the cavity at the end of the tunnel. The eggs are very spherical and glossy white.<sup id="cite_ref-whistler_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whistler-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Clutch size varies with rainfall and insect food density. Both sexes incubate. The eggs hatch asynchronously with an incubation period of about 14 days and the chicks grow fledge in 3 to 4 weeks and in the fledging stage show a reduction in body weight.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A study suggested that Green Bee-eaters may be capable of interpreting the behaviour of human observers. They showed an ability to predict whether a human at a particular location would be capable of spotting the nest entrance and then behaved appropriately to avoid giving away the nest location. The ability to look at a situation from another's point of view was previously believed to be possessed only by primates.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Riverside habitats were found to support high populations in southern India (157 birds per square kilometre) dropping off too 101 per km² in agricultural areas and 43-58 per square km near human habitations.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>They feed on flying insects and can sometimes be nuisance to bee-keepers.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The preferred prey was mostly beetles followed by <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">hymenopterans</a>. <a href="/wiki/Orthoptera" title="Orthoptera">Orthopterans</a> appear to be avoided.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> They are sometimes known to take crabs.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Like most other birds they regurgitate the hard parts of their prey as pellets.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>An endoparasitic nematode (<i>Torquatoides balanocephala</i>) sometimes infects their gizzard.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
