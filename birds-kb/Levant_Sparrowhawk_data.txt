<p>The <b>Levant sparrowhawk</b> (<i>Accipiter brevipes</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a>. It measures 32–38&#160;cm (13–15&#160;in) in length with a wingspan of 65–75&#160;cm (26–30&#160;in). The female is larger than the male, but the difference is not as marked as with Eurasian sparrowhawk. The adult male is blue-grey above, with dark wingtips, and barred reddish below.
</p><p>The adult female is slate-grey above with darkish wingtips. She is barred reddish brown below, and may show a dark throat line. The juvenile is dark brown above and has dark-streaked underparts. It shows a dark throat line. It breeds in forests from <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a> east to southern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering from <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> across to southwestern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. It will migrate in large flocks, unlike the more widespread <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">Eurasian sparrowhawk</a>.
</p><p>The Levant sparrowhawk nests in trees, building a new nest, lined with green leaves, each year. The normal clutch is 3–5 eggs. It hunts small <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, rodents, and <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a> in woodland or semi-desert areas, relying on surprise as it flies from a perch to catch its prey unaware.
</p><p>This bird is a small raptor with short broad wings and a longish tail, both adaptations to manoeuvring through trees. It is similar to the Eurasian sparrowhawk, but its shorter tail and more pointed wings give it a more <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon-like</a> appearance.
</p><p>The flight of this hawk is a characteristic <i>flap – flap – glide</i>.
</p><p>The call is a sharp <i>kee-wick</i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levant_sparrowhawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>First described by <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Alekseevich_Severtzov" class="mw-redirect" title="Nikolai Alekseevich Severtzov">Nikolai Alekseevich Severtzov</a> in 1850, the Levant sparrowhawk is a small <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> in the genus <i>Accipiter</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Clark126_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clark126-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is sometimes considered to be a subspecies of the <a href="/wiki/Shikra" title="Shikra">shikra</a>, though it differs in measurements, proportions and plumage, and breeds <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/contiguous" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:contiguous">contiguously</a> with the latter (typically considered a reliable indicator of <a href="/wiki/Speciation" title="Speciation">speciation</a>) over at least part of its range. Along with the shikra, the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_sparrowhawk" title="Chinese sparrowhawk">Chinese sparrowhawk</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nicobar_sparrowhawk" title="Nicobar sparrowhawk">Nicobar sparrowhawk</a>, it makes up a complex <a href="/wiki/Species_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Species group">species group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferg-Lees531_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferg-Lees531-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is known to have <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridised</a> with the shikra and the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">Eurasian sparrowhawk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-McCarthy_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Despite its extensive range, it has no <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Clark130_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clark130-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The genus name <i>Accipiter</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word meaning "hawk" (from the Latin word <i>accipere</i>, meaning "to grasp").<sup id="cite_ref-Jobling_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jobling-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The species name <i>brevipes</i> is a combination of the Latin words <i>brevis</i>, meaning "short" and <i>pes</i>, meaning "foot"; this is a reference to the bird's short toes.<sup id="cite_ref-Clark130_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clark130-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levant_sparrowhawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Levant sparrowhawk is small for a raptor, measuring 32–38&#160;cm (13–15&#160;in) in length with a <a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">wingspan</a> of 65–75&#160;cm (26–30&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Beaman193_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beaman193-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> As with all raptors, the <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">female is larger</a> than the male.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferg-Lees529_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferg-Lees529-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The adult male is blue-grey above and pale below, with underparts and leg feathers finely barred in rufous and white. His head is blue-grey as well, with a white throat bisected by a dark central stripe (sometimes quite faint). The female is similar, though her plumage is generally a darker brownish-grey. Both sexes have orangish-yellow legs and a yellow <a href="/wiki/Cere" class="mw-redirect" title="Cere">cere</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Clark126_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clark126-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levant_sparrowhawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levant_sparrowhawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Levant sparrowhawk breeds from mid-May through August.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferg-Lees531_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferg-Lees531-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The pair is territorial while breeding, often performing high-circling aerial displays.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferg-Lees531_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferg-Lees531-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Clark128_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clark128-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The female is thought to make the <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Perrins_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Perrins-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> She builds a new one every year, a small structure of twigs on a branch or in a fork of a <a href="/wiki/Broad-leaved_tree" title="Broad-leaved tree">broad-leaved tree</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferg-Lees531_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferg-Lees531-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wimberger_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wimberger-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The nest tree is often near running water, typically in open woodland, on a forest edge, or in an isolated clump of trees. The nest, which measures up to 30&#160;cm (12&#160;in) across and 15&#160;cm (5.9&#160;in) deep, is lined with green leaves. Most nests are located between 5–10&#160;m (16–33&#160;ft) above the ground, but they have been found as low as 4&#160;m (13&#160;ft) and as high as 20&#160;m (66&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-Ferg-Lees531_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferg-Lees531-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Accipiter_brevipes_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.94.2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Accipiter_brevipes_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.94.2.jpg/220px-Accipiter_brevipes_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.94.2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Accipiter_brevipes_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.94.2.jpg/330px-Accipiter_brevipes_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.94.2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Accipiter_brevipes_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.94.2.jpg/440px-Accipiter_brevipes_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.94.2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3307" data-file-height="3307" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Accipiter_brevipes_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.94.2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Accipiter brevipes</i> - <a href="/wiki/MHNT" class="mw-redirect" title="MHNT">MHNT</a></div></div></div>
<p>The female lays a <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> of <span class="nowrap">2–5 eggs</span> which she alone <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation" title="Egg incubation">incubates</a> for <span class="nowrap">30–35 days</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-Perrins_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Perrins-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ABCC_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABCC-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Hatching is <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/asynchronous" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:asynchronous">asynchronous</a>. Nestlings fledge some <span class="nowrap">40–45 days</span> after hatching, and are independent about <span class="nowrap">15 days</span> later.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferg-Lees531_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferg-Lees531-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_and_migration">Flight and migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levant_sparrowhawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Flight and migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Though able to flap continually (like most birds), Levant sparrowhawks typically use soaring-and-gliding flight while migrating; they also use thermals when those are available,<sup id="cite_ref-Ferg-Lees45_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferg-Lees45-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and are known to occasionally hover.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferg-Lees64_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferg-Lees64-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levant_sparrowhawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Armenia the population of Levant Sparrowhawks is estimated as 180-220 breeding pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-ABCC_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABCC-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_and_threats">Conservation and threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Levant_sparrowhawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conservation and threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of its vast range and stable population, the Levant Sparrowhawk is listed as a species of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a> by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_the_Conservation_of_Nature" class="mw-redirect" title="International Union for the Conservation of Nature">International Union for the Conservation of Nature</a>. The development of wind farms may affect its numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
