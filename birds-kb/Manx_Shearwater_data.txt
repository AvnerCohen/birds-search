<p>The <b>Manx shearwater</b> (<i>Puffinus puffinus</i>) is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Shearwater" title="Shearwater">shearwater</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> family <a href="/wiki/Procellariidae" title="Procellariidae">Procellariidae</a>. The scientific name of this <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> records a name shift: Manx shearwaters were called Manks puffins in the 17th century. Puffin is an <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">Anglo-Norman</a> word (<a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> <i>pophyn</i>) for the cured carcasses of nestling shearwaters. The <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_puffin" title="Atlantic puffin">Atlantic puffin</a> acquired the name much later, possibly because of its similar nesting habits.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manx_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Shearwater" title="Shearwater">shearwaters</a> form part of the family <a href="/wiki/Procellariidae" title="Procellariidae">Procellariidae</a>, a widespread group containing nearly 100 species of medium to large <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a>. They have long, narrow wings and the characteristic “tube nose”.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Although it was considered a monotypic species, recently an endemic subspecies for the Canary Islands has been proposed <i>P. puffinus canariensis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  The large genus <i>Puffinus</i> includes several species formerly considered to be subspecies of the Manx shearwater, including the <a href="/wiki/Yelkouan_shearwater" title="Yelkouan shearwater">yelkouan shearwater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Balearic_shearwater" title="Balearic shearwater">Balearic shearwater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hutton%27s_shearwater" title="Hutton&#39;s shearwater">Hutton's shearwater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black-vented_shearwater" title="Black-vented shearwater">black-vented shearwater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fluttering_shearwater" title="Fluttering shearwater">fluttering shearwater</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Townsend%27s_shearwater" title="Townsend&#39;s shearwater">Townsend's shearwater</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_shearwater" class="mw-redirect" title="Hawaiian shearwater">Hawaiian shearwater</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-austin_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-austin-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-murphy_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-murphy-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  Of these, the Hawaiian and possibly Townsend's shearwaters seem to be most closely related to the Manx shearwater.<sup id="cite_ref-austin_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-austin-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Three extinct species appear to be closely related to the Manx shearwater, the <a href="/wiki/Lava_shearwater" title="Lava shearwater">lava shearwater</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-lava_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lava-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Dune_shearwater" title="Dune shearwater">dune shearwater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scarlett%27s_shearwater" title="Scarlett&#39;s shearwater">Scarlett's shearwater</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-jo_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jo-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-scarlett_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scarlett-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> recovered from the lava shearwater of the Canary Islands suggests that it is the Manx shearwater's <a href="/wiki/Sister_species" class="mw-redirect" title="Sister species">sister species</a> despite being significantly smaller.<sup id="cite_ref-ramirez_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ramirez-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Manx shearwater was first described by Danish zoologist <a href="/wiki/Morten_Thrane_Br%C3%BCnnich" title="Morten Thrane Brünnich">Morten Thrane Brünnich</a> as <i>Procellaria puffinus</i> in 1764.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-brun_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brun-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  The current scientific name <i>Puffinus</i> derives from "puffin" and its variants, such as poffin, pophyn, and puffing,<sup id="cite_ref-oedpuffin_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oedpuffin-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> which referred to the cured carcass of the fat nestling of the shearwater, a former delicacy.<sup id="cite_ref-job323_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job323-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The original usage dates from at least 1337, but from as early as 1678, the term gradually came to be used for another seabird, the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_puffin" title="Atlantic puffin">Atlantic puffin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-oedpuffin_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oedpuffin-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The current English name was first recorded in 1835 and refers to the former nesting of this species on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-oedmanx_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oedmanx-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manx_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Puffinus_puffinus_-Iceland_-flying-6.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Puffinus_puffinus_-Iceland_-flying-6.jpg/220px-Puffinus_puffinus_-Iceland_-flying-6.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="135" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Puffinus_puffinus_-Iceland_-flying-6.jpg/330px-Puffinus_puffinus_-Iceland_-flying-6.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Puffinus_puffinus_-Iceland_-flying-6.jpg/440px-Puffinus_puffinus_-Iceland_-flying-6.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="491" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Puffinus_puffinus_-Iceland_-flying-6.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Flying in Iceland</div></div></div>
<p>The Manx shearwater is 30–38&#160;cm (12–15&#160;in) with a 76–89&#160;cm (30–35&#160;in) wingspan and weighs 350–575&#160;g (<span class="frac nowrap">12<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span>–<span class="frac nowrap">20<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span>&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It has the typically "shearing" flight of the genus, dipping from side to side on stiff wings with few wingbeats, the wingtips almost touching the water. This bird looks like a flying cross, with its wings held at right angles to the body, and it changes from black to white as the black upper parts and white under sides are alternately exposed as it travels low over the sea.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manx_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This shearwater is mainly silent at sea, even when birds are gathered off the breeding colonies. It calls on its nocturnal visits to the nesting burrows in flight, on the ground, and in the burrows, although moonlight depresses the amount of calling. The vocalisations largely consists of a raucous series of croons, howls, and screams, typically in groups of a few syllables, which become weaker and throatier. The male has some clear ringing and shrieking tones absent from the harsher repertoire of the female, the difference being obvious when a pair duets. Females can recognise the voice of their mates, but not of their young.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> They do not provide postnesting care, and  a chick in their burrow is likely their own, so voice identification is not needed.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooke195_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooke195-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vision">Vision</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manx_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vision">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Manxshearwaterretina.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Manxshearwaterretina.svg/220px-Manxshearwaterretina.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Manxshearwaterretina.svg/330px-Manxshearwaterretina.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Manxshearwaterretina.svg/440px-Manxshearwaterretina.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1052" data-file-height="744" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Manxshearwaterretina.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Each retina of the Manx shearwater has one <a href="/wiki/Fovea_centralis" title="Fovea centralis">fovea</a> and an elongated strip of high photoreceptor density. The <a href="/wiki/Pecten_oculi" title="Pecten oculi">pecten</a> has many blood vessels and appears to keep the retina supplied with nutrients.<sup id="cite_ref-retina_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-retina-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bird_vision" title="Bird vision">vision</a> of the Manx shearwater has a number of adaptations to its way of life. Like other tube-nosed seabirds, it has a long, narrow area of visual sensitivity containing the <a href="/wiki/Fovea_centralis" title="Fovea centralis">fovea</a> across the <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">retina</a> of the eye.<sup id="cite_ref-Sturkie_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sturkie-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> This region is characterised by the presence of <a href="/wiki/Retinal_ganglion_cell" title="Retinal ganglion cell">ganglion cells</a> that are regularly arrayed and larger than those found in the rest of the retina. This feature helps in the detection of items in a small area projecting below and around the bill. It may assist in the detection of prey near the sea surface as a bird flies low over it.<sup id="cite_ref-Hayes_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hayes-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since it visits its breeding colony at night, a shearwater has adaptations for nocturnal vision, too. In the shearwater's eyes, the <a href="/wiki/Lens_(anatomy)" title="Lens (anatomy)">lens</a> does most of the bending of light necessary to produce a focused image on the retina. The <a href="/wiki/Cornea" title="Cornea">cornea</a>, the outer covering of the eye, is relative flat, so of low <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refractive</a> power. In a diurnal bird like a <a href="/wiki/Rock_dove" title="Rock dove">pigeon</a>, the reverse is true; the cornea is highly curved and is the principal refractive component. The ratio of refraction by the lens to that by the cornea is 1.6 for the shearwater and 0.4 for the pigeon. The shorter focal length of shearwater eyes give them a smaller, but brighter, image than is the case for pigeons. Although the Manx shearwater has adaptations for night vision, the effect is small, and  these birds likely also use smell and hearing to locate their nests.<sup id="cite_ref-Martinbrooke_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martinbrooke-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manx_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Manx shearwater is entirely marine, typically flying within  10&#160;m (30&#160;ft) of the sea surface. It nests in burrows on small islands, which it visits only at night.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Its nesting colonies are in the north <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> in the United Kingdom, Ireland, Iceland, the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, France, the Isle of Man, the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>. The most important colonies, with a total of more than 300,000 pairs, are on islands off Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. Three-quarters of the British and Irish birds breed on just three islands; <a href="/wiki/Skomer" title="Skomer">Skomer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skokholm" title="Skokholm">Skokholm</a>, and <a href="/wiki/R%C3%B9m" title="Rùm">Rùm</a>. Around 7000–9000 pairs breed in Iceland, with at least 15,000 pairs on the Faeroes. Other populations are of at most a few hundred pairs. The northeast of North America has recently been colonised from <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a> to <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>;  although breeding was first recorded in 1973, populations remain small. Records in the northeast <a href="/wiki/Pacific" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific">Pacific</a> are increasing, and breeding has been suspected in <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the breeding season birds regularly commute between their colony and offshore feeding grounds that can be up to 1,500&#160;km away.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> For example, adult Manx shearwaters rearing their chick on the west coast of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> have been observed to travel all the way to the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a> to feed when the conditions are right.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The breeding colonies are deserted from July to March, when the birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> to the South Atlantic, wintering mainly off Brazil and Argentina, with smaller numbers off southwest South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Onley_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Onley-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
The journey south can be over 10,000&#160;km (6,000&#160;mi),<sup id="cite_ref-guild_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guild-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> so a 50-year-old bird has probably covered over a million&#160;km (600,000&#160;mi) on migration alone. The migration also appears to be quite complex, containing many stopovers and foraging zones throughout the Atlantic Ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-MigrationBehaviour2013_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MigrationBehaviour2013-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Chris_Mead" title="Chris Mead">Chris Mead</a> estimated that a bird ringed in 1957 when aged about 5 years and still breeding on <a href="/wiki/Bardsey_Island" title="Bardsey Island">Bardsey Island</a> off <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> in April 2002 had flown over 8&#160;million&#160;km (5&#160;million&#160;mi) in total during its 50-year life.<sup id="cite_ref-CNN2002_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNN2002-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Manx shearwaters are able to fly directly back to their burrows when released hundreds of kilometres away, even inland.<sup id="cite_ref-Birkhead168_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birkhead168-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manx_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Manx shearwaters are long-lived birds. A Manx shearwater breeding on <a href="/wiki/Copeland_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Copeland Island">Copeland Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, was as of 2003/04, the oldest known living wild bird in the world: <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">ringed</a> as an adult (at least 5 years old) in July 1953, it was retrapped in July 2003, at least 55 years old.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This is a gregarious species, which can be seen in large numbers from boats or headlands, especially on <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migration</a> in autumn. It is silent at sea, but at night, the breeding colonies are alive with raucous cackling calls.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manx_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Puffinus_puffinus_MWNH_0540.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Puffinus_puffinus_MWNH_0540.JPG/220px-Puffinus_puffinus_MWNH_0540.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="218" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Puffinus_puffinus_MWNH_0540.JPG/330px-Puffinus_puffinus_MWNH_0540.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Puffinus_puffinus_MWNH_0540.JPG/440px-Puffinus_puffinus_MWNH_0540.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2398" data-file-height="2376" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Puffinus_puffinus_MWNH_0540.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>Although shearwaters return to the breeding colonies from March onwards, the females often then leave again for 2–3 weeks before egg-laying in early May. Males return to the colonies in which they were hatched, but up to half of females may move elsewhere. The nest is a <a href="/wiki/Burrow_nest" class="mw-redirect" title="Burrow nest">burrow</a>, often previously excavated by a <a href="/wiki/European_rabbit" title="European rabbit">European rabbit</a>, although shearwaters can dig their own holes. Suitable holes under rocks may also be used. The burrows may be reused in subsequent years.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The single white egg averages 61&#160;mm ×&#160;42&#160;mm (<span class="frac nowrap">2<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span>&#160;in ×&#160;<span class="frac nowrap">1<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>3</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span>&#160;in) and weighs 57&#160;g (2&#160;oz), of which 7% is shell.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_feeding">Food and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manx_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Food and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Manx shearwater feeds on small <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> (<a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herrings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sprat" title="Sprat">sprats</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sand_eel" title="Sand eel">sand eels</a>), <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">cephalopods</a>, and surface <a href="/wiki/Offal" title="Offal">offal</a>. The bird catches food off the surface or by pursuit diving, and forages alone or in small flocks. It can be attracted by feeding <a href="/wiki/Cetacea" title="Cetacea">cetaceans</a>, but rarely follows boats or associates with other shearwater species.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Tube-nosed seabirds can detect food items at a distance of several tens of kilometres using their sense of smell to detect offal and compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfoxide" title="Dimethyl sulfoxide">dimethyl sulfoxide</a> produced when <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> is consumed by <a href="/wiki/Krill" title="Krill">krill</a>. They track across the wind until they find a scent and then follow it upwind to its origin.<sup id="cite_ref-Birkhead154_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birkhead154-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:270px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Flesh-footed_Shearwater_(5041605168).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Flesh-footed_Shearwater_%285041605168%29.jpg/268px-Flesh-footed_Shearwater_%285041605168%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="268" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Flesh-footed_Shearwater_%285041605168%29.jpg/402px-Flesh-footed_Shearwater_%285041605168%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Flesh-footed_Shearwater_%285041605168%29.jpg/536px-Flesh-footed_Shearwater_%285041605168%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1152" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flesh-footed_Shearwater_(5041605168).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rafting, a common behaviour in shearwaters</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rafting">Rafting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manx_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Rafting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Manx shearwaters engage in a behaviour termed "rafting", where birds sit, often in large groups of more than 10,000, on the water adjacent to their <a href="/wiki/Skomer" title="Skomer">Skomer Island</a>, breeding colony before and after visiting their chicks. Rafts move closer to the island during the night and further away in the morning which produces a "halo" effect - where no birds are found close to the island during daylight. These day-night cycles of rafting distributions are prominent for Manx shearwaters around Skomer Island and might provide a way of waiting for dusk that reduces <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predation</a> risk.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators_and_parasites">Predators and parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manx_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Predators and parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of their lack of mobility on land, Manx shearwaters are vulnerable to attack by large gulls, such as the <a href="/wiki/Great_black-backed_gull" title="Great black-backed gull">great black-backed gull</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-walestrust_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-walestrust-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Great_skua" title="Great skua">great skua</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hedge_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hedge-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">Birds of prey</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">peregrine falcon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">golden eagle</a> are also recorded as killing adult birds.<sup id="cite_ref-BB58_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB58-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Rats and cats are a serious problem where they are present; the large shearwater colony on the Calf of Man was destroyed by rats that arrived from a shipwreck in the late 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-JNCC_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JNCC-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/European_hedgehog" title="European hedgehog">European hedgehogs</a> eat the eggs of nesting seabirds where they have been introduced.<sup id="cite_ref-hedge_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hedge-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Red_deer" title="Red deer">Red deer</a> have been recorded killing and eating young shearwaters on at least <a href="/wiki/Foula" title="Foula">Foula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skokholm" title="Skokholm">Skokholm</a>, and Rùm; on the latter island, 4% of the chicks are killed by deer, and sheep have also been involved.<sup id="cite_ref-Brookeix_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brookeix-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The reason for the carnivorous behaviour is thought to be a need for extra calcium.<sup id="cite_ref-furness_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-furness-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Manx shearwaters frequently carry <a href="/wiki/Feather_louse" class="mw-redirect" title="Feather louse">feather lice</a> (<a href="/wiki/Mallophaga" title="Mallophaga">Mallophaga</a>) most of which are either the feather-eaters in the groups  <a href="/wiki/Ischnocera" title="Ischnocera">Ischnocera</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Amblycera" title="Amblycera">Amblycera</a>, which also consume blood. The most common are the ischnocerans <i>Halipeurus diversus</i> and  <i>Trabeculus aviator</i>. The nests of breeding birds frequently contain the shearwater flea; <i>Ornithopsylla laetitiae</i> is also commonly present, which shares a common ancestry with North American rabbit fleas.<sup id="cite_ref-Rothschild63_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rothschild63-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Where their burrows are near those of Atlantic puffins, the <a href="/wiki/Tick" title="Tick">tick</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Ixodes_uriae" title="Ixodes uriae">Ixodes uriae</a></i> is common.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooke16_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooke16-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The  <a href="/wiki/Mite" title="Mite">mite</a> <i>Neotrombicula autumnalis</i> is often present, and has been implicated in spreading <a href="/wiki/Puffinosis" title="Puffinosis">puffinosis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooke16_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooke16-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Puffinosis is a <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viral</a> disease of in which young birds get blisters on their feet, <a href="/wiki/Conjunctivitis" title="Conjunctivitis">conjunctivitis</a>, and problems with movement. The death rate can reach 70% in infected birds.<sup id="cite_ref-BB1965_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB1965-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BB1967_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB1967-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Internal parasites include the <a href="/wiki/Cestode" class="mw-redirect" title="Cestode">tapeworm</a> <i>Tetrabothrius cylindricus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rothschild197_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rothschild197-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manx_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European population of the Manx shearwater has been estimated at 350,000–390,000 breeding pairs or 1,050,000–1,700,000 individual birds, and makes up 95% of the world total numbers. Although this species' population now appears to be declining, the decrease is not rapid or large enough to trigger conservation vulnerability criteria. Given its high numbers, this shearwater is therefore classified by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> as being of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the north of its range, numbers are stable and the range is expanding, but human activities are affecting populations in the  <a href="/wiki/Macaronesia" title="Macaronesia">Macaronesian</a> islands in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>. These include birds stranded when dazzled by artificial lighting. As with other shearwaters and petrels, newly fledged Manx shearwaters are susceptible to grounding in built-up areas due to artificial light. The moon cycle and strong onshore winds largely influence grounding events in west Scotland, and visibility conditions to a lesser extent.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>  Around 1000–5000 chicks a year are legally taken for food in the <a href="/wiki/Faroes" class="mw-redirect" title="Faroes">Faroes</a>. Introduced mammals are a problem, although populations can recover when rats and cats are removed from islands. Rabbits may try to occupy burrows, but also dig new tunnels.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manx_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The large chicks of the Manx shearwater are very rich in oil from their fish diet and have been eaten since prehistoric times. They are easily extricated from their burrows, and the annual crop from the <a href="/wiki/Calf_of_Man" title="Calf of Man">Calf of Man</a> may have been as high as 10,000 birds per year in the 17th century. The young birds were also eaten in Ireland, Scotland, and the Scottish islands.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The eerie, nocturnal cries of nesting shearwaters and petrels has led to associations with the supernatural. The breeding colonies at Trollaval on <a href="/wiki/R%C3%B9m" title="Rùm">Rùm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%B8llanes" title="Trøllanes">Trøllanes</a> and Trøllhøvdi in the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a> are believed to have acquired their <a href="/wiki/Troll" title="Troll">troll</a> associations from the night-time clamour.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker2013_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker2013-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
