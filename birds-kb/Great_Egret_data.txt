<p>The <b>great egret </b>(<i>Ardea alba</i>), also known as the <b>common egret</b>, <b>large egret</b>, or (in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a>) <b>great white egret</b><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> or <b>great white heron</b><sup id="cite_ref-Bewick1809_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bewick1809-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bruun1970_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bruun1970-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ali1993_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ali1993-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> is a large, widely distributed <a href="/wiki/Egret" title="Egret">egret</a>, with four subspecies found in Asia, Africa, the Americas, and southern Europe. Distributed across most of the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical</a> and warmer <a href="/wiki/Temperate" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate">temperate</a> regions of the world, it builds tree nests in colonies close to water.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Systematics_and_taxonomy">Systematics and taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Systematics and taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like all egrets, it is a member of the heron <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ardeidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Ardeidae">Ardeidae</a>. Traditionally classified with the <a href="/wiki/Stork" title="Stork">storks</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Ciconiiformes" class="mw-redirect" title="Ciconiiformes">Ciconiiformes</a>, the Ardeidae are closer relatives of <a href="/wiki/Pelican" title="Pelican">pelicans</a> and belong in the <a href="/wiki/Pelecaniformes" title="Pelecaniformes">Pelecaniformes</a>, instead. The great egret—unlike the typical egrets—does not belong to the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Egretta" title="Egretta">Egretta</a></i>, but together with the great herons is today placed in <i><a href="/wiki/Ardea_(genus)" title="Ardea (genus)">Ardea</a></i>. In the past, however, it was sometimes placed in <i>Egretta</i> or separated in a <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monotypic">monotypic</a> genus <i>Casmerodius</i>.
</p><p>The Old World population is often referred to as the "great white egret". This species is sometimes confused with the great white heron of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, which is a white <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(biology)" title="Polymorphism (biology)">morph</a> of the closely related <a href="/wiki/Great_blue_heron" title="Great blue heron">great blue heron</a>.
</p><p>The scientific name comes from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>ardea</i>, "heron", and <i>alba</i>, "white".<sup id="cite_ref-job_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Four <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are found in various parts of the world, which differ but little.<sup id="cite_ref-IOC_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IOC-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Differences are bare-part coloration in the breeding season and size; the smallest <i>A. a. modesta</i> from Asia and <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a> some taxonomists consider a full species, the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_great_egret" title="Eastern great egret">eastern great egret</a> (<i>Ardea modesta</i>), but most scientists treat it as a subspecies.
</p>
<ul><li><i>A. a. alba</i> <small><a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, 1758</small> – <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies#Nominotypical_subspecies_and_subspecies_autonyms" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate</a>, found in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>.</li>
<li><i>A. a. egretta</i> <small><a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Gmelin" title="Johann Friedrich Gmelin">Gmelin, JF</a>, 1789</small> – found in <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a></li>
<li><i>A. a. melanorhynchos</i> <small>Wagler, 1827</small> – found in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Ardea_alba_modesta" class="mw-redirect" title="Ardea alba modesta">A. a. modesta</a></i> <small>Gray, JE, 1831</small> – eastern great egret,  found in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Oceania</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The great egret is a large heron with all-white <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>. Standing up to 1&#160;m (3.3&#160;ft) tall, this species can measure 80 to 104&#160;cm (31 to 41&#160;in) in length and have a <a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">wingspan</a> of 131 to 170&#160;cm (52 to 67&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-AAB_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAB-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-seaworld_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seaworld-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Body mass can range from 700 to 1,500&#160;g (1.5 to 3.3&#160;lb), with an average around 1,000&#160;g (2.2&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It is thus only slightly smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Great_blue_heron" title="Great blue heron">great blue</a> or <a href="/wiki/Grey_heron" title="Grey heron">grey heron</a> (<i>A. cinerea</i>). Apart from size, the great egret can be distinguished from other white egrets by its yellow <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> and black legs and feet, though the bill may become darker and the lower legs lighter in the breeding season. In breeding plumage, delicate ornamental feathers are borne on the back. Males and females are identical in appearance; juveniles look like nonbreeding adults. Differentiated from the <a href="/wiki/Intermediate_egret" title="Intermediate egret">intermediate egret</a> (<i>Mesophoyx intermedius</i>) by the <a href="/wiki/Gape" class="mw-redirect" title="Gape">gape</a>, which extends well beyond the back of the eye in case of the great egret, but ends just behind the eye in case of the intermediate egret.
</p><p>It has a slow flight, with its neck retracted. This is characteristic of herons and <a href="/wiki/Bittern" title="Bittern">bitterns</a>, and distinguishes them from <a href="/wiki/Stork" title="Stork">storks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crane_(bird)" title="Crane (bird)">cranes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ibis" title="Ibis">ibises</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Spoonbill" title="Spoonbill">spoonbills</a>, which extend their necks in flight. The great egret walks with its neck extended and wings held close. The great egret is not normally a vocal bird; it gives a low, hoarse croak when disturbed, and at breeding colonies, it often gives a loud croaking <i>cuk cuk cuk</i> and higher-pitched squawks.<sup id="cite_ref-audubon3_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-audubon3-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_conservation">Distribution and conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The great egret is generally a very successful species with a large and expanding range, occurring worldwide in temperate and tropical habitats. It is ubiquitous across the <a href="/wiki/Sun_Belt" title="Sun Belt">Sun Belt</a> of the United States and in the <a href="/wiki/Neotropics" class="mw-redirect" title="Neotropics">Neotropics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, large numbers of great egrets were killed around the end of the 19th century so that their plumes could be used to decorate hats. Numbers have since recovered as a result of conservation measures. Its range has expanded as far north as southern <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. However, in some parts of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">southern United States</a>, its numbers have declined due to habitat loss, particularly wetland degradation through drainage, grazing, clearing, burning, increased salinity, groundwater extraction and invasion by exotic plants. Nevertheless, the species adapts well to human habitation and can be readily seen near wetlands and bodies of water in urban and suburban areas.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The great egret is partially <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern hemisphere">northern hemisphere</a> birds moving south from areas with colder winters. It is one of the species to which the <a href="/wiki/Agreement_on_the_Conservation_of_African-Eurasian_Migratory_Waterbirds" title="Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a> (AEWA) applies.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In 1953, the great egret in flight was chosen as the symbol of the <a href="/wiki/National_Audubon_Society" title="National Audubon Society">National Audubon Society</a>, which was formed in part to prevent the killing of birds for their feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-audubon_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-audubon-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-audubon2_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-audubon2-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 22 May 2012,  a pair of great egrets was announced to be nesting in the UK for the first time at the <a href="/wiki/Shapwick_Heath" title="Shapwick Heath">Shapwick Heath</a> nature reserve in <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Aviss_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aviss-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The species is a rare visitor to the UK and Ben Aviss of the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> stated that the news could mean the UK's first great egret colony is established.<sup id="cite_ref-Aviss_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aviss-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Aviss2_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aviss2-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The following week, Kevin Anderson of <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a> confirmed a great egret chick had hatched, making it a new breeding bird record for the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-Hallett_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hallett-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In 2017, seven nests in Somerset fledged 17 young,<sup id="cite_ref-BB2017_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB2017-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and a second breeding site was announced at <a href="/wiki/Holkham_National_Nature_Reserve" title="Holkham National Nature Reserve">Holkham National Nature Reserve</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a> where a pair fledged three young.<sup id="cite_ref-RBA_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RBA-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2018, a pair of great egrets nested in Finland for the first time, raising four young in a grey heron colony in Porvoo.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The species breeds in <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonies</a> in trees close to large lakes with <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed beds</a> or other extensive <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a>, preferably at height of 10–40 feet (3.0–12.2&#160;m).<sup id="cite_ref-audubon3_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-audubon3-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It begins to breed at 2–3 years of age by forming monogamous pairs each season. If the pairing carries over to the next season is not known. The male selects the nest area, starts a nest, and then attracts a female. The nest, made of sticks and lined with plant material, could be up to 3 feet across. Up to six bluish green eggs are laid at one time. Both sexes incubate the eggs and the incubation period is 23–26 days. The young are fed by regurgitation by both parents and they are able to fly within 6–7 weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Egret_strikes_for_a_Fish_-_crop.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Great_Egret_strikes_for_a_Fish_-_crop.JPG/220px-Great_Egret_strikes_for_a_Fish_-_crop.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Great_Egret_strikes_for_a_Fish_-_crop.JPG/330px-Great_Egret_strikes_for_a_Fish_-_crop.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Great_Egret_strikes_for_a_Fish_-_crop.JPG/440px-Great_Egret_strikes_for_a_Fish_-_crop.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="1500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Egret_strikes_for_a_Fish_-_crop.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Spearing a fish</div></div></div>
<p>The great egret feeds in shallow water or drier habitats, feeding mainly on <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, and occasionally small <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, spearing them with its long, sharp bill most of the time by standing still and allowing the prey to come within its striking distance of its bill, which it uses as a spear. It often waits motionless for prey, or slowly stalks its victim.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parasites">Parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A long-running field study (1962–2013) suggested that the central European great egrets host 17 different <a href="/wiki/Helminth" class="mw-redirect" title="Helminth">helminth</a> species. Juvenile great egrets were shown to host fewer species, but the intensity of infection was higher in the juveniles than in the adults. Of the digeneans found in central European great egrets, numerous species likely infected their definitive hosts outside of central Europe itself.<sup id="cite_ref-Sitko_&amp;_Heneberg_2015_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sitko_&amp;_Heneberg_2015-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The great egret is depicted on the <a href="/wiki/Obverse_and_reverse" title="Obverse and reverse">reverse</a> side of a 5-<a href="/wiki/Brazilian_real" title="Brazilian real">Brazilian reais</a> banknote.
</p><p>The great egret is the symbol of the <a href="/wiki/National_Audubon_Society" title="National Audubon Society">National Audubon Society</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nationalgeographic_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nationalgeographic-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An airbrushed photograph of a Great Egret in breeding plumage by Werner Krutein is featured in the cover art of the 1992 Faith No More album <i>Angel Dust</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>, a commemorative coin has the image of a great egret.
</p><p>The great egret also features on the New Zealand $2 coin and on the Hungarian 5-forint coin.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
