<p>The <b>White Wagtail</b> (<i>Motacilla alba</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Wagtail" title="Wagtail">wagtail</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> Motacillidae, which also includes the <a href="/wiki/Pipit" title="Pipit">pipits</a> and <a href="/wiki/Longclaw" title="Longclaw">longclaws</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> breeds in much of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and parts of north <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It is resident in the mildest parts of its range, but otherwise <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrates</a> to <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It has a toehold in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> as a scarce breeder. In some areas, notably Britain and Ireland, the sub-species <b>Pied Wagtail</b> (<i>M. a. yarrellii</i>) predominates.</p>
<p>This is an insectivorous <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of open country, often near habitation and water. It prefers bare areas for feeding, where it can see and pursue its prey. In urban areas it has adapted to foraging on paved areas such as car parks. It nests in crevices in stone walls and similar natural and man-made structures.</p>
<p>The White Wagtail is the national bird of <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=White_Wagtail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/w/index.php?title=File:MotacillaAlbaDistribution.svg&amp;page=1" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/MotacillaAlbaDistribution.svg/400px-MotacillaAlbaDistribution.svg.png" width="400" height="279" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/MotacillaAlbaDistribution.svg/600px-MotacillaAlbaDistribution.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/MotacillaAlbaDistribution.svg/800px-MotacillaAlbaDistribution.svg.png 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MotacillaAlbaDistribution.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Breeding ranges of the major races</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The White Wagtail was one of the many species originally described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in his 18th century work, <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>, and it still bears its original name of <i>Motacilla alba</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> genus name originally meant "little mover", but certain <a href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval" class="mw-redirect">medieval</a> writers thought it meant "wag-tail", giving rise to a new Latin word <i>cilla</i> for "tail".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The specific epithet <i>alba</i> is Latin for "white".</p>
<p>Within the wagtail genus <i>Motacilla</i>, the White Wagtail's closest relatives appear to be other black-and-white wagtails such as the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Wagtail" title="Japanese Wagtail">Japanese Wagtail</a>, <i>Motacilla grandis</i>, and the <a href="/wiki/White-browed_Wagtail" title="White-browed Wagtail">White-browed Wagtail</a>, <i>Motacilla madaraspatensis</i>, (and possibly the <a href="/wiki/Mekong_Wagtail" title="Mekong Wagtail">Mekong Wagtail</a>, <i>Motacilla samveasnae</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic" title="Phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect">phylogenetic</a> position of which is mysterious) with which it appears to form a <a href="/wiki/Superspecies" title="Superspecies">superspecies</a>. However <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA" class="mw-redirect">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome <i>b</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/NADH_dehydrogenase" title="NADH dehydrogenase">NADH dehydrogenase</a> <a href="/wiki/Protein_subunit" title="Protein subunit">subunit</a> 2 <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">sequence</a> data suggests that the White Wagtail is itself <a href="/wiki/Polyphyletic" title="Polyphyletic" class="mw-redirect">polyphyletic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Paraphyletic" title="Paraphyletic" class="mw-redirect">paraphyletic</a> (i.e. the species is not itself a single coherent grouping).<sup id="cite_ref-Voelker_2002_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voelker_2002-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Other phylogenetic studies using mtDNA however suggest that there is considerable gene flow within the races and the resulting closeness makes <i>Motacilla alba</i> a single species.<sup id="cite_ref-Pavlova_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pavlova-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Some studies have suggested the existence of only two groups&#160;: the <i>alboides</i> group, with <i>M. a. alboides</i>, <i>M. a. leucopsis</i> and <i>M. a. personata</i>; and the <i>alba</i> group, with <i>M. a. alba</i>, <i>M. a. yarrellii</i>, <i>M. a. baicalensis</i>, <i>M. a. ocularis</i>, <i>M. a. lugens</i>, and <i>M. a. subpersonata</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=White_Wagtail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>This is a slender bird, 16.5–19&#160;cm (6½–7½&#160;in) in length (East Asian subspecies are longer, to 21&#160;cm (8¼&#160;in), with the characteristic long, constantly wagging tail of its genus. The <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" title="Nominate subspecies" class="mw-redirect">nominate subspecies</a> <i>Motacilla alba alba</i> is basically grey above and white below, with a white face, black cap and black throat.</p>
<p>There are a number of other subspecies, some of which may have arisen because of partial geographical isolation, such as the resident British form, the Pied Wagtail <i>M. a. yarrellii</i>, which now also breeds in adjacent areas of the neighbouring European mainland. Pied Wagtail, named for naturalist <a href="/wiki/William_Yarrell" title="William Yarrell">William Yarrell</a>, exchanges the grey colour of the nominate form with black (or very dark grey in females), but is otherwise identical in its behaviour. Other subspecies, the validity of some of which is questionable, differ in the colour of the wings, back, and head, or other features. Some races show <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a> during the breeding season. As many as six subspecies may be present in the wintering ground in India or Southeast Asia and here they can be difficult to distinguish.<sup id="cite_ref-pipwag_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pipwag-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Phylogenetic studies using mtDNA suggest that some morphological features have evolved more than once including the back and chin colour. Breeding <i>M. a. yarrellii</i> look much like the nominate race except for the black back, and <i>M. a. alboides</i> of the Himalayas differs from the Central Asian <i>M. a. personata</i> only by its black back. <i>M. a. personata</i> has been recorded breeding in the Siddar Valley of Kashmir of the Western Himalayas.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> It has also been noted that both back and chin change colour during the <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">pre-basic moult</a>; all black-throated subspecies develop white chins and throats in winter and some black-backed birds are grey-backed in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-Pavlova_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pavlova-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pipwag_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pipwag-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The call of the White Wagtail is a sharp <i>chisick</i>, slightly softer than the version given by Pied Wagtail. The song is a pleasant twittering, more regular in White than Pied, but with little territorial significance, since the male uses a series of contact calls to attract the female.<sup id="cite_ref-Simms_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simms-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=White_Wagtail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:White_wagtail_distribution.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/White_wagtail_distribution.PNG/300px-White_wagtail_distribution.PNG" width="300" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/White_wagtail_distribution.PNG/450px-White_wagtail_distribution.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/White_wagtail_distribution.PNG/600px-White_wagtail_distribution.PNG 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:White_wagtail_distribution.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Worldwide distribution of the White Wagtail. Yellow denotes summer range, green year round range, blue winter range.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This species breeds throughout Eurasia up to latitudes 75°N, only being absent in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> from areas where the July isotherm is less than 4 °C. It also breeds in the mountains of <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. It occupies a wide range of habitats, but is absent from deserts.<sup id="cite_ref-Simms_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simms-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>White Wagtail is resident in the milder parts of its range such as western Europe and the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> in much of the rest of its range. Northern European breeders winter around the Mediterranean and in tropical and subtropical <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> and Asiatic birds move to the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/SouthEast_Asia" title="SouthEast Asia" class="mw-redirect">SouthEast Asia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Arlott_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arlott-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Birds from the North American population also winter in tropical Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-Sibley_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sibley-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=White_Wagtail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h3>
<p>This species has a large range, with an estimated extent of more than 10&#160;million&#160;km² (3.8&#160;sq&#160;mi). The population size is unknown, but it is believed to be large, as the species is described as "common" in at least parts of its range. Population trends have not been quantified, but the species is not believed to approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the IUCN Red List (i.e. declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations). For these reasons, the species is evaluated as Least Concern.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The population in Europe appears to be stable.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The species has adapted well to human changes to the environment, and has in fact exploited human changes such as man-made structures which are used for nesting sites and increased open areas which are used for foraging.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_14-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> In a number of cities, notably <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, large flocks gather in winter to roost.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=White_Wagtail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<p>The most conspicuous habit of this species is a near-constant tail wagging, a trait that has given the species, and indeed the genus, its common name. In spite of the ubiquity of this behaviour, the reasons for it are poorly understood. It has been suggested that it may flush prey, or signal submissiveness to other wagtails. A recent study has suggested instead that it is a signal of vigilance to potential predators.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=White_Wagtail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diet and feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Diet_and_feeding">Diet and feeding</span></h3>
<p>The exact composition of the diet of White Wagtails varies by location, but terrestrial and aquatic <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and other small invertebrates form the major part of the diet. These range from <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dragonfly" title="Dragonfly">dragonflies</a>, small snails, spiders, worms, crustaceans, to maggots found in carcasses and, most importantly, <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a> in the order Diptera.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_14-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Small fish fry have also been recorded in the diet. The White Wagtail is somewhat unusual in the parts of it range where it is non-migratory as it is an <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" title="Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect">insectivorous</a> bird that continues to feed on insects during the winter (most other insectivorous birds in temperate climates migrate or switch to more vegetable matter).<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=White_Wagtail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Motacilla_alba_-Rutland_Water,_Rutland,_England-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Motacilla_alba_-Rutland_Water%2C_Rutland%2C_England-8.jpg/220px-Motacilla_alba_-Rutland_Water%2C_Rutland%2C_England-8.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Motacilla_alba_-Rutland_Water%2C_Rutland%2C_England-8.jpg/330px-Motacilla_alba_-Rutland_Water%2C_Rutland%2C_England-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Motacilla_alba_-Rutland_Water%2C_Rutland%2C_England-8.jpg/440px-Motacilla_alba_-Rutland_Water%2C_Rutland%2C_England-8.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Motacilla_alba_-Rutland_Water,_Rutland,_England-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>White Wagtails are <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a> and defend breeding <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territories</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_14-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The breeding season for most is from April to August, with the season starting later further north. Both sexes are responsible for building the <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a>, with the male responsible for initiating the nest building and the female for finishing the process. For second broods in the subspecies <i>personata</i> the female alone builds the nest as the male is still provisioning the young.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> which is a rough cup assembled from twigs, grass, leaves and other plant matter. It is lined with soft materials, including animal hair. The nest is set into a crevice or hole; traditionally in a bank next to a river or ditch, but the species has also adapted to nesting in walls, bridges and buildings. One nest was found in the skull of a walrus. They species will nest in association with other animals, particularly where available the dams of <a href="/wiki/Beaver" title="Beaver">beavers</a> and also inside the nests of <a href="/wiki/Golden_Eagle" title="Golden Eagle">Golden Eagles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Around 3-8 eggs are laid, with the usual number being 4-6. Its <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> are cream-coloured, often with a faint bluish-green or turquoise tint, and heavily spotted with reddish brown; they measure, on average, 21 x 15&#160;mm (0.83 x 0.59&#160;inches).<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Both parents <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" title="Avian incubation" class="mw-redirect">incubate</a> the eggs, although the female generally does so for longer and incubates at night. The eggs begin to hatch after 12 days (sometimes as late as 16 days). Both parents feed the chicks until they <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> at around 14 days, and the chicks are fed for another week after fledging.</p>
<p>Though it is known to be a host species for the <a href="/wiki/Common_Cuckoo" title="Common Cuckoo">Common Cuckoo</a>, the White Wagtail typically deserts its nest if it has been parasitised. Scientists theorise that this occurs because the wagtail is too small to push the intruding egg out of the nest, and too short-billed to destroy the egg by puncturing it.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
