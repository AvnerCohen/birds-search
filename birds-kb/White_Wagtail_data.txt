<p>The <b>white wagtail</b> (<i>Motacilla alba</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Motacillidae" title="Motacillidae">Motacillidae</a>, which also includes <a href="/wiki/Pipit" title="Pipit">pipits</a> and <a href="/wiki/Longclaw" title="Longclaw">longclaws</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> breeds in much of Europe and the Asian <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>. It has a toehold in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> as a scarce breeder. It is resident in the mildest parts of its range, but otherwise <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrates</a> to Africa. In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, the darker <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>, the <b>pied wagtail</b> or <b>water wagtail</b><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> (<i>M. a. yarrellii</i>) predominates. In total, there are between 9 and 11 subspecies.
</p><p>The white wagtail is an insectivorous <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of open country, often near habitation and water.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It prefers bare areas for feeding, where it can see and pursue its prey. In urban areas it has adapted to foraging on paved areas such as car parks. It nests in crevices in stone walls and similar natural and man-made structures.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is the national bird of <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a> and has featured on the stamps of several countries.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Though it is 'of least concern', there are several threats against it, like being kept as pets and being used as food.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White_wagtail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MotacillaAlbaDistribution.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/MotacillaAlbaDistribution.svg/220px-MotacillaAlbaDistribution.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/MotacillaAlbaDistribution.svg/330px-MotacillaAlbaDistribution.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/MotacillaAlbaDistribution.svg/440px-MotacillaAlbaDistribution.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="14343" data-file-height="10000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MotacillaAlbaDistribution.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Breeding ranges of the major races</div></div></div>
<p>The white wagtail was one of the many species originally described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his landmark 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a>, and it still bears its original name <i>Motacilla alba</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:2_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> genus name originally meant "little mover", but certain <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> writers thought it meant "wag-tail", giving rise to a new Latin word <i>cilla</i> for "tail".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The specific epithet <i>alba</i> is Latin for "white".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Within the wagtail genus <i>Motacilla</i>, the white wagtail's closest genetic relatives appear to be other black-and-white wagtails such as the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_wagtail" title="Japanese wagtail">Japanese wagtail</a>, <i>Motacilla grandis</i>, and the <a href="/wiki/White-browed_wagtail" title="White-browed wagtail">white-browed wagtail</a>, <i>Motacilla madaraspatensis</i> (and possibly the <a href="/wiki/Mekong_wagtail" title="Mekong wagtail">Mekong wagtail</a>, <i>Motacilla samveasnae</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Phylogenetic">phylogenetic</a> position of which is mysterious), with which it appears to form a <a href="/wiki/Superspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Superspecies">superspecies</a>. However, <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" class="mw-redirect" title="MtDNA">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome <i>b</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/NADH_dehydrogenase" title="NADH dehydrogenase">NADH dehydrogenase</a> <a href="/wiki/Protein_subunit" title="Protein subunit">subunit</a> 2 <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" class="mw-redirect" title="DNA sequence">sequence</a> data suggests that the white wagtail is itself <a href="/wiki/Polyphyletic" class="mw-redirect" title="Polyphyletic">polyphyletic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Paraphyletic" class="mw-redirect" title="Paraphyletic">paraphyletic</a> (i.e. the species is not itself a single coherent grouping).<sup id="cite_ref-Voelker_2002_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voelker_2002-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Other phylogenetic studies using mtDNA still suggest that there is considerable gene flow within the races and the resulting closeness makes <i>Motacilla alba</i> a single species.<sup id="cite_ref-Pavlova_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pavlova-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> A study has suggested the existence of only two groups: the <i>alboides</i> group, with <i>M. a. alboides</i>, <i>M. a. leucopsis</i> and <i>M. a. personata</i>; and the <i>alba</i> group, with <i>M. a. alba</i>, <i>M. a. yarrellii</i>, <i>M. a. baicalensis</i>, <i>M. a. ocularis</i>, <i>M. a. lugens</i>, and <i>M. a. subpersonata</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White_wagtail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Motacilla_alba_-Kazakhstan_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Motacilla_alba_-Kazakhstan_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg/220px-Motacilla_alba_-Kazakhstan_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Motacilla_alba_-Kazakhstan_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg/330px-Motacilla_alba_-Kazakhstan_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Motacilla_alba_-Kazakhstan_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg/440px-Motacilla_alba_-Kazakhstan_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1510" data-file-height="1059" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Motacilla_alba_-Kazakhstan_-adult_and_juvenile-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An adult with a juvenile in Kazakhstan</div></div></div>
<p>The white wagtail is a slender bird, 16.5 to 19&#160;cm (6.5 to 7.5&#160;in) in length (East Asian subspecies are longer, measuring up to 21&#160;cm (8.3&#160;in)), with the characteristic long, constantly wagging tail of its genus. Its average weight is 25&#160;g (0.88&#160;oz) and the maximum lifespan in the wild is about 12 years.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are a number of other subspecies, some of which may have arisen because of partial geographical isolation, such as the resident British and Irish form, the pied wagtail <i>M. a. yarrellii</i>, which now also breeds in adjacent areas of the neighbouring European mainland. The pied wagtail, named for naturalist <a href="/wiki/William_Yarrell" title="William Yarrell">William Yarrell</a>, exchanges the grey colour of the nominate form with black (or very dark grey in females), but is otherwise identical in its behaviour. Other subspecies, the validity of some of which is questionable, differ in the colour of the wings, back, and head, or other features. Some races show <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a> during the breeding season. As many as six subspecies may be present in the wintering ground in India or Southeast Asia and here they can be difficult to distinguish.<sup id="cite_ref-pipwag_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pipwag-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Phylogenetic studies using mtDNA suggest that some morphological features have evolved more than once, including the back and chin colour. Breeding <i>M. a. yarrellii</i> look much like the nominate race except for the black back, and <i>M. a. alboides</i> of the Himalayas differs from the Central Asian <i>M. a. personata</i> only by its black back. <i>M. a. personata</i> has been recorded breeding in the Siddar Valley of Kashmir of the Western Himalayas.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> It has also been noted that both back and chin change colour during the <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">pre-basic moult</a>; all black-throated subspecies develop white chins and throats in winter and some black-backed birds are grey-backed in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-Pavlova_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pavlova-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pipwag_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pipwag-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The call of the white wagtail is a sharp <i>chisick</i>, slightly softer than the version given by the pied wagtail. The song is more regular in white than pied, but with little territorial significance, since the male uses a series of contact calls to attract the female.<sup id="cite_ref-Simms_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simms-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White_wagtail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:White_wagtail_distribution.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/White_wagtail_distribution.PNG/220px-White_wagtail_distribution.PNG" decoding="async" width="220" height="112" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/White_wagtail_distribution.PNG/330px-White_wagtail_distribution.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/White_wagtail_distribution.PNG/440px-White_wagtail_distribution.PNG 2x" data-file-width="2759" data-file-height="1404" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:White_wagtail_distribution.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Worldwide distribution of the white wagtail. Yellow denotes summer range, green year round range, blue winter range.</div></div></div>
<p>This species breeds throughout Eurasia up to latitudes 75°N, only being absent in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> from areas where the July isotherm is less than 4&#160;°C. It also breeds in the mountains of <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. It occupies a wide range of habitats, but is absent from deserts.<sup id="cite_ref-Simms_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simms-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> White wagtails are residents in the milder parts of its range such as western Europe and the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> in much of the rest of its range. Northern European breeders winter around the Mediterranean and in tropical and subtropical Africa,<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> and Asiatic birds move to the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Arlott_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arlott-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Birds from the North American population also winter in tropical Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-Sibley_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sibley-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White_wagtail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WhiteWagtailGould.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/WhiteWagtailGould.jpg/220px-WhiteWagtailGould.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="250" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/WhiteWagtailGould.jpg/330px-WhiteWagtailGould.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/WhiteWagtailGould.jpg/440px-WhiteWagtailGould.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4367" data-file-height="4972" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WhiteWagtailGould.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Painting by <a href="/wiki/John_Gould" title="John Gould">John Gould</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The most conspicuous habit of this species is a near-constant tail wagging, a trait that has given the species, and indeed the genus, its common name. In spite of the ubiquity of this behaviour, the reasons for it are poorly understood. It has been suggested that it may flush prey, or signal submissiveness to other wagtails. A study in 2004 has suggested instead that it is a signal of vigilance to potential predators.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet_and_feeding">Diet and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White_wagtail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Diet and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The exact composition of the diet of white wagtails varies by location, but terrestrial and aquatic <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and other small invertebrates form the major part of the diet. These range from <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dragonfly" title="Dragonfly">dragonflies</a>, small snails, spiders, worms, crustaceans, to maggots found in carcasses and, most importantly, <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_19-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Small fish fry have also been recorded in the diet. The white wagtail is somewhat unusual in the parts of its range where it is non-migratory as it is an <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a> bird that continues to feed on insects during the winter (most other insectivorous birds in temperate climates migrate or switch to more vegetable matter).<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White_wagtail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Motacilla_alba_MWNH_1575.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Motacilla_alba_MWNH_1575.JPG/220px-Motacilla_alba_MWNH_1575.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Motacilla_alba_MWNH_1575.JPG/330px-Motacilla_alba_MWNH_1575.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Motacilla_alba_MWNH_1575.JPG/440px-Motacilla_alba_MWNH_1575.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3472" data-file-height="2220" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Motacilla_alba_MWNH_1575.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden,_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Museum Wiesbaden, Germany">Museum Wiesbaden, Germany</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Motacilla_alba_juvenile_(Kirkenes,_2012).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Motacilla_alba_juvenile_%28Kirkenes%2C_2012%29.jpg/220px-Motacilla_alba_juvenile_%28Kirkenes%2C_2012%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Motacilla_alba_juvenile_%28Kirkenes%2C_2012%29.jpg/330px-Motacilla_alba_juvenile_%28Kirkenes%2C_2012%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Motacilla_alba_juvenile_%28Kirkenes%2C_2012%29.jpg/440px-Motacilla_alba_juvenile_%28Kirkenes%2C_2012%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4059" data-file-height="2706" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Motacilla_alba_juvenile_(Kirkenes,_2012).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Juvenile <i>M. a. alba</i> in northern Norway, showing the grey face and chest</div></div></div>
<p>White wagtails are <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a> and defend breeding <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territories</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_19-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The breeding season for most is from April to August, with the season starting later further north. Both sexes are responsible for building the <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a>, with the male responsible for initiating the nest building and the female for finishing the process. For second broods in the subspecies <i>personata</i> the female alone builds the nest, which is a rough cup assembled from twigs, grass, leaves and other plant matter, as the male is still provisioning the young.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> It is lined with soft materials, including animal hair. The nest is set into a crevice or hole—traditionally in a bank next to a river or ditch&#8212;but the species has also adapted to nesting in walls, bridges and buildings. One nest was found in the skull of a walrus. White wagtails will nest in association with other animals: particularly, where available, the dams of <a href="/wiki/Beaver" title="Beaver">beavers</a> and also inside the nests of <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">golden eagles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Around three to eight eggs are laid, with the usual number being four to six. The <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> are cream-coloured, often with a faint bluish-green or turquoise tint, and heavily spotted with reddish brown; they measure, on average, 21&#160;mm ×&#160;15&#160;mm (0.83&#160;in ×&#160;0.59&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Both parents <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" class="mw-redirect" title="Avian incubation">incubate</a> the eggs, although the female generally does so for longer and incubates at night. The eggs begin to hatch after 12 days (sometimes as late as 16 days). Both parents feed the chicks until they <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> after between 12 and 15 days, and the chicks are fed for another week after fledging.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Though it is known to be a host species for the <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common cuckoo</a>, the white wagtail typically deserts its nest if it has been <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">parasitised</a>. Moksnes et al. theorised that this occurs because the wagtail is too small to push the intruding egg out of the nest, and too short-billed to destroy the egg by puncturing it.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White_wagtail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species has a large range, with an estimated extent of more than 10&#160;million&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (3.8&#160;million sq&#160;mi). The population size is between 130 and 230 million.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
Population trends have not been quantified, but the species is not believed to approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> (i.e. declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations). For these reasons, the species is evaluated to be of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The population in Europe appears to be stable.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The species has adapted well to human changes to the environment and has exploited human changes such as man-made structures that are used for nesting sites and increased open areas that are used for foraging.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_19-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In a number of cities, notably <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, large flocks gather in winter to roost. They are therefore rated as of least concern. However, they are caught for sport and often then placed into collections. They are also kept as pets and eaten as food. Climate change may be affecting the time of their migration.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White_wagtail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>They have featured on stamps from <a href="/wiki/Bahrain" title="Bahrain">Bahrain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The white wagtail is the national bird of <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a>, and has been often mentioned in Latvian <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk songs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
