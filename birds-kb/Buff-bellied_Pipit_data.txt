<p>The <b>Buff-bellied Pipit</b> (<i>Anthus rubescens</i>), or <b>American Pipit</b> as it is known in North America, is a small <a href="/wiki/Songbird" title="Songbird">songbird</a> found on both sides of the northern <a href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific" class="mw-redirect">Pacific</a>. It was first described by <a href="/wiki/Marmaduke_Tunstall" title="Marmaduke Tunstall">Marmaduke Tunstall</a> in his 1771 <i>Ornithologia Britannica</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> It was formerly classified as a form of the <a href="/wiki/Water_Pipit" title="Water Pipit">Water Pipit</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Buff-bellied_Pipit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>Like most other <a href="/wiki/Pipit" title="Pipit">pipits</a>, the Buff-bellied Pipit is an undistinguished-looking <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> which usually can be seen to run around on the ground. The American Pipit has lightly streaked grey-brown upperparts and is diffusely streaked below on the buff breast and flanks. The belly is whitish, the bill and legs are dark. The Japanese Pipit is darker above and has bolder black streaking on its whiter underparts; its legs have a reddish hue.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The call is a squeaky <i>sip</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-svenssonetal1999_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-svenssonetal1999-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Buff-bellied_Pipit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>It has two distinctive subspecies, but <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphological</a> and <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">DNA sequence</a> differences between them are rather pronounced and they might be considered distinct species pending further research:<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>A. r. rubescens</i> <small>(Tunstall, 1771)</small>, <b>American Pipit</b> – breeds in northern <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, extending further south in mountainous areas</li>
<li><i>A. r. japonicus</i>, <b>Japanese Pipit</b> or <b>Siberian Pipit</b> – breeds in most of eastern <a href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate" class="mw-redirect">temperate</a> <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>)</li>
</ul>
<p>This species is closely related to <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Rock_Pipit" title="Eurasian Rock Pipit">Eurasian Rock Pipit</a> (<i>A. petrosus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Water_Pipit" title="Water Pipit">Water Pipit</a> (<i>A. spinoletta</i>), all three forms having previously been considered conspecific. They can differentiated by their vocalizations and some visual cues, but Rock and Buff-bellied Pipit are not found <a href="/wiki/Sympatric" title="Sympatric" class="mw-redirect">sympatrically</a> except as vagrant individuals, and the ranges of Buff-bellied and Water Pipits overlap only in a small area in <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Buff-bellied_Pipit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<p>Both <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of the Buff-bellied Pipit are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>. The Buff-bellied Pipit winters on the <a href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific" class="mw-redirect">Pacific</a> coast of North America, and on the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_(ocean)" title="Atlantic (ocean)" class="mw-redirect">Atlantic</a> coast from the southern <a href="/wiki/USA" title="USA" class="mw-redirect">USA</a> to <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> At least regarding the Buff-bellied Pipit, its wintering range seems to have expanded northwards in the 20th century and the birds seem to spend less time in winter quarters: in northern <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> for example, the species was recorded as "not common" during migration in May and September/October in the 1900s (decade), but today it is considered a "widespread migrant" in that region, found between March and May and from late September to November, with many birds actually wintering this far north. Asian birds winter mainly from <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> east to and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, with occasional birds found as far north as <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a> and some in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> apparently being all-year residents or migrating but a little. The American and Asian subspecies are rare vagrants to <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Like its relatives, this species is <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" title="Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect">insectivorous</a>. The breeding habitat of Buff-bellied Pipit is <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>, but outside the breeding season it is found in open lightly vegetated areas, similar to those favoured by the <a href="/wiki/Water_Pipit" title="Water Pipit">Water Pipit</a> (<i>A. spinoletta</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-svenssonetal1999_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-svenssonetal1999-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Buff-bellied_Pipit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reproduction: From pairing to fledging">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction:_From_pairing_to_fledging">Reproduction: From pairing to fledging</span></h2>
<p>The first thing Buff-bellied pipits do when they arrive on the breeding site, during snowmelt, is pairing. Indeed, males will start to fight one on one to win over the female and pair with it during the entire breeding season.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> They also fight for the snow-free sites that would be better for nesting. The moment is also very important because the melting snow implies an increase in arthropods abundance, which constitutes the main food source for these birds. After the fight and the pairing, nesting is the next step. Nests are most often found on the ground in dry or wet meadows, always with a helpful protection, but they are never placed in shrubs or trees.<sup id="cite_ref-Norment.2C_C.J_2004_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Norment.2C_C.J_2004-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pickwell.2C_G_1947_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickwell.2C_G_1947-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The composition of the ideal nest depends on whatever is around the nesting area, but it is usually made of sedge, remains or new fine grass and sometimes some horse hairs.<sup id="cite_ref-Pickwell.2C_G_1947_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickwell.2C_G_1947-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The final issue American pipits have to deal with is nest success. The nest is indeed the target for numerous predators such as ants or hawks. If this step is successful, an egg can be produced.<sup id="cite_ref-Pickwell.2C_G_1947_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickwell.2C_G_1947-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The female will not lay an egg if the conditions are not optimal (temperature, nesting site,…) and if the first attempt fails, her time to lay an egg is reduced. In general, American pipits continuously lay eggs over a period of 4 to 5 days after snow-melt (in April–May) to mid-July. After this period, the male testes decrease in size and the female refuses any copulation.<sup id="cite_ref-Hendricks.2C_P_2003_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hendricks.2C_P_2003-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> In general, the clutch size is 5 eggs but it can vary according to snowfalls, the parents’ reproductive ability and predation.<sup id="cite_ref-Norment.2C_C.J_2004_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Norment.2C_C.J_2004-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Eggs are incubated for 13–14 days <sup id="cite_ref-Hendricks.2C_P_2003_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hendricks.2C_P_2003-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bna.birds.cornell.edu_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bna.birds.cornell.edu-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> During this time, the female does not leave the nest but is still very reactive to any movement around its habitat. It communicates by singing to the male that brings her food and defends its territory. Four or five days after hatching, the young is skinny, blue-gray in color and only has its secondary feathers. For a week, the female will brood its clutch but both parents will feed them. After these 7 days, the birds are ready for fledging but they will still be fed by their parents 14 days after their departure. Finally, immature birds will form little flocks with other immature birds and wander off.<sup id="cite_ref-Pickwell.2C_G_1947_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickwell.2C_G_1947-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bna.birds.cornell.edu_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bna.birds.cornell.edu-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Buff-bellied_Pipit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h2>
<p>It is a widespread and common species and not considered threatened by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN" class="mw-redirect">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
