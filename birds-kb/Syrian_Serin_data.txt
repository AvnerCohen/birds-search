<p>The <b>Syrian Serin</b> is a brightly coloured small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird in the finch family <a href="/wiki/Fringillidae" title="Fringillidae" class="mw-redirect">Fringillidae</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrian_Serin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The Syrian Serin is prettily coloured with bright yellow and pale grey feathers. The eyes are large and are surrounded by a bright yellow ring. The beak is grey and the legs are pale pinkish-grey. It has a long trilling call, and may also chirp and twitter.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrian_Serin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>The Syrian serin breeds in <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a> at altitudes of between 900 – 1,900 metres. The population in Jordan makes local movements in winter, but the birds of Lebanon, Israel and Syria migrate to wintering grounds in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>. They inhabit rocky areas with oak and conifer shrubs or trees and frequent grasslands and fields feeding mainly on the seeds of annuals and grasses. In Southwestern Jordan, its main diet during winter is the seeds of <i>Artemisia</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrian_Serin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Phylogeny">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Phylogeny">Phylogeny</span></h2>
<p>This species is phylogenetically included within the group of <a href="/wiki/Serinus_alario" title="Serinus alario" class="mw-redirect">Serinus alario</a> now thriving around the southern Africa tip, together with <a href="/wiki/Serinus_canicollis" title="Serinus canicollis" class="mw-redirect">Serinus canicollis</a> (African distribution) and <a href="/wiki/Serinus_pusillus" title="Serinus pusillus" class="mw-redirect">Serinus pusillus</a> (Asian distribution) <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://mbe.oxfordjournals.org/content/16/1/2.full.pdf">Arnaiz-Villena et al, 1999</a> and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.ardeola.org/files/1260.pdf">Arnaiz-Villena et al, 2006</a></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrian_Serin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h2>
<p>Males court females with a song display, and each pair builds a nest in a tree once the snow has begun to melt in April or May. Four pale blue, glossy eggs are laid in April and May and the female incubates these for 12 – 14 days. The young fledge after just 14 – 16 days and the parents then move up to around 1,750 metres in July and August to produce a second clutch. When conditions allow, the pair can produce three broods. In southwest Jordan, most pairs apparently breed only once per year as suitable breeding habitat does not exist at higher elevations.</p>
