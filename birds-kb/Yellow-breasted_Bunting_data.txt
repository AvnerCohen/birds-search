<p>The <b>yellow-breasted bunting</b> (<i>Emberiza aureola</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird in the <a href="/wiki/Bunting_(bird)" title="Bunting (bird)">bunting</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> Emberizidae that is found across the Boreal and East <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>. The genus name <i>Emberiza</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old German</a> <i>Embritz</i>, a bunting. The specific <i>aureola</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "golden".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The bird's call is a distinctive <i>zick</i>, and the song is a clear <i>tru-tru, tri-tri</i>.
</p><p>Until 2004, the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_the_Conservation_of_Nature" class="mw-redirect" title="International Union for the Conservation of Nature">International Union for the Conservation of Nature</a> considered the yellow-breasted bunting to be a species of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>. Since 2004, it has been gradually upgraded to a status of <a href="/wiki/Critically_Endangered" class="mw-redirect" title="Critically Endangered">Critically Endangered</a> due to rapid drops in population sizes.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is subject to heavy hunting pressure in China, through which most specimens pass during migration.<sup id="cite_ref-IBT_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IBT-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-breasted_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The yellow-breasted bunting is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a>, ranging from 14 to 16&#160;cm (5.5 to 6.3&#160;in) in length, and weighing 17 to 26&#160;g (0.6 to 0.9&#160;oz). For a <a href="/wiki/Bunting_(bird)" title="Bunting (bird)">bunting</a>, it is large and rather stocky.<sup id="cite_ref-Brazil_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brazil-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The breeding male has bright yellow <a href="/wiki/Underparts" class="mw-redirect" title="Underparts">underparts</a> with black flank streaks, brown <a href="/wiki/Upperparts" class="mw-redirect" title="Upperparts">upperparts</a>, black face and throat <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bar_(marking)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bar (marking) (page does not exist)">bar</a>, and a pink lower mandible. The female has a heavily streaked grey-brown back, and less intensely yellow underparts.  She has a whitish face with dark crown, eye and cheek stripes. The juvenile is similar, but the background colour of the underparts and face is buff.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-breasted_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Schoeniclus aureolus aureolus</i> breeds in boreal forests of Finland to Bering Sea migrating to Indochina <i>Schoeniclus aureolus ornatus</i> breeds from the Amur River to Manchuria, N Korea, Kamchatka and Kuril Islands. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in south-east Asia, India, and southern China. It is a rare but regular wanderer to western Europe. There are also ~4 records from the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Islands" title="Aleutian Islands">Aleutian Islands</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and a 2017 record from <a href="/wiki/Labrador" title="Labrador">Labrador</a>, Canada. The species winters in large flocks in cultivated areas, rice fields and grasslands, preferring to roost in rice-fields.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-breasted_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The yellow-breasted bunting breeds in open scrubby areas that consist of dry water rice fields for foraging and reedbeds for roosting, often near water, and is present in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. It lays four to six eggs in a nest on the ground. Its food consists of insects when feeding young, and otherwise seeds.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-breasted_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Populations have declined precipitously since the early 2000s, and the species is now considered to be <a href="/wiki/Critically_endangered" title="Critically endangered">critically endangered</a>. The decline of the yellow-breasted bunting is likely to be caused by substantial trapping during migration and most specifically at winter sites. Birds are flushed then caught in mist-nets, to be sold for consumption as "sparrows" or "rice birds". Even though the actions have been restricted to a small area in southern China, it has become more widespread and popular to increasing wealth, and hunters now travel long distances to find sufficient birds. Shifts in rice paddy irrigation practices have reduced the quality and quantity of wintering habitats, including the loss of water stubble, and the loss of reedbeds has reduced available roost sites.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
