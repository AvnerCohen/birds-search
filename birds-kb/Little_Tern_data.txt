<p>The <b>little tern</b> (<i>Sternula albifrons</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> of the family <a href="/wiki/Laridae" title="Laridae">Laridae</a>. It was formerly placed into the genus <i>Sterna</i>, which now is restricted to the large white terns.<sup id="cite_ref-Bridge_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bridge-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The genus name is a diminutive of <i><a href="/wiki/Sterna" title="Sterna">Sterna</a></i>, "tern". The specific <i>albifrons</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>albus</i>, "white", and "frons", <i>forehead</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The former <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> (<i>S. a. antillarum</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> <i>S. a. saundersi</i> <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are now considered to be separate species, the <a href="/wiki/Least_tern" title="Least tern">least tern</a> (<i>Sternula antillarum</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Saunders%27s_tern" title="Saunders&#39;s tern">Saunders's tern</a> (<i>Sternula saundersi</i>).
</p><p>This <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> breeds on the coasts and inland waterways of temperate and tropical <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. It is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in the subtropical and tropical oceans as far south as <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>.
</p><p>There are three subspecies, the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_(taxonomy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate (taxonomy)">nominate</a> <i>albifrons</i> occurring in Europe to North Africa and western Asia; <i>guineae</i> of western and central Africa; and <i>sinensis</i> of East Asia (SE Russia to Japan, SE Asia, Philippines) and the north and east coasts of Australia and New Guinea<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The little tern breeds in colonies on gravel or shingle coasts and islands. It lays two to four eggs on the ground. Like all white terns, it is defensive of its nest and young and will attack intruders.
</p><p>Like most other white terns, the little tern feeds by plunge-diving for fish, usually from saline environments.  The offering of fish by the male to the female is part of the courtship display.
</p><p>This is a small tern, 21–25&#160;cm long with a 41–47&#160;cm wingspan. It is not likely to be confused with other species, apart from fairy tern and Saunders's tern, because of its size and white forehead in breeding plumage. Its thin sharp bill is yellow with a black tip and its legs are also yellow. In winter, the forehead is more extensively white, the bill is black and the legs duller. The call is a loud and distinctive creaking noise.
</p><p>The little tern was described by the German naturalist <a href="/wiki/Peter_Simon_Pallas" title="Peter Simon Pallas">Peter Simon Pallas</a> in 1764 and given the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> <i>Sterna albifrons</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Populations_on_European_rivers">Populations on European rivers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Populations on European rivers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sterna_albifrons_MWNH_0425.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Sterna_albifrons_MWNH_0425.JPG/220px-Sterna_albifrons_MWNH_0425.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="224" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Sterna_albifrons_MWNH_0425.JPG/330px-Sterna_albifrons_MWNH_0425.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Sterna_albifrons_MWNH_0425.JPG/440px-Sterna_albifrons_MWNH_0425.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1716" data-file-height="1749" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sterna_albifrons_MWNH_0425.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>At the beginning of the 19th century the little tern was a common bird of European shores, rivers and wetlands, but in the 20th century populations of coastal areas decreased because of habitat loss, pollution and human disturbance.
</p><p>The loss of inland populations has been even more severe, since due to dams, river regulation and sediment extraction it has lost most of its former habitats. The Little Tern population has declined or become extinct in many European countries, and former breeding places on large rivers like the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elbe" title="Elbe">Elbe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> ceased. Nowadays, only few river systems in Europe possess suitable habitats; the Loire/Allier in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, the Vistula/Odra in <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, the Po/Ticino in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, the Daugava in <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a>, the Nemunas in <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a>, the Sava in <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a> and the Drava in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> and Croatia. The status of the little tern on the rivers Tagus and lower Danube is uncertain.
</p><p>The Drava population is one of the most threatened. Old fashioned water management practices, including river regulation and sediment extraction, endanger the remaining pairs. Only 15 pairs still breed on extensive sand or gravel banks along the border between Hungary and Croatia. The WWF and its partners are involved in working for the protection of this bird and this unique European river ecosystem. The little tern is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>
