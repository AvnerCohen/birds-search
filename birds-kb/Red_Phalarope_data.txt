<p>The <b>red phalarope</b> or <b>grey phalarope</b> (<i>Phalaropus fulicarius</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Phalarope" title="Phalarope">phalarope</a> breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> regions of North America and <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurasia</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, and, unusually for a wader, migrating mainly on oceanic routes and wintering at sea on tropical oceans.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_phalarope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The red phalarope was one of the many <a href="/wiki/Aves_in_the_10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="Aves in the 10th edition of Systema Naturae">bird species originally described</a> by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his landmark 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a>, where it was given the binomial name of <i>Tringa fulicaria</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Linnaeus1758_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linnaeus1758-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The English and genus names for phalaropes come through French <i>phalarope</i> and scientific <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>Phalaropus</i> from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>phalaris</i>, "coot", and <i>pous</i>, "foot". The specific <i>fulicarius</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>fulica</i>, "coot". Coots and phalaropes both have lobed toes.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-job_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_phalarope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:229px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RedPhalarope-12NOV2016.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Red phalarope" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/RedPhalarope-12NOV2016.jpg/227px-RedPhalarope-12NOV2016.jpg" decoding="async" width="227" height="122" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/RedPhalarope-12NOV2016.jpg/341px-RedPhalarope-12NOV2016.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/RedPhalarope-12NOV2016.jpg/454px-RedPhalarope-12NOV2016.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="1073" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RedPhalarope-12NOV2016.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nonbreeding in <a href="/wiki/Bodega_Bay,_California" title="Bodega Bay, California">Bodega Bay, California</a>. November 2016.</div></div></div>
<p>The red phalarope is about 21&#160;cm (8.3&#160;in) in length, with lobed toes and a straight bill, somewhat thicker than that of <a href="/wiki/Red-necked_phalarope" title="Red-necked phalarope">red-necked phalarope</a>. The breeding female is predominantly dark brown and black above, with red underparts and white cheek patches. The bill is yellow, tipped black. The breeding male is a duller version of the female. Young birds are light grey and brown above, with buff underparts and a dark patch through the eye. In winter, the plumage is essentially grey above and white below, but the black eyepatch is always present. The bill is black in winter. Their call is a short <i>beek</i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_phalarope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Phalaropus_fulicarius_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.119.14.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Phalaropus_fulicarius_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.119.14.jpg/220px-Phalaropus_fulicarius_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.119.14.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Phalaropus_fulicarius_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.119.14.jpg/330px-Phalaropus_fulicarius_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.119.14.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Phalaropus_fulicarius_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.119.14.jpg/440px-Phalaropus_fulicarius_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.119.14.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3307" data-file-height="2362" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Phalaropus_fulicarius_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.119.14.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Phalaropus fulicarius</i> - <a href="/wiki/MHNT" class="mw-redirect" title="MHNT">MHNT</a></div></div></div>
<p>The typical avian sex roles are reversed in the three phalarope species. Females are larger and more brightly coloured than males. The females pursue males, compete for nesting territory, and will aggressively defend their nests and chosen mates. Once the females lay their olive-brown eggs, they begin their southward migration, leaving the males to incubate the eggs and care for the young. Three to six eggs are laid in a ground nest near water. Incubation lasts 18 or 19 days.<sup id="cite_ref-BOC_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BOC-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The young mainly feed themselves and are able to fly within 18 days of birth.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_phalarope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When feeding, a red phalarope will often swim in a small, rapid circle, forming a small whirlpool. This behaviour is thought to aid feeding by raising food from the bottom of shallow water. The bird will reach into the outskirts of the vortex with its bill, plucking small <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> or <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> caught up therein. They sometimes fly up to catch insects in flight. On the open ocean, they are found in areas where converging ocean currents produce upwellings and are often found near groups of <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">whales</a>. Outside of the <a href="/wiki/Nesting_season" title="Nesting season">nesting season</a> they often travel in flocks.
</p><p>This species is often very tame and approachable.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_and_conservation">Status and conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_phalarope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Status and conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The red phalarope is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>
