<p>The <b>pintail</b> or <b>northern pintail</b> (<i>Anas acuta</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">duck</a> with wide geographic <a href="/wiki/Range_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Range (biology)">distribution</a> that breeds in the northern areas of Europe and across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> and North America. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and winters south of its <a href="/wiki/Breeding_range" class="mw-redirect" title="Breeding range">breeding range</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>. Unusually for a bird with such a large range, it has no geographical <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> if the possibly <a href="/wiki/Conspecific" class="mw-redirect" title="Conspecific">conspecific</a> duck <a href="/wiki/Eaton%27s_pintail" title="Eaton&#39;s pintail">Eaton's pintail</a> is considered to be a separate species.
</p><p>This is a large duck, and the male's long central tail feathers give rise to the species' English and scientific names. Both sexes have blue-grey bills and grey legs and feet. The drake is more striking, having a thin white stripe running from the back of its chocolate-coloured head down its neck to its mostly white undercarriage. The drake also has attractive grey, brown, and black patterning on its back and sides. The hen's plumage is more subtle and subdued, with drab brown feathers similar to those of other female <a href="/wiki/Dabbling_duck" class="mw-redirect" title="Dabbling duck">dabbling ducks</a>. Hens make a coarse quack and the drakes a flute-like whistle.
</p><p>The northern pintail is a bird of open <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a> which nests on the ground, often some distance from water. It feeds by dabbling for plant food and adds small <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> to its diet during the <a href="/wiki/Nesting_season" title="Nesting season">nesting season</a>. It is highly gregarious when not breeding, forming large mixed flocks with other species of duck. This duck's population is affected by predators, parasites and avian diseases. Human activities, such as agriculture, hunting and fishing, have also had a significant impact on numbers. Nevertheless, owing to the huge range and large population of this species, it is not threatened globally.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_pintail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his landmark 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a> as <i>Anas acuta</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Linnaeus_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linnaeus-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The scientific name comes from two <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> words: <i>anas</i>, meaning "duck", and <i>acuta</i>, which comes from the verb <i>acuere</i>, "to sharpen"; the species term, like the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> name, refers to the pointed tail of the male in breeding plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-job90_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job90-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
Within the large <a href="/wiki/Anatinae" title="Anatinae">dabbling duck</a> genus <i>Anas</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Linnaeus_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linnaeus-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> the northern pintail's closest relatives are other pintails, such as the <a href="/wiki/Yellow-billed_pintail" title="Yellow-billed pintail">yellow-billed pintail</a> (<i>A. georgica</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Eaton%27s_pintail" title="Eaton&#39;s pintail">Eaton's pintail</a> (<i>A. eatoni</i>). The pintails are sometimes separated in the genus <i>Dafila</i> (described by <a href="/wiki/James_Francis_Stephens" title="James Francis Stephens">Stephens</a>, 1824), an arrangement supported by morphological, molecular and behavioural data.<sup id="cite_ref-Johnson1999_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson1999-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Johnson2000_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson2000-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Livezey_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Livezey-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The famous <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Ornithology" title="Ornithology">ornithologist</a> Sir <a href="/wiki/Peter_Scott" title="Peter Scott">Peter Scott</a> gave this name to his daughter, the artist Dafila Scott.<sup id="cite_ref-Dafila_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dafila-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Eaton's pintail has two subspecies, <i>A. e. eatoni</i> (the Kerguelen pintail) of <a href="/wiki/Kerguelen_Islands" title="Kerguelen Islands">Kerguelen Islands</a>, and <i>A. e. drygalskyi</i> (the Crozet pintail) of <a href="/wiki/Crozet_Islands" title="Crozet Islands">Crozet Islands</a>, and was formerly considered conspecific with the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere's</a> northern pintail. <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">Sexual dimorphism</a> is much less marked in the southern pintails, with the male's breeding appearance being similar to the female plumage. Unusually for a species with such a large range, northern pintail has no geographical subspecies if Eaton's pintail is treated as a separate species.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A claimed extinct subspecies from <a href="/wiki/Manra_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Manra Island">Manra Island</a>, Tristram's pintail, <i>A. a. modesta</i>, appears to be indistinguishable from the nominate form.<sup id="cite_ref-Hume_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hume-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The three <a href="/wiki/Type_(biology)" title="Type (biology)">syntype specimens</a> of <i>Dafila modesta</i> Tristram (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.biodiversitylibrary.org/item/92048">Proceedings of the Zoological Society of London, 1886, p.79. pl. VII</a>), the extinct subspecies, are held in the collections of <a href="/wiki/National_Museums_Liverpool" title="National Museums Liverpool">National Museums Liverpool</a> at <a href="/wiki/World_Museum" title="World Museum">World Museum</a>, with accession numbers T11792 (male immature), T11795 (female adult) and T11797 (female adult). The specimens were collected by J. V. Arundel in Sydney Island (<a href="/wiki/Manra_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Manra Island">Manra Island</a>), Phoenix Islands in 1885 and came to the Liverpool national collection via Canon <a href="/wiki/Henry_Baker_Tristram" title="Henry Baker Tristram">Henry Baker Tristram</a>'s collection which was purchased in 1896.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_pintail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ljubljana_-_Anas_acuta_male.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Ljubljana_-_Anas_acuta_male.jpg/220px-Ljubljana_-_Anas_acuta_male.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Ljubljana_-_Anas_acuta_male.jpg/330px-Ljubljana_-_Anas_acuta_male.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Ljubljana_-_Anas_acuta_male.jpg/440px-Ljubljana_-_Anas_acuta_male.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2865" data-file-height="1914" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ljubljana_-_Anas_acuta_male.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Male in <a href="/wiki/River_Ljubljanica" class="mw-redirect" title="River Ljubljanica">river Ljubljanica</a>, Slovenia</div></div></div>
<p>The northern pintail is a fairly large duck with a <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> of 23.6–28.2&#160;cm (9.3–11.1&#160;in) and wingspan of 80–95&#160;cm (31–37&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The male is 59–76&#160;cm (23–30&#160;in) in length and weighs 450–1,360&#160;g (0.99–3.00&#160;lb), and therefore is considerably larger than the female, which is 51–64&#160;cm (20–25&#160;in) long and weighs 454–1,135&#160;g (1.001–2.502&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-umich_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The northern pintail broadly overlaps in size with the similarly-widespread <a href="/wiki/Mallard" title="Mallard">mallard</a>, but is more slender, elongated and gracile, with a relatively longer neck and (in males) a longer tail. The unmistakable breeding <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumaged</a> male has a chocolate-brown head and white breast with a white stripe extending up the side of the neck. Its upperparts and sides are grey, but elongated grey feathers with black central stripes are draped across the back from the shoulder area. The vent area is yellow, contrasting with the black underside of the tail,<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> which has the central feathers elongated to as much as 10&#160;cm (3.9&#160;in). The bill is bluish and the legs are blue-grey.<sup id="cite_ref-Gooders_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gooders-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The adult female is mainly scalloped and mottled in light brown with a more uniformly grey-brown head, and its pointed tail is shorter than the male's; it is still easily identified by its shape, long neck, and long grey bill.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In non-breeding (eclipse) plumage, the drake pintail looks similar to the female, but retains the male upperwing pattern and long grey shoulder feathers. Juvenile birds resemble the female, but are less neatly scalloped and have a duller brown <a href="/wiki/Speculum_feathers" title="Speculum feathers">speculum</a> with a narrower trailing edge.<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The pintail walks well on land, and swims well.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It has a very fast flight, with its wings slightly swept-back, rather than straight out from the body like other ducks. In flight, the male shows a black speculum bordered white at the rear and pale rufous at the front, whereas the female's speculum is dark brown bordered with white, narrowly at the front edge but very prominently at the rear, being visible at a distance of 1,600&#160;m (0.99&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The male's call is a soft <i>proop-proop</i> whistle, similar to that of the <a href="/wiki/Common_teal" class="mw-redirect" title="Common teal">common teal</a>, whereas the female has a <a href="/wiki/Mallard" title="Mallard">mallard</a>-like descending quack, and a low croak when flushed.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_pintail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Northern_Pintail_(Males)_near_Hodal_I_IMG_9252.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Northern_Pintail_%28Males%29_near_Hodal_I_IMG_9252.jpg/220px-Northern_Pintail_%28Males%29_near_Hodal_I_IMG_9252.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="143" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Northern_Pintail_%28Males%29_near_Hodal_I_IMG_9252.jpg/330px-Northern_Pintail_%28Males%29_near_Hodal_I_IMG_9252.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Northern_Pintail_%28Males%29_near_Hodal_I_IMG_9252.jpg/440px-Northern_Pintail_%28Males%29_near_Hodal_I_IMG_9252.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="519" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Northern_Pintail_(Males)_near_Hodal_I_IMG_9252.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Non-breeding males wintering in India</div></div></div>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Dabbling_duck" class="mw-redirect" title="Dabbling duck">dabbling duck</a> breeds across northern areas of the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> south to about <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-umich_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwestern United States</a>. Mainly in winters south of its breeding range, reaches almost to the equator in <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a> and tropical <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>. Small numbers migrate to <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a> islands, particularly <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, where a few hundred birds winter on the main islands in shallow <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a> and flooded agricultural habitats.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">Transoceanic</a> journeys also occur: a bird that was caught and <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">ringed</a> in <a href="/wiki/Labrador" title="Labrador">Labrador</a>, Canada, was shot by a hunter in England nine days later,<sup id="cite_ref-umich_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese-ringed</a> birds have been recovered from six US states east to <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ESPN_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ESPN-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In parts of the range, such as <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and the northwestern United States, the pintail may be present all year.<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The northern pintail's breeding habitat is open unwooded wetlands, such as wet grassland, lakesides or tundra. In winter, it will utilise a wider range of open habitats, such as sheltered estuaries, brackish marshes and coastal lagoons. It is highly gregarious outside the breeding season and forms very large mixed flocks with other ducks.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_pintail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_pintail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anas_acuta_2005.01.10-10.18.52.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Anas_acuta_2005.01.10-10.18.52.jpg/220px-Anas_acuta_2005.01.10-10.18.52.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Anas_acuta_2005.01.10-10.18.52.jpg/330px-Anas_acuta_2005.01.10-10.18.52.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Anas_acuta_2005.01.10-10.18.52.jpg/440px-Anas_acuta_2005.01.10-10.18.52.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1092" data-file-height="716" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anas_acuta_2005.01.10-10.18.52.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Breeding pair</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anas_acuta_MWNH_0965.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Anas_acuta_MWNH_0965.JPG/220px-Anas_acuta_MWNH_0965.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="196" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Anas_acuta_MWNH_0965.JPG/330px-Anas_acuta_MWNH_0965.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Anas_acuta_MWNH_0965.JPG/440px-Anas_acuta_MWNH_0965.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2580" data-file-height="2299" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anas_acuta_MWNH_0965.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>Both sexes reach sexual maturity at one year of age. The male mates with the female by swimming close to her with his head lowered and tail raised, continually whistling. If there is a group of males, they will chase the female in flight until only one drake is left. The female prepares for copulation, which takes place in the water, by lowering her body; the male then bobs his head up and down and mounts the female, taking the feathers on the back of her head in his mouth. After mating, he raises his head and back and whistles.<sup id="cite_ref-umich_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Breeding takes place between April and June, with the nest being constructed on the ground and hidden amongst vegetation in a dry location, often some distance from water. It is a shallow scrape on the ground lined with plant material and down.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The female lays seven to nine cream-coloured eggs at the rate of one per day;<sup id="cite_ref-umich_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> the eggs are 55&#160;mm ×&#160;38&#160;mm (2.2&#160;in ×&#160;1.5&#160;in) in size and weigh 45&#160;g (1.6&#160;oz), of which 7% is shell.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> If predators destroy the first clutch, the female can produce a replacement clutch as late as the end of July.<sup id="cite_ref-umich_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
The hen alone incubates the eggs for 22 to 24 days before they hatch. The <a href="/wiki/Precocial" class="mw-redirect" title="Precocial">precocial</a> downy chicks are then led by the female to the nearest body of water, where they feed on dead insects on the water surface. The chicks fledge in 46 to 47 days after hatching, but stay with the female until she has completed <a href="/wiki/Moult" class="mw-redirect" title="Moult">moulting</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-umich_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Around three-quarters of chicks live long enough to fledge, but not more than half of those survive long enough to reproduce.<sup id="cite_ref-umich_11-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The maximum recorded age is 27 years and 5 months for a <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> bird.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_pintail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Northern_Pintails_(Male_%26_Female)-_Up-ending_I_342.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Northern_Pintails_%28Male_%26_Female%29-_Up-ending_I_342.jpg/220px-Northern_Pintails_%28Male_%26_Female%29-_Up-ending_I_342.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="125" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Northern_Pintails_%28Male_%26_Female%29-_Up-ending_I_342.jpg/330px-Northern_Pintails_%28Male_%26_Female%29-_Up-ending_I_342.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Northern_Pintails_%28Male_%26_Female%29-_Up-ending_I_342.jpg/440px-Northern_Pintails_%28Male_%26_Female%29-_Up-ending_I_342.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Northern_Pintails_(Male_%26_Female)-_Up-ending_I_342.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Up-ending to feed (male on right)</div></div></div>
<p>The pintail feeds by dabbling and upending in shallow water for plant food mainly in the evening or at night, and therefore spends much of the day resting.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_8-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Its long neck enables it to take food items from the bottom of water bodies up to 30&#160;cm (12&#160;in) deep, which are beyond the reach of other dabbling ducks like the mallard.<sup id="cite_ref-Gooders_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gooders-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The winter diet is mainly plant material including seeds and <a href="/wiki/Rhizome" title="Rhizome">rhizomes</a> of aquatic plants, but the pintail sometimes feeds on roots, grain and other seeds in fields, though less frequently than other <i>Anas</i> ducks.<sup id="cite_ref-Gooders_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gooders-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> During the nesting season, this bird eats mainly <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrate</a> animals, including aquatic <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mollusca" title="Mollusca">molluscs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-umich_11-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_pintail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Northern_Pintail_(Male)-_Preening_I_IMG_1119.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Northern_Pintail_%28Male%29-_Preening_I_IMG_1119.jpg/220px-Northern_Pintail_%28Male%29-_Preening_I_IMG_1119.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Northern_Pintail_%28Male%29-_Preening_I_IMG_1119.jpg/330px-Northern_Pintail_%28Male%29-_Preening_I_IMG_1119.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Northern_Pintail_%28Male%29-_Preening_I_IMG_1119.jpg/440px-Northern_Pintail_%28Male%29-_Preening_I_IMG_1119.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="583" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Northern_Pintail_(Male)-_Preening_I_IMG_1119.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Male preening</div></div></div>
<p>Pintail nests and chicks are vulnerable to predation by mammals, such as <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">foxes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Badger" title="Badger">badgers</a>, and birds like <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magpie" title="Magpie">magpies</a>. The adults can take flight to escape terrestrial predators, but nesting females in particular may be surprised by large <a href="/wiki/Carnivore" title="Carnivore">carnivores</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Bobcat" title="Bobcat">bobcats</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-umich_11-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Large <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">birds of prey</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Northern_goshawk" title="Northern goshawk">northern goshawks</a>, will take ducks from the ground, and some <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcons</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Gyrfalcon" title="Gyrfalcon">gyrfalcon</a>, have the speed and power to catch flying birds.<sup id="cite_ref-Forsman_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forsman-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is susceptible to a range of <a href="/wiki/Parasitism" title="Parasitism">parasites</a> including <i><a href="/wiki/Cryptosporidium" title="Cryptosporidium">Cryptosporidium</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Giardia_lamblia" class="mw-redirect" title="Giardia lamblia">Giardia</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Cestoda" title="Cestoda">tapeworms</a>, blood parasites and external feather lice,<sup id="cite_ref-AEM_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AEM-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-tape_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tape-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Williams_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lice_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lice-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and is also affected by other avian diseases. It is often the dominant species in major mortality events from avian <a href="/wiki/Botulism" title="Botulism">botulism</a> and avian <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Friend_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Friend-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and can also contract <a href="/wiki/Avian_influenza" title="Avian influenza">avian influenza</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Influenza_A_virus_subtype_H5N1" title="Influenza A virus subtype H5N1">H5N1</a> strain of which is highly pathogenic and occasionally infects humans.<sup id="cite_ref-USDA_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USDA-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The northern pintail is a popular species for game shooting because of its speed, agility, and excellent eating qualities, and is hunted across its range.<sup id="cite_ref-Marrone_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marrone-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Although one of the world's most numerous ducks,<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> the combination of hunting with other factors has led to population declines, and local restrictions on hunting have been introduced at times to help conserve numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-USFWSnews_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USFWSnews-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species' preferred habitat of shallow water is naturally susceptible to problems such as drought or the encroachment of vegetation, but this duck's habitat might be increasingly threatened by climate change.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Populations are also affected by the conversion of wetlands and grassland to arable crops, depriving the duck of feeding and nesting areas. Spring planting means that many nests of this early breeding duck are destroyed by farming activities,<sup id="cite_ref-Audubon_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Audubon-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and a Canadian study showed that more than half of the surveyed nests were destroyed by agricultural work such as ploughing and <a href="/wiki/Harrow_(tool)" title="Harrow (tool)">harrowing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-usdafs_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usdafs-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Northern_Pintail_Drake_on_Seedskadee_National_Wildlife_Refuge_(23267347871).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Northern_Pintail_Drake_on_Seedskadee_National_Wildlife_Refuge_%2823267347871%29.jpg/220px-Northern_Pintail_Drake_on_Seedskadee_National_Wildlife_Refuge_%2823267347871%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Northern_Pintail_Drake_on_Seedskadee_National_Wildlife_Refuge_%2823267347871%29.jpg/330px-Northern_Pintail_Drake_on_Seedskadee_National_Wildlife_Refuge_%2823267347871%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Northern_Pintail_Drake_on_Seedskadee_National_Wildlife_Refuge_%2823267347871%29.jpg/440px-Northern_Pintail_Drake_on_Seedskadee_National_Wildlife_Refuge_%2823267347871%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Northern_Pintail_Drake_on_Seedskadee_National_Wildlife_Refuge_(23267347871).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Male flying above <a href="/wiki/Seedskadee_National_Wildlife_Refuge" title="Seedskadee National Wildlife Refuge">Seedskadee National Wildlife Refuge</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Northern_Pintail_(Female)-_Feeding_I_IMG_0910.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Northern_Pintail_%28Female%29-_Feeding_I_IMG_0910.jpg/220px-Northern_Pintail_%28Female%29-_Feeding_I_IMG_0910.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Northern_Pintail_%28Female%29-_Feeding_I_IMG_0910.jpg/330px-Northern_Pintail_%28Female%29-_Feeding_I_IMG_0910.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Northern_Pintail_%28Female%29-_Feeding_I_IMG_0910.jpg/440px-Northern_Pintail_%28Female%29-_Feeding_I_IMG_0910.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="542" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Northern_Pintail_(Female)-_Feeding_I_IMG_0910.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Female</div></div></div>
<p>Hunting with lead shot, along with the use of lead sinkers in <a href="/wiki/Angling" title="Angling">angling</a>, has been identified as a major cause of <a href="/wiki/Lead_poisoning" title="Lead poisoning">lead poisoning</a> in waterfowl, which often feed off the bottom of lakes and wetlands where the shot collects.<sup id="cite_ref-Scheuhammer_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scheuhammer-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> study showed that northern pintail and <a href="/wiki/Common_pochard" title="Common pochard">common pochard</a> were the species with the highest levels of lead shot ingestion, higher than in northern countries of the western Palearctic flyway, where lead shot has been banned.<sup id="cite_ref-Mateo_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mateo-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In the United States, Canada, and many western European countries, all shot used for <a href="/wiki/Waterfowl" class="mw-redirect" title="Waterfowl">waterfowl</a> must now be non-toxic, and therefore may not contain any <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-USFWS2_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USFWS2-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Newsc_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newsc-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BASC_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BASC-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_pintail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The northern pintail has a large range, estimated at 28,400,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (11,000,000&#160;sq&#160;mi), and a population estimated at 5.3–5.4 million individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-BirdLife_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLife-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> It is therefore not believed to meet the IUCN Red List threshold criterion of a population decline of more than 30% in ten years or three generations, and is evaluated as of least concern.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic_realm" title="Palearctic realm">Palaearctic</a>, breeding populations are declining in much of the range, including its stronghold in Russia. In other regions, populations are stable or fluctuating.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Pintails in North America at least have been badly affected by avian diseases, with the breeding population falling from more than 10 million in 1957 to 3.5 million by 1964. Although the species has recovered from that low point, the breeding population in 1999 was 30% below the long-term average, despite years of major efforts focused on restoring the species. In 1997, an estimated 1.5&#160;million water birds, the majority being northern pintails, died from avian botulism during two outbreaks in Canada and Utah.<sup id="cite_ref-Friend_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Friend-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The northern pintail is one of the species to which the Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies,<sup id="cite_ref-AEWA_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AEWA-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> but it has no special status under the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (<a href="/wiki/CITES" title="CITES">CITES</a>), which regulates international trade in specimens of wild animals and plants.<sup id="cite_ref-umich_11-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
