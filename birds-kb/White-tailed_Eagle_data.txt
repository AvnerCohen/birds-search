<p>The <b>White-tailed Eagle</b> (<i>Haliaeetus albicilla</i><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup>) — also called the <b>Sea Eagle</b>, <b>Erne</b> (sometimes <b>Ern</b>), and <b>White-tailed Sea-eagle</b> — is a large <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a> which includes other raptors such as <a href="/wiki/Hawk" title="Hawk">hawks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kite_(bird)" title="Kite (bird)">kites</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Harrier_(bird)" title="Harrier (bird)">harriers</a>. It is considered a close cousin of the <a href="/wiki/Bald_Eagle" title="Bald Eagle">Bald Eagle</a> and occupies the same <a href="/wiki/Ecological_niche" title="Ecological niche">ecological niche</a>, but in <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=White-tailed_Eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:White-tailed-eagle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/White-tailed-eagle.jpg/220px-White-tailed-eagle.jpg" width="220" height="200" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:White-tailed-eagle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Adult, wild eagle from <a href="/wiki/Svolv%C3%A6r" title="Svolvær">Svolvær</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> showing characteristic long, broad, fingered wings, heavy bill and short wedge-shaped tail</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The White-tailed Eagle is a very large bird. It measures 66–94 cm (26–37 in) in length with a 1.78–2.45 m (5.8–8.0 ft) wingspan. The wingspan, with a midpoint of 2.18&#160;m (7.2&#160;ft), is on average the largest of any eagle.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Steller%27s_Sea_Eagle" title="Steller's Sea Eagle">Steller's Sea Eagle</a>, larger in both weight and total length, is the closest rival for median wingspan amongst living eagles.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The Bald Eagle is roughly the same size as the White-tailed Eagle, although has a shorter average wingspan and usually longer total length, due to a longer tail.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Females, typically weighing 4–6.9 kg (8.8–15 lb), are slightly larger than males, which weigh 3.1–5.4 kg (6.8–12 lb)n<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The record weight for the species was 7.5&#160;kg (17&#160;lb) for a specimen from <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, while a more recent huge female from <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> reportedly spanned 2.53&#160;m (8.3&#160;ft) across the wings.<sup id="cite_ref-Wood_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Among standard measurements, the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> is 55.2–71.7 cm (21.7–28.2 in), the <a href="/wiki/Tail" title="Tail">tail</a> is 25–33 cm (9.8–13 in), the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> is 9.2–10.1 cm (3.6–4.0 in) and the <a href="/wiki/Culmen_(bird)" title="Culmen (bird)" class="mw-redirect">exposed culmen</a> is 6–6.5 cm (2.4–2.6 in).<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The measurements of eagles from Greenland are in general larger than in other populations of the species.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The White-tailed Eagle is sometimes considered the fourth largest eagle in the world <sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Nature_of_Scotland49_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Nature_of_Scotland49-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> and is on average the fourth heaviest eagle in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species has broad "barn door" wings, a large head and a large thick beak. The adult is mainly grayish-brown except for the slightly paler head and neck, blackish flight feathers, and distinctive white tail. All bare parts are yellow in color, including both the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> and the legs. In juvenile birds, the tail and bill are darker, with the tail becoming white with a dark terminal band in sub-adults.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnowPerrins1998_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnowPerrins1998-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears from May 2011">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The combination of mousy-brown coloration, broad, evenly-held wings, white tail, strong yellow bill and overall large size render the White-tailed Eagle essentially unmistakable in its native range.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Some individuals have been found to live over 25 years,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> 21 years being the average.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Nature_of_Scotland49_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Nature_of_Scotland49-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>This large <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagle</a> breeds in northern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. The largest population in Europe is found along the coast of <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>. The World population in 2008 stands at only 9,000–11,000 pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Nature_of_Scotland49_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Nature_of_Scotland49-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> They are mostly resident, only the northernmost birds such as the eastern <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberian</a> population <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrating</a> south in winter. Birds from eastern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> rarely vagrate into <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>.</p>

<p>Small disjunct resident populations occur in southwesternmost <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>. The former has been proposed as a distinct <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> <i>groenlandicus</i> based on their very large size and body proportions. However, the species is now considered <a href="/wiki/Monotype_(biology)" title="Monotype (biology)" class="mw-redirect">monotypic</a> and the size variation is <a href="/wiki/Cline_(biology)" title="Cline (biology)">clinal</a> according to <a href="/wiki/Bergmann%27s_Rule" title="Bergmann's Rule" class="mw-redirect">Bergmann's Rule</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEdel_HoyoElliottSargatal1994_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEdel_HoyoElliottSargatal1994-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears from May 2011">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A recent genetic study of <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHailerHelanderFolkestadGanusevich2007_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHailerHelanderFolkestadGanusevich2007-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> is consistent with this idea. Greenlandic white-tailed eagles are, on <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolutionary</a> time scales, a relatively recently founded population that has not yet accumulated a lot of unique genetic characteristics. However, the population appears to be demographically isolated and deserves special protection.</p>
<p>The White-tailed Eagle forms a <a href="/wiki/Cryptic_species_complex" title="Cryptic species complex">species pair</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Bald_Eagle" title="Bald Eagle">Bald Eagle</a>. These diverged from other sea eagles at the beginning of the early <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> (c. 10&#160;<a href="/wiki/Mya_(unit)" title="Mya (unit)" class="mw-redirect">mya</a>) at the latest, possibly (if the most ancient fossil record is correctly assigned to this genus) as early as the early or middle <a href="/wiki/Oligocene" title="Oligocene">Oligocene</a>, about 28&#160;<a href="/wiki/Mya_(unit)" title="Mya (unit)" class="mw-redirect">mya</a> ago.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWinkHeidrichFentzloff1996_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWinkHeidrichFentzloff1996-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>As in other sea-eagle species pairs, this one consists of a white-headed (the Bald Eagle) and a tan-headed species. They probably diverged in the North <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a>, spreading westwards into Eurasia and eastwards into <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. Like the third large northern species, <a href="/wiki/Steller%27s_Sea_Eagle" title="Steller's Sea Eagle">Steller's Sea Eagle</a>, they have yellow talons, beaks, and eyes in adults.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=White-tailed_Eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:White_tailed_Eagle_DG.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/White_tailed_Eagle_DG.JPG/220px-White_tailed_Eagle_DG.JPG" width="220" height="168" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:White_tailed_Eagle_DG.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Diving for fish near the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Skye" title="Isle of Skye" class="mw-redirect">Isle of Skye</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Eagle's diet is varied, opportunistic and seasonal. Prey specimens can often include <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>. Many birds live largely as scavengers, regularly pirating food from <a href="/wiki/Otter" title="Otter">otters</a> and other birds including <a href="/wiki/Cormorant" title="Cormorant">cormorants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osprey" title="Osprey">Ospreys</a> and various other <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Carrion is often the primary food source during lean winter months, with fish and ungulates being preferred but everything from <a href="/wiki/Cetacean" title="Cetacean" class="mw-redirect">cetaceans</a> to <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> to even <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a> being eaten after death.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rspb_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> They are often dominant among the scavengers in their range, over all but perhaps the largest carnivorous mammals (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Gray_wolf" title="Gray wolf">gray wolves</a>, etc.)<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> However, this eagle can be a powerful hunter as well. In <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, this species regularly competes fiercely with <a href="/wiki/Golden_Eagle" title="Golden Eagle">Golden Eagles</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hare" title="Hare">hares</a> either eagle may catch. In <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, where both large eagles also occur in some numbers, this competition is not known to occur.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_2-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The daily food requirement is in the region for a White-tailed eagle is in range of 500–600 g (1.1–1.3 lb).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1980_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1980-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears from May 2011">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Although generally a less active hunter than the Golden Eagle, competition over food can go either way depending on the individual eagle. They can exist at higher population densities and typically outnumber Golden Eagles because of their longer gut and more efficient digestive system, being able to live better with less food.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHalley1998_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHalley1998-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Virtually any fish found near the surface is potential prey for the White-tailed eagle.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_2-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Commercial fisheries and carp ponds are readily exploited by the eagles when available.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_2-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Although they occasionally kill and harass some land birds given the opportunity, White-tailed eagles usually target water-based birds as prey.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_2-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> In the Baltic, the diet of this species consists mainly of sea birds (from the <a href="/wiki/Little_Tern" title="Little Tern">Little Tern</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Skua" title="Great Skua">Great Skua</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Esox" title="Esox">pike</a>. Recently they are reported to have attacked and eaten <a href="/wiki/Great_Cormorant" title="Great Cormorant">Great Cormorants</a> and in some cases destroyed whole colonies.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonian</a> island of <a href="/wiki/Hiiumaa" title="Hiiumaa">Hiiumaa</a>, home to at least 25 pairs of sea eagles, as many as 26 individuals have been observed simultaneously culling a single cormorant colony.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> In the UK, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Fulmar" title="Northern Fulmar">fulmar</a> are noted as a common prey species and may contribute to locally high levels of DDT and PCB chemicals in nesting eagles.<sup id="cite_ref-rspb_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Additionally, <a href="/wiki/Loon" title="Loon">loons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grebe" title="Grebe">grebes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">ducks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coot" title="Coot">coots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Auk" title="Auk">auks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geese" title="Geese" class="mw-redirect">geese</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Swan" title="Swan">swans</a> have been preyed upon.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_2-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Adult, nestling and eggs of other birds are all regularly consumed. When targeting non-nesting birds, they often fly towards the waterbird repeatedly, forcing to dive again and again, until the bird is exhausted and is more easily caught.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_2-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> When very large prey is killed, such as swans, the prey may be dragged pulled along the surface of the water to the shore to be consumed.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_2-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Live mammals consumed have ranged in size from <a href="/wiki/Vole" title="Vole">voles</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">lambs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a> calves, the latter likely around the same size as the record-sized deer flown with by Bald Eagles in North America.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_2-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The single confirmed case of an eagle flying with a human being involved the White-tailed Eagle. One June 5, 1932, Svanhild Hansen, a 4-year-old girl, was playing next to her parent's farm house in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> when a White-tailed Eagle grabbed her by the back of her dress and flew with her to its eyrie. The eyrie was 800&#160;m (2,600&#160;ft) up the side of the nearby mountain and about 1.6&#160;km (0.99&#160;mi) away in flying distance. The eagle dropped the young girl onto a narrow ledge about 15.2&#160;m (50&#160;ft) below the nest. After being discovered by a quickly formed search party, the little girl survived with no major injuries, ultimately having kept her talon-pierced dress throughout her life.<sup id="cite_ref-Wood_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=White-tailed_Eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:White-tailed_Eagle_with_2_Buzzards.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/White-tailed_Eagle_with_2_Buzzards.jpg/220px-White-tailed_Eagle_with_2_Buzzards.jpg" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" /></a>

A juvenile (right) being mobbed by a pair of <a href="/wiki/Buzzard" title="Buzzard">buzzards</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Canna,_Scotland" title="Canna, Scotland">Isle of Canna</a></div>

<p>White-tailed Eagles are sexually mature at four or five years of age. They pair for life, though if one dies replacement can occur quickly. A bond is formed when a permanent home range is chosen. They have a characteristic aerial courtship display which culminates in the pair locking talons mid-air and whirling earthwards in series of spectacular cartwheels. White-tailed Eagles are much more vocal than Golden Eagles, particularly during the breeding season and especially the male when near the eyrie. Calls can sometimes take on the form of a duet between the pair.</p>
<p>The nest is a huge edifice of sticks in a <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">tree</a> or on a coastal <a href="/wiki/Cliff" title="Cliff">cliff</a>. Being faithful to their territories, once they breed, nests are often reused, sometimes for decades by successive generations of birds; one nest in Iceland has been in use for over 150 years.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnowPerrins1998_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnowPerrins1998-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears from May 2011">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, trees have been known to collapse under the weight of enormous, long established nests.</p>
<p>The territory of the White-tailed Eagle ranges between 30 and 70&#160;km², normally in sheltered coastal locations. Sometimes they are found inland by lakes and along rivers. The territory of the White-tailed Eagles can overlap with the territory of the Golden Eagle, and competition between the two species is limited. Golden Eagles prefer mountains and moorland, while the White-tailed Eagle prefers the coast and the sea. In adulthood, the White-tailed Eagle has no natural predators and is thus considered an <a href="/wiki/Apex_predator" title="Apex predator">apex predator</a>.</p>
<p>Mated pairs produce one to three <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> per year. The eggs are laid two to five days apart in March or April and are incubated for 38 days by both parents. Once hatched, chicks are quite tolerant of one another, although the first hatched is often larger and dominant at feeding times. The female does most of the brooding and direct feeding, with the male taking over now and then. Young are able to feed themselves from five to six weeks and they fledge at eleven to twelve weeks, remaining in the vicinity of the nest, dependent on their parents for a further six to ten weeks. The sex of nestlings can be identified using field methods, or using <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHelanderHailerVil.C3.A02007_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHelanderHailerVil.C3.A02007-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Surplus chicks are sometimes removed from nests to use in <a href="/wiki/Reintroduction" title="Reintroduction">reintroduction</a> programs in areas where the species has died out. If left in the nest, they are often killed by the first-hatched sooner or later, as in most large eagles.</p>
<p>In such programs, the birds are raised in boxes on platforms in the tree canopy and fed in such a way that they cannot see the person supplying their food, until they are old enough to fly and thus find their own food.</p>

<p>White-tailed Eagles are <a href="/wiki/Apex_predator" title="Apex predator">apex predators</a>. Therefore, they tend to experience <a href="/wiki/Bioaccumulation" title="Bioaccumulation">bioaccumulation</a> from environmental <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollutants</a> that are present in their prey, and also suffered intensive persecution by <a href="/wiki/Shepherd" title="Shepherd">shepherds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gamekeeper" title="Gamekeeper">gamekeepers</a> who considered them (usually wrongly<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHalley1998_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHalley1998-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup>) to be a threat to their <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Game_(food)" title="Game (food)">gamebirds</a>. During the period 1800-1970, White-tailed Eagles in most of Europe underwent dramatic declines, and became extinct in many regions of western, central, and southern Europe. While Norway, Germany, Poland, and Iceland harboured the largest surviving populations, pockets of reproducing pairs remained in several other countries.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHailerHelanderFolkestadGanusevich2006_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHailerHelanderFolkestadGanusevich2006-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Intense conservation actions throughout much of the remaining European distribution range (legal protection to decrease hunting, protection of breeding sites, and winter feeding) led to a recovery of many local populations. Since the 1980s, the European White-tailed Eagle population has recovered steadily, and is spreading back westward. It has today recolonised several traditional breeding areas in Europe and the recovery is still on-going, assisted in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> by reintroduction schemes.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnowPerrins1998_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnowPerrins1998-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears from May 2011">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1980_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1980-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears from May 2011">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Some threats still remain, notably illegal persecution by gamebird shooting interests and egg thieves in Scotland,<sup id="cite_ref-rspb_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> and a new threat from <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbines</a> is emerging with significant mortality (considerably in excess of the area's population productivity) occurring at the Smøla Windfarm in Norway.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It was successfully reintroduced to the <a href="/wiki/R%C3%B9m" title="Rùm">Isle of Rum</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Small_Isles" title="Small Isles">Small Isles</a> archipelago in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, in 1975 and now breeds throughout the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Hebrides" title="Outer Hebrides">Western Isles</a> and the mainland coast of <a href="/wiki/Wester_Ross" title="Wester Ross">Wester Ross</a>. One of its Gaelic names is 'iolaire sùil na grèine' or 'eagle of the sunlit eye.'<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Nature_of_Scotland49_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Nature_of_Scotland49-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> In August 2008 fifteen chicks raised in Norway were released at a secret location in <a href="/wiki/Fife" title="Fife">Fife</a>, in expectation of reintroducing the species in the east coast of Scotland as well.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> The White-tailed Eagle is still a rare breeder in Britain following its extinction and reintroduction, with 36 pairs in 2006 and 40 in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Nature_of_Scotland49_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Nature_of_Scotland49-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnowPerrins1998_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnowPerrins1998-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears from May 2011">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-rspb_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On 22 May 2006 it was announced that a pair of White-tailed Eagles was breeding in the <a href="/wiki/Oostvaardersplassen" title="Oostvaardersplassen">Oostvaardersplassen</a> nature reserve in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>. They arrived on their own, not as a reintroduction. This is the first time the bird has bred in the Netherlands in modern times. In 2007, 2008 and 2009 the eagles returned to their nest.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2010, it turned out that the White-tailed eagle was also breeding in the Zwarte Meer nature district and in the Lauwersmeer area.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The White-tailed Eagle is being reintroduced to <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> where its <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a> name of Iolar Mara (sea eagle) reflects its historic association with the long coast of the island. The program started in the summer of 2007. Fifteen to twenty young eagles from <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> are being released each spring into the <a href="/wiki/Killarney_National_Park" title="Killarney National Park">Killarney National Park</a> in the south-west of Ireland. This comprehensive project will last a number of years with many more eagles being released. The species has a rich history on the island but became extinct in Ireland in the 1900s due to persecution from landowners.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> The last pair bred on the coast of Maigh Eo in 1912.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, one hundred local sheep farmers gathered at Kerry airport to protest about the eagles arrival. Irish Farming Association Hill Committee chairman Mr O'Leary said he had no doubt the eagles would take lambs.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Since their reintroduction seven eagles have been confirmed poisoned in County Kerry, two are suspected of having been poisoned and one was shot. A 13th eagle released in Kerry was shot in Northern Ireland.</p>
<p>Twenty more eagles were due for release in 2010. However, Dr Allan Mee, in charge of the sea eagle project, stated "the continuing loss of eagles to poisoning had cast a shadow over the future of the ambitious programme."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> The first white-tailed sea eagle breeding pair since 1912 nested 100 years later on Lough Derg (Loch Deirgeirt) marking a great success for the Irish reintroduction programme.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Studies of <a href="/wiki/Microsatellite_(genetics)" title="Microsatellite (genetics)" class="mw-redirect">microsatellite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> in White-tailed Eagles from North-central Europe have shown that the recovering European population has retained appreciable amounts of <a href="/wiki/Genetic_diversity" title="Genetic diversity">genetic diversity</a>, implying low risk of <a href="/wiki/Inbreeding_depression" title="Inbreeding depression">inbreeding depression</a> (a serious concern in species with low <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a>). Therefore, the recovery of this formerly endangered species is a true success story for nature conservation. The story also shows how local protection of a species can be successful, and important for preserving the species' evolutionary potential.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHailerHelanderFolkestadGanusevich2006_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHailerHelanderFolkestadGanusevich2006-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHailer2006_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHailer2006-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHailerGautschiHelander2005_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHailerGautschiHelander2005-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=White-tailed_Eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Heraldry">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Heraldry">Heraldry</span></h2>
<p>The White-tailed Eagle is believed to be the White Eagle shown in the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_Arms_of_Poland" title="Coat of Arms of Poland" class="mw-redirect">Polish Coat of Arms</a> but also in the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_Arms_of_Serbia" title="Coat of Arms of Serbia" class="mw-redirect">Serbian Coat of Arms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> The sea eagle is often blazoned holding a fish (usually a <a href="/wiki/Esox" title="Esox">pike</a>) on his talons, distinguishing him from an ordinary eagle.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=White-tailed_Eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Prehistory">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory">Prehistory</span></h2>
<p>On Orkney <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, sea eagle bones have been found in 6000 year old burial mounds, suggesting that the birds were revered by the prehistoric people there, a belief strengthened by the Pictish stone carvings of sea eagles from Orkney.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Nature_of_Scotland49_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Nature_of_Scotland49-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=White-tailed_Eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Folklore">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Folklore">Folklore</span></h2>
<p>In the Shetland Isles <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, fishermen believed that as soon as a sea eagle appeared fish would rise to the surface, belly up; this led to some fishermen using eagle fat, smeared on their bait, to increase their catch.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Nature_of_Scotland49_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Nature_of_Scotland49-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
