<p>The <b>common redshank</b> or simply <b>redshank</b> (<i>Tringa totanus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurasian</a> <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the large <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Scolopacidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Scolopacidae">Scolopacidae</a>. The genus name <i>Tringa</i> is the <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">New Latin</a> name given to the <a href="/wiki/Green_sandpiper" title="Green sandpiper">green sandpiper</a> by <a href="/wiki/Ulisse_Aldrovandi" title="Ulisse Aldrovandi">Aldrovandus</a> in 1599 based on <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>trungas</i>, a thrush-sized, white-rumped, tail-bobbing wading bird mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>. The specific <i>totanus</i> is from <i>Tótano</i>, the Italian name for this bird.<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Common redshanks in breeding <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> are a marbled brown color, slightly lighter below. In winter plumage they become somewhat lighter-toned and less patterned, being rather plain greyish-brown above and whitish below. They have red legs and a black-tipped red bill, and show white up the back and on the wings in flight.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Spotted_redshank" title="Spotted redshank">spotted redshank</a> (<i>T. erythropus</i>), which breeds in the Arctic, has a longer <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> and legs; it is almost entirely black in breeding plumage and very pale in winter. It is not a particularly close relative of the common redshank, but rather belongs to a high-<a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> lineage of largish shanks. <i>T. totanus</i> on the other hand is closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Marsh_sandpiper" title="Marsh sandpiper">marsh sandpiper</a> (<i>T. stagnatilis</i>), and closer still to the small <a href="/wiki/Wood_sandpiper" title="Wood sandpiper">wood sandpiper</a> (<i>T. glareola</i>). The ancestors of the latter and the common redshank seem to have diverged around the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a>-<a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a> boundary, about 5–6 <a href="/wiki/Million_years_ago" class="mw-redirect" title="Million years ago">million years ago</a>. These three <a href="/wiki/Subarctic" title="Subarctic">subarctic</a>- to <a href="/wiki/Temperate" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate">temperate</a>-region <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> form a group of smallish shanks with have red or yellowish legs, and in breeding plumage are generally a subdued light brown above with some darker mottling, and have somewhat diffuse small brownish spots on the breast and neck.<sup id="cite_ref-Pereira2005_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pereira2005-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_redshank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several subspecies have been identified. These include:
</p>
<ul><li><i>T. t. robusta</i> - <small>(<a href="/wiki/Eiler_Lehn_Schi%C3%B8ler" title="Eiler Lehn Schiøler">Schiøler</a>, 1919)<sup id="cite_ref-Schioler1919_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schioler1919-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></small>: found in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>.</li>
<li><i>T. t. ussuriensis</i> - <small><a href="/wiki/Sergei_Buturlin" title="Sergei Buturlin">Buturlin</a>, 1934<sup id="cite_ref-Buturlin1934_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Buturlin1934-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></small>:  found in southern Siberia, Mongolia and east Asia</li>
<li><i>T. t. terrignotae</i> - <small><a href="/wiki/Richard_Meinertzhagen" title="Richard Meinertzhagen">Meinertzhagen, R.</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Annie_Meinertzhagen" title="Annie Meinertzhagen">Meinertzhagen, A.</a>, 1926</small>: found in southern Manchuria and eastern China</li>
<li><i>T. t. craggi</i> - <small>Hale, 1971</small>: found in north west China</li>
<li><i>T. t. eurhina</i> - <small>(<a href="/wiki/Harry_C._Oberholser" title="Harry C. Oberholser">Oberholser</a>, 1900)<sup id="cite_ref-Oberholser1900_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oberholser1900-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></small>: found in Tajikistan, north India and Tibet<sup id="cite_ref-IOC_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IOC-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hale1971_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hale1971-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_redshank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common redshank is a widespread breeding bird across <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">temperate Eurasia</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> species, wintering on coasts around the Mediterranean, on the Atlantic coast of Europe from Ireland and Great Britain southwards, and in South Asia. They are uncommon <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrants</a> outside these areas; on <a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">Palau</a> in <a href="/wiki/Micronesia" title="Micronesia">Micronesia</a> for example, the species was recorded in the mid-1970s and in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiles2004_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiles2004-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tringa_totanus_MWNH_0210.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Tringa_totanus_MWNH_0210.JPG/220px-Tringa_totanus_MWNH_0210.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Tringa_totanus_MWNH_0210.JPG/330px-Tringa_totanus_MWNH_0210.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Tringa_totanus_MWNH_0210.JPG/440px-Tringa_totanus_MWNH_0210.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3328" data-file-height="2848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tringa_totanus_MWNH_0210.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>They are wary and noisy birds which will alert everything else with their loud piping call. Like most <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">waders</a>, they feed on small <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>. Redshanks will nest in any wetland, from damp meadows to saltmarsh, often at high densities.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> They lay 3–5 eggs.
</p><p>The common redshank is one of the species to which the <a href="/wiki/Agreement_on_the_Conservation_of_African-Eurasian_Migratory_Waterbirds" title="Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a> (AEWA) applies.
</p><p>It is widely distributed and quite plentiful in some regions, and thus not considered a <a href="/wiki/Threatened_species" title="Threatened species">threatened species</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BLI2008_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BLI2008-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
