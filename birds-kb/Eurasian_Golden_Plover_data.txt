<p>The <b>European Golden Plover</b> (<i>Pluvialis apricaria</i>) is a largish <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">plover</a>. This species is similar to two other golden plovers. <a href="/wiki/American_Golden_Plover" title="American Golden Plover">American Golden Plover</a>, <i>Pluvialis dominiica</i>, and <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Golden_Plover" title="Pacific Golden Plover">Pacific Golden Plover</a>, <i>Pluvialis fulva</i>, are both smaller, slimmer and relatively longer-legged than European Golden Plover, and both have grey rather than white <a href="/wiki/Axilla" title="Axilla">axillary</a> feathers (only properly visible in flight).</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Golden_Plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pluvialis_apricaria2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Pluvialis_apricaria2.jpg/220px-Pluvialis_apricaria2.jpg" width="220" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Pluvialis_apricaria2.jpg/330px-Pluvialis_apricaria2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Pluvialis_apricaria2.jpg/440px-Pluvialis_apricaria2.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pluvialis_apricaria2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Winter plumage</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Breeding adults are spotted gold and black on the crown, back and wings. Their face and neck are black with a white border; they have a black breast and a dark rump. The legs are black. In winter, the black is lost and the plover then has a yellowish face and breast and white underparts.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Golden_Plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Breeding habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding_habitat">Breeding habitat</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pluvialis_apricaria_-Spain_-flock-8a.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Pluvialis_apricaria_-Spain_-flock-8a.jpg/220px-Pluvialis_apricaria_-Spain_-flock-8a.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Pluvialis_apricaria_-Spain_-flock-8a.jpg/330px-Pluvialis_apricaria_-Spain_-flock-8a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Pluvialis_apricaria_-Spain_-flock-8a.jpg/440px-Pluvialis_apricaria_-Spain_-flock-8a.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pluvialis_apricaria_-Spain_-flock-8a.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A flock in winter plumage flying in Spain</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Their breeding habitat is moorland and tundra in the northernmost parts of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. They nest on the ground in a dry open area. They are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and winter in southern Europe and north <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. Around 500,000 birds winter in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>. Although generally common, its range has contracted somewhat in the past due to <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a>. For example, in the 19th century it disappeared as a breeding bird in <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> and only occurs there as a migrant nowadays; its breeding population in Central Europe apparently was a <a href="/wiki/Relict" title="Relict">relict</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Last_ice_age" title="Last ice age" class="mw-redirect">last ice age</a> (Tomek &amp; Bocheński, 2005).</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Golden_Plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h2>
<p>These birds forage for food on tundra, fields, beaches and tidal flats, usually by sight, although they will also feed by moonlight. They eat insects and crustaceans, also berries.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Golden_Plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Protection">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Protection">Protection</span></h2>
<p>The European Golden Plover is one of the species to which the Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>
