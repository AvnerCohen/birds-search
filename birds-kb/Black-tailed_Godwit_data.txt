<p>The <b>Black-tailed Godwit</b> (<i>Limosa limosa</i>) is a large, long-legged, long-billed <a href="/wiki/Shorebird" title="Shorebird" class="mw-redirect">shorebird</a> first described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Carolus Linnaeus</a> in 1758. It is a member of the <i>Limosa</i> genus, the <a href="/wiki/Godwit" title="Godwit">godwits</a>. There are three subspecies, all with orange head, neck and chest in breeding plumage and dull grey-brown winter coloration, and distinctive black and white wingbar at all times.</p>
<p>Its breeding range stretches from <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> through <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and areas of central <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. Black-tailed Godwits spend winter in areas as diverse as the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Subcontinent" title="Indian Subcontinent" class="mw-redirect">Indian Subcontinent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and west <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. The species breeds in <a href="/wiki/Fen" title="Fen">fens</a>, lake edges, damp meadows, moorlands and bogs and uses <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuaries</a>, swamps and floods in winter; it is more likely to be found inland and on freshwater than the similar <a href="/wiki/Bar-tailed_Godwit" title="Bar-tailed Godwit">Bar-tailed Godwit</a>. The world population is estimated to be 634,000 to 805,000 birds and is classified as <a href="/wiki/Near_Threatened" title="Near Threatened">Near Threatened</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-tailed_Godwit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The Black-tailed Godwit is a large <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> with long bill (7.5 to 12&#160;cm long), neck and legs. During the breeding season, the bill has a yellowish or orange-pink base and dark tip; the base is pink in winter. The legs are dark grey, brown or black. The sexes are similar,<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> but in breeding plumage, they can be separated by the male's brighter, more extensive orange breast, neck and head. In winter, adult Black-tailed Godwits have a uniform brown-grey breast and upperparts (in contrast to the <a href="/wiki/Bar-tailed_Godwit" title="Bar-tailed Godwit">Bar-tailed Godwit</a>'s streaked back). Juveniles have a pale orange wash to the neck and breast.<sup id="cite_ref-collins_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In flight, its bold black and white wingbar and white rump can be seen readily. When on the ground it can be difficult to separate from the similar Bar-tailed Godwit, but the Black-tailed Godwit's longer, straighter bill and longer legs are diagnostic.<sup id="cite_ref-collins_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Black-tailed Godwits are similar in body size and shape to Bar-taileds, but stand taller.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It measures 42&#160;cm from bill to tail with a wingspan of 70–82&#160;cm.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Males weigh around 280 g and females 340 g.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The female is around 5% larger than the male,<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> with a bill 12-15% longer.<sup id="cite_ref-birdwatch_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdwatch-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The most common call is a strident <i>weeka weeka weeka</i>. A study of Black-tailed Godwits in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> found a mortality rate of 37.6% in the first year of life, 32% in the second year, and 36.9% thereafter.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-tailed_Godwit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>This species was first described, as <i>Scolopax limosa</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Carolus Linnaeus</a> in 1758. Its <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">scientific name</a> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language" class="mw-redirect">Latin</a> <i>limus</i>, meaning 'mud'. The Black-tailed Godwit is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Godwit" title="Godwit">Limosa</a></i> (godwits), family <i><a href="/wiki/Scolopacidae" title="Scolopacidae" class="mw-redirect">Scolopacidae</a></i> (sandpipers) and order <i><a href="/wiki/Charadriiformes" title="Charadriiformes">Charadriiformes</a></i>, the waders.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> There are three <a href="/wiki/Subspecies_(biology)" title="Subspecies (biology)" class="mw-redirect">subspecies</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>L. limosa limosa</i>, the European Black-tailed Godwit, which breeds from western and central <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> to central <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and Asiatic <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, as far east as the <a href="/wiki/Yenisei_River" title="Yenisei River">Yenisei River</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Its head, neck and chest are pale orange.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i>L. limosa islandica</i>, the Icelandic Black-tailed Godwit, which breeds mostly in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, but also on the <a href="/wiki/Faeroe_Islands" title="Faeroe Islands" class="mw-redirect">Faeroe Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lofoten_Islands" title="Lofoten Islands" class="mw-redirect">Lofoten Islands</a>. It has a shorter bill, shorter legs and more rufous coloration extending onto the belly, compared to <i>limosa</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i>L. limosa melanuroides</i>, the Asian Black-tailed Godwit, which breeds in <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> and far eastern Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Its plumage is similar to <i>islandica</i>, but the bird is distinctly smaller.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-tailed_Godwit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Black-tailed_Goodwit_(Limosa_limosa)_near_Hodal_W_IMG_6621.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Black-tailed_Goodwit_%28Limosa_limosa%29_near_Hodal_W_IMG_6621.jpg/220px-Black-tailed_Goodwit_%28Limosa_limosa%29_near_Hodal_W_IMG_6621.jpg" width="220" height="192" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Black-tailed_Goodwit_(Limosa_limosa)_near_Hodal_W_IMG_6621.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Winter-plumaged Black-tailed Godwit, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Black-tailed Godwits have a discontinuous breeding range stretching from <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> to the far east of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Their breeding habitat is river valley <a href="/wiki/Fen" title="Fen">fens</a>, floods at the edges of large lakes, damp <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raised_bog" title="Raised bog" class="mw-redirect">raised bogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moorlands</a>. An important proportion of the European population now uses secondary habitats: lowland wet grasslands, coastal <a href="/wiki/Grazing_marsh" title="Grazing marsh">grazing marshes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pastures</a>, wet areas near fishponds or <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">sewage works</a>, and saline lagoons. Breeding can also take place in <a href="/wiki/Sugar_beet" title="Sugar beet">sugar beet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potato</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a> fields in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bin_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bin-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In spring, Black-tailed Godwits feed largely in grasslands, moving to muddy <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuaries</a> after breeding and for winter.<sup id="cite_ref-bin_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bin-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> On African wintering grounds, swamps, floods and irrigated <a href="/wiki/Paddy_field" title="Paddy field">paddy fields</a> can attract flocks of birds. In <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, inland pools, lakes and marshes are used, and occasionally brackish lakes, tidal creeks and estuaries.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Godwits from the Icelandic population winter mainly in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and the Netherlands, though some fly on to <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> and perhaps <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ibtg_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibtg-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Birds of the <i>limosa</i> subspecies from western Europe fly south to Morocco and then on to <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guinea-Bissau" title="Guinea-Bissau">Guinea-Bissau</a>. Birds from the eastern European populations migrate to <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, then on to <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Young birds from the European populations stay on in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> after their first winter and return to Europe at two years old.<sup id="cite_ref-bin_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bin-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Asian Black-tailed Godwits winter in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Limosa_limosa_(flying)-2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Limosa_limosa_%28flying%29-2.jpg/220px-Limosa_limosa_%28flying%29-2.jpg" width="220" height="142" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Limosa_limosa_(flying)-2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
In flight</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Black-tailed Godwits are much more likely to be found on inland wetlands than the more coastal <a href="/wiki/Bar-tailed_Godwit" title="Bar-tailed Godwit">Bar-tailed Godwit</a>. They <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> in flocks to western Europe, Africa, south Asia and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. Interestingly, although this species occurs in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> all year-round, they are not the same birds. The breeding birds depart in autumn, but are replaced in winter by the larger <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Icelandic</a> race. These birds occasionally appear in the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Islands" title="Aleutian Islands">Aleutian Islands</a> and, rarely, on the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_(ocean)" title="Atlantic (ocean)" class="mw-redirect">Atlantic</a> coast of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.</p>
<p>There is an estimated global population of between 634,000 to 805,000 birds and estimated range of 7,180,000 square kilometres.<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2006 <a href="/wiki/BirdLife_International" title="BirdLife International">BirdLife International</a> classified this species as Near Threatened due to a decline in numbers of around 25% in the previous 15 years.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> It is also among the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-tailed_Godwit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-tailed_Godwit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Grutto.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Grutto.jpg/220px-Grutto.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Grutto.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Displaying Black-tailed Godwit</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Limosa_limosa_MHNT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Limosa_limosa_MHNT.jpg/220px-Limosa_limosa_MHNT.jpg" width="220" height="195" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Limosa_limosa_MHNT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>Limosa limosa</i> - <a href="/wiki/MHNT" title="MHNT" class="mw-redirect">MHNT</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Black-tailed Godwits are mostly <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a>; although it was not recorded in a four-year study of 50-60 pairs, <a href="/wiki/Bigamy" title="Bigamy">bigamy</a> was considered "probably frequent".<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> A study of the Icelandic population showed that despite spending winter apart, pairs are reunited on their breeding grounds within an average of three days of each other. If one partner does not arrive on time, 'divorce' occurs.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> They nest in loose colonies. Unpaired males defend a temporary territory and perform display flights to attract a mate. Several nest scrapes are made away from the courtship territory, and are defended from other godwits. Once eggs are laid, an area of 30–50 metres around the nest is defended.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The nest is a shallow scrape on the ground, usually in short vegetation.<sup id="cite_ref-cbb_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbb-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The eggs may be hidden with vegetation by the incubating parent.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_1-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The single brood of three to six eggs, coloured olive-green to dark brown,<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_1-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> measure 55 x 37&#160;mm and weigh 39 g each (of which 6% is shell).<sup id="cite_ref-bto_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Incubation lasts 22–24 days and is performed by both parents. The young are downy and <a href="/wiki/Precocial" title="Precocial">precocial</a> and are brooded while they are small and at night during colder weather. After hatching, they are led away from the nest and may move to habitats such as sewage farms, lake edges, marshes and mudflats.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_1-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The chicks fledge after 25–30 days.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-tailed_Godwit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Food and feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_feeding">Food and feeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
<div id="ogg_player_1">

</div>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Black-tailed_Godwit.ogg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Manly Marina, SE Queensland, Australia</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><br style="clear:both" /></p>
<p>They mainly eat <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>, but also aquatic plants in winter and on migration. In the breeding season, prey includes include <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, flies, <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dragonfly" title="Dragonfly">dragonflies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mayfly" title="Mayfly">mayflies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Annelid" title="Annelid">annelid</a> worms and <a href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc" class="mw-redirect">molluscs</a>. Occasionally, fish eggs, <a href="/wiki/Frogspawn" title="Frogspawn" class="mw-redirect">frogspawn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tadpole" title="Tadpole">tadpoles</a> are eaten. In water, the most common feeding method is to probe vigorously, up to 36 times per minute, and often with the head completed submerged. On land, Black-tailed Godwits probe into soft ground and also pick prey items from the surface.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_1-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-tailed_Godwit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Relationship to humans">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_humans">Relationship to humans</span></h2>
<p>In Europe, Black-tailed Godwits are only hunted in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, with the annual total killed estimated at 6,000 to 8,000 birds. This puts additional pressure on the western European population, and the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> has a management plan in place for the species in its member states.<sup id="cite_ref-eu_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eu-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, Black-tailed Godwits were formerly much prized for the table.<sup id="cite_ref-brit_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brit-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Browne" title="Thomas Browne">Sir Thomas Browne</a> (1605–1682) said: <i>"[Godwits] were accounted the daintiest dish in England and I think, for the bignesse, of the biggest price."</i> Old names included Blackwit,<sup id="cite_ref-brit_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brit-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Whelp, Yarwhelp, Shrieker, Barker and Jadreka Snipe.<sup id="cite_ref-air_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-air-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a> name for the species is Jaðrakan.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><br style="clear:both" /></p>
