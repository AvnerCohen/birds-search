<p>The <b>Tawny Owl</b> or <b>Brown Owl</b> (<i>Strix aluco</i>) is a stocky, medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owl</a> commonly found in <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodlands</a> across much of <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>. Its underparts are pale with dark streaks, and the upperparts are either brown or grey. Several of the eleven recognised <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> have both variants. The <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a> is typically in a tree hole where it can protect its eggs and young against potential predators. This owl is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">non-migratory</a> and highly territorial. Many young birds starve if they cannot find a vacant territory once parental care ceases.</p>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal" class="mw-redirect">nocturnal</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> hunts mainly <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>, usually by dropping from a perch to seize its prey, which it swallows whole; in more urban areas its diet includes a higher proportion of birds. Vision and hearing adaptations and silent flight aid its night hunting. The Tawny is capable of catching smaller owls, but is itself vulnerable to the <a href="/wiki/Eagle_Owl" title="Eagle Owl" class="mw-redirect">Eagle Owl</a> or <a href="/wiki/Northern_Goshawk" title="Northern Goshawk">Northern Goshawk</a>.</p>
<p>Although many people believe this owl has exceptional night vision, its <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">retina</a> is no more sensitive than a human's. Rather, it is its asymmetrically placed ears that are key to its hunting because they give the Tawny Owl excellent directional hearing. Its nocturnal habits and eerie, easily imitated call, have led to a mythical association of the Tawny with bad luck and death.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Tawny_Owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The Tawny Owl is a robust bird, 37–46 cm (15–18 in) in length, with an 81–105 cm (32–41 in) wingspan. Weight can range from 385 to 800 g (0.85 to 1.8 lb).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Its large rounded head lacks ear tufts, and the facial disc surrounding the dark brown eyes is usually rather plain. The <a href="/wiki/Subspecies#Nomenclature" title="Subspecies">nominate race</a> has two <a href="/wiki/Morphotype" title="Morphotype" class="mw-redirect">morphs</a> which differ in their <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> colour, one form having rufous brown upperparts and the other greyish brown, although intermediates also occur. The underparts of both morphs are whitish and streaked with brown.<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> This species is <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexually dimorphic</a>; the female is much larger than the male, 5% longer and more than 25% heavier.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Tawny Owl flies with long glides on rounded wings, less undulating and with fewer wingbeats than other Eurasian owls, and typically at a greater height. The flight of the Tawny Owl is rather heavy and slow, particularly at its first entering on the wing. <sup id="cite_ref-BWP_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> As with most owls, its flight is silent because of its feathers' soft, furry upper surfaces and a fringe on the leading edge of the outer <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Primaries" title="Flight feather">primaries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Its size, squat shape and broad wings distinguish it from other owls found within its range; <a href="/wiki/Great_Grey_Owl" title="Great Grey Owl">Great Grey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eagle_Owl" title="Eagle Owl" class="mw-redirect">Eagle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ural_Owl" title="Ural Owl">Ural Owls</a> are similar in shape, but much larger.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>An owl's eyes are placed at the front of the head and have a field overlap of 50–70%, giving it better <a href="/wiki/Binocular_vision" title="Binocular vision">binocular vision</a> than diurnal birds of prey (overlap 30–50%).<sup id="cite_ref-Burton_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burton-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The Tawny Owl's <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">retina</a> has about 56,000 light-sensitive <a href="/wiki/Rod_cell" title="Rod cell">rod cells</a> per square millimetre (36&#160;million per square inch); although earlier claims that it could see in the <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> part of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">spectrum</a> have been dismissed,<sup id="cite_ref-Hecht_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hecht-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> it is still often said to have <a href="/wiki/Bird_vision" title="Bird vision">eyesight</a> 10 to 100 times better than humans in low-light conditions. However, the experimental basis for this claim is probably inaccurate by at least a factor of 10.<sup id="cite_ref-Martin_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The owl's actual visual acuity is only slightly greater than that of humans, and any increased sensitivity is due to optical factors rather than to greater retinal sensitivity; both humans and owl have reached the limit of resolution for the retinas of terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Martin_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Adaptations to night vision include the large size of the eye, its tubular shape, large numbers of closely packed retinal rods, and an absence of <a href="/wiki/Cone_cell" title="Cone cell">cone cells</a>, since <a href="/wiki/Rod_cell" title="Rod cell">rod cells</a> have superior light sensitivity. There are few coloured oil drops, which would reduce the light intensity.<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Unlike diurnal birds of prey, owls normally have only one <a href="/wiki/Fovea" title="Fovea" class="mw-redirect">fovea</a>, and that is poorly developed except in daytime hunters like the <a href="/wiki/Short-eared_Owl" title="Short-eared Owl">Short-eared Owl</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Burton_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burton-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Hearing is important for a nocturnal <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a>, and as with other owls, the Tawny's two ear openings differ in structure and are asymmetrically placed to improve directional hearing. A passage through the skull links the eardrums, and small differences in the time of arrival of a sound at each ear enables its source to be pinpointed. The left ear opening is higher on the head than the larger right ear and tilts downward, improving sensitivity to sounds from below.<sup id="cite_ref-Burton_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burton-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Both ear openings are hidden under the facial disk feathers, which are structurally specialized to be transparent to sound, and are supported by a movable fold of skin (the pre-aural flap).<sup id="cite_ref-Voous_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voous-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

</div>
</div>
<p>The internal structure of the ear, which has large numbers of auditory <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neurons</a>, gives an improved ability to detect low-frequency sounds at a distance, which could include rustling made by prey moving in vegetation.<sup id="cite_ref-Voous_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voous-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The Tawny Owl's hearing is ten times better than a human's,<sup id="cite_ref-Voous_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voous-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> and it can hunt using this sense alone in the dark of a woodland on an overcast night, but the patter of raindrops makes it difficult to detect faint sounds, and prolonged wet weather can lead to starvation if the owl cannot hunt effectively.<sup id="cite_ref-Burton_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burton-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The commonly heard contact call is a shrill, <i>kew-wick</i> but the male has a quavering advertising song <i>hoo...ho, ho, hoo-hoo-hoo-hoo</i>. <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> used this owl's song in <i><a href="/wiki/Love%27s_Labour%27s_Lost" title="Love's Labour's Lost">Love's Labour's Lost</a></i> (Act 5, Scene 2) as "Then nightly sings the staring owl, Tu-whit; Tu-who, a merry note, While greasy Joan doth keel the pot", but this <a href="/wiki/Stereotype" title="Stereotype">stereotypical</a> call is actually a duet, with the female making the <i>kew-wick</i> sound, and the male responding <i>hooo</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The call is easily imitated by blowing into cupped hands through slightly parted thumbs, and a study in <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a> found that this <a href="/wiki/Mimicry" title="Mimicry">mimicry</a> produced a response from the owl within 30&#160;minutes in 94% of trials.<sup id="cite_ref-Waterton_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Waterton-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> A male’s response to a broadcast song appears to be indicative of his health and vigour; owls with higher blood parasite loads use fewer high frequencies and a more limited range of frequencies in their responses to an apparent intruder.<sup id="cite_ref-Redpath_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Redpath-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Tawny_Owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geographical variation">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Geographical_variation">Geographical variation</span></h3>
<p>Although both colour <a href="/wiki/Morphotype" title="Morphotype" class="mw-redirect">morphs</a> occur in much of the European range, brown birds predominate in the more humid climate of western Europe, with the grey phase becoming more common further east; in the northernmost regions, all the owls are a cold-grey colour. <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asian</a> subspecies have grey and white plumage, the <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North African</a> race is dark grey-brown, and <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South</a> and East Asian birds have barred, not striped, underparts, and fine lines around the facial disc. The Siberian and <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> subspecies are 12% larger and 40% heavier, and have 13% longer wings than western European birds,<sup id="cite_ref-Voous_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voous-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> in accordance with <a href="/wiki/Bergmann%27s_rule" title="Bergmann's rule">Bergmann's rule</a> which predicts that northern forms will typically be bigger than their southern counterparts.<sup id="cite_ref-Bergmann_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bergmann-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The plumage colour is <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genetically</a> controlled, and studies in Finland and Italy indicate that grey-morph Tawny Owls have more reproductive success, better immune resistance, and fewer <a href="/wiki/Parasitism" title="Parasitism">parasites</a> than brown birds. Although this might suggest that eventually the brown morph could disappear, the owls show no colour preference when choosing a mate, so the adverse selection pressure is reduced. There are also environmental factors involved. The Italian study showed that brown-morph birds were found in denser woodland, and in Finland, <a href="/wiki/Gloger%27s_rule" title="Gloger's rule">Gloger's rule</a> would suggest that paler birds would in any case predominate in the colder climate.<sup id="cite_ref-Brommer_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brommer-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Galeotti_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Galeotti-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Tawny_Owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tawnyowl03.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Tawnyowl03.jpg/170px-Tawnyowl03.jpg" width="170" height="298" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tawnyowl03.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
An individual probably of the western subspecies <i>S. a. sylvatica</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_naturae" title="Systema naturae" class="mw-redirect">Systema naturae</a></i> in 1758 under its current scientific name.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial</a> derives from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>strix</i> "owl" and <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> <i>allocco</i>, "Tawny Owl" (from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>ulucus</i> "screech-owl").<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Tawny Owl is a member of the wood-owl genus <i><a href="/wiki/Strix_(genus)" title="Strix (genus)">Strix</a></i>, part of the <a href="/wiki/True_owl" title="True owl">typical owl</a> family Strigidae, which contains all species of owl other than the <a href="/wiki/Tytonidae" title="Tytonidae">barn owls</a>. Within its genus, the Tawny's closest relatives are <a href="/wiki/Hume%27s_Owl" title="Hume's Owl">Hume's Owl</a>, <i>Strix butleri</i>, (formerly considered to be <a href="/wiki/Conspecificity" title="Conspecificity">conspecific</a>), its larger northern neighbour, the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Owl" title="Ural Owl">Ural Owl</a>, <i>S. uralensis</i>, and the <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> <a href="/wiki/Barred_Owl" title="Barred Owl">Barred Owl</a>, <i>S. varia</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Voous_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voous-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Early_Pleistocene" title="Early Pleistocene">Early</a>–<a href="/wiki/Middle_Pleistocene" title="Middle Pleistocene">Middle Pleistocene</a> <i>Strix intermedia</i> is sometimes considered a <a href="/wiki/Paleosubspecies" title="Paleosubspecies" class="mw-redirect">paleosubspecies</a> of the Tawny Owl, which would make it that species' immediate ancestor.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Tawny Owl subspecies are often poorly differentiated, and may be at a flexible stage of subspecies formation with features related to the ambient temperature, the colour tone of the local habitat, and the size of available prey. Consequently, various authors have described between 10 and 15 subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-Voous_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voous-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The currently recognised subspecies are listed below.<sup id="cite_ref-WOT_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WOT-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Tawny_Owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Epping_Forest_3.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Epping_Forest_3.JPG/220px-Epping_Forest_3.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Epping_Forest_3.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Ancient deciduous woodland is a favoured habitat.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Tawny Owl has a distribution stretching discontinuously across temperate Eurasia from Great Britain and the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> eastwards to <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, and south to Iran and the Himalayas. The subspecies <i>S. a. mauritanica</i> extends the range into northwest Africa. This essentially <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">non-migratory</a> owl is absent from <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, and only a rare vagrant to the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species is found in <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> and mixed forests, and sometimes mature <a href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer" class="mw-redirect">conifer</a> plantations, preferring locations with access to water. Cemeteries, gardens and parks have allowed it to spread into urban areas, including central <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>. The Tawny Owl is mainly a lowland bird in the colder parts of its range, but breeds to 550 metres (1,800&#160;ft) in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, 1,600&#160;m (5,250&#160;ft) in the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>, 2,350&#160;m (7,700&#160;ft) in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> and up to 2,800&#160;m (9,180&#160;ft) in <a href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">Burma</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Voous_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voous-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Tawny Owl has a geographical range of at least 10&#160;million&#160;km² (3.8&#160;million&#160;mi²) and a large population including an estimated 970,000–2,000,000 individuals in Europe alone. Population trends have not been quantified, but there is evidence of an overall increase. This owl is not believed to meet the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> criterion of declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations and is therefore evaluated as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> This species has expanded its range in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, and populations are stable or increasing in most European countries. Declines have occurred in <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Tawny_Owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Tawny_Owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Strix_aluco_3young.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Strix_aluco_3young.jpg/220px-Strix_aluco_3young.jpg" width="220" height="156" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Strix_aluco_3young.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
The young leave the nest before fledging.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Tawny Owls pair off from the age of one year, and stay together in a usually <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a> relationship for life. An established pair's territory is defended year-round and maintained with little, if any, boundary change from year to year. The pair sit in cover on a branch close to a tree trunk during the day, and usually roost separately from July to October.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Roosting owls may be discovered and "mobbed" by small birds during the day, but they normally ignore the disturbance.<sup id="cite_ref-Voous_11-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voous-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Tawny Owl typically nests in a hole in a tree, but will also use old <a href="/wiki/European_Magpie" title="European Magpie" class="mw-redirect">European Magpie</a> nests, <a href="/wiki/Squirrel" title="Squirrel">squirrel</a> <a href="/wiki/Nest#Names_of_nests" title="Nest">dreys</a> or holes in buildings, and readily takes to <a href="/wiki/Nest_box" title="Nest box">nest boxes</a>. It nests from February onwards in the south of its range, but rarely before mid-March in <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The glossy white eggs are 48&#160;x&#160;39&#160;mm (1.89&#160;x&#160;1.54&#160;in) in size and weigh 39.0&#160;g (1.4&#160;oz) of which 7% is shell. The typical clutch of two or three eggs is incubated by the female alone for 30&#160;days to hatching, and the <a href="/wiki/Altricial" title="Altricial">altricial</a>, downy chicks <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> in a further 35–39&#160;days.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The young usually leave the nest up to ten days before fledging, and hide on nearby branches.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species is fearless in defence of its nest and young, and, like other <i><a href="/wiki/Strix_(genus)" title="Strix (genus)">Strix</a></i> owls, strikes for the intruder's head with its sharp talons. Because its flight is silent, it may not be detected until it is too late to avoid the danger. Dogs, cats and humans may be assaulted, sometimes without provocation.<sup id="cite_ref-Voous_11-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voous-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Perhaps the best-known victim of the Tawny Owl's fierce attack was the renowned bird photographer <a href="/wiki/Eric_Hosking" title="Eric Hosking">Eric Hosking</a>, who lost an eye when struck by a bird he was attempting to photograph near its nest. He later called his autobiography <i>An Eye for a Bird</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hosking_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hosking-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The parents care for young birds for two or three months after they fledge, but from August to November the juveniles disperse to find a territory of their own to occupy. If they fail to find a vacant territory, they usually starve.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The juvenile survival rate is unknown, but the annual survival rate for adults is 76.8%. The typical lifespan is five years,<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> but an age of over 18 years has been recorded for a wild Tawny Owl, and of over 27&#160;years for a captive bird.<sup id="cite_ref-Voous_11-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voous-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Predators of the Tawny Owl include large birds such as <a href="/wiki/Ural_Owl" title="Ural Owl">Ural</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eagle_Owl" title="Eagle Owl" class="mw-redirect">Eagle Owls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Goshawk" title="Northern Goshawk">Northern Goshawks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Golden_Eagle" title="Golden Eagle">Golden Eagles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Common_Buzzard" title="Common Buzzard">Common Buzzards</a>. <a href="/wiki/Pine_Marten" title="Pine Marten" class="mw-redirect">Pine Martens</a> may raid nests, especially where artificial nest boxes make the owls easy to find, and several instances have been recorded of <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Jackdaw" title="Eurasian Jackdaw" class="mw-redirect">Eurasian Jackdaws</a> building nests on top of a brooding female Tawny Owl leading to the death of the adult and chicks.<sup id="cite_ref-Voous_11-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voous-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> study showed that predation by mammals, especially <a href="/wiki/Red_Fox" title="Red Fox" class="mw-redirect">Red Foxes</a>, was an important cause of mortality in newly fledged young, with 36% dying between fledging and independence. The mortality risk increased with fledging date from 14% in April to more than 58% in June, and increasing predation of late broods may be an important selective agent for early breeding in this species.<sup id="cite_ref-Sunde_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sunde-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<p>This species is increasingly affected by <a href="/wiki/Avian_malaria" title="Avian malaria">avian malaria</a>, the incidence of which has tripled in the last 70 years, in parallel with increasing global temperatures. An increase of one degree Celsius produces a two- to three-fold increase in the rate of malaria. In 2010, the incidence in British Tawny Owls was 60&#160;percent, compared to 2–3&#160;percent in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-GaramszegI_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GaramszegI-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Tawny_Owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:R%C3%B6telmaus_I.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/R%C3%B6telmaus_I.jpg/220px-R%C3%B6telmaus_I.jpg" width="220" height="136" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>The Tawny Owl hunts almost entirely at night, watching from a perch before dropping or gliding silently down to its victim, but very occasionally it will hunt in daylight when it has young to feed. This species takes a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">prey</a>, mainly woodland <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>, but also other <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> up to the size of a young <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbit</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>. In urban areas, birds make up a larger proportion of the diet, and species as unlikely as <a href="/wiki/Mallard" title="Mallard">Mallard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black-legged_Kittiwake" title="Black-legged Kittiwake">Kittiwake</a> have been killed and eaten.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Prey is typically swallowed whole, with indigestible parts regurgitated as <a href="/wiki/Pellet_(ornithology)" title="Pellet (ornithology)">pellets</a>. These are medium-sized and grey, consisting mainly of rodent fur and often with bones protruding, and are found in groups under trees used for roosting or nesting.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Less powerful woodland owls such as the <a href="/wiki/Little_Owl" title="Little Owl">Little Owl</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Long-eared_Owl" title="Long-eared Owl">Long-eared Owl</a> cannot usually co-exist with the stronger Tawny, which may take them as food items, and are found in different habitats; in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> the absence of the Tawny allowed the Long-eared to become the dominant owl. Similarly, where the Tawny Owl has moved into built-up areas, it tends to displace <a href="/wiki/Barn_Owl" title="Barn Owl">Barn Owls</a> from their traditional nesting sites in buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-Voous_11-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voous-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Tawny_Owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Strix_aluco.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Strix_aluco.jpg/220px-Strix_aluco.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Strix_aluco.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A grey individual, probably subspecies <i>S. a. aluco</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Tawny Owl, like its relatives, has often been seen as an omen of bad luck; <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> used it as such in <i><a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar_(play)" title="Julius Caesar (play)">Julius Caesar</a></i> (Act 1 Scene 3): "And yesterday the bird of night did sit/ Even at noon-day upon the market-place/ Hooting and shrieking." <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a> is quoted as saying "Whatever wise people may say of them, I at least have found the owl's cry always prophetic of mischief to me".<sup id="cite_ref-Armstrong_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Armstrong-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Wordsworth described the technique for calling an owl in his poem <i>There was a Boy</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wordsworth_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wordsworth-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>And there, with fingers interwoven, both hands<br />
Pressed closely palm to palm and to his mouth<br />
Uplifted, he, as through an instrument,<br />
Blew mimic hootings to the silent owls,<br />
That they might answer him.--And they would shout<br />
Across the watery vale, and shout again,<br />
Responsive to his call,--with quivering peals,<br />
And long halloos, and screams, and echoes loud<br />
Redoubled and redoubled; concourse wild<br />
Of jocund din!</p>
</blockquote>
