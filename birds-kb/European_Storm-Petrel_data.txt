<p>The <b>European Storm Petrel</b> or <b>Storm Petrel</b> (<i>Hydrobates pelagicus</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Storm-petrel" title="Storm-petrel" class="mw-redirect">storm-petrel</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a>, Hydrobatidae, part of the <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> order <a href="/wiki/Procellariiformes" title="Procellariiformes">Procellariiformes</a>. It is the <a href="/wiki/Monotypic_taxon" title="Monotypic taxon">only member</a> of the genus <i>Hydrobates</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Storm_Petrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The Storm Petrel is a small <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a>, only the size of a <a href="/wiki/House_Martin" title="House Martin" class="mw-redirect">House Martin</a>, which it superficially resembles with its dark <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> and white rump. It is 15–16&#160;cm in length with a 38–42&#160;cm wingspan. It can be distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Leach%27s_Storm_Petrel" title="Leach's Storm Petrel">Leach's Storm Petrel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wilson%27s_Storm_Petrel" title="Wilson's Storm Petrel">Wilson's Storm Petrel</a> by its smaller size, different rump pattern and flight behaviour.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Storm_Petrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<p>It breeds on inaccessible islands in the north <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a>, with the core population in western <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, northwest <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, where the worldwide biggest colony breeds on the island of <a href="/wiki/N%C3%B3lsoy" title="Nólsoy">Nólsoy</a>. The largest colony in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a> is found in <a href="/wiki/Filfla" title="Filfla">Filfla</a> island <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a> were there is up to 5,000 pairs It is strictly pelagic outside the breeding season, and this, together with its remote breeding sites, makes Storm Petrel a difficult bird to see from land. Only in Atlantic storms might this species be pushed into the headlands of south-western <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and England.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Storm_Petrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Storm_Petrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<p>It nests in colonies close to the sea in burrows or rock crevices. It lays a single white <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">egg</a>. It is strictly <a href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal" class="mw-redirect">nocturnal</a> at the breeding sites to avoid predation by <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skua" title="Skua">skuas</a>, and will even avoid coming to land on clear moonlit nights. Like most petrels, its walking ability is limited to a short shuffle to the burrow.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Storm_Petrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<p>This species has a fluttering flight, and patters on the water surface as it picks <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">planktonic</a> food items from the ocean surface. It is readily seen from ships, which it will follow. It is attracted by "chum" (a mixture of fish offal, fish oil, <a href="/wiki/Popcorn" title="Popcorn">popcorn</a> and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfide" title="Dimethyl sulfide">dimethyl sulfide</a>) used by <a href="/wiki/Birding" title="Birding" class="mw-redirect">birders</a> to lure seabirds, and an apparently empty ocean will soon fill with hundreds of these birds.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Storm_Petrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Folklore">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Folklore">Folklore</span></h2>
<p>This bird is familiar to <a href="/wiki/Sailor" title="Sailor">sailors</a>, and much folklore is associated with this <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/harbinger" class="extiw" title="wikt:harbinger">harbinger</a> of stormy weather. Its most common folk name is "Mother Carey's Chicken",<sup id="cite_ref-Cooper92_0-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper92-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mother_Carey" title="Mother Carey">Mother Carey</a> being a supernatural figure representing the sea. The birds were sometimes thought to be the souls of perished sailors.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
