<p>The <b>red-billed tropicbird</b> (<i>Phaethon aethereus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Tropicbird" title="Tropicbird">tropicbird</a>, one of three closely related species of <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> of tropical oceans. Superficially resembling a <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">tern</a> in appearance, it has mostly white plumage with some black markings on the wings and back, a black mask and, as its <a href="/wiki/Birds_of_the_World:_Recommended_English_Names" title="Birds of the World: Recommended English Names">common name</a> suggests, a red bill. Most adults have <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#tail_streamers" title="Glossary of bird terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of bird terms § tail streamers" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">tail streamers</span></a></dfn> that are about two times their body length, with those in males being generally longer than those in females. The red-billed tropicbird itself has three subspecies recognized, including the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate</a>. The subspecies <i>mesonauta</i> is distinguished from the nominate by the rosy tinge of its fresh plumage, and the subspecies <i>indicus</i> can be differentiated by its smaller size, more restricted mask, and more orange bill. This species ranges across the tropical <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_(ocean)" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic (ocean)">Atlantic</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/Pacific" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific">Pacific</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Oceans</a>. The nominate is found in the southern Atlantic Ocean, the subspecies <i>indicus</i> in the waters off of the Middle East and in the Indian Ocean, and the subspecies <i>mesonauta</i> in the eastern portions of both the Atlantic and the Pacific Oceans and in the Caribbean. It was one of the many species described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a>.
</p><p>Nesting takes place in loose <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonies</a>, as they nest a scrape found on a cliff face that is easy to take off from. A single egg is laid and is incubated by both sexes for about six weeks. Whether the egg hatches or not can be influenced by pollution and weather, although the latter has a minimal effect on whether a chick fledges or not. After a chick fledges, the parents will usually stop visiting the nest and the chick will leave. Birds of all ages feed on fish and squid, catching them by diving from the air into the water. However, the red-billed tropicbird sometimes follows surface-feeding predators. The predators will drive the prey to the surface, which are then seized by the tropicbird.
</p><p>In some areas, introduced <a href="/wiki/Black_rat" title="Black rat">black</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brown_rat" title="Brown rat">brown rats</a> raid nests for eggs and young. Cats also threaten the red-billed tropicbird. This bird is considered to be a <a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least-concern species</a> according to the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN), though populations are thought to be declining. In some places, such as Brazil and Mexico, this bird is considered to be threatened.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_etymology">Taxonomy and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-billed_tropicbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>English naturalist <a href="/wiki/Francis_Willughby" title="Francis Willughby">Francis Willughby</a> wrote about the red-billed tropicbird in the 17th century, having seen a specimen held by the Royal Society.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It was one of the many <a href="/wiki/Aves_in_the_10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="Aves in the 10th edition of Systema Naturae">bird species originally described</a> by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a> in the landmark 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and still bears its original scientific name, <i>Phaethon aethereus</i>. The genus name is derived from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>phaethon</i>, "sun" while the species name comes from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>aetherius</i>, "heavenly".<sup id="cite_ref-job90_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job90-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This bird is called the red-billed tropicbird due to its red bill and its location in the tropicbird genus. An alternative common name was "bosun bird", also spelt "boatswain bird", from the similarity of its shrill call to a <a href="/wiki/Boatswain" title="Boatswain">boatswain</a>'s whistle.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> An alternative derivation of the name is from the semblance of the tail feathers to marlin spikes.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Local names used in the West Indies include "truphit", "trophic", "white bird", "paille-en-queue", "paille-en-cul", "flèche-en-cul", and "fétu".<sup id="cite_ref-westinides_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-westinides-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In a 1945 paper, American ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Waldo_Lee_McAtee" title="Waldo Lee McAtee">Waldo Lee McAtee</a> proposed it be called the barred-backed tropicbird after its most distinguishing feature.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The red-billed tropicbird is <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basal</a> (the earliest offshoot) in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Tropicbird" title="Tropicbird">Phaethon</a></i>, the sole extant genus in the family <a href="/wiki/Phaethontidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Phaethontidae">Phaethontidae</a>, the tropicbirds. The split between this tropicbird and the other tropicbirds, the <a href="/wiki/Red-tailed_tropicbird" title="Red-tailed tropicbird">red-tailed</a> and <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_tropicbird" title="White-tailed tropicbird">white-tailed tropicbird</a>, is thought to have occurred about six million years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-KennedySpencer2004_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KennedySpencer2004-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are three subspecies, including the nominate, of this tropicbird:

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-billed_tropicbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The red-billed tropicbird measures 90 to 105&#160;cm (35 to 41&#160;in) on average,<sup id="cite_ref-Hauber2014_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hauber2014-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> which includes the 46 to 56&#160;cm (18 to 22&#160;in)-long <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#tail_streamers" title="Glossary of bird terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of bird terms § tail streamers" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">tail streamers</span></a></dfn>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Without them the tropicbird measures about 48&#160;cm (19&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Hauber2014_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hauber2014-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It has a wingspan of 99 to 106&#160;cm (39 to 42&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In overall appearance it is <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">tern</a>-like in shape.<sup id="cite_ref-Kricher_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kricher-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Its plumage is white, with black wing tips, and a back that is finely barred in black.<sup id="cite_ref-Vanner2004_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vanner2004-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> It has a black mask that extends up from just above the <a href="/wiki/Lore_(anatomy)" title="Lore (anatomy)">lores</a> to the sides of its nape, with gray <a href="/wiki/Mottle" title="Mottle">mottling</a> usually seen near the nape and hindneck. The tail has black shaft streaks, as do tail streamers. The <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#underparts" title="Glossary of bird terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of bird terms § underparts" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">underparts</span></a></dfn> are white, with some black on the outermost <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Primaries" title="Flight feather">primaries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Tertial" title="Flight feather">tertials</a> and occasionally with black markings on the flanks. The iris is blackish-brown, and the bill is red.<sup id="cite_ref-Vanner2004_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vanner2004-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The legs, base of the central toe, and parts of the outer toes are orange-yellow while the rest of the feet are black. Although the sexes are similar,<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> the males are generally larger than females,<sup id="cite_ref-NunesLeal2013_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NunesLeal2013-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> with the tail streamers being around 12&#160;cm (4.7&#160;in) longer on the male than on the female.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977179_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977179-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The subspecies of this bird can usually be distinguished by their difference in size and plumage. The subspecies <i>Phaethon aethereus mesonauta</i> can be differentiated by its slightly rosy tinge when its plumage is fresh, the bolder look of the black barring on the upper wing, and the more solid look of the black on the outer wing. The subspecies <i>P. a. indicus</i> can be distinguished by its smaller size, its smaller mask on the face, which often does not extend far behind the eye, and its more orange bill with a black cutting edge.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977179_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977179-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Red-billed_tropicbird_(Phaethon_aethereus_mesonauta)_chick.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A chick of the subspecies mesonauta can be seen with a yellow beak turning its head to look at the camera." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Red-billed_tropicbird_%28Phaethon_aethereus_mesonauta%29_chick.jpg/220px-Red-billed_tropicbird_%28Phaethon_aethereus_mesonauta%29_chick.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Red-billed_tropicbird_%28Phaethon_aethereus_mesonauta%29_chick.jpg/330px-Red-billed_tropicbird_%28Phaethon_aethereus_mesonauta%29_chick.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Red-billed_tropicbird_%28Phaethon_aethereus_mesonauta%29_chick.jpg/440px-Red-billed_tropicbird_%28Phaethon_aethereus_mesonauta%29_chick.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4384" data-file-height="2922" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Red-billed_tropicbird_(Phaethon_aethereus_mesonauta)_chick.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>P. a. mesonauta</i> chick, <a href="/wiki/Little_Tobago" title="Little Tobago">Little Tobago</a></div></div></div>
<p>When the chicks hatch, they are covered with gray down.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_10-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This down is eventually cleared in about 40 to 50 days.<sup id="cite_ref-ascensionisland_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ascensionisland-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The young chicks lack tail streamers.<sup id="cite_ref-Vanner2004_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vanner2004-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The juvenile looks similar to the adult with a mostly white crown. In the juvenile, the stripes above the eye usually are connected at the nape. The tail feathers usually have black tips or subterminal dots and without the tail streamers that are distinctive on the adult. Occasionally, a juvenile will have black markings on its flanks and under tail coverts.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_10-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The red-billed tropicbird can be differentiated from the other tropicbirds by its red bill in combination with its white tail streamers. The slightly smaller red-tailed tropicbird has red rather than white tail streamers, and the white-tailed tropicbird can be differentiated by its smaller size, black stripe along its upper wing coverts, and its yellow-orange bill.<sup id="cite_ref-ozbirdguide_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ozbirdguide-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Juvenile red-billed tropicbirds have more heavily barred upper parts than juveniles of other species.<sup id="cite_ref-Kricher_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kricher-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In flight, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_tern" title="Royal tern">royal tern</a> can be confused with the adult red-billed tropicbird but can be distinguished by the former's less direct flight pattern and its lack of tail streamers.<sup id="cite_ref-Vanner2004_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vanner2004-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This tropicbird moults once every year as an adult, following a complex basic strategy.<sup id="cite_ref-Floyd2008_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Floyd2008-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> This <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#prebasic_moult" title="Glossary of bird terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of bird terms § prebasic moult" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">prebasic moult</span></a></dfn> is completed before courtship and lasts between 19 and 29 weeks, with most being completed in 24 weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-ascensionisland_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ascensionisland-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Birds gain their adult plumage at two to three years of age.<sup id="cite_ref-Nellis_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nellis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The red-billed tropicbird usually only calls near breeding <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonies</a>, where it joins in with groups of other adults, numbering from 2 to 20, in circling above the sea and making loud, harsh <i>kreeeee-kreeeee-kri-kri-kri-kr</i> screams. If disturbed at the nest, the chicks will vocalize a loud and piercing shriek, either rasping or reeling.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_10-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-billed_tropicbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The red-billed tropicbird has the smallest range of the three tropicbird species,<sup id="cite_ref-Kricher_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kricher-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> yet it still ranges across the <a href="/wiki/Neotropics" class="mw-redirect" title="Neotropics">Neotropics</a>, as well as the tropical <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_(ocean)" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic (ocean)">Atlantic</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/Pacific" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific">Pacific</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indian_ocean" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian ocean">Indian oceans</a>. The nominate subspecies <i>Phaethon aethereus aethereus</i> breeds on islands in the Atlantic south of the equator, including <a href="/wiki/Ascension_Island" title="Ascension Island">Ascension</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Helena" title="Saint Helena">Saint Helena</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fernando_de_Noronha" title="Fernando de Noronha">Fernando de Noronha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Abrolhos_Archipelago" title="Abrolhos Archipelago">Abrolhos Archipelago</a> in Brazilian waters. It is a vagrant to the coastline of Namibia and South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-underhill02_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-underhill02-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The subspecies <i>P. a. mesonauta</i> is found in the east Atlantic, the east Pacific, and in the Caribbean.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_10-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This subspecies was restricted to the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Cape Verde Islands">Cape Verde Islands</a> in the eastern Atlantic but it has colonised the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> in the 21st century, especially <a href="/wiki/Fuerteventura" title="Fuerteventura">Fuerteventura</a> but also on other islands in that archipelago.<sup id="cite_ref-RBA_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RBA-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The Indian Ocean subspecies, <i>P. a. indicus</i> is found in waters off Pakistan, western India, southwestern Sri Lanka,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> the Horn of Africa and Arabian Peninsula.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977180_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977180-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The subspecies is also a rare but regular vagrant to <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">Seychelles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Within the West Indies, this species is most common in the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>, Virgin Islands and small islands east of Puerto Rico.<sup id="cite_ref-westinides_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-westinides-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Breeding in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Palearctic">Western Palearctic</a> occurs on the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> Islands and the <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Eles_des_Madeleines" class="mw-redirect" title="Îles des Madeleines">Îles des Madeleines</a> off <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>. In 2000, the total number of pairs there was probably less than 150.<sup id="cite_ref-birdguides_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdguides-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> In the Pacific Ocean, it breeds from the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_California" title="Gulf of California">Gulf of California</a> and <a href="/wiki/Revillagigedo_Islands" title="Revillagigedo Islands">Revillagigedo Islands</a>, Mexico in the north, to the <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_Islands" title="Galápagos Islands">Galápagos Islands</a>, Isla Plata, Ecuador and <a href="/wiki/San_Lorenzo_Island_(Peru)" title="San Lorenzo Island (Peru)">San Lorenzo Island</a>, Peru. Researchers Larry Spear and David Ainley estimated the minimum population of the Pacific at around 15,750 birds in 1995 after 15 years of field observations.<sup id="cite_ref-atsea_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atsea-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Red-billed tropicbirds disperse widely when not breeding, the juveniles more so than the adults,<sup id="cite_ref-JavedKhan2008_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JavedKhan2008-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> with birds in the Pacific reaching the <a href="/wiki/45th_parallel_north" title="45th parallel north">45th parallel north</a> off Washington State and <a href="/wiki/32nd_parallel_south" title="32nd parallel south">32nd parallel south</a> off Chile,<sup id="cite_ref-atsea_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atsea-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> with 19 records as of 2007 from Hawaii—some 4,300 kilometers (2,700&#160;mi) from Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-vanderwerf07_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vanderwerf07-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> It sometimes wanders further, including five records from <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> and two from Australia: October–December 2010 on Lord Howe Island and September 2014 on <a href="/wiki/Ashmore_and_Cartier_Islands" title="Ashmore and Cartier Islands">Ashmore Reef</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ozbirdguide_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ozbirdguide-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In July 2005, one was found in eastern <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, while another has been seen at <a href="/wiki/Matinicus_Rock_Light" title="Matinicus Rock Light">Matinicus Rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> regularly since 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-billed_tropicbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The red-billed tropicbird can reach speeds of 44 kilometers per hour (27&#160;mph) when flying out at sea,<sup id="cite_ref-Nellis_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nellis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> cruising a minimum of 30 meters (100&#160;ft) above the sea.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977180_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977180-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> It cannot stand and is not proficient at walking, and requires an unobstructed takeoff to fly from land.<sup id="cite_ref-ascensionisland_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ascensionisland-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Conversely it can lift off the sea without much effort. Its plumage is waterproof and it floats on water.<sup id="cite_ref-Nellis_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nellis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The red-billed tropicbird usually nests on isolated cliff faces,<sup id="cite_ref-Hauber2014_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hauber2014-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> in loose colonies.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_10-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It uses a simple <a href="/wiki/Scrape_nest" class="mw-redirect" title="Scrape nest">scrape nest</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Hauber2014_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hauber2014-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> located in a place it can easily take off from.<sup id="cite_ref-ascensionisland_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ascensionisland-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The age of first breeding is usually five years, although this age is variable; a three-year-old tropicbird was once seen breeding.<sup id="cite_ref-Nellis_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nellis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In some locations, breeding happens year-round, while in others, breeding occurs seasonally.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_10-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> For example, for islands in the <a href="/wiki/California_Current" title="California Current">California Current</a>, breeding starts in November or December, while it occurs year round in the Galápagos.<sup id="cite_ref-González-Zamoraetal.2017_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-González-Zamoraetal.2017-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Breeding is influenced by the availability of food, with an increase in food generally causing an increase in breeding.<sup id="cite_ref-Castillo-GuerreroGuevara-Medina2011_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Castillo-GuerreroGuevara-Medina2011-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Individually, this bird only breeds every nine to twelve months. A breeding bird usually returns to its partner and nest location from the previous breeding cycle.<sup id="cite_ref-ascensionisland_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ascensionisland-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Phaethon_aethereus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.2.7.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Phaethon_aethereus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.2.7.jpg/220px-Phaethon_aethereus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.2.7.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Phaethon_aethereus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.2.7.jpg/330px-Phaethon_aethereus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.2.7.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Phaethon_aethereus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.2.7.jpg/440px-Phaethon_aethereus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.2.7.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3496" data-file-height="3496" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Phaethon_aethereus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.2.7.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Red-billed tropicbird egg</div></div></div>
<p>Courtship and pairing usually lasts three to five weeks,<sup id="cite_ref-ascensionisland_19-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ascensionisland-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> during which this bird performs aerial courtship displays to potential mates.<sup id="cite_ref-Hauber2014_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hauber2014-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The courtship displays include flying in the air which takes the form of gliding interspersed with short periods of rapid wing-beating.<sup id="cite_ref-ascensionisland_19-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ascensionisland-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In one display, a pair glide together for 100–300 meters (330–980&#160;ft), with one bird around 30&#160;cm (12&#160;in) above the other. The upper bird bends its wings down and the lower lifts its up, so they are almost touching. The two sink to about 6 meters (20&#160;ft) above the sea before breaking off.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977181_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977181-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At nest sites, battles sometimes occur between two or more pairs before the original owners declare themselves as the owner of the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-ascensionisland_19-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ascensionisland-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Red-billed tropicbirds are aggressive at nest sites,<sup id="cite_ref-effectroofrats_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-effectroofrats-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> fighting with each other and ousting species such as <a href="/wiki/Shearwater" title="Shearwater">shearwaters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petrel" title="Petrel">petrels</a>, and white-tailed tropicbirds. They have also been recorded taking over nests of white-tailed tropicbirds and raising their young if they failed to destroy their eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-Nellis_22-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nellis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Vagrant red-billed tropicbirds have been implicated in egg loss in red-tailed tropicbird nests in Hawaii.<sup id="cite_ref-vanderwerf07_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vanderwerf07-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This tropicbird usually lays a <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> of one white buff to pale purple egg with reddish-brown spots. The egg usually measures 45 by 60 millimeters (1.8 by 2.4&#160;in) and weighs around 67 grams (2.4&#160;oz)—10% of the adult female's weight.<sup id="cite_ref-Nellis_22-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nellis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> It is incubated by both sexes for 42 to 46 days. If the egg does not survive the first few days in the nest, the female will usually lay a replacement egg.<sup id="cite_ref-Hauber2014_15-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hauber2014-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The chicks that hatch eventually fledge in about 10 to 15 weeks after hatching,<sup id="cite_ref-Castillo-GuerreroGuevara-Medina2011_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Castillo-GuerreroGuevara-Medina2011-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> although most fledge after about 80 to 90 days.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_10-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Normally, the maximum weight of the chicks is about 725 grams (1.6&#160;lb), but on years that are hotter than average, this can drop to about 600 grams (1.3&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-Castillo-GuerreroGuevara-Medina2011_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Castillo-GuerreroGuevara-Medina2011-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Born helpless and unable to move around (<a href="/wiki/Nidicolous" title="Nidicolous">nidicolous</a> and semi-<a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977178_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977178-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> the chicks are constantly brooded by the parents until they are 3 to 5 days old, when they can thermoregulate their body temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-Nellis_22-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nellis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> They grow their first feathers—scapulars—at 13–15 days, followed by primaries at 24–27 days, tail feathers at 30–35 days and are fully feathered by 55 days.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977182_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977182-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> They are attended by the parents more between the 30th and 60th days; a behavior possibly related to the greater food requirements of the chicks during those days. Semi-digested food is regurgitated and then fed to younger birds, with older birds being fed solid food.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_family_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw_family-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The parents can be seen at normal rates with the chicks up to about the 70th day, after which the attendance by the parents falls rapidly.<sup id="cite_ref-ascensionisland_19-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ascensionisland-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Chicks receive no care after fledging,<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_10-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> with only about one out of seven chicks receiving food after the 80th day, and almost no chicks are visited after about 90 days. After fledging, the chicks will leave the nest, with few remaining after about 100 days.<sup id="cite_ref-ascensionisland_19-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ascensionisland-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-billed_tropicbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although it is a poor swimmer,<sup id="cite_ref-Hauber2014_15-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hauber2014-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> the red-billed tropicbird feeds on fish and squid.<sup id="cite_ref-Vanner2004_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vanner2004-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The fish are usually small, between about 10 and 20&#160;cm (4 and 8&#160;in), although some caught are up to 30&#160;cm (12&#160;in). The aquatic prey is mostly caught by diving into the water from the air, although <a href="/wiki/Flying_fish" title="Flying fish">flying fish</a>, the preferred fish of this species, are sometimes caught while in the air.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_10-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Fish species eaten include <a href="/wiki/Pacific_thread_herring" title="Pacific thread herring">Pacific thread herring</a> (<i>Opisthonema libertate</i>), <a href="/wiki/Fodiator_acutus" title="Fodiator acutus">sharpchin flying fish</a> (<i>Fodiator acutus</i>), flying fish of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Hirundichthys" title="Hirundichthys">Hirundichthys</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Sailfin_flyingfish" class="mw-redirect" title="Sailfin flyingfish">sailfin flying fish</a> (<i>Parexocoetus brachypterus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Ornamented_flying_fish" title="Ornamented flying fish">ornamented flying fish</a> (<i>Cypselurus callopterus</i>), bigwing halfbeak (<i><a href="/wiki/Oxyporhamphus_micropterus" title="Oxyporhamphus micropterus">Oxyporhamphus micropterus</a></i>), longfin halfbeak (<i><a href="/wiki/Hemiramphus_saltator" class="mw-redirect" title="Hemiramphus saltator">Hemiramphus saltator</a></i>),<sup id="cite_ref-velarde14_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-velarde14-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> tropical two-wing flying fish (<i>Exocoetus volitans</i>), redlip blenny (<i><a href="/wiki/Ophioblennius_atlanticus" title="Ophioblennius atlanticus">Ophioblennius atlanticus</a></i>), squirrelfish (<i><a href="/wiki/Holocentrus_adscensionis" title="Holocentrus adscensionis">Holocentrus adscensionis</a></i>),<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977181_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977181-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mackerel_scad" title="Mackerel scad">mackerel scad</a> (<i>Decapterus macarellus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Shortjaw_leatherjacket" title="Shortjaw leatherjacket">shortjaw leatherjacket</a> (<i>Oligoplites refulgens</i>), and mackerels (<i><a href="/wiki/Scomber" title="Scomber">Scomber</a></i> spp.).<sup id="cite_ref-velarde14_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-velarde14-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Squid eaten include the glass squid (<i><a href="/wiki/Hyaloteuthis_pelagica" class="mw-redirect" title="Hyaloteuthis pelagica">Hyaloteuthis pelagica</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977181_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977181-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As they grow, the chicks are fed increasingly larger quantities of fish and squid by their parents,<sup id="cite_ref-ascensionisland_19-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ascensionisland-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> generally partly digested and regurgitated.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977181_37-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977181-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Most fish that the chicks are fed are below 6&#160;cm (2.4&#160;in) in length, although some fish fed to larger chicks can be up to 30&#160;cm (12&#160;in) in length.<sup id="cite_ref-Nellis_22-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nellis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species of tropicbird usually forages alone.<sup id="cite_ref-SpearAinley2005_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SpearAinley2005-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> It usually dives into waters away from the coastline, diving from the air,<sup id="cite_ref-De_GraafTilghman1985_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-De_GraafTilghman1985-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> at heights up to 40 meters (130&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-SpearAinley2005_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SpearAinley2005-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> It will usually hover over the water before diving.<sup id="cite_ref-Nellis_22-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nellis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Sometimes, this bird follows predators that feed near the surface, such as <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">dolphins</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">tuna</a>. The red-billed tropicbird will feed on the fish driven either to or above the surface by the aforementioned predators.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_10-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It usually forages in warmer waters, though does hunt in areas of cooler currents such as the Gulf of California. The species has also been recorded foraging in <a href="/wiki/Estuary#Salt_wedge" title="Estuary">salt wedge</a> estuaries.<sup id="cite_ref-velarde14_42-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-velarde14-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-billed_tropicbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The red-billed tropicbird, a bird not indigenous to <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, was displayed in error on the $50 <a href="/wiki/Bermudian_dollar" title="Bermudian dollar">Bermudian dollar</a> and was replaced in 2012 by the white-tailed tropicbird, the tropicbird that can be found in Bermuda.<sup id="cite_ref-banknoteerror_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-banknoteerror-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-billed_tropicbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Accurate assessment of red-billed tropicbird numbers is difficult due to the remote locations of nesting sites and vast areas of the sea where they might be found.<sup id="cite_ref-Nellis_22-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nellis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> This bird is considered to be a <a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least-concern species</a> according to the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>. This is due to the fact that the range, declination, and numbers of this species, although small, do not meet the criteria required to be considered a <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable species</a>. The range of this species is believed to be 86.3 million square kilometers (33.3 million sq&#160;mi), with an estimated 3,300 to 13,000 mature individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In the western Atlantic, a more precise number was given for the population there in 2000; about 4,000 to 5,000 pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-LeeWalsh-McGehee2000_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LeeWalsh-McGehee2000-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The population is declining, mainly due to human exploitation of the bird's environment and predation by invasive species,<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> such as rats. These predators have the potential to drive populations of the red-billed tropicbird into serious decline.<sup id="cite_ref-SarmentoBrito2014_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SarmentoBrito2014-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> It is estimated that this bird experienced a <a href="/wiki/Population_bottleneck" title="Population bottleneck">population bottleneck</a> about 450 to 750 years ago, likely due to exploitation by humans. This has resulted in low genetic diversity in this tropicbird, which makes the likelihood of it adapting to sudden environmental changes low.<sup id="cite_ref-NunesEfe2017_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NunesEfe2017-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> In Mexico<sup id="cite_ref-Castillo-GuerreroGuevara-Medina2011_36-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Castillo-GuerreroGuevara-Medina2011-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> and Brazil, it is considered to be threatened.<sup id="cite_ref-SarmentoBrito2014_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SarmentoBrito2014-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats_and_survival">Threats and survival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-billed_tropicbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Threats and survival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The eggs and chicks of red-billed tropicbirds are prey for both <a href="/wiki/Brown_rat" title="Brown rat">brown</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black_rat" title="Black rat">black rats</a> in places like Abrolhos Archipelago, where these rats are invasive species.<sup id="cite_ref-SarmentoBrito2014_47-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SarmentoBrito2014-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Feral_cat" title="Feral cat">Feral cats</a> are also predators of breeding tropicbirds, with the birds providing about 3% of the diet of the cats in locations such as the Caribbean island <a href="/wiki/Saba" title="Saba">Saba</a>. On Saba, the problem has only arisen since about 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-Debrot_et._al_2014_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Debrot_et._al_2014-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> On Ascension Island, the effect of the eradication of feral cats was the increase of the red-billed tropicbird population there by about 1.6% in a year.<sup id="cite_ref-RatcliffeBell2009_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RatcliffeBell2009-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> On the Galápagos Islands, the <a href="/wiki/Short-eared_owl" title="Short-eared owl">short-eared owl</a> (<i>Asio flammeus</i>) occasionally eats young birds.<sup id="cite_ref-snow65_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow65-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Toxoplasma_gondii" title="Toxoplasma gondii">Toxoplasma gondii</a></i>, an <a href="/wiki/Intracellular" class="mw-redirect" title="Intracellular">intracellular</a> <a href="/wiki/Parasite" class="mw-redirect" title="Parasite">parasite</a>, can be found in this bird. About 28% of red-billed tropicbirds produce antibodies for <i>T. gondii</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-GennariNiemeyer2016_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GennariNiemeyer2016-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Under normal conditions about 75% of the eggs hatch. Hatching success can drop to about 35% in unusually hot conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-Castillo-GuerreroGuevara-Medina2011_36-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Castillo-GuerreroGuevara-Medina2011-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Egg shell thinning, a potential cause of egg mortality, can be caused by pollutants.<sup id="cite_ref-LeeWalsh-McGehee2000_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LeeWalsh-McGehee2000-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> About 78% of chicks fledge in normal years, with that percentage only dropping slightly, to 77%, in abnormally hot years.<sup id="cite_ref-Castillo-GuerreroGuevara-Medina2011_36-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Castillo-GuerreroGuevara-Medina2011-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Most egg and chick mortality during periods of normal climate is caused by nest fights between the parents and other birds.<sup id="cite_ref-ascensionisland_19-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ascensionisland-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Breeding adults usually survive the year, with only about 18% dying every year. The young have a lower survival rate, with about 29% dying each year.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris1979_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris1979-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The lifespan of this bird is anywhere from 16 to 30 years.<sup id="cite_ref-SarmentoBrito2014_47-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SarmentoBrito2014-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
