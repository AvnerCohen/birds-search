<p>The <b>Red-billed Tropicbird,</b> <i>Phaethon aethereus,</i> also known as the <b>Boatswain Bird</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Tropicbird" title="Tropicbird">tropicbird</a>, one of three closely related <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a> of tropical oceans.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-billed_Tropicbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>The Red-billed Tropicbird occurs in the tropical <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_(ocean)" title="Atlantic (ocean)" class="mw-redirect">Atlantic</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific" class="mw-redirect">Pacific</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Oceans</a>. The Indian Ocean race, <i>P. a. indicus</i>, was at one time considered a full species, the <b>Lesser Red-billed Tropicbird</b> from <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and western <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. It breeds on tropical islands laying a single egg directly onto the ground or a cliff ledge. It disperses widely when not breeding, and sometimes wanders far, including an amazing record from <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>. One has recently been found in eastern Nova Scotia, Canada and another sighting was confirmed on Lord Howe Island near Australia in November 2010. They feed on fish and squid, but are poor swimmers. Red billed Tropicbirds have been sighted at Matinicus Rock, Maine.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-billed_Tropicbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PhaethonIndicusSmit.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/PhaethonIndicusSmit.jpg/220px-PhaethonIndicusSmit.jpg" width="220" height="133" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PhaethonIndicusSmit.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Subspecies <i>P. a. indicus</i>; illustration by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Smit" title="Joseph Smit">Joseph Smit</a>, 1898</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The adult is a slender, mainly white bird, 48&#160;cm long, excluding the central tail feathers which double the total length, and a one metre wingspan. The long wings have black markings on the flight feathers. There is black through the eye. The bill is red. Sexes are similar, although males average longer tails. Juveniles lack the tail streamers, are greyer-backed, and have a yellow bill. <i>P. a. indicus</i> has a reduced black eye stripe, and a more orange-tinted bill.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-billed_Tropicbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relations with humans">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Relations_with_humans">Relations with humans</span></h2>
<p>The Red-billed Tropicbird is set to be featured on the currency of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, despite having a negligible presence there. The bird was selected over Bermuda's national bird, the <a href="/wiki/Endemism_in_birds" title="Endemism in birds" class="mw-redirect">endemic</a> <a href="/wiki/Bermuda_Petrel" title="Bermuda Petrel">Bermuda Petrel</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_(ecology)" title="Indigenous (ecology)">native</a> <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_Tropicbird" title="White-tailed Tropicbird">White-tailed Tropicbird</a>.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.royalgazette.com/siftology.royalgazette/Article/article.jsp?articleId=7d8be4330030005&amp;sectionId=60">[1]</a> The story was carried globally by the <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">Associated Press</a>.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.google.com/hostednews/ap/article/ALeqM5jVGbxkfslWnZAVTZ7R6XIb-2tGOQD94O1K001">[2]</a></p>
