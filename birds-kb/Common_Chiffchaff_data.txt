<p>The <b>common chiffchaff</b> (<i>Phylloscopus collybita</i>), or simply the <b>chiffchaff</b>, is a common and widespread <a href="/wiki/Leaf_warbler" title="Leaf warbler">leaf warbler</a> which breeds in open woodlands throughout northern and temperate Europe and the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>.
</p><p>It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> which winters in southern and western Europe, southern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and north <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. Greenish-brown above and off-white below, it is named <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeically</a> for its simple <i>chiff-chaff</i> song. It has a number of subspecies, some of which are now treated as full species. The female builds a domed nest on or near the ground, and assumes most of the responsibility for brooding and feeding the chicks, whilst the male has little involvement in nesting, but defends <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">his territory</a> against rivals, and attacks potential predators.
</p><p>A small <a href="/wiki/Insectivore" title="Insectivore">insectivorous bird</a>, it is subject to predation by mammals, such as cats and <a href="/wiki/Mustelidae" title="Mustelidae">mustelids</a>, and birds, particularly hawks of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Accipiter" title="Accipiter">Accipiter</a></i>. Its large range and population mean that its status is secure, although one subspecies is probably extinct.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chiffchaff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">British</a> naturalist <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_White" title="Gilbert White">Gilbert White</a> was one of the first people to separate the similar-looking common chiffchaff, <a href="/wiki/Willow_warbler" title="Willow warbler">willow warbler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wood_warbler" title="Wood warbler">wood warbler</a> by their songs, as detailed in 1789 in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Natural_History_and_Antiquities_of_Selborne" title="The Natural History and Antiquities of Selborne">The Natural History and Antiquities of Selborne</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-white_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-white-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> but the common chiffchaff was first formally described as <i>Sylvia collybita</i> by <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Ornithology" title="Ornithology">ornithologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Louis_Jean_Pierre_Vieillot" title="Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot">Louis Vieillot</a> in 1817 in his <i>Nouveau Dictionnaire d'Histoire Naturelle</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-viellot_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-viellot-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Described by German zoologist <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Boie" title="Friedrich Boie">Friedrich Boie</a> in 1826, the genus <i>Phylloscopus</i> contains about 50 species of small insectivorous Old World woodland warblers which are either greenish or brown above and yellowish, white or buff below. The genus was formerly part of the <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">Old World warbler</a> family Sylvidae, but has now been split off as a separate family Phylloscopidae.<sup id="cite_ref-alstrom_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alstrom-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The chiffchaff's closest relatives, other than former subspecies, are a group of leaf warblers which similarly lack crown stripes, a yellow rump or obvious wing bars; they include the willow, Bonelli's, wood and <a href="/wiki/Plain_leaf_warbler" title="Plain leaf warbler">plain leaf warblers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An old synonym, used for the chiffchaff was <i>Phylloscopus rufus</i> (Bechstein).<sup id="cite_ref-rufus_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rufus-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common chiffchaff has three still commonly accepted subspecies, together with some from the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> which are now more often treated as full species.<sup id="cite_ref-ClementHelbig1998_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ClementHelbig1998-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sangster2002_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sangster2002-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chiffchaff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Spectrogramcollybita2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Spectrogramcollybita2.jpg/220px-Spectrogramcollybita2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Spectrogramcollybita2.jpg/330px-Spectrogramcollybita2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Spectrogramcollybita2.jpg/440px-Spectrogramcollybita2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2751" data-file-height="1841" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Spectrogramcollybita2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sketch <a href="/wiki/Spectrogram" title="Spectrogram">spectrograms</a> comparing calls of, from left to right, the subspecies <i>collybita</i>, <i>abietinus</i> and <i>tristis</i></div></div></div>
<ul><li><i>P. c. collybita</i>, the nominate form, breeds in Europe east to <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, and is described below. It mainly winters in the south of its breeding range around the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a> and in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It has been expanding its range northwards into Scandinavia since 1970 and close to the southern edge of the range of <i>P. c. abietinus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hanson_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hanson-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. c. abietinus</i> occurs in <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, and winters from southeastern Europe and northeastern Africa east to <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. It is intermediate in appearance between <i>P. c. tristis</i> and <i>P. c. collybita</i>, being grey-washed olive-green above with a pale yellow <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a>, and underparts whiter than in <i>P. c. collybita</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> but it has very similar vocalisations to the nominate subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Due to individual variation, it can be difficult to reliably separate <i>P. c. abietinus</i> and <i>P. c. collybita</i> outside their main breeding and wintering ranges.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Some common chiffchaffs in the Middle East are browner and have a more disyllabic <i>swee-hu</i> call than <i>P. c. abietinus</i>, and may belong to a poorly known taxon "<i>brevirostris</i>";<sup id="cite_ref-dubois2008_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dubois2008-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> further research is needed to clarify the affinities of this form.<sup id="cite_ref-dubois2010_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dubois2010-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. (c.) tristis</i>, the <b><a href="/wiki/Siberian_chiffchaff" title="Siberian chiffchaff">Siberian chiffchaff</a></b>, breeds in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> east of the <a href="/wiki/Pechora_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Pechora River">Pechora River</a> and winters in the lower <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It is also regularly recorded in western Europe in winter, and it is likely that the numbers involved have been underestimated due to uncertainties over identification criteria, lack of good data and recording policies (Sweden and Finland only accept trapped birds).<sup id="cite_ref-dean8_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dean8-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> It is a dull subspecies, grey or brownish above and whitish below, with little yellow in the plumage, and the buff-white supercilium is often longer than in the western subspecies. It has a higher pitched <i>suitsistsuisit</i> song and a short high-pitched <i>cheet</i> call.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It is sometimes considered to be a full species due to its distinctive plumage and vocalisations, being similar to <i>P. s. sindianus</i> in these respects.<sup id="cite_ref-Martens1982_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martens1982-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Helbig1996_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helbig1996-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Nominate <i>P. c. collybita</i> and <i>P. c. tristis</i> do not recognize each other's songs.<sup id="cite_ref-Schubert_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schubert-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MartensMeincke1989_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MartensMeincke1989-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Pending resolution of the status of <i>P. (c.) fulvescens</i>, which is found where the ranges of <i>P. c. abietinus</i> and <i>P. c. tristis</i> connect and may<sup id="cite_ref-Marova_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marova-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> or may not<sup id="cite_ref-MartensMeincke1989_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MartensMeincke1989-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> be a hybrid between these, <i>tristis</i> is maintained in <i>P. collybita</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sangster2002_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sangster2002-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Former_subspecies">Former subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chiffchaff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Former subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><i>P. ibericus</i>, the <b><a href="/wiki/Iberian_chiffchaff" title="Iberian chiffchaff">Iberian chiffchaff</a></b> is brighter, greener on the rump, and yellower below than <i>P. collybita</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and has a <i>tit-tit-tit-tswee-tswee</i> song. It was initially named <i>P. brehmii</i>, but the <a href="/wiki/Type_(biology)" title="Type (biology)">type specimen</a> of that <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxon</a> is not an Iberian chiffchaff.<sup id="cite_ref-Iberian_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Iberian-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> This species is found in <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, west of a line stretching roughly from the western <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a><sup id="cite_ref-Salomon1989_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salomon1989-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> via the mountains of central Spain to the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a>; the Iberian and common chiffchaffs co-occur in a narrow band along this line.<sup id="cite_ref-Balmori_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Balmori-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Apart from the northernmost section, the precise course of the contact zone is not well documented. A long-distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>, this species winters in western Africa. It differs from <i>P. c. collybita</i> in vocalisations,<sup id="cite_ref-Helbig1996_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helbig1996-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Salomon1989_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salomon1989-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SalomonHemim_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SalomonHemim-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> external <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Salomon1997_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salomon1997-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid_sequence" title="Nucleic acid sequence">sequences</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Helbig1996_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helbig1996-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Helbig2001_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helbig2001-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> There is  <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridization</a> in the contact zone,<sup id="cite_ref-Salomon1989_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salomon1989-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SalomonHemim_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SalomonHemim-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BB101_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB101-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> almost always between male <i>P. ibericus</i> and female <i>P. c. collybita</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-BB101_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB101-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>  and hybrids apparently show much decreased <a href="/wiki/Fitness_(biology)" title="Fitness (biology)">fitness</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-Helbig2001_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helbig2001-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  hybrid females appear to be <a href="/wiki/Infertility" title="Infertility">sterile</a> according to <a href="/wiki/Haldane%27s_Rule" class="mw-redirect" title="Haldane&#39;s Rule">Haldane's Rule</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Helbig1993_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helbig1993-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Regarding the latter aspect, the Iberian chiffchaff apparently is the oldest lineage of chiffchaffs and quite distinct from the common chiffchaff.<sup id="cite_ref-Helbig1996_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helbig1996-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lz10_pano_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Lz10_pano_2.jpg/220px-Lz10_pano_2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="93" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Lz10_pano_2.jpg/330px-Lz10_pano_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Lz10_pano_2.jpg/440px-Lz10_pano_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2591" data-file-height="1095" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lz10_pano_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Essentially the entire historic range of the extinct <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Canary_Islands_chiffchaff" title="Eastern Canary Islands chiffchaff">eastern Canary Islands chiffchaff</a> (<i>P. canariensis exsul</i>) is shown in this photo.</div></div></div>
<ul><li><i>P. canariensis</i>, the <b><a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands_chiffchaff" title="Canary Islands chiffchaff">Canary Islands chiffchaff</a></b> is a non-migratory species formerly occurring on the major Canary Islands, which is differentiated from <i>P. collybita</i> by morphology, vocalisations and genetic characteristics, and, of course, is not <a href="/wiki/Sympatric" class="mw-redirect" title="Sympatric">sympatric</a> with any other chiffchaffs. The nominate western subspecies <i>P. c. canariensis</i> of <a href="/wiki/El_Hierro" title="El Hierro">El Hierro</a>, <a href="/wiki/La_Palma" title="La Palma">La Palma</a>, <a href="/wiki/La_Gomera" title="La Gomera">La Gomera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tenerife" title="Tenerife">Tenerife</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gran_Canaria" title="Gran Canaria">Gran Canaria</a> is smaller than common chiffchaff, and has shorter, rounder wings.<sup id="cite_ref-Helbig1996_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helbig1996-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It is olive-brown above and has a buff breast and flanks;<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> it has a rich deep <i>chip-cheep-cheep-chip-chip-cheep</i> song, and a call similar to the nominate race.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The eastern <i><a href="/wiki/Eastern_Canary_Islands_chiffchaff" title="Eastern Canary Islands chiffchaff">P. c. exsul</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Lanzarote" title="Lanzarote">Lanzarote</a> and possibly <a href="/wiki/Fuerteventura" title="Fuerteventura">Fuerteventura</a> is paler above and less rufous below than its western relative,<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and had a harsher call;<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> it might have been a distinct species,<sup id="cite_ref-Sangster2002_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sangster2002-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> but it became <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinct</a> in 1986 at latest, probably much earlier. The reasons for its extinction are unclear, but it appears always to have been scarce and localised, occurring only in the Haria Valley of Lanzarote.<sup id="cite_ref-Simms_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simms-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. sindianus</i>, the <b><a href="/wiki/Mountain_chiffchaff" title="Mountain chiffchaff">mountain chiffchaff</a></b>, is found in the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> (<i>P. s. lorenzii</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> (<i>P. s. sindianus</i>), and is an altitudinal migrant, moving to lower levels in winter. The nominate subspecies is similar to <i>P. c. tristis</i>, but with a finer darker bill, browner upperparts and buff flanks; its song is almost identical to <i>P. collybita</i>, but the call is a weak <i>psew</i>. <i>P. s. lorenzii</i> is warmer and darker brown than the nominate race;<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> it is sympatric with common chiffchaff in a small area in the Western Caucasus, but interbreeding occurs rarely, if ever.<sup id="cite_ref-Martens1982_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martens1982-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The mountain chiffchaff differs from <i>tristis</i> in vocalisations,<sup id="cite_ref-Martens1982_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martens1982-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MartensHänel_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MartensHänel-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> external <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Cramp1992_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cramp1992-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid_sequence" title="Nucleic acid sequence">sequences</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Helbig1996_15-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helbig1996-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Its two subspecies appear to be distinct vocally,<sup id="cite_ref-Martens1982_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martens1982-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and also show some difference in mtDNA sequences;<sup id="cite_ref-Helbig1996_15-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helbig1996-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> they are maintained at subspecies rank pending further research.<sup id="cite_ref-Sangster2002_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sangster2002-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chiffchaff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The common chiffchaff's <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> name is onomatopoeic, referring to the repetitive <i>chiff-chaff</i> song of the European subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> There are similar names in some other European languages, such as the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> <i lang="nl" title="Dutch language text">tjiftjaf</i>, the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i lang="de" title="German language text">Zilpzalp</i>, <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> <i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">siff-saff</i> and  <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a> <i lang="fi" title="Finnish language text">tiltaltti</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> is of <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> origin; <i>Phylloscopus</i> comes from <i><i lang="el-Latn" title="Greek-language romanization">phyllon</i></i>/<span lang="el" title="Greek language text">φυλλον</span>, "leaf", and <i><i lang="el-Latn" title="Greek-language romanization">skopeo</i></i>/<span lang="el" title="Greek language text">σκοπεω</span>, "to look at" or "to see",<sup id="cite_ref-Terres_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Terres-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> since this genus comprises species that spend much of their time feeding in trees, while <i>collybita</i> is a corruption of <i>kollubistes</i>, "money changer", the song being likened to the jingling of coins.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chiffchaff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common chiffchaff is a small, dumpy, 10–12&#160;centimetres (4&#160;in) long leaf warbler.  The male weighs 7–8&#160;grammes (0.28–0.31&#160;oz), and the female 6–7&#160;grammes (0.25–0.28&#160;oz). The spring adult of the western <a href="/wiki/Subspecies#Nomenclature" title="Subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> <i> P. c. collybita</i> has brown-washed dull green upperparts, off-white underparts becoming yellowish on the flanks, and a short whitish <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a>. It has dark legs, a fine dark bill, and short <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Primary_extension" title="Flight feather">primary projection</a> (extension of the <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">flight feathers</a> beyond the folded wing). As the plumage wears, it gets duller and browner, and the yellow on the flanks tends to be lost, but after the breeding season there is a prolonged complete <a href="/wiki/Moulting" title="Moulting">moult</a> before migration.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The newly fledged juvenile is browner above than the adult, with yellow-white underparts, but moults about 10 weeks after acquiring its first plumage. After moulting, both the adult and the juvenile have brighter and greener upperparts and a paler supercilium.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Phylloscopus_collybita.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Phylloscopus_collybita.jpg/220px-Phylloscopus_collybita.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Phylloscopus_collybita.jpg/330px-Phylloscopus_collybita.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Phylloscopus_collybita.jpg/440px-Phylloscopus_collybita.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Phylloscopus_collybita.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nominate subspecies <br /><i>P. c. collybita</i></div></div></div>
<p>This warbler gets its name from its simple distinctive song, a repetitive cheerful <i>chiff-chaff</i>. This song is one of the first <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">avian</a> signs that spring has returned. Its call is a <i>hweet</i>, less <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">disyllabic</a> than the <i>hooeet</i> of the willow warbler or <i>hu-it</i> of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Bonelli%27s_warbler" title="Western Bonelli&#39;s warbler">western Bonelli's warbler</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The song differs from that of the Iberian chiffchaff, which has a shorter <i>djup djup djup wheep wheep chittichittichiittichitta</i>. However, mixed singers occur in the hybridisation zone and elsewhere, and can be difficult to allocate to species.<sup id="cite_ref-BB101_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB101-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When not singing, the common chiffchaff can be difficult to distinguish from other leaf warblers with greenish upperparts and whitish underparts, particularly the willow warbler. However, that species has a longer primary projection, a sleeker, brighter appearance and generally pale legs. Bonelli's warbler (<i>P. bonelli)</i> might be confused with the common chiffchaff subspecies <i>tristis</i>, but it has a plain face and green in the wings.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The common chiffchaff also has rounded wings in flight, and a diagnostic tail movement consisting of a dip, then sidewards wag, that distinguishes it from other <i>Phylloscopus</i> warblers<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> and gives rise to the name "tailwagger" in India.<sup id="cite_ref-Simms_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simms-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Perhaps the greatest challenge is distinguishing non-singing birds of the nominate subspecies from Iberian chiffchaff in the field. In <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, all accepted records of vagrant Iberian chiffchaffs relate to singing males.<sup id="cite_ref-BB101_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB101-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chiffchaff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common chiffchaff breeds across Europe and Asia east to eastern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> and north to about 70°N, with isolated populations in northwest Africa, northern and western <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and northwestern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It is migratory, but it is one of the first <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> birds to return to its breeding areas in the spring and among the last to leave in late autumn.<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BWP_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> When breeding, it is a bird of open woodlands with some taller trees and ground cover for nesting purposes. These trees are typically at least 5&#160;metres (16&#160;ft) high, with undergrowth that is an open, poor to medium mix of grasses, <a href="/wiki/Bracken" title="Bracken">bracken</a>, nettles or similar plants. Its breeding habitat is quite specific, and even near relatives do not share it; for example, the <a href="/wiki/Willow_warbler" title="Willow warbler">willow warbler</a> (<i>P. trochilus</i>) prefers younger trees, while the <a href="/wiki/Wood_warbler" title="Wood warbler">wood warbler</a> (<i>P. sibilatrix</i>) prefers less undergrowth.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In winter, the common chiffchaff uses a wider range of habitats including scrub, and is not so dependent on trees. It is often found near water, unlike the willow warbler which tolerates drier habitats. There is an increasing tendency to winter in western Europe well north of the traditional areas, especially in coastal southern <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and the mild urban <a href="/wiki/Microclimate" title="Microclimate">microclimate</a> of <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> These overwintering common chiffchaffs include some visitors of the eastern subspecies <i>abietinus</i> and <i>tristis</i>, so they are certainly not all birds which have bred locally, although some undoubtedly are.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chiffchaff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Territory">Territory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chiffchaff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Territory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Siberian_Chiffchaff_I_IMG_9454.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Siberian_Chiffchaff_I_IMG_9454.jpg/170px-Siberian_Chiffchaff_I_IMG_9454.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="195" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Siberian_Chiffchaff_I_IMG_9454.jpg/255px-Siberian_Chiffchaff_I_IMG_9454.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Siberian_Chiffchaff_I_IMG_9454.jpg/340px-Siberian_Chiffchaff_I_IMG_9454.jpg 2x" data-file-width="435" data-file-height="500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Siberian_Chiffchaff_I_IMG_9454.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Siberian chiffchaff near <a href="/wiki/Hodal" title="Hodal">Hodal</a>, India</div></div></div>
<p>The male common chiffchaff is highly territorial during the breeding season, with a core territory typically 20&#160;metres (66&#160;ft) across, which is fiercely defended against other males. Other small birds may also be attacked. The male is inquisitive and fearless, attacking even dangerous predators like the <a href="/wiki/Stoat" title="Stoat">stoat</a> if they approach the nest, as well as egg-thieves like the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_jay" title="Eurasian jay">Eurasian jay</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> His song, given from a favoured prominent vantage point, appears to be used to advertise an established territory and contact the female, rather than as a paternity guard strategy.<sup id="cite_ref-Rodrigues_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rodrigues-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Beyond the core territory, there is a larger feeding range which is variable in size, but typically ten or more times the area of the breeding territory. It is believed that the female has a larger feeding range than the male.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> After breeding has finished, this species abandons its territory, and may join small flocks including other warblers prior to migration.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_34-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chiffchaff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Phylloscopus_collybita_MWNH_2266.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Phylloscopus_collybita_MWNH_2266.JPG/220px-Phylloscopus_collybita_MWNH_2266.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="185" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Phylloscopus_collybita_MWNH_2266.JPG/330px-Phylloscopus_collybita_MWNH_2266.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Phylloscopus_collybita_MWNH_2266.JPG/440px-Phylloscopus_collybita_MWNH_2266.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3020" data-file-height="2540" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Phylloscopus_collybita_MWNH_2266.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden,_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Museum Wiesbaden, Germany">Museum Wiesbaden, Germany</a></div></div></div>
<p>The male common chiffchaff returns to its breeding territory two or three weeks before the female and immediately starts singing to establish ownership and attract a female. When a female is located, the male will use a slow butterfly-like flight as part of the courtship ritual, but once a pair-bond has been established, other females will be driven from the territory. The male has little involvement in the nesting process other than defending the territory.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The female's nest is built on or near the ground in a concealed site in brambles, nettles or other dense low vegetation. The domed nest has a side entrance, and is constructed from coarse plant material such as dead leaves and grass, with finer material used on the interior before the addition of a lining of feathers.  The typical nest is 12.5&#160;centimetres (5&#160;in) high and 11&#160;centimetres (4&#160;in) across.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The clutch is two to seven (normally five or six) cream-coloured eggs which have tiny ruddy, purple or blackish spots and are about 1.5&#160;centimetres (0.6&#160;in) long and 1.2&#160;centimetres (0.5&#160;in) across. They are incubated by the female for 13–14 days before hatching as naked, blind <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a> chicks.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The female broods and feeds the chicks for another 14–15 days until they fledge. The male rarely participates in feeding, although this sometimes occurs, especially when bad weather limits insect supplies or if the female disappears. After fledging, the young stay in the vicinity of the nest for three to four weeks, and are fed by and roost with the female, although these interactions reduce after approximately the first 14 days. In the north of the range there is only time to raise one brood, due to the short summer, but a second brood is common in central and southern areas.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although pairs stay together during the breeding season and <a href="/wiki/Polygamy" title="Polygamy">polygamy</a> is uncommon, even if the male and female return to the same site in the following year there is no apparent recognition or fidelity. Interbreeding with other species, other than those formerly considered as subspecies of <i>P. collybita</i>,  is rare, but a few examples are known of hybridisation with the willow warbler. Such hybrids give mixed songs, but the latter alone is not proof of interspecific breeding.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chiffchaff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like most Old World warblers, this small species is <a href="/wiki/Insectivore" title="Insectivore">insectivorous</a>, moving restlessly though foliage or briefly hovering. It has been recorded as taking <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, mainly <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>, from more than 50 families, along with other small and medium-sized invertebrates. It will take the eggs and <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">butterflies and moths</a>, particularly those of the <a href="/wiki/Winter_moth" title="Winter moth">winter moth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The chiffchaff has been estimated to require about one-third of its weight in insects daily, and it feeds almost continuously in the autumn to put on extra fat as fuel for the long migration flight.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators_and_threats">Predators and threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chiffchaff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Predators and threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As with most small birds, mortality in the first year of life is high, but adults aged three to four years are regularly recorded, and the record is more than seven years. Eggs, chicks and fledglings of this ground-nesting species are taken by <a href="/wiki/Stoat" title="Stoat">stoats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weasel" title="Weasel">weasels</a> and crows such as the <a href="/wiki/European_magpie" class="mw-redirect" title="European magpie">European magpie</a>, and the adults are hunted by <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">birds of prey</a>, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">sparrowhawk</a>. Small birds are also at the mercy of the weather, particularly when migrating, but also on the breeding and wintering grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common chiffchaff is occasionally a host of <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Cuckoo" title="Cuckoo">cuckoos</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common</a> and <a href="/wiki/Horsfield%27s_cuckoo" class="mw-redirect" title="Horsfield&#39;s cuckoo">Horsfield's cuckoos</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Johnsgard_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnsgard-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> but it recognises and rejects <a href="/wiki/Mimicry" title="Mimicry">non-mimetic</a> eggs and is therefore only rarely successfully brood-parasitised.<sup id="cite_ref-Moksne_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moksne-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Like other passerine birds, the common chiffchaff can also acquire intestinal <a href="/wiki/Nematode" title="Nematode">nematode</a> parasites and external ticks.<sup id="cite_ref-Cork_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cork-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jaenson_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jaenson-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The main effect of humans on this species is indirect, through woodland clearance which affects the habitat, predation by cats, and collisions with windows, buildings and cars. Only the first of these has the potential to seriously affect populations, but given the huge geographical spread of <i>P. c. abietinus</i> and <i>P. c. tristis</i>, and woodland conservation policies in the range of <i>P. c. collybita</i>, the chiffchaff's future seems assured.<sup id="cite_ref-chiffchaff_10-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chiffchaff-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chiffchaff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common chiffchaff has an enormous range, with an estimated global extent of 10&#160;million&#160;square kilometres (3.8&#160;million square miles) and a population of 60–120 million individuals in Europe alone. Although global population trends have not been quantified, the species is not believed to approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> (that is, declining more than 30 percent in ten years or three generations). For these reasons, the species is evaluated as "least concern".<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>None of the major subspecies is under threat, but <i>exsul</i>, as noted above, is probably extinct. There is a slow population increase of common chiffchaff in the Czech Republic.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The range of at least <i>P. c. collybita</i> seems to be expanding, with northward advances in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and a large population increase in <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_34-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
