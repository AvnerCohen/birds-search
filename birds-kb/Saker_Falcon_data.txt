<p>The <b>Saker Falcon</b> (<i>Falco cherrug</i>) is a very large <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon</a>. This species breeds from eastern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> eastwards across <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a>. It is mainly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> except in the southernmost parts of its range, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_peninsula" title="Arabian peninsula" class="mw-redirect">Arabian peninsula</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and western <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. During the end of the <a href="/wiki/Last_ice_age" title="Last ice age" class="mw-redirect">last ice age</a>–<a href="/wiki/Oxygen_isotope_stage" title="Oxygen isotope stage" class="mw-redirect">oxygen isotope stages</a> 3-2, some 40,000 to 10,000 years ago–it also occurred in <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> (Tomek &amp; Bocheński 2005).</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Saker_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description and systematics">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_systematics">Description and systematics</span></h2>
<p>The Saker Falcon is a large <a href="/wiki/Hierofalcon" title="Hierofalcon">hierofalcon</a>, larger than the Lanner Falcon and almost as large as <a href="/wiki/Gyrfalcon" title="Gyrfalcon">Gyrfalcon</a> at 47–55&#160;cm (18-22 inches) length with a wingspan of 105–129&#160;cm (42-50 inches). Its broad blunt wings give it a shadow similar to Gyrfalcon, but its plumage is more similar to a <a href="/wiki/Lanner_Falcon" title="Lanner Falcon">Lanner Falcon</a>'s.</p>
<p>Saker Falcons have brown upperbellies and contrasting grey flight feathers. The head and underparts are paler brown, with streaking from the breast down. <a href="/wiki/Male" title="Male">Males</a> (called <b>sakrets</b> in <a href="/wiki/Falconry" title="Falconry">falconry</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Female" title="Female">females</a> are similar, as are young birds, although these tend to be a duller brown. The call is a sharp <i>kiy-ee</i>.</p>
<p>Adults can be distinguished from the similar <a href="/wiki/Lanner_Falcon" title="Lanner Falcon">Lanner Falcon</a> since the Lanner is blue-grey above with a reddish back to the head. However, juveniles of the two species can be very similar although the Saker Falcon always has a uniformly buff top of the head with dark streaks, and a less clear pattern on the sides of the head.</p>
<p>A further complication is that some Asian birds have grey barred upperparts; these must be separated from Lanner on size, structure, and a weaker moustache stripe. Saker Falcons at the northeast edge of the range in the <a href="/wiki/Altai_Mountains" title="Altai Mountains">Altai Mountains</a> are slightly larger, and darker and more heavily spotted on the underparts than other populations. These, known as the <b><a href="/wiki/Altai_Falcon" title="Altai Falcon">Altai Falcon</a></b>, have been treated in the past either as a distinct species <i><b>"Falco altaicus"</b></i> or as a <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrid</a> between Saker Falcon and Gyrfalcon, but modern opinion (e.g. Orta 1994) is to tentatively treat it as a form of Saker Falcon, until comprehensive studies of its <a href="/wiki/Population_genetics" title="Population genetics">population genetics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> are available.</p>
<p>Unfortunately, this <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> belongs to the close-knit hierofalcon complex. In this group, there is ample evidence for rampant hybridization and incomplete <a href="/wiki/Lineage_sorting" title="Lineage sorting" class="mw-redirect">lineage sorting</a> which confounds analyses of <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">DNA sequence</a> data to a massive extent; molecular studies with small <a href="/wiki/Sample_size" title="Sample size" class="mw-redirect">sample sizes</a> can simply not be expected to yield reliable conclusions in the entire hierofalcon group. The <a href="/wiki/Radiation_(biology)" title="Radiation (biology)" class="mw-redirect">radiation</a> of the entire living diversity of hierofalcons seems to have taken place in the <a href="/wiki/Eemian_interglacial" title="Eemian interglacial" class="mw-redirect">Eemian interglacial</a> at the start of the <a href="/wiki/Late_Pleistocene" title="Late Pleistocene">Late Pleistocene</a>, a mere 130,000-115,000 years ago; the Saker Falcon represents a lineage that expanded out of northeastern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> into the interior of southeastern Europe and Asia, by way of the eastern <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a> region.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In captivity, Lanner and Saker can interbreed. For example, one such hybrid lives at the <a href="/wiki/African_Lion_Safari" title="African Lion Safari">African Lion Safari</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. <a href="/wiki/Gyrfalcon" title="Gyrfalcon">Gyrfalcon</a>-Saker hybrids are also available (see bird flu experiment described in "Ecology and status").</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungarian</a> mythological bird, the <a href="/wiki/Turul" title="Turul">Turul</a>, was probably a saker falcon (Kerecsensólyom).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Saker_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecology and status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology_and_status">Ecology and status</span></h2>
<p>The Saker Falcon is a raptor of open grasslands preferably with some trees or cliffs. It often hunts by horizontal pursuit, rather than the <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_Falcon" title="Peregrine Falcon">Peregrine's</a> stoop from a height, and feeds mainly on <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a> and birds. In Europe, <a href="/wiki/European_Ground_Squirrel" title="European Ground Squirrel" class="mw-redirect">Ground Squirrels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feral_pigeon" title="Feral pigeon" class="mw-redirect">feral pigeons</a> are the most common prey items. This species usually builds no nest of its own, but lays its 3-6 eggs in an old stick nest in a tree which was previously used by other birds such as storks, ravens or buzzards. It also often nests on cliffs.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/BirdLife_International" title="BirdLife International">BirdLife International</a> categorises this bird as <a href="/wiki/Endangered" title="Endangered" class="mw-redirect">endangered</a>, due to a rapid population decline, particularly on the central Asian breeding grounds. Every since the collapse of the Soviet Union, the United Arab Emirates has been the main destination for thousands of falcons caught and sold illegally for hefty sums at the black market. <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> is estimated to lose up to 1,000 Saker Falcons per year.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The species also faces pressure from habitat loss and destruction. The population was estimated to be between 7,200 and 8,800 mature individuals in 2004. In the United States there are several <a href="/wiki/Captive_breeding" title="Captive breeding">captive breeding</a> projects. There are currently several successful breeding projects by falconers in Canada. The most dramatic decline of the Saker falcon in Asia has been in <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a>. On the contrary, a strongly protected and relatively abundant population persist in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>.</p>
<p>Saker nests support a species-rich assemblage of <a href="/wiki/Commensal" title="Commensal" class="mw-redirect">commensal</a> insects (Merkl et al. 2004).</p>
<p>Saker Falcons are known to be very susceptible to <a href="/wiki/Avian_influenza" title="Avian influenza">avian influenza</a>, individuals having been found infected with highly pathogenic H5N1 (in <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>) and H7N7 (in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>) strains. Therefore an experiment was done with <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrid</a> <a href="/wiki/Gyrfalcon" title="Gyrfalcon">gyr</a>-saker falcons, which found that 5 falcons vaccinated with a commercial H5N2 influenza vaccine survived infection with a highly pathogenic H5N1 strain, whereas 5 unvaccinated falcons died. This means that Sakers could be protected from bird flu by vaccination, at least in captivity.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Saker_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span></h2>
<p>Saker Falcon is the national bird of <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, known as <a href="/wiki/Turul" title="Turul">Turul</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_mythology" title="Hungarian mythology">Hungarian mythology</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from November 2012">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Saker_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h3>
<p>The specific part of the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_name" title="Scientific name" class="mw-redirect">scientific name</a>, <i>cherrug</i>, comes from the <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a> name <i>charg</i> for a female Saker.<sup id="cite_ref-DSBN_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DSBN-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">common name</a> <i>Saker</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic" class="mw-redirect">Arabic</a> <i>saqr</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
