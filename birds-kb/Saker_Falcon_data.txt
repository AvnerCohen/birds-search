<p>The <b>saker falcon</b> (<i>Falco cherrug</i>) is a large species of <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon</a>. This species breeds from central Europe eastwards across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> to <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a>. It is mainly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> except in the southernmost parts of its range, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabian peninsula">Arabian peninsula</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and western <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. The saker falcon is the national bird of Hungary, United Arab Emirates, and Mongolia.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saker_falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> belongs to the close-knit hierofalcon complex. In this group, there is ample evidence for rampant hybridization and <a href="/wiki/Incomplete_lineage_sorting" title="Incomplete lineage sorting">incomplete lineage sorting</a> which confounds analyses of <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" class="mw-redirect" title="DNA sequence">DNA sequence</a> data to a massive extent; molecular studies with small <a href="/wiki/Sample_size" class="mw-redirect" title="Sample size">sample sizes</a> can simply not be expected to yield reliable conclusions in the entire hierofalcon group. The <a href="/wiki/Radiation_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Radiation (biology)">radiation</a> of the entire living diversity of hierofalcons seems to have taken place in the <a href="/wiki/Eemian_interglacial" class="mw-redirect" title="Eemian interglacial">Eemian interglacial</a> at the start of the <a href="/wiki/Late_Pleistocene" title="Late Pleistocene">Late Pleistocene</a>, a mere 130,000–115,000 years ago; the saker falcon represents a lineage that expanded out of northeastern Africa into the interior of southeastern Europe and Asia, by way of the eastern <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> region.<sup id="cite_ref-Helbig1994_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helbig1994-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wink1998_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wink1998-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wink2004_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wink2004-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Nittinger2005_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nittinger2005-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In captivity, lanners and sakers can interbreed, and <a href="/wiki/Gyrfalcon" title="Gyrfalcon">gyrfalcon</a>-saker hybrids are also available (see bird flu experiment described in "Ecology and status").
The <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific name</a>, <i>cherrug</i>, comes from the <a href="/wiki/Sindhi_language" title="Sindhi language">Sindhi</a> name <i>charg</i> for a female saker.<sup id="cite_ref-DSBN_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DSBN-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">common name</a> <i>saker</i> comes from the (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl">صقر</span>&#8206;, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Arabic" title="Romanization of Arabic">romanized</a>:&#160;</small><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Ṣaqr</i>) meaning "falcon".<sup id="cite_ref-collinsdict_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collinsdict-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Tristram1859_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tristram1859-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Saker falcons at the northeast edge of the range in the <a href="/wiki/Altai_Mountains" title="Altai Mountains">Altai Mountains</a> are slightly larger, and darker and more heavily spotted on the underparts than other populations. These, known as the <b><a href="/wiki/Altai_falcon" title="Altai falcon">Altai falcon</a></b>, have been treated in the past either as a distinct species <i><b>"Falco altaicus"</b></i> or as a <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrid</a> between saker falcon and gyrfalcon, but modern opinion (e.g. (<a href="#CITEREFOrta1994">Orta 1994</a>)) is to tentatively treat it as a form of saker falcon, until comprehensive studies of its <a href="/wiki/Population_genetics" title="Population genetics">population genetics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> are available.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saker_falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The saker falcon is a large <a href="/wiki/Hierofalcon" title="Hierofalcon">hierofalcon</a>, larger than the <a href="/wiki/Lanner_falcon" title="Lanner falcon">lanner falcon</a> and almost as large as <a href="/wiki/Gyrfalcon" title="Gyrfalcon">gyrfalcon</a> at 45–57&#160;cm (18–22&#160;in) length with a wingspan of 97–126&#160;cm (38–50&#160;in). Males weigh between 730–990&#160;g (26–35&#160;oz) and females 970–1,300&#160;g (34–46&#160;oz). It resembles a larger but browner gyrfalcon. It is larger and more heavily built than the related <a href="/wiki/Lanner_falcon" title="Lanner falcon">lanner falcon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Saker falcons tend to have variable plumage. <a href="/wiki/Male" title="Male">Males</a> and <a href="/wiki/Female" title="Female">females</a> are similar, except in size, as are young birds, although these tend to be darker and more heavily streaked. The call is a sharp <i>kiy-ee</i> or a repeated <i>kyak-kyak-kyak</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saker_falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The saker falcon is a raptor of open grasslands preferably with some trees or cliffs. It often hunts by horizontal pursuit, rather than the <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">peregrine's</a> stoop from a height, and feeds mainly on <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a> and birds. In Europe, <a href="/wiki/European_ground_squirrel" title="European ground squirrel">ground squirrels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feral_pigeon" title="Feral pigeon">feral pigeons</a> are the most common prey items. This species usually builds no nest of own, but lays its 3–6 eggs in an old stick nest in a tree which was previously used by other birds such as storks, ravens or buzzards. It also often nests on cliffs.
</p><p>Saker nests support a species-rich assemblage of <a href="/wiki/Commensal" class="mw-redirect" title="Commensal">commensal</a> insects.<sup id="cite_ref-Merkl2004_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merkl2004-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_and_conservation">Status and conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saker_falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Status and conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/BirdLife_International" title="BirdLife International">BirdLife International</a> categorises this bird as <a href="/wiki/Endangered" class="mw-redirect" title="Endangered">endangered</a>, due to a rapid population decline, particularly on the central Asian breeding grounds. Ever since the collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a> have been the main destination for thousands of falcons caught and sold illegally for hefty sums at the black market. <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> is estimated to lose up to 1,000 saker falcons per year.<sup id="cite_ref-Antelava2007_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Antelava2007-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The species also faces pressure from habitat loss and destruction. The population was estimated to be between 7,200 and 8,800 mature individuals in 2004. However, sakers live at low densities across large ranges in remote regions, making distribution status difficult to assess. A climatic niche modelling study pinpointed certain remote areas for targeted population surveys.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In the United States, Canada and Europe there are several <a href="/wiki/Captive_breeding" title="Captive breeding">captive breeding</a> projects. The most dramatic decline of the saker falcon in Asia has been in <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a>. In contrast, a strongly protected and relatively abundant population persists in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>.
</p><p>Saker falcons are known to be very susceptible to <a href="/wiki/Avian_influenza" title="Avian influenza">avian influenza</a>, individuals having been found infected with highly pathogenic H5N1 (in <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>) and H7N7 (in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>) strains. Therefore, an experiment was done with <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrid</a> <a href="/wiki/Gyrfalcon" title="Gyrfalcon">gyr</a>-saker falcons, which found that five falcons vaccinated with a commercial H5N2 influenza vaccine survived infection with a highly pathogenic H5N1 strain, whereas five unvaccinated falcons died. This means that sakers could be protected from bird flu by vaccination, at least in captivity.<sup id="cite_ref-Lierz2007_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lierz2007-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saker_falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungarian</a> mythological bird, the <a href="/wiki/Turul" title="Turul">Turul</a>, was probably a saker falcon (<i>kerecsensólyom</i>), and the saker falcon is the national bird of <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-savethesaker_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-savethesaker-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In 2012, the saker falcon was selected as the national bird of <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Disney" class="mw-redirect" title="Disney">Disney</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Mulan_(1998_film)" title="Mulan (1998 film)">Mulan</a></i>, the Hun leader Shan Yu owns a saker falcon called Hayabusa.
</p><p>In the children's book <i>Tiger Wars</i> by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Backshall" title="Steve Backshall">Steve Backshall</a>, one of the main characters is called Saker after the falcon.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_falconry">Use in falconry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saker_falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Use in falconry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The saker falcon has been used in falconry for thousands of years, and like its very close relative the <a href="/wiki/Gyrfalcon" title="Gyrfalcon">gyrfalcon</a> is a highly regarded falconry bird. Swift and powerful, it is effective against medium and large game bird species.<sup id="cite_ref-Beebe1984_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beebe1984-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In recent years it is possible that hybrids of saker falcon and <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">peregrine falcon</a> have been developed in order to provide falconers a bird with greater size and horizontal speed than the peregrine, with greater propensity for diving stoops on game than the saker.
</p>
