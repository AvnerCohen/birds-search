<p>The <b>whinchat</b> (<i>Saxicola rubetra</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> breeding in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">western Asia</a> and wintering in central <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. At one time considered to be in the thrush family, <a href="/wiki/Turdidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Turdidae">Turdidae</a>, it is now placed in the Old World flycatcher family, <a href="/wiki/Muscicapidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Muscicapidae">Muscicapidae</a>. Both sexes have a strong <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a>, brownish upper parts mottled darker, a pale throat and breast, a pale buff to whitish belly, and a blackish tail with white bases to the outer tail feathers, but in the breeding season, the male has an orange-buff throat and breast.
</p><p>The whinchat is a solitary species, favouring open grassy country with rough vegetation and scattered small shrubs. It perches in elevated locations ready to pounce on the insects and other small invertebrates that form its diet. The nest is built by the female on the ground in coarse vegetation, with a clutch of four to seven eggs being laid. The hen incubates the eggs for about thirteen days and then both parents feed the nestlings. Fledging takes place about eighteen days after hatching and the parents continue to feed the young for another fortnight. Moulting takes place in late summer before the migration southwards, and again on the wintering grounds in Africa before the migration northwards in spring. The whinchat is a common species with a wide range and the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> has classified it as being of "<a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least concern</a>".
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whinchat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species represents a fairly <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basal</a> divergence of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Saxicola" title="Saxicola">Saxicola</a></i>. It retains the <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a> found in many <a href="/wiki/Muscicapidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Muscicapidae">Muscicapidae</a> but lost in the more derived <i>Saxicola</i> species such as the <a href="/wiki/European_stonechat" title="European stonechat">European stonechat</a> or <a href="/wiki/African_stonechat" title="African stonechat">African stonechat</a> (<i>S. torquatus</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As with other species of <i>Saxicola</i>, it was formerly considered a member of the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family (Turdidae), but is now placed in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a> family Muscicapidae.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It, and similar thrush-like Muscicapidae <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, are often called <a href="/wiki/Chat_(bird)" title="Chat (bird)">chats</a>.
</p><p>The English name is derived from <i>whin</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Ulex_europaeus" title="Ulex europaeus">common gorse</a> and <i>chat</i> referring to the nature of its calls.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OED2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Its <a href="/wiki/Scientific_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific name">scientific name</a> means "small rock-dweller", in reference to its <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a>. <i>Saxicola</i> derives from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>saxum</i> ("rock") + <i>incola</i> ("dwelling"); <i>rubetra</i> is a Latin term for a small bird.<sup id="cite_ref-Urquhart_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BTO_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> No subspecies are recognised.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Very rarely, <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridisation</a> occurs between the whinchat and the <a href="/wiki/Siberian_stonechat" title="Siberian stonechat">Siberian stonechat</a> (<i>Saxicola maurus</i>), with a case being reported in Finland in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It has also been reported to hybridise with the European stonechat in western and southern Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whinchat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The whinchat is a short-tailed bird, moving on the ground with small, rapid hops and frequently bobbing and flicking its wings and tail.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> It is similar in size to its relative the <a href="/wiki/European_robin" title="European robin">European robin</a> (<i>Erithacus rubecula</i>), being 12 to 14&#160;cm (4.7 to 5.5&#160;in) long and weighing 13 to 26&#160;g (0.46 to 0.92&#160;oz). Both sexes have brownish upperparts mottled darker, a buff throat and breast, a pale buff to whitish belly, and a blackish tail with white bases to the outer tail feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-Urquhart_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The male in breeding plumage has a blackish face mask almost encircled by a strong white <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cheek" title="Cheek">malar</a> stripe, a bright orange-buff throat and breast, and small white wing patches on the greater coverts and inner median coverts. The female is duller overall, in particular having browner face mask, pale buffy-brown breast, and a buff supercilium and malar stripe, and smaller or no white wing patches. Males in immature and winter plumage are similar to females, except that adult males retain the white wing patches all year round.<sup id="cite_ref-Urquhart_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Though fairly similar to females and immatures of the <a href="/wiki/European_stonechat" title="European stonechat">European stonechat</a> (<i>S. rubicola</i>), the whinchat can readily be distinguished by its conspicuous supercilium and whiter belly, and also in western Europe, by being paler overall than the western European stonechat subspecies <i>S. rubicola hibernans</i>. It also differs structurally in being slightly slimmer and less 'dumpy', and having longer wingtips (an adaptation to its long-distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migration</a>). It is more easily confused with female or immature <a href="/wiki/Siberian_stonechat" title="Siberian stonechat">Siberian stonechat</a> (<i>S. maura</i>), which (also being a long-distance migrant) shares the longer wingtips; however, Siberian stonechat can be distinguished by its conspicuous unmarked pale orange-buff rump (in whinchat, the rump is the same mottled brown colour as the back).<sup id="cite_ref-Urquhart_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Its main call is described as a <i>hue-tac-tac</i>, the 'tac' softer and less grating than that of the European stonechat; the call is used both for contact between birds and predator alarms. The male has a whistling, crackly but soft song used during the breeding season, consisting of a mixture of soft whistles, tacs and more grating sounds; it is often <a href="/wiki/Mimetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Mimetic">mimetic</a>, including phrases from the songs of at least 12 other assorted bird species.<sup id="cite_ref-Urquhart_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It sings from a fence, bush, tree or wire, or occasionally from the ground or in flight, between about April and July.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> On the wintering grounds, it often gives alarm calls but only occasionally sings, being most likely to do so at the end of winter, when starting spring migration.<sup id="cite_ref-Urquhart_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whinchat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Whinchat_Saxicola_rubetra_Young.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Whinchat_Saxicola_rubetra_Young.jpg/220px-Whinchat_Saxicola_rubetra_Young.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Whinchat_Saxicola_rubetra_Young.jpg/330px-Whinchat_Saxicola_rubetra_Young.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Whinchat_Saxicola_rubetra_Young.jpg/440px-Whinchat_Saxicola_rubetra_Young.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1315" data-file-height="1052" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Whinchat_Saxicola_rubetra_Young.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Juvenile. Dubna, Russia</div></div></div>
<p>The whinchat is a migratory species breeding in Europe and western Asia from <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> east to the <a href="/wiki/Ob_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Ob River">Ob River</a> basin near <a href="/wiki/Novosibirsk" title="Novosibirsk">Novosibirsk</a>, and from northern <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> south to central <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, central <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus_Mountains" title="Caucasus Mountains">Caucasus Mountains</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Urquhart_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Birds arrive on the breeding grounds between the end of April and mid May, and depart between mid August and mid September (odd birds lingering to October). They winter primarily in tropical <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a> from <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> east to <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a> and south to <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a>, arriving in western Africa at the start of the <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">dry season</a> in late September to November, and leaving between February and March. Small numbers also winter in northwestern Africa in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Urquhart_7-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Available stopover sites are important for the successful migration of whinchat  between Africa and Europe, where they face the barriers of the Sahara and Mediterranean Sea.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Vagrants have reached northwest of the breeding range to <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, west on migration to the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>, and south of the wintering range to northern <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Urquhart_7-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the breeding season in the UK, at the landscape-scale, whinchats favour habitats with high plant species richness and steeper slopes.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  However, at the territory scale, low-elevation areas with a heterogeneous vegetation structure and high density of perches and tussocks are preferred.<sup id="cite_ref-Border_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Border-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Whinchats wintering in Nigeria have a large breeding range that spreads across continental Europe indicating low connectivity, arising from wide migratory dispersal, with high mixing of breeding populations during the non-breeding season.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> A lone vagrant was sighted for the first time in India by birder R. Mohammed Saleem during their Great Indian Bird Expedition SEEK2019 at Chambal National Park.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whinchat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Saxicola_rubetra_MWNH_1810.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Saxicola_rubetra_MWNH_1810.JPG/220px-Saxicola_rubetra_MWNH_1810.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="132" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Saxicola_rubetra_MWNH_1810.JPG/330px-Saxicola_rubetra_MWNH_1810.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Saxicola_rubetra_MWNH_1810.JPG/440px-Saxicola_rubetra_MWNH_1810.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4036" data-file-height="2420" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Saxicola_rubetra_MWNH_1810.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>The whinchat is a largely solitary bird though it may form small family groups in autumn.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> It favours rough low vegetation habitats such as open rough pasture or similar minimally cultivated <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a> with scattered small shrubs such as <a href="/wiki/Crataegus_monogyna" title="Crataegus monogyna">hawthorn</a> (<i>Crataegus monogyna</i>), and <a href="/wiki/Bracken" title="Bracken">bracken</a> (<i>Pteridium aquilinum</i>) or <a href="/wiki/Calluna" title="Calluna">heather</a> (<i>Calluna vulgaris</i>) stands on rock-strewn ground. It also commonly inhabits new and clear-felled conifer <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantations</a> until the new tree crop is about five to six years old and a metre or two tall. It always needs at least a few perching points (shrubs, tall weeds, or fence posts) to scan from for food and for use as song posts.<sup id="cite_ref-Urquhart_7-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Breeding takes place in late April and May. The nest is built solely by the female, and is made of dried grasses and moss, and lined with hairs and fine <a href="/wiki/Agrostis" title="Agrostis">bents</a>. It is built on the ground, hidden in dense low vegetation, often at the foot of a bush.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The female lays and <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation" title="Egg incubation">incubates</a> a clutch of four to seven eggs which hatch after eleven to fourteen days. Both parents bring food to the young which leave the nest ten to fourteen days later, while still too young to fly. The chicks <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> at seventeen to nineteen days after hatching and remain largely dependent on the parents for a further two weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-Urquhart_7-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Whinchats are short-lived, typically only surviving two years, to a maximum recorded of just over five years in the wild; breeding starts when birds are a year old.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Predators include <a href="/wiki/Least_weasel" title="Least weasel">weasels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stoat" title="Stoat">stoats</a>, and small <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptors</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Merlin_(bird)" title="Merlin (bird)">merlin</a> and nest predators such as <a href="/wiki/Corvidae" title="Corvidae">crows and magpies</a>. Nests are also lost due to agricultural operations such as <a href="/wiki/Silage" title="Silage">silage</a> cutting (the main factor in the species' decline in western Europe)<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> or trampling by livestock, and are sometimes parasitised by the <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common cuckoo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Urquhart_7-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Whinchats are <a href="/wiki/Insectivore" title="Insectivore">insectivorous</a>, feeding largely (about 80–90%) on <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, but also consume a wide range of other <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> including <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a> and <a href="/wiki/Worm" title="Worm">worms</a>. They also eat small amounts of <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Blackberry" title="Blackberry">blackberries</a>, primarily in autumn. The birds like to perch on elevated spots such as shrubs, from where they make sallies to catch insects, mostly taken off the ground, but also flying insects. While so perched, males in particular frequently flick their tail and sometimes their wings to show the white tail and wing flashes, for display or territorial communication signals to other whinchats.<sup id="cite_ref-Urquhart_7-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Adult whinchats have a single complex complete <a href="/wiki/Moulting" title="Moulting">moult</a> in late summer (late July onward) after breeding and mainly completed before southbound migration. Juveniles have a partial moult at the same time, growing new body feathering but retaining the flight feathers. All ages also have a partial moult in early spring on the wintering grounds before northbound migration.<sup id="cite_ref-Urquhart_7-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_and_conservation">Status and conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whinchat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Status and conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fairly common across its wide range, the whinchat is classified as a species of "<a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least concern</a>" by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Some populations are however in serious decline, particularly in the west of its range in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, primarily due to <a href="/wiki/Intensive_farming" title="Intensive farming">agricultural intensification</a>. In Britain it is amber-listed with an unfavourable conservation status; it had all but disappeared from former lowland breeding areas in the south and east between surveys in 1968–72 and 1988–91, remaining common only in upland areas of the north and west where low-intensity <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> rearing is the main land use.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gibbons_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbons-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> There has been a similar decline in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, where it is now classified as "rare".<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
