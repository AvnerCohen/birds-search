<p>The <b>American golden plover</b> (<i>Pluvialis dominica</i>) is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">plover</a>. The genus name is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means relating to rain, from <i>pluvia</i>, "rain".  It was believed that golden plovers flocked when rain was imminent.  The species name <i>dominica</i> refers to Santo Domingo, now <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_golden_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Americangoldie39.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Americangoldie39.jpg/220px-Americangoldie39.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="80" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Americangoldie39.jpg/330px-Americangoldie39.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Americangoldie39.jpg/440px-Americangoldie39.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2670" data-file-height="974" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Americangoldie39.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>American golden plover taking flight, showing its dusky back and axillaries</div></div></div>
<p><b>Measurements</b>:<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Length</b>: 9.4-11.0 in (24-28 cm)</li>
<li><b>Weight</b>: 4.3-6.8 oz (122-194 g)</li>
<li><b>Wingspan</b>: 25.6-26.4 in (65-67 cm)</li></ul>
<p>The breeding adult American golden plover has a black face, neck, breast, and belly, with a white crown and nape that extends to the side of the breast. The back is mottled black and white with pale, gold spots. The breeding female is similar, but with less black. When in <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#winter_plumage" title="Glossary of bird terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of bird terms § winter plumage" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">winter plumage</span></a></dfn>, both sexes have grey-brown upperparts, pale grey-brown underparts, and a whitish eyebrow. The head is small, along with the bill.<sup id="cite_ref-Vanner2004_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vanner2004-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is similar to two other golden plovers, European and Pacific. The American golden plover is smaller, slimmer and relatively longer-legged than <a href="/wiki/European_golden_plover" title="European golden plover">European golden plover</a> (<i>Pluvialis apricaria</i>) which also has white axillary (armpit) feathers. It is more similar to <a href="/wiki/Pacific_golden_plover" title="Pacific golden plover">Pacific golden plover</a> (<i>Pluvialis fulva</i>) with which it was once considered <a href="/wiki/Conspecific" class="mw-redirect" title="Conspecific">conspecific</a> under the name "lesser golden plover".<sup id="cite_ref-Sangster2002_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sangster2002-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The Pacific golden plover is slimmer than the American species, has a shorter primary projection, and longer legs, and is usually yellower on the back.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_golden_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The breeding habitat of American golden plover is <a href="/wiki/Arctic_tundra" class="mw-redirect" title="Arctic tundra">Arctic tundra</a> from northern <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. They nest on the ground in a dry open area. They are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and winter in southern <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. They follow an elliptical migration path; northbound birds pass through <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> about January–April<sup id="cite_ref-Strewe2004_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Strewe2004-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Herrera2006_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Herrera2006-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and stage in great numbers in places like <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> before their final push north. In fall, they take a more easterly route, flying mostly over the western <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> to the wintering grounds in <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a>. The bird has one of the longest known migratory routes of over 40,000&#160;km (25,000&#160;mi). Of this, 3,900&#160;km (2,400&#160;mi) is over open ocean where it cannot stop to feed or drink. It does this from body fat stores that it stocks up on prior to the flight. It is a regular vagrant to western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.
</p><p>A comparison of dates and migratory patterns leads to the conclusion that <a href="/wiki/Eskimo_curlew" title="Eskimo curlew">Eskimo curlews</a> and American golden plovers were the most likely shore birds to have attracted the attention of <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> to the nearby <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> in early October 1492, after 65 days at sea out of sight of land.<sup id="cite_ref-NPWRC_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPWRC-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_golden_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_golden_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pluvialis_dominica_eggs_and_nest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Pluvialis_dominica_eggs_and_nest.jpg/220px-Pluvialis_dominica_eggs_and_nest.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Pluvialis_dominica_eggs_and_nest.jpg/330px-Pluvialis_dominica_eggs_and_nest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Pluvialis_dominica_eggs_and_nest.jpg/440px-Pluvialis_dominica_eggs_and_nest.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2288" data-file-height="1712" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pluvialis_dominica_eggs_and_nest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Scrape nest with four eggs</div></div></div>
<p>This bird uses <a href="/wiki/Scrape_nest" class="mw-redirect" title="Scrape nest">scrape nests</a>, lining them with lichens, grass, and leaves. At its breeding grounds, it is very territorial, displaying aggressively to neighbors. Some American plovers are also territorial in their wintering grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-Hauber2014_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hauber2014-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The American golden plover lays a <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> of four white to buff eggs that are heavily blotched with both black and brown spots. The eggs generally measure around 48 by 33 millimetres (1.9 by 1.3&#160;in). These eggs are incubated for a period of 26 to 27 days, with the male incubating during the day and the female during the night. The chicks then hatch <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#precocial" title="Glossary of bird terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of bird terms § precocial" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">precocial</span></a></dfn>, leaving the nest within hours and feeding themselves within a day.<sup id="cite_ref-Hauber2014_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hauber2014-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_golden_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These birds forage for food on tundra, fields, beaches and tidal flats, usually by sight. They eat <a href="/wiki/Insects" class="mw-redirect" title="Insects">insects</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crustaceans" class="mw-redirect" title="Crustaceans">crustaceans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Vanner2004_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vanner2004-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_golden_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Large numbers were shot in the late 19th century and the population has never fully recovered.
</p>
