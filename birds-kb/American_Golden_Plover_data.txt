<p>The <b>American Golden Plover</b> (<i>Pluvialis dominica</i>) is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">plover</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Americangoldie39.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Americangoldie39.jpg/300px-Americangoldie39.jpg" width="300" height="109" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Americangoldie39.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
American Golden Plover taking flight.<br />
Note dusky back and axillaries.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Adults are spotted gold and black on the crown, back and wings. Their face and neck are black with a white border; they have a black breast and a dark rump. The legs are black.</p>
<p>It is similar to two other golden plovers, Eurasian and Pacific. The American Golden Plover is smaller, slimmer and relatively longer-legged than <a href="/wiki/European_Golden_Plover" title="European Golden Plover">European Golden Plover</a> (<i>Pluvialis apricaria</i>) which also has white axillary (armpit) feathers. It is more similar to <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Golden_Plover" title="Pacific Golden Plover">Pacific Golden Plover</a> (<i>Pluvialis fulva</i>) with which it was once considered conspecific under the name "Lesser Golden Plover".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The Pacific Golden Plover is slimmer than the American species, has a shorter primary projection, and longer legs, and is usually yellower on the back.</p>
<p>These birds forage for food on tundra, fields, beaches and tidal flats, usually by sight. They eat insects and crustaceans, also berries.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pluvialis_dominica_eggs_and_nest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Pluvialis_dominica_eggs_and_nest.jpg/200px-Pluvialis_dominica_eggs_and_nest.jpg" width="200" height="150" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pluvialis_dominica_eggs_and_nest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Scrape nest with four eggs</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The breeding habitat of American Golden Plover is Arctic tundra from northern <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. They nest on the ground in a dry open area. They are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and winter in southern <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. They follow an elliptical migration path; northbound birds pass through <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> about January–April<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and stage in great numbers in places like Illinois before their final push north. In fall, they take a more easterly route, flying mostly over the western <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic" class="mw-redirect">Atlantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> to the wintering grounds in <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a>. The bird has one of the longest known migratory routes of over 25,000 miles. Of this, 2,400 miles is over open ocean where it cannot stop to feed or drink. It does this from body fat stores that it stocks up on prior to the flight. It is a regular vagrant to western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.</p>
<p>A comparison of dates and migratory patterns leads to the conclusion that <a href="/wiki/Eskimo_Curlew" title="Eskimo Curlew">Eskimo Curlews</a> and American Golden Plovers were the most likely shore birds to have attracted the attention of <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> to nearby <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">America</a> in early October 1492, after 65 days at sea out of sight of land.<sup id="cite_ref-NPWRC_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPWRC-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Large numbers were shot in the late 19th century and the population has never fully recovered.</p>
