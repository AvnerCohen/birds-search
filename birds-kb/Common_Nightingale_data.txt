<p>The <b>common nightingale</b>, <b>rufous nightingale</b> or simply <b>nightingale</b> (<i>Luscinia megarhynchos</i>), is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> best known for its powerful and beautiful <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">song</a>. It was formerly classed as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family <a href="/wiki/Turdidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Turdidae">Turdidae</a>, but is now more generally considered to be an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muscicapidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Muscicapidae">Muscicapidae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It belongs to a group of more terrestrial species, often called <a href="/wiki/Chat_(bird)" title="Chat (bird)">chats</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_nightingale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Nightingale" is derived from "night", and the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>galan</i>, "to sing".<sup id="cite_ref-OED_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OEDgale_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OEDgale-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The genus name <i>Luscinia</i> is  <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "nightingale" and <i>megarhynchos</i> is from  <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>megas</i>, "great" and <i>rhunkhos</i> "bill".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_nightingale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><b>western nightingale</b> (<i>L. m. megarhynchos</i>) - Western Europe, North Africa and Asia Minor, wintering in tropical Africa</li>
<li><b>Caucasian nightingale</b> (<i>L. m. africana</i>) - The Caucasus and eastern Turkey to southwestern Iran and Iraq, wintering in East Africa</li>
<li><b>eastern nightingale</b> (<i>L. m. golzii</i>) - The Aral Sea to Mongolia, wintering in coastal East Africa</li></ul>

<p>The common nightingale is slightly larger than the <a href="/wiki/European_robin" title="European robin">European robin</a>, at 15â16.5&#160;cm (5.9â6.5&#160;in) length. It is plain brown above except for the reddish tail. It is buff to white below. The sexes are similar. The eastern <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> (<i>L. m. golzi</i>) and the Caucasian subspecies (<i>L. m. africana</i>) have paler upperparts and a stronger face-pattern, including a pale <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a>.  The song of the nightingale<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> has been described as one of the most beautiful sounds in nature, inspiring <a href="/wiki/Nightingale_(Carole_King_song)" title="Nightingale (Carole King song)">songs</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Nightingale_(fairy_tale)" title="The Nightingale (fairy tale)">fairy tales</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Nightingale_(opera)" title="The Nightingale (opera)">opera</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Nightingale%27s_Song" title="The Nightingale&#39;s Song">books</a>, and a great deal of poetry.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_nightingale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> insectivorous species breeding in forest and scrub in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>, and wintering in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>. It is not found naturally in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>. The distribution is more southerly than the very closely related <a href="/wiki/Thrush_nightingale" title="Thrush nightingale">thrush nightingale</a> <i>Luscinia luscinia</i>. It nests on or near the ground in dense vegetation.  Research in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> found that favoured breeding <a href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Habitat (ecology)">habitat</a> of nightingales was defined by a number of <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geographical</a> factors.<sup id="cite_ref-Wink1973_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wink1973-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>less than 400&#160;m (1300&#160;ft) <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Above mean sea level">above mean sea level</a></li>
<li>mean <a href="/wiki/Air_temperature" class="mw-redirect" title="Air temperature">air temperature</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Growing_season" title="Growing season">growing season</a> above 14&#160;Â°C (57&#160;Â°F)</li>
<li>more than 20 days/year on which temperatures exceed 25&#160;Â°C (77&#160;Â°F)</li>
<li>annual <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> less than 750 millimetres (30&#160;in)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aridity_index" title="Aridity index">aridity index</a> lower than 0.35</li>
<li>no closed <a href="/wiki/Canopy_(forest)" class="mw-redirect" title="Canopy (forest)">canopy</a></li></ul>
<p>In the U.K., the bird is at the northern limit of its range which has contracted in recent years, placing it on the red list for conservation.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Despite local efforts to safeguard its favoured coppice and scrub habitat, numbers fell by 53 percent between 1995 and 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> A survey conducted by the British Trust for Ornithology in 2012 and 2013 recorded some 3,300 territories, with most of these clustered in a few counties in the southeast of England, notably Kent, Essex, Suffolk, and East and West Sussex.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By contrast, the European breeding population is estimated at between 3.2 and 7 million pairs, giving it green conservation status (least concern).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_nightingale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Common nightingales are so named because they frequently sing at night as well as during the day. The name has been used for more than 1,000 years, being highly recognisable even in its <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> form <i>nihtegale</i>, which means "night songstress". Early writers assumed the female sang when it is in fact the male. The song is loud, with an impressive range of whistles, trills and gurgles. Its song is particularly noticeable at night because few other birds are singing. This is why its name includes "night" in several languages. Only unpaired males sing regularly at night, and nocturnal song probably serves to attract a mate. Singing at dawn, during the hour before sunrise, is assumed to be important in defending the bird's territory. Nightingales sing even more loudly in urban or near-urban environments, in order to overcome the background noise. The most characteristic feature of the song is a loud whistling crescendo, absent from the song of thrush nightingale. It has a frog-like alarm call.
</p><p>The bird is a host of the <a href="/wiki/Acanthocephala" title="Acanthocephala">acanthocephalan</a> intestinal parasite <i><a href="/wiki/Apororhynchus_silesiacus" class="mw-redirect" title="Apororhynchus silesiacus">Apororhynchus silesiacus</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dimitrova_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dimitrova-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_connotations">Cultural connotations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_nightingale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cultural connotations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Birds_in_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Birds in culture">Birds in culture</a></div>
<p>The common nightingale is an important symbol for poets from a variety of ages, and has taken on a number of symbolic connotations. <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a> evokes the nightingale in <a href="/wiki/The_Odyssey" class="mw-redirect" title="The Odyssey">the <i>Odyssey</i></a>, suggesting the myth of <a href="/wiki/Philomela_(princess_of_Athens)" class="mw-redirect" title="Philomela (princess of Athens)">Philomela and Procne</a> (one of whom, depending on the myth's version, is turned into a nightingale<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This myth is the focus of <a href="/wiki/Sophocles" title="Sophocles">Sophocles</a>' tragedy, <i><a href="/wiki/Tereus_(Sophocles)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tereus (Sophocles)">Tereus</a></i>, of which only fragments remain. <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>, too, in his <i><a href="/wiki/Metamorphoses" title="Metamorphoses">Metamorphoses</a></i>, includes the most popular version of this myth, imitated and altered by later poets, including <a href="/wiki/Chr%C3%A9tien_de_Troyes" title="ChrÃ©tien de Troyes">ChrÃ©tien de Troyes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Geoffrey Chaucer</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Gower" title="John Gower">John Gower</a>, and <a href="/wiki/George_Gascoigne" title="George Gascoigne">George Gascoigne</a>. <a href="/wiki/T.S._Eliot" class="mw-redirect" title="T.S. Eliot">T.S. Eliot</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/The_Waste_Land" title="The Waste Land">The Waste Land</a>" also evokes the common nightingale's song (and the myth of Philomela and Procne).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Because of the violence associated with the myth, the nightingale's song was long interpreted as a lament.
</p><p>The common nightingale has also been used as a symbol of poets or their poetry.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Poets chose the nightingale as a symbol because of its creative and seemingly spontaneous song. <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes" title="Aristophanes">Aristophanes</a>'s <i>Birds</i> and <a href="/wiki/Callimachus" title="Callimachus">Callimachus</a> both evoke the bird's song as a form of poetry. <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a> compares the mourning of Orpheus to the âlament of the nightingaleâ.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Sonnet_102" title="Sonnet 102">Sonnet 102</a> Shakespeare compares his love poetry to the song of the common nightingale (Philomel):
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>"Our love was new, and then but in the spring,</dd>
<dd>When I was wont to greet it with my lays;</dd>
<dd>As Philomel in summer's front doth sing,</dd>
<dd>And stops his pipe in growth of riper days:"</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romantic</a> era the bird's symbolism changed once more: poets viewed the nightingale not only as a poet in his own right, but as âmaster of a superior art that could inspire the human poetâ.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> For some romantic poets, the nightingale even began to take on qualities of the muse. The nightingale has a long history with symbolic associations ranging from "creativity, the muse, nature's purity, and, in Western spiritual tradition, virtue and goodness."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Coleridge and Wordsworth saw the nightingale more as an instance of natural poetic creation: the nightingale became a voice of nature. <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">John Keats</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Ode_to_a_Nightingale" title="Ode to a Nightingale">Ode to a Nightingale</a>" pictures the nightingale as an idealized poet who has achieved the poetry that Keats longs to write. Invoking a similar conception of the nightingale, <a href="/wiki/Percy_Bysshe_Shelley" title="Percy Bysshe Shelley">Shelley</a> wrote in his âA Defense of Poetry":
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>"A poet is a nightingale who sits in darkness and sings to cheer its own solitude with sweet sounds; his auditors are as men entranced by the melody of an unseen musician, who feel that they are moved and softened, yet know not whence or why.â<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>The nightingale is the <a href="/wiki/National_bird" class="mw-redirect" title="National bird">national bird</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>. One legend tells how nightingales once only lived in <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">India</a>, when one nightingale visited Ukraine. Hearing sad songs from the people, the nightingale sang its song to cheer them up. The people responded with happy songs, and since then, nightingales have visited Ukraine every <a href="/wiki/Spring_(season)" title="Spring (season)">spring</a> to hear <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Ukraine" title="Music of Ukraine">Ukrainian songs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> National poet <a href="/wiki/Taras_Shevchenko" title="Taras Shevchenko">Taras Shevchenko</a> observed that "even the memory of the nightingale's song makes man happy."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
