<p>The <b>Common Nightingale</b> or simply <b>Nightingale</b>(<i>Luscinia megarhynchos</i>), also known as <b>Rufous Nightingale</b>, is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> that was formerly classed as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family <a href="/wiki/Turdidae" title="Turdidae" class="mw-redirect">Turdidae</a>, but is now more generally considered to be an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muscicapidae" title="Muscicapidae" class="mw-redirect">Muscicapidae</a>. It belongs to a group of more terrestrial species, often called <a href="/wiki/Chat_(bird)" title="Chat (bird)">chats</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Nightingale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Range and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Range_and_habitat">Range and habitat</span></h2>
<p>It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> insectivorous species breeding in forest and scrub in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and south-west <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, but is not found naturally in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>. The distribution is more southerly than the very closely related <a href="/wiki/Thrush_Nightingale" title="Thrush Nightingale">Thrush Nightingale</a> <i>Luscinia luscinia</i>. It nests on the ground within or next to dense bushes. It winters in southern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. At least in the <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">Rhineland</a> (<a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>), the breeding <a href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" title="Habitat (ecology)" class="mw-redirect">habitat</a> of nightingales agrees with a number of <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geographical</a> parameters.<sup id="cite_ref-Wink1973_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wink1973-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>less than 400&#160;m (1300&#160;ft) <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" title="Above mean sea level">above mean sea level</a></li>
<li>mean <a href="/wiki/Air_temperature" title="Air temperature" class="mw-redirect">air temperature</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Growing_season" title="Growing season">growing season</a> above <span style="white-space:nowrap;">14 °C</span> <span style="white-space:nowrap;">(57&#160;°F)</span></li>
<li>more than 20 days/year on which temperatures exceed <span style="white-space:nowrap;">25 °C</span> <span style="white-space:nowrap;">(77&#160;°F)</span></li>
<li>annual <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> less than 750mm</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aridity_index" title="Aridity index">aridity index</a> lower than 0.35</li>
<li>no closed <a href="/wiki/Canopy_(forest)" title="Canopy (forest)" class="mw-redirect">canopy</a></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Nightingale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The Common Nightingale is slightly larger than the <a href="/wiki/European_Robin" title="European Robin">European Robin</a>, at 15–16.5 cm (5.9–6.5 in) length. It is plain brown above except for the reddish tail. It is buff to white below. Sexes are similar. The eastern <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> <i>L. m. hafizi</i> and <i>L. m. africana</i> have paler upperparts and a stronger face-pattern, including a pale <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a>.</p>
<p>Common Nightingales are named so because they frequently sing at night as well as during the day. The name has been used for well over 1,000 years, being highly recognizable even in its Anglo-Saxon form – 'nightingale'. It means 'night songstress'. Early writers assumed the female sang when it is in fact the male. The song is loud, with an impressive range of whistles, trills and gurgles. Its song is particularly noticeable at night because few other birds are singing. This is why its name includes "night" in several languages. Only unpaired males sing regularly at night, and nocturnal song is likely to serve to attract a mate. Singing at dawn, during the hour before sunrise, is assumed to be important in defending the bird's territory. Nightingales sing even more loudly in urban or near-urban environments, in order to overcome the background noise. The most characteristic feature of the song is a loud whistling crescendo, absent from the song of Thrush Nightingale. It has a frog-like alarm call.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Nightingale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Symbolism">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Symbolism">Symbolism</span></h2>
<p>The Common Nightingale is an important symbol for poets from a variety of ages, and has taken on a number of symbolic connotations. <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a> evokes the Nightingale in <a href="/wiki/The_Odyssey" title="The Odyssey" class="mw-redirect">the <i>Odyssey</i></a>, suggesting the myth of <a href="/wiki/Philomela_(princess_of_Athens)" title="Philomela (princess of Athens)">Philomela and Procne</a> (one of whom, depending on the myth's version, is turned into a nightingale<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> ).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> This myth is the focus of <a href="/wiki/Sophocles" title="Sophocles">Sophocles'</a> tragedy, <i><a href="/wiki/Tereus_(Sophocles)" title="Tereus (Sophocles)">Tereus</a></i>, of which only fragments remain. <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>, too, in his <i><a href="/wiki/Metamorphoses" title="Metamorphoses">Metamorphoses</a></i>, includes the most popular version of this myth, imitated and altered by later poets, including <a href="/wiki/Chr%C3%A9tien_de_Troyes" title="Chrétien de Troyes">Chrétien de Troyes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Geoffrey Chaucer</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Gower" title="John Gower">John Gower</a>, and <a href="/wiki/George_Gascoigne" title="George Gascoigne">George Gascoigne</a>. <a href="/wiki/T.S._Eliot" title="T.S. Eliot" class="mw-redirect">T.S. Eliot's</a> "<a href="/wiki/The_Waste_Land" title="The Waste Land">The Waste Land</a>" also evokes the Common Nightingale's song (and the myth of Philomela and Procne).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Because of the violence associated with the myth, the nightingale's song was long interpreted as a lament.</p>
<p>The Common Nightingale has also been used as a symbol of poets or their poetry.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Poets chose the nightingale as a symbol because of its creative and seemingly spontaneous song. <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes" title="Aristophanes">Aristophanes'</a> <i>Birds</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Callimachus" title="Callimachus">Callimachus</a></i> both evoke the bird's song as a form of poetry. <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a> compares the mourning of Orpheus to the “lament of the nightingale”.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sonnet_102" title="Sonnet 102">Sonnet 102</a> Shakespeare compares his love poetry to the song of the Common Nightingale (Philomel):</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>"Our love was new, and then but in the spring,</dd>
<dd>When I was wont to greet it with my lays;</dd>
<dd>As Philomel in summer's front doth sing,</dd>
<dd>And stops his pipe in growth of riper days:"</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romantic</a> era the bird's symbolism changed once more: poets viewed the nightingale not only as a poet in his own right, but as “master of a superior art that could inspire the human poet”.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> For some romantic poets, the nightingale even began to take on qualities of the muse. Coleridge and Wordsworth saw the nightingale more as an instance of natural poetic creation: the nightingale became a voice of nature. <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">John Keats'</a> "<a href="/wiki/Ode_to_a_Nightingale" title="Ode to a Nightingale">Ode to a Nightingale</a>" pictures the nightingale as an idealized poet who has achieved the poetry that Keats longs to write. Invoking a similar conception of the nightingale, <a href="/wiki/Percy_Bysshe_Shelley" title="Percy Bysshe Shelley">Shelley</a> wrote in his “A Defense of Poetry":</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>"A poet is a nightingale who sits in darkness and sings to cheer its own solitude with sweet sounds; his auditors are as men entranced by the melody of an unseen musician, who feel that they are moved and softened, yet know not whence or why.”<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Nightingale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The <i>Aēdōn</i> (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ὰηδών</span>, "Nightingale") is a minor character in <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes" title="Aristophanes">Aristophanes</a>' <a href="/wiki/414_BC" title="414 BC">414 BC</a> <a href="/wiki/Attic_comedy" title="Attic comedy" class="mw-redirect">Attic comedy</a> "<a href="/wiki/The_Birds_(play)" title="The Birds (play)">The Birds</a>".</li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/The_Owl_and_the_Nightingale" title="The Owl and the Nightingale">The Owl and the Nightingale</a>" (12th or 13th century) is a <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> poem about an argument between these two birds.</li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/A_Nightingale_Sang_in_Berkeley_Square_(song)" title="A Nightingale Sang in Berkeley Square (song)">A Nightingale Sang in Berkeley Square</a>" was one of the most popular songs in Britain during World War 2</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">John Keats</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Ode_to_a_Nightingale" title="Ode to a Nightingale">Ode to a Nightingale</a>" was described by <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Clarence_Stedman" title="Edmund Clarence Stedman">Edmund Clarence Stedman</a> as "one of our shorter English lyrics that still seems to me... the nearest to perfection, the one I would surrender last of all"<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> and by <a href="/wiki/Algernon_Charles_Swinburne" title="Algernon Charles Swinburne">Algernon Charles Swinburne</a> as "one of the final masterpieces of human work in all time and for all ages".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">John Milton</a>'s sonnet "To the Nightingale" contrasts the symbolism of the nightingale as a bird for lovers, with the cuckoo as the bird that called when wives were unfaithful to (or "cuckolded") their husbands.</li>
<li>The love of the nightingale for the rose is widely used, often metaphorically, in <a href="/wiki/Persian_literature" title="Persian literature">Persian literature</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The beauty of the nightingale's song is a theme in <a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_Andersen" title="Hans Christian Andersen">Hans Christian Andersen</a>'s story "<a href="/wiki/The_Nightingale_(fairy_tale)" title="The Nightingale (fairy tale)">The Nightingale</a>" from 1843.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>In <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.gutenberg.org/files/38252/38252-h/38252-h.htm#THE_BIRD_OF_SHADOWS">The Bird of Shadows and the Sun-Bird</a>, a fairy tale by Maud Margaret Key Statwell, a young girl wishes to become a nightingale.</li>
<li>A nightingale is depicted on the <a href="/wiki/Obverse_and_reverse" title="Obverse and reverse">reverse</a> of the Croatian 1 <a href="/wiki/Croatian_kuna" title="Croatian kuna">kuna</a> coin, minted since 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>A recording of nightingale song is included, as directed by the score, in "<a href="/wiki/Pines_of_Rome#Sections" title="Pines of Rome">The Pines of Janiculum</a>", the third movement of <a href="/wiki/Ottorino_Respighi" title="Ottorino Respighi">Ottorino Respighi</a>'s 1924 <a href="/wiki/Symphonic_poem" title="Symphonic poem">symphonic poem</a> "<a href="/wiki/Pines_of_Rome" title="Pines of Rome">Pines of Rome</a>" (<i>Pini di Roma</i>).</li>
</ul>
