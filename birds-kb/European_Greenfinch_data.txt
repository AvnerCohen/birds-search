<p>The <b>European greenfinch</b> or simply the <b>greenfinch</b> (<i>Chloris chloris</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird in the <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finch</a> family Fringillidae.
</p><p>This bird is widespread throughout Europe, <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Southwest Asia</a>. It is mainly resident, but some northernmost populations <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> further south. The greenfinch has also been introduced into <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>. In Malta, it is considered a prestigious songbird, and it has been trapped for many years. It has been domesticated, and many Maltese people breed them. In <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, it is threatened. It has just recently been recorded in <a href="/wiki/Quetta" title="Quetta">Quetta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paki_(slur)" title="Paki (slur)">Pakistan</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_greenfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The greenfinch was described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his landmark 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a> under the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Loxia chloris</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPaynter1968&#91;httpswwwbiodiversitylibraryorgpage14481436_235–236&#93;_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPaynter1968[httpswwwbiodiversitylibraryorgpage14481436_235–236]-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELinnaeus1758&#91;httpswwwbiodiversitylibraryorgpage727081_174&#93;_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinnaeus1758[httpswwwbiodiversitylibraryorgpage727081_174]-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Specific_epithet" class="mw-redirect" title="Specific epithet">specific epithet</a> is from <i>khloris</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> name for this bird, from <i>khloros</i>, "green".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJobling2010102_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJobling2010102-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The finch family, <a href="/wiki/Fringillidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Fringillidae">Fringillidae</a>, is divided into two subfamilies, the <a href="/wiki/Carduelinae" title="Carduelinae">Carduelinae</a>, containing around 28 <a href="/wiki/Genera" class="mw-redirect" title="Genera">genera</a> with 141 <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Fringillinae" class="mw-redirect" title="Fringillinae">Fringillinae</a> containing a single genus, <i><a href="/wiki/Fringilla" title="Fringilla">Fringilla</a></i>, with three species. The finch family are all seed-eaters with stout conical bills. They have similar skull morphologies, nine large <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Primaries" title="Flight feather">primaries</a>, 12 tail feathers and no <a href="/wiki/Crop_(anatomy)#Birds" title="Crop (anatomy)">crop</a>. In all species the female bird builds the nest, incubates the eggs and broods the young. Fringilline finches raise their young almost entirely on <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a>, while the cardueline finches raise their young on regurgitated seeds.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollarNewtonClementArkhipov2010440–441_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollarNewtonClementArkhipov2010440–441-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular phylogenetic">molecular phylogenetic</a> study published in 2012 found that the greenfinches are not closely related to other members of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Carduelis" title="Carduelis">Carduelis</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> They have therefore been placed in the resurrected genus <i><a href="/wiki/Chloris_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chloris (bird)">Chloris</a></i> that had originally been introduced by the French naturalist <a href="/wiki/Georges_Cuvier" title="Georges Cuvier">Georges Cuvier</a> in 1800 with the European greenfinch as the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-iou_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iou-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_greenfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are 10 recognised <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-iou_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iou-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>British greenfinch</b> (<i>C. c. harrisoni</i>) <a href="/wiki/Phillip_Clancey" title="Phillip Clancey">Clancey</a>, 1940 – Great Britain (except northern Scotland) and Ireland</li>
<li><b>Northern European greenfinch</b> (<i>C. c. chloris</i>) (<a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">1758</a>) – northern Scotland, northern and central France and Norway to western Siberia</li>
<li><b>Eastern Mediterranean greenfinch</b> (<i>C. c. muehlei</i>) Parrot, 1905 – Serbia and Montenegro to Moldovia, Bulgaria and Greece</li>
<li><b>Central Mediterranean greenfinch</b> (<i>C. c. aurantiiventris</i>) (<a href="/wiki/Jean_Cabanis" title="Jean Cabanis">Cabanis</a>, 1851) – southern Spain through southern Europe to western Greece</li>
<li><b>Corsican greenfinch</b> (<i>C. c. madaraszi</i>) <a href="/wiki/Viktor_von_Tschusi_zu_Schmidhoffen" title="Viktor von Tschusi zu Schmidhoffen">Tschusi</a>, 1911 – Corsica and Sardinia</li>
<li><b>Western Mediterranean greenfinch</b> (<i>C. c. vanmarli</i>) <a href="/wiki/Karel_Voous" title="Karel Voous">Voous</a>, 1952 – northwestern Spain, Portugal and northwestern Morocco</li>
<li><b>Atlas greenfinch</b> (<i>C. c. voousi</i>) (Roselaar, 1993) – central Morocco and northern Algeria</li>
<li><b>Levant greenfinch</b> (<i>C. c. chlorotica</i>) (<a href="/wiki/Charles_Lucien_Bonaparte" title="Charles Lucien Bonaparte">Bonaparte</a>, 1850) – south-central Turkey to northeastern Egypt</li>
<li><b>Caucasian greenfinch</b> (<i>C. c. bilkevitchi</i>) <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Zarudny" title="Nikolai Zarudny">Zarudny</a>, 1911 – southern Ukraine, the Caucasus and northeastern Turkey to northern Iran and southwestern Turkmenistan</li>
<li><b>Turkestan greenfinch</b> (<i>C. c. turkestanica</i>) Zarudny, 1907 – southern Kazakhstan to Kyrgyzstan and central Tajikistan</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_greenfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European greenfinch is 15&#160;cm (5.9&#160;in) long with a wingspan of 24.5 to 27.5&#160;cm (9.6 to 10.8&#160;in).  It is similar in size and shape to a <a href="/wiki/House_sparrow" title="House sparrow">house sparrow</a>, but is mainly green, with yellow in the wings and tail. The female and young birds are duller and have brown tones on the back. The bill is thick and conical.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The song contains a lot of trilling twitters interspersed with wheezes, and the male has a "butterfly" display flight. 
Male greenfinch birds exhibit higher degrees of fluctuating asymmetry. The development of bones of males may be more easily disrupted than that of females.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_greenfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Woodland edges, farmland hedges and gardens with relatively thick vegetation are favoured for breeding.<sup id="cite_ref-Bensouilah_2015_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bensouilah_2015-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> It nests in trees or bushes, laying 3 to 6 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bensouilah_2014_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bensouilah_2014-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kosiński_2001_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kosiński_2001-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species can form large flocks outside the breeding season, sometimes mixing with other finches and buntings. They feed largely on seeds, but also take berries.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_greenfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Breeding season occurs in spring, starting in the second half of March, until June, with fledging young in early July. Incubation lasts about 13–14 days, by the female. The male feeds her at the nest during this period. Chicks are covered with thick, long, greyish-white down at hatching. They are fed on insect larvae by both adults during the first days, and later, by a frequently regurgitated yellowish paste made of seeds. They leave the nest about 13 days later, but they are not able to fly. Usually, they fledge 16–18 days after hatching. This species produces two or three broods per year.<sup id="cite_ref-Bensouilah_2014_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bensouilah_2014-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kosiński_2001_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kosiński_2001-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a>, the European greenfinch's breeding season is from October to March.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators_and_parasites">Predators and parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_greenfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Predators and parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoal</a> parasite <i><a href="/wiki/Trichomonas_gallinae" title="Trichomonas gallinae">Trichomonas gallinae</a></i> was known to infect pigeons and raptors but beginning in Great Britain in 2005, carcasses of dead European greenfinches and <a href="/wiki/Common_chaffinch" title="Common chaffinch">common chaffinches</a> were found to be infected with the parasite.<sup id="cite_ref-robinson2010_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-robinson2010-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The disease spread and in 2008, infected carcasses were found in Norway, Sweden and Finland and a year later in Germany. The spread of the disease is believed to have been mediated by common chaffinches, as large numbers of the birds breed in northern Europe and winter in Great Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-lawson2011_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lawson2011-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In Great Britain, the number of infected carcasses recovered each year declined after a peak in 2006. There was a reduction in the number of European greenfinches from around 4.3 million to around 2.8 million, but no significant decline in the overall number of common chaffinches.<sup id="cite_ref-lawson2012_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lawson2012-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> A similar pattern occurred in Finland where, after the arrival of the disease in 2008, there was a reduction in the number of European greenfinches but only a small change in the number of common chaffinches.<sup id="cite_ref-lehikoinen2013_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lehikoinen2013-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

</p>
