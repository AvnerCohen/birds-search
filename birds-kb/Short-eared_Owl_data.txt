<p>The <b>short-eared owl</b> (<i>Asio flammeus</i>) is a widespread grassland species in the (family <a href="/wiki/Strigidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Strigidae">Strigidae</a>). Owls belonging to genus <i><a href="/wiki/Asio" title="Asio">Asio</a></i> are known as the eared owls, as they have tufts of feathers resembling mammalian ears. These "ear" tufts may or may not be visible. <i>Asio flammeus</i> will display its tufts when in a defensive pose, although its very short tufts are usually not visible. The short-eared owl is found in open country and grasslands. The genus name <i>Asio</i> is a type of eared owl, and <i>flammeus</i> means "flame-coloured".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short-eared_owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The short-eared owl is a medium-sized owl measuring 34–43&#160;cm (13–17&#160;in) in length and weighing 206–475&#160;g (7.3–16.8&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-AAB_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAB-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It has large eyes, a big head, a short neck, and broad wings. Its bill is short, strong, hooked and black. Its plumage is mottled tawny to brown with a barred tail and wings. The upper breast is significantly streaked.<sup id="cite_ref-Alsop2001_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alsop2001-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Its flight is characteristically floppy due to its irregular wingbeats. The short-eared owl may also be described as "moth or bat-like" in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-dec_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dec-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Wingspans range from 85 to 110&#160;cm (33 to 43&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-ADW_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ADW-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Females are slightly larger than males. The yellow-orange eyes of <i>A. flammeus</i> are exaggerated by black rings encircling each eye, giving the appearance of them wearing <a href="/wiki/Mascara" title="Mascara">mascara</a>, and large, whitish disks of plumage surrounding the eyes like a mask.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Separation_from_the_long-eared_owl">Separation from the long-eared owl</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short-eared_owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Separation from the long-eared owl">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Through much of its range, short-eared owls occurs with the similar-looking <a href="/wiki/Long-eared_owl" title="Long-eared owl">long-eared owl</a>. At rest, the ear-tufts of the long-eared owl serve to easily distinguish the two (although long-eared owls can sometimes hold their ear-tufts flat). The iris-colour differs: yellow in the short-eared, and orange in the long-eared, and the black surrounding the eyes is vertical on the long-eared, and horizontal on the short-eared. Overall the short-eared tends to be a paler, sandier bird than the long-eared. There are a number of other ways in which the two species the differ which are best seen when they are flying: a) the short-eared often has a broad white band along the rear edge of the wing, which is not shown by the long-eared; b) on the upperwing, short-eared owls' primary-patches are usually paler and more obvious; c) the band on the upper side of the short-eared owl's tail are usually bolder than those of the long-eared; d) the short-eared's innermost secondaries are often dark-marked, contrasting with the rest of the underwing; e) the long-eared owl has streaking throughout its underparts, whereas on the short-eared the streaking ends at the breast; f) the dark markings on the underside of the tips of the longest primaries are bolder on the short-eared owl; g) the upper parts are coarsely blotched, whereas on the long-eared they are more finely marked. The short-eared owl also differs structurally from the long-eared, having longer, slimmer wings: the long-eared owl has wings shaped more like those of a <a href="/wiki/Tawny_owl" title="Tawny owl">tawny owl</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris1989_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris1989-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The long-eared owl generally has different habitat preferences from the short-eared, most often being found concealed in areas with dense wooded thickets. The short-eared owl is often most regularly seen flying about in early morning or late day as it hunts over open habitats.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Range">Range</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short-eared_owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Range">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The short-eared owl occurs on all continents except <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>; thus it has one of the most widespread distributions of any bird. <i>A. flammeus</i> breeds in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_Islands" title="Galápagos Islands">Galápagos Islands</a>. It is partially <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, moving south in winter from the northern parts of its range. The short-eared owl is known to relocate to areas of higher rodent populations.<sup id="cite_ref-Ehrlich1988_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrlich1988-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> It will also wander nomadically in search of better food supplies during years when <a href="/wiki/Vole" title="Vole">vole</a> populations are low. (See a <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.birds.cornell.edu/AllAboutBirds/BirdGuide/Short-eared_Owl.html#fig1">map of the short-eared owl's distribution</a> across the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>.)
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nesting_and_reproduction">Nesting and reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short-eared_owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nesting and reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Asio_flammeus_MWNH_0620.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Asio_flammeus_MWNH_0620.JPG/220px-Asio_flammeus_MWNH_0620.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Asio_flammeus_MWNH_0620.JPG/330px-Asio_flammeus_MWNH_0620.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Asio_flammeus_MWNH_0620.JPG/440px-Asio_flammeus_MWNH_0620.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2530" data-file-height="1722" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Asio_flammeus_MWNH_0620.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>Sexual maturity is attained at one year. Breeding season in the northern hemisphere lasts from March to June, peaking in April. During this time these owls may gather in flocks. During breeding season, the males make great spectacles of themselves in flight to attract females. The male swoops down over the nest flapping its wings in a courtship display.<sup id="cite_ref-Ehrlich1988_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrlich1988-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> These owls are generally <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" class="mw-redirect" title="Monogamous">monogamous</a>.
</p><p>The short-eared owl nests on the ground in prairie, tundra, savanna, or meadow <a href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Habitat (ecology)">habitats</a>. Nests are concealed by low vegetation, and may be lightly lined by weeds, grass, or feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-Ehrlich1988_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrlich1988-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Approximately 4 to 7 white eggs are found in a typical <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a>, but clutch size can reach up to a dozen eggs in years when <a href="/wiki/Vole" title="Vole">voles</a> are abundant. There is one <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">brood</a> per year. The eggs are incubated mostly by the female for 21–37 days. Offspring <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> at a little over four weeks. This owl is known to lure predators away from its nest by appearing to have a crippled wing.<sup id="cite_ref-Alsop2001_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alsop2001-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet_and_foraging_habits">Diet and foraging habits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short-eared_owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diet and foraging habits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Asio_flammeus_-Fazenda_Campo_de_Ouro,_Piraju,_Sao_Paulo,_Brasil-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Asio_flammeus_-Fazenda_Campo_de_Ouro%2C_Piraju%2C_Sao_Paulo%2C_Brasil-8.jpg/220px-Asio_flammeus_-Fazenda_Campo_de_Ouro%2C_Piraju%2C_Sao_Paulo%2C_Brasil-8.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="206" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Asio_flammeus_-Fazenda_Campo_de_Ouro%2C_Piraju%2C_Sao_Paulo%2C_Brasil-8.jpg/330px-Asio_flammeus_-Fazenda_Campo_de_Ouro%2C_Piraju%2C_Sao_Paulo%2C_Brasil-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Asio_flammeus_-Fazenda_Campo_de_Ouro%2C_Piraju%2C_Sao_Paulo%2C_Brasil-8.jpg/440px-Asio_flammeus_-Fazenda_Campo_de_Ouro%2C_Piraju%2C_Sao_Paulo%2C_Brasil-8.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1524" data-file-height="1424" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Asio_flammeus_-Fazenda_Campo_de_Ouro,_Piraju,_Sao_Paulo,_Brasil-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In Piraju, São Paulo, Brazil</div></div></div>
<p>Hunting occurs mostly at night, but this owl is known to be <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_animal" class="mw-redirect" title="Diurnal animal">diurnal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crepuscular" class="mw-redirect" title="Crepuscular">crepuscular</a> as well. Its daylight hunting seems to coincide with the high-activity periods of <a href="/wiki/Vole" title="Vole">voles</a>, its preferred prey.<sup id="cite_ref-daylight_hunting_explanation_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daylight_hunting_explanation-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> It tends to fly only feet above the ground in open fields and grasslands until swooping down upon its prey feet-first.<sup id="cite_ref-Alsop2001_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alsop2001-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Several owls may hunt over the same open area.<sup id="cite_ref-Kaufman2000_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaufman2000-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Its food consists mainly of <a href="/wiki/Rodents" class="mw-redirect" title="Rodents">rodents</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Vole" title="Vole">voles</a>, but it will eat other small <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">mice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ground_squirrel" title="Ground squirrel">ground squirrels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shrew" title="Shrew">shrews</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muskrat" title="Muskrat">muskrats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mole_(animal)" title="Mole (animal)">moles</a>. It will also occasionally predate smaller birds, especially when near sea-coasts and adjacent wetlands at which time they attack <a href="/wiki/Shorebird" class="mw-redirect" title="Shorebird">shorebirds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">terns</a> and small <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a> with semi-regularity. Avian prey is more infrequently preyed on inland and centers on passerines such as <a href="/wiki/Lark" title="Lark">larks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Icterid" title="Icterid">icterids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Starling" title="Starling">starlings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tyrant_flycatcher" title="Tyrant flycatcher">tyrant flycatchers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pipit" title="Pipit">pipits</a>. <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">Insects</a> supplement the diet and short-eared owls may prey on <a href="/wiki/Cockroach" title="Cockroach">roaches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Katydid" class="mw-redirect" title="Katydid">katydids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a>. Competition can be fierce in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Northern_harrier" title="Northern harrier">northern harrier</a>, with which the owl shares similar habitat and prey preferences. Both species will readily harass the other when prey is caught.<sup id="cite_ref-op_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-op-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pellets">Pellets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short-eared_owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pellets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of the high pH in the stomach of owls they have a reduced ability to digest bone and other hard parts, they eject pellets containing the remains of their prey.<sup id="cite_ref-Smiddy2013_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smiddy2013-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calls">Calls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short-eared_owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Calls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Short-eared owls have a scratchy bark-like call. Raspy <i>waowk, waowk, waowk</i> or <i>toot-toot-toot-toot-toot</i> sounds are common. A loud <i>eeee-yerp</i> is also heard on breeding grounds. However, short-eared owls are silent on the wintering grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-Alsop2001_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alsop2001-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short-eared_owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>It is listed as declining in the southern portion of its US range. It is common in the northern portion of its breeding range.<sup id="cite_ref-AAB_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAB-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is listed as endangered in <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>. Its appearance at the <a href="/wiki/Calverton_Executive_Airpark" title="Calverton Executive Airpark">Calverton Executive Airpark</a> on <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a> has prompted the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Department_of_Environmental_Conservation" title="New York State Department of Environmental Conservation">New York State Department of Environmental Conservation</a> to take the lead on ruling whether a massive redevelopment of the airport will receive the necessary environmental permits.<sup id="cite_ref-AAB_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAB-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
