<p>The <b>European serin</b>, or simply the <b>serin</b> (<i>Serinus serinus</i>), is the smallest European species of the family of <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finches</a> (Fringillidae) and is closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_canary" title="Atlantic canary">Atlantic canary</a>. Its diet consists mainly of a combination of <a href="/wiki/Bud" title="Bud">buds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_serin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European serin was described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1766 in the 12th edition of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> under the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Fringilla serinus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Latin <i>serinus</i> comes from the French word <i>serin</i> for a canary. The French word may be a corruption of the Latin word <i>citrinus</i> meaning "lemon-coloured".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_serin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European serin is a small short-tailed bird, 11–12&#160;cm in length. The upper parts are dark-streaked greyish green, with a yellow rump. The yellow breast and white belly are also heavily streaked.  The male has a brighter yellow face and breast, yellow wing bars and yellow tail sides. The song of this bird is a buzzing trill, very familiar in <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> countries.
</p><p>It breeds across southern and central <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and North <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. Southern and Atlantic coast populations are largely resident, but the northern breeders <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> further south in Europe for the winter. Open woodland and cultivation, often with some <a href="/wiki/Conifer" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifer">conifers</a>, is favoured for breeding. It builds its nest in a shrub or tree, laying 3–5 eggs. It forms flocks outside the breeding season, sometimes mixed with other finches.
</p><p>The food is mainly seeds, and, in the breeding season, insects. This small <a href="/wiki/Serin" class="mw-redirect" title="Serin">serin</a> is an active and often conspicuous bird.
</p>
