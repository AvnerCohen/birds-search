<p>The <b>Meadow Pipit</b> (<i>Anthus pratensis</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> which breeds in much of the northern half of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and also northwestern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, from southeastern <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> east to just east of the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural Mountains</a> in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, and south to central <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>; there is also an isolated population in the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus_Mountains" title="Caucasus Mountains">Caucasus Mountains</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> over most of its range, wintering in southern Europe, north <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and southwestern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, but is resident in Ireland, Great Britain, and neighbouring areas of western Europe. However, even here, many birds move to the coast or lowlands in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anthus_pratensis.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Anthus_pratensis.jpg/220px-Anthus_pratensis.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anthus_pratensis.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A Meadow Pipit perched on a bale of twine</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This is a widespread and often abundant small <a href="/wiki/Pipit" title="Pipit">pipit</a>, 14.5–15&#160;cm long and 15–22 g weight. It is an undistinguished looking species on the ground, mainly brown above and buff below, with darker streaking on most of its plumage; the tail is brown, with narrow white side edges. It has a thin bill and pale pinkish-yellow legs; the hind claw is notably long, longer than the rest of the hind toe. The call is a weak <i>tsi-tsi</i>. The simple repetitive song is given in a short song flight.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Birds breeding in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> are slightly darker coloured than those in other areas, and are often distinguished as the <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> <i>Anthus pratensis whistleri</i>, though it intergrades clinally with nominate <i>Anthus pratensis pratensis</i> found in the rest of the species' range.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is primarily a species of open habitats, either uncultivated or low-intensity agriculture, such as pasture, bogs, and moorland, but also occurs in low numbers in arable croplands. In winter, it also uses saltmarshes and sometimes open woodlands. It is a fairly terrestrial pipit, always feeding on the ground, but will use elevated perches such as shrubs, fence lines or electricity wires as vantage points to watch for predators.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ebcc_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebcc-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is similar to the <a href="/wiki/Red-throated_Pipit" title="Red-throated Pipit">Red-throated Pipit</a> <i>Anthus cervinus</i>, which is more heavily streaked and (in summer only) has an orange-red throat, and to the <a href="/wiki/Tree_Pipit" title="Tree Pipit">Tree Pipit</a> <i>Anthus trivialis</i>, which is slightly larger, less heavily streaked, and has stronger facial markings and a shorter hind claw. The song of the Meadow Pipit accelerates towards the end while that of the Tree Pipit slows down.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anthus_pratensis_nest_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Anthus_pratensis_nest_2.jpg/220px-Anthus_pratensis_nest_2.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anthus_pratensis_nest_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Nest with eggs</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The food is primarily <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>, mostly small items less than 5&#160;mm long. It also eats the seeds of <a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">grasses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyperaceae" title="Cyperaceae">sedges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juncaceae" title="Juncaceae">rushes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calluna" title="Calluna">heather</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Empetrum" title="Empetrum" class="mw-redirect">crowberry</a> berries, mainly in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The nest is on the ground hidden in dense vegetation, with 2–7 (most often 3–5) eggs; the eggs hatch after 11–15 days, with the chicks fledging 10–14 days after hatching. Two broods are commonly raised each year. This species is one of the most important nest hosts of the <a href="/wiki/Common_Cuckoo" title="Common Cuckoo">Cuckoo</a>, and it is also an important prey species for <a href="/wiki/Merlin_(bird)" title="Merlin (bird)">Merlins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hen_Harrier" title="Hen Harrier">Hen Harriers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The estimated total population is 12 million pairs. It is an abundant species in the north of its range, and generally the commonest breeding bird in most of upland Britain, but less common further south. Breeding densities range from 80 pairs per square kilometre in northern Scandinavia, to 5–20 pairs per square kilometre in grassland in the south of the breeding range, and just one pair per square kilometre in arable farmland.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ebcc_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebcc-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> There are a small number of isolated breeding records from south of the main range, in the mountains of <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, and the northern <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The generic name 'pipit', first documented by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Pennant" title="Thomas Pennant">Thomas Pennant</a> in 1768, is <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeic</a>, from the call note of this species.<sup id="cite_ref-Lockwood_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lockwood-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> In colloquial use by <a href="/wiki/Birdwatching" title="Birdwatching">birders</a> in the UK the name Meadow Pipit is often abbreviated to "mippit".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Old folk names, no longer used, include "chit lark", "peet lark", "tit lark" and "titling"; these refer to its small size and superficial similarity to a <a href="/wiki/Lark" title="Lark">lark</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lockwood_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lockwood-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
