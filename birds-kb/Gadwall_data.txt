<p>The <b>Gadwall</b> (<i><b>Anas strepera</b></i>) is a common and widespread <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">duck</a> of the family <a href="/wiki/Anatidae" title="Anatidae">Anatidae</a>.</p>

<p>The Gadwall is 46–56&#160;cm (18–22&#160;in) long with a 78–90&#160;cm (31–35&#160;in) wingspan.<sup id="cite_ref-Floyd_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Floyd-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The male is slightly larger than the female, weighing on average 990&#160;g (35&#160;oz) against her 850&#160;g (30&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The breeding male is patterned grey, with a black rear end, light chestnut wings, and a brilliant white <a href="/wiki/Speculum_feathers" title="Speculum feathers">speculum</a>, obvious in flight or at rest.<sup id="cite_ref-Dunn_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunn-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> In non-breeding (eclipse) plumage, the drake looks more like the female, but retains the male wing pattern, and is usually greyer above and has less orange on the bill.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The female is light brown, with plumage much like a female <a href="/wiki/Mallard" title="Mallard">Mallard</a>. It can be distinguished from that species by the dark orange-edged bill, smaller size, the white <a href="/wiki/Speculum_feathers" title="Speculum feathers">speculum</a>, and white belly.<sup id="cite_ref-Dunn_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunn-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Both sexes go through two moults annually, following a juvenile moult.<sup id="cite_ref-Floyd_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Floyd-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Gadwall is a quieter duck, except during its <a href="/wiki/Courtship_display" title="Courtship display">courtship display</a>. Females give a call similar to the <i>quack</i> of a female Mallard but higher-pitched, transcribed as <i>gag-ag-ag-ag</i>. Males give a grunt, transcribed as <i>nheck</i>, and a whistle.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Gadwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<p>The Gadwall breeds in the northern areas of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, and central <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. In North America, its breeding range lies along the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lawrence_River" title="Saint Lawrence River">Saint Lawrence River</a>, through the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dakotas" title="Dakotas" class="mw-redirect">Dakotas</a>, south to <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>, west to <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, and along coastal <a href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific" class="mw-redirect">Pacific</a> <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and southern coastal <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Floyd_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Floyd-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dunn_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunn-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The range of this bird appears to be expanding into eastern North America. This <a href="/wiki/Dabbling_duck" title="Dabbling duck" class="mw-redirect">dabbling duck</a> is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, and winters farther south than its breeding range, from coastal <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, south into <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, and east into <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, and then south all the way into Central America.<sup id="cite_ref-Floyd_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Floyd-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dunn_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunn-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Its conservation status is <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, the Gadwall is a scarce-breeding bird and winter visitor, though its population has increased in recent years. It is likely that its expansion was partly through introduction, mainly to England, and partly through colonization to <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, with continental birds staying to breed in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. It has been reported in the <a href="/wiki/River_Avon_(Hampshire)" title="River Avon (Hampshire)">River Avon</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> a small breeding population has recently become established, centred on <a href="/wiki/Wexford" title="Wexford">Wexford</a> in the south and <a href="/wiki/Lough_Neagh" title="Lough Neagh">Lough Neagh</a> in the north.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Gadwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>

<p>The Gadwall is a bird of open wetlands, such as <a href="/wiki/Prairie" title="Prairie">prairie</a> or <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a> lakes, wet grassland or marshes with dense fringing vegetation, and usually feeds by dabbling for plant food with head submerged. It nests on the ground, often some distance from water. It is not as gregarious as some dabbling ducks outside the breeding season and tends to form only small flocks. This is a fairly quiet species; the male has a hoarse whistling call, and the female has a Mallard-like quack. The young birds are fed <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> at first; adults also eat some molluscs and insects during the nesting season. The Gadwall is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Gadwall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in his <i>Systema naturae</i> in 1758 under its current scientific name.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Gadwall's closest relative within the genus <i>Anas</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Falcated_Duck" title="Falcated Duck">Falcated Duck</a>, followed by the <a href="/wiki/Wigeon" title="Wigeon">wigeons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Johnson_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>There are two subspecies although one is <a href="/wiki/Extinct" title="Extinct" class="mw-redirect">extinct</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>A. strepera strepera</i>, <b>Common Gadwall</b> the nominate subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-Clements_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clements-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>A. strepera couesi</i>, <b>Coues' Gadwall</b>, extinct circa 1874, was located on <a href="/wiki/Tabuaeran" title="Tabuaeran">Fanning Island</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Clements_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clements-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> of the word <i>Gadwall</i> is not known but the term has been recorded in usage from 1666.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
