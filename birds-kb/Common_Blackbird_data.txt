<p>The <b>common blackbird</b> (<i>Turdus merula</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/True_thrush" title="True thrush">true thrush</a>. It is also called the <b>Eurasian blackbird</b> (especially in North America, to distinguish it from the unrelated <a href="/wiki/New_World_blackbird" class="mw-redirect" title="New World blackbird">New World blackbirds</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-natgeo_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-natgeo-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> or simply the <b>blackbird</b> where this does not lead to confusion with a <span class="nowrap">similar-looking</span> local species. It breeds in Europe, Asiatic Russia, and North Africa, and has been <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced</a> to Australia and New Zealand.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It has a number of <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> across its large range; a few of the Asian subspecies are sometimes considered to be full species. Depending on <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>, the common blackbird may be <a href="/wiki/Resident_bird" class="mw-redirect" title="Resident bird">resident</a>, partially <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, or fully migratory.
</p><p>The adult male of the common blackbird (<i>Turdus merula merula</i> which is the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate subspecies</a>), which is found throughout most of Europe, is all black except for a yellow eye-ring and <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> and has a rich, melodious <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">song</a>; the adult female and juvenile have mainly dark brown <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>. This species breeds in woods and gardens, building a neat, mud-lined, cup-shaped nest. It is <a href="/wiki/Omnivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Omnivorous">omnivorous</a>, eating a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berry" title="Berry">berries</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruits</a>.
</p><p>Both sexes are <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territorial</a> on the breeding grounds, with distinctive threat displays, but are more gregarious during migration and in wintering areas. Pairs stay in their territory throughout the year where the climate is sufficiently <a href="/wiki/Temperate" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate">temperate</a>. This common and conspicuous species has given rise to a number of literary and cultural references, frequently related to its song.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_blackbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common blackbird was described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his landmark 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a> as <i>Turdus merula</i> (characterised as <i>T. ater, rostro palpebrisque fulvis</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The binomial name derives from two <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> words, <i>turdus</i>, "thrush", and <i>merula</i>, "blackbird", the latter giving rise to its French name, <i>merle</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and its <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a> name, <i>merl</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>About 65 species of medium to large thrushes are in the genus <i>Turdus</i>, characterised by rounded heads, longish, pointed wings, and usually melodious songs. Although two European thrushes, the <a href="/wiki/Song_thrush" title="Song thrush">song thrush</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mistle_thrush" title="Mistle thrush">mistle thrush</a>, are early offshoots from the Eurasian lineage of <i>Turdus</i> thrushes after they spread north from Africa, the blackbird is descended from ancestors that had colonised the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> from Africa and subsequently reached Europe from there.<sup id="cite_ref-reilly_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reilly-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It is close in evolutionary terms to the <a href="/wiki/Island_thrush" title="Island thrush">island thrush</a> (<i>T.&#160;poliocephalus</i>) of Southeast Asia and islands in the southwest Pacific, which probably diverged from <i>T. merula</i> stock fairly recently.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It may not immediately be clear why the name "blackbird", first recorded in 1486, was applied to this species, but not to one of the various other common black English birds, such as the <a href="/wiki/Carrion_crow" title="Carrion crow">carrion crow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_raven" title="Common raven">raven</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rook_(bird)" title="Rook (bird)">rook</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Jackdaw" class="mw-redirect" title="Jackdaw">jackdaw</a>. However, in <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Modern_English" title="Modern English">modern English</a> up to about the 18th century, "bird" was used only for smaller or young birds, and larger ones such as crows were called "fowl". At that time, the blackbird was therefore the only widespread and conspicuous "black bird" in the British Isles.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Until about the 17th century, another name for the species was <i>ouzel</i>, <i>ousel</i> or <i>wosel</i> (from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>osle</i>, cf. German <i>Amsel</i>). Another variant occurs in Act 3 of <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/A_Midsummer_Night%27s_Dream" title="A Midsummer Night&#39;s Dream">A Midsummer Night's Dream</a></i>, where <a href="/wiki/Nick_Bottom" title="Nick Bottom">Bottom</a> refers to "The Woosell cocke, so blacke of hew, With Orenge-tawny bill". The ouzel usage survived later in poetry, and still occurs as the name of the closely related <a href="/wiki/Ring_ouzel" title="Ring ouzel">ring ouzel</a> (<i>Turdus torquatus</i>), and in water ouzel, an alternative name for the unrelated but superficially similar <a href="/wiki/White-throated_dipper" title="White-throated dipper">white-throated dipper</a> (<i>Cinclus cinclus</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-birdnames_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdnames-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Two related Asian <i>Turdus</i> <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrushes</a>, the <a href="/wiki/White-collared_blackbird" title="White-collared blackbird">white-collared blackbird</a> (<i>T. albocinctus</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Grey-winged_blackbird" title="Grey-winged blackbird">grey-winged blackbird</a> (<i>T. boulboul</i>), are also named blackbirds,<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Somali_thrush" title="Somali thrush">Somali thrush</a> (<i>T. (olivaceus) ludoviciae</i>) is alternatively known as the Somali blackbird.<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Icterid" title="Icterid">icterid</a> family of the New World is sometimes called the blackbird family because of some species' superficial resemblance to the common blackbird and other Old World thrushes, but they are not evolutionarily close, being related to the <a href="/wiki/New_World_warbler" title="New World warbler">New World warblers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tanager" title="Tanager">tanagers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jaramillo_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jaramillo-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The term is often limited to smaller species with mostly or entirely black plumage, at least in the breeding male, notably the <a href="/wiki/Cowbird" title="Cowbird">cowbirds</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Quiscalus" title="Quiscalus">grackles</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and for around 20 species with "blackbird" in the name, such as the <a href="/wiki/Red-winged_blackbird" title="Red-winged blackbird">red-winged blackbird</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Melodious_blackbird" title="Melodious blackbird">melodious blackbird</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jaramillo_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jaramillo-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_blackbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As would be expected for a widespread passerine bird species, several geographical subspecies are recognised. The treatment of subspecies in this article follows Clement et al. (2000).<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Amsel_Weibchen_aufgeplustert_edit2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Amsel_Weibchen_aufgeplustert_edit2.jpg/220px-Amsel_Weibchen_aufgeplustert_edit2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Amsel_Weibchen_aufgeplustert_edit2.jpg/330px-Amsel_Weibchen_aufgeplustert_edit2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Amsel_Weibchen_aufgeplustert_edit2.jpg/440px-Amsel_Weibchen_aufgeplustert_edit2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2643" data-file-height="1800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Amsel_Weibchen_aufgeplustert_edit2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Female of subspecies <i>merula</i></div></div></div>
<ul><li>The <b>European blackbird</b> (<i>T. m. merula</i>), the nominate subspecies, breeds commonly throughout much of Europe from <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Faroes" class="mw-redirect" title="Faroes">Faroes</a> and the British Isles east to the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural Mountains</a> and north to about 70 N, where it is fairly scarce. A small population breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Nile_Valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Nile Valley">Nile Valley</a>. Birds from the north of the range winter throughout Europe and around the Mediterranean, including <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a> and North Africa. The introduced birds in Australia and New Zealand are of the nominate race.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <b>Azores blackbird</b> (<i>T. m. azorensis</i>) is a small race which breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>. The male is darker and glossier than <i>merula</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <b>Madeiran blackbird</b> (<i>T. m. cabrerae</i>), named for <a href="/wiki/%C3%81ngel_Cabrera_(naturalist)" title="Ángel Cabrera (naturalist)">Ángel Cabrera</a>, the Spanish <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoologist</a>, resembles <i>azorensis</i> and breeds in <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a> and the western <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <b>Northwestern African blackbird</b> (<i>T. m. mauritanicus</i>), another small dark subspecies with a glossy black male plumage, breeds in central and northern <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, coastal <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Turdus_merula_-autumn_leaves_-Budapest-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Turdus_merula_-autumn_leaves_-Budapest-8.jpg/220px-Turdus_merula_-autumn_leaves_-Budapest-8.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Turdus_merula_-autumn_leaves_-Budapest-8.jpg/330px-Turdus_merula_-autumn_leaves_-Budapest-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Turdus_merula_-autumn_leaves_-Budapest-8.jpg/440px-Turdus_merula_-autumn_leaves_-Budapest-8.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Turdus_merula_-autumn_leaves_-Budapest-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>First-summer male, probably subspecies <i>aterrimus</i></div></div></div></li>
<li>The <b>Southeastern European blackbird</b> (<i>T m. aterrimus</i>) breeds in Hungary, south and east to southern Greece, <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. It winters in southern Turkey, northern <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> and southern Iran. It is smaller than <i>merula</i> with a duller male and paler female plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <b>Levant blackbird</b> (<i>T. m. syriacus</i>) breeds on the Mediterranean coast of southern Turkey south to <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and the northern <a href="/wiki/Sinai_Peninsula" title="Sinai Peninsula">Sinai</a>. It is mostly resident, but part of the population moves southwest or west to winter in the <a href="/wiki/Jordan_River" title="Jordan River">Jordan Valley</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Nile_Delta" title="Nile Delta">Nile Delta</a> of northern Egypt south to about <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>. Both sexes of this subspecies are darker and greyer than the equivalent <i>merula</i> plumages.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <b>Central Asian blackbird</b> (<i>T. m. intermedius</i>) is an Asian race breeding from Central Russia to <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a>, western and northeastern Afghanistan, and eastern China. Many birds are resident, but some are altitudinal migrants and occur in southern Afghanistan and southern Iraq in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This is a large subspecies, with a sooty-black male and a blackish-brown female.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW3_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW3-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The Central Asian subspecies, the relatively large <i>intermedius</i>, also differs in structure and voice, and may represent a distinct species.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW3_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW3-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Alternatively, it has been suggested that it should be considered a subspecies of <i><a href="/wiki/Tibetan_blackbird" title="Tibetan blackbird">T. maximus</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> but it differs in structure, voice and the appearance of the eye-ring.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW3_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW3-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HBW2_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW2-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_species">Similar species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_blackbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Similar species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Europe, the common blackbird can be confused with the paler-winged first-winter <a href="/wiki/Ring_ouzel" title="Ring ouzel">ring ouzel</a> (<i>Turdus torquatus</i>) or the superficially similar <a href="/wiki/Common_starling" title="Common starling">common starling</a> (<i>Sturnus vulgaris</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> A number of similar <i><a href="/wiki/Turdus" class="mw-redirect" title="Turdus">Turdus</a></i> thrushes exist far outside the range of the common blackbird, for example the South American <a href="/wiki/Chiguanco_thrush" title="Chiguanco thrush">Chiguanco thrush</a> (<i>Turdus chiguanco</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Fjeldsaa_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fjeldsaa-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Indian_blackbird" title="Indian blackbird">Indian blackbird</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_blackbird" title="Tibetan blackbird">Tibetan blackbird</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_blackbird" title="Chinese blackbird">Chinese blackbird</a> were formerly considered subspecies of the common blackbird.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_blackbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common blackbird of the <a href="/wiki/Subspecies#Nominate_subspecies_and_subspecies_autonyms" title="Subspecies">nominate</a> subspecies <i>T. m. merula</i> is 23.5 to 29&#160;centimetres (9.25 to 11.4&#160;in) in length, has a long tail, and weighs 80–125&#160;grams (2.8 to 4.4&#160;<a href="/wiki/Ounce" title="Ounce">oz</a>). The adult male has glossy black <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>, blackish-brown legs, a yellow eye-ring and an orange-yellow <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a>. The bill darkens somewhat in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The adult female is sooty-brown with a dull yellowish-brownish bill, a brownish-white throat and some weak mottling on the breast. The <a href="/wiki/Juvenile_(organism)" title="Juvenile (organism)">juvenile</a> is similar to the female, but has pale spots on the upperparts, and the very young juvenile also has a speckled breast. Young birds vary in the shade of brown, with darker birds presumably males.<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The first year male resembles the adult male, but has a dark bill and weaker eye ring, and its folded wing is brown, rather than black like the body plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_blackbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common blackbird breeds in temperate Eurasia, North Africa, the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, and South Asia. It has been introduced to Australia and New Zealand.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_8-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Populations are <a href="/wiki/Sedentism" title="Sedentism">sedentary</a> in the south and west of the range, although northern birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> south as far as northern Africa and tropical Asia in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_8-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Urban males are more likely to <a href="/wiki/Overwinter" class="mw-redirect" title="Overwinter">overwinter</a> in cooler climes than rural males, an adaptation made feasible by the warmer microclimate and relatively abundant food that allow the birds to establish territories and start reproducing earlier in the year.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Recoveries of blackbirds ringed on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_May" title="Isle of May">Isle of May</a> show that these birds commonly migrate from southern Norway (or from as far north as <a href="/wiki/Trondheim" title="Trondheim">Trondheim</a>) to Scotland, and some onwards to Ireland. Scottish-ringed birds have also been recovered in England, Belgium, Holland, Denmark, and Sweden.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Female blackbirds in Scotland and the north of England migrate more (to Ireland) in winter than do the males<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Common over most of its range in woodland, the common blackbird has a preference for deciduous trees with dense undergrowth. However, gardens provide the best breeding habitat with up to 7.3 pairs per hectare (nearly three pairs per acre), with woodland typically holding about a tenth of that density, and open and very built-up habitats even less.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> They are often replaced by the related <a href="/wiki/Ring_ouzel" title="Ring ouzel">ring ouzel</a> in areas of higher altitude.<sup id="cite_ref-Observerbirdseggs_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Observerbirdseggs-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The common blackbird also lives in parks, gardens and hedgerows.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common blackbird occurs up to 1,000&#160;metres (3300&#160;ft) in Europe, 2,300&#160;metres (7,590&#160;ft) in North Africa, and at 900–1,820&#160;metres (3,000–6,000&#160;ft) in peninsular India and Sri Lanka, but the large Himalayan subspecies range much higher, with <i>T. m. maximus</i> breeding at 3,200–4,800&#160;metres (10560–16000&#160;ft) and remaining above 2,100&#160;metres (6,930&#160;ft) even in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_8-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This widespread species has occurred as a vagrant in many locations in Eurasia outside its normal range, but records from North America are normally considered to involve escapees, including, for example, the 1971 bird in <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-McNeil_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McNeil-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> However, a 1994 record from <a href="/wiki/Bonavista,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Bonavista, Newfoundland and Labrador">Bonavista, Newfoundland</a>, has been accepted as a genuine wild bird,<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_8-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and the species is therefore on the <a href="/wiki/List_of_birds_of_North_America" title="List of birds of North America">North American list</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AOU_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOU-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The male common blackbird defends its breeding territory, chasing away other males or utilising a "bow and run" threat display. This consists of a short run, the head first being raised and then bowed with the tail dipped simultaneously. If a fight between male blackbirds does occur, it is usually short and the intruder is soon chased away. The female blackbird is also aggressive in the spring when it competes with other females for a good nesting territory, and although fights are less frequent, they tend to be more violent.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill's</a> appearance is important in the interactions of the common blackbird. The territory-holding male responds more aggressively towards models with orange bills than to those with yellow bills, and reacts least to the brown bill colour typical of the first-year male. The female is, however, relatively indifferent to bill colour, but responds instead to shinier bills.<sup id="cite_ref-AB64_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AB64-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As long as winter food is available, both the male and female will remain in the territory throughout the year, although occupying different areas. Migrants are more gregarious, travelling in small flocks and feeding in loose groups in the wintering grounds. The flight of migrating birds comprises bursts of rapid wing beats interspersed with level or diving movement, and differs from both the normal fast agile flight of this species and the more dipping action of larger thrushes.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_blackbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Turdus_merula_MWNH_2249.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Turdus_merula_MWNH_2249.JPG/220px-Turdus_merula_MWNH_2249.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Turdus_merula_MWNH_2249.JPG/330px-Turdus_merula_MWNH_2249.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Turdus_merula_MWNH_2249.JPG/440px-Turdus_merula_MWNH_2249.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Turdus_merula_MWNH_2249.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>The male common blackbird attracts the female with a courtship display which consists of oblique runs combined with head-bowing movements, an open beak, and a "strangled" low song. The female remains motionless until she raises her head and tail to permit copulation.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> This species is monogamous, and the established pair will usually stay together as long as they both survive.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_15-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Pair separation rates of up to 20% have been noted following poor breeding.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Although the species is socially monogamous, there have been studies showing as much as 17% extra-pair paternity.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The nominate <i>T. merula</i> may commence breeding in March, but eastern and Indian races are a month or more later, and the introduced New Zealand birds start nesting in August (late winter).<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_8-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Observerbirdseggs_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Observerbirdseggs-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The breeding pair prospect for a suitable nest site in a creeper or bush, favouring evergreen or thorny species such as <a href="/wiki/Ivy" class="mw-redirect" title="Ivy">ivy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holly" title="Holly">holly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crataegus" title="Crataegus">hawthorn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honeysuckle" title="Honeysuckle">honeysuckle</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pyracantha" title="Pyracantha">pyracantha</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NatEngland_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NatEngland-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Sometimes the birds will nest in sheds or outbuildings where a ledge or cavity is used. The cup-shaped <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a> is made with grasses, leaves and other vegetation, bound together with mud. It is built by the female alone. She lays three to five (usually four) bluish-green <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> marked with reddish-brown blotches,<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> heaviest at the larger end;<sup id="cite_ref-Observerbirdseggs_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Observerbirdseggs-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> the eggs of nominate <i>T. merula</i> are 2.9×2.1&#160;centimetres (1.14×0.93&#160;in) in size and weigh 7.2&#160;grammes (0.25&#160;oz), of which 6% is shell.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Eggs of birds of the southern Indian races are paler than those from the northern subcontinent and Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_8-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
The female incubates for 12–14 days before the <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a> chicks are hatched naked and blind. Fledging takes another 10–19 (average 13.6) days, with both parents feeding the young and removing faecal sacs.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_15-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The nest is often ill-concealed compared with those of other species, and many breeding attempts fail due to predation.<sup id="cite_ref-Game_and_Wildfowl_Conservation_Trust_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Game_and_Wildfowl_Conservation_Trust-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The young are fed by the parents for up to three weeks after leaving the nest, and will follow the adults begging for food. If the female starts another nest, the male alone will feed the fledged young.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_24-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Second broods are common, with the female reusing the same nest if the brood was successful, and three broods may be raised in the south of the common blackbird's range.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_8-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A common blackbird has an average <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">life expectancy</a> of 2.4 years,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> and, based on data from <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">bird ringing</a>, the oldest recorded age is 21 years and 10 months.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In its native <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> range, the first-year male common blackbird of the nominate race may start singing as early as late January in fine weather in order to establish a territory, followed in late March by the adult male. The male's song is a varied and melodious low-pitched fluted warble, given from trees, rooftops or other elevated perches mainly in the period from March to June, sometimes into the beginning of July. It has a number of other calls, including an aggressive <i>seee</i>, a <i>pook-pook-pook</i> alarm for terrestrial predators like cats, and various <i>chink</i> and <i>chook, chook</i> vocalisations. The territorial male invariably gives <i>chink-chink</i> calls in the evening in an (usually unsuccessful) attempt to deter other blackbirds from roosting in its territory overnight.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_24-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> During the northern winter, blackbirds can be heard quietly singing to themselves, so much so that September and October are the only months in which the song cannot be heard.<sup id="cite_ref-gbirds_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gbirds-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Like other passerine birds, it has a thin high <i>seee</i> alarm call for threats from <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">birds of prey</a> since the sound is rapidly attenuated in vegetation, making the source difficult to locate.<sup id="cite_ref-burton_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-burton-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At least two subspecies, <i>T. m. merula</i> and <i>T. m. nigropileus</i>, will mimic other species of birds, cats, humans or alarms, but this is usually quiet and hard to detect.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_blackbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Male_common_blackbird_with_feed_in_Lausanne.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Male_common_blackbird_with_feed_in_Lausanne.jpg/220px-Male_common_blackbird_with_feed_in_Lausanne.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="184" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Male_common_blackbird_with_feed_in_Lausanne.jpg/330px-Male_common_blackbird_with_feed_in_Lausanne.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Male_common_blackbird_with_feed_in_Lausanne.jpg/440px-Male_common_blackbird_with_feed_in_Lausanne.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2088" data-file-height="1745" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Male_common_blackbird_with_feed_in_Lausanne.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult male feeding on berries in <a href="/wiki/Lausanne" title="Lausanne">Lausanne</a>, Switzerland</div></div></div>
<p>The common blackbird is <a href="/wiki/Omnivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Omnivorous">omnivorous</a>, eating a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a>, seeds and berries. It feeds mainly on the ground, running and hopping with a start-stop-start progress. It pulls earthworms from the soil, usually finding them by sight, but sometimes by hearing, and roots through leaf litter for other <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>. Small <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a> are occasionally hunted. This species will also perch in bushes to take berries and collect <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a> and other active insects.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_24-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Animal prey predominates, and is particularly important during the breeding season, with windfall apples and berries taken more in the autumn and winter. The nature of the fruit taken depends on what is locally available, and frequently includes exotics in gardens.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_threats">Natural threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_blackbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Natural threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Blackbird_and_Kestrel.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Blackbird_and_Kestrel.jpg/170px-Blackbird_and_Kestrel.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="208" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Blackbird_and_Kestrel.jpg/255px-Blackbird_and_Kestrel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Blackbird_and_Kestrel.jpg/340px-Blackbird_and_Kestrel.jpg 2x" data-file-width="550" data-file-height="672" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Blackbird_and_Kestrel.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A male attempting to distract a male <a href="/wiki/Common_kestrel" title="Common kestrel">kestrel</a> close to its nest</div></div></div>
<p>Near human habitation the main predator of the common blackbird is the domestic cat, with newly fledged young especially vulnerable. <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">Foxes</a> and predatory birds, such as the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">sparrowhawk</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Accipiter" title="Accipiter">accipiters</a>, also take this species when the opportunity arises.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lambeth_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lambeth-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> However, there is little direct evidence to show that either predation of the adult blackbirds or loss of the eggs and chicks to <a href="/wiki/Corvidae" title="Corvidae">corvids</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/European_magpie" class="mw-redirect" title="European magpie">European magpie</a> or <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_jay" title="Eurasian jay">Eurasian jay</a>, decrease population numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-NatEngland_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NatEngland-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species is occasionally a host of <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">parasitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Cuckoo" title="Cuckoo">cuckoos</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common cuckoo</a> (<i>Cuculus canorus</i>), but this is minimal because the common blackbird recognizes the adult of the parasitic species and its <a href="/wiki/Mimicry" title="Mimicry">non-mimetic</a> eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-daviesnb_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daviesnb-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> In the UK, only three nests of 59,770 examined (0.005%) contained cuckoo eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-glue_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glue-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The introduced <i>merula</i> blackbird in New Zealand, where the cuckoo does not occur, has, over the past 130 years, lost the ability to recognize the adult common cuckoo but still rejects non-mimetic eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-JAB_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAB-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As with other passerine birds, parasites are common. <a href="/wiki/Intestinal_parasite" class="mw-redirect" title="Intestinal parasite">Intestinal parasites</a> were found in 88% of common blackbirds, most frequently <i><a href="/wiki/Isospora" title="Isospora">Isospora</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Capillaria_(nematode)" title="Capillaria (nematode)">Capillaria</a></i> species.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> and more than 80% had haematozoan parasites (<i><a href="/wiki/Leucocytozoon" title="Leucocytozoon">Leucocytozoon</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Plasmodium" title="Plasmodium">Plasmodium</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Haemoproteus" title="Haemoproteus">Haemoproteus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Trypanosoma" title="Trypanosoma">Trypanosoma</a></i> species).<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Common blackbirds spend much of their time looking for food on the ground where they can become infested with ticks, which are external parasites that most commonly attach to the head of a blackbird.<sup id="cite_ref-Gregoire_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gregoire-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> In France, 74% of rural blackbirds were found to be infested with <i><a href="/wiki/Ixodes" title="Ixodes">Ixodes</a></i> ticks, whereas, only 2% of blackbirds living in urban habitats were infested.<sup id="cite_ref-Gregoire_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gregoire-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> This is partly because it is more difficult for ticks to find another host on lawns and gardens in urban areas than in uncultivated rural areas, and partly because ticks are likely to be commoner in rural areas, where a variety of tick hosts, such as foxes, deer and boar, are more numerous.<sup id="cite_ref-Gregoire_46-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gregoire-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Although ixodid ticks can transmit <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogenic</a> viruses and bacteria, and are known to transmit <i><a href="/wiki/Borrelia" title="Borrelia">Borrelia</a></i> bacteria to birds,<sup id="cite_ref-Kipp_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kipp-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> there is no evidence that this affects the fitness of blackbirds except when they are exhausted and run down after migration.<sup id="cite_ref-Gregoire_46-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gregoire-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common blackbird is one of a number of species which has <a href="/wiki/Unihemispheric_slow-wave_sleep" title="Unihemispheric slow-wave sleep">unihemispheric slow-wave sleep</a>. One hemisphere of the brain is effectively asleep, while a low-voltage <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">EEG</a>, characteristic of wakefulness, is present in the other. The benefit of this is that the bird can rest in areas of high predation or during long migratory flights, but still retain a degree of alertness.<sup id="cite_ref-Perspectives_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Perspectives-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_and_conservation">Status and conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_blackbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Status and conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common blackbird has an extensive range, estimated at 10&#160;million&#160;square kilometres (3.8&#160;million square miles), and a large population, including an estimated 79 to 160 million individuals in Europe alone. The species is not believed to approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the IUCN Red List (i.e., declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations), and is therefore evaluated as <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> In the western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>, populations are generally stable or increasing,<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_15-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> but there have been local declines, especially on farmland, which may be due to agricultural policies that encouraged farmers to remove hedgerows (which provide nesting places), and to drain damp grassland and increase the use of <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, both of which could have reduced the availability of invertebrate food.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common blackbird was introduced to Australia by a bird dealer visiting <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> in early 1857,<sup id="cite_ref-OzPests_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OzPests-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> and its range has expanded from its initial foothold in Melbourne and <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a> to include all of southeastern Australia, including <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bass_Strait" title="Bass Strait">Bass Strait</a> islands.<sup id="cite_ref-backyard_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-backyard-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> The introduced population in Australia is considered a pest because it damages a variety of soft fruits in orchards, parks and gardens, including berries, cherries, stone fruit and grapes.<sup id="cite_ref-OzPests_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OzPests-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> It is thought to spread weeds, such as <a href="/wiki/Blackberry" title="Blackberry">blackberry</a>, and may compete with native birds for food and nesting sites.<sup id="cite_ref-OzPests_50-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OzPests-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Oz_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oz-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The introduced common blackbird is, together with the native <a href="/wiki/Silvereye" title="Silvereye">silvereye</a> (<i>Zosterops lateralis</i>), the most widely distributed avian seed disperser in New Zealand. Introduced there along with the <a href="/wiki/Song_thrush" title="Song thrush">song thrush</a> (<i>Turdus philomelos</i>) in 1862, it has spread throughout the country up to an elevation of 1,500 metres (4,921&#160;ft), as well as outlying islands such as the <a href="/wiki/Campbell_Island_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Campbell Island group">Campbell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kermadec_Islands" title="Kermadec Islands">Kermadecs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Fallaetal79_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fallaetal79-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> It eats a wide range of native and exotic fruit, and makes a major contribution to the development of communities of naturalised woody weeds. These communities provide fruit more suited to non-endemic native birds and naturalised birds than to <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> birds.<sup id="cite_ref-NZ_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NZ-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_blackbird&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SingSong6dcaldecott.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/SingSong6dcaldecott.jpg/220px-SingSong6dcaldecott.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="251" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/SingSong6dcaldecott.jpg/330px-SingSong6dcaldecott.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/SingSong6dcaldecott.jpg/440px-SingSong6dcaldecott.jpg 2x" data-file-width="850" data-file-height="970" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SingSong6dcaldecott.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"<a href="/wiki/Sing_a_Song_of_Sixpence" title="Sing a Song of Sixpence">Sing a Song for Sixpence</a>" cover illustration</div></div></div>
<p>The common blackbird was seen as a sacred though destructive bird in Classical Greek folklore, and was said to die if it consumed <a href="/wiki/Pomegranate" title="Pomegranate">pomegranates</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cooper92_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper92-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Like many other small birds, it has in the past been trapped in rural areas at its night roosts as an easily available addition to the diet,<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> and in medieval times the practice of placing live birds under a pie crust just before serving may have been the origin of the familiar <a href="/wiki/Nursery_rhyme" title="Nursery rhyme">nursery rhyme</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sing_a_song_of_sixpence" class="mw-redirect" title="Sing a song of sixpence">Sing a song of sixpence</a>,<br />
A pocket full of rye;<br />
Four and twenty blackbirds baked in a pie!<br />
When the pie was opened the birds began to sing,<br />
</p><p>
Oh, wasn't that a dainty dish to set before the king?<sup id="cite_ref-pie_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pie-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>The common blackbird's melodious, distinctive song is mentioned in the poem <i>Adlestrop</i> by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Thomas_(poet)" title="Edward Thomas (poet)">Edward Thomas</a>;
</p>
<blockquote><p>And for that minute a blackbird sang<br />
</p><p>Close by, and round him, mistier, <br />
Farther and farther, all the birds <br />
</p><p>
Of Oxfordshire and Gloucestershire.<sup id="cite_ref-adelstrop_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-adelstrop-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>In the English <a href="/wiki/Christmas_carol" title="Christmas carol">Christmas carol</a> "<a href="/wiki/The_Twelve_Days_of_Christmas_(song)" title="The Twelve Days of Christmas (song)">The Twelve Days of Christmas</a>", the line commonly sung today as "four calling birds" is believed to have originally been written in the 18th century as "four colly birds", an <a href="/wiki/Archaism" title="Archaism">archaism</a> meaning "black as coal" that was a popular English nickname for the common blackbird.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common blackbird, unlike many black creatures, is not normally seen as a symbol of bad luck,<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> but <a href="/wiki/R._S._Thomas" title="R. S. Thomas">R. S. Thomas</a> wrote that there is "a suggestion of dark Places about it",<sup id="cite_ref-rst_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rst-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> and it symbolised resignation in the 17th century <a href="/wiki/Tragedy" title="Tragedy">tragic</a> play <i><a href="/wiki/The_Duchess_of_Malfi" title="The Duchess of Malfi">The Duchess of Malfi</a></i>;<sup id="cite_ref-deVries76_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deVries76-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> an alternate connotation is vigilance, the bird's clear cry warning of danger.<sup id="cite_ref-deVries76_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deVries76-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common blackbird is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_national_birds" title="List of national birds">national bird</a> of Sweden,<sup id="cite_ref-sweden_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sweden-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> which has a breeding population of 1–2 million pairs,<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_15-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and was featured on a 30&#160;<a href="/wiki/%C3%96re" title="Öre">öre</a> <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamp" title="Postage stamp">postage stamp</a> in 1970;<sup id="cite_ref-stamp_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stamp-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> it has also featured on a number of other stamps issued by European and Asian countries, including a 1966 4d British stamp and a 1998 Irish 30p stamp.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> This bird—arguably—also gives rise to the <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">Serbian</a> name for <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_Province_of_Kosovo_and_Metohija" title="Autonomous Province of Kosovo and Metohija">Kosovo</a> (and Metohija), which is the possessive adjectival form of Serbian <i>kos</i> ("blackbird") as in <a href="/wiki/Kosovo_Polje" title="Kosovo Polje">Kosovo Polje</a> ("Blackbird Field").<sup id="cite_ref-kosovo_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kosovo-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A common blackbird can be heard singing on <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles</a> song "<a href="/wiki/Blackbird_(Beatles_song)" title="Blackbird (Beatles song)">Blackbird</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the Netherlands and parts of Belgium, the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> translation of the word blackbird, Merel, is a fairly <a href="/wiki/Merel" title="Merel">common girl’s name</a>. The French version, Merle, is also used in this region, albeit less often.
</p><p>The famous Lebanese singer, <a href="/wiki/Sabah_(singer)" title="Sabah (singer)">Sabah</a>, one of the biggest recording and movie stars of the 20th century in the <a href="/wiki/Arab_world" title="Arab world">Arab world</a>, was nicknamed the "blackbird of the valley" at the launch of her artistic career due to her beautiful voice.<sup id="cite_ref-GulfTimes_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GulfTimes-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
