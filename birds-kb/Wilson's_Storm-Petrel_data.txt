<p><b>Wilson's Storm Petrel</b> (<i>Oceanites oceanicus</i>), also known as <b>Wilson's Petrel</b>, is a small <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Storm_petrel" title="Storm petrel">storm petrel</a> family. It is one of the most abundant bird species in the world and has a circumpolar distribution mainly in the seas of the southern hemisphere but extending northwards during the summer of the northern hemisphere. The world population has been estimated to be more than 50 million pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The name commemorates the Scottish-American ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Wilson" title="Alexander Wilson">Alexander Wilson</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Wilson%27s_Storm_Petrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wilsons_Storm_Petrel_(Oceanites_oceanicus)_webbed_feet_Oct_2011_Karnataka_Pelagic_Udupi.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Wilsons_Storm_Petrel_%28Oceanites_oceanicus%29_webbed_feet_Oct_2011_Karnataka_Pelagic_Udupi.jpg/220px-Wilsons_Storm_Petrel_%28Oceanites_oceanicus%29_webbed_feet_Oct_2011_Karnataka_Pelagic_Udupi.jpg" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Wilsons_Storm_Petrel_%28Oceanites_oceanicus%29_webbed_feet_Oct_2011_Karnataka_Pelagic_Udupi.jpg/330px-Wilsons_Storm_Petrel_%28Oceanites_oceanicus%29_webbed_feet_Oct_2011_Karnataka_Pelagic_Udupi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Wilsons_Storm_Petrel_%28Oceanites_oceanicus%29_webbed_feet_Oct_2011_Karnataka_Pelagic_Udupi.jpg/440px-Wilsons_Storm_Petrel_%28Oceanites_oceanicus%29_webbed_feet_Oct_2011_Karnataka_Pelagic_Udupi.jpg 2x" /></a>

<p>Wilson's Storm Petrel is a small <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a>, 16–18.5&#160;cm (6.3–7.3&#160;in) in length with a 38–42&#160;cm (15–16.5&#160;in) wingspan. It is slightly larger than the <a href="/wiki/European_Storm_Petrel" title="European Storm Petrel">European Storm Petrel</a> and is essentially dark brown in all plumages, except for the white rump and flanks. The feet jut beyond the square ended tail in flight. The European Storm Petrel has a very distinct whitish lining to the underwing and a nearly all dark upperwing. Wilson's Storm Petrel has a diffuse pale band along the upper wing coverts and lacks the distinctive white underwing lining.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The webbing between the toes is yellow with black spots in pre-breeding age individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Wilson%27s_Storm_Petrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wilsons_Storm_Petrel_(Oceanites_oceanicus)_hovering_Oct_2011_Karnataka_Pelagic_Udupi.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Wilsons_Storm_Petrel_%28Oceanites_oceanicus%29_hovering_Oct_2011_Karnataka_Pelagic_Udupi.jpg/220px-Wilsons_Storm_Petrel_%28Oceanites_oceanicus%29_hovering_Oct_2011_Karnataka_Pelagic_Udupi.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Wilsons_Storm_Petrel_%28Oceanites_oceanicus%29_hovering_Oct_2011_Karnataka_Pelagic_Udupi.jpg/330px-Wilsons_Storm_Petrel_%28Oceanites_oceanicus%29_hovering_Oct_2011_Karnataka_Pelagic_Udupi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Wilsons_Storm_Petrel_%28Oceanites_oceanicus%29_hovering_Oct_2011_Karnataka_Pelagic_Udupi.jpg/440px-Wilsons_Storm_Petrel_%28Oceanites_oceanicus%29_hovering_Oct_2011_Karnataka_Pelagic_Udupi.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wilsons_Storm_Petrel_(Oceanites_oceanicus)_hovering_Oct_2011_Karnataka_Pelagic_Udupi.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Showing the pale band on the upper wing secondary coverts</div>

<p>Originally described in the genus <i>Procellaria</i> it has been placed under the genus <i>Oceanites</i>. Two or three subspecies are recognized and one population <i>maorianus</i> from New Zealand may be extinct. The nominate population breeds from Cape Horn to the Kerguelen Islands while <i>exasperatus</i> breeds along the Antarctic coast in the South Shetland and other islands. The population from Tierra del Fuego was described as <i>chilensis</i> (=<i>wollastoni</i>, <i>magellanicus</i>) but this is considered a <i><a href="/wiki/Nomen_nudum" title="Nomen nudum">nomen nudum</a></i> although some authors have reinstated it, noting that it can be distinguished by white mottling on the belly.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The name <i>Mother Carey's Chicken</i> was used in early literature and often applied to several petrel species while the generic name of stormy petrel referred to the idea that their appearance foretold stormy weather. Some authors called it the Yellow-webbed Storm-petrel.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Wilson%27s_Storm_Petrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<p>This species breeds on the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a> coastlines and nearby islands such as the <a href="/wiki/South_Shetland_Islands" title="South Shetland Islands">South Shetland Islands</a> during the summer of the southern hemisphere. It spends the rest of the year at sea, and moves into the northern oceans in the southern hemisphere's winter. It is much more common in the north <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> than the <a href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific" class="mw-redirect">Pacific</a>. Wilson's Storm Petrel is common off eastern <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> in the northern summer and the seasonal abundance of this <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in suitable <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> waters has been revealed through pelagic boat trips, most notably in the area of the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>.</p>
<p>It is strictly pelagic outside the breeding season, and this, together with its remote breeding sites, makes Wilson's Petrel a difficult bird to see from land. Only in severe storms might this species be pushed into headlands.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Wilson%27s_Storm_Petrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span></h2>
<p>Wilson's Storm Petrel has a more direct gliding flight than other small petrels, and like most others it flies low over the seas surface and has the habit of pattering on the water surface as it picks <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">planktonic</a> food items from the ocean surface. Their unique fluttering and hovering flight is achieved often with their wings held high. Even in calm weather, they can make use of the slight breeze produced by the waves and in effect soar while using their feet to stabilize themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Like European Storm Petrel, it is highly gregarious, and will also follow ships. A soft peeping noise is often heard while the birds are feeding. They feed predominantly on planktonic invertebrates close to the surface, rarely plunging below the surface to capture prey. They may however sometimes take 3–8&#160;cm long fish in the family <a href="/wiki/Myctophidae" title="Myctophidae" class="mw-redirect">Myctophidae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>At 40 g on average, it is the smallest warm-blooded animal that breeds in the Antarctic region.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> It nests in colonies close to the sea in rock crevices or small burrows in soft earth and lays a single white egg. Like most petrels, its walking ability is limited to a short shuffle to the burrow. In the Antarctic, nests may sometimes get snowed over leading to destruction of the nest or chicks. This <a href="/wiki/Storm_petrel" title="Storm petrel">storm petrel</a> is strictly nocturnal at the breeding sites to avoid predation by <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skua" title="Skua">skuas</a>, and will even avoid coming to land on clear moonlit nights. Both parents tend the nest and feed the single chick.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The chicks call and beg for food, more vigorously when hungry.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Chicks remain at nest for about 60 days and are fed on krill, fish and amphipods.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Adults have the ability to identify their nest burrows<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> in the dark and their mates by olfactory cues.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Widespread throughout its large range, Wilson's Storm Petrel is evaluated as Least Concern on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> of Threatened Species.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
