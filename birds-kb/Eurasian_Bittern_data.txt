<p>The <b>Eurasian bittern</b> or <b>great bittern</b> (<i>Botaurus stellaris</i>) is a wading <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bittern" title="Bittern">bittern</a> subfamily (Botaurinae) of the <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">heron</a> family <a href="/wiki/Ardeidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Ardeidae">Ardeidae</a>. There are two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>, the northern race (<i>B. s. stellaris</i>) breeding in parts of Europe and across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>, as well as on the northern coast of Africa, while the southern race (<i>B. s. capensis</i>) is <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> to parts of southern Africa. It is a secretive bird, seldom seen in the open as it prefers to skulk in <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed beds</a> and thick vegetation near water bodies. Its presence is apparent in the spring, when the booming call of the male during the breeding season can be heard. It feeds on fish, small mammals, fledgling birds, amphibians, crustaceans and insects.
</p><p>The nest is usually built among reeds at the edge of bodies of water. The female incubates the <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> of eggs and feeds the young chicks, which leave the nest when about two weeks old. She continues to care for them until they are fully <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledged</a> some six weeks later.
</p><p>With its specific habitat requirements and the general reduction in wetlands across its range, the population is thought to be in decline globally. However the decline is slow, and the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> has assessed its overall conservation status as being of "<a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least concern</a>". Nevertheless, some local populations are at risk and the population of the southern race has declined more dramatically and is cause for concern. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> it is one of the most threatened of all bird species.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_etymology">Taxonomy and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_bittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">first described</a> as <i>Ardea stellaris botaurus</i>  as early as the 1660s by the ornithologist Thomas Browne <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  and as <i>Ardea stellaris</i> by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> of 1758. It is placed in the subfamily <a href="/wiki/Botaurinae" class="mw-redirect" title="Botaurinae">Botaurinae</a>, and its closest relatives are the <a href="/wiki/American_bittern" title="American bittern">American bittern</a> (<i>Botaurus lentiginosus</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Pinnated_bittern" title="Pinnated bittern">pinnated bittern</a> (<i>Botaurus pinnatus</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Australasian_bittern" title="Australasian bittern">Australasian bittern</a> (<i>Botaurus poiciloptilus</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Two races of Eurasian bittern are recognised; the <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> <i>B. s. stellaris</i> has a <a href="/wiki/Palearctic_realm" title="Palearctic realm">palearctic</a> distribution and occurs across a broad swathe of Europe, North Africa and Asia, while the other subspecies, <i>B. s. capensis</i>, occurs only in southern Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The name <i>capensis</i> was used for species found in the <a href="/wiki/Afrotropical_realm" title="Afrotropical realm">Afrotropics</a> for which no exact range was known.<sup id="cite_ref-job14_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job14-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The generic name <i>Botaurus</i> was given by the English naturalist <a href="/wiki/James_Francis_Stephens" title="James Francis Stephens">James Francis Stephens</a>, and is derived from <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> <i>butaurus</i>, "bittern", itself constructed from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> name for the bird, <i>botor</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-job_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny</a> gave a fanciful derivation from <i>Bos</i> (ox) and <i>taurus</i> (bull), because the bittern's call resembles the bellowing of a bull.<sup id="cite_ref-oedbitt_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oedbitt-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The species name <i>stellaris</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "starred", from <i>stella</i>, "star", and refers to the speckled <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">plumage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-job_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Its folk names, often local, include many variations on the themes of "barrel-maker", "bog-bull", "bog hen", "bog-trotter", "bog-bumper", "mire drum[ble]", "butter bump", "bitter bum",<sup id="cite_ref-Bewick_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bewick-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> "bog blutter", "bog drum", "boom bird", "bottle-bump", "bull of the bog", "bull of the mire", "bumpy cors", and "heather blutter".<sup id="cite_ref-BB_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Most of these were <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeic</a> colloquial names for the bird; the call was described as "bumping"<sup id="cite_ref-Browne1646_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Browne1646-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> or "booming". <a href="/wiki/Mire" title="Mire">Mire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bog</a> denote the bird's habitat.<sup id="cite_ref-wildlifeupclose_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wildlifeupclose-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_bittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_Bittern_(Botaurus_stellaris)_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Eurasian_Bittern_%28Botaurus_stellaris%29_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG/260px-Eurasian_Bittern_%28Botaurus_stellaris%29_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG" decoding="async" width="260" height="101" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Eurasian_Bittern_%28Botaurus_stellaris%29_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG/390px-Eurasian_Bittern_%28Botaurus_stellaris%29_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Eurasian_Bittern_%28Botaurus_stellaris%29_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG/520px-Eurasian_Bittern_%28Botaurus_stellaris%29_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3433" data-file-height="1332" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_Bittern_(Botaurus_stellaris)_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Eurasian bittern skull</div></div></div>
<p>Bitterns are thickset herons with bright, pale, buffy-brown plumage covered with dark streaks and bars As its alternate name suggests, this species is the largest of the bitterns, with males being rather larger than females.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock1999_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock1999-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The Eurasian or great bittern is 69–81&#160;cm (27–32&#160;in) in length, with a 100–130&#160;cm (39–51&#160;in) wingspan and a body mass of 0.87–1.94&#160;kg (1.9–4.3&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-Brazil2009_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brazil2009-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The crown and nape are black, with the individual feathers rather long and loosely arranged, tipped with buff narrowly barred with black. The sides of the head and neck are a more uniform tawny-buff, irregularly barred with black. The mantle, scapulars and back are of a similar colour but are more heavily barred, the individual feathers having black centres and barring. The head has a yellowish-buff superciliary stripe and a brownish-black moustachial stripe. The sides of the neck are a rusty-brown with faint barring. The chin and throat are buff, the central feathers on the throat having longitudinal stripes of rusty-brown. The breast and belly are yellowish-buff, with broad stripes of brown at the side and narrow stripes in the centre. The tail is rusty-buff with black streaks in the centre and black mottling near the edge. The wings are pale rusty-brown irregularly barred, streaked and mottled with black.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The plumage has a loose texture, and elongated feathers on the crown, neck and breast can be erected.<sup id="cite_ref-Voisin_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voisin-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The powerful bill is greenish-yellow with a darker tip to the upper mandible. The eye has a yellow iris and is surrounded by a ring of greenish or bluish bare skin. The legs and feet are greenish, with some yellow on the <a href="/wiki/Tarsometatarsus" title="Tarsometatarsus">tarsal joint</a> and yellow soles to the feet. Juveniles have similar plumage to adults but are somewhat paler with less distinct markings.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_bittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The breeding range of <i>B. s. stellaris</i> extends across temperate parts of Europe and Asia from the British Isles, Sweden and Finland eastwards to <a href="/wiki/Sakhalin" title="Sakhalin">Sakhalin Island</a> in eastern Siberia and <a href="/wiki/Hokkaido" title="Hokkaido">Hokkaido Island</a> in Japan. The bird's northern extent of occurrence is around 57°N in the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural Mountains</a> and 64°N in eastern Siberia. Its southern limit is the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>, Iran, Afghanistan, Kazakhstan, Mongolia and <a href="/wiki/Hebei" title="Hebei">Hebei Province</a> in northern China. Small resident populations also breed in Morocco, Algeria and Tunisia.<sup id="cite_ref-Voisin_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voisin-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> It typically inhabits <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed beds</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Phragmites" title="Phragmites">Phragmites</a></i>) and swamps, as well as lakes, lagoons and sluggish rivers fringed by rank vegetation. It sometimes nests by ponds in agricultural areas, and even quite near habitations where suitable habitat exists,<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> but for preference, chooses large reed beds of at least 20 hectares (49 acres) in which to breed.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Botaurus_stellaris_Gosforth_Park.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Botaurus_stellaris_Gosforth_Park.jpg/170px-Botaurus_stellaris_Gosforth_Park.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="177" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Botaurus_stellaris_Gosforth_Park.jpg/255px-Botaurus_stellaris_Gosforth_Park.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Botaurus_stellaris_Gosforth_Park.jpg/340px-Botaurus_stellaris_Gosforth_Park.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1212" data-file-height="1263" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Botaurus_stellaris_Gosforth_Park.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A bittern, well <a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">camouflaged</a> in typical <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed bed</a> habitat</div></div></div>
<p>Some populations are sedentary and stay in the same areas throughout the year. More northerly populations usually <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> to warmer regions but some birds often remain; birds in northern Europe tend to move south and west to southern Europe, northern and central Africa, and northern Asian birds migrate to parts of the Arabian peninsula, the Indian sub-continent, and the provinces of <a href="/wiki/Heilongjiang" title="Heilongjiang">Heilongjiang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jilin" title="Jilin">Jilin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Inner Mongolia</a> in eastern China.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Outside the breeding season it has less restrictive habitat requirements, and as well as living in reed beds, it visits <a href="/wiki/Paddy_field" title="Paddy field">rice fields</a>, <a href="/wiki/Watercress" title="Watercress">watercress beds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fish_farming" title="Fish farming">fish farms</a>, gravel pits, <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">sewage works</a>, ditches, flooded areas and marshes.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The subspecies <i>B. s. capensis</i> is <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> to southern Africa, where it is found sparingly in marshes near the east coast, the <a href="/wiki/Okavango_Delta" title="Okavango Delta">Okavango Delta</a> and the upland foothills of the <a href="/wiki/Drakensberg" title="Drakensberg">Drakensberg Mountains</a>. This population is sedentary.<sup id="cite_ref-Voisin_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voisin-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ibc_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibc-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_bittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gro%C3%9Fe_Rohrdommel,_Botaurus_stellaris_1b.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Gro%C3%9Fe_Rohrdommel%2C_Botaurus_stellaris_1b.JPG/170px-Gro%C3%9Fe_Rohrdommel%2C_Botaurus_stellaris_1b.JPG" decoding="async" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Gro%C3%9Fe_Rohrdommel%2C_Botaurus_stellaris_1b.JPG/255px-Gro%C3%9Fe_Rohrdommel%2C_Botaurus_stellaris_1b.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Gro%C3%9Fe_Rohrdommel%2C_Botaurus_stellaris_1b.JPG/340px-Gro%C3%9Fe_Rohrdommel%2C_Botaurus_stellaris_1b.JPG 2x" data-file-width="981" data-file-height="1471" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gro%C3%9Fe_Rohrdommel,_Botaurus_stellaris_1b.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In defensive pose with elongated feathers spread</div></div></div>
<p>Usually solitary, the Eurasian bittern forages in reed beds, walking stealthily or remaining still above a body of water where prey may occur. It is a shy bird, and if disturbed, often points its bill directly upwards and freezes in that position, causing its <a href="/wiki/Crypsis" title="Crypsis">cryptic</a> plumage to blend into the surrounding reeds, an action known as <i>bitterning</i>. While in this position, the shield of elongated feathers on throat and breast droop downwards and hide the neck, so that the outline of the head and body is obscured. Sometimes it resorts to applying <a href="/wiki/Down_feather#Powder_down" title="Down feather">powder down</a> produced by patches of specialist <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">down feathers</a> at the side of its breast. This white dusty material seems to help it to rid its head and neck of slime after feeding on <a href="/wiki/Eel" title="Eel">eels</a>. It then removes the excess powder by scratching vigorously before applying <a href="/wiki/Uropygial_gland#Gland_secretion" title="Uropygial gland">preen oil</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Uropygial_gland" title="Uropygial gland">gland</a> at the base of its tail.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The bird has a secretive nature, keeping largely hidden in the reeds and coarse vegetation. Occasionally, especially in hard winter weather, it stands in the open beside the water's edge, although usually close to cover to facilitate a hasty retreat. In flight, its wings can be seen to be broad and rounded, and its legs trail behind it in typical heron fashion. Its neck is extended when it takes off, but is retracted when it has picked up speed. It seldom flies however, except when feeding young, preferring to move through the vegetation stealthily on foot. Its gait is slow and deliberate and it can clamber over reeds by gripping several at a time with its toes. It is most active at dawn and dusk, but also sometimes forages by day.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_17-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Eurasian bitterns feed on fish, small mammals, amphibians and invertebrates, hunting along the reed margins in shallow water. British records include eels up to 35&#160;cm (14&#160;in) and other fish, mice and voles, small birds and fledglings, frogs, newts, crabs, shrimps, molluscs, spiders and insects. In continental Europe, members of over twenty families of beetle are eaten, as well as <a href="/wiki/Dragonfly" title="Dragonfly">dragonflies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earwig" title="Earwig">earwigs</a>. Some vegetable matter such as aquatic plants is also consumed.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_17-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Males are polygamous, mating with up to five females. The nest is built in the previous year's standing reeds and consists of an untidy platform some 30&#160;cm (12&#160;in) across. It may be on a tussock surrounded by water or on matted roots close to water and is built by the female using bits of reed, sedges and grass stalks, with a lining of finer fragments. The eggs average 52 by 38&#160;mm (2.0 by 1.5&#160;in) and are non-glossy, olive-brown, with some darker speckling at the broader end. Four to six eggs are laid in late March and April and incubated by the female for about twenty-six days. After hatching, the chicks spend about two weeks in the nest before leaving to swim amongst the reeds. The female rears them without help from the male, regurgitating food into the nest from her crop, the young seizing her bill and pulling it down. They become fully fledged at about eight weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_17-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_bittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mating_call" title="Mating call">mating call</a> or contact call of the male is a deep, sighing <a href="/wiki/Fog-horn" class="mw-redirect" title="Fog-horn">fog-horn</a> or bull-like <i>boom</i> with a quick rise and an only slightly longer fall, easily audible from a distance of 3&#160;mi (5&#160;km) on a calm night. The call is mainly given between January and April during the mating season. Surveys of Eurasian bitterns are carried out by noting the number of distinct male booms in a given area. Prior to modern science, it was unknown how such a small bird produced a call so low-pitched: common explanations included that the bird made its call into a straw or that it blew directly into the water. It is now known that the sound is produced by expelling air from the <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">oesophagus</a> with the aid of powerful muscles surrounding it.<sup id="cite_ref-BBCRadio4_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBCRadio4-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_bittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Botaurus_stellaris_-Minsmere_RSPB_reserve,_Suffolk,_England-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Botaurus_stellaris_-Minsmere_RSPB_reserve%2C_Suffolk%2C_England-8.jpg/220px-Botaurus_stellaris_-Minsmere_RSPB_reserve%2C_Suffolk%2C_England-8.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Botaurus_stellaris_-Minsmere_RSPB_reserve%2C_Suffolk%2C_England-8.jpg/330px-Botaurus_stellaris_-Minsmere_RSPB_reserve%2C_Suffolk%2C_England-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Botaurus_stellaris_-Minsmere_RSPB_reserve%2C_Suffolk%2C_England-8.jpg/440px-Botaurus_stellaris_-Minsmere_RSPB_reserve%2C_Suffolk%2C_England-8.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3454" data-file-height="2307" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Botaurus_stellaris_-Minsmere_RSPB_reserve,_Suffolk,_England-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Foraging in shallow water</div></div></div>
<p>The Eurasian bittern has a very wide range and a large total population, estimated to be 110,000 to 340,000 individuals. The <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> has assessed its overall conservation status as being of "<a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least concern</a> because although the population trend is downward, the rate of decline is insufficient to justify rating it in a more threatened category. The chief threat the bird faces is destruction of reed beds and drainage and disturbance of its wetland habitats.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is one of the species to which the <a href="/wiki/Agreement_on_the_Conservation_of_African-Eurasian_Migratory_Waterbirds" title="Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a> (AEWA) applies.<sup id="cite_ref-AEWA_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AEWA-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The southern race has suffered catastrophic decline during the 20th century due to wetland degradation and, unlike the northern race, is of high conservation concern.<sup id="cite_ref-Allan1997_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allan1997-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United Kingdom the species was once thought extinct,<sup id="cite_ref-gwent_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gwent-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> but by 2007 there were an estimated 44 breeding pairs, concentrated mainly in <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Avery_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avery-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The Lancashire population at <a href="/wiki/Leighton_Moss_RSPB_reserve" title="Leighton Moss RSPB reserve">Leighton Moss RSPB reserve</a> declined in recent decades,<sup id="cite_ref-IBA_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IBA-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> while bitterns have been attracted to new reed beds in the <a href="/wiki/West_Country" title="West Country">West Country</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WMN_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WMN-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> After extensive reedbed restoration, nesting and breeding was observed in north Wales, while in 2020, two pairs successfully bred at <a href="/wiki/Newport_Wetlands" title="Newport Wetlands">Newport Wetlands</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gwent_(county)" title="Gwent (county)">Gwent</a>, south Wales. These were the first bitterns to breed in the county in some 250 years.<sup id="cite_ref-gwent_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gwent-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In the 21st century, bitterns are regular winter visitors to the <a href="/wiki/London_Wetland_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="London Wetland Centre">London Wetland Centre</a>, enabling city dwellers to view these scarce birds.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In Ireland, it died out as a breeding species in the mid-19th century, but in 2011 a single bird was spotted in <a href="/wiki/County_Wexford" title="County Wexford">County Wexford</a> and there have been a number of subsequent sightings.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature">In literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_bittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Bittern_Thomas_Bewick_1804.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/The_Bittern_Thomas_Bewick_1804.jpg/220px-The_Bittern_Thomas_Bewick_1804.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="308" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/The_Bittern_Thomas_Bewick_1804.jpg/330px-The_Bittern_Thomas_Bewick_1804.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/The_Bittern_Thomas_Bewick_1804.jpg/440px-The_Bittern_Thomas_Bewick_1804.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1449" data-file-height="2031" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Bittern_Thomas_Bewick_1804.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wood engraving "The Bittern, Bog-Bumper, Bitter-Bum or Mire-Drum" from <i><a href="/wiki/A_History_of_British_Birds" title="A History of British Birds">A History of British Birds</a></i>, Volume 2, "Water Birds", by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bewick" title="Thomas Bewick">Thomas Bewick</a>, 1804</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bewick" title="Thomas Bewick">Thomas Bewick</a> records that the bittern "was formerly held in much estimation at the tables of the great".<sup id="cite_ref-Bewick_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bewick-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Booming">Booming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_bittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Booming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Eurasian bittern is proposed as one of rational explanations behind the <a href="/wiki/Drekavac" title="Drekavac">drekavac</a>, a creature of the graveyard and darkness originating in south Slavic mythology.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> It is mentioned in the short story "Brave Mita and Drekavac from the Pond" by <a href="/wiki/Branko_%C4%86opi%C4%87" title="Branko Ćopić">Branko Ćopić</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Copic_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Copic-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 18th-century Scottish poet <a href="/wiki/James_Thomson_(poet)" class="mw-redirect" title="James Thomson (poet)">James Thomson</a> refers to the bittern's "boom" in his poem "Spring" (written 1728), published as part of his <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seasons_(Thomson)" title="The Seasons (Thomson)">The Seasons</a></i> (1735):<sup id="cite_ref-Thomson_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomson-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r960796168">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The Bittern knows the time, with bill ingulpht<br />To shake the sounding marsh<sup id="cite_ref-Thomson_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomson-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>The species is mentioned in <a href="/wiki/George_Crabbe" title="George Crabbe">George Crabbe</a>'s 1810 narrative poem <i><a href="/wiki/The_Borough_(George_Crabbe_poem)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Borough (George Crabbe poem)">The Borough</a></i>, to emphasise the ostracised, solitary life of the poem's villain, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Grimes" title="Peter Grimes">Peter Grimes</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-Crabbe_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crabbe-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r960796168"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>And the loud Bittern from the bull-rush home<br />Gave from the Salt-ditch side the bellowing boom:<sup id="cite_ref-Crabbe_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crabbe-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>The Irish poet <a href="/wiki/Thomas_MacDonagh" title="Thomas MacDonagh">Thomas MacDonagh</a> translated the Gaelic poem "The Yellow Bittern" ("<a href="/wiki/An_Bonn%C3%A1n_Bu%C3%AD" title="An Bonnán Buí">An Bonnán Buí</a>" in Irish) by <a href="/wiki/Cathal_Bu%C3%AD_Mac_Giolla_Ghunna" title="Cathal Buí Mac Giolla Ghunna">Cathal Buí Mac Giolla Ghunna</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> His friend the poet <a href="/wiki/Francis_Ledwidge" title="Francis Ledwidge">Francis Ledwidge</a> wrote a  "Lament for Thomas MacDonagh" with the opening line "He shall not hear the bittern cry".<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a> novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hound_of_the_Baskervilles" title="The Hound of the Baskervilles">The Hound of the Baskervilles</a></i> by Sir <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Arthur Conan Doyle">Arthur Conan Doyle</a>, the naturalist Stapleton proposes the boom of a bittern as an explanation for the howl attributed to the mystical hound.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>
Because of its secretive and skulking nature, it was for long unclear exactly how the bittern produced its distinctive booming call. A <a href="/wiki/Mediaeval" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediaeval">Mediaeval</a> theory held that the bittern thrust its beak into the boggy ground of the marsh in which it lived, making its vocalization which was amplified and deepened as it reverberated through the water. A reference to this theory appears in 1476 in <a href="/wiki/Chaucer" class="mw-redirect" title="Chaucer">Chaucer</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Wife_of_Bath%27s_Tale" title="The Wife of Bath&#39;s Tale">The Wife of Bath's Tale</a>, lines 972-73:<sup id="cite_ref-chaucer_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chaucer-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r960796168"/></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>And, as a bitore bombleth in the myre,<br />She leyde hir mouth un-to the water doun<sup id="cite_ref-chaucer_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chaucer-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>The English 17th century physician <a href="/wiki/Sir_Thomas_Browne" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Thomas Browne">Sir Thomas Browne</a> disputed this claim, stating in his <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudodoxia_Epidemica" title="Pseudodoxia Epidemica">Pseudodoxia Epidemica</a></i>, Book III, Ch.27: "That a Bittor maketh that mugient noise, or as we term it Bumping, by putting its bill into a reed as most believe, or as <i>Bellonius</i> and <i>Aldrovandus</i> conceive, by putting the same in water or mud, and after a while retaining the air by suddenly excluding it again, is not so easily made out. For my own part, though after diligent enquiry, I could never behold them in this motion".<sup id="cite_ref-Browne1646_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Browne1646-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Browne even kept a captive bittern to discover how its "boom" was produced.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Invisibility">Invisibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_bittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Invisibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The artist <a href="/wiki/Abbott_Handerson_Thayer" title="Abbott Handerson Thayer">Abbott Handerson Thayer</a> argued in his ill-advised<sup id="cite_ref-Roosevelt_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roosevelt-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> 1909 venture into zoology, <i><a href="/wiki/Concealing-Coloration_in_the_Animal_Kingdom" title="Concealing-Coloration in the Animal Kingdom">Concealing-Coloration in the Animal Kingdom</a></i>, that animals were concealed by a combination of <a href="/wiki/Countershading" title="Countershading">countershading</a> and <a href="/wiki/Disruptive_coloration" title="Disruptive coloration">disruptive coloration</a>, which together "obliterated" their self-shadowing and their shape. On the disruptive effect of bittern plumage, he wrote:<sup id="cite_ref-Thayer_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thayer-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r960796168"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>I was watching a standing [American] Bittern at a distance of about ten feet. The light stripes on the bill were repeated and continued by the light stripes on the sides of the head and neck, and together they imitated very closely the look of separate, bright reed-stems; while the <i>dark</i> stripes pictured reeds in shadow, or the shadowed interstices between the stems.<sup id="cite_ref-Thayer_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thayer-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>On the Eurasian bittern's markings, he wrote:<sup id="cite_ref-Thayer_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thayer-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r960796168"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Reed-like patterns occur also ... on the necks of some of the true herons ... The beautiful European Bittern has kindred markings with a strong admixture of richly brindled grass-pattern—a pattern at once bold and subtile, whose obliterative effect is the bird's normal environment must be consummate.<sup id="cite_ref-Thayer_40-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thayer-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>The zoologist <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Cott" class="mw-redirect" title="Hugh Cott">Hugh Cott</a>, in his classic 1940 study of <a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">camouflage</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Adaptive_Coloration_in_Animals" title="Adaptive Coloration in Animals">Adaptive Coloration in Animals</a></i>, cites William Palmer's account of seeing a bittern:<sup id="cite_ref-Cott_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cott-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r960796168"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>he once marked the place in a marsh where one of these birds had alighted: on reaching the spot he had the 'greatest difficulty in finding it clinging motionless, with bill almost erect, to a stem of wild oats'.<sup id="cite_ref-Cott_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cott-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
