<p><b>Merlin</b> (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Myrddin</i>, <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">Marzhin</i>, <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a>: <i lang="br">Merzhin</i>) is a mythological figure prominently featured in the legend of <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a> and best known as an <a href="/wiki/Magician_(fantasy)" title="Magician (fantasy)">enchanter or wizard</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup> His standard depiction, based on an amalgamation of historical and legendary figures, was introduced by the 12th-century British author <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_of_Monmouth" title="Geoffrey of Monmouth">Geoffrey of Monmouth</a>. Geoffrey  primarily combined existing stories of <a href="/wiki/Myrddin_Wyllt" title="Myrddin Wyllt">Myrddin Wyllt</a> (or Merlinus Caledonensis), a <a href="/wiki/Hen_Ogledd" title="Hen Ogledd">North Brythonic</a> prophet and madman with no connection to Arthur, with tales of the <a href="/wiki/Romano-British" class="mw-redirect" title="Romano-British">Romano-British</a> war leader <a href="/wiki/Ambrosius_Aurelianus" title="Ambrosius Aurelianus">Ambrosius Aurelianus</a> to form the composite figure he called <b>Merlinus Ambrosius</b> (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Myrddin Emrys</i>, <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a>: <i lang="br">Merzhin Ambroaz</i>).
Geoffrey's rendering of the character was immediately popular, especially in <a href="/wiki/Wales_in_the_Middle_Ages" title="Wales in the Middle Ages">Wales</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Later writers in France and elsewhere expanded the account to produce a fuller image, creating one of the most important figures in the imagination and literature of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.
</p><p>Merlin's traditional biography casts him as a <a href="/wiki/Cambion" title="Cambion">cambion</a>, a being born of a mortal woman, sired by an <a href="/wiki/Incubus" title="Incubus">incubus</a>, from whom he inherits his supernatural powers and abilities,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> most commonly and notably <a href="/wiki/Prophecy" title="Prophecy">prophecy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shapeshifting" title="Shapeshifting">shapeshifting</a>. Merlin matures to an ascendant sagehood and engineers the birth of Arthur through magic and intrigue.<sup id="cite_ref-thorpe_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thorpe-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Later authors have Merlin serve as the king's advisor and mentor until he disappears from the story after having been bewitched and forever sealed or killed by his student known as the <a href="/wiki/Lady_of_the_Lake" title="Lady of the Lake">Lady of the Lake</a> after falling madly in love with her. He is popularly said to be buried in the magical forest of <a href="/wiki/Broc%C3%A9liande" title="Brocéliande">Brocéliande</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-The_enchanted_wood_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_enchanted_wood-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Merlin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nuremberg_chronicles_f_138r_5.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Nuremberg_chronicles_f_138r_5.jpg/170px-Nuremberg_chronicles_f_138r_5.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="178" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c9/Nuremberg_chronicles_f_138r_5.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="215" data-file-height="225" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nuremberg_chronicles_f_138r_5.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Merlinus (Merlin) in the <i><a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Chronicle" title="Nuremberg Chronicle">Nuremberg Chronicle</a></i> (1493)</div></div></div>
<p>The name "Merlin" is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Myrddin" class="mw-redirect" title="Myrddin">Myrddin</a></i>, the name of the <a href="/wiki/Bard" title="Bard">bard</a> who was one of the chief sources for the later legendary figure. <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_of_Monmouth" title="Geoffrey of Monmouth">Geoffrey of Monmouth</a> <a href="/wiki/Latinisation_(literature)" class="mw-redirect" title="Latinisation (literature)">Latinised</a> the name to <i>Merlinus</i> in his works. Medievalist <a href="/wiki/Gaston_Paris" title="Gaston Paris">Gaston Paris</a> suggests that Geoffrey chose the form <i>Merlinus</i> rather than the expected <i>*Merdinus</i> to avoid a resemblance to the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">Anglo-Norman</a> word <i>merde</i> (from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>merda</i>) for <a href="/wiki/Faeces" class="mw-redirect" title="Faeces">feces</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Other suggestions are that 'Merlin' is an adjective, from the French <i>merle</i> meaning '<a href="/wiki/Common_blackbird" title="Common blackbird">blackbird</a>',<sup id="cite_ref-MPN_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MPN-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>79</span></sup> or that the 'many names' deriving from Myrddin stem from the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">myrdd</i>: myriad.<sup id="cite_ref-dames_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dames-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LGW_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LGW-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Clas_Myrddin" title="Clas Myrddin">Clas Myrddin</a> or <i>Merlin's Enclosure</i> is an early name for <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> stated in the Third Series of <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Triads" title="Welsh Triads">Welsh Triads</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Celticist A. O. H. Jarman suggests that the Welsh name <i><i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">Myrddin</i></i> (<small>Welsh pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Welsh" title="Help:IPA/Welsh">[ˈmərðin]</a></span>) was derived from the toponym <i>Caerfyrddin</i>, the Welsh name for the town known in English as <a href="/wiki/Carmarthen" title="Carmarthen">Carmarthen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Koch321_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koch321-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> This contrasts with the popular <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">folk etymology</a> that the town was named after the bard. The name Carmarthen is derived from the town's previous <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> name <a href="/wiki/Moridunum_(Carmarthen)" title="Moridunum (Carmarthen)">Moridunum</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-OED_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Koch321_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koch321-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> in turn derived from Celtic <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">Brittonic</a> <i>moridunon</i>, 'sea fortress'.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Geoffrey's composite Merlin is based mostly on the madman, poet and seer <a href="/wiki/Myrddin_Wyllt" title="Myrddin Wyllt">Myrddin Wyllt</a>, a madman, <a href="/wiki/Poet" title="Poet">poet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seer" class="mw-disambig" title="Seer">seer </a> known also as "Myrddin the Wild" (or <i>Merlinus Caledonensis</i> in later sources influenced by Geoffrey). He was also influenced by Emrys (<a href="/wiki/Old_Welsh" title="Old Welsh">Old Welsh</a>: <i>Embreis</i>), a character based in part on the 5th century historical war leader <a href="/wiki/Ambrosius_Aurelianus" title="Ambrosius Aurelianus">Ambrosius Aurelianus</a>, who was mentioned in one of Geoffrey's primary sources, the early 9th-century <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_Brittonum" title="Historia Brittonum">Historia Brittonum</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In British poetry, Myrddin was a <a href="/wiki/Bard" title="Bard">bard</a> driven mad after witnessing the horrors of war, who fled civilization to become a <a href="/wiki/Wild_man" title="Wild man">wild man of the wood</a> in the 6th century.<sup id="cite_ref-dames_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dames-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This madman, also known as <a href="/wiki/Lailoken" title="Lailoken">Lailoken</a>, has parallels with the Irish <a href="/wiki/Buile_Shuibhne" title="Buile Shuibhne">Suibhne</a> (Sweeney),<sup id="cite_ref-MPN_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MPN-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>58</span></sup> roams the <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_Forest" title="Caledonian Forest">Caledonian Forest</a>, until cured of his madness by Kentigern (<a href="/wiki/Saint_Mungo" title="Saint Mungo">Saint Mungo</a>). Geoffrey had Myrddin in mind when he wrote his earliest surviving work, the <i><a href="/wiki/Prophetiae_Merlini" title="Prophetiae Merlini">Prophetiae Merlini</a></i> ("Prophecies of Merlin", c. 1130), which he claimed were the actual words of the legendary poet and madman. Geoffrey's <i>Prophetiae</i> reveal little about Merlin's background. 
</p><p>Gefoffrey included the prophet in his next work, <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_Regum_Britanniae" title="Historia Regum Britanniae">Historia Regum Britanniae</a></i> (c. 1136). He supplemented his characterisation by attributing to Merlin stories concernig Aurelius Ambrosius, taken from <a href="/wiki/Nennius" title="Nennius">Nennius</a>' <i>Historia Brittonum</i>. In Nennius' account, Ambrosius was discovered when the British king <a href="/wiki/Vortigern" title="Vortigern">Vortigern</a> attempted to errect a tower at <a href="/wiki/Dinas_Emrys" title="Dinas Emrys">Dinas Emrys</a>. More than once, the tower collapsed before completion. Vortigen's wise men advised him that the only solution was to sprinkle the foundation with the blood of a child born without a father. Ambrosius was rumoured to be such a child. When brought before the king, Ambrosius revealed that below the foundation of the tower was a lake containing two dragons, battling into each other. (This represented the struggle between the invading <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxons</a> and the native <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Britons" title="Celtic Britons">Celtic Britons</a>.) Geoffrey retold the story in his <i>Historia Regum Britanniæ</i> with some embellishments, and gives the fatherless child the name of the prophetic bard Merlin. He went on to add new episodes that tie Merlin with <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a> and his predecessors. Geoffrey kept this new character separate from Aurelius Ambrosius and stated that Ambrosius was also called "Merlin", therefore Ambrosius Merlinus.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BLEgerton3028Fol30rStonehengeCropped.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/BLEgerton3028Fol30rStonehengeCropped.jpg/170px-BLEgerton3028Fol30rStonehengeCropped.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="179" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/BLEgerton3028Fol30rStonehengeCropped.jpg/255px-BLEgerton3028Fol30rStonehengeCropped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/BLEgerton3028Fol30rStonehengeCropped.jpg/340px-BLEgerton3028Fol30rStonehengeCropped.jpg 2x" data-file-width="613" data-file-height="645" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BLEgerton3028Fol30rStonehengeCropped.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Giants help the young Merlin build <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">Stonehenge</a> in a manuscript of <a href="/wiki/Wace" title="Wace">Wace</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Roman_de_Brut" title="Roman de Brut">Roman de Brut</a></i> (c. 1325-1350)</div></div></div>
<p>Geoffrey's account of Merlin Ambrosius' early life is based on the tale of Ambrosius in the <i>Historia Brittonum</i>. He added his own embellishments to the tale, which he set in <a href="/wiki/Carmarthen" title="Carmarthen">Carmarthen</a>, Wales (Welsh: Caerfyrddin). While Nennius' Ambrosius eventually reveals himself to be the son of a Roman <a href="/wiki/Consul" title="Consul">consul</a>, Geoffrey's Merlin is begotten on a king's daughter by an <a href="/wiki/Incubus" title="Incubus">incubus</a> demon. The name of Merlin's mother is not usually stated, but is given as Adhan in the oldest version of the <a href="/wiki/Brut_Chronicle" title="Brut Chronicle">Prose <i>Brut</i></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The story of Vortigern's tower is the same; the underground dragons, one white and one red, represent the Saxons and the Britons, and their final battle is a portent of things to come. At this point Geoffrey inserted a long section of Merlin's prophecies, taken from his earlier <i>Prophetiae Merlini</i>. He told two further tales of the character. In the first, Merlin creates <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">Stonehenge</a> as a burial place for Aurelius Ambrosius, bringing the stones from Ireland. (The stones, in actuality, came from the <a href="/wiki/Preseli_Hills" title="Preseli Hills">Preseli Hills</a> in south-west Wales.)<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In the second, Merlin's magic enables the new British king <a href="/wiki/Uther_Pendragon" title="Uther Pendragon">Uther Pendragon</a> to enter into <a href="/wiki/Tintagel_Castle" title="Tintagel Castle">Tintagel Castle</a> in disguise and to father his son Arthur with his enemy's wife, Igerna (<a href="/wiki/Igraine" title="Igraine">Igraine</a>). These episodes appear in many later adaptations of Geoffrey's account. As <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Thorpe" title="Lewis Thorpe">Lewis Thorpe</a> notes, Merlin disappears from the narrative subsequently. He does not tutor and advise Arthur as in later versions.<sup id="cite_ref-thorpe_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thorpe-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Geoffrey dealt with Merlin again in his third work, <i><a href="/wiki/Vita_Merlini" title="Vita Merlini">Vita Merlini</a></i> (1150). He based it on stories of the original 6th-century Myrddin, set long after his time frame for the life of Merlin Ambrosius. Geoffrey asserted that the characters are the same with references to King Arthur and his death, as told in the <i>Historia Regum Britanniae</i>. Here, Merlin survives Arthur, marries a woman named Guendoloena (inspired by the male Gwenddoleu ap Ceidio),<sup id="cite_ref-thorpe_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thorpe-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>44</span></sup> and eventually spends his time observing stars from his <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esplumoir&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Esplumoir (page does not exist)">esplumoir</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;&#91;<a href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esplumoir_Merlin" class="extiw" title="fr:Esplumoir Merlin">fr</a>&#93;</span></i> with seventy windows, in the remote woods in the land of <a href="/wiki/Rhydderch_Hael" title="Rhydderch Hael">Rhydderch</a>. There, he is often visited by his sister <a href="/wiki/Gwenddydd" title="Gwenddydd">Ganieda</a> (based on Myrddin's sister Gwenddydd) who has become queen of the <a href="/wiki/Cumbrians" class="mw-redirect" title="Cumbrians">Cumbrians</a> and is also endowed with prophetic powers.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Merlin_and_the_Knight.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Merlin_and_the_Knight.png/170px-Merlin_and_the_Knight.png" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Merlin_and_the_Knight.png/255px-Merlin_and_the_Knight.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Merlin_and_the_Knight.png/340px-Merlin_and_the_Knight.png 2x" data-file-width="1545" data-file-height="2067" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Merlin_and_the_Knight.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An illustration of Merlin as druid<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> in <i>The Rose</i> (1848)</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Tolstoy" title="Nikolai Tolstoy">Nikolai Tolstoy</a> hypothesized that Merlin is based on a historical personage, probably a 6th century <a href="/wiki/Druid" title="Druid">druid</a> living in southern Scotland. His argument was based on the fact that early references to Merlin describe him as possessing characteristics which modern scholarship (but not that of the time the sources were written) would recognize as druidical, the inference being that those characteristics were not invented by the early chroniclers, but belonged to a real person.<sup id="cite_ref-tolstoy_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tolstoy-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> If so, the hypothetical Merlin would have lived about a century after the hypothetical historical Arthur. A late version of the <i><a href="/wiki/Annales_Cambriae" title="Annales Cambriae">Annales Cambriae</a></i> (dubbed the "B-text", written at the end of the 13th century) and influenced by Geoffrey,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> records for the year 573, that after "the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Arfderydd" title="Battle of Arfderydd">battle of Arfderydd</a>, between the sons of Eliffer and Gwenddolau son of Ceidio; in which battle Gwenddolau fell; Merlin went mad." The earliest version of the <i>Annales Cambriae</i> entry (in the "A-text", written c. 1100), as well as a later copy (the "C-text", written towards the end of the 13th century) do not mention Merlin.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Myrddin/Merlin also shares similarities with the shamanic bard figure of <a href="/wiki/Taliesin" title="Taliesin">Taliesin</a>, alongside whom he appears in the Welsh Triads and in <i>Vita Merlini</i>. <i><a href="/wiki/Clas_Myrddin" title="Clas Myrddin">Clas Myrddin</a></i> is also one of the early names for <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> given in the Welsh Triads. </p><div style="clear:left;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_legend">Later legend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Merlin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Later legend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Histoire_de_Merlin.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Histoire_de_Merlin.png/220px-Histoire_de_Merlin.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="143" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Histoire_de_Merlin.png/330px-Histoire_de_Merlin.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Histoire_de_Merlin.png/440px-Histoire_de_Merlin.png 2x" data-file-width="1351" data-file-height="880" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Histoire_de_Merlin.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The story of Merlin's unholy birth as told in <i><a href="/wiki/Merlin_(poem)" title="Merlin (poem)">Merlin</a></i>. A prose version illumination by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Colombe" title="Jean Colombe">Jean Colombe</a> (c. 1480–1485)</div></div></div>
<p>Decades after, <a href="/wiki/Robert_de_Boron" title="Robert de Boron">Robert de Boron</a> retold and expanded on this material in his influential <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> poem <i><a href="/wiki/Merlin_(poem)" title="Merlin (poem)">Merlin</a></i>. Only a few lines of the poem have survived, but a prose version became popular and was incorporated into Arthurian <a href="/wiki/Chivalric_romance" title="Chivalric romance">chivalric romance</a> literature. In Robert's account, as in Geoffrey's <i>Historia</i>, Merlin was created as a demon spawn, but here explicitly to become the <a href="/wiki/Antichrist" title="Antichrist">Antichrist</a> who is to reverse the effect of the <a href="/wiki/Harrowing_of_Hell" title="Harrowing of Hell">Harrowing of Hell</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;note 2&#93;</a></sup> This infernal plot is thwarted when a priest named <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blaise_(Arthurian_legend)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Blaise (Arthurian legend) (page does not exist)">Blaise</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;&#91;<a href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blaise_(l%C3%A9gende_arthurienne)" class="extiw" title="fr:Blaise (légende arthurienne)">fr</a>&#93;</span> immediately baptizes the boy at birth (in <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>), thus freeing him from the power of Satan and his intended destiny.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The demonic legacy invests Merlin with a preternatural knowledge of the past and present, which is supplemented by God, who gives the boy a prophetic knowledge of the future. Robert lays great emphasis on Merlin's power to <a href="/wiki/Shapeshift" class="mw-redirect" title="Shapeshift">shapeshift</a>, on his joking personality, and on his connection to the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a>, the quest for which he foretells. Inspired by <a href="/wiki/Wace" title="Wace">Wace</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Roman_de_Brut" title="Roman de Brut">Roman de Brut</a></i>, an <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">Anglo-Norman</a> adaptation of Geoffrey's <i>Historia</i>, <i>Merlin</i> was originally a part of a cycle of Robert's poems telling the story of the Grail over the centuries. The narrative of <i>Merlin</i> is largely based on Geoffrey's familiar tale of Vortigern's Tower, Uther's war against the Saxons, and Arthur's conception. What follows is a new episode of the young Arthur's drawing of the <a href="/wiki/Excalibur" title="Excalibur">sword from the stone</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> an event orchestrated by Merlin. He also earlier instructs Uther to establish the original order of the <a href="/wiki/Round_Table" title="Round Table">Round Table</a>, after creating the table itself. The prose version of Robert's poem was then continued in the 13th-century <i>Merlin Continuation</i> or the <i>Suite de Merlin</i>, describing King Arthur's early wars and Merlin's role in them as he predicts and influences the course of battles.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;note 3&#93;</a></sup> He also helps Arthur in other ways, including providing him with the magic sword <a href="/wiki/Excalibur" title="Excalibur">Excalibur</a> through a <a href="/wiki/Lady_of_the_Lake" title="Lady of the Lake">Lady of the Lake</a>. Here, Merlin's shapeshifting powers are also featured prominently.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;note 4&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Conception_of_Merlin.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Conception_of_Merlin.jpg/170px-Conception_of_Merlin.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="201" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Conception_of_Merlin.jpg/255px-Conception_of_Merlin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Conception_of_Merlin.jpg/340px-Conception_of_Merlin.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1014" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Conception_of_Merlin.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Conception of Merlin in <i><a href="/wiki/Lancelot_en_prose" class="mw-redirect" title="Lancelot en prose">Lancelot en prose</a></i> (c. 1494)</div></div></div>
<p>The extended prose rendering became the foundation for the vast <a href="/wiki/Lancelot-Grail" title="Lancelot-Grail">Lancelot-Grail</a> cyclical series of Old French prose works also known as the Vulgate Cycle. Eventually, it was directly incorporated into the Vulgate Cycle as the <i>Estoire de Merlin</i>, also known as the Vulgate <i>Merlin</i> or the Prose <i>Merlin</i>. A further reworking and continuation of the Prose <i>Merlin</i> was included within the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Post-Vulgate_Cycle" title="Post-Vulgate Cycle">Post-Vulgate Cycle</a> as the Post-Vulgate <i>Suite du Merlin</i> or the Huth <i>Merlin</i>. All these variants have been adapted and translated into several other languages, and further modified. Notably, the Post-Vulgate <i>Suite</i> (along with an earlier version of the Prose <i>Merlin</i>) was the main source for the opening part of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Malory" title="Thomas Malory">Thomas Malory</a>'s English-language compilation work <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Morte_d%27Arthur" title="Le Morte d&#39;Arthur">Le Morte d'Arthur</a></i> that formed a now-iconic version of the legend. Compared to his French sources, Malory limited the extent of the negative association of Merlin and his powers, relatively rarely being condemned as demonic by other characters such as <a href="/wiki/King_Lot" title="King Lot">King Lot</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Conversely, Merlin even appeared to be inherently evil in the early, pre-cycle version of the <a href="/wiki/Prose_Lancelot" class="mw-redirect" title="Prose Lancelot">Prose <i>Lancelot</i></a>  in which he was born from not a supernatural rape of a virgin but a consensual union between a princess and a demon,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> and was never baptized.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Merlin_by_Louis_Rhead.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Merlin_by_Louis_Rhead.jpg/170px-Merlin_by_Louis_Rhead.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="283" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Merlin_by_Louis_Rhead.jpg/255px-Merlin_by_Louis_Rhead.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/83/Merlin_by_Louis_Rhead.jpg 2x" data-file-width="332" data-file-height="553" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Merlin_by_Louis_Rhead.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Merlin, the Enchanter</i> by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Rhead" title="Louis Rhead">Louis Rhead</a> (1923)</div></div></div>
<p>Later medieval works also deal with the Merlin legend, including through unusual stories such as <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Roman_de_Silence" title="Le Roman de Silence">Le Roman de Silence</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> As the Arthurian myths were retold, Merlin's prophetic aspects were sometimes de-emphasised in favour of portraying him as a <a href="/wiki/Magician_(fantasy)" title="Magician (fantasy)">wizard</a> and an advisor to the young Arthur, sometimes in struggle between good and evil sides of his character, and living in deep forests connected with nature. Through his ability to change his shape, he may appear as a "wild man" figure evoking that of his prototype Myrddin Wyllt,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> as a civilized man of any age, or even as a talking animal.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;note 5&#93;</a></sup> In the <i>Perceval en prose</i> (also known as the Didot <i>Perceval</i> and too attributed to Robert), where Merlin is the initiator of the Grail Quest, he eventually retires by turning himself into a bird. In the Vulgate Cycle's version of <i>Merlin</i>, his acts include arranging consummation of Arthur's desire for "the most beautiful maiden ever born," Lady Lisanor of Cardigan, resulting in the birth of Arthur's illegitimate son <a href="/wiki/Lohot" class="mw-redirect" title="Lohot">Lohot</a> from before the marriage to <a href="/wiki/Guinevere" title="Guinevere">Guinevere</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> But <a href="/wiki/Fate" class="mw-redirect" title="Fate">fate</a> cannot always be changed: the Post-Vulgate Cycle has Merlin warn Arthur of how the birth of his other son will bring great misfortune and ruin to his kingdom, which then becomes a <a href="/wiki/Self-fulfilling_prophecy" title="Self-fulfilling prophecy">self-fulfilling prophecy</a>. Eventually, long after Merlin is gone, his advice to dispose of the baby <a href="/wiki/Mordred" title="Mordred">Mordred</a> through an event evoking the Biblical <a href="/wiki/Massacre_of_the_Innocents" title="Massacre of the Innocents">Massacre of the Innocents</a> leads leads to the deaths of many, among them Arthur.
</p><p>The earliest English verse romance concerning Merlin is <i><a href="/wiki/Of_Arthour_and_of_Merlin" title="Of Arthour and of Merlin">Of Arthour and of Merlin</a></i>, which drew from the chronicles and the Vulgate Cycle. In English-language medieval texts that conflate <a href="/wiki/Sub-Roman_Britain" title="Sub-Roman Britain">Britain</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a>, the Anglo-Saxon enemies against whom Merlin aids first Uther and then Arthur tend to be replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Saracen" title="Saracen">Saracens</a><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> or simply just invading pagans. Meanwhile, some of the many Welsh works predicting the Celtic revenge and victory over the Saxons have been reinterpreted as Merlin's (Myrddin's) prophecies, later used by propaganda of the Welsh-descent king <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII of England</a>. The <i>Prophéties de Merlin</i> (c. 1276) contains long prophecies of Merlin (mostly concerned with 11th to 13th-century Italian history and contemporary politics), some by his ghost after his death, interspersed with episodes relating Merlin's deeds and with assorted Arthurian adventures in which Merlin does not appear at all. Even more political Italian text was <a href="/wiki/Joachim_of_Fiore" title="Joachim of Fiore">Joachim of Fiore</a>'s <i>Expositio Sybillae et Merlini</i>, directed against <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor</a> whom the author regarded as the Antichrist. The earliest Merlin text in German was <a href="/wiki/Caesarius_of_Heisterbach" title="Caesarius of Heisterbach">Caesarius of Heisterbach</a>'s <i>Dialogus Miraculorum</i> (1220), originally in Latin. <a href="/wiki/Ulrich_F%C3%BCetrer" title="Ulrich Füetrer">Ulrich Füetrer</a>'s 15th-century <i>Buch der Abenteuer</i> presents Merlin as Uter's father, effectively making his grandson Arthur a part-devil too.
</p>

<p>In chivalric romance tradition, Merlin has a major weakness that eventually leads him to his doom: young beautiful women of <i><a href="/wiki/Femme_fatale" title="Femme fatale">femme fatale</a></i> archetype. His apprentice is often Arthur's half-sister <a href="/wiki/Morgan_le_Fay" title="Morgan le Fay">Morgan le Fay</a> (in the <i>Prophéties de Merlin</i> along with <a href="/wiki/Sebile" title="Sebile">Sebile</a> and two other witch queens), who is sometimes depicted as Merlin's lover<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> and sometimes as just an <a href="/wiki/Unrequited_love" title="Unrequited love">unrequited love</a> interest.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;note 6&#93;</a></sup> While Merlin does share his magic with them, his prophetic powers cannot be passed on. Contrary to the many modern works in which they are archenemies, Merlin and Morgan are never opposed to each other in any medieval tradition, other than Morgan forcibly rejecting him in some texts; in fact, his love for Morgan is so great that he even lies to the king in order to save her in the Huth <i>Merlin</i>, which is the only instance of him ever intentionally misleading Arthur.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;note 7&#93;</a></sup> Instead, Merlin's eventual undoing comes from his lusting after another of his female students, named <a href="/wiki/Lady_of_the_Lake" title="Lady of the Lake">Viviane</a> (among other names and spellings, including Malory's popular Nimue); also called a <a href="/wiki/Fairy" title="Fairy">fairy</a> (French <i>fee</i>) like Morgan, Viviane is first found in the Lancelot-Grail cycle, having been inserted into the legend by either de Boron or his continuator. Merlin's fate of either demise or eternal imprisonment, along with his destroyer or captor's motivation (from her fear of Merlin and protecting her own virginity, to her jealously for his relationship with Morgan), is recounted differently in variants of this motif but is usually placed within the <a href="/wiki/Enchanted_forest" title="Enchanted forest">enchanted forest</a> of <a href="/wiki/Broc%C3%A9liande" title="Brocéliande">Brocéliande</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;note 8&#93;</a></sup> The form of his prison or grave can be variably a crystal cave, a hole under a large rock (as in <i>Le Morte d'Arthur</i>), a magic tower, or a tree.<sup id="cite_ref-loomis_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-loomis-27">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In some texts, including in <i>Le Morte d'Arthur</i>, she then replaces Merlin in the role of Arthur's court mage and adviser as a Lady of the Lake (the chief Lady in case of Malory's Nimue) following the 'last enchantement'.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Malory's telling of this episode would later become a major inspiration for <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romantic</a> authors and artists of the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;note 9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:315_The_Romance_of_King_Arthur.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/315_The_Romance_of_King_Arthur.jpg/220px-315_The_Romance_of_King_Arthur.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="272" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/315_The_Romance_of_King_Arthur.jpg/330px-315_The_Romance_of_King_Arthur.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/315_The_Romance_of_King_Arthur.jpg/440px-315_The_Romance_of_King_Arthur.jpg 2x" data-file-width="517" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:315_The_Romance_of_King_Arthur.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Merlin and <a href="/wiki/Lady_of_the_Lake" title="Lady of the Lake">Nimue</a> in <i>Romance of King Arthur</i> (1917) abridged from <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Morte_d%27Arthur" title="Le Morte d&#39;Arthur">Le Morte d'Arthur</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_W._Pollard" title="Alfred W. Pollard">Alfred W. Pollard</a>, illustrated by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Rackham" title="Arthur Rackham">Arthur Rackham</a>:<br /><small>"How by her subtle working she made Merlin to go under the stone to let wit of the marvels there and she wrought so there for him that he came never out for all the craft he could do."</small></div></div></div>
<p>There are many different versions of their story. Common themes in most of them include Merlin usually having the prior prophetic knowledge of her plot against him (one exception is the Spanish Post-Vulgate <i>Baladro</i> where his ability is dampened by lust<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>), but lacking either ability or will to counteract it in any way, along with her usually using one of his own spells against him. Niniane, as the Lady is known in the <i>Livre d'Artus</i> continuation of <i>Merlin</i>, breaks his heart prior to his later second relationship with Morgan, but here the text actually does not tell how exactly Merlin did vanish, other than relating his farewell to Blaise. In the Post-Vulgate <i>Suite</i>, the young King <a href="/wiki/Bagdemagus" title="Bagdemagus">Bagdemagus</a> manages to find the rock under which Merlin is entombed alive by Niviene; he communicates with Merlin, but cannot lift it. What follows next is supposedly narrated in the mysterious text <i>Conte del Brait</i> (<i>Tale of the Cry</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;note 10&#93;</a></sup> In the <i>Prophéties de Merlin</i> version, his tomb is unsuccessfully searched for by various parties, including by Morgan and her enchantresses, but cannot be accessed due to the deadly magic traps around it,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> while the Lady of the Lake comes to taunt Merlin by asking did he rot there yet.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-49">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In the Vulgate <i>Lancelot</i>, which predated the later Vulgate <i>Merlin</i>, she (aged just 12 at the time) instead makes Merlin sleep forever in a pit in the forest of Darnantes, "and that is where he remained, for never again did anyone see or hear of him or have news to tell of him."<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> In a version with a happier ending, contained within the <i>Premiers Faits</i> section of the <i>Livre du Graal</i>, Niniane peacefully confines him in Brocéliande with walls of air, visible only as a mist to others but as a beautiful yet unbreakable crystal tower to him (however Merlin's disembodied voice can escape his air prison, as he does speak to <a href="/wiki/Gawain" title="Gawain">Gawain</a><sup id="cite_ref-:0_49-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-49">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>), where they then spend almost every night together.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Besides evoking the final scenes from <i>Vita Merlini</i>, this one shares similarities with reverse scenarios in other works, where either Merlin himself is an object of one-sided desire by an amorous sorceress who plots to trap him or it is him who traps an unwilling lover.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;note 11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The legendary Brocéliande is often identified as the real-life <a href="/wiki/Paimpont_forest" title="Paimpont forest">Paimpont forest</a> in Brittany.<sup id="cite_ref-The_enchanted_wood_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_enchanted_wood-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Other purported sites of Merlin's burial include a cave deep inside Merlin's Hill (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Bryn Myrddin</i>), outside <a href="/wiki/Carmarthen" title="Carmarthen">Carmarthen</a>. Carmarthen is also associated with Merlin more generally, including through the 13th-century manuscript known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Book_of_Carmarthen" title="Black Book of Carmarthen">Black Book of Carmarthen</a></i> and the local lore of <a href="/wiki/Merlin%27s_Oak" title="Merlin&#39;s Oak">Merlin's Oak</a>. In <a href="/wiki/North_Wales" title="North Wales">North Welsh</a> tradition, Merlin retires to <a href="/wiki/Bardsey_Island" title="Bardsey Island">Bardsey Island</a> (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Ynys Enlli</i>), where he lives in a house of glass (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Tŷ Gwydr</i>) with the <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Treasures_of_the_Island_of_Britain" title="Thirteen Treasures of the Island of Britain">Thirteen Treasures of the Island of Britain</a> (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Tri Thlws ar Ddeg Ynys Prydain</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-ashe08_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ashe08-57">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>200</span></sup> One site of his tomb is said to be <a href="/wiki/Marlborough_Mound" title="Marlborough Mound">Marlborough Mound</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> known in medieval times as <i>Merlebergia</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;note 12&#93;</a></sup> Another site associated with Merlin's burial, in his 'Merlin Silvestris' aspect, is the <a href="/wiki/Confluence" title="Confluence">confluence</a> of the Pausalyl Burn and <a href="/wiki/River_Tweed" title="River Tweed">River Tweed</a> in <a href="/wiki/Drumelzier" title="Drumelzier">Drumelzier</a>, Scotland. The 15th-century <i><a href="/wiki/Scotichronicon" title="Scotichronicon">Scotichronicon</a></i> tells that Merlin himself underwent a <a href="/wiki/Threefold_death" title="Threefold death">triple-death</a>, at the hands of some shepherds of the under-king <a href="/wiki/Meldred" title="Meldred">Meldred</a>: stoned and beaten by the shepherds, he falls over a cliff and is impaled on a stake, his head falls forward into the water, and he drowns.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;note 13&#93;</a></sup> The fulfilment of another prophecy, ascribed to <a href="/wiki/Thomas_the_Rhymer" title="Thomas the Rhymer">Thomas the Rhymer</a>, came about when a spate of the Tweed and Pausayl occurred during the reign of the Scottish <a href="/wiki/James_VI_and_I" title="James VI and I">James VI and I</a> on the English throne: "When Tweed and Pausayl meet at Merlin's grave, / Scotland and England one king shall have."<sup id="cite_ref-dames_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dames-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>62</span></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_modern_fiction">In modern fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Merlin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In modern fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fiction_featuring_Merlin" title="Fiction featuring Merlin">Fiction featuring Merlin</a></div><p>
Merlin and stories involving him have continued to be popular from the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> to the presaent day, especially since the renewed interest in the legend of Arthur in modern times. As noted by Arthurian scholar Alan Lupack, "numerous novels, poems and plays center around Merlin. In <a href="/wiki/American_literature" title="American literature">American literature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular culture</a>, Merlin is perhaps the most frequently portrayed Arthurian character."<sup id="cite_ref-:1_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-61">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Diverting from his traditional role in the legends, Merlin is sometimes portrayed as villain, as in <a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">Mark Twain</a>'s humorous novel <i><a href="/wiki/A_Connecticut_Yankee_in_King_Arthur%27s_Court" title="A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur&#39;s Court">A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur's Court</a></i> (1889).<sup id="cite_ref-:1_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-61">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> </p><div style="clear:left;"></div>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Merlin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns"><ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-2"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-2">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">According to Alan Lupack, "Merlin plays many roles in Arthurian literature, including bard, prophet, magician, advisor, and warrior. Though usually a figure who supports Arthur and his vision of <a href="/wiki/Camelot" title="Camelot">Camelot</a>, Merlin is, because of the stories in which he is said to be the son of a devil, sometimes presented as a villain."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-22"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-22">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">One version of the <a href="/wiki/Prose_Tristan" title="Prose Tristan">Prose <i>Tristan</i></a> makes him by the same token a "half-brother" of the monster known as the <a href="/wiki/Questing_Beast" title="Questing Beast">Questing Beast</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-26"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-26">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In an example of Merlin's interventions, the Vulgate version has him conjure a magical mist that causes the forces of Arthur's enemy King Amant to clash with the Saxon army at Carmelide.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-28"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-28">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Merlin appears as a woodcutter with an axe about his neck, big shoes, a torn coat, bristly hair, and a large beard. He is later found in the forest of <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a> by a follower of Uther disguised as an ugly man and tending a great herd of beasts. He then appears first as a handsome man and then as a beautiful boy. Years later, he approaches Arthur disguised as a peasant wearing leather boots, a wool coat, a hood, and a belt of knotted sheepskin. He is described as tall, black and bristly, and as seeming cruel and fierce. Finally, he appears as an old man with a long beard, short and hunchbacked, in an old torn woolen coat, who carries a club and drives a multitude of beasts before him.<sup id="cite_ref-loomis_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-loomis-27">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-35"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-35">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In the <i>Livre d'Artus</i>, for instance, Merlin enters Rome in the form of a huge stag with a white fore-foot. He bursts into the presence of <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a> (here Arthur's contemporary) and tells the emperor that only the wild man of the woods can interpret the dream that has been troubling him. Later, he returns in the form of a black, shaggy man, barefoot, with a torn coat. In another episode, he decides to do something that will be spoken of forever. Going into the forest of Brocéliande, he transforms himself into a herdsman carrying a club and wearing a wolf-skin and leggings. He is large, bent, black, lean, hairy and old, and his ears hang down to his waist. His head is as big as a buffalo's, his hair is down to his waist, he has a hump on his back, his feet and hands are backwards, he is hideous, and is over 18 feet tall. By his arts, he calls a herd of deer to come and graze around him.<sup id="cite_ref-loomis_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-loomis-27">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-41"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-41">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">As summarized by <a href="/wiki/Anne_Berthelot" title="Anne Berthelot">Anne Berthelot</a>, depending on the version of the narrative, "it may be that a lustful Merlin seduces an (almost) innocent Morgue [Morgan], thus pushing her to her <i>déchéance</i> (downfall). Or Morgue may appear as an ambitious and unscrupulous bitch ready to seduce an old tottering Merlin in order to gain the wisdom he alone can dispense."<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-43"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-43">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Merlin also otherwise protects Morgan in several other texts, including warning her of Arthur's wrath in Malory's telling of the plot of <a href="/wiki/Accolon" title="Accolon">Accolon</a>.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-44"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-44">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In the Post-Vulgate <i>Suite du Merlin</i>, for example, Viviane (Niviene) is introduced as a young teenage princess. She is about to depart from Arthur's court following her initial episode but, with some encouragement from Merlin, Arthur asks her to stay in his castle with the queen. During her stay, Merlin falls in love with her and desires her. Viviane, frightened that Merlin might take advantage of her with his spells, swears that she will never love him unless he swears to teach her all of his magic. Merlin consents, unaware that throughout the course of her lessons, Viviane will use Merlin's own powers against him, forcing him to do her bidding. When Viviane finally goes back to her country, Merlin escorts her. However, along the way, Merlin receives a vision that Arthur is in need of assistance. Viviane and Merlin rush back to Arthur's castle, but have to stop for the night in a stone chamber once inhabited by two lovers (a king's son Anasteu and a peasant woman in their forbidden affair). Merlin relates that when the lovers died, they were placed in a magic tomb within a room in the chamber. That night, while Merlin is asleep, Viviane, still disgusted with Merlin's desire for her, as well as his demonic heritage, casts a spell over him and places him in the magic tomb so that he can never escape, thus causing his death.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-47"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-47">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In <a href="/wiki/Alfred,_Lord_Tennyson" title="Alfred, Lord Tennyson">Tennyson's</a> <i>Merlin and the Gleam</i>, for instance, Merlin tells a 'young mariner' as he is about to actually die: "I am Merlin, / And I am dying, / I am Merlin / Who follow The Gleam."<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-51"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-51">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The <i>Conte</i> referred to in the story is an unknown, supposedly separate text that might have been just fictitious.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-49">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> However, in the Spanish Post-Vulgate manuscript known as the <i>Baladro del sabio Merlin</i> (<i>The Shriek of the Sage Merlin</i>), Merlin tells Bagdemagus that only <a href="/wiki/Tristan" title="Tristan">Tristan</a> could opened the cave's iron door, but Tristan is by then still just a baby. He than gives the story's eponymous great cry in a demonic voice, calling for his father to come and take him, and dies amidst a terrific supernatural event.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-56"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-56">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In the Italian romance <i><a href="/wiki/Tavola_Ritonda" class="mw-redirect" title="Tavola Ritonda">Tavola Ritonda</a></i>, a fairy enchantress named Escorducarla (the mother of <a href="/wiki/Annowre" title="Annowre">Elergia</a>) falls in love with Merlin and plans to trap him for herself in her purpose-built Palace of Great Desire, but Merlin instead banishes her to <a href="/wiki/Avalon" title="Avalon">Avalon</a>. It also reflect Gaucher de Dourdan's continuation of <i><a href="/wiki/Perceval,_the_Story_of_the_Grail" title="Perceval, the Story of the Grail">Perceval, the Story of the Grail</a></i>, featuring Merlin magically abducting a maiden who did not want to love him and then building a house for them to live together.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-59"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-59">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In 1215, the Abbot of <a href="/wiki/Cirencester_Abbey" title="Cirencester Abbey">Cirencester</a> wrote: "Merlin's tumulus gave you your name, Merlebergia."<sup id="cite_ref-ashe08_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ashe08-57">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>93</span></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-60"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-60">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Merlin is credited with predicting this: "Today I will perish, overwhelmed by stones and cudgels. / Today by body will be pierced through by a sharp stake / of wood, and so my life will expire. / Today I shall end my present life engulfed in the waves."<sup id="cite_ref-dames_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dames-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>67</span></sup></span>
</li>
