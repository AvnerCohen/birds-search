<p>The <b>red-billed teal</b> or <b>red-billed duck</b> (<i>Anas erythrorhyncha</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Dabbling_duck" class="mw-redirect" title="Dabbling duck">dabbling duck</a> which is an abundant resident breeder in southern and eastern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> typically south of 10° S. This <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">duck</a> is not <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, but will fly great distances to find suitable waters. It is highly gregarious outside the <a href="/wiki/Breeding_season" class="mw-redirect" title="Breeding season">breeding season</a> and forms large <a href="/wiki/Flock_(birds)" title="Flock (birds)">flocks</a>.
</p><p>The red-billed teal is 43–48 centimetres (17–19&#160;in) long and has a blackish cap and nape, contrasting pale face, and bright red <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a>. The body <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> is a dull dark brown scalloped with white. Flight reveals that the secondary <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">flight feathers</a> are <a href="/wiki/Buff_(color)" class="mw-redirect" title="Buff (color)">buff</a> with a black stripe across them. The sexes are similar, but juveniles are duller than adults.
</p><p>This is a quiet species, but the displaying male has a <i>whzzt</i> <a href="/wiki/Bird_call" class="mw-redirect" title="Bird call">call</a>, whereas the female has a soft <a href="/wiki/Mallard" title="Mallard">Mallard-like</a> quack.
</p><p>The red-billed teal is a bird of <a href="/wiki/Freshwater" class="mw-redirect" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitats</a> in fairly open country and is an <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivore</a>. It feeds by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dabbling&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dabbling (page does not exist)">dabbling</a> for plant food, or <a href="/wiki/Foraging" title="Foraging">foraging</a> on land mainly in the evening or at night. It nests on the ground in dense vegetation near water.
</p><p>This is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>
