<p>The <b>woodlark</b> or <b>wood lark</b> (<i>Lullula arborea</i>) is the only extant species in the <a href="/wiki/Lark" title="Lark">lark</a> genus <i>Lullula</i>. It is found across most of Europe, the Middle East, western Asia and the mountains of north Africa. It is mainly resident (non-<a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>) in the west of its range, but eastern populations of this <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird are more migratory, moving further south in winter.
</p><p>There are two subspecies of woodlark, <i>L. a. arborea</i> and <i>L. a. pallida</i>. The former is native to northern regions of Europe, while the latter can be found in the south of the woodlark's range. Their diet is mostly composed of seeds but also includes insects during the breeding period. A comparatively small bird, the woodlark is between 13.5 and 15 centimetres long and roughly 20% shorter than the <a href="/wiki/Skylark" class="mw-redirect" title="Skylark">skylark</a>. It is a brown bird with a pale underside and has a white-tipped tail.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woodlark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The woodlark was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">tenth edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> and given the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Alauda arborea</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This binomial name is identical to the Latin name used in 1676 by English ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Francis_Willughby" title="Francis Willughby">Francis Willughby</a> in his <i>Ornithologiae libri tres</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The woodlark is now placed in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Lullula" title="Lullula">Lullula</a></i> that was introduced by the German naturalist <a href="/wiki/Johann_Jakob_Kaup" title="Johann Jakob Kaup">Johann Jakob Kaup</a> in 1829.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The current genus name is <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeic</a> from French “Lulu”, the name given to the bird by <a href="/wiki/Georges-Louis_Leclerc,_Comte_de_Buffon" title="Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon">de Buffon</a>. The French name, <i>Alouette lulu</i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">scientific name</a>, <i>Lullula arborea</i>, are derived from the sound of its song.<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell_1999_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell_1999-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The specific <i>arborea</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>arbor, arboris</i>, "tree".<sup id="cite_ref-job_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woodlark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are two subspecies of woodlark:<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<ul><li><b>Northern woodlark</b> (<i>L. a. arborea</i>) - <small>(<a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">1758</a>)</small>: Found from northern, western and central Europe to western Russia and Ukraine</li>
<li><b>Southern woodlark</b> (<i>L. a. pallida</i>) - <small><a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Zarudny" title="Nikolai Zarudny">Zarudny</a>, 1902</small>: Found from southern Europe and north-western Africa through the Middle East to Iran and Turkmenistan, Crimea and Caucasus.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woodlark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lullula_arborea.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Drawing of a woodlark" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Lullula_arborea.jpg/220px-Lullula_arborea.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="203" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Lullula_arborea.jpg/330px-Lullula_arborea.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Lullula_arborea.jpg/440px-Lullula_arborea.jpg 2x" data-file-width="802" data-file-height="740" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lullula_arborea.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coloured lithograph by <a href="/wiki/Magnus_von_Wright" title="Magnus von Wright">Magnus von Wright</a></div></div></div>
<p>This is a 13.5- to 15-cm-long bird, slightly smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Skylark" class="mw-redirect" title="Skylark">skylark</a><sup id="cite_ref-Cerny_1975_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cerny_1975-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> being roughly 20% shorter.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_1998_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP_1998-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  The woodlark is mainly brown above and pale below, but with a distinctive white superciliar meeting on the nape.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It has a crest which is quite small and at most times inconspicuous.<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell_1999_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell_1999-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In flight it shows a short tail and short broad wings. The tail is tipped with white, but unlike the skylark, the tail sides and the rear edge of the wings are not edged with white.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Songbird" title="Songbird">songbird</a>, the woodlark has a melodious, warbling song often described <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeically</a> as a <i>lu-lu-lu-</i><sup id="cite_ref-Harbard_1988_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harbard_1988-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> or, more precisely, as a "serial <i>lū-lū-lū-lū-lū-</i>", <i>toolooeet toolooeet toolooeet</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cerny_1975_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cerny_1975-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woodlark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Found mainly in Europe, the mountains of northern Africa and western Asia, the woodlark is present across much of its range. In Europe, the bird seems most at home in the sandy heaths of Belgium, where its density was 7.5 pairs per square kilometre (km<sup>2</sup>) in 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-Simms_1992_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simms_1992-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In the same year, densities in <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a> ranged from 0.29 to 5.0 pairs per km<sup>2</sup> and between 0.1 and 0.25 pairs per km<sup>2</sup> in southern <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, with more optimal habitats being more densely populated. However, populations fluctuated across Europe in the 1990s and 2000s and more up-to-date density figures are unavailable. The extent of the woodlark's range is England in the west, parts of northern Egypt to the south, Iran and Turkmenistan to the east and the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_Peninsula" title="Scandinavian Peninsula">Scandinavian Peninsula</a> in the north.<sup id="cite_ref-Cerny_1975_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cerny_1975-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BWP_1998_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP_1998-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Harbard_1988_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harbard_1988-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Declining populations have resulted in the woodlark's range contracting, for example in Britain it once bred in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and central England but is now found only in southern England. Within its range it is mainly resident in the west, with eastern populations migrating south in the winter.
</p><p>The woodlark's natural habitat is heathland and open spaces sparsely populated with trees.<sup id="cite_ref-Burton_1979_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burton_1979-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> They prefer clearings in <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a> forests and heathland and like newly planted areas with pine saplings.<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell_1999_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell_1999-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Experimental work showed that annual ground‐disturbance can increase Woodlark abundance within lowland grass‐heaths.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The bird can also be found more rarely in urban areas. For example, in 1950 a pair were recorded on a main road near <a href="/wiki/Wimbledon_Common#Putney_Heath" title="Wimbledon Common">Putney Heath</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell_1999_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell_1999-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woodlark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Heidelerche_(1).JPG" class="image"><img alt="Woodlark in flight" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Heidelerche_%281%29.JPG/220px-Heidelerche_%281%29.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Heidelerche_%281%29.JPG/330px-Heidelerche_%281%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Heidelerche_%281%29.JPG/440px-Heidelerche_%281%29.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1174" data-file-height="782" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Heidelerche_(1).JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bird in flight at <a href="/wiki/S%C3%BCdheide_Nature_Park" title="Südheide Nature Park">Südheide Nature Park</a></div></div></div>
<p>The male woodlark has a song flight similar to that of the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_skylark" title="Eurasian skylark">Eurasian skylark</a> but flutters more as he rises and spirals upwards, circling the ground as he sings at a fairly constant height. Both male and female birds will also sing from the ground or a perch.<sup id="cite_ref-Harbard_1988_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harbard_1988-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Birds start singing early in the season, usually around February in Britain.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woodlark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lullula_arborea_MHNT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Lullula_arborea_MHNT.jpg/220px-Lullula_arborea_MHNT.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="212" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Lullula_arborea_MHNT.jpg/330px-Lullula_arborea_MHNT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Lullula_arborea_MHNT.jpg/440px-Lullula_arborea_MHNT.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3181" data-file-height="3071" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lullula_arborea_MHNT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Four eggs, part of the collections at the <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9um_de_Toulouse" title="Muséum de Toulouse">Muséum de Toulouse</a></div></div></div>
<p>The nest is generally made from grass, bracken, roots and moss and constructed in a depression on the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-Harbard_1988_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harbard_1988-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Nesting starts early, sometimes with the first eggs laid before end of March. Usually between three and five <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> are laid. The female will incubate the eggs, which are whitish with brown speckles, for 13 to 15 days.<sup id="cite_ref-Cerny_1975_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cerny_1975-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Both parents will then feed the young in the nest and the chicks leave the nest after a further 11 to 13 days.<sup id="cite_ref-Burton_1979_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burton_1979-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Families stay together for the summer and into the autumn. Two, sometimes three broods will be raised each year.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_feeding">Food and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woodlark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Food and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As with many of the birds in the <a href="/wiki/Lark" title="Lark">lark</a> family, the woodlark is primarily vegetarian as an adult but during the breeding season will also eat medium-sized insects.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_1998_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP_1998-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The diet is mainly composed of seeds and such insects as beetles, flies and moths.<sup id="cite_ref-Cerny_1975_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cerny_1975-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Harbard_1988_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harbard_1988-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> During the breeding season, the female incubates the eggs in spells of approximately 45 minutes and intervals of eight minutes feeding.<sup id="cite_ref-Burton_1979_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burton_1979-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats">Threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woodlark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Populations of woodlark across Europe have been in decline and ranges have been contracting in recent years.<sup id="cite_ref-Harbard_1988_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harbard_1988-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Habitat loss is thought to be a major contributor to this, with dry grassland, fallow land, lowland heathland and pasture being lost to agriculture, abandonment and development across Northern Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-JNCC_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JNCC-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Recent wildfires in England are also thought to have damaged the population, with some protected woodlark habitats having been destroyed.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_2011_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_2011-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woodlark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:250px;&#32;; background-color: #FFFFF0;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r887775652">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%;max-width:100%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style=""><div class="poem">
<p><i>Teevo cheevo cheevio chee</i>:<br />
O where, what can tháat be? <br />
<i>Weedio-weedio</i>: there again! <br />
So tiny a trickle of sóng-strain; <br />
And all round not to be found<br />
For brier, bough, furrow, or gréen ground <br />
Before or behind or far or at hand <br />
Either left either right <br />
Anywhere in the súnlight. <br />
Well, after all! Ah but hark—<br />
‘I am the little wóodlark.
</p>
</div></div>
<p><cite class="right-aligned" style="">"The Woodlark" by <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Manley_Hopkins" title="Gerard Manley Hopkins">Gerard Manley Hopkins</a></cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>The woodlark is commemorated in the works of two major poets. "The Woodlark", written by <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Manley_Hopkins" title="Gerard Manley Hopkins">Gerard Manley Hopkins</a>, departs from the standard tradition of British nature poetry by trying to transliterate the bird's song into made-up words.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Scottish poet <a href="/wiki/Robert_Burns" title="Robert Burns">Robert Burns</a> wrote of the bird's "melting art" in his poem "To the Woodlark".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> As there are currently no woodlarks in Scotland, and Burns never travelled south of Carlisle, many have speculated that Burns never came in contact with the bird and was in fact writing about the <a href="/wiki/Tree_pipit" title="Tree pipit">tree pipit</a>, which was commonly referred to as the woodlark in Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-Jobling_2010_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jobling_2010-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The woodlark's song is also thought to be melodious<sup id="cite_ref-Harbard_1988_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harbard_1988-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> while Burns' poem has an "underlying sense of grief" which may be attributed to the languishing notes at the end of the tree pipit's song.<sup id="cite_ref-Jobling_2010_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jobling_2010-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hudson_1919_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hudson_1919-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> However, the woodlark has been spotted in Scotland on occasion<sup id="cite_ref-Yarrell_1874_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yarrell_1874-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and it is possible that Burns was writing about this bird. This is backed up by the entry of a minister from Clinic, <a href="/wiki/Perthshire" title="Perthshire">Perthshire</a> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Old_Statistical_Account" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Statistical Account">Old Statistical Account</a></i>, which reads "The notes of the wood-lark are heard, delightful along the banks of the Lunan in spring and autumn; its nocturnal song has a dying cadence peculiarly melodious and has often been mistaken for the song of the Philomel [nightingale]."<sup id="cite_ref-Jobling_2010_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jobling_2010-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hull_2001_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hull_2001-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woodlark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Europe in 2004, the breeding population of woodlarks was estimated to number 1.3 million to 3.3 million breeding pairs. Europe accounts for 75-94% of the global population, meaning between 4.15 million and 13.2 million individuals in its world range.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Populations of woodlark have fluctuated across Europe, with specific figures available for Britain showing these fluctuations. An estimated 400 breeding pairs were present in England in 1981.<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell_1999_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell_1999-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> A series of systematic national surveys found 241 pairs in 1986, which increased to 1633 pairs in 1997 followed by an 88% increase to 3064 pairs in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  The woodlark has been categorised by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List of Threatened Species</a> as being of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>, meaning that it is not currently threatened with extinction.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
