<p>The <b>Woodlark</b> (<i>Lullula arborea</i>) is the only <a href="/wiki/Lark" title="Lark">lark</a> in the genus <i>Lullula</i>. It breeds across most of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and the mountains of north <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It is mainly resident in the west of its range, but eastern populations of this passerine bird are more <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, moving further south in winter. Even in the milder west of its range, many birds move south in winter.</p>
<p>This is a 13.5–15&#160;cm long bird of open heath with some trees, and other open woodlands, especially those with <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pines</a> and light <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>. Its generic name derives from its sweet plaintive song, delivered in flight from heights of 100 m or more.</p>
<p>Like most other <a href="/wiki/Lark" title="Lark">larks</a>, this is an undistinguished-looking species on the ground, mainly brown above and pale below, but with distinctive white superciliar meeting on the nape. In flight it shows a short tail and short broad wings. The tail is tipped with white, but unlike the <a href="/wiki/Skylark" title="Skylark" class="mw-redirect">Skylark</a>, the tail sides and the rear edge of the wings are not edged with white.</p>
<p>The nest is on the ground, with up to 6 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> being laid. Food is seeds supplemented with <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> in the breeding season.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Woodlark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span></h2>
<p>The <b>Woodlark</b> is commemorated by two famous British poets for its beautiful song. In The Woodlark<sup>2</sup> <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Manley_Hopkins" title="Gerard Manley Hopkins">Gerard Manley Hopkins</a> departs from the standard tradition of British nature poetry by trying to transliterate the bird's song into made-up words, saying:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Teevo cheevo cheevio chee:</dd>
<dd>O where, what can tháat be?</dd>
<dd>Weedio-weedio: there again!</dd>
<dd>So tiny a trickle of sóng-strain;</dd>
</dl>
<p>The Scottish poet <a href="/wiki/Robert_Burns" title="Robert Burns">Robert Burns</a> writes of the bird's "melting art" in his Address to the Woodlark<sup>3</sup>. However, since the <b>Woodlark</b> is not currently found in Scotland, it is possible that he confused the <b>Woodlark</b> with another bird. Another possibility is that the bird, whose range has contracted but is now expanding<sup>4</sup>, was found in Scotland during Burns's era.</p>
