<p>The <b>Common Gull</b> (European and Asian subspecies; see below) or <b>Mew Gull</b> (North American subspecies) <i><b>Larus canus</b></i> is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a> which breeds in northern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and northwestern <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. It <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrates</a> further south in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Its name does not indicate that it is an abundant species, but that during the winter it feeds on <a href="/wiki/Common_land" title="Common land">common land</a>, short pasture used for grazing.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Adults are 40–46&#160;cm long, obviously smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Herring_Gull" title="Herring Gull">Herring Gull</a>, and slightly smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Ring-billed_Gull" title="Ring-billed Gull">Ring-billed Gull</a>, also differing from this in its shorter, more tapered bill with a more greenish shade of yellow, as well as being unmarked during the breeding season. The body is grey above and white below. The legs are greenish-yellow. In winter, the head is streaked grey, and the bill often has a poorly-defined blackish band near the tip (sometimes sufficiently obvious to cause confusion with Ring-billed Gull). They have black wingtips with large white "mirrors". Young birds have scaly black-brown upperparts and a neat wing pattern, and grey legs. They take two to three years to reach maturity. The call is a high-pitched "laughing" cry.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwp_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>There are four <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>, two of them considered distinct species by some authorities:<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-olsen_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-olsen-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Larus canus canus</b></i> Linnaeus, 1758 – <b>Common Gull</b>. Europe and western Asia. Small; mantle medium grey (palest subspecies); wingtips with extensive black; iris dark. Wingspan 110–125&#160;cm; mass 290–480&#160;g.</li>
<li><i><b>Larus canus heinei</b></i> Homeyer, 1853 – <b>Russian Common Gull</b> . Central northern Asia. Medium size; mantle dark grey (darkest subspecies); wingtips with extensive black; iris dark. Mass 315–550&#160;g.</li>
<li><i><b>Larus canus kamtschatschensis</b></i> (Bonaparte, 1857); syn. <i>L. kamtschatschensis</i> – <b>Kamchatka Gull</b>. Northeastern Asia. Large; mantle medium-dark grey; wingtips with extensive black; iris pale. Mass 394–586&#160;g.</li>
<li><i><b>Larus canus brachyrhynchus</b></i> Richardson, 1831; syn. <i>L. brachyrhynchus</i> – <b>Mew Gull</b> or <b>Short-billed Gull</b>. <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Small; mantle medium-dark grey; wingtips with little black and much white; iris pale. Wingspan 96–102&#160;cm; mass 320–550&#160;g.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Larus_canus_winter_plumage.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Larus_canus_winter_plumage.jpg/220px-Larus_canus_winter_plumage.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Larus_canus_winter_plumage.jpg/330px-Larus_canus_winter_plumage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Larus_canus_winter_plumage.jpg/440px-Larus_canus_winter_plumage.jpg 2x" /></a>

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Larus_canus_winter_plumage.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>Both Common and Mew Gulls breed colonially near water or in marshes, making a lined nest on the ground or in a small tree; <a href="/wiki/Group_size_measures" title="Group size measures">colony size</a> varies from 2 to 320 or even more pairs. Usually three eggs are laid (sometimes just one or two); they hatch after 24–26 days, with the chicks fledging after a further 30–35 days. Like most gulls, they are omnivores and will scavenge as well as hunt small prey. The global population is estimated to be about one million pairs; they are most numerous in Europe, with over half (possibly as much as 80-90%) of the world population.<sup id="cite_ref-ebcc_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebcc-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> By contrast, the Alaskan population is only about 10,000 pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vagrancy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Vagrancy">Vagrancy</span></h2>
<p>The Common Gull occurs as a scarce winter visitor to coastal eastern Canada and as a vagrant to the northeastern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">USA</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> and there is one recent record of Mew Gull in Europe on the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The scientific name <i>Larus canus</i> simply translates from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> as <i>grey</i> or <i>hoary gull</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-DSBN_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DSBN-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The name Common Gull was coined by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Pennant" title="Thomas Pennant">Thomas Pennant</a> in 1768 because he considered it the most numerous of its <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODBBN_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODBBN-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Ray" title="John Ray">John Ray</a> earlier used the name <i>Common Sea-Mall</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODBBN_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODBBN-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> There are many old British regional names for this species with variations on <i>maa</i>, <i>mar</i> and <i>mew.</i><sup id="cite_ref-BNB_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNB-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
