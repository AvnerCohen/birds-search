<p>The <b>Black Redstart</b> (<i>Phoenicurus ochruros</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Redstart" title="Redstart">redstart</a> genus <i><a href="/wiki/Phoenicurus" title="Phoenicurus">Phoenicurus</a></i>. Like its relatives, it was formerly classed as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">Thrush</a> family (Turdidae), but is now known to be an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a> (Muscicapidae).</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Redstart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The Black Redstart is 13–14.5&#160;cm in length and 12–20 g in weight, similar to the <a href="/wiki/Common_Redstart" title="Common Redstart">Common Redstart</a>. The adult male is overall dark grey to black on the upperparts and with a black breast; the lower rump and tail are orange-red, with the two central tail feathers dark red-brown. The belly and undertail are either blackish-grey (western subspecies; see Systematics, below) or orange-red (eastern subspecies); the wings are blackish-grey with pale fringes on the secondaries forming a whitish panel (western subspecies) or all blackish (eastern subspecies). The female is grey (western subspecies) to grey-brown (eastern subspecies) overall except for the orange-red lower rump and tail, greyer than the Common Redstart; at any age the grey axillaries and underwing coverts are also distinctive (in the Common Redstart these are buff to orange-red). One-year old males are similar to females but blacker; the whitish wing panel of the western subspecies does not develop until the second year.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Snow_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Redstart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>It is a widespread breeder in south and central <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and northwest <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> (where local) south to <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, east to central <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. It is resident in the milder parts of its range, but northeastern birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> to winter in southern and western Europe and Asia, and north Africa. It nests in crevices or holes in buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Snow_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In Britain it is most common in as a passage and winter visitor, with only 20–50 pairs breeding.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> On passage it is fairly common on the east and south coasts, and in winter on the coasts of Wales and western and southern England, with a few also at inland sites. Migrant Black Redstarts arrive in Britain in October or November and either move on or remain to winter, returning eastward in March or April. They also winter on the south and east coasts of Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The species originally inhabited stony ground in <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountains</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Cliff" title="Cliff">cliffs</a>, but since about 1900 has expanded to include similar urban habitats including bombed areas during and after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, and large industrial complexes that have the bare areas and cliff-like buildings it favours; in Great Britain, most of the small breeding population nests in such industrial areas. It will catch passing <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> in flight, and migrants often hunt in coastal tide-wrack for <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a> or tiny <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>. Its quick ducks of head and body are <a href="/wiki/European_Robin" title="European Robin">robin</a>-like, and its tail is often flicked. The male has a rattling song and a <i>tick</i> call.</p>
<p>Eastern race birds are very rare vagrants in western Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Steijn_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steijn-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Redstart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Systematics">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Systematics">Systematics</span></h2>
<p>This species belongs to a Eurasian <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> which also includes the <a href="/wiki/Daurian_Redstart" title="Daurian Redstart">Daurian Redstart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hodgson%27s_Redstart" title="Hodgson's Redstart">Hodgson's Redstart</a>, the <a href="/wiki/White-winged_Redstart" title="White-winged Redstart" class="mw-redirect">White-winged Redstart</a>, and maybe the <a href="/wiki/Ala_Shan_Redstart" title="Ala Shan Redstart" class="mw-redirect">Ala Shan Redstart</a>. The present species' ancestors diverged from about 3 <a href="/wiki/Mya_(unit)" title="Mya (unit)" class="mw-redirect">mya</a> (<a href="/wiki/Late_Pliocene" title="Late Pliocene" class="mw-redirect">Late Pliocene</a>) onwards and spread throughout much of <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> from 1.5 mya onward.<sup id="cite_ref-Ertan_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ertan-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> It is not very closely related to the Common Redstart. As these are separated by different <a href="/wiki/Behaviour" title="Behaviour" class="mw-redirect">behaviour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a> requirements and have not evolved <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fertilisation_barriers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fertilisation barriers (page does not exist)">fertilisation barriers</a>, the two European species can nonetheless produce apparently fertile and viable <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrids</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Steijn_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steijn-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Grosch_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grosch-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>There are a number of <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> which differ mainly in underpart colours of the adult males; different authorities accept between five and seven subspecies. They can be separated into three major groups, according to <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a>, and <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA" class="mw-redirect">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome <i>b</i></a> <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">sequence</a> data.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Snow_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ertan_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ertan-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Grosch_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grosch-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><b><i>P. o. phoenicuroides</i> group</b>. <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basal</a> central and eastern Asian forms which diverged from the ancestral stock as the species slowly spread west (c.3-1.5 mya). Females and juveniles light grey-brown.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Phoenicurus ochruros phoenicuroides</i>. <a href="/wiki/Tian_Shan" title="Tian Shan">Tian Shan</a> eastwards to <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>. Small; adult males have lower breast, belly and flanks deep rufous, pale wing-patch absent, sometimes white forehead. Overall quite similar to a much darker Common Redstart with black chest. Females and juveniles are similar to Common Redstart but have an overall sandier, paler colour and often a distinct buff eye-ring.</li>
<li><i>Phoenicurus ochruros rufiventris</i>. <a href="/wiki/Turkmenistan" title="Turkmenistan">Turkmenistan</a> eastwards through <a href="/wiki/Pamir_Mountains" title="Pamir Mountains">Pamir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alay_Mountains" title="Alay Mountains">Alay Mountains</a> to <a href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya" class="mw-redirect">Himalaya</a>. Usually large; adult males like <i>P. o. phoenicuroides</i>, but darker overall, with black back and rufous-chestnut underside. Females with rufous tinge to underside. Exact limits with <i>P. o. phoenicuroides</i> unresolved.
<ul>
<li><i>Phoenicurus ochruros xerophilus</i>. <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> east of and between ranges of preceding two. Large; colour pattern like <i>P. o. phoenicuroides</i> but paler. Included in <i>P. o. rufiventris</i> by many authorities.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><b><i>P. o. ochruros</i> group</b>. Western Asian forms, whose lineage separated from the <i>gibraltariensis</i> group c.1.5-0.5 mya. Females and juveniles intermediate.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Phoenicurus ochruros ochruros</i>. Eastern <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alborz" title="Alborz">Alborz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>. Small, somewhat intermediate between <i>P. o. phoenicuroides</i> and <i>P. o. gibraltariensis</i>. Generally like latter, but rufous underside, pale wing patch weakly developed.</li>
<li><i>Phoenicurus ochruros semirufus</i>. <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a>. Small; adult males somewhat similar to <i>rufiventris</i> except in size. Black areas extensive.</li>
</ul>
<p><b><i>P. o. gibraltariensis</i> group</b>. European population, which formed as a distinct subspecies probably during the <a href="/wiki/Last_ice_age" title="Last ice age" class="mw-redirect">last ice age</a>. Females and juveniles dark grey.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Phoenicurus ochruros gibraltariensis</i>. Western Europe east to the <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a> and western Turkey. Neck, upper back and shoulders dark slate grey to black in adult males, lighter than face and neck, pale wing patch strongly developed.
<ul>
<li><i>Phoenicurus ochruros aterrimus</i>. <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberia</a> and Morocco.&#160;:Neck, upper back and shoulders black in adult males. Wide intergradation with <i>P. o. gibraltariensis</i> and treated as a synonym of it by many authorities.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
