<p>The <b>Pin-tailed Sandgrouse</b> (<i>Pterocles alchata</i>) is a medium large <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Sandgrouse" title="Sandgrouse">sandgrouse</a> family.</p>
<p>The nominate race breeds in <a href="/wiki/Iberian_peninsula" title="Iberian peninsula" class="mw-redirect">Iberia</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, and the eastern form <i>P. a. caudacutus</i> (<a href="/wiki/Samuel_Gottlieb_Gmelin" title="Samuel Gottlieb Gmelin">Gmelin</a>, 1774) is found in northwest <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, and from southeast <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> east to <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>. It is a partial <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>, with some <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> birds moving to the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> in winter.</p>
<p>This gregarious species breeds on dry open treeless plains and similar habitats. Its nest is a ground scrape into which 2 or 3 creamy eggs with cryptic markings are laid. Both sexes incubate.</p>
<p>The Pin-tailed Sandgrouse is 31–39&#160;cm long. Its head and upperparts are yellowish-green. The underparts are white with a chestnut breast band separating the belly from the green neck. Sexes are similar, but the female is shorter tailed than the male.</p>
<p>Males of the eastern race have duller underparts than the <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> birds, and the females have white, rather than yellow, wing coverts.</p>
<p>This sandgrouse has a small, pigeon like head and neck, but sturdy compact body. It has long pointed wings, which are white underneath, a long tail and a fast direct flight. Flocks fly to watering holes at dawn. The call is a loud <i>gatar-gatar</i>.</p>
