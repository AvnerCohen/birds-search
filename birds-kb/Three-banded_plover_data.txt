<p>The <b>three-banded plover</b>, or <b>three-banded sandplover</b> (<i>Charadrius tricollaris</i>), is a small <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a>. This  <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">plover</a> is resident in much of eastern and southern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, mainly on inland rivers, pools, and lakes. Its nest is a bare scrape on shingle. This species is often seen as single individuals, but it will form small flocks. It hunts by sight for insects, worms and other <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>. Three-banded plovers have a sharp whistled <i>weeet-weet</i> call.
</p>

<p>The adult three-banded plover is 18&#160;cm in length. It has long wings and a long tail, and therefore looks different from most other small plovers in flight, the exception being the closely related <a href="/wiki/Forbes%27s_plover" title="Forbes&#39;s plover">Forbes's plover</a> that replaces it in west Africa.
</p><p>The adult three-banded plover has medium brown upperparts, and the underparts are white except for the two black breast bands, separated by a white band, which give this species its common and scientific names. The head is strikingly patterned, with a black crown, white <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilia</a> extending from the white forehead to meet on the back of the neck, and a grey face becoming brown on the neck. The eye ring and the base of the otherwise black bill are red.
</p><p>The Madagascan subspecies <i>C. t. bifrontatus</i> has a grey band between the bill and the white forehead, and the sides of the head are grey. A genetic study reported genetic differentiation between Madagascar and the mainland population.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The sexes are similar, and the juveniles of the nominate and Madagascan subspecies also resemble the adults, although the forehead is brownish for a short time.
This species is distinguished from the larger, darker Forbes's plover in that the latter species has a brown forehead and lacks a white wingbar.
</p>
