<p>The <b>Dunnock</b> (<i>Prunella modularis</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> found throughout temperate <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and into <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. It is by far the most widespread member of the <a href="/wiki/Accentor" title="Accentor">accentor</a> family, which otherwise consists of mountain species. It is sometimes called the <b>Hedge Accentor</b>, <b>Hedge Sparrow</b> or <b>Hedge Warbler</b>. The name "dunnock" comes from the <a href="/wiki/British_language" title="British language">Ancient British</a> <i>*dunnākos</i>, meaning "little brown one".</p>
<p>It is a resident in the milder west and south of its range, but as an <a href="/wiki/Insectivore" title="Insectivore">insectivore</a>, must <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> south from the colder parts of its range. It is a bird of woodland, shrub and gardens. It builds a neat nest low in a bush or <a href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer" class="mw-redirect">conifer</a>, laying 3-5 unspotted blue <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a>.</p>
<p>It is a host of the <a href="/wiki/Common_Cuckoo" title="Common Cuckoo">Common Cuckoo</a>. It is likely that this association is recent, since other Cuckoo hosts have learned to discriminate between eggs, and the Cuckoo has consequently evolved eggs that match those of its host. In the case of the Dunnock, there is no resemblance, yet the Cuckoo eggs are accepted.</p>
<p>This is a <a href="/wiki/European_Robin" title="European Robin">Robin-sized</a> bird, 13.5–14&#160;cm in length, fairly drab in appearance, and somewhat resembling a small <a href="/wiki/House_Sparrow" title="House Sparrow">House Sparrow</a> with its streaked back and general shape. It is also brownish underneath, and has a fine pointed bill. Adults have a grey head. The sexes are similarly coloured. They were introduced to <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> between 1867 and 1882 and now breed throughout the country including on the <a href="/wiki/Chatham_Islands" title="Chatham Islands">Chatham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antipodes_Islands" title="Antipodes Islands">Antipodes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Auckland_Islands" title="Auckland Islands">Auckland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Campbell_Island,_New_Zealand" title="Campbell Island, New Zealand">Campbell</a> islands.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species makes up for its drab appearance with its breeding behaviour. Females are often <a href="/wiki/Polyandry" title="Polyandry">polyandrous</a>, breeding with two males at once, and thus giving rise to <a href="/wiki/Sperm_competition" title="Sperm competition">sperm competition</a>. Males compete for mating access to the female, but <a href="/wiki/DNA_fingerprinting" title="DNA fingerprinting" class="mw-redirect">DNA fingerprinting</a> has shown that chicks within broods often have different fathers, depending on their success at monopolising access to the fertile female. Males try to ensure their paternity during courtship by pecking at the <a href="/wiki/Cloaca" title="Cloaca">cloaca</a> of the female to stimulate her to eject the sperm of other males with whom the female has recently mated.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Dunnocks take just one-tenth of a second to copulate, and have sex more than 100 times a day.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Males provide parental care in proportion to their mating success, so it is not uncommon to see two males and a female provisioning nestlings at one nest. Polyandry is rare in birds, with only about 2% of species showing such a mating system; the majority are <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" title="Monogamous" class="mw-redirect">monogamous</a>, where one male and one female breed together.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from February 2012">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p><br style="clear:left;" />
The main call is a shrill, persistent "tseep" and a high trilling note,<sup id="cite_ref-Peterson_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peterson-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> which often betray the bird's otherwise inconspicuous presence. The song is rapid, thin and tinkling, a sweet warble which can be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Wren" title="Eurasian Wren">Wren</a> but is shorter and weaker.<sup id="cite_ref-Peterson_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peterson-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The Wren's <a href="/wiki/Bird_song" title="Bird song" class="mw-redirect">song</a> incorporates repeated trill sounds but the Dunnock's does not.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from March 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> While the Dunnock's bubbling song is very pleasant, it has been described as being similar to a squeaky trolley wheel.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from March 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
