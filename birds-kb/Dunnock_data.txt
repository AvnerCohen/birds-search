<p>The <b>dunnock</b> (<i>Prunella modularis</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a>, or perching <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a>, found throughout temperate Europe and into Asian Russia. Dunnocks have also been successfully introduced into New Zealand. It is by far the most widespread member of the <a href="/wiki/Accentor" title="Accentor">accentor</a> family, which otherwise consists of mountain species. Other common names of the dunnock include the <b>hedge accentor</b>, <b>hedge sparrow</b>, or <b>hedge warbler</b>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dunnock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The dunnock was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">tenth edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>. He coined the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> of <i>Motacilla modularis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Specific_epithet" class="mw-redirect" title="Specific epithet">specific epithet</a> is from the Latin <i>modularis</i> "modulating" or "singing".<sup id="cite_ref-hbwkey_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwkey-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This species is now placed in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Prunella_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Prunella (bird)">Prunella</a></i> that was introduced by the French ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Louis_Jean_Pierre_Vieillot" title="Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot">Louis Vieillot</a> in 1816.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The name "dunnock" comes from the English <i>dun</i> (dingy brown, dark-coloured) and the diminutive <i>ock</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-OED1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and "accentor" is from post-classical <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means a person who sings with another.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The genus name <i>Prunella</i> is from the German <i>Braunelle</i>, "dunnock", a diminutive of <i>braun</i>, "brown".<sup id="cite_ref-job_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Eight <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are recognised:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>P. m. hebridium</i> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Meinertzhagen" title="Richard Meinertzhagen">Meinertzhagen</a>, R, 1934 – Ireland and the Hebrides  (west of Scotland)</li>
<li><i>P. m. occidentalis</i> (<a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a>, 1910) – Scotland (except the Hebrides), England and west France</li>
<li><i>P. m. modularis</i> (<a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, 1758) – north and central Europe</li>
<li><i>P. m. mabbotti</i> <a href="/wiki/Francis_Harper_(biologist)" title="Francis Harper (biologist)">Harper</a>, 1919 – Iberian Peninsula, southcentral France and Italy</li>
<li><i>P. m. meinertzhageni</i> <a href="/wiki/James_M._Harrison" title="James M. Harrison">Harrison, JM</a> &amp; Pateff, 1937 – Balkans</li>
<li><i>P. m. fuscata</i> Mauersberger, 1971 – south Crimean Peninsula (north coast of the Black Sea)</li>
<li><i>P. m. euxina</i> Watson, 1961 – northwest and north Turkey</li>
<li><i>P. m. obscura</i> (<a href="/wiki/Carl_Ludwig_Hablitz" title="Carl Ludwig Hablitz">Hablizl</a>, 1783) – northeast Turkey, Caucasus and north Iran</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dunnock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/European_robin" title="European robin">European robin</a>-sized bird, the dunnock typically measures 13.5–14&#160;cm (5.3–5.5&#160;in) in length. It possesses a streaked back, somewhat resembling a small <a href="/wiki/House_sparrow" title="House sparrow">house sparrow</a>. Like that species, the dunnock has a drab appearance which may have evolved to avoid predation. It is brownish underneath, and has a fine pointed bill. Adults have a grey head, and both sexes are similarly coloured.<sup id="cite_ref-field_guide_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-field_guide-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
Unlike any similar sized small brown bird, dunnocks exhibit frequent wing flicking, especially when engaged in territorial disputes or when competing for mating rights.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The main call of the dunnock is a shrill, persistent <i>tseep</i> along with a high trilling note, which betrays the bird's otherwise inconspicuous presence. The song is rapid, thin and tinkling, a sweet warble which can be confused with that of the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_wren" title="Eurasian wren">Eurasian wren</a>, but is shorter and weaker.<sup id="cite_ref-Peterson_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peterson-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dunnock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dunnocks are native to large areas of Eurasia, inhabiting much of Europe including Lebanon, northern Iran, and the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>. They are the only commonly found accentor in lowland areas; all the others inhabit upland areas.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Dunnocks were successfully introduced into New Zealand during the 19th century, and are now widely distributed around the country and some offshore islands.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Favoured habitats include <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodlands</a>, shrubs, <a href="/wiki/Garden" title="Garden">gardens</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hedgerow" class="mw-redirect" title="Hedgerow">hedgerows</a> where they typically feed on the ground, often seeking out <a href="/wiki/Detritivore" title="Detritivore">detritivores</a> as food.<sup id="cite_ref-brittannica_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brittannica-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Territoriality">Territoriality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dunnock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Territoriality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dunnock_singing_steve_childs.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Dunnock_singing_steve_childs.jpg/220px-Dunnock_singing_steve_childs.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="158" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Dunnock_singing_steve_childs.jpg/330px-Dunnock_singing_steve_childs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Dunnock_singing_steve_childs.jpg/440px-Dunnock_singing_steve_childs.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1109" data-file-height="797" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dunnock_singing_steve_childs.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dunnock singing from high perch</div></div></div>
<p>Dunnocks are territorial and may engage in conflict with other birds that encroach upon their nests.<sup id="cite_ref-brittannica_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brittannica-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Males sometimes share a territory and exhibit a strict dominance hierarchy. Nevertheless, this social dominance is not translated into benefits to the alpha male in terms of reproduction, since paternity is usually equally shared between males of the group.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, members of a group are rarely related, and so competition can result.<sup id="cite_ref-food_patchiness_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-food_patchiness-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Female territorial ranges are almost always exclusive. However, sometimes, multiple males will co-operate to defend a single territory containing multiple females. Males exhibit a strong <a href="/wiki/Dominance_hierarchy" title="Dominance hierarchy">dominance hierarchy</a> within groups: older birds tend to be the dominant males and first-year birds are usually sub-dominant. Studies have found that close male relatives almost never share a territory.<sup id="cite_ref-food_patchiness_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-food_patchiness-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The male's ability to access females generally depends on female range size, which is affected by the distribution of food. When resources are distributed in dense patches, female ranges tend to be small and easy for males to monopolise. Subsequent mating systems, as discussed below, reflect high reproductive success for males and relatively lower success for females. In times of scarcity, female territories expand to accommodate the lack of resources, causing males to have a more difficult time monopolising females. Hence, females gain a reproductive advantage over males in this case.<sup id="cite_ref-food_patchiness_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-food_patchiness-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-success_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-success-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dunnock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mating_systems">Mating systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dunnock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mating systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dunnocks_cloaca_pecking.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Dunnocks_cloaca_pecking.jpg/220px-Dunnocks_cloaca_pecking.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Dunnocks_cloaca_pecking.jpg/330px-Dunnocks_cloaca_pecking.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Dunnocks_cloaca_pecking.jpg/440px-Dunnocks_cloaca_pecking.jpg 2x" data-file-width="840" data-file-height="574" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dunnocks_cloaca_pecking.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Male dunnock pecking cloaca of female before mating</div></div></div><p>The dunnock possesses variable mating systems. Females are often <a href="/wiki/Polyandry" title="Polyandry">polyandrous</a>, breeding with two or more males at once,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> which is quite rare among birds. This multiple mating system leads to the development of <a href="/wiki/Sperm_competition" title="Sperm competition">sperm competition</a> amongst the male suitors. <a href="/wiki/DNA_fingerprinting" class="mw-redirect" title="DNA fingerprinting">DNA fingerprinting</a> has shown that chicks within a brood often have different fathers, depending on the success of the males at monopolising the female.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  Males try to ensure their paternity by pecking at the <a href="/wiki/Cloaca" title="Cloaca">cloaca</a><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> of the female to stimulate ejection of rival males' sperm.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Dunnocks take just one-tenth of a second to copulate and can mate more than 100 times a day.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Males provide parental care in proportion to their mating success, so two males and a female can commonly be seen provisioning nestlings at one nest.
</p><p>Other mating systems also exist within dunnock populations, depending on the ratio of male to females and the overlap of territories. When only one female and one male territory overlap, <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamy</a> is preferred. Sometimes, two or three adjacent female territories overlap one male territory, and so <a href="/wiki/Polygyny_in_animals" title="Polygyny in animals">polygyny</a> is favored, with the male monopolising several females.  <a href="/wiki/Polygynandry" title="Polygynandry">Polygynandry</a> also exists, in which two males jointly defend a territory containing several females.  Polyandry, though, is the most common mating system of dunnocks found in nature.  Depending on the population, males generally have the best reproductive success in polygynous populations, while females have the advantage during polyandry.<sup id="cite_ref-food_patchiness_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-food_patchiness-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-success_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-success-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Studies have illustrated the fluidity of dunnock mating systems. When given food in abundance, female territory size is reduced drastically. Consequently, males can more easily monopolise the females. Thus, the mating system can be shifted from one that favours female success (polyandry), to one that promotes male success (monogamy, polygynandry, or polygyny).<sup id="cite_ref-food_distribution_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-food_distribution-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nest">Nest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dunnock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dunnock_(Hedge_Sparrow)_Nest_11.04.11.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Dunnock_%28Hedge_Sparrow%29_Nest_11.04.11.jpg/220px-Dunnock_%28Hedge_Sparrow%29_Nest_11.04.11.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Dunnock_%28Hedge_Sparrow%29_Nest_11.04.11.jpg/330px-Dunnock_%28Hedge_Sparrow%29_Nest_11.04.11.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Dunnock_%28Hedge_Sparrow%29_Nest_11.04.11.jpg/440px-Dunnock_%28Hedge_Sparrow%29_Nest_11.04.11.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dunnock_(Hedge_Sparrow)_Nest_11.04.11.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dunnock nest and eggs</div></div></div><p>The dunnock builds a nest (predominantly from twigs and moss and lined with soft materials such as wool or feathers), low in a bush or <a href="/wiki/Conifer" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifer">conifer</a>, where adults typically lay three to five unspotted blue <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-brittannica_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brittannica-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parental_care_and_provisioning">Parental care and provisioning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dunnock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Parental care and provisioning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Broods, depending on the population, can be raised by a lone female, multiple females with the part-time help of a male, multiple females with full-time help by a male, or by multiple females and multiple males. In pairs, the male and the female invest parental care at similar rates. However, in trios, the female and alpha male invest more care in chicks than does the beta male. In territories in which females are able to escape from males, both the alpha and beta males share provisioning equally. This last system represents the best case scenario for females, as it helps to ensure maximal care and the success of the young.
</p><p>A study has found that males tend to not discriminate between their own young and those of another male in polyandrous or polygynandrous systems. However, they do vary their feeding depending on the certainty of paternity. If a male has greater access to a female, and therefore a higher chance of a successful fertilisation, during a specific mating period, it would provide more care towards the young.<sup id="cite_ref-food_distribution_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-food_distribution-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
