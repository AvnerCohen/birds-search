<p>The <b>common reed bunting</b> (<i>Emberiza schoeniclus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bunting_(bird)" title="Bunting (bird)">bunting</a> family Emberizidae, a group now separated by most modern authors from the <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finches</a>, Fringillidae. The genus name <i>Emberiza</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old German</a> <i>Embritz</i>, a bunting. The specific <i>schoeniclus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>skhoiniklos</i>, a now unknown waterside bird.<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It breeds across  Europe and much of the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>. Most birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> south in winter, but those in the milder south and west of the range are resident. It is common in reedbeds and also breeds in drier open areas such as moorland and cultivation. For example, it is a component of the <a href="/wiki/Purple_moor_grass_and_rush_pastures" title="Purple moor grass and rush pastures">purple moor grass and rush pastures</a>,  a type of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_action_plan" title="Biodiversity action plan">Biodiversity Action Plan</a> habitat in the UK. It occurs on poorly drained neutral and acidic soils of the lowlands and upland fringe.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_reed_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common reed bunting was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">tenth edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> under the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial</a> name <i>Fringilla schoeniclus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This bunting is now placed in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Emberiza" class="mw-redirect" title="Emberiza">Emberiza</a></i> that Linnaeus had introduced in the same edition of his <i>Systema Naturae</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ioc_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The specific epithet <i>schoeniclus </i> is from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>skhoiniklos</i>, a word that was used by Greek authors for an unidentified bird.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Linnaeus specified the <a href="/wiki/Type_locality_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Type locality (biology)">type locality</a> as Europe but this is now restricted to Sweden.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Nineteen <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are recognised.<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The bird family <a href="/wiki/Emberizidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Emberizidae">Emberizidae</a> contains around 300 seed-eating species, the majority of which are found in the Americas, although the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Emberiza" class="mw-redirect" title="Emberiza">Emberiza</a></i>, with more than 40 members, is confined to the Old World.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Within its genus, the reed bunting is most closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_reed_bunting" title="Japanese reed bunting">Japanese reed bunting</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pallas%27s_reed_bunting" title="Pallas&#39;s reed bunting">Pallas's reed bunting</a>, which are sometimes classified as being in the genus Schoeniclus. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_reed_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nineteen subspecies are recognised, including- <i>E. s. schoeniclus</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate subspecies</a>, which occurs in most of Europe, <i>E. s. witherbyi</i> which is found in south <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, western <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, France and Sardinia, <i>E. s. intermedia</i> from <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_coast" class="mw-redirect" title="Adriatic coast">Adriatic coast</a> to northwest <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a>, <i>E. s. reiseri</i> from southeast Albania, northwest <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, south <a href="/wiki/North_Macedonia" title="North Macedonia">North Macedonia</a> and west and central <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <i>E. s. caspia</i> from east Turkey and northwest <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <i>E. s. korejewi</i> from southwest and eastern Iran and south <a href="/wiki/Turkmenistan" title="Turkmenistan">Turkmenistan</a>, <i>E. s. pyrrhuloides</i> from north Caspian sea region to western <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, southeast <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> and central <a href="/wiki/Tien_Shan" class="mw-redirect" title="Tien Shan">Tien Shan</a>, <i>E. s. passerina</i> from northwest Siberia, wintering in south Asia, <i>E. s. parvirostris</i> from central Siberia wintering in northern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <i>E. s. pyrrhulina</i> from <a href="/wiki/Kamchatka" class="mw-redirect" title="Kamchatka">Kamchatka</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, wintering in central Japan, Korea and eastern China, <i>E. s. pallidior</i> from southwestern Siberia wintering in southwest Asia, <i>E. s. minor</i> from <a href="/wiki/Russian_Far_East" title="Russian Far East">Russian Far East</a> and northeast China, wintering in east China, <i>E. s. ukrainae</i> from Ukraine and adjacent areas of Russia, <i>E. s. incognita</i> from southeastern <a href="/wiki/European_Russia" title="European Russia">European Russia</a> to north Kazakhstan and <i>E. s. zaidamensis</i>, endemic to northwest <a href="/wiki/Qinghai" title="Qinghai">Qinghai</a>, China.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_reed_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common reed bunting is a medium-sized bird, 13.5–15.5&#160;cm long, with a small but sturdy seed-eater's bill. The male has a black head and throat, white neck collar and underparts, and a heavily streaked brown back. The female is much duller, with a streaked brown head, and is more streaked below.
The song of the male is a repetitive <i>srip</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_reed_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Its natural food consists of insects when feeding young, and otherwise seeds.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_reed_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Breeding normally starts in early April, finishing in late August depending on location and altitude. The species is <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" class="mw-redirect" title="Monogamous">monogamous</a>. The nest is built using twigs, grass and reeds lined with finer materials such as hair, moss and rootlets in a bush or reed tussock. 4–5 olive-grey eggs are laid, which show the hair-like markings characteristic of those of buntings. The incubation period is 12-15 days where the chicks are fed by both parents.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_reed_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The reed bunting is not globally threatened and classified as least concern by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>. The estimated European population is that of at least 4.8 million pairs, with particular strongholds in Sweden, Poland and Norway. However the reed bunting is reportedly declining in Norway, Sweden and Germany. 
</p>
