<p>The <b>Common Tern</b><sup id="cite_ref-gill_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gill-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> (<i>Sterna hirundo</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">tern</a> family Sternidae. This <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> has a circumpolar distribution, its four <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> breeding in <a href="/wiki/Temperateness" title="Temperateness">temperate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subarctic" title="Subarctic">subarctic</a> regions of Europe, Asia and North America. It is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in coastal <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">subtropical</a> regions. Breeding adults have light grey upperparts, white to very light grey underparts, a black cap, orange-red legs, and a narrow pointed bill. Depending on the subspecies, the bill may be mostly red with a black tip or all black. There are a number of similar species, including the partly <a href="/wiki/Sympatry" title="Sympatry">sympatric</a> <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Tern" title="Arctic Tern">Arctic Tern</a>, which can be separated on <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> details, leg and bill colour, or <a href="/wiki/Animal_communication" title="Animal communication">vocalisations</a>.</p>
<p>Breeding in a wider range of habitats than any of its relatives, the Common Tern nests on any flat, poorly vegetated surface close to water, including beaches and islands, and it readily adapts to artificial substrates such as floating rafts. The nest may be a bare <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest#Scrape" title="Bird nest">scrape</a> in sand or gravel, but it is often lined or edged with whatever debris is available. Up to three eggs may be laid, their dull colours and blotchy patterns providing <a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">camouflage</a> on the open beach. <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation#Avian_incubation" title="Egg incubation">Incubation</a> is by both sexes, and the eggs hatch in around 21–22 days, longer if the colony is disturbed by predators. The downy chicks <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> in 22–28&#160;days. Like most terns, this species feeds by plunge-diving for fish, either in the sea or in freshwater, but <a href="/wiki/Mollusca" title="Mollusca">molluscs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrate</a> prey may form a significant part of the diet in some areas.</p>
<p>Eggs and young are vulnerable to predation by mammals such as <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_mink" title="American mink">American mink</a>, and large birds including <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">herons</a>. Common Terns may be infected by <a href="/wiki/Louse" title="Louse">lice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parasitic_worm" title="Parasitic worm">parasitic worms</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mite" title="Mite">mites</a>, although <a href="/wiki/Haematozoa" title="Haematozoa">blood parasites</a> appear to be rare. Its large population and huge breeding range mean that this species is classed as being of <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>, although numbers in North America have declined sharply in recent decades. Despite international legislation protecting the Common Tern, in some areas populations are threatened by <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat loss</a>, pollution or the disturbance of <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">breeding colonies</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The terns, family Sternidae, are small to medium-sized seabirds closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skimmer" title="Skimmer">skimmers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skua" title="Skua">skuas</a>. They are gull-like in appearance, but typically have a lighter build, long pointed wings (which give them a fast, buoyant flight), a deeply forked tail, slender legs,<sup id="cite_ref-BWP764_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP764-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and webbed feet.<sup id="cite_ref-wassink_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wassink-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Most species are grey above and white below, and have a black cap which is reduced or flecked with white in the non-breeding season.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP764_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP764-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Common Tern's closest relatives appear to be the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Tern" title="Antarctic Tern">Antarctic Tern</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-nisbet_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nisbet-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> followed by the <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasian</a> Arctic and <a href="/wiki/Roseate_Tern" title="Roseate Tern">Roseate Terns</a>. <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">Genetic</a> evidence suggests that the Common Tern may have diverged from an ancestral stock earlier than its relatives.<sup id="cite_ref-bridge_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bridge-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> No <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> are known from North America, and those claimed in Europe are of uncertain age and species.<sup id="cite_ref-nisbet_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nisbet-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Common Tern was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> in 1758 under its current scientific name, <i>Sterna hirundo</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Linnaeus_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linnaeus-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The word "stearn" was used in <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> and a similar word was used by the <a href="/wiki/Frisians" title="Frisians">Frisians</a> for tern.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> "Stearn" appears in the poem <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seafarer_(poem)" title="The Seafarer (poem)">The Seafarer</a></i>, written around 1000 AD.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Linnaeus adopted this word for the genus name <i>Sterna</i>. The Latin for <a href="/wiki/Swallow" title="Swallow">swallow</a> is "hirundo" and refers here to the tern's superficial likeness to that unrelated bird, which has a similar light build and long forked tail.<sup id="cite_ref-hume12_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume12-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> This resemblance also leads to the informal name "Sea Swallow",<sup id="cite_ref-rspb_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> recorded from at least the seventeenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-hume12_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume12-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_English" title="Scottish English">Scottish</a> names <i>pictar</i>, <i>tarrock</i> and their many variants are believed to be <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeic</a>, derived from the distinctive call.<sup id="cite_ref-hume12_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume12-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Due to the difficulty in distinguishing the two species, all the informal common names are shared with the Arctic Tern.<sup id="cite_ref-cocker_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cocker-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Four subspecies of the Common Tern are generally recognized, although <i>S.&#160;h.&#160;minussensis</i> is sometimes considered to be an <a href="/wiki/Intergradation" title="Intergradation">intergrade</a> between <i>S.&#160;h.&#160;hirundo</i> and <i>S.&#160;h.&#160;longipennis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-hume88_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume88-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The nominate subspecies of the Common Tern is 31–35&#160;cm (12.2–13.8&#160;in) long, including a 6–9&#160;cm (2.4–3.5&#160;in) fork in the tail, with a 77–98&#160;cm (31–39&#160;in) wingspan. It weighs 110–141&#160;g (3.9–5.0&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-olsen_14-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-olsen-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Breeding adults have pale grey upperparts, very pale grey underparts, a black cap, orange-red legs, and a narrow pointed bill that can be mostly red with a black tip, or all black, depending on the subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-hume21_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume21-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The Common Tern's upperwings are pale grey, but as the summer wears on, the dark feather shafts of the outer <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">flight feathers</a> become exposed, and a grey wedge appears on the wings. The rump and tail are white, and on a standing bird the long tail extends no further than the folded wingtips, unlike the Arctic and Roseate Terns in which the tail protrudes beyond the wings. There are no significant differences between the sexes.<sup id="cite_ref-vinicombe_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vinicombe-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> In non-breeding adults the forehead and underparts become white, the bill is all black or black with a red base, and the legs are dark red or black.<sup id="cite_ref-vinicombe_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vinicombe-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The upperwings have an obvious dark area at the front edge of the wing, the carpal bar. Terns that have not bred successfully may start <a href="/wiki/Moulting" title="Moulting">moulting</a> into non-breeding adult plumage from June, but late July is more typical, with the moult suspended during migration. There is also some geographical variation, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">Californian</a> birds often being in non-breeding plumage during migration.<sup id="cite_ref-olsen_14-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-olsen-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Juvenile Common Terns have pale grey upperwings with a dark carpal bar. The crown and <a href="/wiki/Nape" title="Nape">nape</a> are brown, and the forehead is ginger, wearing to white by autumn. The upperparts are ginger with brown and white scaling, and the tail lacks the adult's long outer feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-olsen_14-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-olsen-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Birds in their first post-juvenile plumage, which normally remain in their wintering areas, resemble the non-breeding adult, but have a duskier crown, dark carpal bar, and often very worn plumage. By their second year, most young terns are either indistinguishable from adults, or show only minor differences such as a darker bill or white forehead.<sup id="cite_ref-harrison_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harrison-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Common Tern is an agile flyer, capable of rapid turns and swoops, hovering, and vertical take-off. When commuting with fish, it flies close to the surface in a strong head wind, but 10–30&#160;m (30–90&#160;ft) above the water in a following wind. Unless migrating, normally it stays below 100&#160;m (300&#160;ft), and averages 30&#160;km/h (18.6&#160;mph) in the absence of a tail wind.<sup id="cite_ref-nisbet_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nisbet-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Its average flight speed during the <a href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal" class="mw-redirect">nocturnal</a> migration flight is 43–54&#160;km/h (27–33&#160;mph)<sup id="cite_ref-alerstam_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alerstam-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> at a height of 1,000–3,000&#160;m (3,200–9,700&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-nisbet_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nisbet-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Moult">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Moult">Moult</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Commonternprimary.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Commonternprimary.jpg/300px-Commonternprimary.jpg" width="300" height="73" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Commonternprimary.jpg/450px-Commonternprimary.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Commonternprimary.jpg/600px-Commonternprimary.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Commonternprimary.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf3/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Detail of primary feather</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The juvenile starts moulting into adult plumage in its first October; the head, tail and body plumage is replaced first, mostly by February, then the wing feathers. The <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Primaries" title="Flight feather">primaries</a> are replaced in stages; the innermost feathers moult first, then replacement is suspended during the southern winter (birds of this age staying in their wintering areas) and recommences in the autumn. In May to June of the second year a similar moult sequence starts, with a pause during primary moult for birds that return north, but not for those that stay in the winter quarters. A major moult to adult breeding plumage occurs in the next February to June, between 40–90&#160;percent of feathers being replaced.<sup id="cite_ref-olsen_14-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-olsen-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Old primary feathers wear away to reveal the blackish barbs beneath. The moult pattern means that the oldest feathers are those nearest the middle of the wing, so as the northern summer progresses, a dark wedge appears on the wing due to this feather ageing process.<sup id="cite_ref-hume21_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume21-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Terns are unusual in the frequency in which they moult their primaries, which are replaced at least twice, occasionally three times in a year. The visible difference in feather age is accentuated in the greater <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> reflectance of new primaries, and the freshness of the wing feathers is used by females in mate selection.<sup id="cite_ref-bridgeeaton_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bridgeeaton-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Experienced females tend to accept mates which best show their <a href="/wiki/Fitness_(biology)" title="Fitness (biology)">fitness</a> through the quality of their wing feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-bridge2004_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bridge2004-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Rarely, a very early moult at the nesting colony may be linked to breeding failure, both the onset of moult and reproductive behaviour being linked to falling levels of the <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormone</a> <a href="/wiki/Prolactin" title="Prolactin">prolactin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BB105_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB105-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Similar species">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_species">Similar species</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Arctic_Tern_(Sterna_paradisaea),_Ward_of_Clugan_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1357999.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Arctic_Tern_%28Sterna_paradisaea%29%2C_Ward_of_Clugan_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1357999.jpg/220px-Arctic_Tern_%28Sterna_paradisaea%29%2C_Ward_of_Clugan_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1357999.jpg" width="220" height="226" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Arctic_Tern_%28Sterna_paradisaea%29%2C_Ward_of_Clugan_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1357999.jpg/330px-Arctic_Tern_%28Sterna_paradisaea%29%2C_Ward_of_Clugan_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1357999.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Arctic_Tern_%28Sterna_paradisaea%29%2C_Ward_of_Clugan_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1357999.jpg/440px-Arctic_Tern_%28Sterna_paradisaea%29%2C_Ward_of_Clugan_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1357999.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Arctic_Tern_(Sterna_paradisaea),_Ward_of_Clugan_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1357999.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf3/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
This <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Tern" title="Arctic Tern">Arctic Tern</a> shows the all-red bill, greyer underparts, translucent flight feathers, and narrow black border to the wings which separate it from the Common Tern.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There are several terns of a similar size and general appearance to the Common Tern. A traditionally difficult species to separate is the Arctic Tern, and until the key characteristics were clarified, distant or flying birds of the two species were often jointly recorded as "commic terns". Although similar in size, the two terns differ in structure and flight. The Common Tern has a larger head, thicker neck, longer legs, and more triangular and stiffer wings than its relative, and has a more powerful, direct flight.<sup id="cite_ref-BB86_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB86-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> The Arctic Tern has greyer underparts than the Common, which make its white cheeks more obvious, whereas the rump of the Common Tern can be greyish in non-breeding plumage, compared to the white of its relative. The Common Tern develops a dark wedge on the wings as the breeding season progresses, but the wings of Arctic stay white throughout the northern summer. All the <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">flight feathers</a> of the Arctic Tern are translucent against a bright sky, only the four innermost wing feathers of the Common Tern share this property.<sup id="cite_ref-BB86_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB86-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vanDuivendijk_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vanDuivendijk-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> The trailing edge of the outer flight feathers is a thin black line in the Arctic Tern, but thicker and less defined in the Common.<sup id="cite_ref-vinicombe_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vinicombe-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The bill of an adult Common Tern is orange-red with a black tip, except in black-billed <i>S.&#160;h.&#160;longipennis</i>, and its legs are bright red, while both features are a darker red colour in the Arctic Tern, which also lacks the black bill tip.<sup id="cite_ref-BB86_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB86-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the breeding areas, the Roseate Tern can be distinguished by its pale plumage, long, mainly black bill and very long tail feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-vanDuivendijk_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vanDuivendijk-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> The non-breeding plumage of Roseate is pale above and white, sometimes pink-tinged, below. It retains the long tail streamers, and has a black bill.<sup id="cite_ref-olsen2_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-olsen2-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> In flight, the Roseate's heavier head and neck, long bill and faster, stiffer wingbeats are also characteristic.<sup id="cite_ref-Blomdahl_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blomdahl-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> It feeds further out to sea than the Common Tern.<sup id="cite_ref-olsen2_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-olsen2-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> In North America, the <a href="/wiki/Forster%27s_Tern" title="Forster's Tern">Forster's Tern</a> in breeding plumage is obviously larger than the Common, with relatively short wings, a heavy head and thick bill, and long, strong legs; in all non-breeding plumages, its white head and dark eye patch make the American species unmistakable.<sup id="cite_ref-olsen2_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-olsen2-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the wintering regions, there are also confusion species, including the Antarctic Tern of the southern oceans, the <a href="/wiki/South_American_Tern" title="South American Tern">South American Tern</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasian</a> <a href="/wiki/White-fronted_Tern" title="White-fronted Tern">White-fronted Tern</a> and the <a href="/wiki/White-cheeked_Tern" title="White-cheeked Tern">White-cheeked Tern</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>. Identification may be aided by the plumage differences due to "opposite" breeding seasons. The Antarctic Tern is more sturdy than the Common, with a heavier bill. In breeding condition, its dusky underparts and full black cap outline a white cheek stripe. In non-breeding plumages, it lacks, or has only an indistinct, carpal bar, and young birds show dark bars on the <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Tertials" title="Flight feather">tertials</a>, obvious on the closed wing and in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-Enticott196_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Enticott196-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SASOL_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SASOL-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> The South American Tern is larger than the Common, with a larger, more curved red bill, and has a smoother, more extensive black cap in non-breeding plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-peru_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peru-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> Like Antarctic, it lacks a strong carpal bar in non-breeding plumages, and it also shares the distinctive barring of the tertials in young birds.<sup id="cite_ref-Enticott192_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Enticott192-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> The White-fronted Tern has a white forehead in breeding plumage, a heavier bill, and in non-breeding plumage is paler below than the Common, with white underwings.<sup id="cite_ref-Simpson_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simpson-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> The White-cheeked Tern is smaller, has uniform grey upperparts, and in breeding pumage is darker above with whiter cheeks.<sup id="cite_ref-grimmett_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grimmett-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Juvenile Common Terns are easily separated from similar-aged birds of related species. They show extensive ginger colouration to the back, and have a pale base to the bill. Young Arctic Terns have a grey back and black bill, and juvenile Roseate Terns have a distinctive scalloped "saddle".<sup id="cite_ref-vinicombe_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vinicombe-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">Hybrids</a> between Common and Roseate Terns have been recorded, particularly from the US, and the intermediate plumage and calls shown by these birds is a potential identification pitfall. Such birds may have more extensive black on the bill, but confirmation of mixed breeding may depend on the exact details of individual flight feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-olsen_14-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-olsen-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The Common Tern has a wide repertoire of calls, which have a lower pitch than the equivalent calls of Arctic Terns. The most distinctive sound is the <a href="/wiki/Alarm_signal" title="Alarm signal">alarm</a> <i>KEE-yah</i>, stressed on the first syllable, in contrast to the second-syllable stress of the Arctic Tern. The alarm call doubles up as a warning to intruders, although serious threats evoke a <i>kyar</i>, given as a tern takes flight, and quietens the usually noisy colony while its residents assess the danger.<sup id="cite_ref-hume68_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume68-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> A down-slurred <i>keeur</i> is given when an adult is approaching the nest while carrying a fish, and is possibly used for individual recognition (chicks emerge from hiding when they hear their parents giving this call). Another common call is a <i>kip</i> uttered during social contact. Other vocalizations include a <i>kakakakaka</i> when attacking intruders, and a staccato <i>kek-kek-kek</i> from fighting males.<sup id="cite_ref-hume68_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume68-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Parents and chicks can locate one another by call, and <a href="/wiki/Sibling" title="Sibling">siblings</a> also recognise each other's vocalisations from about the twelfth day from hatching, which helps to keep the brood together.<sup id="cite_ref-sibling_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sibling-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Stevenson_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stevenson-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sterna_hirundo_-Bojuru,_Rio_Grande_do_Sul,_Brasil-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Sterna_hirundo_-Bojuru%2C_Rio_Grande_do_Sul%2C_Brasil-8.jpg/220px-Sterna_hirundo_-Bojuru%2C_Rio_Grande_do_Sul%2C_Brasil-8.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Sterna_hirundo_-Bojuru%2C_Rio_Grande_do_Sul%2C_Brasil-8.jpg/330px-Sterna_hirundo_-Bojuru%2C_Rio_Grande_do_Sul%2C_Brasil-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Sterna_hirundo_-Bojuru%2C_Rio_Grande_do_Sul%2C_Brasil-8.jpg/440px-Sterna_hirundo_-Bojuru%2C_Rio_Grande_do_Sul%2C_Brasil-8.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sterna_hirundo_-Bojuru,_Rio_Grande_do_Sul,_Brasil-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf3/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Non-breeding adults in Brazil</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Most populations of the Common Tern are strongly migratory, wintering south of their temperate and subarctic Northern Hemisphere breeding ranges. First summer birds usually remain in their wintering quarters, although a few return to breeding colonies some time after the arrival of the adults.<sup id="cite_ref-harrison_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harrison-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> In North America, the Common Tern breeds along the Atlantic coast from <a href="/wiki/Labrador" title="Labrador">Labrador</a> to <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, and inland throughout much of Canada east of the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a>. In the United States, some breeding populations can also be found in the states bordering the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>, and locally on the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf</a> coast.<sup id="cite_ref-cuthbert4_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cuthbert4-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> There are small, only partially migratory, colonies in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>; these are in The Bahamas and Cuba,<sup id="cite_ref-raff_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-raff-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> and off Venezuela in the <a href="/wiki/Los_Roques_archipelago" title="Los Roques archipelago">Los Roques</a> and <a href="/wiki/Las_Aves_archipelago" title="Las Aves archipelago">Las Aves archipelagos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hilty_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hilty-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>New World birds winter along both coasts of Central and South America, to Argentina on the east coast and to Northern Chile on the west coast.<sup id="cite_ref-harrison_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harrison-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cuthbert4_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cuthbert4-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> Records from South America and the Azores show that some birds may cross the Atlantic in both directions on their migration.<sup id="cite_ref-lima_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lima-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-neves_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-neves-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Common Tern breeds across most of Europe, with the highest numbers in the north and east of the continent. There are small populations on the north African coast, and in the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>. Most winter off western or southern Africa, birds from the south and west of Europe tending to stay north of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> and other European birds moving further south.<sup id="cite_ref-snow779_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow779-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> The breeding range continues across the temperate and <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a> zones of Asia, with scattered outposts on the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> and the coast of Iran.<sup id="cite_ref-hume39_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume39-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> Small populations breed on islands off Sri Lanka,<sup id="cite_ref-hoffmann_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hoffmann-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hoffmann2_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hoffmann2-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> and in the <a href="/wiki/Ladakh" title="Ladakh">Ladakh</a> region of the Tibetan plateau.<sup id="cite_ref-ras_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ras-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> Western Asian birds winter in the northern <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-harrison_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harrison-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> and <i>S.&#160;h.&#160;tibetana</i> appears to be common off <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a> during the northern hemisphere winter.<sup id="cite_ref-Zimmerman_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zimmerman-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> Birds from further north and east in Asia, such as <i>S.&#160;h.&#160;longipennis</i>, move through Japan, Thailand and the western <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a> as far as southern Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-harrison_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harrison-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> There are small and erratic colonies in <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>, in Nigeria and Guinea-Bissau, unusual in that they are within what is mainly a wintering area.<sup id="cite_ref-hume39_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume39-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> Only a few Common Terns have been recorded in New Zealand,<sup id="cite_ref-Robertson_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robertson-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> and this species' status in <a href="/wiki/Polynesia" title="Polynesia">Polynesia</a> is unclear.<sup id="cite_ref-watling170_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-watling170-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> A bird <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">ringed</a> at the nest in Sweden was found dead on <a href="/wiki/Stewart_Island" title="Stewart Island" class="mw-redirect">Stewart Island</a>, New Zealand, five months later, having flown an estimated 25,000&#160;km (15,000&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-newton_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newton-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Common_Terns_nesting.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Common_Terns_nesting.jpg/220px-Common_Terns_nesting.jpg" width="220" height="93" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Common_Terns_nesting.jpg/330px-Common_Terns_nesting.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Common_Terns_nesting.jpg/440px-Common_Terns_nesting.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Common_Terns_nesting.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf3/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Nest site, <a href="/wiki/Elliston,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Elliston, Newfoundland and Labrador">Elliston, Newfoundland and Labrador</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As long distance migrants, Common Terns sometimes occur well outside their normal range. Stray birds have been found inland in Africa (Zambia and Malawi), and on the Maldives and Comoros islands;<sup id="cite_ref-BirdLife2_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLife2-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> the nominate subspecies has reached Australia,<sup id="cite_ref-Simpson_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simpson-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> the Andes, and the interior of South America.<sup id="cite_ref-peru_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peru-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Costanzo_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Costanzo-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> Asian <i>S.&#160;h.&#160;longipennis</i> has recent records from western Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-BW_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BW-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Common Tern breeds over a wider range of habitats than any of its relatives, nesting from the <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a> of Asia to tropical shores,<sup id="cite_ref-hume30_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume30-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> and at altitudes up to 2,000&#160;m (6,500&#160;ft) in Armenia, and 4,800&#160;m (15,500&#160;ft) in Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-snow779_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow779-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> It avoids areas which are frequently exposed to excessive rain or wind, and also icy waters, so it does not breed as far north as the Arctic Tern. The Common Tern breeds close to freshwater or the sea on almost any open flat habitat, including sand or <a href="/wiki/Shingle_beach" title="Shingle beach">shingle beaches</a>, firm dune areas, <a href="/wiki/Salt_marsh" title="Salt marsh">salt marsh</a>, or, most commonly, islands. Flat grassland or <a href="/wiki/Heath" title="Heath">heath</a>, or even large flat rocks may be suitable in an island environment.<sup id="cite_ref-hume30_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume30-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> In mixed colonies, Common Terns will tolerate somewhat longer ground vegetation than Arctic Terns, but avoid the even taller growth acceptable to Roseate Terns; the relevant factor here is the different leg lengths of the three species.<sup id="cite_ref-Fisher_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fisher-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> Common Terns adapt readily to artificial floating rafts, and may even nest on flat factory roofs.<sup id="cite_ref-hume30_53-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume30-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> Unusual nest sites include hay bales, a stump 0.6&#160;m (2&#160;ft) above the water, and floating logs or vegetation. There is a record of a Common Tern taking over a <a href="/wiki/Spotted_Sandpiper" title="Spotted Sandpiper">Spotted Sandpiper</a> nest and laying its eggs with those of the <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ontario_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ontario-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> Outside the breeding season, all that is needed in terms of habitat is access to fishing areas, and somewhere to land. In addition to natural beaches and rocks, boats, buoys and piers are often used both as perches and night-time roosts.<sup id="cite_ref-hume30_53-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume30-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Territory">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Territory">Territory</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sterna_hirundo_-nest_with_three_eggs-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Sterna_hirundo_-nest_with_three_eggs-8.jpg/220px-Sterna_hirundo_-nest_with_three_eggs-8.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Sterna_hirundo_-nest_with_three_eggs-8.jpg/330px-Sterna_hirundo_-nest_with_three_eggs-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Sterna_hirundo_-nest_with_three_eggs-8.jpg/440px-Sterna_hirundo_-nest_with_three_eggs-8.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sterna_hirundo_-nest_with_three_eggs-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf3/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>The Common Tern breeds in colonies which do not normally exceed 2,000 <a href="/wiki/Breeding_pair" title="Breeding pair">pairs</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-snow779_41-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow779-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> but may occasionally number more than 20,000 pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-wolf_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wolf-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> Colonies inland tend to be smaller than on the coast. Common Terns often nest alongside other coastal species, such as Arctic,<sup id="cite_ref-rob_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rob-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> Roseate and <a href="/wiki/Sandwich_Tern" title="Sandwich Tern">Sandwich Terns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black-headed_Gull" title="Black-headed Gull">Black-headed Gulls</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ramos_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ramos-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fuchs_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fuchs-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Black_Skimmer" title="Black Skimmer">Black Skimmers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-erwin_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-erwin-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> Especially in the early part of the breeding season, for no known reason, most or all of the terns will fly in silence low and fast out to sea. This phenomenon is called a "dread".<sup id="cite_ref-snow779_41-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow779-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sterna_hirundo_-Massachusetts,_USA_-juvenile-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Sterna_hirundo_-Massachusetts%2C_USA_-juvenile-8.jpg/220px-Sterna_hirundo_-Massachusetts%2C_USA_-juvenile-8.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Sterna_hirundo_-Massachusetts%2C_USA_-juvenile-8.jpg/330px-Sterna_hirundo_-Massachusetts%2C_USA_-juvenile-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Sterna_hirundo_-Massachusetts%2C_USA_-juvenile-8.jpg/440px-Sterna_hirundo_-Massachusetts%2C_USA_-juvenile-8.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sterna_hirundo_-Massachusetts,_USA_-juvenile-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf3/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
This autumn juvenile in Massachusetts has a white forehead, having lost the ginger colouration characteristic of younger birds</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On their return to the breeding sites, the terns may loiter for a few days before settling into a territory,<sup id="cite_ref-hume86_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume86-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> and the actual start of nesting may be linked to a high availability of fish.<sup id="cite_ref-safina_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-safina-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> Terns defend only a small area, with distances between nests sometimes being as little as 50&#160;cm (20&#160;in), although 150–350&#160;cm (60–130&#160;in) is more typical. As with many birds, the same site is re-used year after year, with a record of one pair returning for 17 successive breeding seasons. Around 90&#160;percent of experienced birds reuse their former territory, so young birds must nest on the periphery, find a bereaved mate, or move to another colony.<sup id="cite_ref-hume86_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume86-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> A male selects a nesting territory a few days after his arrival in the spring, and is joined by his previous partner unless she is more than five days late, in which case the pair may separate.<sup id="cite_ref-solis_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-solis-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The defence of the territory is mainly by the male, who repels intruders of either sex. He gives an alarm call, opens his wings, raises his tail and bows his head to show the black cap. If the intruder persists, the male stops calling and fights by bill grappling until the intruder submits by raising its head to expose the throat. Aerial trespassers are simply attacked, sometimes following a joint upward spiralling flight.<sup id="cite_ref-hume86_61-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume86-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> Despite the aggression shown to adults, wandering chicks are usually tolerated, whereas in a gull colony they would be attacked and killed. The nest is defended until the chicks have fledged, and all the adults in the colony will collectively repel potential predators.<sup id="cite_ref-hume79_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume79-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<p>Pairs are established or confirmed through aerial <a href="/wiki/Courtship_display" title="Courtship display">courtship displays</a> in which a male and a female fly in wide circles up to 200&#160;m (600&#160;ft) or more, calling all the while, before the two birds descend together in zigzag glides. If the male is carrying a fish, he may attract the attention of other males too. On the ground, the male courts the female by circling her with his tail and neck raised, head pointing down, and wings partially open. If she responds, they may both adopt a posture with the head pointed skywards. The male may tease a female with the fish, not parting with his offering until she has displayed to him sufficiently.<sup id="cite_ref-hume91_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume91-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> Once courtship is complete, the male makes a shallow depression in the sand, and the female scratches in the same place. Several trials may take place until the pair settle on a site for the actual nest.<sup id="cite_ref-hume91_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume91-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> The eggs may be laid on bare sand, gravel or soil, but a lining of debris or vegetation is often added if available,<sup id="cite_ref-snow779_41-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow779-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> or the nest may be rimmed with seaweed, stones or shells. The saucer-shaped scrape is typically 4&#160;cm (1.5&#160;in) deep and 10&#160;cm (4&#160;in) across, but may extend to as much as 24&#160;cm (10&#160;in) wide including the surrounding decorative material.<sup id="cite_ref-hume100_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume100-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> Breeding success in areas prone to flooding has been enhanced by the provision of artificial mats made from <a href="/wiki/Zostera" title="Zostera">eelgrass</a>, which encourage the terns to nest in higher, less vulnerable areas, since many prefer the mats to bare sand.<sup id="cite_ref-Palestis_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palestis-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> The Common Tern tends to use more nest material than Roseate or Arctic Terns, although Roseate often nests in areas with more growing vegetation.<sup id="cite_ref-Lloyd_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lloyd-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bent_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bent-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sterna_hirundo_-hovering_to_protect_nest-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Sterna_hirundo_-hovering_to_protect_nest-8.jpg/220px-Sterna_hirundo_-hovering_to_protect_nest-8.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Sterna_hirundo_-hovering_to_protect_nest-8.jpg/330px-Sterna_hirundo_-hovering_to_protect_nest-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Sterna_hirundo_-hovering_to_protect_nest-8.jpg/440px-Sterna_hirundo_-hovering_to_protect_nest-8.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sterna_hirundo_-hovering_to_protect_nest-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf3/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>Terns are expert at locating their nests in a large colony. Studies show that terns can find and excavate their eggs when they are buried, even if the nest material is removed and the sand smoothed over. They will find a nest placed 5&#160;m (15&#160;ft) from its original site, or even further if it is moved in several stages. Eggs are accepted if reshaped with <a href="/wiki/Plasticine" title="Plasticine">plasticine</a> or coloured yellow (but not red or blue). This ability to locate the eggs is an adaptation to life in an unstable, wind-blown and tidal environment.<sup id="cite_ref-Fisher_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fisher-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The peak time for egg production is early May, with some birds, particularly first-time breeders, laying later in the month or in June.<sup id="cite_ref-ontario_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ontario-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hume100_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume100-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> size is normally three eggs; larger clutches probably result from two females laying in the same nest. Egg size averages 41&#160;×&#160;31&#160;mm (1.6&#160;×&#160;1.2&#160;in), although each successive egg in a clutch is slightly smaller than the first laid.<sup id="cite_ref-hume100_66-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume100-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> The average egg weight is 20.2&#160;g (0.7&#160;oz), of which 5&#160;percent is shell.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> The egg weight depends on how well-fed the female is, as well as on its the position in the clutch. The eggs are cream, <a href="/wiki/Buff_(colour)" title="Buff (colour)">buff</a>, or pale brown, marked with streaks, spots or blotches of black, brown or grey which help to camouflage them.<sup id="cite_ref-hume100_66-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume100-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> Incubation is by both sexes, although more often by the female, and lasts 21–22&#160;days,<sup id="cite_ref-bto_70-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> extending to 25&#160;days if there are frequent disturbances at the colony which cause the adults to leave the eggs unattended;<sup id="cite_ref-hume100_66-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume100-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> nocturnal predation may lead to incubation taking up to 34&#160;days.<sup id="cite_ref-ontario_55-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ontario-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> On hot days the incubating parent may fly to water to wet its belly feathers before returning to the eggs, thus affording the eggs some cooling.<sup id="cite_ref-nisbet_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nisbet-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Except when the colony suffers disaster, 90&#160;percent of the eggs hatch.<sup id="cite_ref-hume112_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume112-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Precocial" title="Precocial">precocial</a> <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">downy</a> chick is yellowish with black or brown markings,<sup id="cite_ref-hume100_66-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume100-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> and like the eggs, is similar to the equivalent stage of the Arctic Tern.<sup id="cite_ref-pearson121_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pearson121-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> The chicks <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> in 22–28&#160;days,<sup id="cite_ref-bto_70-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> usually 25–26.<sup id="cite_ref-snow779_41-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow779-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> Fledged juveniles are fed at the nest for about five days, and then accompany the adults on fishing expeditions. The young birds may receive supplementary feeds from the parents until the end of the breeding season, and beyond. Common Terns have been recorded feeding their offspring on migration and in the wintering grounds, at least until the adults move further south in about December.<sup id="cite_ref-nisbet_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nisbet-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hume120_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume120-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Like many terns, this species is very defensive of its nest and young, and will harass humans, dogs, <a href="/wiki/Muskrat" title="Muskrat">muskrats</a> and most <a href="/wiki/Diurnality" title="Diurnality">diurnal</a> birds, but unlike the more aggressive Arctic Tern, it rarely hits the intruder, usually swerving off at the last moment. Adults can discriminate between individual humans, attacking familiar people more intensely than strangers.<sup id="cite_ref-Burger_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burger-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup> Nocturnal predators do not elicit similar attacks;<sup id="cite_ref-hunter_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hunter-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup> colonies can be wiped out by rats, and adults desert the colony for up to eight hours when <a href="/wiki/Great_Horned_Owl" title="Great Horned Owl">Great Horned Owls</a> are present.<sup id="cite_ref-nisbet3_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nisbet3-76"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Common Terns usually breed once a year. Second clutches are possible if the first is lost. Rarely, a second clutch may be laid and incubated while some chicks from the first clutch are still being fed.<sup id="cite_ref-hays_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hays-77"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup> The first breeding attempt is usually at four years of age, sometimes at three years. The average number of young per pair surviving to fledging can vary from zero in the event of the colony being flooded to over 2.5 in a good year. In North America, productivity was between 1.0 and 2.0 on islands, but less than 1.0 at coastal and inland sites. Birds become more successful at raising chicks with age. This continues throughout their breeding lives, but the biggest increase is in the first five years.<sup id="cite_ref-nisbet_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nisbet-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pearson121_72-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pearson121-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> The maximum documented lifespan in the wild is 23&#160;years in North America<sup id="cite_ref-nisbet2_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nisbet2-78"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-austin_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-austin-79"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup> and 33&#160;years in Europe,<sup id="cite_ref-agebi_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-agebi-80"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ageeu_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ageeu-81"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup> but 12&#160;years is a more typical lifespan.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_70-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Food and feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_feeding">Food and feeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sterna_hirundo_-West_Bromwich,_England_-flying-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Sterna_hirundo_-West_Bromwich%2C_England_-flying-8.jpg/220px-Sterna_hirundo_-West_Bromwich%2C_England_-flying-8.jpg" width="220" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Sterna_hirundo_-West_Bromwich%2C_England_-flying-8.jpg/330px-Sterna_hirundo_-West_Bromwich%2C_England_-flying-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Sterna_hirundo_-West_Bromwich%2C_England_-flying-8.jpg/440px-Sterna_hirundo_-West_Bromwich%2C_England_-flying-8.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sterna_hirundo_-West_Bromwich,_England_-flying-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf3/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>Like all <i>Sterna</i> terns, the Common Tern feeds by plunge-diving for fish, from a height of 1–6&#160;m (3–18&#160;ft), either in the sea or in freshwater lakes and large rivers. The bird may submerge for a second or so, but to no more than 50&#160;cm (18&#160;in) below the surface.<sup id="cite_ref-hume55_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume55-82"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup> When seeking fish, this tern flies head-down and with its bill held vertically.<sup id="cite_ref-Fisher_54-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fisher-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> It may circle or hover before diving, and then plunges directly into the water, whereas the Arctic Tern favours a "stepped-hover" technique,<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup> and the Roseate Tern dives at speed from a greater height, and submerges for longer.<sup id="cite_ref-BB80_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB80-84"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup> The Common Tern typically forages up to 5–10&#160;km (3–6&#160;mi) away from the breeding colony, sometimes as far as 15&#160;km (9&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-BirdLife_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLife-85"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup> It will follow schools of fish, and its west African migration route is affected by the location of huge shoals of <a href="/wiki/Sardine" title="Sardine">sardines</a> off the coast of Ghana;<sup id="cite_ref-hume55_82-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume55-82"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup> it will also track groups of <a href="/wiki/Predatory_fish" title="Predatory fish">predatory fish</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">dolphins</a>, waiting for their prey to be driven to the sea's surface.<sup id="cite_ref-BirdLife_85-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLife-85"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bugoni_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bugoni-86"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup> Terns often feed in flocks, especially if food is plentiful, and the fishing success rate in a flock is typically about one-third higher than for individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-hume55_82-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume55-82"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Terns have red oil droplets in the <a href="/wiki/Cone_cell" title="Cone cell">cone cells</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">retinas</a> of their eyes. This improves contrast and sharpens distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_vision" title="Bird vision">vision</a>, especially in hazy conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair-87"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup> Birds that have to see through an air/water interface, such as terns and gulls, have more strongly coloured <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">carotenoid</a> <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigments</a> in the cone oil drops than other avian species.<sup id="cite_ref-Varela_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Varela-88"><span>[</span>88<span>]</span></a></sup> The improved eyesight helps terns to locate shoals of fish, although it is uncertain whether they are sighting the <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> on which the fish feed, or observing other terns diving for food.<sup id="cite_ref-Lythgoe_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lythgoe-89"><span>[</span>89<span>]</span></a></sup> Tern's eyes are not particularly <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> sensitive, an adaptation more suited to terrestrial feeders like the gulls.<sup id="cite_ref-Hastad_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hastad-90"><span>[</span>90<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The Common Tern preferentially hunts fish 5–15&#160;cm (2–6&#160;in) long.<sup id="cite_ref-ontario_55-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ontario-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hume55_82-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume55-82"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup> The species caught depend on what is available, but if there is a choice, terns feeding several chicks will take larger prey than those with smaller broods.<sup id="cite_ref-Stephens_91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stephens-91"><span>[</span>91<span>]</span></a></sup> The proportion of fish fed to chicks may be as high as 95&#160;percent in some areas, but <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrate</a> prey may form a significant part of the diet elsewhere. This may include <a href="/wiki/Annelid" title="Annelid">worms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leech" title="Leech">leeches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mollusca" title="Mollusca">molluscs</a> such as small <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> (<a href="/wiki/Prawn" title="Prawn">prawns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hippoidea" title="Hippoidea">mole crabs</a>). In freshwater areas, large <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> may be caught, such as <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cockchafer" title="Cockchafer">cockchafers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moths</a>. Adult insects may be caught in the air, and <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> picked from the ground or from the water surface. Prey is caught in the bill and either swallowed head-first, or carried back to the chicks. Occasionally, two or more small fish may be carried simultaneously.<sup id="cite_ref-hume55_82-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume55-82"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup> When adults take food back to the nest, they recognise their young by call, rather than visual identification.<sup id="cite_ref-Stevenson_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stevenson-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Common Tern may attempt to steal fish from Arctic Terns,<sup id="cite_ref-hopkins_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hopkins-92"><span>[</span>92<span>]</span></a></sup> but might itself be harassed by <a href="/wiki/Kleptoparasitism" title="Kleptoparasitism">kleptoparasitic</a> skuas,<sup id="cite_ref-belisle_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-belisle-93"><span>[</span>93<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Laughing_Gull" title="Laughing Gull">Laughing Gulls</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-hatch_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hatch-94"><span>[</span>94<span>]</span></a></sup> Roseate Terns,<sup id="cite_ref-dunn_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dunn-95"><span>[</span>95<span>]</span></a></sup> or by other Common Terns while bringing fish back to its nest.<sup id="cite_ref-hopkins_92-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hopkins-92"><span>[</span>92<span>]</span></a></sup> In one study, two males whose mates had died spent much time stealing food from neighbouring broods.<sup id="cite_ref-wilson_96-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wilson-96"><span>[</span>96<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Terns normally drink in flight, usually taking seawater in preference to freshwater, if both are available.<sup id="cite_ref-nisbet_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nisbet-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Chicks do not drink before fledging, reabsorbing water, and, like adults, excreting excess salt in a concentrated solution from a specialised nasal gland.<sup id="cite_ref-hughes_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hughes-97"><span>[</span>97<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Karleskint_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Karleskint-98"><span>[</span>98<span>]</span></a></sup> Fish bones and the hard <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeletons</a> of crustaceans or insects are regurgitated as <a href="/wiki/Pellet_(ornithology)" title="Pellet (ornithology)">pellets</a>. Adults fly off the nest to <a href="/wiki/Defecation" title="Defecation">defecate</a>, and even small chicks walk a short distance from the scrape to deposit their <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">faeces</a>. Adults attacking animals or humans will often defecate as they dive, often successfully fouling the intruder.<sup id="cite_ref-nisbet_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nisbet-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Predators and parasites">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Predators_and_parasites">Predators and parasites</span></h2>
<p>Rats will take tern eggs, and may even store large numbers in <a href="/wiki/Hoarding_(animal_behavior)" title="Hoarding (animal behavior)">caches</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-austin_79-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-austin-79"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/American_mink" title="American mink">American mink</a> is an important predator of hatched chicks, both in North America, and in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> where it has been <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hume112_71-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume112-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Red_fox" title="Red fox">red fox</a> can also be a local problem.<sup id="cite_ref-jncc_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jncc-99"><span>[</span>99<span>]</span></a></sup> Because Common Terns nest on islands, the most common predators are normally other birds rather than mammals. The <a href="/wiki/Ruddy_Turnstone" title="Ruddy Turnstone">Ruddy Turnstone</a> will take eggs from unattended nests,<sup id="cite_ref-parkes_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-parkes-100"><span>[</span>100<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fara_101-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fara-101"><span>[</span>101<span>]</span></a></sup> and gulls may take chicks.<sup id="cite_ref-houde_102-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-houde-102"><span>[</span>102<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-whittam_103-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whittam-103"><span>[</span>103<span>]</span></a></sup> Great Horned Owls and <a href="/wiki/Short-eared_Owl" title="Short-eared Owl">Short-eared Owls</a> will kill both adults and chicks, and <a href="/wiki/Black-crowned_Night_Heron" title="Black-crowned Night Heron">Black-crowned Night Herons</a> will also eat small chicks.<sup id="cite_ref-nisbet_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nisbet-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-morris_104-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morris-104"><span>[</span>104<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Merlin" title="Merlin">Merlins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_Falcon" title="Peregrine Falcon">Peregrine Falcons</a> may attack flying terns; as with other birds, it seems likely that one advantage of flocking behaviour is to confuse fast-flying predators.<sup id="cite_ref-hume79_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume79-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Common Tern hosts <a href="/wiki/Bird_louse" title="Bird louse">feather lice</a>, which are quite different from those found in Arctic Terns, despite the close relationship of the two birds.<sup id="cite_ref-Rothschild135_105-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rothschild135-105"><span>[</span>105<span>]</span></a></sup> It may also be infected by parasitic worms, such as the widespread <i><a href="/wiki/Diphyllobothrium" title="Diphyllobothrium">Diphyllobothrium</a></i> species, the duck parasite <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ligula_intestinalis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ligula intestinalis (page does not exist)">Ligula intestinalis</a></i>, and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schistocephalus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Schistocephalus (page does not exist)">Schistocephalus</a></i> species carried initially by fish. <a href="/wiki/Cestoda" title="Cestoda">Tapeworms</a> of the family <a href="/wiki/Cyclophyllidea" title="Cyclophyllidea">Cyclophyllidea</a> may also infect this species. The <a href="/wiki/Mite" title="Mite">mite</a> <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reighardia_sternae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Reighardia sternae (page does not exist)">Reighardia sternae</a></i> has been found in Common Terns from Italy, North America and China.<sup id="cite_ref-Rothschild194_106-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rothschild194-106"><span>[</span>106<span>]</span></a></sup> A study of 75 breeding Common Terns found that none carried blood parasites.<sup id="cite_ref-Fiorello_107-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fiorello-107"><span>[</span>107<span>]</span></a></sup> Colonies have been affected by <a href="/wiki/Fowl_cholera" title="Fowl cholera">avian cholera</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psittacosis" title="Psittacosis">ornithosis</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-nisbet_5-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nisbet-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> and it is possible that the Common Tern may be threatened in the future by outbreaks of <a href="/wiki/Avian_influenza" title="Avian influenza">avian influenza</a> to which it is susceptible.<sup id="cite_ref-BirdLife_85-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLife-85"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1961 the Common Tern was the first wild bird species identified as infected with avian influenza, the <a href="/wiki/Influenza_A_virus_subtype_H5N3" title="Influenza A virus subtype H5N3">H5N3</a> variant being found in an outbreak of South African birds.<sup id="cite_ref-olsen2006_108-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-olsen2006-108"><span>[</span>108<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The Common Tern is classed as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> It has a large population of 1,600,000–4,600,000 mature individuals and a huge breeding range estimated at 29,200,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (11,300,000&#160;mi<sup>2</sup>). Breeding numbers have been estimated at 250,000–500,000 pairs, the majority breeding in Asia. Up to 140,000 pairs breed in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Enticott_109-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Enticott-109"><span>[</span>109<span>]</span></a></sup> Fewer than 80,000 pairs breed in North America, with most breeding on the northeast Atlantic coast<sup id="cite_ref-kress_110-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kress-110"><span>[</span>110<span>]</span></a></sup> and a declining population of less than 10,000 pairs breeding in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes_region" title="Great Lakes region">Great Lakes region</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cuthbert1_111-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cuthbert1-111"><span>[</span>111<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the nineteenth century, the use of tern feathers and wings in the <a href="/wiki/Hatmaking" title="Hatmaking">millinery</a> trade was the main cause of large reductions in Common Tern populations in both Europe and North America, especially on the Atlantic coasts and inland. Sometimes entire stuffed birds were used to make hats. Numbers largely recovered early in the twentieth century mainly due to legislation and the work of <a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">conservation</a> organizations.<sup id="cite_ref-nisbet_5-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nisbet-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jncc_99-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jncc-99"><span>[</span>99<span>]</span></a></sup> Although some Eurasian populations are stable, numbers in North America have fallen by more than 70&#160;percent in the last 40&#160;years, and there is an overall negative trend in the global estimates for this species.<sup id="cite_ref-BirdLife_85-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLife-85"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Threats come from habitat loss through building, pollution or vegetation growth, or disturbance of breeding birds by humans, vehicles, boats or dogs. Local natural flooding may lead to nest losses, and some colonies are vulnerable to predation by rats and large gulls. Gulls also compete with terns for nest sites. Some birds are hunted in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> for commercial sale as food.<sup id="cite_ref-BirdLife_85-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLife-85"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup> Breeding success may be enhanced by the use of floating nest rafts, man-made islands or other artificial nest sites, and by preventing human disturbance. Overgrown vegetation may be burned to clear the ground, and gulls can be killed or discouraged by deliberate disturbance.<sup id="cite_ref-BirdLife_85-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLife-85"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup> Contamination with <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyl" title="Polychlorinated biphenyl">polychlorinated biphenyls</a> (PCBs) resulted in enhanced levels of <a href="/wiki/Feminization_(biology)" title="Feminization (biology)">feminisation</a> in male embryos, which seemed to disappear prior to fledging, with no effect on colony productivity,<sup id="cite_ref-hart_112-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hart-112"><span>[</span>112<span>]</span></a></sup> but <a href="/wiki/Dichlorodiphenyldichloroethylene" title="Dichlorodiphenyldichloroethylene">dichlorodiphenyldichloroethylene</a> (DDE), which results from the breakdown of <a href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a>, led to very low levels of successful breeding in some US locations.<sup id="cite_ref-nisbet_5-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nisbet-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Common Tern is one of the species to which the <a href="/wiki/Agreement_on_the_Conservation_of_African-Eurasian_Migratory_Waterbirds" title="Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a> (AEWA) and the US-Canada <a href="/wiki/Migratory_Bird_Treaty_Act_of_1918" title="Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918">Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918</a> apply.<sup id="cite_ref-AEWA_113-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AEWA-113"><span>[</span>113<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-1918treaty_114-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1918treaty-114"><span>[</span>114<span>]</span></a></sup> Parties to the AWEA agreement are required to engage in a wide range of conservation strategies described in a detailed action plan. The plan is intended to address key issues such as species and habitat conservation, management of human activities, research, education, and implementation.<sup id="cite_ref-AEWAintro_115-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AEWAintro-115"><span>[</span>115<span>]</span></a></sup> The North American legislation is similar, although there is a greater emphasis on protection.<sup id="cite_ref-law_116-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-law-116"><span>[</span>116<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
