<p>The <b>Brown-necked Raven</b> (<i>Corvus ruficollis</i>) is a larger bird (52–56&#160;cm in length) than the <a href="/wiki/Carrion_Crow" title="Carrion Crow">Carrion Crow</a> though not as large as the <a href="/wiki/Common_Raven" title="Common Raven">Common Raven</a>. It has similar proportions to the common raven but the bill is not so large or deep and the wings tend to be a little more pointed in profile. The head and throat are a distinct brownish-black giving the bird its <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> name, while the rest of the plumage is black glossed with purple, blue or purplish-blue. The feathers of this species often fade quite quickly to a brownish black (even the truly black feathers) and the bird can look distinctly brown by the time it moults. The feet, legs and bill are black. The <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_Raven" title="Dwarf Raven" class="mw-redirect">Dwarf Raven</a> was formerly considered a subspecies (<i>Corvus ruficollis edithae</i>) but this bird now appears to be closer to the <a href="/wiki/Pied_Crow" title="Pied Crow">Pied Crow</a> (<i>C. albus</i>) than this species. Hybrids between this species and the Pied Crow seem to reinforce their close relationship. This species is found principally in <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a> and neighbouring areas.</p>
<p>This species has a wide range across virtually the whole of <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, down as far as <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_peninsula" title="Arabian peninsula" class="mw-redirect">Arabian peninsula</a> and up into the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Middle_East" title="Greater Middle East">Greater Middle East</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. It lives in a predominantly <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> environment visiting oases and <a href="/wiki/Palm_tree" title="Palm tree" class="mw-redirect">palm</a> groves.</p>
<p>Food consists of a wide range of items, including carrion, snakes, locusts and other grasshoppers, stranded fish (in coastal areas), grain stolen from bags, dates and other fruits. It is quite fearless when not persecuted but is quick to become wary and shy if too much attention is paid to it. A 2009 Israeli study showed the species to cooperatively hunt lizards, with birds blocking exits while others hunted.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The nest is very much like the Common Raven's, and may be found in trees, on cliffs or in old and ruined buildings. The Dwarf Raven seems to prefer thorn trees for its nest building. There are usually 4-5 eggs laid and incubated over 20–22 days. The young usually leave the nest by the 37th or 38th day and can fly well by 42–45 days.</p>
<p>The voice is very similar to the Common Raven's consisting mainly of croaks, though higher in pitch; and a harsh <i>"karr-karr-karr"</i> very much like a Carrion Crow too. In flight, it will utter a <i>"kuerk-kuerk"</i> call.</p>
