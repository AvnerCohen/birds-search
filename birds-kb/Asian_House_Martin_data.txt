<p>The <b>Asian house martin</b> (<i>Delichon dasypus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Swallow" title="Swallow">swallow</a> family Hirundinidae. It has mainly blue-black upperparts, other than its white rump, and has pale grey underparts. Its three <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> breed in the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> and in central and eastern Asia, and spend the winter lower in the mountains or in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>. This species is locally abundant and is expanding northward in Siberia, so there are no concerns about its conservation status.
</p><p>This martin breeds in colonies, building mud nests under an overhang on a vertical cliff or the wall of a building. Both sexes build the <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a>, incubate the three or four white <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> and feed the chicks. The Asian house martin feeds on small <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> taken in flight, usually caught high in the air. The presence of terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Springtail" title="Springtail">springtails</a> and Lepidoptera <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> in its diet indicates that food is sometime picked from the ground.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asian_house_martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Asian house martin was first formally described from a bird collected in <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a> by French <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">naturalist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ornithology" title="Ornithology">ornithologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lucien_Bonaparte" title="Charles Lucien Bonaparte">Charles Lucien Bonaparte</a> in 1850 as <i>Chelidon dasypus</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Bonaparte_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bonaparte-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> shortly before it was moved to the new genus <i>Delichon</i> by British <a href="/wiki/Entomologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Entomologist">entomologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Moore" title="Frederic Moore">Frederic Moore</a> and American naturalist <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Horsfield" title="Thomas Horsfield">Thomas Horsfield</a> in 1854.<sup id="cite_ref-moore_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moore-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <i>Delichon</i> is an <a href="/wiki/Anagram" title="Anagram">anagram</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a> term <i>χελιδών</i> (<i>chelīdōn</i>), meaning "swallow",<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_bird_facts_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO_bird_facts-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and <i>dasypus</i> is from Greek <i>δασύπους</i> "rough-legged". This martin's closest relatives are the two other members of the genus <i>Delichon</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Nepal_house_martin" title="Nepal house martin">Nepal house martin</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Common_house_martin" title="Common house martin">common house martin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ITIS_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITIS-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
There are three subspecies:<sup id="cite_ref-turner_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>D. d. dasypus</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Subspecies#Nomenclature" title="Subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> described by Bonaparte, which breeds in eastern Russia and nearby islands</li>
<li><i>D. d. cashmeriensis</i>, the Himalayan and central Asian form described by English <a href="/wiki/Ornithology" title="Ornithology">ornithologist</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Gould" title="John Gould">John Gould</a> in 1858 from a <a href="/wiki/Kashmir" title="Kashmir">Kashmiri</a> specimen obtained by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Leith_Adams" title="Andrew Leith Adams">Andrew Leith Adams</a><sup id="cite_ref-gould_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gould-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>D. d. nigrimentalis</i>, the form which is found in the south east of the breeding range, was described by German ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Ernst Hartert</a> in 1910 from a specimen taken in <a href="/wiki/Fujian" title="Fujian">Fujian</a>, southeast China.<sup id="cite_ref-hartert_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hartert-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asian_house_martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The adult Asian house martin of the nominate subspecies is 12&#160;cm (4.7&#160;in) long, dark steel blue above with a contrasting white rump, grey-washed white underparts, and a slightly forked tail. The tail and upperwings are brownish-black, and the underwings are grey-brown. The legs and feet are brownish-pink and covered with white feathers, the eyes are brown, and the bill is black.<sup id="cite_ref-turner_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> There are few differences in appearance between the sexes, although the male is somewhat whiter below than the female, especially in fresh plumage. The juvenile bird is less glossy and has dark brown upperparts, sometimes with a brownish wash to the rump, and grey-white underparts.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>D. d. cashmiriensis</i> has brighter blue upperparts and a whiter rump than the slightly larger nominate race. The third, smallest, race is <i>D. d. nigrimentalis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-turner_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  All three subspecies can be distinguished from the similar Nepal house martin by the latter species' black chin, black undertail <a href="/wiki/Covert_(feather)" class="mw-redirect" title="Covert (feather)">coverts</a> and much squarer tail. The Asian house martin is more similar to the common house martin, but is darker underneath and has a less deeply forked tail.<sup id="cite_ref-turner_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Confusion is most likely between adult male Asian house martins, which have paler underparts, and the eastern race of common house martin, <i>D. urbicum lagopodum</i> which has a less forked tail than the western subspecies, although it still shows a more pronounced fork than Asian.<sup id="cite_ref-turner_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species’ song is a rippling metallic trill, and is a sibilant twitter, and call is a dry metallic cheep, often with two or three syllables. It is similar to that of common house martin, but more rasping.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asian_house_martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The nominate subspecies of the Asian house martin, <i>D. d. dasypus</i>, breeds in the southeast of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kuril_Islands" title="Kuril Islands">Kuril Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>. It <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrates</a> through eastern China to winter in the <a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">Malay Peninsula</a>, Borneo, the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a>; a few birds remain around hot springs in Japan. <i>D. d. cashmeriensis</i> breeds in the Himalayas from <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> east to <a href="/wiki/Sikkim" title="Sikkim">Sikkim</a> and northwards into <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a> and western and central China.<sup id="cite_ref-turner_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It is found between 1,500–5,000&#160;m (4,900–16,400&#160;ft) altitude, although mainly in the 2,400–4,000&#160;m (7,900–13,100&#160;ft) range.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This martin is a short-range migrant, mainly wintering at lower altitudes in the foothills of the Himalayas, but with some birds on the plains of North-eastern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and North-eastern and South-eastern <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, and smaller numbers further afield in <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>. The third race, <i>D. d. nigrimentalis</i>, breeds in southeastern China and southern Siberia. Its wintering grounds are unknown,<sup id="cite_ref-turner_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> but birds in Taiwan just move to lower altitudes in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-taiwan_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taiwan-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Non-breeding Asian house martin have been recorded as far west as the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-uea_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uea-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The range of <i>D. d. cashmeriensis</i> overlaps with that of the Nepal house martin, although they breed at somewhat different altitudes. The height separation and the small differences in appearance seem sufficient to prevent interbreeding.<sup id="cite_ref-Dickinson_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dickinson-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The preferred habitat of the Asian house martin is valleys and gorges in mountainous areas or coastal cliffs, where natural caves or crevices provide nest sites. It will also breed on large man-made sites like temples, hotels or power stations.<sup id="cite_ref-turner_7-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This martin tends to move to lower altitude open or hilly country in its wintering areas, although it has been recorded at up to 2,565&#160;m (8,415&#160;ft) in Thailand.<sup id="cite_ref-Robson_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robson-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asian_house_martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asian_house_martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The Asian house martin is a cliff nester, breeding in colonies sited under an overhang on a vertical cliff, usually with the nests not touching. It also frequently nests on large buildings such as temples and bridges, but not to the same extent as the common house martin. The nest is a deep mud cone lined with grasses or feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-turner_7-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Unlike its relatives, the Asian house martin frequently does not complete the enclosure of its nest, leaving it open instead like a deeper version of a barn swallow nest. A Russian study found half the nests in its <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Baikal</a> research area to be of the open type,<sup id="cite_ref-durnev_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-durnev-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and the Himalayan subspecies <i>D. d. cashmiriensis</i> has also been recorded as building a shallow cup nest.<sup id="cite_ref-oates_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oates-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-murray_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-murray-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The normal clutch is three or four (occasionally up to six) plain white eggs averaging 20.2&#160;mm ×&#160;14.1&#160;mm (0.80&#160;in ×&#160;0.56&#160;in) and weighing 2.1&#160;g (0.074&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The incubation and fledging times are unknown, but are probably similar to those of the common house martin, which has an incubation period of 14 to 16 days until the eggs hatch, and a further 22 to 32 days to fledging. Both sexes build the nest, incubate the eggs and feed the chicks.<sup id="cite_ref-turner_7-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asian_house_martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This martin feeds on insects taken in flight. As with its relatives it tends to feed high in the air, taking mostly small <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aphis" title="Aphis">aphid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">Hymenoptera</a> such as winged ants. A wide range of other insects are caught, including <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">Lepidoptera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neuroptera" title="Neuroptera">lacewings</a>. The presence in the diet of terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Springtail" title="Springtail">springtails</a> and Lepidoptera <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> indicate that food is sometime picked from the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-turner_7-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators_and_parasites">Predators and parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asian_house_martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Predators and parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Birds often carry parasites, both external lice and fleas, and internal blood parasites. The Asian house martin is a host of the house martin flea <i>Ceratophyllus hirundinis</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Miriam_Rothschild_1953_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miriam_Rothschild_1953-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and has recently been shown to carry signs of avian <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kim_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kim-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The predators of this martin appear to be little studied, but are presumably similar to those of the common house martin, namely fast flying falcons such as <a href="/wiki/Oriental_hobby" title="Oriental hobby">Oriental hobby</a> which can chase down their prey in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asian_house_martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Asian house martin has a large range that does not appear to be contracting, and its numbers appear to be stable, although the total population is unknown. Since the range is more than 20,000 square kilometres (7,700&#160;sq&#160;mi), and there are more 10,000 mature individuals, in the absence of any large decline in distribution or numbers the species does not appear to meet the criteria to be considered vulnerable, and is currently evaluated as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BirdLife_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLife-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> This species is locally abundant and appears to be expanding its range northwards in southern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-turner_7-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
