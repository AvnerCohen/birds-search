<p>The <b>Rock Martin</b> (<i>Ptyonoprogne fuligula</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Swallow" title="Swallow">swallow</a> family that is resident in Africa, and in southwestern Asia east to Pakistan. It breeds mainly in the mountains, but also at lower altitudes, especially in rocky areas and around towns, and, unlike most swallows, it is often found far from water. It is 12–15&#160;cm (4.7–5.9&#160;in) long, with mainly brown <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>, paler-toned on the upper breast and underwing <a href="/wiki/Covert_(feather)" title="Covert (feather)" class="mw-redirect">coverts</a>, and with white "windows" on the spread tail in flight. The sexes are similar in appearance, but juveniles have pale fringes to the upperparts and <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">flight feathers</a>. The northern <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are smaller, paler, and whiter-throated than southern African forms, and are sometimes split as a separate species, the "Pale Crag Martin". The Rock Martin hunts along cliff faces for flying insects using a slow flight with much gliding. Its call is a soft twitter.</p>
<p>This martin builds a deep bowl nest on a sheltered horizontal surface, or a neat quarter-sphere against a vertical rock face or wall. The nest is constructed with mud pellets and lined with grass or feathers, and may be built on natural sites under cliff overhangs or on man-made structures such as buildings, dam walls, culverts and bridges. It is often reused for subsequent broods or in later years. This species is a solitary breeder, and is not gregarious, but small groups may breed close together in suitable locations. The two or three eggs of a typical <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> are white with brown and grey blotches, and are incubated by both adults for 16–19 days prior to hatching. Both parents then feed the chicks. <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">Fledging</a> takes another 22–24 days, but the young birds will return to the nest to roost for a few days after the first flight.</p>
<p>This small martin is caught in flight by several fast, agile <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon</a> species, such as <a href="/wiki/Hobby_(bird)" title="Hobby (bird)">hobbies</a>, and it sometimes carries parasites, but it faces no major threats. Because of its range of nearly 10&#160;million&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (4&#160;million&#160;sq&#160;mi) and large, apparently stable, population, it is not seen as vulnerable and is assessed as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The Rock Martin was formally described in 1842 as <i>Hirundo fuligula</i> by German physician, explorer and zoologist <a href="/wiki/Martin_Lichtenstein" title="Martin Lichtenstein">Martin Lichtenstein</a><sup id="cite_ref-Lichtenstein_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lichtenstein-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> and was moved to the new genus <i>Ptyonoprogne</i> by German ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Gustav_Reichenbach" title="Heinrich Gustav Reichenbach">Heinrich Gustav Reichenbach</a> in 1850.<sup id="cite_ref-reichenbach_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reichenbach-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Its nearest relatives are the two other members of the genus, the <a href="/wiki/Dusky_Crag_Martin" title="Dusky Crag Martin">Dusky Crag Martin</a> <i>P. concolor</i> of southern Asia and the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Crag_Martin" title="Eurasian Crag Martin">Eurasian Crag Martin</a> <i>P. rupestris</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-turner158_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner158-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The genus name is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>ptuon</i> (φτυον), "a fan", referring to the shape of the opened tail, and Procne (Πρόκνη), a mythological girl who was turned into a swallow.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific name</a> <i>fuligula</i> means "sooty-throated", from Latin <i>fuligo</i> "soot" and <i>gula</i> "throat".<sup id="cite_ref-chambers_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chambers-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The three <i>Ptyonoprogne</i> species are members of the swallow family of birds, and are classed as members of the Hirundininae subfamily, which comprises all swallows and martins except the very distinctive <a href="/wiki/River_martin" title="River martin">river martins</a>. <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">DNA sequence</a> studies suggest that there are three major groupings within the Hirundininae, broadly correlating with the type of nest built.<sup id="cite_ref-sheldon_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sheldon-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The groups are the "core martins" including burrowing species like the <a href="/wiki/Sand_Martin" title="Sand Martin">Sand Martin</a>, the "nest-adopters", which are birds like the <a href="/wiki/Tree_Swallow" title="Tree Swallow">Tree Swallow</a> that utilise natural cavities, and the "mud nest builders". The <i><a href="/wiki/Ptyonoprogne" title="Ptyonoprogne" class="mw-redirect">Ptyonoprogne</a></i> species construct open mud nests and therefore belong to the last group. <i><a href="/wiki/Hirundo" title="Hirundo">Hirundo</a></i> species also build open nests, <i><a href="/wiki/Delichon" title="Delichon">Delichon</a></i> house martins have a closed nest, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Cecropis" title="Cecropis">Cecropis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Petrochelidon" title="Petrochelidon">Petrochelidon</a></i> swallows have <a href="/wiki/Retort" title="Retort">retort-like</a> closed nests with an entrance tunnel.<sup id="cite_ref-sheldon2_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sheldon2-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The genus <i>Ptyonoprogne</i> is closely related to the larger swallow genus <i>Hirundo</i>, but a DNA analysis showed that a coherent enlarged <i>Hirundo</i> genus should contain <i>all</i> the mud-builder genera. Although the nests of the <i>Ptyonoprogne</i> crag martins resembles those of typical <i>Hirundo</i> species like the <a href="/wiki/Barn_Swallow" title="Barn Swallow">Barn Swallow</a>, the DNA research showed that if the <i><a href="/wiki/Delichon" title="Delichon">Delichon</a></i> house martins are considered to be a separate genus, as is normally the case, <i><a href="/wiki/Cecropis" title="Cecropis">Cecropis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Petrochelidon" title="Petrochelidon">Petrochelidon</a></i> and <i>Ptyonoprogne</i> should also be split off.<sup id="cite_ref-sheldon_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sheldon-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In areas of Pakistan where its range overlaps with that of the Dusky Crag Martin, and in North Africa where it meets the Eurasian Crag Martin, the Rock Martin breeds at a different altitude from the other <i>Ptyonoprogne</i> species. This altitudinal separation means that it is not known whether these closely related martins could <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridise</a>. If they were shown to do so, it would cast doubts on their specific distinctness.<sup id="cite_ref-turner163_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner163-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span></h3>
<p>There are several subspecies differing in plumage shade or size, although the differences are <a href="/wiki/Cline_(biology)" title="Cline (biology)">clinal</a>, and races interbreed where their ranges meet. The small, pale subspecies (<i>obsoleta, peroplasta, perpallida, presaharica, spatzi, arabica</i> and <i>buchanani</i>) found in the mountains of <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Arabia" title="Arabia" class="mw-redirect">Arabian</a> peninsula and southwest Asia are sometimes split as a separate species, the Pale Crag Martin, <i>Ptyonoprogne obsoleta</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-turner160_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner160-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bergier_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bergier-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> following German <a href="/wiki/Ornithology" title="Ornithology">ornithologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Jean_Cabanis" title="Jean Cabanis">Jean Cabanis</a>, who first formally described these birds,<sup id="cite_ref-cabanis_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cabanis-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> but the changes in size and colour are continuous, and the forms often intergrade where they meet, so the evidence for separate species is not strong.<sup id="cite_ref-turner160_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner160-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The southern forms can weigh more than twice as much as the smallest northern subspecies. The average weight for <i>P.&#160;f.&#160;fusciventris</i> is 22.4&#160;g (0.79&#160;oz) against 10&#160;g (0.35&#160;oz) for <i>P.&#160;f.&#160;obsoleta</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dunning_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunning-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The robust, large-billed southernmost forms (<i>P. f. fuligula</i>, <i>P. f. pretoriae</i>, and <i>P. f. anderssoni</i>) are sufficiently different from dark, fine-billed <i>P. f. fusciventris</i> that the latter could also be regarded as a potentially different species.<sup id="cite_ref-irwin_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irwin-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> However, Rhodesian ornithologist Michael Irwin collected specimens from southern Zimbabwe (then Rhodesia) which were dark above like <i>P. f. fusciventris</i> and rich reddish below like <i>P. f. fuligula</i>. This led him to suggest that the two groups had previously been isolated, but were probably hybridising following secondary contact.<sup id="cite_ref-irwin_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irwin-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pale_Crag-martin.ogg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>The Rock Martin of the nominate subspecies <i>P. f. fuligula</i> is 12–15&#160;cm (4.7–5.9&#160;in)<sup id="cite_ref-SASOL_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SASOL-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> long with earth-brown upperparts and a short square tail that has small white patches near the tips of all but the central and outermost pairs of feathers. It has a cinnamon chin, throat, upper breast and underwing <a href="/wiki/Covert_(feather)" title="Covert (feather)" class="mw-redirect">coverts</a>, with the rest of the underparts being a similar brown to the upperparts. The eyes are brown, the small bill is mainly black, and the legs are brownish-pink. The sexes are similar in appearance, but juveniles have pale edges to the upperparts and flight feathers. The other subspecies differ from the nominate form as detailed above. Northern subspecies are generally smaller and have paler plumage than those in southern regions; they also have a white chin and throat, whereas southern forms have these areas <a href="/wiki/Rufous" title="Rufous">rufous</a> brown. Feather <a href="/wiki/Moulting" title="Moulting">moult</a> starts during the breeding season for the northern subspecies, but southern races can moult in any month, with only a minority doing so in the July to September nesting peak.<sup id="cite_ref-turner160_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner160-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Rock Martin's flight is slow, with rapid wing beats interspersed with flat-winged glides, and it is more acrobatic than the larger Eurasian Crag Martin.<sup id="cite_ref-Bergier_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bergier-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> It is a quiet bird; the song is a muffled twitter, and other calls include a <i>trrt</i> resembling the call of the <a href="/wiki/Common_House_Martin" title="Common House Martin">Common House Martin</a>, a nasal <i>vick</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-collins_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> and a high pitched <i>twee</i> contact call.<sup id="cite_ref-turner160_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner160-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Rock Martin is much drabber than most African swallows, and confusion is unlikely except with other crag martins or with sand martins of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Riparia" title="Riparia">Riparia</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-turner160_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner160-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The northern races are 15% smaller, paler and greyer than the Eurasian Crag Martin,<sup id="cite_ref-bwp_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> and have smaller tail spots.<sup id="cite_ref-barlow80_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barlow80-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Although only slightly larger than the <a href="/wiki/Sand_Martin" title="Sand Martin">Sand Martin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brown-throated_Sand_Martin" title="Brown-throated Sand Martin" class="mw-redirect">Brown-throated Sand Martin</a>, the Rock Martin is more robust, has white tail spots, and lacks a breast band.<sup id="cite_ref-bwp_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Even the dark southern races are still paler on the throat, breast and underwings than the all-dark form of the Brown-throated Sand Martin.<sup id="cite_ref-SASOL_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SASOL-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> In the far east of its range, the Rock Martin always has lighter, more contrasted underparts than the Dusky Crag Martin.<sup id="cite_ref-turner158_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner158-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>The Rock Martin breeds in suitable habitat throughout Africa and through the Middle East as far as Afghanistan and Pakistan. It is largely resident apart from local movements or a descent to lower altitudes after breeding. In addition, there is some short-range movement, including martins from southern Arabia crossing the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> and joining the local breeding birds,<sup id="cite_ref-turner160_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner160-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> and pale northern birds (<i>P. f. spatzi</i> and <i>P. f. presaharica</i>) wintering in Mali and Mauritania.<sup id="cite_ref-Barlow_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barlow-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> This species has been recorded as a <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrant</a> in Kuwait and Gabon, and its status in Congo is uncertain.<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwp_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The natural breeding habitat is hilly or mountainous country with cliffs, gorges and caves up to 3,700&#160;m (12,000&#160;ft) above sea level,<sup id="cite_ref-baker2_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker2-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> but this martin also breeds in lowlands, especially if rocks or buildings are available, and may be found far from water. This species readily uses man-made structures as a substitute for natural precipices,<sup id="cite_ref-turner160_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner160-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> and has bred on houses in southern Israel since the 1970s. In Egypt it may breed near monuments like <a href="/wiki/Abu_Simbel_temples" title="Abu Simbel temples">Abu Simbel</a> or in desert towns such as <a href="/wiki/Aswan" title="Aswan">Aswan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bwp_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> In Pakistan, the breeding range of the subspecies <i>P. f. peloplasta</i> overlaps with that of the Dusky Crag Martin, although that species breeds at much lower levels,<sup id="cite_ref-turner160_9-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner160-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> and in North Africa <i>P. f. obsoleta</i> occupies desert habitats whilst the Eurasian Crag Martin is found in the mountains.<sup id="cite_ref-Bergier_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bergier-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:City_of_Aswan_seen_from_the_air.jpg" class="image"><img alt=" Building next to lake with bare sand-coloured hills in the background" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/City_of_Aswan_seen_from_the_air.jpg/220px-City_of_Aswan_seen_from_the_air.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/City_of_Aswan_seen_from_the_air.jpg/330px-City_of_Aswan_seen_from_the_air.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/City_of_Aswan_seen_from_the_air.jpg/440px-City_of_Aswan_seen_from_the_air.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:City_of_Aswan_seen_from_the_air.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Desert towns like <a href="/wiki/Aswan" title="Aswan">Aswan</a> in Egypt provide man-made nest sites</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Rock Martin pairs often nest alone, although where suitable sites are available small loose <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonies</a> may form. These are more common south of the Sahara, where up to 40 pairs together have been recorded. These martins aggressively defend their nesting territory against <a href="/wiki/Conspecificity" title="Conspecificity">conspecifics</a> and other species. In Africa breeding dates vary geographically and with local weather conditions, and April to June is normal in Asia. Two broods are common, and three have been raised in a season. The nest, built by both adults over several weeks, is made from several hundred mud pellets and lined with soft dry grass or sometimes feathers. It may be a half-cup when constructed under an overhang on a vertical wall or cliff, or a shaped as a bowl like that of the Barn Swallow when placed on a sheltered ledge. The nest may be built on a rock cliff face, in a crevice or on a man-made structure, and is re-used for the second brood and in subsequent years.<sup id="cite_ref-turner160_9-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner160-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-baker2_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker2-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The clutch is usually two or three buff-white eggs blotched with sepia or grey-brown particularly at the wide end. The average egg size varies from 20.8&#160;x&#160;14.1&#160;mm (0.82&#160;x&#160;0.56&#160;in) with a weight of 2.17&#160;g (0.077&#160;oz) in South Africa to 19.3&#160;x&#160;12.9&#160;mm (0.76&#160;x&#160;0.51&#160;in) with a weight of 1.7&#160;g (0.06&#160;oz) in Asia. Both adults incubate the eggs for 16–19 days prior to hatching and feed the chicks about ten times an hour until they <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> and for several days after they can fly. The fledging time can vary from 22–24&#160;days to 25–30&#160;days, though the latter estimates probably take into account fledged young returning to the nest for food.<sup id="cite_ref-turner160_9-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner160-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<p>The Rock Martin feeds mainly on insects caught in flight, although it will occasionally feed on the ground. When breeding, birds often fly back and forth along a rock face catching insects in their bills and feed close to the nesting territory. At other times, they may hunt low over open ground. The insects caught depend on what is locally available, but may include <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">mosquitoes</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">Hymenoptera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>. This martin often feeds alone, but sizeable groups may gather at <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">grass fires</a> to feast on the fleeing insects, and outside the breeding season flocks of up to 300 may form where food is abundant.<sup id="cite_ref-turner160_9-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner160-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Cliff faces generate <a href="/wiki/Standing_wave" title="Standing wave">standing waves</a> in the airflow which concentrate insects near vertical areas. Crag martins exploit the area close to the cliff when they hunt, relying on their high manoeuvrability and ability to perform tight turns.<sup id="cite_ref-fantur_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fantur-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A study of nine bird species including four hirundinines showed that the more young there are in a nest, the more frequent are the parents' feeding visits, but the visits do not increase in proportion to the number of young. On average a solitary nestling therefore gets more food than a member of a pair or of a trio. Since the nestling period is not prolonged in proportion to the drop in feeding rate, an individual fledgling from a larger brood is likely to weigh less when it leaves the nest. However, a subspecies of the Rock Martin (<i>P. f. fusciventris</i>) was an anomaly in respect of both feeding rate and nestling time. There was no difference in parental feeding rate for members of a pair and members of a trio, but the nestling period averaged 1.5&#160;days longer for trios than pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-Moreau_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moreau-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Predators and parasites">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Predators_and_parasites">Predators and parasites</span></h2>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcons</a> have the speed and agility to catch swallows and martins in flight, and Rock Martins may be hunted by species such as the <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_Falcon" title="Peregrine Falcon">Peregrine Falcon</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-peregrine_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peregrine-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Taita_Falcon" title="Taita Falcon">Taita Falcon</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Dowsett_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dowsett-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/African_Hobby" title="African Hobby">African Hobby</a> and wintering <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Hobby" title="Eurasian Hobby">Eurasian Hobby</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Barlow165_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barlow165-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Rock Martins often share their nesting sites with <a href="/wiki/Little_Swift" title="Little Swift">Little Swifts</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Chantler_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chantler-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> which sometimes forcibly take over martin's nests.<sup id="cite_ref-Carr_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carr-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1975 one of the first findings of the tick <i>Argas (A.) africolumbae</i> was in a nest of <i>Ptyonoprogne fuligula fusciventris</i> in <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Hoogstraal_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoogstraal-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> at that time the martin was described under its <a href="/wiki/Synonym_(taxonomy)" title="Synonym (taxonomy)">synonym</a> <i>Ptyonoprogne fuligula rufigula</i> (<a href="/wiki/Gustav_Fischer" title="Gustav Fischer">Fischer</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Anton_Reichenow" title="Anton Reichenow">Reichenow</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Bergier_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bergier-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h2>
<p>The Rock Martin has a very large range of 9.5&#160;million&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (3.7&#160;million&#160;sq&#160;mi). The total population is unknown, but the bird is described as generally common, although scarce in Botswana and Namibia. The population is thought to be stable, mainly due to the absence of evidence of any declines or substantial threats. Its large range and presumably high numbers mean that the Rock Martin is not considered to be threatened, and it is classed as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_0-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
