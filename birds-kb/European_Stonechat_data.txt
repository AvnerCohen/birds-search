<p>The <b>European stonechat</b> (<i>Saxicola rubicola</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> that was formerly classed as a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Common_stonechat" title="Common stonechat">common stonechat</a>. Long considered a member of the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family, Turdidae, genetic evidence has placed it and its relatives in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a> family, Muscicapidae.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_stonechat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two weakly defined <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are currently accepted:<sup id="cite_ref-Urquhart_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>Saxicola rubicola rubicola</i>. In the south and east of its range, from <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> southwest to <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, east to <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, and southeast to <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>.</li>
<li><i>Saxicola rubicola hibernans</i>. Northwestern Europe in <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> coastal areas, in southwestern <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and northwestern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. Birds in coastal <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> are also often listed as this subspecies<sup id="cite_ref-Urquhart_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> but this is disputed.<sup id="cite_ref-Siddle_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Siddle-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The two subspecies differ in colour intensity following <a href="/wiki/Gloger%27s_rule" title="Gloger&#39;s rule">Gloger's rule</a>, with <i>S. r. rubicola</i> paler and with larger white patches in the drier European <a href="/wiki/Continental_climate" title="Continental climate">continental</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">mediterranean climates</a>, and <i>S. r. hibernans</i> darker brown with less white in the humid Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a>. They intergrade broadly where their ranges meet, from southeastern England<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> south through France and Spain, and many individuals are not identifiable to subspecies. Extreme examples of <i>S. r. rubicola</i> from the driest southern areas of its range such as the <a href="/wiki/Algarve" title="Algarve">Algarve</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> are particularly pale and with a large white rump, and can be very similar to <a href="/wiki/Siberian_stonechat" title="Siberian stonechat">Siberian stonechats</a> in appearance.<sup id="cite_ref-Siddle_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Siddle-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Corso_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Corso-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/NDNA" class="mw-redirect" title="NDNA">nDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Microsatellite_(genetics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Microsatellite (genetics)">microsatellite</a> <a href="/wiki/DNA_fingerprinting" class="mw-redirect" title="DNA fingerprinting">fingerprinting</a> reveals a very small degree of separation between the two subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-Wink_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wink-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the past, the European stonechat was generally considered <a href="/wiki/Conspecific" class="mw-redirect" title="Conspecific">conspecific</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Siberian_stonechat" title="Siberian stonechat">Siberian stonechat</a> and <a href="/wiki/African_stonechat" title="African stonechat">African stonechat</a>, lumped together as <a href="/wiki/Common_stonechat" title="Common stonechat">common stonechat</a> <i>S. torquatus</i>. A new review adding <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" class="mw-redirect" title="MtDNA">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome <i>b</i></a> <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" class="mw-redirect" title="DNA sequence">sequence</a> and nuclear DNA microsatellite fingerprinting evidence strongly supports their separation into distinct species.<sup id="cite_ref-Urquhart_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wink_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wink-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Due to a misunderstanding of the rules of Zoological nomenclature, for a short time the name <i>S. torquatus</i> was erroneously used for the European stonechat rather than the African stonechat.<sup id="cite_ref-Wink_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wink-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Together with the <a href="/wiki/Siberian_stonechat" title="Siberian stonechat">Siberian stonechat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands_stonechat" title="Canary Islands stonechat">Canary Islands stonechat</a> it constitutes eastern and western representatives of a <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasian</a> lineage; the Asian and European populations separated during the <a href="/wiki/Late_Pliocene" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Pliocene">Late Pliocene</a> or <a href="/wiki/Early_Pleistocene" title="Early Pleistocene">Early Pleistocene</a>, roughly 1.5-2.5 <a href="/wiki/Mya_(unit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mya (unit)">mya</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fuerteventura" title="Fuerteventura">Fuerteventura</a> was colonised by western European or northwest African birds somewhat later in the Early Pleistocene, about 1-2 mya.<sup id="cite_ref-Wink_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wink-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> of the English name derives from its call, sounding like two stones knocked together. The scientific name <i>Saxicola</i> means "rock-dweller", from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>saxum</i>, a rock + <i>incola</i>, dwelling in; and <i>rubicola</i>, "bramble-dweller", from Latin <i><a href="/wiki/Rubus" title="Rubus">Rubus</a></i>, brambles + <i>incola</i>;<sup id="cite_ref-job_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> the subspecies name <i>hibernans</i> refers to Ireland (Latin, <i>Hibernia</i>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_stonechat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The stonechat is 11.5–13&#160;cm long and weighs 13–17 g, slightly smaller than the <a href="/wiki/European_robin" title="European robin">European robin</a>. Both sexes have distinctively short wings, shorter than those of the more migratory <a href="/wiki/Whinchat" title="Whinchat">whinchat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siberian_stonechat" title="Siberian stonechat">Siberian stonechat</a>. The summer male has black upperparts, a black head, an orange throat and breast, and a white belly and vent. It also has a white half-collar on the sides of its neck, a small white scapular patch on the wings, and a very small white patch on the rump often streaked with black. The female has brown upperparts and head, and no white neck patches, rump or belly, these areas being streaked dark brown on paler brown, the only white being the scapular patch on the wings and even this often being buffy-white.<sup id="cite_ref-Urquhart_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_stonechat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>European stonechats breed in <a href="/wiki/Heath_(habitat)" class="mw-redirect" title="Heath (habitat)">heathland</a>, coastal <a href="/wiki/Dune" title="Dune">dunes</a> and rough <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a> with scattered small <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrubs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bramble" title="Bramble">bramble</a>, open <a href="/wiki/Gorse" class="mw-redirect" title="Gorse">gorse</a>, tussocks or <a href="/wiki/Calluna" title="Calluna">heather</a>. They are short-distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrants</a> or non-migratory, with part of the population (particularly from northeastern parts of the range, where winters are colder) moving south to winter further south in Europe and more widely in north Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Urquhart_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The male's song is high and twittering like a <a href="/wiki/Dunnock" title="Dunnock">dunnock</a>. Both sexes have a clicking call like stones knocking together.
</p><p>In 2015, <a href="/wiki/John_Callion" title="John Callion">John Callion</a> published the results of his 25-year study of the bird in <i><a href="/wiki/British_Birds_(magazine)" title="British Birds (magazine)">British Birds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BB_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></i>
</p>
