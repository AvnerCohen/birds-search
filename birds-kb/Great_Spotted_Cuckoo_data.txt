<p>The <b>great spotted cuckoo</b> (<i>Clamator glandarius</i>) is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Cuckoo" title="Cuckoo">cuckoo</a> order of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, the Cuculiformes, which also includes the <a href="/wiki/Geococcyx" class="mw-redirect" title="Geococcyx">roadrunners</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ani_(bird)" title="Ani (bird)">anis</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Coucal" title="Coucal">coucals</a>. The genus name <i>clamator</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "shouter" from <i>clamare</i>, "to shout". The specific <i>glandarius</i> is derived from Latin <i>glans, glandis</i>, "acorn".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is widely spread throughout <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">Mediterranean Basin</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasite</a> that lays its eggs in the nests of <a href="/wiki/Corvidae" title="Corvidae">corvids</a>, in particular the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_magpie" title="Eurasian magpie">Eurasian magpie</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chicks">Chicks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_spotted_cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Chicks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clamator_glandarius_MWNH_1216.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Clamator_glandarius_MWNH_1216.JPG/220px-Clamator_glandarius_MWNH_1216.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Clamator_glandarius_MWNH_1216.JPG/330px-Clamator_glandarius_MWNH_1216.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Clamator_glandarius_MWNH_1216.JPG/440px-Clamator_glandarius_MWNH_1216.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3646" data-file-height="2337" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clamator_glandarius_MWNH_1216.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>Unlike the <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common cuckoo</a>, neither the hen nor the hatched chick of this species evict the host's eggs, but the young magpies often die because they cannot compete successfully with the cuckoo for food. However it has been shown that this species' chicks secrete a repellent scent when predators threaten.  The repellent protects great spotted cuckoo chicks themselves as well as the host's chicks from predators.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Carrion_crow" title="Carrion crow">Carrion crow</a>  (<i>Corvus corone corone</i>) chicks survive better if a great spotted cuckoo chick shares their nest.  <a href="/wiki/Birds_of_prey" class="mw-redirect" title="Birds of prey">Birds of prey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feral_cat" title="Feral cat">feral cats</a> less frequently prey on crow's nests that include a great spotted cuckoo chick.  Crow chicks benefit only when predators are very active; when there is less predation, losing food to great spotted cuckoo chicks harms the crow chicks without compensation.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adults">Adults</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_spotted_cuckoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Adults">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species is slightly larger than the common cuckoo at 35–39&#160;cm (14–15&#160;in) in length, but looks much larger with its broad wings and long narrow tail.  The adult is grey above with a slender body, long tail and strong legs.  It has a grey cap, grey wings, a yellowish face and upper breast, and white underparts. Sexes are similar. The juveniles have blackish upperparts and cap, and chestnut primary wing feathers. This species has a magpie-like flight.
</p><p>It is a bird of warm open country with trees. It feeds on <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a> and hairy <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a>, which are distasteful to many birds.
</p><p>The great spotted cuckoo's call is a loud <i>cher-cher-kri-kri</i> and variations.
</p>
