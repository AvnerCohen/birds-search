<p>The <b>Great Spotted Cuckoo</b> (<i>Clamator glandarius</i>) is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Cuckoo" title="Cuckoo">cuckoo</a> order of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, the Cuculiformes, which also includes the <a href="/wiki/Geococcyx" title="Geococcyx">roadrunners</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ani_(bird)" title="Ani (bird)">anis</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Coucal" title="Coucal">coucals</a>.</p>
<p>It is a widespread summer <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a> to southeast and southwest <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, and winters in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasite</a>, which lays its eggs in the nests of <a href="/wiki/Corvidae" title="Corvidae">corvids</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Magpie" title="Magpie">magpies</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Sturnidae" title="Sturnidae" class="mw-redirect">starlings</a>.</p>
<p>This species is slightly larger than the <a href="/wiki/Common_Cuckoo" title="Common Cuckoo">Common Cuckoo</a> at 35–39&#160;cm length, but looks much larger with its broad wings and long narrow tail.</p>
<p>Unlike the Common Cuckoo, neither the hen nor the hatched chick of this species evict the host's eggs, but the young magpies often die because they cannot compete successfully with the cuckoo for food.</p>
<p>The adult is grey above with a slender body, long tail and strong legs. It has a grey cap, grey wings, a yellowish face and upper breast, and white underparts. Sexes are similar. The juveniles have blackish upperparts and cap, and chestnut primary wing feathers. This species has a magpie-like flight.</p>
<p>It is a bird of warm open country with trees. Its food is <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, with hairy <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a>, which are distasteful to many birds, being a speciality.</p>
<p>The Great Spotted Cuckoo's call is a loud <i>cher-cher-kri-kri</i> and variations.</p>
