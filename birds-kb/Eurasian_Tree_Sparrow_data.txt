<p>The <b>Eurasian Tree Sparrow</b> (<i>Passer montanus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird in the <a href="/wiki/Sparrow" title="Sparrow">sparrow</a> family with a rich chestnut <a href="/wiki/Crown_(anatomy)" title="Crown (anatomy)">crown</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nape" title="Nape">nape</a>, and a black patch on each pure white cheek. The sexes are similarly <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumaged</a>, and young birds are a duller version of the adult. This sparrow breeds over most of temperate <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, where it is known as the <b>Tree Sparrow</b>, and it has been introduced elsewhere including the United States, where it is known as the Eurasian Tree Sparrow or <b>German Sparrow</b> to differentiate it from the native unrelated <a href="/wiki/American_Tree_Sparrow" title="American Tree Sparrow">American Tree Sparrow</a>. Although several subspecies are recognised, the appearance of this bird varies little across its extensive range.</p>
<p>The Eurasian Tree Sparrow's untidy nest is built in a natural cavity, a hole in a building or the large nest of a <a href="/wiki/European_Magpie" title="European Magpie" class="mw-redirect">European Magpie</a> or <a href="/wiki/White_Stork" title="White Stork">White Stork</a>. The typical <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is five or six eggs which hatch in under two weeks. This sparrow feeds mainly on seeds, but invertebrates are also consumed, particularly during the breeding season. As with other small birds, infection by parasites and diseases, and predation by <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">birds of prey</a> take their toll, and the typical life span is about two years.</p>
<p>The Eurasian Tree Sparrow is widespread in the towns and cities of eastern Asia, but in Europe it is a bird of lightly wooded open countryside, with the <a href="/wiki/House_Sparrow" title="House Sparrow">House Sparrow</a> breeding in the more urban areas. The Eurasian Tree Sparrow's extensive range and large population ensure that it is not endangered globally, but there have been large declines in western European populations, in part due to changes in farming practices involving increased use of herbicides and loss of winter stubble fields. In eastern Asia and western Australia, this species is sometimes viewed as a pest, although it is also widely celebrated in oriental art.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Tree_Sparrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The Eurasian Tree Sparrow is 12.5–14&#160;cm (5–5½&#160;in) long,<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> with a <a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">wingspan</a> of about 21&#160;cm (8.25&#160;in) and a weight of 24&#160;g (0.86&#160;oz),<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> making it roughly 10% smaller than the House Sparrow.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The adult's crown and nape are rich <a href="/wiki/Chestnut_(color)" title="Chestnut (color)">chestnut</a>, and there is a kidney-shaped black ear patch on each pure white cheek; the chin, throat, and the area between the bill and throat are black. The upperparts are light brown, streaked with black, and the brown wings have two distinct narrow white bars. The legs are pale brown, and the bill is lead-blue in summer, becoming almost black in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This sparrow is distinctive even within its genus in that it has no plumage differences between the sexes; the juvenile also resembles the adult, although the colours tend to be duller.<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney2_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney2-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Its contrasting face pattern makes this species easily identifiable in all plumages;<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> the smaller size and brown, not grey, crown are additional differences from the male House Sparrow.<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Adult and juvenile Eurasian Tree Sparrows undergo a slow complete <a href="/wiki/Moult" title="Moult" class="mw-redirect">moult</a> in the autumn, and show an increase in body mass despite a reduction in stored fat. The change in mass is due to an increase in blood volume to support active feather growth, and a generally higher water content in the body.<sup id="cite_ref-lind2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lind2-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Eurasian Tree Sparrow has no true song, but its <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">vocalisations</a> include an excited series of <i>tschip</i> calls given by unpaired or courting males. Other monosyllabic chirps are used in social contacts, and the flight call is a harsh <i>teck</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> A study comparing the vocalisations of the introduced Missouri population with those of birds from Germany showed that the US birds had fewer shared syllable types (<a href="/wiki/Meme" title="Meme">memes</a>) and more structure within the population than the European sparrows. This may have resulted from the small size of the founding North American population and a consequent loss of <a href="/wiki/Genetic_diversity" title="Genetic diversity">genetic diversity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-lang_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lang-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Tree_Sparrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Linnaeuspage183.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Linnaeuspage183.jpg/220px-Linnaeuspage183.jpg" width="220" height="173" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Linnaeuspage183.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Description of the House and Eurasian Tree Sparrows from the <i>Systema naturae</i><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Old World <a href="/wiki/Sparrow" title="Sparrow">sparrow</a> genus <i><a href="/wiki/Passer" title="Passer">Passer</a></i> is a group of small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> birds that is believed to have originated in Africa, and which contains 15–25 species depending on the authority.<sup id="cite_ref-anderson_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anderson-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Its members are typically found in open, lightly wooded, habitats, although several species, notably the <a href="/wiki/House_Sparrow" title="House Sparrow">House Sparrow</a> (<i>P. domesticus</i>) have adapted to human habitations. Most species in the genus are typically 10–20&#160;cm (4–8&#160;in) long, predominantly brown or greyish birds with short square tails and stubby conical beaks. They are primarily ground-feeding seed-eaters, although they also consume invertebrates, especially when breeding.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement2_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement2-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Genetic studies show that the Eurasian Tree Sparrow diverged from the other Eurasian members of its genus relatively early, before the <a href="/wiki/Speciation" title="Speciation">speciation</a> of the House, <a href="/wiki/Pegu_Sparrow" title="Pegu Sparrow" class="mw-redirect">Pegu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Sparrow" title="Spanish Sparrow">Spanish Sparrows</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Allende_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allende-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The Eurasian species is not closely related to the <a href="/wiki/American_Tree_Sparrow" title="American Tree Sparrow">American Tree Sparrow</a> (<i>Spizella arborea</i>), which is an <a href="/wiki/American_sparrow" title="American sparrow">American sparrow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Byers_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Byers-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Eurasian Tree Sparrow's binomial name is derived from two <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> words: <i>passer</i>, "sparrow", and <i>montanus</i>, "of the mountains" (from <i>mons</i> "mountain").<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The Eurasian Tree Sparrow was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his 1758 <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> as <i>Fringilla montana</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-linn_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linn-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> but, along with the House Sparrow, it was soon moved from the <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finches</a> (family Fringillidae) into the new genus <i>Passer</i> created by French zoologist <a href="/wiki/Mathurin_Jacques_Brisson" title="Mathurin Jacques Brisson">Mathurin Jacques Brisson</a> in 1760.<sup id="cite_ref-brisson_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brisson-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The Eurasian Tree Sparrow's common name is given because of its preference of tree holes for nesting. This name, and the scientific name <i>montanus</i>, do not appropriately describe this species's habitat preferences: the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> name <i>Feldsperling</i> ("field sparrow") comes closer to doing so.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Tree_Sparrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tree-Sparrow-2009-16-02.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Tree-Sparrow-2009-16-02.jpg/220px-Tree-Sparrow-2009-16-02.jpg" width="220" height="161" class="thumbimage" /></a>

<p>This species varies little in appearance across its large range, and the differences between the eight extant <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> recognised by Clement are slight. At least 15 other subspecies have been proposed, but are considered to be intermediates of the listed <a href="/wiki/Race_(biology)" title="Race (biology)">races</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>P. m. montanus</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Subspecies#Nomenclature" title="Subspecies">nominate subspecies</a>, ranges across Europe except southwestern <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberia</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, and the former <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>. It also breeds in Asia east to the <a href="/wiki/Lena_River" title="Lena River">Lena River</a> and south to the northern regions of <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>.</li>
<li><i>P. m. transcaucasicus</i>, described by <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Aleksandrovich_Buturlin" title="Sergei Aleksandrovich Buturlin">Sergei Aleksandrovich Buturlin</a> in 1906, breeds from the southern Caucasus east to northern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. It is duller and greyer than the nominate race.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. m. dilutus</i>, described by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wallace_Richmond" title="Charles Wallace Richmond">Charles Wallace Richmond</a> in 1856, is resident in the extreme northeast of Iran, northern <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and northwest <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. It also occurs further north, from <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a> east to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Compared to <i>P. m. montanus</i>, it is paler, with sandy-brown upperparts.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. m. tibetanus</i>, the largest race by size, was described by <a href="/wiki/E._C._Stuart_Baker" title="E. C. Stuart Baker">Stuart Baker</a> in 1925. It is found in the northern <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a> east through <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a> to northwest China. It resembles <i>P. m. dilutus</i>, but is darker.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. m. saturatus</i>, described by <a href="/wiki/Leonhard_Hess_Stejneger" title="Leonhard Hess Stejneger">Leonhard Hess Stejneger</a> in 1885, breeds in <a href="/wiki/Sakhalin" title="Sakhalin">Sakhalin</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kuril_Islands" title="Kuril Islands">Kuril Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>. It is deeper brown than the nominate subspecies and has a larger bill.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. m. malaccensis</i>, described by <a href="/wiki/Alphonse_Joseph_Charles_Dubois" title="Alphonse Joseph Charles Dubois">Alphonse Dubois</a> in 1885, is found from the southern Himalayas east to <a href="/wiki/Hainan" title="Hainan">Hainan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>. It is a dark race, like <i>P. m. saturatus</i>, but is smaller and more heavily streaked on its upperparts.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. m. hepaticus</i>, described by <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Dillon_Ripley" title="Sidney Dillon Ripley">Sidney Dillon Ripley</a> in 1948, breeds from northeast <a href="/wiki/Assam" title="Assam">Assam</a> to northwest <a href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">Burma</a>. It is similar to <i>P. m. saturatus</i>, but redder on its head and upperparts.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Tree_Sparrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>The Eurasian Tree Sparrow's natural breeding range comprises most of <a href="/wiki/Temperateness" title="Temperateness">temperate</a> Europe and Asia south of about <a href="/wiki/68th_parallel_north" title="68th parallel north">latitude 68°N</a> (north of this the summers are too cold, with July average temperatures below 12°C) and through Southeast Asia to <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bali" title="Bali">Bali</a>. It formerly bred in the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gozo" title="Gozo">Gozo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Clement_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> In South Asia it is found mainly in the temperate zone.<sup id="cite_ref-raju_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-raju-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rasmussen_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rasmussen-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> It is sedentary over most of its extensive range, but northernmost breeding populations <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> south for the winter,<sup id="cite_ref-Arlott_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arlott-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> and small numbers leave southern Europe for North Africa and the Middle East.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The eastern subspecies <i>P. m. dilutus</i> reaches coastal Pakistan in winter and thousands of birds of this race move through eastern China in autumn.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Eurasian Tree Sparrow has been <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced</a> outside its native range, but has not always become established, possibly due to competition with the House Sparrow. It was introduced successfully to <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a>, eastern Indonesia, the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Micronesia" title="Micronesia">Micronesia</a>, but introductions to <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> did not take root. Ship-carried birds colonised <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a>. This sparrow has occurred as a natural <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrant</a> to <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In North America, a population of about 15,000 birds has become established around <a href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" title="St. Louis, Missouri" class="mw-redirect">St. Louis</a> and neighbouring parts of Illinois and southeastern Iowa.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> These sparrows are descended from 12 birds imported from Germany and released in late April 1870 as part of a project to enhance the native North American avifauna. Within its limited US range, the Eurasian Tree Sparrow has to compete with the House Sparrow in urban centres, and is therefore mainly found in parks, farms and rural woods.<sup id="cite_ref-lang_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lang-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-barlow_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barlow-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> The American population is sometimes referred to as the "German Sparrow", to distinguish it from both the native American Tree Sparrow species and the much more widespread "English" House Sparrow.<sup id="cite_ref-forbush_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbush-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

Urban nest under a roof tile of a wooden house in Japan</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In Australia, the Eurasian Tree Sparrow is present in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, towns in central and northern <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a> and some centres in the <a href="/wiki/Riverina" title="Riverina">Riverina</a> region of <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>. It is a prohibited species in <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>, where it often arrives on ships from Southeast Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-oz_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oz-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Despite its scientific name, <i>Passer montanus</i>, this is not typically a mountain species, and reaches only 700&#160;m (2,300&#160;ft) in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, although it has bred at 1,700&#160;m (5,600&#160;ft) in the northern Caucasus and as high as 4,270&#160;m (14,000&#160;ft) in Nepal.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Clement_4-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> In Europe, it is frequently found on coasts with cliffs, in empty buildings, in <a href="/wiki/Pollarding" title="Pollarding">pollarded</a> willows along slow water courses, or in open countryside with small isolated patches of woodland.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The Eurasian Tree Sparrow shows a strong preference for nest-sites near <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetland</a> habitats, and avoids breeding on intensively managed mixed farmland.<sup id="cite_ref-field_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-field-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>When the Eurasian Tree Sparrow and the larger House Sparrow occur in the same area, the House Sparrow generally breeds in urban areas while the smaller Eurasian Tree Sparrow nests in the countryside.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_4-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Where trees are in short supply, as in Mongolia, both species may utilise man-made structures as nest sites.<sup id="cite_ref-Melville_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Melville-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> The Eurasian Tree Sparrow is rural in Europe, but is an urban bird in eastern Asia; in southern and central Asia, both <i>Passer</i> species may be found around towns and villages.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_4-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> In parts of the Mediterranean, such as Italy, both the Tree and the Italian or Spanish sparrows may be found in settlements.<sup id="cite_ref-Summers-Smith_1988_220_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Summers-Smith_1988_220-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> In Australia, the Eurasian Tree Sparrow is largely an urban bird, and it is the House Sparrow which utilises more natural habitats.<sup id="cite_ref-oz_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oz-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Tree_Sparrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Tree_Sparrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>

</div>
<p>The Eurasian Tree Sparrow reaches breeding maturity within a year from hatching,<sup id="cite_ref-anage_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anage-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> and typically builds its nest in a cavity in an old tree or rock face. Some nests are not in holes as such, but are built among roots of overhanging <a href="/wiki/Gorse" title="Gorse">gorse</a> or similar bush.<sup id="cite_ref-Coward_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> Roof cavities in houses may be used,<sup id="cite_ref-Coward_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> and in the tropics, the crown of a palm tree or the ceiling of a verandah can serve as a nest site.<sup id="cite_ref-robinson_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-robinson-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> This species will breed in the disused domed nest of a <a href="/wiki/European_Magpie" title="European Magpie" class="mw-redirect">European Magpie</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Coward_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> or an active or unused stick nest of a large bird such as the <a href="/wiki/White_Stork" title="White Stork">White Stork</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-bochenski_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bochenski-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_Eagle" title="White-tailed Eagle">White-tailed Eagle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osprey" title="Osprey">Osprey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_Kite" title="Black Kite">Black Kite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Grey_Heron" title="Grey Heron">Grey Heron</a>. It will sometimes attempt to take over the nest of other birds that breed in holes or enclosed spaces, such as the <a href="/wiki/Barn_Swallow" title="Barn Swallow">Barn Swallow</a>, <a href="/wiki/House_Martin" title="House Martin" class="mw-redirect">House Martin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sand_Martin" title="Sand Martin">Sand Martin</a> or <a href="/wiki/European_Bee-eater" title="European Bee-eater">European Bee-eater</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Czechowski_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Czechowski-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Pairs may breed in isolation or in loose colonies,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> and will readily use <a href="/wiki/Nest_box" title="Nest box">nest boxes</a>. In a Spanish study, boxes made from a mixture of wood and concrete (woodcrete) had a much higher occupancy rate than wooden boxes (76.5% versus 33.5%), and birds nesting in woodcrete sites had earlier clutches, a shorter incubation period and more breeding attempts per season. Clutch size and chick condition did not differ between nest box types, but reproductive success was higher in woodcrete, perhaps because the synthetic nests were 1.5&#160;°C warmer than their wooden counterparts.<sup id="cite_ref-garcia_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-garcia-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The male calls from near the nest site in spring to proclaim ownership and attract a mate. He may also carry nest material into the nest hole.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_4-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The display and nest building is repeated in autumn. The preferred locations for the autumn display are old Eurasian Tree Sparrow nests, particularly those where nestlings had hatched. Empty nest boxes, and sites used by House Sparrows or other hole nesting birds, such as <a href="/wiki/Tit_(bird)" title="Tit (bird)">tits</a>, <a href="/wiki/European_Pied_Flycatcher" title="European Pied Flycatcher">Pied Flycatchers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Common_Redstart" title="Common Redstart">Common Redstarts</a>, are rarely used for the autumn display.<sup id="cite_ref-Pinowski_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pinowski-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Passer_montanus_050526.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Passer_montanus_050526.jpg/220px-Passer_montanus_050526.jpg" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Passer_montanus_050526.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A woodcrete <a href="/wiki/Nest_box" title="Nest box">nest box</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The untidy nest is composed of hay, grass, <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a> or other material and lined with <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Coward_28-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> which improve the thermal insulation.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> A complete nest consists of three layers; base, lining and dome.<sup id="cite_ref-Pinowski_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pinowski-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> The typical clutch is five or six <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> (rarely more than four in Malaysia),<sup id="cite_ref-robinson_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-robinson-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> white to pale grey and heavily marked with spots, small blotches, or speckling;<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell2_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell2-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> they are 20&#160;x&#160;14&#160;mm (0.8&#160;x&#160;0.6&#160;in) in size and weigh 2.1&#160;g (0.08&#160;oz), of which 7% is shell.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The eggs are incubated by both parents for 12–13 days before the <a href="/wiki/Altricial" title="Altricial">altricial</a>, naked chicks hatch, and a further 15–18 days elapse before they <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a>. Two or three broods may be raised each year;<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> birds breeding in colonies produce more eggs and fledglings from their first broods than solitary pairs, but the reverse is true for second and third clutches.<sup id="cite_ref-Sasvari_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sasvari-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> Females which copulate frequently tend to lay more eggs and have a shorter incubation time, so within-pair mating may be an indicator of the pairs' reproductive ability.<sup id="cite_ref-heeb_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heeb-38"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> There is a significant level of promiscuity; in a Hungarian study, more than 9% of chicks were sired by extra-pair males, and 20% of the broods contained at least one extra-pair young.<sup id="cite_ref-Seress_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seress-39"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">Hybridisation</a> between the Eurasian Tree Sparrow and the House Sparrow has been recorded in many parts of the world with male hybrids tending to resemble the Eurasian Tree Sparrow while females have more similarities with the House Sparrow.<sup id="cite_ref-Cordero_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cordero-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> A breeding population in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Ghats" title="Eastern Ghats">Eastern Ghats</a> of India,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> said to be introduced,<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_4-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> may also hybridise with House Sparrows.<sup id="cite_ref-Rasmussen_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rasmussen-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> On at least one occasion a mixed pair has resulted in fertile young.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> A wild hybridisation with the resident sparrows of Malta, which are intermediate between the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Sparrow" title="Spanish Sparrow">Spanish Sparrow</a> (<i>P. hispaniolensis</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Italian_Sparrow" title="Italian Sparrow">Italian Sparrows</a> (<i>P. italiae</i>), was recorded in Malta in 1975.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_4-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Tree_Sparrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Passer_montanus_bl.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Passer_montanus_bl.jpg/220px-Passer_montanus_bl.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Passer_montanus_bl.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
At a feeder</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Tree Sparrow is a predominantly seed and grain eating bird which feeds on the ground in flocks, often with House Sparrows, finches, or buntings. It eats weed seeds, such as <a href="/wiki/Caryophyllaceae" title="Caryophyllaceae">chickweeds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chenopodium" title="Chenopodium">goosefoot</a>, spilled grain,<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_4-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> and it may also visit <a href="/wiki/Bird_feeder" title="Bird feeder">feeding stations</a>, especially for <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanuts</a>. It will also feed on <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>, especially during the breeding season when the young are fed mainly on animal food; it takes insects, <a href="/wiki/Woodlouse" title="Woodlouse">woodlice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Millipede" title="Millipede">millipedes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Centipede" title="Centipede">centipedes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Opiliones" title="Opiliones">harvestmen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-naturalengland_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-naturalengland-45"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Adults use a variety of wetlands when foraging for invertebrate prey to feed nestlings, and aquatic sites play a key role in providing adequate diversity and availability of suitable invertebrate prey to allow successful chick rearing throughout the long breeding season of this multi-brooded species. Large areas of formerly occupied farmland no longer provide these invertebrate resources due to the effects of <a href="/wiki/Intensive_farming" title="Intensive farming">intensive farming</a>, and the availability of supplementary seed food within 1 kilometre (0.62&#160;mi) of the nest-site does not influence nest-site choice, or affect the number of young raised.<sup id="cite_ref-field_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-field-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In winter, seed resources are most likely to be a key limiting factor.<sup id="cite_ref-field_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-field-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> At this time of year, individuals in a flock form linear <a href="/wiki/Dominance_hierarchy" title="Dominance hierarchy">dominance hierarchies</a>, but there is no strong relation between the size of the throat patch and position in that hierarchy. This is in contrast to the House Sparrow; in that species, fights to establish dominance are reduced by the display of the throat patch, the size of which acts as a <a href="/wiki/Signalling_theory" title="Signalling theory">signalling</a> "badge" of <a href="/wiki/Fitness_(biology)" title="Fitness (biology)">fitness</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The risk of predation affects feeding strategies. A study showed increased distance between shelter and a food supply meant that birds visited a feeder in smaller flocks, spent less time on it and were more vigilant when far from shelter. Sparrows can feed as "producers", searching for food directly, or "scroungers", just joining other flock members who have already discovered food. Scrounging was 30% more likely at exposed feeding sites, although this is not due to increased anti-predator vigilance. A possible explanation is that riskier places are used by individuals with lower fat reserves.<sup id="cite_ref-barta_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barta-47"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Tree_Sparrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Survival">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Survival">Survival</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sparrowhawk-Male.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Sparrowhawk-Male.JPG/220px-Sparrowhawk-Male.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sparrowhawk-Male.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
The Sparrowhawk is a widespread predator</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Predators of the Tree Sparrow include a variety of <a href="/wiki/Accipiter" title="Accipiter">accipiters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owls</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian Sparrowhawk">Eurasian Sparrowhawk</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-naturalengland_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-naturalengland-45"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Common_Kestrel" title="Common Kestrel">Common Kestrel</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Little_Owl" title="Little Owl">Little Owl</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Long-eared_Owl" title="Long-eared Owl">Long-eared Owl</a> and White Stork.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> It does not appear to be at an increased risk of predation during its autumn moult, despite having fewer flight feathers at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-lind_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lind-53"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> Nests may be raided by Eurasian Magpies, <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Jay" title="Eurasian Jay">Jays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Least_Weasel" title="Least Weasel" class="mw-redirect">Least Weasels</a>, rats, cats and constricting snakes such as the <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe_Whip_Snake" title="Horseshoe Whip Snake">Horseshoe Whip Snake</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-veiga_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-veiga-54"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CorderoSalaet_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CorderoSalaet-55"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cordero2_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cordero2-56"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Many species of <a href="/wiki/Bird_louse" title="Bird louse">birdlice</a> are present on the birds and in their nests,<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> and mites of the genus <i>Knemidocoptes</i> have been known to infest populations, resulting in lesions on the legs and toes.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> Parasitisation of nestlings by <i><a href="/wiki/Protocalliphora" title="Protocalliphora">Protocalliphora</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Blow-fly" title="Blow-fly" class="mw-redirect">blow-fly</a> larvae is a significant factor in nestling mortality.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> Egg size does not influence nestling mortality, but chicks from large eggs grow faster.<sup id="cite_ref-Pinowska_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pinowska-61"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Tree Sparrows are also subject to bacterial and viral infections. Bacteria have been shown to be an important factor in the failure of eggs to hatch and in nestling mortality,<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> and mass deaths due to <i><a href="/wiki/Salmonella" title="Salmonella">Salmonella</a></i> infection have been noted in Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Avian_malaria" title="Avian malaria">Avian malaria</a> parasites have been found in the blood of many populations,<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> and birds in China were found to harbour a strain of <a href="/wiki/Influenza_A_virus_subtype_H5N1" title="Influenza A virus subtype H5N1">H5N1</a> that was highly virulent to chickens.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The immune response of Tree Sparrows is less robust than that of the House Sparrow and has been proposed as a factor in the greater invasive potential of the latter.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> The House Sparrow and Tree Sparrow are the most frequent victims of <a href="/wiki/Roadkill" title="Roadkill">roadkill</a> on the roads of Central, Eastern and Southern Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> The maximum recorded age is 13.1 years,<sup id="cite_ref-anage_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anage-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> but three years is a typical lifespan.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Tree_Sparrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stubble_below_Tinto.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Stubble_below_Tinto.jpg/220px-Stubble_below_Tinto.jpg" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stubble_below_Tinto.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Winter stubble is a seasonal food resource<sup id="cite_ref-europea_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-europea-68"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Tree Sparrow has a large, currently unquantified range; its world population is also unknown, but includes an estimated 52–96 million individuals in Europe. Although population trends have not been evaluated, the species is not believed to approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> (that is, declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations). For these reasons, the species' <a href="/wiki/Conservation_status" title="Conservation status">conservation status</a> is evaluated at the global level as "<a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Although the Tree Sparrow has been expanding its range in <a href="/wiki/Fennoscandia" title="Fennoscandia">Fennoscandia</a> and eastern Europe, populations have been declining in much of western Europe,<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-birdseurope_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdseurope-69"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> a trend reflected in other farmland birds such as the <a href="/wiki/Skylark" title="Skylark" class="mw-redirect">Skylark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corn_Bunting" title="Corn Bunting">Corn Bunting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Lapwing" title="Northern Lapwing">Northern Lapwing</a>. From 1980 to 2003, common farmland bird numbers fell by 28%.<sup id="cite_ref-europea_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-europea-68"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> The collapse in populations seems to have been particularly severe in Great Britain, where there was a 95% decline between 1970 and 1998,<sup id="cite_ref-rspb_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb-70"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> and Ireland, which had only 1,000–1,500 pairs in the late 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> In the British Isles, such declines may be due to natural fluctuations, to which Tree Sparrows are known to be prone.<sup id="cite_ref-Summers-Smith_1988_220_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Summers-Smith_1988_220-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Breeding performance has improved substantially as population sizes have decreased, suggesting that decreases in productivity were not responsible for the decline and that survival was the critical factor.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO2_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO2-72"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> The large decline in Tree Sparrow numbers is probably the result of agricultural intensification and specialisation, particularly the increased use of herbicides and a trend towards autumn-sown crops (at the expense of spring-sown crops that produce stubble fields in winter). The change from mixed to specialised farming and the increased use of insecticides has reduced the amount of insect food available for nestlings.<sup id="cite_ref-europea_68-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-europea-68"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Tree_Sparrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Relationships with humans">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships_with_humans">Relationships with humans</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Crioceris_asparagi.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Crioceris_asparagi.jpg/220px-Crioceris_asparagi.jpg" width="220" height="132" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Crioceris_asparagi.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A horticultural pest, the <a href="/wiki/Common_asparagus_beetle" title="Common asparagus beetle">common asparagus beetle</a> is a prey item</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Tree Sparrow is seen as a pest in some areas. In Australia, it damages many cereal and fruit crops and spoils cereal crops, animal feed and stored grain with its droppings. <a href="/wiki/Quarantine" title="Quarantine">Quarantine</a> rules prohibit the transport of this species into Western Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-oz_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oz-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Chairman <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a> of China attempted in April 1958 to reduce crop damage by Tree Sparrows, estimated at 4.5&#160;kg (9.9&#160;lb) of grain per bird each year, by mobilising three million people and many scarecrows to drive the birds to death by exhaustion. Although initially successful, the "<a href="/wiki/Great_sparrow_campaign" title="Great sparrow campaign" class="mw-redirect">great sparrow campaign</a>" had overlooked the numbers of locusts and other insect pests consumed by the birds, and crop yields fell, exacerbating a famine which led to the deaths of 30&#160;million people between 1959 and 1961.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-independent_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent-73"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup> The Tree Sparrow's consumption of insects has led to its use in agriculture to control fruit tree pests and the <a href="/wiki/Common_asparagus_beetle" title="Common asparagus beetle">common asparagus beetle</a>, <i>Crioceris aspergi</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-dix_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dix-74"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The Tree Sparrow has long been depicted in Chinese and Japanese art, often on a plant spray or in a flying flock,<sup id="cite_ref-independent_73-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent-73"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup> and representations by oriental artists including <a href="/wiki/Hiroshige" title="Hiroshige">Hiroshige</a> have featured on the postage stamps of <a href="/wiki/Antigua_and_Barbuda" title="Antigua and Barbuda">Antigua and Barbuda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Republic" title="Central African Republic">Central African Republic</a>, China and <a href="/wiki/The_Gambia" title="The Gambia">The Gambia</a>. More straightforward illustrations were used on the stamps of <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup> The fluttering of the bird gave rise to a traditional Japanese dance, the <i>Suzume Odori</i>, which was depicted by artists such as <a href="/wiki/Hokusai" title="Hokusai">Hokusai</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-japan_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-japan-76"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, where it is known as <i>maya</i>, the Tree Sparrow is the most common bird in the cities. Many urban Filipinos confuse it with the former national bird of the Philippines (the <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Eagle" title="Philippine Eagle">Philippine Eagle</a> since 1995), the <a href="/wiki/Black-headed_Munia" title="Black-headed Munia" class="mw-redirect">Black-headed Munia</a>, also called <i>maya</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Inquirer_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Inquirer-77"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
