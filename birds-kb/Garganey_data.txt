<p>The <b>Garganey</b> (<i>Anas querquedula</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Dabbling_duck" title="Dabbling duck" class="mw-redirect">dabbling duck</a>. It breeds in much of Europe and western Asia, but is strictly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, with the entire population moving to southern Africa, India (in particular) <a href="/wiki/Santragachi" title="Santragachi" class="mw-redirect">Santragachi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a> in winter,<sup id="cite_ref-Clements_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clements-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> where large flocks can occur. This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in 1758 under its current scientific name.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Like other small <a href="/wiki/Ducks" title="Ducks" class="mw-redirect">ducks</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Common_Teal" title="Common Teal" class="mw-redirect">Common Teal</a>, this species rises easily from the water with a fast twisting <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a>-like flight.</p>
<p>Their breeding habitat is grassland adjacent to shallow <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a> lakes.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Garganey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The adult male is unmistakable, with its brown head and breast with a broad white crescent over the eye. The rest of the plumage is grey, with loose grey scapular feathers It has a grey bill and legs. In flight it shows a pale blue <a href="/wiki/Speculum_feathers" title="Speculum feathers">speculum</a> with a white border. When swimming it will show prominent white edges on its <a href="/wiki/Tertials" title="Tertials" class="mw-redirect">tertials</a>. His <a href="/wiki/Crown_(anatomy)" title="Crown (anatomy)">crown (anatomy)</a> is dark and face is reddish-brown.<sup id="cite_ref-Dunn_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunn-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Garganey_(Anas_querquedula)_in_AP_W_IMG_2844.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Garganey_%28Anas_querquedula%29_in_AP_W_IMG_2844.jpg/250px-Garganey_%28Anas_querquedula%29_in_AP_W_IMG_2844.jpg" width="250" height="178" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Garganey_(Anas_querquedula)_in_AP_W_IMG_2844.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Females</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Some care is needed in separating the brown female from the similar <a href="/wiki/Common_Teal" title="Common Teal" class="mw-redirect">Common Teal</a>, but the stronger face markings and more frequent head-shaking when dabbling are good indicators. Confusion with the female of the <a href="/wiki/Blue-winged_Teal" title="Blue-winged Teal">Blue-winged Teal</a> is also possible, but the head and bill shape is different, and the latter species has yellow legs. Pale eyebrow, dark eye line, pale <a href="/wiki/Lore_(anatomy)" title="Lore (anatomy)">lore</a> spot bordered by a second dark line.<sup id="cite_ref-Dunn_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunn-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>These birds feed mainly by skimming rather than upending.</p>
<p>The male has a distinctive crackling mating call; the female is rather silent for a female duck, but can manage a feeble <i>quack</i>.</p>
<p>Garganey are rare breeding birds in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, with most breeding in quiet marshes in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> a few pairs now breed annually in <a href="/wiki/Wexford" title="Wexford">Wexford</a>.</p>
<p>The Garganey is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies. The status of the Garganey on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> is <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Garganey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The common English name dates from the 17th century and comes from dialect <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> <i>gargenei</i>, a variant of <i>garganello</i>, which ultimately comes from the <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">Late Latin</a> <i>gargala</i> "tracheal artery".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The English usage owes its origins to Conrad <a href="/wiki/Gesner" title="Gesner">Gesner</a> who used the Italian name in the third volume of his <i><a href="/wiki/Historiae_animalium_(Gesner)" title="Historiae animalium (Gesner)">Historiae Animalium</a></i> (History of Animals) of 1555.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
