<p>The <b>garganey</b> (<i>Spatula querquedula</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Dabbling_duck" class="mw-redirect" title="Dabbling duck">dabbling duck</a>. It breeds in much of Europe and across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>, but is strictly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, with the entire population moving to southern Africa, India (in particular <a href="/wiki/Santragachi" class="mw-redirect" title="Santragachi">Santragachi</a>), Bangladesh (in the natural reservoirs of <a href="/wiki/Sylhet" title="Sylhet">Sylhet</a> district) and <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a> in winter,<sup id="cite_ref-Clements_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clements-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> where large flocks can occur. This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his landmark 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a>. Like other small <a href="/wiki/Ducks" class="mw-redirect" title="Ducks">ducks</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Common_teal" class="mw-redirect" title="Common teal">common teal</a>, this species rises easily from the water with a fast twisting <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a>-like flight.
</p><p>Their breeding habitat is grassland adjacent to shallow <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a> lakes.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garganey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">formal description</a> of the garganey was by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">tenth edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>. He introduced the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Anas querquedula</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogentic" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular phylogentic">molecular phylogentic</a> study comparing <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> sequences published in 2009 found that the genus <i>Anas</i>, as then defined, was <a href="/wiki/Monophyletic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monophyletic">non-monophyletic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The genus was subsequently split into four monophyletic genera with ten species including the garganey moved into the resurrected genus <i><a href="/wiki/Spatula_(genus)" title="Spatula (genus)">Spatula</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This genus had been originally proposed by the German zoologist <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Boie" title="Friedrich Boie">Friedrich Boie</a> in 1822.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The name <i>Spatula</i> is the Latin for a "spoon" or "spatula". The specific epithet is derived from Latin <i>querquedula</i>, a word believed to represent to its call.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common English name dates from the 17th century and comes from <a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">Lombard language</a> <i>gargenei</i>, the plural of <i>garganell</i>, which ultimately comes from the <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">Late Latin</a> <i>gargala</i> "tracheal artery".<sup id="cite_ref-AHD_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AHD-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The English usage owes its origins to <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Gesner" class="mw-redirect" title="Conrad Gesner">Conrad Gesner</a> who used the Italian name in the third volume of his <i><a href="/wiki/Historiae_animalium_(Gesner)" class="mw-redirect" title="Historiae animalium (Gesner)">Historiae Animalium</a></i> (History of Animals) of 1555.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garganey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Garganey_(Anas_querquedula)_in_AP_W_IMG_2844.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Garganey_%28Anas_querquedula%29_in_AP_W_IMG_2844.jpg/220px-Garganey_%28Anas_querquedula%29_in_AP_W_IMG_2844.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Garganey_%28Anas_querquedula%29_in_AP_W_IMG_2844.jpg/330px-Garganey_%28Anas_querquedula%29_in_AP_W_IMG_2844.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Garganey_%28Anas_querquedula%29_in_AP_W_IMG_2844.jpg/440px-Garganey_%28Anas_querquedula%29_in_AP_W_IMG_2844.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="568" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Garganey_(Anas_querquedula)_in_AP_W_IMG_2844.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Females</div></div></div>

<p>The adult male is unmistakable, with its brown head and breast with a broad white crescent over the eye. The rest of the plumage is grey, with loose grey scapular feathers It has a grey bill and legs. In flight it shows a pale blue <a href="/wiki/Speculum_feathers" title="Speculum feathers">speculum</a> with a white border. When swimming it will show prominent white edges on its <a href="/wiki/Tertials" class="mw-redirect" title="Tertials">tertials</a>. His <a href="/wiki/Crown_(anatomy)" title="Crown (anatomy)">crown (anatomy)</a> is dark and face is reddish brown.<sup id="cite_ref-Dunn_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunn-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some care is needed in separating the brown female from the similar <a href="/wiki/Common_teal" class="mw-redirect" title="Common teal">common teal</a>, but the stronger face markings and more frequent head-shaking when dabbling are good indicators. Confusion with the female of the <a href="/wiki/Blue-winged_teal" title="Blue-winged teal">blue-winged teal</a> is also possible, but the head and bill shape is different, and the latter species has yellow legs. Pale eyebrow, dark eye line, pale <a href="/wiki/Lore_(anatomy)" title="Lore (anatomy)">lore</a> spot bordered by a second dark line.<sup id="cite_ref-Dunn_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunn-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Measurements</b><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Size</b>: 41 cm</li>
<li><b>Wingspan</b>: 58 - 69 cm.</li>
<li><b>Weight</b>: 300- 440 g</li></ul>
<p>These birds feed mainly by skimming rather than upending.
</p><p>The male has a distinctive crackling <a href="/wiki/Mating_call" title="Mating call">mating call</a>; the female is rather silent for a female duck, but can manage a feeble <i>quack</i>.
</p><p>Garganey are rare breeding birds in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, with most breeding in quiet marshes in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> a few pairs  breed in <a href="/wiki/Wexford" title="Wexford">Wexford</a>, with occasional breeding elsewhere.
</p><p>The garganey is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies. The status of the garganey on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> is <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
