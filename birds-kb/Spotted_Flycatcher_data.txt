<p>The <b>spotted flycatcher</b> (<i>Muscicapa striata</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a> family.  It breeds in most of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> to Siberia, and is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and south western Asia. It is declining in parts of its range.
</p><p>This is an undistinguished looking bird with long wings and tail. The adults have grey-brown upperparts and whitish underparts, with a streaked crown and breast, giving rise to the bird's common name.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The legs are short and black, and the bill is black and has the broad but pointed shape typical of aerial insectivores. Juveniles are browner than adults and have spots on the upperparts.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spotted_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The spotted flycatcher was described by the German naturalist <a href="/wiki/Peter_Simon_Pallas" title="Peter Simon Pallas">Peter Simon Pallas</a> in 1764 and given the binomial name <i>Motacilla striata</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The genus name <i>Muscicapa</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>musca</i>, a fly and <i>capere</i>, to catch. The <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific epithet</a> <i>striata</i> is from the Latin <i>striatus</i> meaning striated.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are five recognised <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> all of which winter in southern Africa. The breeding range is given below.<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbwalive_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwalive-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>M. s. striata</i> (<a href="/wiki/Peter_Simon_Pallas" title="Peter Simon Pallas">Pallas</a>, 1764) – Europe to west Siberia, northwest Africa</li>
<li><i>M. s. inexpectata</i> <a href="/wiki/Georgi_Petrovich_Dementiev" title="Georgi Petrovich Dementiev">Dementiev</a>, 1932 – Crimea  (southern Ukraine)</li>
<li><i>M. s. neumanni</i> Poche, 1904 – islands of the Aegean Sea through to the Middle East, the Caucasus, northern Iran and central Siberia</li>
<li><i>M. s. sarudnyi</i> <a href="/wiki/Sergey_Snigirewski" title="Sergey Snigirewski">Snigirewski</a>, 1928 – eastern Iran and Turkmenistan to the mountains of central Asia and north Pakistan</li>
<li><i>M. s. mongola</i> Portenko, 1955 – Mongolia and south-central Siberia</li></ul>
<p>Two other subspecies were previously recognised, <i>M. s. tyrrhenica</i> and <i>M. s. balearica</i>. However, a <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular phylogenetic">molecular phylogenetic</a> study published in 2016 found that they were genetically similar to each other but significantly different from the other spotted flycatcher subspecies. The authors proposed that these insular subspecies should be considered as a separate species.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_Ornithologists%27_Union" title="International Ornithologists&#39; Union">International Ornithologists' Union</a> has split the species and it is known as the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_flycatcher" title="Mediterranean flycatcher">Mediterranean flycatcher</a>, while other taxonomic authorities still consider it to be conspecific.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spotted_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The spotted flycatcher is a small slim bird, around 14.5&#160;cm (5.7&#160;in) in length, with a weight of 14–20&#160;g (0.49–0.71&#160;oz). It has dull grey-brown upperparts and off-white underparts. The crown, throat and breast are streaked with brown while the wings and tail feathers are edged with paler thin margins.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The subspecies <i>M. s. tyrrhenica</i> has paler and warmer plumage on the upperparts, with more diffuse markings on the head and breast.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The sexes are alike. Juveniles have ochre-buff spots above and scaly brown spots below.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwalive_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwalive-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spotted_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Spotted flycatchers hunt from conspicuous perches, making <a href="/wiki/Hawking_(birds)" title="Hawking (birds)">sallies</a> after passing flying insects, and often returning to the same perch. Their upright posture is characteristic.
</p><p>Most passerines <a href="/wiki/Moult" class="mw-redirect" title="Moult">moult</a> their <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Primaries" title="Flight feather">primary flight feathers</a> in sequence beginning near the body and proceeding outwards along the wing. The spotted flycatcher is unusual in replacing the outer flight feathers before those nearer the body.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The flycatcher's <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">call</a> is a thin, drawn out soft and high pitched <i>tssssseeeeeppppp</i>, slightly descending in pitch.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spotted_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>They are birds of deciduous woodlands, parks and gardens, with a preference for open areas amongst trees. They build an open nest in a suitable recess, often against a wall, and will readily adapt to an open-fronted nest box. 4-6 eggs are laid.
</p><p>Most European birds cannot discriminate between their own eggs and those of other species. The exception to this are the hosts of the <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common cuckoo</a>, which have had to evolve this skill as a protection against that nest parasite. The spotted flycatcher shows excellent egg recognition, and it is likely that it was once a host of the cuckoo, but became so good at recognising the intruder's eggs that it ceased to be victimised. A contrast to this is the <a href="/wiki/Dunnock" title="Dunnock">dunnock</a>, which appears to be a recent cuckoo host, since it does not show any egg discrimination.<sup id="cite_ref-Davies_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predation">Predation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spotted_flycatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Predation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A study conducted at two different locations in southern England found that one third of nests were predated. The <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_jay" title="Eurasian jay">Eurasian jay</a> (<i>Garrulus glandarius</i>) was the most common aerial predator, consuming both eggs and chicks. The <a href="/wiki/Domestic_cat" class="mw-redirect" title="Domestic cat">domestic cat</a> (<i>Felis catus</i>) predated a small fraction of the nests.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
