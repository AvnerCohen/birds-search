<p>The <b>Common Crane</b> (<i>Grus grus</i>), also known as the <b>Eurasian Crane</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of the family <b>Gruidae</b>, the <a href="/wiki/Crane_(bird)" title="Crane (bird)">cranes</a>. A medium-sized species, it is the only crane commonly found in Europe besides the <a href="/wiki/Demoiselle_Crane" title="Demoiselle Crane">Demoiselle Crane</a>. It is also one of only four crane species (with the <a href="/wiki/Sandhill_Crane" title="Sandhill Crane">Sandhill</a> and Demoiselle Crane and the <a href="/wiki/Brolga" title="Brolga">Brolga</a>) not currently classified as threatened with extinction or conservation dependent at the species level.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Crane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The Common Crane is a large, stately bird and a medium-sized crane. It is 100–130&#160;cm (40–52&#160;in) tall with a 180–240&#160;cm (71–96&#160;in) wingspan. The body weight can range from 3 to 6.1 kg (6.6 to 13 lb), with the nominate subspecies averaging around 5.4&#160;kg (12&#160;lb) and the eastern subspecies (<i>G. g. lilfordi</i>) averaging 4.6&#160;kg (10&#160;lb). Among standard measurements, the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> is 50.7–60.8 cm (20.0–23.9 in) long, the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> is 20.1–25.2 cm (7.9–9.9 in) and the <a href="/wiki/Culmen_(bird)" title="Culmen (bird)" class="mw-redirect">exposed culmen</a> is 9.5–11.6 cm (3.7–4.6 in).</p>
<p>This species is slate-grey overall. The forehead and <a href="/wiki/Lore_(anatomy)" title="Lore (anatomy)">lores</a> are blackish with a bare red crown and a white streak extending from behind the eyes to the upper back. The overall colour is darkest on the back and rump and palest on the breast and wings. The primaries, the tips of secondaries, the alula, the tip of the tail, and the edges of upper tail coverts are all black and the greater coverts droop into explosive plumes. This combination of colouration ultimately distinguishes it from similar species in Asia, like the <a href="/wiki/Hooded_Crane" title="Hooded Crane">Hooded</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black-necked_Crane" title="Black-necked Crane">Black-necked Cranes</a>. The juvenile has yellowish-brown tips to its body feathers and lacks the drooping wing feathers and the bright neck pattern of the adult, and has a fully feathered crown. Every two years, before migration, the adult Common Crane undergoes a complete moult, remaining flightless for six weeks, until the new feathers grow.</p>
<p>It has a loud trumpeting call, given in flight and display. The call is piercing and can be heard from a considerable distance. It has a dancing display, leaping with wings uplifted, described in detail below.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Crane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<p>This species is found in the northern parts of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. The Common Crane is an uncommon breeder in southern Europe, smaller numbers breeding in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. Larger breeding populations can found in <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>. The heart of the breeding population for the species is in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, however, where possibly up to 100,000 cranes of this species can be found seasonally. In Russia, it is distributed as a breeder from the <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> region to the <a href="/wiki/Chukchi_Peninsula" title="Chukchi Peninsula">Chukchi Peninsula</a>. The breeding population extends as far south as <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a> but almost the entire Asian breeding population is restricted to Russia.</p>
<p>The species is a long distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a> predominantly wintering in northern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. Fall migration is from August to October and spring migration is in March through May. Important staging areas occur anywhere from <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> (with a large one around the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>) and many thousand cranes can be seen in one day in the fall. Some birds winter in southern Europe, including <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. Most eastern Common Cranes winter in the river valleys of <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eritrea" title="Eritrea">Eritrea</a> with smaller numbers in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. The third major wintering region is in the northern half of <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>. Minimal wintering also occurs in <a href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">Burma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>. Lastly, they winter in eastern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, where they are often the most common crane (outnumbering Black-necked Cranes ten-to-one). Migrating flocks fly in a V formation.</p>
<p>It is a rare visitor to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, mostly blown over from the Chinese wintering population, and is a rare vagrant to western <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, where birds are occasionally seen with flocks of <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrating</a> <a href="/wiki/Sandhill_Crane" title="Sandhill Crane">Sandhill Cranes</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Crane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span></h2>
<p>In Europe, the Common Crane predominantly breeds in <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">boreal and taiga</a> forest and mixed forests, from an elevation of sea-level to 2,200&#160;m (7,200&#160;ft). In Northern climes, treeless moors, on bogs, or on dwarf heather habitats, usually where small lakes or pools are also found. In <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, breeders are usually found in small, swampy openings amongst pine forests while, in Germany, marshy wetlands are used. Breeding habitat used in Russia are similar, though they can be found nesting in less likely habitat such as <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a> and even semi-desert, so long as water is near. Primarily, the largest number of Common Cranes are found breeding in wooded swamps, bogs and wetlands and seem to require quiet, peaceful enivrons with minimal human interference. They occur at low density as breeders even where common, typically ranging from 1 to 5 pairs per 100&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (39&#160;sq&#160;mi).</p>
<p>While winter, this species moves to flooded areas, shallow sheltered bays, and swampy meadows. During the flightless moulting period there is a need for shallow waters or high reed cover for concealment. Later, after the migration period, the birds winter regularly in open country, often on cultivated lands and sometimes also in <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a>-like areas, for example on the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Crane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Crane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span></h3>
<p>The Common Crane is <a href="/wiki/Omnivorous" title="Omnivorous" class="mw-redirect">omnivorous</a>, as are all cranes. Dietary preferences correspond with those of the American <a href="/wiki/Sandhill_Crane" title="Sandhill Crane">Sandhill Crane</a>, which is the only crane that is more abundant. Both share a preference for plant matter, including <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">roots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhizome" title="Rhizome">rhizomes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tuber" title="Tuber">tubers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant_stem" title="Plant stem">stems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruits</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a>. The most commonly consumed foods include the shoots of <a href="/wiki/Grass" title="Grass">green grasses</a>, the leaves of <a href="/wiki/Herb" title="Herb">herbs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereal grains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhizome" title="Rhizome">rhizomes</a> and the leafy parts of <i><a href="/wiki/Phragmites" title="Phragmites">Phragmites</a></i>. They also commonly eat, as available, pond-weeds, <a href="/wiki/Heath" title="Heath">heath berries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pea" title="Pea">peas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potatoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olive" title="Olive">olives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acorn" title="Acorn">acorns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cedrus" title="Cedrus">cedarnuts</a> and pods of <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanuts</a>. Notably amongst the berries consumed, the <a href="/wiki/Cranberry" title="Cranberry">cranberry</a>, is possibly named after the species.</p>
<p>Animal foods become most imporant during the summer breeding season and may be the primary food source at that time of year. Most abundant among their animal foods are insects, including <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Locust" title="Locust">locusts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">crickets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dragonfly" title="Dragonfly">dragonflies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mantid" title="Mantid" class="mw-redirect">mantids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cockroach" title="Cockroach">cockroaches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterflies</a>. Other invertebrates consumed where abundant can include <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a> and, more seldomly, <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Millipede" title="Millipede">millipedes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woodlice" title="Woodlice" class="mw-redirect">woodlice</a>. Vertebrates consumed with some regularity have include <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a> and small <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, mainly comprised by <a href="/wiki/Mice" title="Mice" class="mw-redirect">mice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shrew" title="Shrew">shrews</a>. More rarely, <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and the eggs and the young and eggs of other <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> are eaten. In one case, a single adult <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">warbler</a> was observed to be swallowed by a crane.</p>
<p>Common Cranes may either forage on land or in shallow water, probing around with their bills for any edible organism. Although crops may locally be damaged by the species, mostly they consume waste grain in winter from previously harvest fields and so actually more commonly benefit farmers by cleaning fields for use in the following year. As in other cranes, all foraging (as well as drinking and roosting) is done in small groups, which may variously consist of pairs, family groups or winter flocks.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Crane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<p>This species usually lays eggs in May, though seldomly will do so earlier or later. Like most cranes, this species displays indefinite monogamous pair bonds. If one mate dies, a crane may attempt to court a new mate the following year. Although a pair may be together for several years, the courtship rituals of the species are inacted by every pair each spring. The dancing of Common Cranes has complex, social meanings and may occur at almost any time of year. Dancing may include bobs, bows, pirouettes, and stops, as in various crane species. Aggressive displays may include ruffled wing feathers, throwing vegetation in the air and pointing the bare red patch on their heads at each other. Courtship displays begin with a male following the female in a stately, march-like walk. The unison call, consists of the female holding her head up and gradually lowering down as she calls out. The female calls out a high note and then the male follows with a longer scream in a similar posture. Copulation consists of a similar, dramatic display.</p>
<p>The nesting territory of Common Cranes is variable and is based on the local habitat. It can range in size from variously 2 to 500 hectares. In common with Sandhill Cranes (and no other crane species), Common Cranes "paint" their bodies with mud or decaying vegetation, apparently in order to blend into their nesting environment. The nest is either in or very near shallow water, often with dense shore vegetation nearby, and may be used over several years. The size and placement of the nest varies considerably over the range, with Arctic birds building relatively small nests. In Sweden, an average nest is around 90&#160;cm (35&#160;in) across.</p>
<p>The clutch of the Common Crane usually contains two eggs, with seldomly one laid and, even more rarely, 3 or 4. If a clutch is lost early in incubation, the cranes may be able to lay another one within a couple of weeks. The incubation period is around 30 days and is done primarily by the female but occasionally by both sexes. If humans approach the nest both parents may engage in a distraction display but known ground predators (including domestic dogs) are physically attacked almost immediately.</p>
<p>New hatchlings are generally quite helpless but are able to crawl away from danger in a few hours, can swim soon after hatching and can run with their parents at 24 hours old. Chicks respond to danger by freezing, using their camoflauged brownish down to defend them beyond their fierce parents. Young chicks use their wings to stabilise them while running, while by 9 weeks of age they can fly short distances. The adult birds go through their postbreeding moult while caring for their young, rendering them flightless for about 5 to 6 weeks around the time the young also can't fly yet. According to figures of cranes wintering in Spain, around 48% birds have surviving young by the time they winter and around 18% are leading two young by winter. By the next breeding season, the previous years young often flock together. The age of sexual maturity in wild birds has been estimated at variously from 3 to 6 years of age. It is thought that it is common for this species to live up to 30 or 40 years of age.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Crane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sociality">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Sociality">Sociality</span></h3>
<p>The Common Crane is a fairly social bird while not breeding. Flocks of up to 400 birds may be seen flying together during migration. Staging sites, where migrating birds gather to rest and feed in the middle of their migration, may witness thousands of cranes gathering at once. However, the flocks of the species are not stable social units but rather groups that ensure greater safety in numbers and collectively draw each other's attention to ideal foraging and roosting sites. Possibly due to a longer moult, younger and non-breeding cranes are usually the earliest fall migrants and may band together at that time of year.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Crane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Interspecies interactions">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Interspecies_interactions">Interspecies interactions</span></h3>
<p>There are few natural predators of adult cranes, although <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_Eagle" title="White-tailed Eagle">White-tailed Eagle</a> are a potential predatory threat to Common Cranes of all ages. The crane has been known to counterattack the eagle both on the land and in mid-flight, using their bill as a weapon and kicking with their feet. Mammals such as <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">wild boar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wolverine" title="Wolverine">wolverine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Red_fox" title="Red fox">red foxes</a> are attacked at the nest, as they are potential predators. When facing off against mammals, cranes jab with their bill, hit with their wings and kick with their feet. The cranes nimbly avoid strikes against themselves by jumping into the air. Herbivorous mammals such as <a href="/wiki/Red_deer" title="Red deer">red deer</a> may also be attacked at the nest, indicating the high aggressiveness of the birds while nesting. The determined attack of a parent crane often assures safety from predators, although the <a href="/wiki/Carrion_crow" title="Carrion crow" class="mw-redirect">carrion crow</a> is locally a successful predator of cranes' eggs, trickily using distraction displays to steal them. Common Cranes may loosely associate with any other crane in the <i>Grus</i> genus in migration or winter as well as <a href="/wiki/Greater_white-fronted_geese" title="Greater white-fronted geese" class="mw-redirect">greater white-fronted geese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bean_geese" title="Bean geese" class="mw-redirect">bean geese</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Crane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h2>
<p>The global population is in the region of 210,000-250,000, with the vast majority nesting in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>. In some areas the breeding population appears to be increasing, such as in Sweden, whereas on the fringes of its range, it is often becoming rare to non-existent. In <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> the Common Crane became <a href="/wiki/Local_extinction" title="Local extinction">extirpated</a> in the 17th century, but a tiny population now breeds again in the <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_Broads" title="Norfolk Broads" class="mw-redirect">Norfolk Broads</a><sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from April 2010">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and is slowly increasing and a reintroduction is planned for the <a href="/wiki/Somerset_levels" title="Somerset levels" class="mw-redirect">Somerset levels</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, it died out as a breeding species in the 18th century, but a flock of about 20 appeared in County <a href="/wiki/County_Cork" title="County Cork">Cork</a> in November 2011. It was additionally extirpated as a breeder from <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> in the late 1800s, from <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> around 1900, from <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> by 1952 and from <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> by 1954. The <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> breeding population is also a fraction of the large numbers that once breed in the country.</p>
<p>The main threat to the species, and the primary reason for its decline in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Palearctic" title="Western Palearctic" class="mw-redirect">Western Palearctic</a>, comes from habitat loss and degradation, as a result of dam construction, urbanisation, agricultural expansion, and drainage of wetlands. Although it has adapted to human settlement in many areas, nest disturbance, continuing changes in land use, and collision with utility lines are still potential problems. Further threats may include persecution due to crop damage, pesticide poisoning, egg collection, and hunting.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The Common Crane is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Kranich_Museum" title="Kranich Museum">Kranich Museum</a> in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hessenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hessenburg (page does not exist)">Hessenburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg-Vorpommern" title="Mecklenburg-Vorpommern">Mecklenburg-Vorpommern</a>, Germany, is dedicated to art and folklore related to the Common Crane.</p>
