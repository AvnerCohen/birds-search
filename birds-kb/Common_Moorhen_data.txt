<p>The <b>common moorhen</b> (<i>Gallinula chloropus</i>), also known as the <b>waterhen</b> or <b>swamp chicken</b>, is a bird species in the <a href="/wiki/Rail_(bird)" title="Rail (bird)">rail</a> family (Rallidae). It is distributed across many parts of the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Avibase_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avibase-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common moorhen lives around well-vegetated marshes, ponds, canals and other wetlands. The species is not found in the <a href="/wiki/Polar_regions" class="mw-redirect" title="Polar regions">polar regions</a> or many <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">tropical rainforests</a>. Elsewhere it is likely the most common rail species, except for the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_coot" title="Eurasian coot">Eurasian coot</a> in some regions.
</p><p>The closely related <a href="/wiki/Common_gallinule" title="Common gallinule">common gallinule</a> of the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> has been recognized as a separate species by most authorities,<sup id="cite_ref-Avibase_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avibase-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> starting with the <a href="/wiki/American_Ornithologists%27_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="American Ornithologists&#39; Union">American Ornithologists' Union</a> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Ornithological_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="International Ornithological Committee">International Ornithological Committee</a> in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-Chesser_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chesser-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_moorhen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name <i>mor-hen</i> has been recorded in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> since the 13th century.<sup id="cite_ref-PDBBN_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PDBBN-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The word <i>moor</i> here is an old sense meaning <i>marsh</i>;<sup id="cite_ref-PDBBN_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PDBBN-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> the species is not usually found in <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moorland</a>. An older name, <b>common waterhen</b>, is more descriptive of the bird's <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a>.
</p><p>A "watercock" is not a male "waterhen" but the rail species <i><a href="/wiki/Gallicrex_cinerea" class="mw-redirect" title="Gallicrex cinerea">Gallicrex cinerea</a></i>, not closely related to the common moorhen. "Water rail" usually refers to <i><a href="/wiki/Rallus_aquaticus" class="mw-redirect" title="Rallus aquaticus">Rallus aquaticus</a></i>, again not closely related.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Scientific_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific name">scientific name</a> <i>Gallinula chloropus</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>Gallinula</i> (a small hen or chicken) and the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>chloropus</i> (<i>khloros</i> <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%87%CE%BB%CF%89%CF%81%CF%8C%CF%82" class="extiw" title="wikt:χλωρός">χλωρός</a> green or yellow, <i>pous</i> <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%80%CE%BF%CF%8D%CF%82" class="extiw" title="wikt:πούς">πούς</a> foot).<sup id="cite_ref-job_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_moorhen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Moorhen_feet.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Moorhen_feet.jpg/220px-Moorhen_feet.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Moorhen_feet.jpg/330px-Moorhen_feet.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Moorhen_feet.jpg/440px-Moorhen_feet.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3630" data-file-height="2371" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Moorhen_feet.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Common moorhen feet have no webbing</div></div></div>
<p>The moorhen is a distinctive species, with dark plumage apart from the white undertail, yellow legs and a red <a href="/wiki/Frontal_shield" title="Frontal shield">frontal shield</a>. The young are browner and lack the red shield. The frontal shield of the adult has a rounded top and fairly parallel sides; the tailward margin of the red unfeathered area is a smooth waving line. In the related common gallinule of the Americas, the frontal shield has a fairly straight top and is less wide towards the bill, giving a marked indentation to the back margin of the red area.
</p><p>The common moorhen gives a wide range of gargling calls and will emit loud hisses when threatened.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> A midsized to large rail, it can range from 30 to 38&#160;cm (12 to 15&#160;in) in length and span 50 to 62&#160;cm (20 to 24&#160;in) across the wings. The body mass of this species can range from 192 to 500&#160;g (6.8 to 17.6&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-arkive_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arkive-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AAB_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAB-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_moorhen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is a common breeding bird in marsh environments,  well-vegetated lakes and even in city parks.  Populations in areas where the waters freeze, such as eastern Europe, will <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> to more temperate climates. In China, common moorhen populations are largely resident south of the Yangtze River, whilst northern populations migrate in the winter, therefore these populations show high genetic diversity. <sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_moorhen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet_and_feeding">Diet and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_moorhen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This species will consume a wide variety of vegetable material and small aquatic creatures. They forage beside or in the water, sometimes walking on lilypads or upending in the water to feed. They are often secretive, but can become tame in some areas. Despite loss of habitat in parts of its range, the common moorhen remains plentiful and widespread.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_moorhen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The birds are territorial during breeding season. The nest is a basket built on the ground in dense vegetation. Laying starts in spring, between mid-March and mid-May in Northern hemisphere temperate regions. About 8 eggs are usually laid per female early in the season; a brood later in the year usually has only 5–8 or fewer eggs. Nests may be re-used by different females. Incubation lasts about three weeks. Both parents <a href="/wiki/Incubate_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Incubate (bird)">incubate</a> and feed the young. These fledge after 40–50 days, become independent usually a few weeks thereafter, and may raise their first brood the next spring. When threatened, the young may cling to the parents' body, after which the adult birds fly away to safety, carrying their offspring with them.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mann_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mann-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>On a global scale – all subspecies taken together – the common moorhen is as abundant as its vernacular name implies. It is therefore considered a species of <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> However, small populations may be prone to <a href="/wiki/Extinct" class="mw-redirect" title="Extinct">extinction</a>. The population of <a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">Palau</a>, belonging to the widespread subspecies <i>G. c. orientalis</i> and locally known as <i>debar</i> (a generic term also used for <a href="/wiki/Ducks" class="mw-redirect" title="Ducks">ducks</a> and meaning roughly "waterfowl"), is very rare, and apparently the birds are hunted by locals. Most of the population on the <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> occurs on <a href="/wiki/Angaur" title="Angaur">Angaur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peleliu" title="Peleliu">Peleliu</a>, while the species is probably already gone from <a href="/wiki/Koror" title="Koror">Koror</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ngardok" title="Lake Ngardok">Lake Ngardok</a> wetlands of <a href="/wiki/Babeldaob" title="Babeldaob">Babeldaob</a>, a few dozen still occur, but the total number of common moorhens on Palau is about in the same region as the Guam population: fewer than 100 adult birds (usually fewer than 50) have been encountered in any survey.<sup id="cite_ref-vanderwerfetal2006_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vanderwerfetal2006-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common moorhen is one of the birds (the other is the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_coot" title="Eurasian coot">Eurasian coot</a>, <i>Fulica atra</i>) from which the <a href="/wiki/Cyclocoelidae" title="Cyclocoelidae">cyclocoelid</a> <a href="/wiki/Flatworm" title="Flatworm">flatworm</a> <a href="/wiki/Parasite" class="mw-redirect" title="Parasite">parasite</a> <i>Cyclocoelum mutabile</i> was first described.<sup id="cite_ref-Dronen_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dronen-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The bird is also parasitised by the <a href="/wiki/Moorhen_flea" title="Moorhen flea">moorhen flea</a>, <i>Dasypsyllus gallinulae</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rothschild_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rothschild-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_moorhen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Five <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are today considered valid; several more have been described that are now considered <a href="/wiki/Junior_synonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Junior synonym">junior synonyms</a>. Most are not very readily recognizable, as differences are rather subtle and often <a href="/wiki/Cline_(biology)" title="Cline (biology)">clinal</a>. Usually, the location of a sighting is the most reliable indication as to subspecies identification, but the migratory tendencies of this species make identifications based on location not completely reliable. In addition to the extant subspecies listed below, an undescribed form from the <a href="/wiki/Early_Pleistocene" title="Early Pleistocene">Early Pleistocene</a> is recorded from Dursunlu in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-McCoy_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McCoy-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Olson_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olson-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Louchart_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Louchart-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<td>Found on the islands of <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">Réunion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Comoros" title="Comoros">Comoros</a>.
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td><b>Mariana common moorhen</b><br /><i>G. c. guami</i> <small>(<a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a>, 1917)</small><br />Called <i><b>pulattat</b></i> in <a href="/wiki/Chamorro_language" title="Chamorro language">Chamorro</a>.
</td>
<td>
</td>
<td>Body plumage is very dark.
</td>
<td>Endemic to the Northern <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Islands" title="Mariana Islands">Mariana Islands</a>, but see also <i>G. c. orientalis</i> above.<br />Population size: About 300 as of 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-Takano_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Takano-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</td></tr></tbody></table>
