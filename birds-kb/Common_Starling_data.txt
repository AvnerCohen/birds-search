<p>The <b>Common Starling</b> (<i>Sturnus vulgaris</i>), also known as the <b>European Starling</b> or just <b>Starling</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Sturnidae" title="Sturnidae" class="mw-redirect">Sturnidae</a>.</p>
<p>This species of <a href="/wiki/Starling" title="Starling">starling</a> is native to most of temperate <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. It is resident in southern and western Europe and southwestern Asia, while northeastern populations <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> south and west in winter to these regions, and also further south to areas where it does not breed in <a href="/wiki/Iberian_peninsula" title="Iberian peninsula" class="mw-redirect">Iberia</a> and north <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It has also been introduced to <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Sturnus" title="Sturnus">Sturnus</a></i>, the Common Starling is the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a>, the one with all the typical characteristics of its genus.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement"><span title="The material in the vicinity of this tag may not be factual or accurate from November 2011">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Common_Starling#Dubious" title="Talk:Common Starling">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> It is in this genus that the terrestrial feeding technique of open-bill probing is most advanced;<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from November 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the technique involves prying into the ground by inserting and opening the bill as a way of searching for hidden food items. Common Starlings have the physical traits that enable them to use this feeding technique, which has undoubtedly helped the species spread far and wide.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Along with <i>Sturnus vulgaris</i>, the <i>Sturnus</i> genus includes a number of species which are apparently more-or-less distantly related, but some contend that if the taxonomy is to be based on natural evolutionary grouping, then only the European and <a href="/wiki/Spotless_Starling" title="Spotless Starling">Spotless Starling</a> ought to be grouped together.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>There are several <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of the European Starling, which vary in the iridescence of adult plumage. With gradual variation over geographic range and extensive <a href="/wiki/Intergradation" title="Intergradation">intergradation</a>, the subspecies are said to be <a href="/wiki/Cline_(biology)" title="Cline (biology)">clinal</a>. Acceptance of different subspecies varies between different authorities.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-svensson_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-svensson-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vaurie_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vaurie-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Sturnus vulgaris vulgaris</b></i> Linnaeus, 1758. Common Starling. Most of Europe, except the far northwest and far southeast; also <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, where it is a recent colonist. Introduced populations worldwide also belong to this subspecies.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Nominate subspecies. The gloss is green on the head, belly and lower back, bronzy purple on the neck to upper chest and back, and purplish on the flanks and upper wing-coverts. Inconspicuous light buff fringes are present on the under wing-coverts. In eastern parts of range, more purplish and less bronzy gloss.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Sturnus vulgaris faroensis</b></i> <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wemyss_Feilden" title="Henry Wemyss Feilden">Feilden</a>, 1872. Faroese Starling; sometimes misspelt <i>faeroensis</i> or <i>faroeensis</i>. <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Slightly larger than nominate, especially bill and feet. Adult with darker and duller green gloss and far less spotting even in fresh plumage. Juvenile sooty black with whitish chin and areas on belly; throat spotted black.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Sturnus vulgaris zetlandicus</b></i> <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a>, 1918. Shetland Starling. <a href="/wiki/Shetland_Islands" title="Shetland Islands" class="mw-redirect">Shetland Islands</a>.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Like <i>faroensis</i> but intermediate in size between that and <i>vulgaris</i>. Birds from <a href="/wiki/Fair_Isle" title="Fair Isle">Fair Isle</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_Kilda,_Scotland" title="St Kilda, Scotland">St Kilda</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Hebrides" title="Outer Hebrides">Outer Hebrides</a> are intermediate between this subspecies and the nominate and placement with <i>vulgaris</i>or <i>zetlandicus</i> varies according to authority. Dark juveniles are occasionally found in Scotland and southwards, indicating some <a href="/wiki/Gene_flow" title="Gene flow">gene flow</a> from <i>faroensis</i> or an original <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(biology)" title="Polymorphism (biology)">polymorphism</a> that became dominant in <i>faroensis</i>.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Sturnus vulgaris granti</b></i> Hartert, 1903. Azores Starling. <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Like nominate, but smaller, especially feet. Often strong purple gloss on upperparts.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Sturnus vulgaris poltaratskyi</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Otto_Finsch" title="Otto Finsch">Finsch</a>, 1878). Eastern <a href="/wiki/Bashkortostan" title="Bashkortostan">Bashkortostan</a> eastwards through <a href="/wiki/Urals" title="Urals" class="mw-redirect">Urals</a> and central <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baykal" title="Lake Baykal" class="mw-redirect">Lake Baykal</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Like nominate, but gloss on head predominantly purple, on back green, on flanks usually purplish-blue, on upper wing-coverts bluish-green. In flight, conspicuous light cinnamon-buff fringes to under wing-coverts and axillaries; these areas may appear very pale in fresh plumage.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Sturnus vulgaris tauricus</b></i> <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Aleksandrovich_Buturlin" title="Sergei Aleksandrovich Buturlin">Buturlin</a>, 1904. From <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a> and E of <a href="/wiki/Dnieper_River" title="Dnieper River">Dnieper River</a> eastwards around coast of <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> to W <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a>, though not in uplands where replaced by <i>purpurascens</i>.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Like nominate, but decidedly long-winged. Gloss of head green, of body bronze-purple, of flanks and upper wing-coverts greenish bronze. Underwing blackish with pale fringes of coverts. Nearly spotless in breeding plumage.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Sturnus vulgaris purpurascens</b></i> <a href="/wiki/John_Gould" title="John Gould">Gould</a>, 1868. E Turkey to <a href="/wiki/Tbilisi" title="Tbilisi">Tbilisi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Sevan" title="Lake Sevan">Lake Sevan</a>, in uplands on E shore of Black sea replacing <i>tauricus</i>.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Like nominate, but wing longer and green gloss restricted to ear-coverts, neck and upper chest. Purple gloss elsewehere except on flanks and upper wing-coverts where more bronzy. Dark underwing with slim white fringes to coverts.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Sturnus vulgaris caucasicus</b></i> <a href="/wiki/Theodore_K_Lorenz" title="Theodore K Lorenz">Lorenz</a>, 1887. <a href="/wiki/Volga_Delta" title="Volga Delta">Volga Delta</a> through eastern <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> and adjacent areas.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Green gloss on head and back, purple gloss on neck and belly, more bluish on upper wing-coverts. Underwing like <i>purpurascens</i>.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Sturnus vulgaris porphyronotus</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Richard_Bowdler_Sharpe" title="Richard Bowdler Sharpe">Sharpe</a>, 1888). Western <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, grading into <i>poltaratskyi</i> between <a href="/wiki/Dzungarian_Alatau" title="Dzungarian Alatau">Dzungarian Alatau</a> and <a href="/wiki/Altai_Mountains" title="Altai Mountains">Altai</a>.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Very similar to <i>tauricus</i> but smaller and completely <a href="/wiki/Allopatric" title="Allopatric" class="mw-redirect">allopatric</a>, being separated by <i>purpurascens</i>, <i>caucasicus</i> and <i>nobilior</i>.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Sturnus vulgaris nobilior</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Allan_Octavian_Hume" title="Allan Octavian Hume">Hume</a>, 1879). <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, SE <a href="/wiki/Turkmenistan" title="Turkmenistan">Turkmenistan</a> and adjacent <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a> to E <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Like <i>purpurascens</i> but smaller and wing shorter; ear-coverts glossed purple, and underside and upperwing gloss quite reddish.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Sturnus vulgaris humii</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/William_Edwin_Brooks" title="William Edwin Brooks">Brooks</a>, 1876). <a href="/wiki/Kashmir" title="Kashmir">Kashmir</a> to <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Small; purple gloss restricted to neck area and sometimes flanks to tail-coverts, otherwise glossed green.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Sturnus vulgaris minor</b></i> (Hume, 1873). Sind Starling. <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Small; green gloss restricted to head and lower belly and back, otherwise glossed purple.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Several other forms have been named, but are generally no longer considered valid. Most are intergrades from where the ranges of various subspecies meet.<sup id="cite_ref-vaurie_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vaurie-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>S. v. ruthenus</i> <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Aleksandrovich_Menzbier" title="Mikhail Aleksandrovich Menzbier">Menzbier</a>, 1891 and <i>S. v. jitkowi</i> Buturlin, 1904 are intergrades between <i>vulgaris</i> and <i>poltaratskyi</i> from western <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>.</li>
<li><i>S. v. graecus</i> <a href="/wiki/Viktor_von_Tschusi_zu_Schmidhoffen" title="Viktor von Tschusi zu Schmidhoffen">Tschusi</a>, 1905 and <i>S. v. balcanicus</i> Buturlin and Harms, 1909 are intergrades between <i>vulgaris</i> and <i>tauricus</i> from the southern <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a> to central <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> (where there is some intergradation with <i>poltaratskyi</i> too) and throughout <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Bosporus" title="Bosporus" class="mw-redirect">Bosporus</a>.</li>
<li><i>S. v. heinrichi</i> <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Stresemann" title="Erwin Stresemann">Stresemann</a>, 1928 is an intergrade between <i>caucasicus</i> and <i>nobilior</i> in northern Iran.</li>
<li><i>S. v. persepolis</i> <a href="/wiki/Claud_Buchanan_Ticehurst" title="Claud Buchanan Ticehurst">Ticehurst</a>, 1928 from southern Iran (<a href="/wiki/Fars_Province" title="Fars Province">Fars Province</a>) is very similar to <i>vulgaris</i>; it is not clear whether it is a distinct resident population of simply migrants from southeastern Europe.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Etourneau_DSC01833.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Etourneau_DSC01833.JPG/220px-Etourneau_DSC01833.JPG" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Etourneau_DSC01833.JPG/330px-Etourneau_DSC01833.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Etourneau_DSC01833.JPG/440px-Etourneau_DSC01833.JPG 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Etourneau_DSC01833.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Adults and juveniles moulting to winter plumage, late summer</div>
</div>

<p>It is among the most familiar of birds in temperate regions. It is 19–23 cm (7.5–9.1 in) long, with a wingspan of 31–44 cm (12–17 in) and a weight of 58–101 g (2.0–3.6 oz).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Among standard measurements, the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> is 11.8 to 13.8 cm (4.6 to 5.4 in), the <a href="/wiki/Tail" title="Tail">tail</a> is 5.8 to 6.8 cm (2.3 to 2.7 in), the <a href="/wiki/Culmen_(bird)" title="Culmen (bird)" class="mw-redirect">culmen</a> is 2.5 to 3.2 cm (0.98 to 1.3 in) and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> is 2.7 to 3.2 cm (1.1 to 1.3 in).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> is shiny black, glossed purple or green, and spangled with white, particularly strongly so in winter. Adult male European Starlings are less spotted below than adult females. The throat feathers are long and loose, and used as a signal in display. Juveniles are grey-brown, and by their first winter resemble adults though often retain some brown juvenile feathering especially on the head in the early part of the winter. The legs are stout, pinkish-red. The bill is narrow conical with a sharp tip; in summer, it is yellow in females, and yellow with a blue-grey base in males, while in winter, and in juveniles, it is black in both sexes. <a href="/wiki/Moult" title="Moult" class="mw-redirect">Moulting</a> occurs once a year, in late summer after the breeding season is finished; the fresh feathers are prominently tipped white (breast feathers) or buff (wing and back feathers). The reduction in the spotting in the breeding season is achieved by the white feather tips largely wearing off. Starlings walk rather than hop. Their flight is quite strong and direct; they look triangular-winged and short-tailed in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-svensson_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-svensson-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Confusion with other species is only likely in Iberia, the western Mediterranean and northwest Africa in winter, when it has to be distinguished from the closely related <a href="/wiki/Spotless_Starling" title="Spotless Starling">Spotless Starling</a>, which, as its name implies, has less spotting on its plumage. The Spotless Starling can also be diagnostically distinguished at close range by its longer throat feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-svensson_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-svensson-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> At a more basic level, adult male <a href="/wiki/European_Blackbird" title="European Blackbird" class="mw-redirect">European Blackbirds</a> can easily be distinguished by more slender body shape, longer tail, and behaviour; they hop instead of walking and do not probe for food with open bills. In flight, only the much paler <a href="/wiki/Waxwing" title="Waxwing">waxwings</a> share a similar flight profile.</p>

<p>The Common Starling is a noisy bird uttering a wide variety of both melodic and mechanical-sounding sounds, including a distinctive "<a href="/wiki/Wolf-whistling" title="Wolf-whistling">wolf-whistle</a>". Starlings are mimics, like many of its family. In captivity, Starlings will learn to imitate all types of sounds and speech earning them the nickname "poor-man's <a href="/wiki/Myna" title="Myna">Myna</a>".</p>
<p>Songs are more commonly sung by males, although females also sing. Songs consist of a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Mimicry" title="Mimicry">mimicry</a>, clicks, wheezes, chattering, whistles, rattles, and piping notes.<sup id="cite_ref-H1924_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1924-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Besides song, 11 other calls have been described, including a Flock Call, Threat Call, Attack Call, Snarl Call, and Copulation Call.<sup id="cite_ref-H1924_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1924-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Birds chatter while roosting and bathing—making a great deal of noise that can frustrate local human inhabitants. Even when a flock of starlings is completely silent, the synchronized movements of the flock make a distinctive whooshing sound that can be heard hundreds of metres away.<sup id="cite_ref-H1924_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1924-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>Common Starlings prefer <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban</a> or <a href="/wiki/Suburban" title="Suburban" class="mw-redirect">suburban</a> areas where artificial structures and trees provide adequate nesting and <a href="/wiki/Roosting" title="Roosting" class="mw-redirect">roosting</a> sites. They also commonly reside in grassy areas where foraging is easy—such as farmland, grazing pastures, playing fields, golf courses, and airfields.<sup id="cite_ref-H1907_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1907-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> They occasionally inhabit open forests and woodlands and more rarely in shrublands such as the Australian <a href="/wiki/Heathland" title="Heathland" class="mw-redirect">heathland</a>. European Starlings rarely inhabit dense, wet forests (i.e. rainforests or wet <a href="/wiki/Sclerophyll" title="Sclerophyll">sclerophyll</a> forests). Common starlings have also adapted to coastal areas, where they nest and roost on cliffs and forage amongst seaweed. Their ability to adapt to a large variety of habitats has allowed for their dispersal and establishment throughout the world—resulting in a habitat range from coastal wetlands to <a href="/wiki/Alpine_climate" title="Alpine climate">alpine</a> forests, from sea level to 1900 metres above sea level.<sup id="cite_ref-H1907_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1907-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Widespread throughout the northern hemisphere, the European Starling is native to <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> and is found throughout <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> to Egypt), northern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>), and north-western <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Furthermore, it has been introduced to and successfully established itself in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a>, and several <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> islands. As a result, it has also been able to migrate to <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-H1908_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1908-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, Common Starlings are present throughout the southeast, although some isolated populations have been observed in northern and <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>. They are prevalent throughout <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>, and scattered sites in the southeastern part of Western Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-H19089_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H19089-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The bird has been observed nesting in gardens as the city of Buenos Aires (Argentina) from 1987. Since then, the species has expanded its breeding range at an average speed of 7.5&#160;km / year, always following the Atlantic coast, just 30&#160;km away inside. In Argentina, the species nests in a variety of media.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_flock_of_starlings_acting_as_a_swarm._-_geograph.org.uk_-_124593.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/The_flock_of_starlings_acting_as_a_swarm._-_geograph.org.uk_-_124593.jpg/220px-The_flock_of_starlings_acting_as_a_swarm._-_geograph.org.uk_-_124593.jpg" width="220" height="161" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/The_flock_of_starlings_acting_as_a_swarm._-_geograph.org.uk_-_124593.jpg/330px-The_flock_of_starlings_acting_as_a_swarm._-_geograph.org.uk_-_124593.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/The_flock_of_starlings_acting_as_a_swarm._-_geograph.org.uk_-_124593.jpg/440px-The_flock_of_starlings_acting_as_a_swarm._-_geograph.org.uk_-_124593.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_flock_of_starlings_acting_as_a_swarm._-_geograph.org.uk_-_124593.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A large flock in England</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It is a highly gregarious species in autumn and winter. <a href="/wiki/Group_size_measures" title="Group size measures">Flock size</a> is highly variable, with huge flocks providing a spectacular sight and sound usually occurring near roosts. These huge flocks often attract <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">birds of prey</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Merlin_(bird)" title="Merlin (bird)">Merlins</a> or <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian Sparrowhawk">Sparrowhawks</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from November 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Flocks form a tight <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">sphere</a>-like formation in flight, frequently expanding and contracting and changing shape, seemingly without any sort of leader. Very large roosts, exceptionally up to 1.5 million birds, can form in city centres, woodlands, or reedbeds, causing problems with their droppings. These may accumulate up to 30&#160;cm deep, killing trees by their chemical concentration; in smaller amounts, the droppings are, however, beneficial as a <a href="/wiki/Fertiliser" title="Fertiliser" class="mw-redirect">fertiliser</a>, and therefore woodland managers may try to move roosts from one area of a wood to another to spread the benefit and avoid large toxic deposits.<sup id="cite_ref-currie_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-currie-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Huge flocks of more than a million Starlings are observed just before sunset in spring in southwestern <a href="/wiki/Jutland" title="Jutland">Jutland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>. There they gather in March until northern <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> birds leave for their breeding ranges by mid-April. Their flocking creates complex shapes against the sky, a phenomenon known locally as <i><a href="/wiki/Sort_sol_(bird_flock)" title="Sort sol (bird flock)">sort sol</a></i> ("Black Sun"). To witness this spectacle, the best places are the seaward <a href="/wiki/Marshland" title="Marshland" class="mw-redirect">marshlands</a> (<i>marsken</i> in <a href="/wiki/Danish_(language)" title="Danish (language)" class="mw-redirect">Danish</a>) of <a href="/wiki/T%C3%B8nder" title="Tønder">Tønder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Esbjerg" title="Esbjerg">Esbjerg</a> municipalities between Tønder and <a href="/wiki/Ribe" title="Ribe">Ribe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Flocks of anything from five to fifty thousand Starlings form in areas of the UK just before sundown during mid winter. These flocks are commonly called a Starling "<i>Moot</i>".<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from November 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Starlings are hunted by birds of prey, including the <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_Falcon" title="Peregrine Falcon">Peregrine Falcon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brown_Falcon" title="Brown Falcon">Brown Falcon</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from November 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, in the 1970s the consumption of chemically treated (<a href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a>) crops by the starlings which were subsequently eaten by Peregrine Falcons caused a dangerous build-up of the toxin in the falcon. As a result, lower reproductive success was observed as a result of thinner eggshells and a build-up of <a href="/wiki/Organochlorine" title="Organochlorine" class="mw-redirect">organochlorine</a> residues in eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-Pruett_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pruett-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>European Starling nests are especially vulnerable to predators such as <a href="/wiki/Stoats" title="Stoats" class="mw-redirect">stoats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Foxes" title="Foxes" class="mw-redirect">foxes</a>, and humans. <a href="/wiki/Common_Myna" title="Common Myna">Common Mynas</a> are also a threat, as they often evict eggs, nestlings, and adult starlings from their nests.<sup id="cite_ref-H1928_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1928-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<p>The European Starling is <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" title="Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect">insectivorous</a>, and typically consumes insects including <a href="/wiki/Caterpillars" title="Caterpillars" class="mw-redirect">caterpillars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moths" title="Moths" class="mw-redirect">moths</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cicadas" title="Cicadas" class="mw-redirect">cicadas</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Spiders" title="Spiders" class="mw-redirect">spiders</a>. While the consumption of <a href="/wiki/Invertebrates" title="Invertebrates" class="mw-redirect">invertebrates</a> is necessary for successful breeding, starlings are <a href="/wiki/Omnivorous" title="Omnivorous" class="mw-redirect">omnivorous</a> and can also eat <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">grains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seeds" title="Seeds" class="mw-redirect">seeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fruits" title="Fruits" class="mw-redirect">fruits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nectars" title="Nectars" class="mw-redirect">nectars</a>, and garbage, if the opportunity arises.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-H1913_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1913-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pestnote_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pestnote-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> There are several methods by which they forage for their food; but for the most part, they forage from or near the ground, taking insects from or beneath the surface of the soil. Generally, starlings prefer foraging amongst short-cropped grasses and are often found between and on top of grazing animals out to pasture.<sup id="cite_ref-pestnote_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pestnote-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Large flocks forage together, in a practice called “roller-feeding”: where the birds at the back of the flock continually fly to the front of the flock as they forage so that every bird has a turn to lead (1957).<sup id="cite_ref-H1914_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1914-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> The larger the flock, the nearer individuals are to one another while foraging. Flocks often forage in one place for some time, and return to previous successfully foraged sites.<sup id="cite_ref-H1914_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1914-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> There are four types of foraging observed in the European Starling:<sup id="cite_ref-H1914_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1914-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Probing: The bird plunges its beak into the ground randomly and repetitively until an insect has been found. Probing is often accompanied by bill gaping (‘zirkelning’) where the bird opens its beak while probing to enlarge a dirt hole or to separate a lump of grass. This instinctual behavior has been observed in starlings eating garbage from plastic garbage bags—the bill gaping results in the opening of holes in the garbage bags that allow for extrication of consumables.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hawking_(birds)" title="Hawking (birds)">Sallying</a>: When the starling grabs an invertebrate directly from the air, a particularly successful behavior among this species.</li>
<li>Lunging: A less common technique where the starling lunges forward to catch a moving target or invertebrate on the surface floor.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gleaning_(birds)" title="Gleaning (birds)">Gleaning</a>: When the bird pulls backwards to extricate an earthworm from the soil.</li>
</ul>
<p>Among European Starling, sallying and probing are the most common foraging behaviors.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Courtship">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Courtship">Courtship</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nest_In_Grill.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Nest_In_Grill.jpeg/220px-Nest_In_Grill.jpeg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Nest_In_Grill.jpeg/330px-Nest_In_Grill.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Nest_In_Grill.jpeg/440px-Nest_In_Grill.jpeg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nest_In_Grill.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Nest constructed on a barbecue grill</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Unpaired males begin to build nests in order to attract single females. Males often decorate the nest with ornaments (such as flowers) and fresh green material which the female later disassembles upon accepting him as a mate .<sup id="cite_ref-Brouwer_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brouwer-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-H1923_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1923-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> The males sing throughout much of the construction and even more so when a female approaches his nest. Following <a href="/wiki/Copulation" title="Copulation" class="mw-redirect">copulation</a>, the male and female continue to build the nest. Common nesting locations include inside hollowed trees, buildings, tree stumps, and man-made nest-boxes. Nests are typically made out of straw, dry grass, twigs and inner lining made up of feathers, wool, and soft leaves. Construction typically takes 4 to 5 days and may continue through incubation.<sup id="cite_ref-H1926_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1926-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Fresh <a href="/wiki/Herbs" title="Herbs" class="mw-redirect">herbs</a> are added to nests and work as insect repellent.<sup id="cite_ref-Brouwer_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brouwer-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gwinner_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gwinner-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Starlings are both <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" title="Monogamous" class="mw-redirect">monogamous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polygamous" title="Polygamous" class="mw-redirect">polygamous</a>; although broods are generally brought up by one male and one female, occasionally the pair may have an extra helper. Pairs may be part of a larger colony, in which case several other nests may occupy the same or nearby trees.<sup id="cite_ref-H1924_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1924-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Baby_European_starlings.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Baby_European_starlings.jpg/220px-Baby_European_starlings.jpg" width="220" height="277" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Baby_European_starlings.jpg/330px-Baby_European_starlings.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Baby_European_starlings.jpg/440px-Baby_European_starlings.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Baby_European_starlings.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Chicks and nest in an air conditioner duct</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The breeding season begins in early spring and summer. In Australia, eggs are typically laid from late September to late November but the range extends from late July to December.<sup id="cite_ref-H1924_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1924-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Following copulation, female European Starlings will lay an egg on a daily basis over a period of several days. If an egg is lost during this time period, she will lay another egg to replace it. The eggs (4-5) are small <a href="/wiki/Elliptical" title="Elliptical" class="mw-redirect">elliptical</a> blue (and occasionally white) eggs that commonly have a glossy appearance to them.<sup id="cite_ref-H1926_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1926-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" title="Avian incubation" class="mw-redirect">Incubation</a> lasts 13 days, although the last egg laid may take 24 hours longer than the first to hatch. Both parents share the responsibility of sitting on top of the eggs. However, the female spends more time incubating the eggs than the male, and is the only parent to do so at night (while the male returns to the communal roost).<sup id="cite_ref-H1927_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1927-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> The young are born blind and naked. They develop light fluffy down within 7 days of hatching and sight within 9 days. <a href="/wiki/Nestlings" title="Nestlings" class="mw-redirect">Nestlings</a> remain in the nest for 3 weeks, where they are fed continuously by both their parents. <a href="/wiki/Fledgling_(birds)" title="Fledgling (birds)" class="mw-redirect">Fledglings</a> continue to be fed by their parents for 1–2 weeks. Pairs can raise up to three broods per breeding season, frequently reusing and relining the same nest.<sup id="cite_ref-H1927_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1927-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Within two months, most juveniles have molted and gained their first basic plumage. Juveniles acquire their adult plumage the following year.<sup id="cite_ref-H1928_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1928-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Intraspecific_hybrid" title="Intraspecific hybrid" class="mw-redirect">Intraspecific</a> <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasites" title="Brood parasites" class="mw-redirect">brood parasites</a> are common in European Starling nests. Female "floaters" (unpaired females during the breeding season) present in colonies often lay eggs in another pair's nest.<sup id="cite_ref-Sandell_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sandell-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Additionally, fledglings have been reported to invade their previous nests or neighboring nests and evicting the new brood.<sup id="cite_ref-H1927_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1927-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Status and conservation">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status_and_conservation">Status and conservation</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sturnus_vulgaris_-four_bathing_in_birdbath_-London-8.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Sturnus_vulgaris_-four_bathing_in_birdbath_-London-8.gif/220px-Sturnus_vulgaris_-four_bathing_in_birdbath_-London-8.gif" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Sturnus_vulgaris_-four_bathing_in_birdbath_-London-8.gif/330px-Sturnus_vulgaris_-four_bathing_in_birdbath_-London-8.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Sturnus_vulgaris_-four_bathing_in_birdbath_-London-8.gif/440px-Sturnus_vulgaris_-four_bathing_in_birdbath_-London-8.gif 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sturnus_vulgaris_-four_bathing_in_birdbath_-London-8.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Bathing in London</div>
</div>

<p>Overall, the European Starling is listed by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN" class="mw-redirect">IUCN</a> as being a species of least concern.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> However, it has been adversely affected in northern Europe by intensive agriculture, and in several countries, it has been <a href="/wiki/Red-listed" title="Red-listed">red-listed</a> due to declines of more than 50%. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, it declined by more than 80% between 1966 and 2004; although populations in some areas—such as <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>—are stable or even increasing, those in other areas—mainly in England—declined even more sharply. The overall decline has been attributed to a loss of food-rich permanent pasture, leading to the low survival rates of young birds.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> Major declines have also been observed from 1980 onward in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Karelia" title="Karelia">Karelia</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_States" title="Baltic States" class="mw-redirect">Baltic States</a>, and smaller declines in much of the rest of northern and central Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> In contrast, there have been increases in southern Europe, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, southern France, and northeastern Spain where the species first began breeding in the 1960s.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Earlier, the European Starling had shown marked increases throughout Europe in the period 1800-1900. Before 1800, it had a disjunct range in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, absent from central and southern <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, with <i>S. vulgaris zetlandicus</i> in the far north and northwest (Shetland, Orkney, Caithness, Outer Hebrides), and <i>S. vulgaris vulgaris</i> south of the Scottish-English border; it was also rare or regionally absent in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, western <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and western and northernmost England. Between 1800 and 1900, <i>S. vulgaris vulgaris</i> colonised north and westward from England to Ireland and all of Scotland except for Shetland (where <i>zetlandicus</i> remains present); since 1935, this subspecies has also spread to <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, where it now breeds in the southeast and southwest.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-holloway_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holloway-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>S. vulgaris vulgaris</i> is also occasionally seen in the Faroes.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Introduced populations">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Introduced_populations">Introduced populations</span></h3>
<table class="metadata plainlinks ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" style="">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" /></td>
<td class="mbox-text" style=""><span class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#Inline_citations" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">citations</a> for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>. <small><i>(November 2011)</i></small></span></td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>This adaptable species is considered to be a pest in several of the countries to which it has been introduced. Unlike other widely introduced species such as <a href="/wiki/House_Sparrow" title="House Sparrow">House Sparrows</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rock_Pigeon" title="Rock Pigeon" class="mw-redirect">Rock Pigeons</a>, starlings easily adapt in areas outside of cities and towns and can thrive in a variety of protected habitats where they are not native. The European Starling is a hole-nesting species and will nest in just about any cavity it finds. It has affected native species where it has been introduced because it fiercely competes for nest sites. For example, in North America, the <a href="/wiki/Purple_Martin" title="Purple Martin">Purple Martin</a> is now widely dependent on artificial nest houses put up by humans, which must be protected from colonization by European Starlings.</p>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span></h4>
<p>The Common Starling was originally introduced to Australia in order to decrease the population of crop pests—insects which the starlings were known to eat. Early settlers looked forward to the bird’s arrival, believing that starlings were also important to the pollination of <a href="/wiki/Flax" title="Flax">flax</a>, an important crop. Nest-boxes for the newly released species were placed on farms and near crops.<sup id="cite_ref-H19112_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H19112-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> The Common Starling was introduced to <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> in 1857 then <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a> in 1880.<sup id="cite_ref-H1910_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1910-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> By the 1880s, established populations were present in the southeast, thanks to the work of acclimatization committees.<sup id="cite_ref-Wooly_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wooly-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> By the 1920s, starlings were widespread through <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, but they were now recognized as pests and <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a> banned their import in 1895.<sup id="cite_ref-H1910_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H1910-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wooly_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wooly-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Common Starling threatens both Australian <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>. They commonly eat and damage fruit from orchards, such as <a href="/wiki/Grape" title="Grape">grapes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peach" title="Peach">peaches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olive" title="Olive">olives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ribes" title="Ribes">currants</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tomato" title="Tomato">tomatoes</a>. In addition, starlings dig up newly sown grain and diminish already growing crops—an easy task for a flock of 500 – 150,000 birds.<sup id="cite_ref-pestnote_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pestnote-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> The Common Starling will also eat and foul food meant for livestock, putting the health of livestock at stake. The accumulation of waste left behind by large flocks may ruin human water supplies and cities, as well as natural habitats for Australian fauna.<sup id="cite_ref-pestnote_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pestnote-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Australia is home to numerous <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> plants and animals — species that live nowhere else on earth. The Common Starling is an aggressive bird that displaces other hollow nesting, endemic <a href="/wiki/Australian_birds" title="Australian birds" class="mw-redirect">Australian birds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pell_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pell-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> With so many of these native species already endangered, the starling poses a significant threat. They also pose a risk to the native and endemic <a href="/wiki/Australian_flora" title="Australian flora" class="mw-redirect">Australian flora</a>. Seeds will often germinate more successfully after ingestion, and, as they are capable of eating almost any kind of seed and flying great distances, Common Starlings are excellent weed dispersers. They are responsible for spreading invasive plant species such as the <a href="/wiki/Bridal_Creeper" title="Bridal Creeper" class="mw-redirect">Bridal Creeper</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WWF_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>As a result, the government of <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a> has gone to great lengths to keep the Common Starling on the other side of their borders. In the state's 2007-2008 budget, an additional <a href="/wiki/A$" title="A$" class="mw-redirect">A$</a>4.9 million (2007) was allocated to the control and eradication program.<sup id="cite_ref-WWF_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> New flocks are routinely shot down, while the less cautious juveniles are trapped and netted.<sup id="cite_ref-Wooly_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wooly-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> New methods are currently being developed, the first of which tags one bird and follows it back to the rest of the flock, exposing a new population for termination.<sup id="cite_ref-Judas_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Judas-39"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> The second analyzes the DNA of Australian Common Starling populations to track where the migration from eastern to western Australia is occurring so that better preventative strategies can be used.<sup id="cite_ref-Rollins_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rollins-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span></h4>
<p>In New Zealand, the Starling was introduced in 1862, and now occurs in most of the country.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from November 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span></h4>

<p>After two failed attempts,<sup id="cite_ref-wdfw.wa.gov_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wdfw.wa.gov-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> about 60 European starlings were released into <a href="/wiki/New_York" title="New York">New York</a>’s <a href="/wiki/Central_Park" title="Central Park">Central Park</a> in 1890 by <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Schieffelin" title="Eugene Schieffelin">Eugene Schieffelin</a>. He was president of the infamous <a href="/wiki/American_Acclimatization_Society" title="American Acclimatization Society">American Acclimatization Society</a> which tried to introduce every bird species mentioned in the works of <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> to North America in 1890.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT1_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT1-42"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> The offspring of the original 60 starlings have spread across the continental United States, northward to southern Canada and Alaska, and southward into Central America. There are now an estimated 150 million starlings in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <sup id="cite_ref-wdfw.wa.gov_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wdfw.wa.gov-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> and 200 million in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a><a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.birds.cornell.edu/AllAboutBirds/BirdGuide/European_Starling.html">[4]</a>.</p>
<p>In 1889 and 1892, the Portland Song Bird Club released 35 pairs of starlings in Portland, Oregon. These birds established themselves, but then disappeared in 1901 or 1902. The next sighting of a starling in the Pacific Northwest was not until the mid 1940s. Presumably these birds could be genetically linked to the 1890 Central Park introduction.<sup id="cite_ref-wdfw.wa.gov_41-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wdfw.wa.gov-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is difficult to reach a consensus on starlings. Some value the species for their creative adaptiveness and their odd beauty. Many hold a strong dislike of starlings because of their aggressive behavior at feeders and nesting sites, and their overwhelming flocks and roosting habits. There is only one thing on which agreement can be reached regarding starlings—they are ubiquitous.<sup id="cite_ref-wdfw.wa.gov_41-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wdfw.wa.gov-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Being an <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced species</a> European starlings are exempt from the <a href="/wiki/Migratory_Bird_Treaty_Act" title="Migratory Bird Treaty Act" class="mw-redirect">Migratory Bird Treaty Act</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> Starlings are among the worst nuisance species in North America. The birds travel in enormous flocks; pose danger to air travel; disrupt farms; displace native birds; and roost on city blocks. Corrosive droppings on structures cause hundreds of millions of dollars of yearly damage. In 2008 the U.S. government poisoned, shot or trapped 1.7 million, the most of any nuisance species.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span></h4>
<p>In South Africa, the Starling was introduced in 1890, and is now common in the southern <a href="/wiki/Cape_Province" title="Cape Province">Cape</a> region, and less common north to the Johannesburg area. It is not as damaging to agriculture in South Africa as it is in the USA.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
