<p>The <b>Long-billed dowitcher</b> (<i>Limnodromus scolopaceus</i>) is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Shorebird" class="mw-redirect" title="Shorebird">shorebird</a> with a relatively long bill belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Sandpiper" title="Sandpiper">sandpiper</a> family, <a href="/wiki/Scolopacidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Scolopacidae">Scolopacidae</a>. In breeding plumage, adults are characterized by a beautiful rufous head and underparts with a darker mottled back and a large white upper rump only seen in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:2_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> They feed in various freshwater habitats with their bill underwater in a "sewing machine" motion and are know to have an exciting mating display where males chase females in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The Genus, <i><a href="/wiki/Dowitcher" title="Dowitcher">Limnodromus</a></i> is  <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>  from <i>limne</i>, "marsh" and <i>dromos</i>, "racer". The specific <i>scolopaceus</i> is <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">New Latin</a> for "snipe-like", from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>scolopax, scolopacis</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Snipe" title="Snipe">snipe</a> or <a href="/wiki/Woodcock" title="Woodcock">woodcock</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-job_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The English name is from <a href="/wiki/Iroquois" title="Iroquois">Iroquois</a> and was first recorded in 1841.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The Long-billed dowitcher is nearly identical in appearance to the <a href="/wiki/Short-billed_dowitcher" title="Short-billed dowitcher">Short-billed dowticher</a> and was only recognized as a separate species in 1950 by Pitelka.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Between the two, the best distinguishing field mark is their flight call, especially in winter where both species are even more difficult to tell apart.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> However, the two species differ ecologically in a few ways, starting with habitat and breeding location. Short-billeds prefer salt-water and breed primarily in southern Alaska and Yukon, as well as central Canada and the Maritime provinces, while Long-billeds generally prefer freshwater and breed mainly from western and northern Alaska to eastern Siberia before migrating as far south as Mexico for the winter.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-billed_dowitcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Long-billed dowitcher is a bird in the order <a href="/wiki/Charadriiformes" title="Charadriiformes">Charadriiformes</a>, which includes shorebirds, <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Auk" title="Auk">alcids</a>. It is part of the <a href="/wiki/Sandpiper" title="Sandpiper">Scolopacidae</a> family, and it belongs to the Scolopacinae subfamily along with snipes and woodcocks. Its genus <i><a href="/wiki/Dowitcher" title="Dowitcher">Limnodromus</a></i> includes only two other species; the <a href="/wiki/Short-billed_dowitcher" title="Short-billed dowitcher">Short-billed dowitcher</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Asian_dowitcher" title="Asian dowitcher">Asian dowitcher</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The long-billed dowitcher was first described by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Say" title="Thomas Say">Thomas Say</a> in 1823 under the name <i>Limnodromus scolopacea</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The taxonomy of the long-billed and short-billed dowitcher has presented difficulties in part due to the variability of the short-billed dowitcher.
</p><p>For around 100 years the long-billed dowitcher and short-billed dowitcher were recognized as two distinct species. By 1927 the long-billed dowitcher was made a sub-species of the short-billed dowitcher, as the western form, due to bird observations which were similar to both species linking the two geographically.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It was not until <a href="/wiki/Frank_Pitelka" title="Frank Pitelka">Frank Pitelka</a> published his monograph, in 1950, that the two dowitcher species were once again accepted as being two distinct species.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Further research has shown that the two species are estimated to have diverged genetically more than four million years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-billed_dowitcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Long-billed dowitcher is a medium-sized, stocky sandpiper with a bill about twice the length of its head. In all <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumages</a>, the long-billed dowitcher has a whitish <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a> and dark <a href="/wiki/Loral" class="mw-disambig" title="Loral">loral</a> stripe that continuous past the eye. The tail is barred black and white with the black being almost twice the width of the white and a large distinctive white rump which extends up to the middle of its back.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Long_Billed_Dowitcher_(7569920384).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Long_Billed_Dowitcher_%287569920384%29.jpg/220px-Long_Billed_Dowitcher_%287569920384%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Long_Billed_Dowitcher_%287569920384%29.jpg/330px-Long_Billed_Dowitcher_%287569920384%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Long_Billed_Dowitcher_%287569920384%29.jpg/440px-Long_Billed_Dowitcher_%287569920384%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2705" data-file-height="2707" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Long_Billed_Dowitcher_(7569920384).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Breeding Adult</div></div></div><p> Long-billed dowitcher are in breeding plumage from approximately May to late August or early September. In breeding plumage, adults are characterized by a dark crown on top of their head and a <a href="/wiki/Rufous" title="Rufous">rufous</a> neck, chest, and belly underneath with black bars on their breast and white barring on flanks when plumage is fresh. The older the feathers get the less the black bars may appear leaving the breast dark redish. The crown and the back are a mix of brown, black and <a href="/wiki/Buff" class="mw-disambig" title="Buff">buff</a> markings.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Wings and upper-back are mottled with black, buff, and white markings looking overall dark brown. 
</p><p>When in the winter plumage the long-billed dowitcher is very difficult to identify in the field with the short-billed dowitcher.In non-breeding plumage, adults are drab grey, with darker upperparts and breast contrasting with paler white belly. The gray of the breast also gradually lightens as it reaches the chin.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:2_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The juvenile plumage of the long-billed dowitcher is similar to that of the breeding adult except for being paler. Juvenile long-billed dowitcher can be distinguished from the short-billed dowitcher by the differences in the <a href="/wiki/Tertiary" title="Tertiary">tertiary</a> feathers. On the long-billed dowitcher these feathers are dark gray with narrow buff edges with internal markings so dull they seem to lack them altogether. In juvenile birds the upper parts are fringed chestnut rather than buffy brown and their uniformly gray breast is slightly demarcated from the pale rufous lower belly and breasts.
</p><p>The bill of the long-billed dowitcher ranges from 62 mm to 72 mm with males having bill lengths near the smaller scale reflecting their smaller body size. Bills are typically very straight and black becoming yellowish olive-green near the base and legs are also yellowish.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:2_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Sexes are almost identical, with females being generally heavier and having longer wings and bill.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Measurements</b>:<sup id="cite_ref-:1_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Length</b>: 11.4 in (29 cm)</li>
<li><b>Weight</b>: 3.1-4.6 oz (88-131 g)</li>
<li><b>Wingspan</b>: 18.5-19.3 in (47-49 cm)</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_Distribution">Habitat and Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-billed_dowitcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitat and Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-billed_dowitcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In North America, the Long-billed dowitcher breeds mainly throughout western and northern Alaska along the coast from Hooper Bay to w. Mackenzie and south to the foothills of Brooks Range. In this range, while nesting it greatly prefers wet, grass or sedge freshwater meadows but it is also sometimes confined to marshes and will move to lakes, ponds, or estuaries to forage after nesting. In eastern Serbia it breeds from the lower Yana River to Chukotka Peninsula and Anadyr Lowlands with an apparent westward expansion in Russia, also commonly nesting along Bering Sea and inland along rivers draining into the East Arctic Sea of Siberia.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-breeding">Non-breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-billed_dowitcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Non-breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the non-breeding season, its range has been difficult to determine due to its similarity to Short-billed dowitchers in winter plumage, especially in areas where both species overlap in which case most birds are identified as "Dowitchers". Along the Pacific coast however, it winters in various locations from south-western British-Columbia to Baja California, also moving inland to Arizona, New Mexico, Texas, and south to Mexico. Along the Atlantic coast, it winters from North Carolina to Florida, also moving west along the Gulf Coast to Mississipi, Louisiana, and Texas. In the winter, this bird can be found in a much larger variety of habitats ranging from mudflats, flooded wetlands, wet meadows and fields to various lakes and marshes preferring water less than three inches deep.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In general, Long-billed dowitchers seem to prefer fresh over salt water and muddy over sandy habitats in comparison to Short-billed dowitchers.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration">Migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-billed_dowitcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The long-billed dowitcher will migrate later in the fall than the short-billed dowitcher and earlier in the spring. The spring migrations occurs from February to May with birds moving up along the Pacific coast and interior. The long-billed dowitcher will also migrate through the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> on the western side with a vast majority moving through <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The fall migrations generally occurs from July to October with the adult long-billed dowitcher beginning to migrate south in July while juveniles begin migrating through September to October. From their breeding grounds the long-billed dowitcher will either migrate south along the Pacific Coast, across the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Prairies" title="Canadian Prairies">Canadian Prairies</a> and down the Great Basin or through <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> towards Florida.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-billed_dowitcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet_and_Foraging">Diet and Foraging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-billed_dowitcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Diet and Foraging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Long-billed dowitchers <a href="/wiki/Forage" title="Forage">forage</a> by jabbing or probing with a characteristic "sewing machine" motion in shallow water or on wet mud, often with their heads underwater and using tactile receptors on the tip of their bill to locate prey by touch.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The long-billed dowitcher, during breeding, consumes large quantities of <a href="/wiki/Chironomidae" title="Chironomidae">chironomidae</a> larva and larva of other insects with occasional plant matter and seeds. During migration and in their wintering region, the long-billed dowitcher consumes a far greater range of food types. Dowitchers eat everything from <a href="/wiki/Polychaetes" class="mw-redirect" title="Polychaetes">polychaetes</a> to insect larva to <a href="/wiki/Crustaceans" class="mw-redirect" title="Crustaceans">crustaceans</a> to <a href="/wiki/Mollusks" class="mw-redirect" title="Mollusks">mollusks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Also, having night vision, they are known to forage at night during migration.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocalization">Vocalization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-billed_dowitcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Vocalization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Long-billed dowitcher's main call, mostly heard in flight but also while on ground, is a high, sharp <i>keek</i> sometimes repeated as an accelerating quick double or triple note series. Its second, less common call is a <i>tu</i> given 1-8 times. Its song is described as <i>pee-witch-er</i>, and its alarm call is an explosive <i>KEEK.</i> The long-billed dowitcher is a more vocal shorebird often making a <i>keek</i> or <i>tu</i> call, heard when in feeding flocks unlike the short-billed dowitcher which are generally silent on the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:2_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-billed_dowitcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Male long-billed dowitcher will court females by first singing to them and then compete with other males by perusing the female in flight, displaying an aerial show of speed and agility. After mating, they are known to sing while hovering 15 feet in the air above their territories.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Once mated the male and female dowitcher form a pair bond. Long-billed dowitchers nest in wet areas of tall grasses in the troughs of raised mounds and ridges. The nest is a simple depression in the ground usually lined with grass and leaves.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Long-billed dowitcher lays four eggs per brood every year having only one brood per season. On rare occasions the long-billed dowitcher will lay three eggs. The eggs are oval to pear shaped and range from being a buff olive to a greenish or blueish <a href="/wiki/Glaucous" title="Glaucous">glaucous</a>. The eggs are also heavily splotched with varying shades of brown near the base of the large end with the underlying marks being dark gray. Incubation of the eggs is approximately twenty days in which both sexes participate. Long-billed dowitcher chicks are <a href="/wiki/Precocial" class="mw-redirect" title="Precocial">precocial</a> and downy being able to feed themselves within a few hours of hatching. In the long-billed dowitcher it is the male which takes care of the chicks until they have <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledged</a>.
</p>
