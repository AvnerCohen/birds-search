<p>The <b>white-crowned wheatear</b>, or <b>white-crowned black wheatear</b> (<i>Oenanthe leucopyga</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Wheatear" title="Wheatear">wheatear</a>, a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> that was formerly classed as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family Turdidae, but is now more generally considered to be an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a>, Muscicapidae.
</p><p>This large 17–18&#160;cm long wheatear breeds in stony deserts from the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabia</a> across to <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>. It is largely resident but has occurred as a rare vagrant to western Europe as far away as <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>. It nests in crevices in rocks or walls laying 3-5 eggs.
</p><p>Although most wheatear species have distinctive male and female plumages, adults of both sexes of this species are black except for a white rump and mainly white tail. Full adults have a white crown, not shown by young birds.
</p><p>The similar <a href="/wiki/Black_wheatear" title="Black wheatear">black wheatear</a>, <i>Oenanthe leucura</i>, also breeds in the western part of the white-crowned wheatear's range, but the latter has a black inverted <b>T</b> on its white tail, whereas white-crowned has only a black centre to its tail. The black wheatear never has a white crown.
</p><p>This species mainly eats insects. This wheatear has a loud song, more varied than most of its relatives and often including mimicry.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White-crowned_wheatear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The genus name <i>Oenanthe</i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>oenos</i> (οίνος) "wine" and <i>anthos</i> (ανθός) "flower".  It refers to the <a href="/wiki/Northern_wheatear" title="Northern wheatear">northern wheatear</a>'s return to Greece in the spring just as the <a href="/wiki/Vitis" title="Vitis">grapevines</a> blossom.  The specific <i>leucopyga</i> is <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> from <i>leukos</i>, "white", and <i>puge</i>, "rump".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> "Wheatear" is not derived from "<a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>" or any sense of "<a href="/wiki/Ear" title="Ear">ear</a>", but is a 16th-century <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_corruption" class="mw-redirect" title="Linguistic corruption">linguistic corruption</a> of "white" and "<a href="/wiki/Buttocks" title="Buttocks">arse</a>", referring to the prominent white rump found in many species.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
