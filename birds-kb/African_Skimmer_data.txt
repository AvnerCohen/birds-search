<p>The <b>African Skimmer</b> (<i>Rynchops flavirostris</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Near-threatened" title="Near-threatened" class="mw-redirect">near-threatened</a> species of bird belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Skimmer" title="Skimmer">skimmer</a> family. It is found along rivers, lakes and lagoons in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=African_Skimmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Appearance">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance">Appearance</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:African_skimmers.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/African_skimmers.jpg/250px-African_skimmers.jpg" width="250" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/African_skimmers.jpg/375px-African_skimmers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/African_skimmers.jpg/500px-African_skimmers.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:African_skimmers.jpg" class="internal" title="放大"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
African Skimmers and <a href="/wiki/White-winged_Tern" title="White-winged Tern">White-winged Terns</a> at Kazinga Channel, Uganda</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>They have very long wings. The back, hindneck, and crown are black. The forehead and rest of the body is white, with a bright, long, orange beak that ends with a yellow tip (black tip in immatures). Their short forked tail is white, and their legs are bright red. The average size is about 38&#160;cm (15&#160;in) long. Their voice is a sharp "kip-kip". Their bill structure is unique. The lower mandible is much longer than the upper mandible, and flattened sideways like scissor blades.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=African_Skimmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<p>The African Skimmer is found from <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> to northern <a href="/wiki/Congo_River" title="Congo River">Congo River</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Nile_Valley" title="Nile Valley" class="mw-redirect">Nile Valley</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Zambezi_Valley" title="Zambezi Valley" class="mw-redirect">Zambezi Valley</a>, and then to <a href="/wiki/KwaZulu-Natal_Province" title="KwaZulu-Natal Province" class="mw-redirect">KwaZulu-Natal Province</a> (South Africa) and <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a>. They live at large tropical rivers with sandbanks, lake shores, and coastal lagoons. The African Skimmer is generally uncommon and the total populations is estimated at 15,000-25,000 individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=African_Skimmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span></h2>
<p>African Skimmers fly in lines over calm waters, and dip their lower <a href="/wiki/Mandible" title="Mandible">mandibles</a> in the water to feed.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> When the mandible touches a fish, the skimmer snaps its mouth shut. They feed mostly at dawn and dusk.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=African_Skimmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span></h3>
<p>Pairs nest in loose colonies on large sandbanks. The colonies typically consist of less than 50 pairs and each pair lays 2–3 (rarely 4) eggs in a scrape in the sand.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
