<p>The <b>Smew</b> (<i>Mergellus albellus</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">duck</a> It is somewhat intermediate between the <a href="/wiki/Mergus" title="Mergus">typical mergansers</a> (<i>Mergus</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Goldeneye_(duck)" title="Goldeneye (duck)">goldeneyes</a> (<i>Bucephala</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Monotypic_taxon" title="Monotypic taxon">only member</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><b>Mergellus</b></i>. Sometimes included in <i>Mergus</i>, this genus is distinct (though closely related) and might actually be closer to the goldeneyes.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The Smew has interbred with the <a href="/wiki/Common_Goldeneye" title="Common Goldeneye">Common Goldeneye</a> (<i>B. clangula</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>An unnamed <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> seaduck, known from a <a href="/wiki/Humerus" title="Humerus">humerus</a> found in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Miocene" title="Middle Miocene">Middle Miocene</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Saj%C3%B3v%C3%B6lgyi_Formation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sajóvölgyi Formation (page does not exist)">Sajóvölgyi Formation</a> (Late <a href="/w/index.php?title=Badenian&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Badenian (page does not exist)">Badenian</a>, 13–12 <a href="/wiki/Million_years_ago" title="Million years ago" class="mw-redirect">million years ago</a>) of <a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%A1trasz%C3%B5l%C3%B5s&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mátraszõlõs (page does not exist)">Mátraszõlõs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, was assigned to <i>Mergus</i>. However, the authors included the Smew therein, and consequently, the bone is more properly assigned to <i>Mergellus</i>—especially as it was more similar to a Smew's than to the <i>Bucephala</i> remains also found at the site. It is sometimes argued (e.g. by Mlíkovský in <i>Cenozoic Birds of the World</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-Ml.C3.ADkovsk.C3.BD123_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ml.C3.ADkovsk.C3.BD123-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> that the Mátraszõlõs fossil is too old to represent any of the modern seaduck genera, but apparently these were all well-distinct even back then.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Fossils from the earliest <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> found in England indicate that the living species was extant 1.5 to 2.0 million years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-Ml.C3.ADkovsk.C3.BD123_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ml.C3.ADkovsk.C3.BD123-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Smew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The drake Smew, with its 'cracked ice' and 'panda' appearance, is unmistakable, and looks very black-and-white in flight. The females and immature males are grey birds with chestnut foreheads and crowns, and can be confused at a distance with the <a href="/wiki/Ruddy_Duck" title="Ruddy Duck">Ruddy Duck</a>; they are often known as "redhead" Smew. It has oval white wing-patches in flight. The Smew's bill has a hooked tip and serrated edges, which help it catch fish when it dives for them.</p>
<p>The Smew is 38–44 cm (15–17 in) long.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Smew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_ecology">Distribution and ecology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MergellusAlbellusTakeOffv3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/MergellusAlbellusTakeOffv3.jpg/220px-MergellusAlbellusTakeOffv3.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MergellusAlbellusTakeOffv3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Smew taking off</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This species breeds in the northern <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a> of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. It needs trees for breeding. The Smew lives on fish-rich lakes and slow rivers. As a migrant it leaves its breeding areas and winters on sheltered coasts or inland lakes of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>, northern Germany and the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a>, with small number reaching <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> (for example, at <a href="/wiki/Dungeness_(headland)" title="Dungeness (headland)">Dungeness</a>), mostly at regular sites. Vagrants have been recorded in North America. On lakes it prefers areas around the edges, often under small trees.</p>
<p>The Smew breeds in May and lays 6–9 cream-colored eggs. It nests in tree holes, such as old <a href="/wiki/Woodpecker" title="Woodpecker">woodpecker</a> nests. It is a shy bird and flushes easily when disturbed.</p>
<p>The Smew is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>
<p>It is not considered threatened on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>, though its population is decreasing.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
