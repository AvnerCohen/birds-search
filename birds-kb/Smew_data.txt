<p>The <b>smew</b> (<i>Mergellus albellus</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">duck</a>, and is the <a href="/wiki/Monotypic_taxon" title="Monotypic taxon">only living member</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><b>Mergellus</b></i>. <i>Mergellus</i> is a diminutive of <i><a href="/wiki/Mergus" title="Mergus">Mergus</a></i> and <i>albellus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>  <i>albus</i> "white".<sup id="cite_ref-job90_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job90-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This genus is closely related to <i>Mergus</i> and is sometimes included in it, though it might be closer to the <a href="/wiki/Goldeneye_(duck)" title="Goldeneye (duck)">goldeneyes</a> (<i>Bucephala</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Livezey1986_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Livezey1986-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The smew has hybridized with the <a href="/wiki/Common_goldeneye" title="Common goldeneye">common goldeneye</a> (<i>B. clangula</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A seaduck fossil from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Miocene" title="Middle Miocene">Middle Miocene</a> shows that birds similar to smew existed up to 13&#160;<a href="/wiki/Million_years_ago" class="mw-redirect" title="Million years ago">million years ago</a>. The extant species dates back to the Late <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>smew</i> has been used since the 17th century and is of uncertain origin. It is believed to be related to the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> <i>smient</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Wigeon" title="Wigeon">wigeon</a>") and the German <i>Schmeiente</i> or <i> Schmünte</i>, "wild duck."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It is probably derived from <i>smee</i>, a dialectal term for a wild duck.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Spinus-smew-2014-11-n019832-w.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Spinus-smew-2014-11-n019832-w.jpg/220px-Spinus-smew-2014-11-n019832-w.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="122" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Spinus-smew-2014-11-n019832-w.jpg/330px-Spinus-smew-2014-11-n019832-w.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Spinus-smew-2014-11-n019832-w.jpg/440px-Spinus-smew-2014-11-n019832-w.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1888" data-file-height="1048" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Spinus-smew-2014-11-n019832-w.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mergellus albellus female (closeup view).</div></div></div>
<p>The drake smew, with its 'cracked ice' and 'panda' appearance, is unmistakable, and looks very black-and-white in flight. The females and immature males are grey birds with chestnut foreheads and crowns, and can be confused at a distance with the <a href="/wiki/Ruddy_duck" title="Ruddy duck">ruddy duck</a>; they are often known as "redhead" smew. It has oval white wing-patches in flight. The smew's bill has a hooked tip and serrated edges, which help it catch fish when it dives for them.
</p><p>The smew is 38–44&#160;cm (15–17&#160;in) long,<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> with a wingspan of 56–69 cm, and a weight 450–650 g<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_ecology">Distribution and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MergellusAlbellusTakeOffv3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/MergellusAlbellusTakeOffv3.jpg/220px-MergellusAlbellusTakeOffv3.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/MergellusAlbellusTakeOffv3.jpg/330px-MergellusAlbellusTakeOffv3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/MergellusAlbellusTakeOffv3.jpg/440px-MergellusAlbellusTakeOffv3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2636" data-file-height="1758" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MergellusAlbellusTakeOffv3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Smew taking off</div></div></div>
<p>This species breeds in the northern <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a> of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>. It needs trees for breeding. The smew lives on fish-rich lakes and slow rivers. As a migrant, it leaves its breeding areas and winters on sheltered coasts or inland lakes of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>, northern Germany and the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a>, with a small number reaching <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> (for example, at <a href="/wiki/Dungeness_(headland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dungeness (headland)">Dungeness</a>), mostly at regular sites. Vagrants have been recorded in North America. On lakes it prefers areas around the edges, often under small trees. The smew breeds in May and lays 7-11 cream-colored eggs, incubated by the female for 26-28 days. Ducklings leave the nest soon after hatching and learn to fly within about 10 weeks<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>.
</p><p>It nests in tree holes, such as old <a href="/wiki/Woodpecker" title="Woodpecker">woodpecker</a> nests. It is a shy bird and flushes easily when disturbed.
</p><p>The smew is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies. It is not considered threatened on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>, though its population is decreasing.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_history">Fossil history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fossil history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An unnamed <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> seaduck, known from a <a href="/wiki/Humerus" title="Humerus">humerus</a> found in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Miocene" title="Middle Miocene">Middle Miocene</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Saj%C3%B3v%C3%B6lgyi_Formation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sajóvölgyi Formation (page does not exist)">Sajóvölgyi Formation</a> (Late <a href="/w/index.php?title=Badenian&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Badenian (page does not exist)">Badenian</a>, 13–12 <a href="/wiki/Million_years_ago" class="mw-redirect" title="Million years ago">million years ago</a>) of <a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%A1trasz%C3%B5l%C3%B5s&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mátraszõlõs (page does not exist)">Mátraszõlõs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, was assigned to <i>Mergus</i>. However, the authors included the smew therein, and consequently, the bone is more properly assigned to <i>Mergellus</i>—especially as it was more similar to a smew's than to the <i>Bucephala</i> remains also found at the site. It is sometimes argued<sup id="cite_ref-Mlíkovský123_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mlíkovský123-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> that the Mátraszõlõs fossil is too old to represent any of the modern seaduck genera, but apparently these were all well-distinct even back then.<sup id="cite_ref-Gal1998_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gal1998-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Subfossils from this species was also found in England indicate that the living species' range extended there a few hundred years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-Mlíkovský123_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mlíkovský123-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
