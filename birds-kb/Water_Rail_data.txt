<p>The <b>Water Rail</b> (<i>Rallus aquaticus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Rallidae" title="Rallidae">rail family</a> which breeds in well-vegetated <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a> across Europe, Asia and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>. Northern and eastern populations are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, but this <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> is a permanent resident in the warmer parts of its breeding range. The adult is 23–28&#160;cm (9–11&#160;in) long, and, like other rails, has a body that is flattened laterally to allow it easier passage through the <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed beds</a> it inhabits. It has mainly brown upperparts and blue-grey underparts, black barring on the flanks, long toes, a short tail and a long reddish bill. The eastern <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>, <i>R.&#160;a.&#160;indicus</i>, has distinctive markings and a call that is very different from the pig-like squeal of the western races, and is sometimes split as a separate species. Immature birds are generally similar in appearance to the adults, but the blue-grey in the <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> is replaced by buff. The downy chicks are black, as with all rails.</p>
<p>The Water Rail breeds in reed beds and other marshy sites with tall, dense vegetation, building its nest a little above the water level from whatever plants are available nearby. The off-white, blotched eggs are <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" title="Avian incubation" class="mw-redirect">incubated</a> mainly by the female, and the <a href="/wiki/Precocial" title="Precocial">precocial</a> downy chicks hatch in 19–22&#160;days. The female will defend her eggs and brood against intruders, or move them to another location if they are discovered. This species can breed after its first year, and it normally raises two <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutches</a> in each season. Water Rails are <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivorous</a>, although they feed mainly on animals. They are territorial even after breeding, and will aggressively defend feeding areas in winter.</p>
<p>These rails are vulnerable to flooding or freezing conditions, loss of habitat and predation by mammals and large birds. The introduced <a href="/wiki/American_Mink" title="American Mink" class="mw-redirect">American mink</a> has exterminated some island populations, but overall the species' huge range and large numbers mean that it is not considered to be threatened.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_Rail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rallidae" title="Rallidae">rails</a> are a bird <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> comprising nearly 150 species. Although the origins of the group are lost in antiquity, the largest number of species and the most primitive forms are found in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a>, suggesting that this family originated there. However, the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Rallus" title="Rallus">Rallus</a></i>, the group of long-billed reed bed specialists to which the Water Rail belongs, arose in the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>. Its Old World members, the Water, <a href="/wiki/African_Rail" title="African Rail">African</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madagascar_Rail" title="Madagascar Rail">Madagascar Rails</a>, form a <a href="/wiki/Superspecies" title="Superspecies">superspecies</a>, and are thought to have <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolved</a> from a single invasion from across the Atlantic.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor29_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor29-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">Genetic</a> evidence suggests that the Water Rail is the most closely related of its genus to the Pacific <i><a href="/wiki/Gallirallus" title="Gallirallus">Gallirallus</a></i> rails,<sup id="cite_ref-Christidis_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christidis-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and is <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basal</a> to that group.<sup id="cite_ref-Trewick_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trewick-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The Water Rail was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> in 1758 under its current scientific name, <i>Rallus aquaticus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> equivalent of the English "Water Rail".<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The subspecies <i>R.&#160;a.&#160;indicus</i> has, in addition to its distinctive plumage, very different vocalisations to the other subspecies, and it was considered a separate species in early works, including the first edition (1898) of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fauna_of_British_India,_Including_Ceylon_and_Burma" title="The Fauna of British India, Including Ceylon and Burma">Fauna of British India</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-blandford_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blandford-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> but later demoted to a subspecies by <a href="/wiki/E._C._Stuart_Baker" title="E. C. Stuart Baker">E. C. Stuart Baker</a> in the second edition (1929).<sup id="cite_ref-baker_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> It was restored as a full species, the Eastern Water Rail, <i>R.&#160;indicus</i>, by <a href="/wiki/Pamela_C._Rasmussen" title="Pamela C. Rasmussen">Pamela Rasmussen</a> in her <i><a href="/wiki/Birds_of_South_Asia._The_Ripley_Guide" title="Birds of South Asia. The Ripley Guide">Birds of South Asia</a></i> (2005). Rasmussen, an expert on Asian birds, also renamed the other forms as the Western Water Rail.<sup id="cite_ref-ras_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ras-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Her treatment has not otherwise been widely adopted, but is followed in <i>Birds of Malaysia and Singapore</i> (2010).<sup id="cite_ref-malaysia_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-malaysia-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> A 2010 study of molecular phylogeny further supported the possibility of specific status for <i>R.&#160;a.&#160;indicus</i>, which is estimated to have diverged from the western forms around 534,000 years ago. The paper also suggested that the differences between the three other races were <a href="/wiki/Cline_(biology)" title="Cline (biology)">clinal</a>, and that they should all be merged into <i>R.&#160;a.&#160;aquaticus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-tavares_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tavares-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_Rail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fossils">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Fossils">Fossils</span></h3>
<p>The oldest known <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> of an ancestral Water Rail are bones from <a href="/wiki/Carpathian_Mountains" title="Carpathian Mountains">Carpathia</a> dated to the <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a> (5.3–1.8 million years ago). By the late <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>, two million years ago, the fossil evidence suggests that the Water Rail was present across most of its present range.<sup id="cite_ref-tavares_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tavares-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> This species is surprisingly well recorded, with over 30 records from Bulgaria alone, and many others from across southern Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-boev2_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boev2-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-marco_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marco-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> and China.<sup id="cite_ref-lucas_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lucas-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> A rail from <a href="/wiki/Ibiza" title="Ibiza">Eivissa</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ibiza_Rail" title="Ibiza Rail">Rallus eivissensis</a></i>, was smaller but more robust than the Water Rail, and probably had poorer flight abilities. In the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">Quaternary</a>, the island lacked terrestrial mammals, and this distinctive form was presumably descended from its continental relative. It became extinct at about the same time as humans arrived on the island,<sup id="cite_ref-Mcminn_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mcminn-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> between 16,700 and 5,300 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-Alcover_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alcover-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The nominate race of Water Rail is now a very rare resident on Eivissa.<sup id="cite_ref-Kellner_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kellner-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_Rail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rallus_aquaticus_portrait.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Rallus_aquaticus_portrait.jpg/220px-Rallus_aquaticus_portrait.jpg" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rallus_aquaticus_portrait.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Head of nominate race</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The adult of the <a href="/wiki/Subspecies#Nomenclature" title="Subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> is a medium-sized rail, 23–28&#160;cm (9–11&#160;in) long with a 38–45&#160;cm (15–17.7&#160;in) wingspan. Males typically weigh 114–164 g (4.0–5.8 oz) and females are slightly lighter at 92–107 g (3.2–3.8 oz).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The upperparts from the forehead to tail are olive-brown with black streaks, especially on the shoulders. The sides of the head and the underparts down to the upper belly are dark slate-blue, except for a blackish area between bill and eye, and brownish sides to the upper breast. The flanks are barred black and white, and the undertail is white with some darker streaks. The long bill and the <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">iris</a> are red, and the legs are flesh-brown. The sexes are similar; although the female averages slightly smaller than the male, with a more slender bill,<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> determining sex through measurements alone is unreliable.<sup id="cite_ref-fuertes_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fuertes-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The juvenile has a blackish crown and a white chin and throat. The underparts are buff or white with darker bars, and the flank markings are brown and buff, rather than black and white. The undertail is buff, and the eye, bill and leg colours are duller than the adult. The downy chick is all black apart from a mainly white bill.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> After breeding, the rail has an extensive <a href="/wiki/Moulting" title="Moulting">moult</a>, and is flightless for about three weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Individual adults can be identified by the markings on the undertail, which are unique to each bird. Adult males have the strongest black undertail streaks.<sup id="cite_ref-ripley_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ripley-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> It has been suggested that the dark barring on the undertail of this species is a compromise between the signalling function of a pure white undertail, as found in open water or <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gregarious" class="extiw" title="wikt:gregarious">gregarious</a> species like the <a href="/wiki/Common_Moorhen" title="Common Moorhen">Common Moorhen</a>, and the need to avoid being too conspicuous.<sup id="cite_ref-Stang_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stang-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Water Rail can readily be distinguished from most other reed bed rails by its white undertail and red bill; the latter is a little longer than the rest of the rail's head (55–58% of the total) and slightly down-curved.<sup id="cite_ref-brown_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brown-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> The somewhat similar <a href="/wiki/Slaty-breasted_Rail" title="Slaty-breasted Rail">Slaty-breasted Rail</a> of tropical Asia has a stouter bill, a chestnut crown and white-spotted upperparts. Juvenile and freshly moulted Water Rails may show a buff undertail like <a href="/wiki/Spotted_Crake" title="Spotted Crake">Spotted Crake</a>, but that species' plumage is spotted with white, and it has a much shorter, mainly yellowish bill. The range of the Water Rail does not overlap with that of any other <i>Rallus</i> species, but vagrants could be distinguished from their American relatives by the lack of rufous or chestnut on the closed wing. The larger <a href="/wiki/African_Rail" title="African Rail">African Rail</a> has unstreaked darker brown upperparts and brighter red legs and feet.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_Rail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span></h3>
<p>The Water Rail is a vocal species which gives its main call, known as "sharming", throughout the year. This is a series of grunts followed by a high-pitched piglet-like squeal and ending in more grunts. It is used as a territorial call, alarm and announcement. Members of a pair may call alternately, the male giving lower and slower notes than his partner. The courtship song, given by both sexes, is a <i>tyick-tyick-tyick</i> often ending with a trill from the female; the male may sing for hours. The flight call is a sharp whistle, and other vocalizations include a loud repeated creak given by the male when showing the nest site to the female, and a purring given by both parents when at the nest with chicks. The rails are most vocal when setting up a territory and early in the breeding season, when calling may continue at night. Chicks initially cheep weakly, but soon develop a <i>tyk-tyk-trik</i> begging call.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The call of the subspecies <i>R. a. indicus</i> is quite different from that of the other forms. The courtship call, again given throughout the year, is a sharp piping <i>kyu</i>, longer and clearer than that of the European race. The song is a series of metallic slurred <i>shrink, shrink</i> notes, about two per second, and repeated after a short pause.<sup id="cite_ref-ras_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ras-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The eastern race does not respond to recorded announcement calls of nominate <i>R. a. aquaticus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Yamashina_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yamashina-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> When researchers played recordings of the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Reed_Warbler" title="Eurasian Reed Warbler">Reed Warbler</a> at night to attract that species for trapping, they found Water Rails and other wetland birds were also grounded, despite a lack of suitable habitat, suggesting that the rails and other nocturnal migrants recognised the warbler’s song and associated it with the marshy habitat in which it is usually found.<sup id="cite_ref-herremans_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-herremans-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_Rail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RallusIndicusNeale.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/RallusIndicusNeale.jpg/220px-RallusIndicusNeale.jpg" width="220" height="138" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RallusIndicusNeale.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>R.&#160;a.&#160;indicus</i> has a dark eye stripe. This 1880 illustration has the colour of the legs incorrectly depicted.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There are four recognised subspecies, one of which is particularly distinctive.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>R.&#160;a.&#160;aquaticus</i> (Linnaeus, 1758). This is the nominate subspecies that breeds in Europe, North Africa, Turkey, western Asia to the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a> and western Kazakhstan, and in a narrow band east to central <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>R.&#160;a.&#160;hibernans</i> (<a href="/wiki/Finn_Salomonsen" title="Finn Salomonsen">Salomonsen</a>, 1931). The Icelandic race, which has slightly warmer brown upperparts than the nominate form. The bars of the flanks are dark brown, not black, and the bill is somewhat shorter; the grey of the underparts may have a brown tinge.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>R.&#160;a.&#160;korejewi</i> (<a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Zarudny" title="Nikolai Zarudny">Zarudny</a>, 1905) (includes the dubious forms <i>deserticolor</i>, <i>tsaidamensis</i> and <i>arjanicus</i>). This subspecies breeds in south central Asia from southern and eastern Iran east to western China, and in the Indian subcontinent in <a href="/wiki/Kashmir" title="Kashmir">Kashmir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ladakh" title="Ladakh">Ladakh</a>. It is slightly larger than the nominate race, with paler brown upperparts and slightly paler slate underparts. It has a weak brown stripe through the eye.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ali_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ali-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>R.&#160;a.&#160;indicus</i> (<a href="/wiki/Edward_Blyth" title="Edward Blyth">Blyth</a>, 1849), (includes the dubious form <i>japonicus</i>). Also known as the Eastern Water Rail or Brown-cheeked Rail, this distinctive race breeds in northern Mongolia, eastern Siberia, northeast China, Korea and northern Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> It differs from the slightly smaller nominate form through its paler upperparts, brown-tinged underparts and a brown stripe through the eye. Compared to <i>R.&#160;a.&#160;korejewi</i>, it is darker above, has a browner breast, white on the throat and a more obvious brown eyestripe.<sup id="cite_ref-ali_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ali-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> As indicated above, it has different vocalisations to the other forms, and is sometimes given full species status, although its behaviour, nest and eggs are identical to those of the other races.<sup id="cite_ref-dresser_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dresser-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_Rail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Harilik_pilliroog.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Harilik_pilliroog.jpg/220px-Harilik_pilliroog.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Harilik_pilliroog.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Common reed is an important habitat for breeding and wintering.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Water Rail breeds across temperate Eurasia from Iceland and the British Isles discontinuously to Siberia, Korea, China and northern Japan, and in suitable habitat in North Africa, Saudi Arabia and Turkey. Its distribution in Asia is poorly studied, and may be more extensive than currently known.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Icelandic population of Water Rail, <i>R. a. hibernans</i>, became extinct around 1965, due to loss of habitat through the draining of wetlands, and predation by introduced <a href="/wiki/American_Mink" title="American Mink" class="mw-redirect">American mink</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tavares_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tavares-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Prior to its extinction, least some birds were present year-round on the island, relying on warm volcanic springs to survive through the coldest months, but this race was also found in winter in the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a> and Ireland, and on passage through the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Hebrides" title="Outer Hebrides">Western Isles</a>, suggesting that the Icelandic form was a partial migrant.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> The nominate subspecies, <i>R. a. aquaticus</i> is resident in the milder south and west of its range, but migrates south from areas that are subject to harsh winters. It winters within its breeding range, and also further south in North Africa, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a> area. The peak migration period is September to October, with most birds returning to the breeding grounds from March to mid-April.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> A specimen of the nominate population labelled as "<a href="/wiki/Balochistan" title="Balochistan">Baluchistan</a>" and collected by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Meinertzhagen" title="Richard Meinertzhagen">Richard Meinertzhagen</a> is considered of doubtful provenance.<sup id="cite_ref-ras_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ras-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>R. a. korejewi</i> is another partial migrant, with some of the population wintering from Iraq and eastern Saudi Arabia eastwards through Pakistan and northern India to western China.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The subspecies <i>R. a. indicus</i> is mainly migratory, wintering in southern Japan, eastern China and northern <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a>. It is uncommon in northern parts of Bangladesh,<sup id="cite_ref-ras_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ras-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Burma, Laos,<sup id="cite_ref-king_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-king-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> and northern and central Thailand,<sup id="cite_ref-Lekagul_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lekagul-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Robson_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robson-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> and does not normally reach further south in mainland southeast Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-king_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-king-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Migrants have been recorded on Sri Lanka in the past, although on the Indian mainland they are found mainly in the northern regions, with a few records from as far south as <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> On arrival in India, rails may be so exhausted that they can be caught by hand.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The breeding birds on the Japanese island of <a href="/wiki/Hokkaido" title="Hokkaido">Hokkaido</a> mostly migrate well south including to Korea but a few remain during winter in the coastal marshes of <a href="/wiki/Honshu" title="Honshu">Honshu</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-korea_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-korea-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-japan_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-japan-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rallusaquaticusnaumann.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Rallusaquaticusnaumann.jpg/220px-Rallusaquaticusnaumann.jpg" width="220" height="226" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rallusaquaticusnaumann.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
The downy black chicks can start finding food a few days after hatching.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The breeding habitat of the Water Rail is permanent wetland with still or slow-moving fresh or brackish water and dense, tall vegetation, which may include <a href="/wiki/Phragmites" title="Phragmites">common reed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Typha" title="Typha">reedmace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iris_(plant)" title="Iris (plant)">irises</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sparganium" title="Sparganium">bur-reed</a> or <a href="/wiki/Carex" title="Carex">sedges</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> In coastal areas, <a href="/wiki/Juncus_maritimus" title="Juncus maritimus">sea rush</a> is common in saltmarsh breeding sites, with sedges and bur-reed dominant in somewhat less saline environments. A study in The Netherlands and Spain showed that the rush provided better concealment than the other maritime plants. As elsewhere, nests were constructed from the nearest available plants.<sup id="cite_ref-DeKroon_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DeKroon-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> Where it occurs, <a href="/wiki/Cladium_mariscus" title="Cladium mariscus">saw-sedge</a> provides good breeding habitat, its tall (1.5&#160;m, 5&#160;ft) dense structure providing good cover for the nesting rails.<sup id="cite_ref-Mommers_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mommers-38"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> The preferred habitat is <i><a href="/wiki/Phragmites" title="Phragmites">Phragmites</a></i> reedbed with the plants standing in water,<sup id="cite_ref-jenkins_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jenkins-39"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> with a depth of 5–30&#160;cm (2–12&#160;in), muddy areas for feeding and a rich diversity of <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrate</a> species. Locations with nearby willows or shrubs are favoured above large areas of uniform habitat. In addition to natural fresh or marine marshes, this rail may use gravel or clay excavations and peat workings as long as there is suitable habitat with good cover. It may be found in rice paddies or on floating islands,<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> and it occurs in Kashmir in flooded sugarcane fields.<sup id="cite_ref-ali_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ali-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> A Finnish study showed that the main factor influencing the distribution of Water Rails was the extent of vegetation cover, with the highest densities in the most vegetated areas; the presence of other marshes nearby was also significant. However, factors such as temperature, rainfall, length of shore line and extent of <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a>, important for some other marsh birds, were not statistically relevant. The areas with the highest densities of the rail also had the greatest numbers of three species considered at risk in Finland, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Reed_Warbler" title="Great Reed Warbler">Great Reed Warbler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Bittern" title="Eurasian Bittern">Eurasian Bittern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Marsh_Harrier" title="Western Marsh Harrier">Marsh Harrier</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-virkkala_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-virkkala-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> The northern limit of breeding seems to be determined by the transition from nutrient-rich wetland to poorer, more acidic water. This leads to the replacement of common reed by a more open vegetation type dominated by <a href="/wiki/Comarum_palustre" title="Comarum palustre">marsh cinquefoil</a>, which is unsuitable for the rails.<sup id="cite_ref-61N_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61N-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Occasionally, more unusual locations are used. One pair in Scotland nested in the open by the side of a road, and when an English <a href="/wiki/Nature_reserve" title="Nature reserve">nature reserve</a> installed nest boxes for <a href="/wiki/Bearded_Reedling" title="Bearded Reedling">Bearded Tits</a> (reed "<a href="/wiki/Wigwam" title="Wigwam">wigwams</a>" with a wooden floor), rails nested both in the boxes and under the wooden floor, in the latter case sometimes with the tits in residence above.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-42"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Although mainly a lowland species, the Water Rail breeds at 1,240&#160;m (4,070&#160;ft) in the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> and 2,000&#160;m (6,560&#160;ft) in Armenia.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> An Italian study suggested that reed bed birds need a minimum area of wetland for breeding, which for the Water Rail is about 1&#160;ha (2.47&#160;acres),<sup id="cite_ref-ijz_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ijz-43"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> although the highest densities are in marshes of 10&#160;ha (25&#160;acres) or more.<sup id="cite_ref-virkkala_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-virkkala-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On migration and in winter, a wider range of wet habitats may be used, including flooded thickets or bracken. Freezing condition may force birds into more open locations such as ditches, rubbish dumps and gardens,<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> or even out onto exposed ice.<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-44"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> A Welsh study suggested that individual winter territories overlap, with each bird using a significant proportion of the reed bed. After site desertion in freezing weather, birds return to their former range. A density of 14 birds per hectare (6.6 per acre) was recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenkins3_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenkins3-45"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> Birds wintering in Iceland rely on warm <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_gradient" title="Geothermal gradient">geothermal</a> streams, and may access streams through tunnels under the snow. When not feeding, they may shelter in holes and crevices in the solidified <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> This species sometimes wanders well outside its normal range and <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrants</a> have been found in the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>, Mauritania, the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, Malaysia and Vietnam.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_Rail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<p>This rail is a skulking species, its streaked plumage making it difficult to see in its wetland habitat. Its laterally compressed body allows it to slip though the densest vegetation, and it will "freeze" if surprised in the open.<sup id="cite_ref-Coward_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-46"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> It walks with a high-stepping gait, although it adopts a crouch when it runs for cover. It swims, when necessary, with the jerky motion typical of rails,<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_29-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> and it flies short distances low over the reeds with its long legs dangling. Although their flight looks weak, Water Rails are capable of long sustained flights during their nocturnal migrations,<sup id="cite_ref-ali_27-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ali-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> and are sometimes killed in collisions with lighthouses or wires.<sup id="cite_ref-Coward_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-46"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> British-ringed birds have been recovered from as far away as Poland, Czechoslovakia and Sweden.<sup id="cite_ref-glue_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glue-47"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species defends its breeding and wintering territories. Birds will charge each other with neck outstretched when breeding, sometimes both members of a pair attacking together. Large strongly-marked males are dominant in winter, when the direct aggression is replaced by sharming while standing upright on tip-toe, head jerking and bill thrusting.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_Rail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:187px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rallusaquaticusegg.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/83/Rallusaquaticusegg.png" width="185" height="184" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rallusaquaticusegg.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Egg</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Water Rail is <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a> and highly territorial when breeding. The birds pair off after arriving at their nesting areas, or possibly even before spring migration. In large wetlands with good conditions birds may nest 20–50&#160;m (22–55&#160;yd) apart. Territories vary in size, but 300&#160;m<sup>2</sup> (360&#160;yd<sup>2</sup>) is typical. The pair give courting and contact calls throughout the breeding season. The male selects the nest site which he shows to the female while posturing with raised back feathers, wings arched over his back, tail spread and bill pointed vertically downwards. This display is accompanied by a loud call. Before mating, he raises his wings and tail, and bows with his bill touching his breast. The male feeds the female during courtship, and, when incubating, she may leave the nest to display to the male, walking round him, calling softly, rubbing her bill against his and taking short runs to and from him.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The nest is made from whatever wetland vegetation is available and built mostly by the male, usually in a single day. It is raised 15&#160;cm (6&#160;in) or more above the level of the marsh, and is sometimes constructed on clumps of roots, tree stumps or similar support. It may be built up higher if the marsh waters start to rise. The nest is 13–16&#160;cm (5–7&#160;in) across and about 7&#160;cm (3&#160;in high). It is well hidden and approached cautiously by narrow tracks.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Coward_46-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-46"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-seebohm_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seebohm-48"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> The average weight of wind-dried nests of <i>R. a. indicus</i> in Japan was 95&#160;g (3.4&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-Yamashina_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yamashina-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The typical <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is 6–11 eggs across most of the range,<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BWP_29-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> but appears to be smaller (5–8) in Kashmir at around 1,500&#160;m (4,900&#160;ft) altitude.<sup id="cite_ref-ali_27-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ali-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Laying dates vary with location, from late March in western Europe and North Africa, to late May in Kashmir and June in Iceland.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The clutch size may be smaller early or late in the breeding season.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenkins2_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenkins2-49"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> The breeding season can be extended by replacement and second clutches. The eggs are blunt and oval, smooth and slightly glossy; the colour varies from off-white to pink-buff, with reddish-brown blotches mainly at the broader end<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_29-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> that sometimes merging into a single patch.<sup id="cite_ref-seebohm_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seebohm-48"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> Variation in egg size across the four subspecies is much less than differences between individual eggs; the average size of the eggs of the nominate subspecies, 36&#160;×&#160;26&#160;mm, (1.4&#160;×&#160;1.0&#160;in) is therefore typical for the species as a whole. The eggs weigh about 13&#160;g, (0.46&#160;oz),<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> of which 7% is shell.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Both parents incubate the eggs, although the female takes the larger share of this duty.<sup id="cite_ref-ali_27-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ali-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> The eggs are incubated for 19–22&#160;days to hatching,<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> with at least 87% success,<sup id="cite_ref-glue_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glue-47"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Precocial" title="Precocial">precocial</a>, downy young <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> in another 20–30&#160;days.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Food is brought to the nest by the other adult and passed to the sitting parent who feeds the chicks, although they also find some of their own food after about five days. After fledging, the young birds fend for themselves. They can fly when aged 7–9&#160;weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> If a nest appears to have been discovered, the female may carry the chicks or eggs one by one to another location; the eggs are carried in the bill,<sup id="cite_ref-Coward_46-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-46"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> but small chicks may be tucked under the wing.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-42"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Sitting birds may stay on the eggs even when approached closely, or attack the intruder, or feign injury as a distraction.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The Water Rail can breed after its first year, and it normally raises two broods.</p>
<p>Average survival after fledging has been estimated as between 17 and 20&#160;months, with an annual survival rate slightly less than 50% per year for the first three years, and somewhat higher thereafter.<sup id="cite_ref-glue_47-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glue-47"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> The maximum recorded age is 8&#160;years 10&#160;months.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_Rail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Parkgatemarsh.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Parkgatemarsh.jpg/220px-Parkgatemarsh.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Parkgatemarsh.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
The marshes at <a href="/wiki/Parkgate,_Cheshire" title="Parkgate, Cheshire">Parkgate, Cheshire</a> may hold hundreds of Water Rails in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-lack_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lack-50"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Water Rails are <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivorous</a>, although they mainly feed on animals. These include <a href="/wiki/Leech" title="Leech">leeches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worm" title="Worm">worms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gastropoda" title="Gastropoda">gastropods</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, spiders, and a wide range of both terrestrial and aquatic insects and their <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a>. Small <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>, fish, birds and mammals may be killed or eaten as <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">carrion</a>. Vertebrates are impaled with a strike of the bill which breaks the prey's spinal cord. Plant food, which is consumed more in autumn and winter, includes the buds, seeds, flowers, shoots and seeds of water plants, berries and fruit.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> In south Asia, paddy (harvested <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">kernels</a>) may sometimes be eaten.<sup id="cite_ref-ali_27-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ali-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> The young rails are fed mainly on insects and spiders. Food obtained on land or from mud is normally washed in water before it is eaten.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> After rain, rails may probe soft ground for earthworms.<sup id="cite_ref-ripley_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ripley-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> This species will occasionally feed in the open even when not forced to do so by cold weather; <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Meade-Waldo" title="Edmund Meade-Waldo">Edmund Meade-Waldo</a> described seven rails feeding in an open meadow.<sup id="cite_ref-m-w_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-m-w-51"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> Despite its skulking nature, the water Rail appears to thrive in captivity when fed on animal food such as raw meat or earthworms;<sup id="cite_ref-yarrell_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yarrell-52"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> one individual was taught to jump for worms suspended from a fishing rod.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_42-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-42"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Water Rails follow definite routes when feeding, frequently returning to good hunting areas. These rails are versatile and opportunist foragers. They will jump to take insects from plants, climb to find berries, or dislodge apples from trees so they can be eaten on the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> They will kill birds by impaling or drowning them, particularly if the bird's ability to escape is restricted. They have been recorded as killing a <a href="/wiki/European_Greenfinch" title="European Greenfinch">European Greenfinch</a> and a <a href="/wiki/King_Quail" title="King Quail">King Quail</a> in an <a href="/wiki/Aviary" title="Aviary">aviary</a>, and small birds trapped in <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">bird ringers'</a> <a href="/wiki/Mist_net" title="Mist net">mist nets</a>. One bird killed <a href="/wiki/Twite" title="Twite">Twite</a> caught with it in a <a href="/wiki/Heligoland_trap" title="Heligoland trap">Heligoland trap</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_42-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-42"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> They are also nest predators, particularly of small birds that nest in reeds such as the <a href="/wiki/Great_Reed_Warbler" title="Great Reed Warbler">Great Reed Warbler</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> Water Rails may defend a winter feeding territory, although this is smaller than when breeding, with individuals perhaps less than 10&#160;m (11&#160;yd) apart;<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> favoured sites may hold hundreds of birds.<sup id="cite_ref-lack_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lack-50"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> Aggressive behaviour outside the breeding season may extend to attacks on other marsh rails such as <a href="/wiki/Spotted_Crake" title="Spotted Crake">Spotted</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baillon%27s_Crake" title="Baillon's Crake">Baillon's Crake</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ciach_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ciach-54"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_Rail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Predators and parasites">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Predators_and_parasites">Predators and parasites</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Borrelia_burgdorferi_(CDC-PHIL_-6631)_lores.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Borrelia_burgdorferi_%28CDC-PHIL_-6631%29_lores.jpg/220px-Borrelia_burgdorferi_%28CDC-PHIL_-6631%29_lores.jpg" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Borrelia_burgdorferi_(CDC-PHIL_-6631)_lores.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>Borrelia burgdorferi</i> spirochaetes shown by <a href="/wiki/Dark-field_microscopy" title="Dark-field microscopy" class="mw-redirect">dark-field microscopy</a>. Rails can be infected with the <i><a href="/wiki/Borrelia_burgdorferi" title="Borrelia burgdorferi">Borrelia burgdorferi</a></i>, which can be transmitted to humans via tick bites causing <a href="/wiki/Lyme_disease" title="Lyme disease">Lyme disease</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Predators of the Water Rail include a number of mammals and large birds. The American mink was partly responsible for the extinction of the Icelandic population,<sup id="cite_ref-tavares_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tavares-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> and cats and dogs have also been recorded as killing this species.<sup id="cite_ref-glue_47-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glue-47"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> At least locally, <a href="/wiki/European_Otter" title="European Otter" class="mw-redirect">otters</a> will also eat rails and other water birds.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> The Eurasian Bittern, another reed bed bird, will consume rails,<sup id="cite_ref-zls_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zls-57"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> as will <a href="/wiki/Grey_Heron" title="Grey Heron">Grey Herons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Coward85_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward85-58"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> Water Rails are particularly vulnerable to the heron when forced out of the cover of the reeds by very high tides.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_42-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-42"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Wetland-hunting <a href="/wiki/Harrier_(bird)" title="Harrier (bird)">harriers</a> are predictable predators,<sup id="cite_ref-clarke_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clarke-59"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> but more unusually, the rail has also been recorded as a prey item of the <a href="/wiki/Tawny_Owl" title="Tawny Owl">Tawny Owl</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Yalden_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yalden-60"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Short-eared_Owl" title="Short-eared Owl">Short-eared Owl</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-glue2_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glue2-61"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Eagle-owl" title="Eurasian Eagle-owl" class="mw-redirect">Eurasian Eagle-owl</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Bayle_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bayle-62"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Greater_Spotted_Eagle" title="Greater Spotted Eagle">Greater Spotted Eagle</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Alivizatos_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alivizatos-63"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Common_Kestrel" title="Common Kestrel">Common Kestrel</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-cave_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cave-64"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> and night-hunting <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_Falcon" title="Peregrine Falcon">Peregrine Falcons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dwt_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dwt-65"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Parasites include the <a href="/wiki/Sucking_louse" title="Sucking louse">sucking lice</a> <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nirmus_cuspidiculus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nirmus cuspidiculus (page does not exist)">Nirmus cuspidiculus</a></i> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pediculus_ralli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pediculus ralli (page does not exist)">Pediculus ralli</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-denny_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-denny-66"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-clay_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clay-67"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Tick" title="Tick">tick</a> <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ixodes_frontalis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ixodes frontalis (page does not exist)">Ixodes frontalis</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-tick_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tick-68"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Hippoboscidae" title="Hippoboscidae">louse fly</a> <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ornithomyia_avicularia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ornithomyia avicularia (page does not exist)">Ornithomyia avicularia</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-hill_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hill-69"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> The Water Rail can be infected by the <a href="/wiki/Avian_influenza" title="Avian influenza">avian influenza</a> virus<sup id="cite_ref-aiv_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aiv-70"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fereidouni_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fereidouni-71"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Bacterium" title="Bacterium" class="mw-redirect">bacterium</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Borrelia_burgdorferi" title="Borrelia burgdorferi">Borrelia burgdorferi</a></i>, carried by <i><a href="/wiki/Ixodes" title="Ixodes">Ixodes</a></i> ticks, which is also a human <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogen</a> causing <a href="/wiki/Lyme_disease" title="Lyme disease">Lyme disease</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> Three <a href="/wiki/Louse" title="Louse">lice</a>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fulicoffula_rallina&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fulicoffula rallina (page does not exist)">Fulicoffula rallina</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pseudomenopon_scopulacorne&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pseudomenopon scopulacorne (page does not exist)">Pseudomenopon scopulacorne</a></i> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rallicola_cuspidatus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rallicola cuspidatus (page does not exist)">Rallicola cuspidatus</a></i> discovered on dead Water Rails in 2005 on the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a> were all species that had not been found on the <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> previously.<sup id="cite_ref-Palma_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palma-73"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Cyclocoelidae" title="Cyclocoelidae">parasitic flatworm</a> <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ophthalmophagus_nasciola&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ophthalmophagus nasciola (page does not exist)">Ophthalmophagus nasciola</a></i> was found in one rail's nasal <a href="/wiki/Sinus_(anatomy)" title="Sinus (anatomy)">sinus</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-drone_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-drone-74"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup> and at least three species of <a href="/wiki/Feather_mite" title="Feather mite">feather mite</a> have been detected on the plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-Atye_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Atye-75"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup> The louse <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philopterus_ralli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Philopterus ralli (page does not exist)">Philopterus ralli</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/Nematode" title="Nematode">nematode</a> <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strongyloides_avium&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Strongyloides avium (page does not exist)">Strongyloides avium</a></i> have been found on the subspecies <i>R. a. indicus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Uchida_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Uchida-77"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup> The eastern subspecies is also one of the many intermediate hosts of another nematode, <i><a href="/wiki/Gnathostoma_spinigerum" title="Gnathostoma spinigerum">Gnathostoma spinigerum</a></i>, which causes human <a href="/wiki/Gnathostomiasis" title="Gnathostomiasis">gnathostomiasis</a>, a disease found in Thailand, Japan and other parts of Southeast Asia. The nematode's first-stage hosts are <a href="/wiki/Copepod" title="Copepod">copepods</a> of the genus <a href="/wiki/Cyclops_(genus)" title="Cyclops (genus)"><i>Cyclops</i></a>, which are consumed directly or indirectly by a wide variety of vertebrates before maturing in the third and final host, a carnivorous mammal. The rail is therefore unlikely to be a source of human gnathostomiasis, which is normally caused by consuming raw or undercooked infected poultry, pork, freshwater fish, or by drinking water contaminated with infected <i>Cyclops</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rusnak_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rusnak-79"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_Rail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Norka_ameryka%C5%84ska_(_neovison_vison_).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Norka_ameryka%C5%84ska_%28_neovison_vison_%29.jpg/220px-Norka_ameryka%C5%84ska_%28_neovison_vison_%29.jpg" width="220" height="123" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Norka_ameryka%C5%84ska_(_neovison_vison_).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Introduced predictors, such as the <a href="/wiki/American_Mink" title="American Mink" class="mw-redirect">American mink</a>, have caused marked declines in populations of rails and other ground-nesting birds.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Water Rail's numbers are declining, but it has a large population of 100,000–1,000,000 adults and a huge breeding range estimated at 15,600,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (6,000,000&#160;mi<sup>2</sup>); it is therefore classed as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> In most European countries, the population is either stable or decreasing slightly due to loss of habitat. The rail's range and numbers are increasing in Morocco, with breeding as far south as <a href="/wiki/Souss-Massa_National_Park" title="Souss-Massa National Park">Oued Massa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_29-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Little is known about the Asian range, but <i>korejewi</i> is a common breeder in Pakistan and Kashmir,<sup id="cite_ref-ras_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ras-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> and <i>indicus</i> is uncommon in Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor293_19-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor293-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Introduced predators are a threat to vulnerable island populations. In addition to the <a href="/wiki/Local_extinction" title="Local extinction">extirpation</a> of the Icelandic race, mink have been responsible for marked declines in the populations of Water Rails and other ground-nesting birds in the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a>, where the mainly fish-eating otter was the only native <a href="/wiki/Carnivore" title="Carnivore">carnivore</a>. The mink derived from <a href="/wiki/Fur_farming" title="Fur farming">fur farms</a> on Lewis,<sup id="cite_ref-nz_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nz-80"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup> from whence they spread through <a href="/wiki/Harris,_Outer_Hebrides" title="Harris, Outer Hebrides" class="mw-redirect">Harris</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Uist" title="North Uist">North Uist</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Uist" title="South Uist">South Uist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ohbp_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ohbp-81"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup> Mink and <a href="/wiki/Ferret" title="Ferret">ferret</a> eradication programmes have enabled the rail to return to islands including Lewis and Harris,<sup id="cite_ref-snh_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snh-82"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup> and further projects are ongoing or planned on the Scottish mainland.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-83"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup> Locally, habitat may be affected by the drainage of marshes, canalisation of water courses and urban encroachment,<sup id="cite_ref-newatlas_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newatlas-84"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup> or by pollution.<sup id="cite_ref-Carpene_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carpene-85"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Water Rails have been eaten by humans for thousands of years;<sup id="cite_ref-boev_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boev-86"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup> they were eaten by the Romans,<sup id="cite_ref-jones_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jones-87"><span>[</span>88<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Grimm_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grimm-88"><span>[</span>89<span>]</span></a></sup> and depicted in wall paintings at <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Watson_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson-89"><span>[</span>90<span>]</span></a></sup> and consumption continued through the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> to modern times.<sup id="cite_ref-japan_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-japan-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Woolgar_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Woolgar-90"><span>[</span>91<span>]</span></a></sup> However, because of the bird's habitat and skulking nature, hunting appears not to be a serious threat. In Japan the Ministry of Agriculture and Forestry reported that "only about 50,000" were killed annually in the mid-20th century, although this number was thought to include several other species of wetland birds.<sup id="cite_ref-japan_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-japan-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
