<p>The <b>Willow Warbler</b> (<i>Phylloscopus trochilus</i>) is a very common and widespread <a href="/wiki/Leaf_warbler" title="Leaf warbler">leaf warbler</a> which breeds throughout northern and temperate Europe and Asia, from <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> east to the <a href="/wiki/Anadyr_River" title="Anadyr River">Anadyr River</a> basin in eastern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. It is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, with almost all of the population wintering in sub-Saharan Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Baker_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of open woodlands with trees and ground cover for nesting, including most importantly <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alder" title="Alder">alder</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willow</a> habitats. The nest is usually built in close contact with the ground, often in low vegetation. Like most <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warblers" title="Old World warblers" class="mw-redirect">Old World warblers</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sylviidae" title="Sylviidae">Sylviidae</a>), this small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> is <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" title="Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect">insectivorous</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> In northern Europe, it is one of the first <a href="/wiki/Warbler" title="Warbler">warblers</a> to return in the spring though is later than the closely related <a href="/wiki/Common_Chiffchaff" title="Common Chiffchaff">Chiffchaff</a> <i>Phylloscopus collybita</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Willow_Warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>It is a typical leaf warbler in appearance, 11â12.5&#160;cm long and 7â15 g weight. It is greenish brown above and off-white to yellowish below; the wings are plain greenish-brown with no wingbars. Juveniles are yellower below than adults. It is very similar to the Chiffchaff, but non-singing birds can be distinguished from that species by their paler pinkish-yellow legs (dark brown to blackish in Chiffchaff), longer paler bill, more elegant shape and longer primary projection (wingtip). Its song is a simple repetitive descending whistle, while the contact call is a disyllabic 'hoo-eet', distinct from the more monosyllabic 'hweet' of Chiffchaffs.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Baker_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Snow_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Willow_Warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span></h2>
<p>Three <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are accepted, with a partly <a href="/wiki/Cline_(biology)" title="Cline (biology)">clinal</a> reduction in green and yellow plumage tones from west to east, with central birds browner and easternmost birds predominantly greyish:<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>Phylloscopus trochilus trochilus</i> (Linnaeus, 1758). Breeds Europe (from the Pyrenees and Alps northward) except northern Scandinavia, winters west Africa.</li>
<li><i>Phylloscopus trochilus acredula</i> (Linnaeus, 1758). Breeds northern Scandinavia east to western Siberia, winters central Africa.</li>
<li><i>Phylloscopus trochilus yakutensis</i> (Ticehurst, 1935). Breeds eastern Siberia, winters eastern and southern Africa.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Willow_Warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<p>All populations are highly migratory, with the subspecies <i>P. t. yakutensis</i> migrating up to 12,000&#160;km from eastern Siberia to southern Africa along the <a href="/wiki/Asian_-_East_African_Flyway" title="Asian - East African Flyway" class="mw-redirect">Asian - East African Flyway</a>, one of the longest migrations of any for a bird of its size.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Snow_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Approximate timings are:</p>
<ul>
<li>October to March: wintering in sub Saharan Africa.</li>
<li>Mid March to mid May: migrates and arrives in the breeding range.</li>
<li>Late April to August: breeding season, usually only one brood but rarely two.</li>
<li>August to October: migrates back to Africa.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Willow_Warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Status and conservation">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status_and_conservation">Status and conservation</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WillowWarbler.jpg" class="image"><img alt="WillowWarbler.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/WillowWarbler.jpg/220px-WillowWarbler.jpg" width="220" height="211" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WillowWarbler.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Willow Warblers prefer young, open, scrubby <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodland</a> with small trees, including human-altered habitats such as <a href="/wiki/Coppice" title="Coppice" class="mw-redirect">coppice</a> and young <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantations</a> up to 10â20 years old. High amounts of birch, alder and willow, with good <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichen</a> amounts, and water features (e.g. streams), fields with large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Bracken" title="Bracken">bracken</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mosses" title="Mosses" class="mw-redirect">mosses</a>, and patches of low <a href="/wiki/Bramble" title="Bramble">bramble</a> (for nest cover) are preferred, but it will use a wide range of other species, including young or open <a href="/wiki/Pinophyta" title="Pinophyta">coniferous</a> forests.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Incorporating woodland ride edge thickets is beneficial, as is 15 metre woodland edges of varying structure and height. They prefer damp woodland areas. Thicket forming shrubs like <a href="/wiki/Blackthorn" title="Blackthorn" class="mw-redirect">blackthorn</a> provide pockets of habitat. <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">Deer</a> browsing can degrade the required low cover.</p>
<p>The highest population densities are found in Scandinavia (where it is the commonest bird of any), with up to 1,100 pairs per square kilometre, and a total population in Sweden and Finland of 24 million pairs. Lower densities occur further east, with peak densities of 27 pairs per square kilometre in central Siberia. Even lower densities are found on the southern edge of the breeding range, with just 9 pairs per square kilometre in Switzerland, and a total of just 100 pairs in the whole of northern Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In England this species has on average decreased in population by 70% within the last 25 years, with the biggest declines in the southeast. In Scotland some increases have occurred. The <a href="/wiki/Forestry_Commission" title="Forestry Commission">Forestry Commission</a> offers grants under a scheme called England's Woodland Improvement Grant (EWIG); as does <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural Englands</a> <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Stewardship" title="Environmental Stewardship" class="mw-redirect">Environmental Stewardship</a> Scheme.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Willow_Warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>The Willow Warbler was first scientifically described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> in 1758 under the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Motacilla" title="Motacilla" class="mw-redirect">Motacilla</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> and then transferred to the genus <i>Phylloscopus</i> (of which it is the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a>) by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Boie" title="Friedrich Boie">Boie</a> in 1826.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Before the English name was standardised to Willow Warbler by <a href="/wiki/William_Yarrell" title="William Yarrell">William Yarrell</a> in 1843, it was sometimes called "willow wren".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
