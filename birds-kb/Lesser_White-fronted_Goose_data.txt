<p>The <b>lesser white-fronted goose</b> (<i>Anser erythropus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Goose" title="Goose">goose</a> closely related to the larger <a href="/wiki/White-fronted_goose" class="mw-redirect" title="White-fronted goose">white-fronted goose</a> (<i>A. albifrons</i>). It breeds in the northernmost <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>, but it is a scarce breeder in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. There is a re-introduction scheme in <a href="/wiki/Fennoscandia" title="Fennoscandia">Fennoscandia</a>. The scientific name comes from <i>anser</i>, the Latin for "goose", and <i>erythropus</i>, "red-footed", derived from the old Greek <i>eruthros</i> "red" and <i>pous</i> "foot".<sup id="cite_ref-job90_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job90-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The lesser white-fronted goose winters further south in Europe and is a rare winter vagrant to <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Individual birds formerly appeared regularly at <a href="/wiki/WWT_Slimbridge" title="WWT Slimbridge">WWT Slimbridge</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a>, England, where they inspired <a href="/wiki/Sir_Peter_Scott" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Peter Scott">Sir Peter Scott</a> to set up The <a href="/wiki/Wildfowl_and_Wetlands_Trust" class="mw-redirect" title="Wildfowl and Wetlands Trust">Wildfowl and Wetlands Trust</a>—modern records, however, are far less frequent, a consequence of the species' decline on its <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> breeding grounds. An attractive species, it is also widely kept in wildfowl collections and, as a result, escapes do occur; individuals seen in <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a>, or in the company of other <a href="/wiki/Feral_animal" class="mw-redirect" title="Feral animal">feral</a> geese, are likely to be of captive origin.
</p><p>The two white-fronted goose species differ little other than in size (the lesser, at 53–66&#160;cm (21–26&#160;in) length and with a 120–135&#160;cm (47–53&#160;in) wingspan, is not much bigger than a <a href="/wiki/Mallard" title="Mallard">mallard</a> (<i>Anas platyrhynchos</i>) but both may be readily distinguished from the <a href="/wiki/Greylag_goose" title="Greylag goose">greylag goose</a> by their bright orange legs and their mouse-coloured upper wing-coverts. The greylag goose has a flesh-coloured bill and legs, and the upper wing-coverts are bluish-grey.
</p><p>Both white-front species have a very conspicuous white face and broad black bars which cross the belly.
</p><p>Adult lesser white-fronted geese, as well as being smaller than white-fronted geese, have an obvious yellow eye-ring, and the white facial blaze goes up to the crown.
</p><p>The lesser white-fronted goose is considered an <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered species</a>, but there are programmes to <a href="/wiki/Reintroduction" class="mw-redirect" title="Reintroduction">reintroduce</a> animals into the wild to strengthen the population. Additionally it is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fennoscandian_population">Fennoscandian population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_white-fronted_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Fennoscandian population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This genetically distinct population is now estimated at about 20 breeding pairs or 60–80 total individuals at most. They breed in Northern Norway and overwinter in Greece, Bulgaria and Turkey. There is a major stopover site at <a href="/wiki/Hortob%C3%A1gy_National_Park" title="Hortobágy National Park">Hortobágy National Park</a>, Hungary where the birds spend up to two months during autumn and one month during the spring migration.<sup id="cite_ref-Lengyel2012_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lengyel2012-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another part of the Fennoscandian population breeds in Northern Sweden. The population size in 2015 is estimated to about 15 breeding pairs or 40-50 individuals in all. These birds follow a western migration route and spend the winter in Netherlands and Germany. According to IUCN Red LIst in 2015 the conservation status of this population is Critically Endangered.
</p>
