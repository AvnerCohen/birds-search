<p>The <b>European turtle dove</b> (<i>Streptopelia turtur</i>) is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> family <a href="/wiki/Columbidae" title="Columbidae">Columbidae</a>, the doves and pigeons. It breeds over a wide area of the south western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> including north Africa but migrates to northern sub-Saharan Africa to winter.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_turtle_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European turtle dove was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">formally described</a> by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">tenth edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>. He placed it with all the other pigeons in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Columba" title="Columba">Columba</a></i> and coined the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Columba turtur</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Specific_epithet" class="mw-redirect" title="Specific epithet">specific epithet</a> <i>turtur</i> is the Latin word for a turtle dove.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJobling2010393_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJobling2010393-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Linnaeus gave the locality as "India". This was an error and the <a href="/wiki/Type_locality_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Type locality (biology)">type locality</a> has been designated as England.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The species is now placed in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Streptopelia" title="Streptopelia">Streptopelia</a></i> that was introduced in 1855 by the French ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lucien_Bonaparte" title="Charles Lucien Bonaparte">Charles Lucien Bonaparte</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ioc_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Four <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are recognised:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>northern turtle dove</b> (<i>S. t. turtur</i>) (<a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, 1758) – Europe, Madeira and the Canary Islands to western Siberia</li>
<li><b>Persian turtle dove</b> (<i>S. t. arenicola</i>) (<a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a>, 1894) – Northwest Africa to Iran and western China</li>
<li><b>Saharan turtle dove</b> (<i>S. t. hoggara</i>) (<a href="/wiki/Hans_Freiherr_Geyr_von_Schweppenburg" title="Hans Freiherr Geyr von Schweppenburg">Geyr von Schweppenburg</a>, 1916) – <a href="/wiki/A%C3%AFr_Mountains" title="Aïr Mountains">Air Massif</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hoggar_Mountains" title="Hoggar Mountains">Hoggar Mountains</a>  (southern Sahara)</li>
<li><b>Egyptian turtle dove</b> (<i>S. t. rufescens</i>) (<a href="/wiki/Christian_Ludwig_Brehm" title="Christian Ludwig Brehm">Brehm, CL</a>, 1845) – Egypt and northern Sudan</li></ul>
<p>Despite the identical spelling, the "turtle" of the name, derived from Middle English <i>turtle</i> (<i lang="enm" title="Middle English (1100-1500) language text">tortle</i>, <i lang="enm" title="Middle English (1100-1500) language text">turtel</i>, <i lang="enm" title="Middle English (1100-1500) language text">turtul</i>), derived from Old English <i lang="ang" title="Old English (ca. 450-1100) language text">turtla</i> (male turtle dove), <i>turtle</i> (female turtle dove), ultimately derived from Latin <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">turtur</i>, has no connection with the <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptile</a>; "turtle" in this case came originally from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>turtur</i>, <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeic" class="mw-redirect" title="Onomatopoeic">onomatopoeic</a> for the song.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OED2_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The genus name <i>Streptopelia</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>streptos</i> meaning "collar" and <i>peleia</i> meaning "dove".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJobling2010367_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJobling2010367-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A few other doves in the genus <i>Streptopelia</i> are also commonly called 'turtle doves':
</p>
<ul><li>the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Asian</a> <a href="/wiki/Oriental_turtle_dove" title="Oriental turtle dove">Oriental turtle dove</a> <i>S. orientalis</i> and <a href="/wiki/Red_collared_dove" title="Red collared dove">red collared dove</a> <i>S. tranquebarica</i>.</li>
<li>the African <a href="/wiki/Dusky_turtle_dove" title="Dusky turtle dove">dusky turtle dove</a> <i>S. lugens</i> and <a href="/wiki/Adamawa_turtle_dove" title="Adamawa turtle dove">Adamawa turtle dove</a> <i>S. hypopyrrha</i>.</li></ul>
<p>A few other species from the related <i><a href="/wiki/Nesoenas" title="Nesoenas">Nesoenas</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Spilopelia" title="Spilopelia">Spilopelia</a></i> genera, which were both formerly included in the genus, also possess common names containing the term 'turtle dove'.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_turtle_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2006_1031turturduva0224.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/2006_1031turturduva0224.JPG/220px-2006_1031turturduva0224.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/2006_1031turturduva0224.JPG/330px-2006_1031turturduva0224.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/2006_1031turturduva0224.JPG/440px-2006_1031turturduva0224.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2848" data-file-height="2136" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2006_1031turturduva0224.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Juvenile photographed in <a href="/wiki/Nyn%C3%A4shamn" title="Nynäshamn">Nynäshamn</a>, Sweden</div></div></div>
<p>Smaller and slighter in build than many other <a href="/wiki/Dove" class="mw-redirect" title="Dove">doves</a>, it measures 26–28&#160;cm (10–11&#160;in) in length, 47–53&#160;cm (19–21&#160;in) in wingspan and weighs 100–156&#160;g (3.5–5.5&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1985353,_362_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1985353,_362-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The European turtle dove may be recognised by its browner colour, and the black-and-white-striped patch on the side of its neck. The tail is notable as the bird flies from the observer; it is wedge shaped, with a dark centre and white borders and tips. When viewed from below, this pattern, owing to the white under-tail coverts obscuring the dark bases, is a blackish chevron on a white ground. This can be seen when the bird stoops to drink and raises its spread tail.
</p><p>The mature bird has the head, neck, flanks, and rump blue grey, and the wings cinnamon, mottled with black. The breast is vinaceous, the abdomen and under tail coverts are white. The bill is black, the legs and eye rims are red. The black and white patch on the side of the neck is absent in the browner and duller juvenile bird, which also has the legs brown.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_status">Distribution and status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_turtle_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The turtle dove is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> species with a western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> range covering most of Europe and the Middle East and including <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">north Africa</a>, although it is rare in northern <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. It winters south of the Sahara.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The turtle dove, one of the latest migrants, rarely appears in Northern Europe before the end of April, returning south again in September.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1985356_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1985356-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is a bird of open rather than dense woodlands, and frequently feeds on the ground. It will occasionally nest in large gardens, but is usually extremely timid, probably due to the heavy hunting pressure it faces during migration. The flight is often described as arrowy, but is not remarkably swift.
</p><p>The nuptial flight, high and circling, is like that of the <a href="/wiki/Common_wood_pigeon" title="Common wood pigeon">common wood pigeon</a>, but the undulations are less decided; it is accompanied by the whip-crack of the downward flicked wings. The arrival in spring is heralded by its <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/coo#Verb" class="extiw" title="wikt:coo">cooing</a> or <a href="/wiki/Purr" title="Purr">purring</a> song, a rather deep, vibrating “turrr, turrr”.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_turtle_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Populations of turtle dove are in rapid decline across Europe and this species has red list conservation status globally. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> its numbers have declined by 93% since 1994 and across Europe numbers fell by 78% 1980–2013.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Environmentalist groups have said that the decline of turtle doves in Europe is partly because changed farming practices mean that the weed seeds and shoots on which it feeds, especially <a href="/wiki/Fumitory" class="mw-redirect" title="Fumitory">fumitory</a>, are scarcer, and partly due to shooting of birds in <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> countries. According to a 2001 study cited by the European Commission, between two and four million birds are shot annually in Malta, Cyprus, France, Italy, Spain and Greece.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Environmentalists have described spring hunting in Malta as particularly problematic as it is the only country with an EU <a href="/wiki/Derogation" title="Derogation">derogation</a> to shoot birds during their spring migration to breeding grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tourterelle_des_bois_MHNT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Tourterelle_des_bois_MHNT.jpg/220px-Tourterelle_des_bois_MHNT.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="203" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Tourterelle_des_bois_MHNT.jpg/330px-Tourterelle_des_bois_MHNT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Tourterelle_des_bois_MHNT.jpg/440px-Tourterelle_des_bois_MHNT.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2214" data-file-height="2040" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tourterelle_des_bois_MHNT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Streptopelia turtur</i> egg</div></div></div>
<p>According to a 2007 study by the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a>, four currently identifiable potential threats to the turtle dove are (1) <a href="/wiki/Habitat_loss" class="mw-redirect" title="Habitat loss">habitat loss</a>/modification (medium to low impact), (2) <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">droughts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> (mostly unknown but likely low impact), (3) <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunting</a> (partly unknown but overall medium impact), and (4) competition with the <a href="/wiki/Collared_dove" class="mw-redirect" title="Collared dove">collared dove</a> (unknown impact).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/British_Trust_for_Ornithology" title="British Trust for Ornithology">British Trust for Ornithology</a> has also highlighted <a href="/wiki/Trichomonosis" class="mw-redirect" title="Trichomonosis">Trichomonosis</a> parasite as a threat to the turtle dove.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_turtle_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The turtle dove was, according to <a href="/wiki/Claudius_Aelianus" title="Claudius Aelianus">Aelian</a>, sacred to <a href="/wiki/Demeter" title="Demeter">Demeter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Roman_Mythology" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Mythology">Roman Mythology</a>, the turtle-dove was one of the emblems of <a href="/wiki/Fides_(deity)" title="Fides (deity)">Fides (deity)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-peck_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peck-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-smith_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smith-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SophieAnderson_TheTurtleDoveSmall.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/SophieAnderson_TheTurtleDoveSmall.jpg/220px-SophieAnderson_TheTurtleDoveSmall.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="263" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/SophieAnderson_TheTurtleDoveSmall.jpg/330px-SophieAnderson_TheTurtleDoveSmall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/SophieAnderson_TheTurtleDoveSmall.jpg/440px-SophieAnderson_TheTurtleDoveSmall.jpg 2x" data-file-width="855" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SophieAnderson_TheTurtleDoveSmall.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>The Turtle Dove</i> by <a href="/wiki/Sophie_Gengembre_Anderson" title="Sophie Gengembre Anderson">Sophie Gengembre Anderson</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Perhaps because of Biblical references (especially verse 2:12 from the <a href="/wiki/Song_of_Songs" title="Song of Songs">Song of Songs</a>), its mournful voice, and the fact that it forms strong <a href="/wiki/Pair_bond" title="Pair bond">pair bonds</a>, European turtle doves have become emblems of devoted love. In the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, two turtle doves are mentioned as the <a href="/wiki/Pidyon_haben" title="Pidyon haben">customary offering</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Presentation_of_Jesus_at_the_Temple" title="Presentation of Jesus at the Temple">Presentation of Jesus at the Temple</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RockSwag_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RockSwag-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  In Renaissance Europe, the European turtle dove was envisaged as the devoted partner of the <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(mythology)" title="Phoenix (mythology)">Phoenix</a>. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Chester_(poet)" title="Robert Chester (poet)">Robert Chester</a>'s poem <i>Love's Martyr</i> is a sustained exploration of this symbolism. It was published along with other poems on the subject, including <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>'s poem "<a href="/wiki/The_Phoenix_and_the_Turtle" title="The Phoenix and the Turtle">The Phoenix and the Turtle</a>" (where "turtle" refers to the turtle dove).
</p><p>The turtle dove is featured in a number of <a href="/wiki/Folk_song" class="mw-redirect" title="Folk song">folk songs</a> about love and loss, including "<a href="/wiki/There_Is_a_Tavern_in_the_Town" title="There Is a Tavern in the Town">There Is a Tavern in the Town</a>". One of these is a setting by <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Vaughan_Williams" title="Ralph Vaughan Williams">Ralph Vaughan Williams</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Turtle doves also are featured in the song, "<a href="/wiki/The_Twelve_Days_of_Christmas_(song)" title="The Twelve Days of Christmas (song)">The Twelve Days of Christmas</a>", as the gift "my true love gave to me" on the second day of Christmas.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  If added cumulatively, by the end of the song, the recipient has been given 22.
</p><p>Turtle doves appear in the title and lyrics of a <a href="/wiki/Spiritual_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spiritual (music)">spiritual</a> from the Georgia <a href="/wiki/Sea_Islands" title="Sea Islands">Sea Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Shaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Shaker">Shaker</a> hymn "In Yonder Valley", it is seen as a good omen and sign of growth that "The turtledove is in our land".
</p>
