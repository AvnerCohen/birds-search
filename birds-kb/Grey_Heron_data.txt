<p>The <b>grey heron</b> (<i>Ardea cinerea</i>) is a long-legged <a href="/wiki/Predator" class="mw-redirect" title="Predator">predatory</a> wading <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">heron</a> family, Ardeidae, native throughout temperate <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Asia</a> and also parts of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It is resident in much of its range, but some populations from the more northern parts <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> southwards in autumn. A bird of wetland areas, it can be seen around lakes, rivers, ponds, marshes and on the sea coast. It feeds mostly on aquatic creatures which it catches after standing stationary beside or in the water or stalking its prey through the shallows.
</p><p>Standing up to 1 m tall, adults weigh from 1 to 2&#160;kg (2.2 to 4.4&#160;lb). They have a white head and neck with a broad black stripe that extends from the eye to the black crest. The body and wings are grey above and the underparts are greyish-white, with some black on the flanks. The long, sharply pointed beak is pinkish-yellow and the legs are brown.
</p><p>The birds breed <a href="/wiki/Colony_(biology)" title="Colony (biology)">colonially</a> in spring in "heronries", usually building their nests high in trees. A clutch of usually three to five bluish-green eggs is laid. Both birds incubate the eggs for  around 25 days, and then both feed the chicks, which fledge when 7-8 weeks old. Many juveniles do not survive their first winter, but if they do, they can expect to live for about 5 years.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">deity</a> <a href="/wiki/Bennu" title="Bennu">Bennu</a> was depicted as a heron in New Kingdom artwork. 
In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Ancient Rome</a>, the heron was a bird of <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">divination</a>. Roast heron was once a specially prized dish; when <a href="/wiki/George_Neville_(bishop)" class="mw-redirect" title="George Neville (bishop)">George Neville</a> became <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_York" title="Archbishop of York">Archbishop of York</a> in 1465, 400 herons were served to the guests.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grey_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GreyHeron3_MJF.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/GreyHeron3_MJF.jpg/220px-GreyHeron3_MJF.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="111" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/GreyHeron3_MJF.jpg/330px-GreyHeron3_MJF.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/GreyHeron3_MJF.jpg/440px-GreyHeron3_MJF.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3294" data-file-height="1660" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GreyHeron3_MJF.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Head, with neck retracted</div></div></div>
<p>The grey heron is a large bird, standing up to 100&#160;cm (39&#160;in) tall and measuring 84–102&#160;cm (33–40&#160;in) long with a 155–195&#160;cm (61–77&#160;in) wingspan.<sup id="cite_ref-arkive_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arkive-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The body weight can range from 1.02–2.08&#160;kg (2.2–4.6&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> is largely ashy-grey above, and greyish-white below with some black on the flanks. Adults have the head and neck white with a broad black <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a> that terminates in the slender, dangling crest, and bluish-black streaks on the front of the neck. The scapular feathers are elongated and the feathers at the base of the neck are also somewhat elongated. Immature birds lack the dark stripe on the head and are generally duller in appearance than adults, with a grey head and neck, and a small, dark grey crest. The pinkish-yellow beak is long, straight and powerful, and is brighter in colour in breeding adults. The <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">iris</a> is yellow and the legs are brown and very long.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The main call is a loud croaking "fraaank", but a variety of guttural and raucous noises is heard at the breeding colony. The male uses an advertisement call to encourage a female to join him at the nest, and both sexes use various greeting calls after a pair bond has been established. A loud, harsh "schaah" is used by the male in driving other birds from the vicinity of the nest and a soft "gogogo" expresses anxiety, as when a predator is nearby or a human walks past the colony. The chicks utter loud chattering or ticking noises.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Herons are a fairly ancient lineage and first appeared in the fossil record in the <a href="/wiki/Paleogene" title="Paleogene">Paleogene</a> period; very few fossil herons have been found, though. By seven million years ago (the late <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a>), birds closely resembling modern forms and attributable to modern genera had appeared.<sup id="cite_ref-HSG_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HSG-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Herons are members of the family <a href="/wiki/Ardeidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Ardeidae">Ardeidae</a>, and the majority of <a href="/wiki/Extant_taxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Extant taxon">extant</a> species are in the subfamily <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ardeinae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ardeinae (page does not exist)">Ardeinae</a> and known as true or typical herons. This subfamily includes the herons and <a href="/wiki/Egret" title="Egret">egrets</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Butorides" title="Butorides">green herons</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pond_heron" title="Pond heron">pond herons</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nycticorax" title="Nycticorax">night herons</a>, and a few other species. The grey heron belongs in this subfamily and is placed in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Ardea_(genus)" title="Ardea (genus)">Ardea</a></i>, which also includes the <a href="/wiki/Cattle_egret" title="Cattle egret">cattle egret</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Great_egret" title="Great egret">great egret</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HSG_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HSG-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The grey heron was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">first described</a> in 1758 by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a>, who gave it the name <i>Ardea cinerea</i>. The scientific name comes from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>  <i>ardea</i> "heron", and <i> cinerea </i>, "ash-grey" (from <i>cineris</i> ashes).<sup id="cite_ref-job_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Four <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are recognised:<sup id="cite_ref-IOC_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IOC-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>A. c. cinerea</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">1758</a></small>: <a href="/wiki/Subspecies#Nominotypical_subspecies_and_subspecies_autonyms" title="Subspecies">nominate</a>, found in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Asia</a></li>
<li><i>A. c. jouyi</i> – <small>Clark, 1907</small>: found in eastern <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Asia</a></li>
<li><i>A. c. firasa</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a>, 1917</small>: found in <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a></li>
<li><i>A. c. monicae</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/Christian_Jouanin" title="Christian Jouanin">Jouanin</a> &amp; Roux, 1963</small>: found on islands off <a href="/wiki/Banc_d%27Arguin_National_Park" title="Banc d&#39;Arguin National Park">Banc d'Arguin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a>.</li></ul>
<p>It is closely related and similar to the North American <a href="/wiki/Great_blue_heron" title="Great blue heron">great blue heron</a> (<i>Ardea herodias</i>), which differs in being larger, and having chestnut-brown flanks and thighs, and to the <a href="/wiki/Cocoi_heron" title="Cocoi heron">cocoi heron</a> (<i>Ardea cocoi</i>) from South America with which it forms a superspecies. Some authorities believe that the subspecies <i>A. c. monicae</i> should be considered a separate species.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It has been known to hybridise with the <a href="/wiki/Great_egret" title="Great egret">great egret</a> (<i>Ardea alba</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Little_egret" title="Little egret">little egret</a> (<i>Egretta garzetta</i>), the great blue heron and the <a href="/wiki/Purple_heron" title="Purple heron">purple heron</a> (<i>Ardea purpurea</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  The Australian <a href="/wiki/White-faced_heron" title="White-faced heron">white-faced heron</a> is often incorrectly called a grey heron.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In Ireland, the grey heron is often colloquially called a "crane".<sup id="cite_ref-AvianWeb_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AvianWeb-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grey_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Garza_Real_(Ardea_cinerea)_(5593437102)_cropped.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Garza_Real_%28Ardea_cinerea%29_%285593437102%29_cropped.jpg/220px-Garza_Real_%28Ardea_cinerea%29_%285593437102%29_cropped.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Garza_Real_%28Ardea_cinerea%29_%285593437102%29_cropped.jpg/330px-Garza_Real_%28Ardea_cinerea%29_%285593437102%29_cropped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Garza_Real_%28Ardea_cinerea%29_%285593437102%29_cropped.jpg/440px-Garza_Real_%28Ardea_cinerea%29_%285593437102%29_cropped.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3642" data-file-height="2564" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Garza_Real_(Ardea_cinerea)_(5593437102)_cropped.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In flight</div></div></div>
<p>The grey heron has an extensive range throughout most of the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic_realm" title="Palearctic realm">Palearctic realm</a>. The range of the nominate subspecies <i>A. c. cinerea</i> extends to 70° N in Norway and 66°N in Sweden, but otherwise its northerly limit is around 60°N across the rest of Europe and Asia eastwards as far as the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural Mountains</a>. To the south, its range extends to northern Spain, France, central Italy, the Balkans, the Caucasus, Iraq, Iran, India, and Myanmar (Burma). It is also present in Africa south of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara Desert</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia, and many of the Mediterranean Islands. It is replaced by <i>A. c. jouyi</i> in eastern Siberia, Mongolia, eastern China, Hainan, Japan, and Taiwan. In Madagascar and the <a href="/wiki/Aldabra_Group" title="Aldabra Group">Aldabra Islands</a>, the subspecies <i>A. c. firasa</i> is found, while the subspecies <i>A. c. monicae</i> is restricted to Mauritania and offshore islands.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Over much of its range, the grey heron is resident, but birds from the more northerly parts of Europe migrate southwards, some remaining in Central and Southern Europe, others travelling on to Africa south of the Sahara Desert.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The grey heron is also known to be <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrant</a> in the Caribbean, Bermuda, Iceland, Greenland, the Aleutian Islands, and Newfoundland, with a few confirmed sightings in other parts of North America including Nova Scotia and Nantucket.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Within its range, the grey heron can be found anywhere with suitable watery habitat that can supply its food. The water body needs to be either shallow enough, or have a shelving margin in it, which it can wade. Although most common in the lowlands, it also occurs in mountain tarns, lakes, reservoirs, large and small rivers, marshes, ponds, ditches, flooded areas, coastal lagoons, estuaries, and the sea shore. It sometimes forages away from water in pasture, and it has been recorded in desert areas, hunting for beetles and lizards. Breeding colonies are usually near feeding areas, but exceptionally may be up to 8&#160;km (5&#160;mi) away, and birds sometimes forage as much as 20&#160;km (12&#160;mi) from the nesting site.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grey_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The grey heron has a slow flight, with its long neck retracted (S-shaped). This is characteristic of herons and <a href="/wiki/Bittern" title="Bittern">bitterns</a>, and distinguishes them from <a href="/wiki/Stork" title="Stork">storks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crane_(bird)" title="Crane (bird)">cranes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Spoonbill" title="Spoonbill">spoonbills</a>, which extend their necks.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It flies with slow wing-beats and sometimes glides for short distances. It sometimes soars, circling to considerable heights, but not as often as the stork. In spring, and occasionally in autumn, birds may soar high above the heronry and chase each other, undertake aerial manoeuvres or swoop down towards the ground. The birds often perch in trees, but spend much time on the ground, striding about or standing still for long periods with an upright stance, often on a single leg.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet_and_feeding">Diet and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grey_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Grey_Heron,_Leighton_Moss.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Grey_Heron%2C_Leighton_Moss.jpg/220px-Grey_Heron%2C_Leighton_Moss.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Grey_Heron%2C_Leighton_Moss.jpg/330px-Grey_Heron%2C_Leighton_Moss.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Grey_Heron%2C_Leighton_Moss.jpg/440px-Grey_Heron%2C_Leighton_Moss.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3872" data-file-height="2592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Grey_Heron,_Leighton_Moss.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Swallowing an <a href="/wiki/Eel" title="Eel">eel</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">Fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> are taken in shallow water with the heron's long bill. It has also been observed catching and killing juvenile birds such as <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">ducklings</a>, and occasionally takes birds up to the size of a <a href="/wiki/Water_rail" title="Water rail">water rail</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pistorius2008_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pistorius2008-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> It may stand motionless in the shallows, or on a rock or sandbank beside the water, waiting for prey to come within striking distance. Alternatively, it moves slowly and stealthily through the water with its body less upright than when at rest and its neck curved in an "S". It is able to straighten its neck and strike with its bill very fast.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Small fish are swallowed head first, and larger prey and eels are carried to the shore where they are subdued by being beaten on the ground or stabbed by the bill. They are then swallowed, or have hunks of flesh torn off. For prey such as small mammals and birds or ducklings, the prey is held by the neck and either drowned, suffocated, or killed by having its neck snapped with the heron's beak, before being swallowed whole. The bird regurgitates <a href="/wiki/Pellet_(ornithology)" title="Pellet (ornithology)">pellets</a> of indigestible material such as fur, bones and the chitinous remains of insects. The main periods of hunting are around dawn and dusk, but it is also active at other times of day. At night it roosts in trees or on cliffs, where it tends to be gregarious.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WilhelmaGraureiher-pjt.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/WilhelmaGraureiher-pjt.jpg/220px-WilhelmaGraureiher-pjt.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/WilhelmaGraureiher-pjt.jpg/330px-WilhelmaGraureiher-pjt.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/WilhelmaGraureiher-pjt.jpg/440px-WilhelmaGraureiher-pjt.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4928" data-file-height="3264" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WilhelmaGraureiher-pjt.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Heronry" title="Heronry">Heronry</a> in <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>, Germany</div></div></div>
<p>This species breeds in <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonies</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Heronry" title="Heronry">heronries</a>, usually in high trees close to lakes, the seashore, or other wetlands. Other sites are sometimes chosen, and these include low trees and bushes, bramble patches, <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed beds</a>, heather clumps and cliff ledges. The same nest is used year after year until blown down; it starts as a small platform of sticks but expands into a bulky nest as more material is added in subsequent years. It may be lined with smaller twigs, strands of root or dead grasses, and in reed beds, it is built from dead reeds. The male usually collects the material, while the female constructs the nest. Breeding activities take place between February and June. When a bird arrives at the nest, a greeting ceremony occurs in which each partner raises and lowers its wings and plumes.<sup id="cite_ref-AvianWeb_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AvianWeb-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In continental Europe, and elsewhere, nesting colonies sometimes include nests of the <a href="/wiki/Purple_heron" title="Purple heron">purple heron</a> and other heron species.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ardea_cinerea_building_nest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ardea_cinerea_building_nest.jpg/220px-Ardea_cinerea_building_nest.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ardea_cinerea_building_nest.jpg/330px-Ardea_cinerea_building_nest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ardea_cinerea_building_nest.jpg/440px-Ardea_cinerea_building_nest.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1733" data-file-height="1220" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ardea_cinerea_building_nest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Building nest</div></div></div>
<p>Courtship involves the male calling from his chosen nesting site. On the arrival of the female, both birds participate in a stretching ceremony, in which each bird extends its neck vertically before bringing it backwards and downwards with the bill remaining vertical, simultaneously flexing its legs, before returning to its normal stance. The snapping ceremony is another behaviour where the neck is extended forward, the head is lowered to the level of the feet, and the mandibles are vigorously snapped together. This may be repeated 20-40 times. When the pairing is settled, the birds may caress each other by attending to the other bird's plumage. The male may then offer the female a stick, which she incorporates into the nest. At this, the male becomes excited, further preening the female and copulation takes place.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ardea_cinerea_MWNH_1927.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Ardea_cinerea_MWNH_1927.JPG/170px-Ardea_cinerea_MWNH_1927.JPG" decoding="async" width="170" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Ardea_cinerea_MWNH_1927.JPG/255px-Ardea_cinerea_MWNH_1927.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Ardea_cinerea_MWNH_1927.JPG/340px-Ardea_cinerea_MWNH_1927.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2488" data-file-height="2536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ardea_cinerea_MWNH_1927.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> of eggs usually numbers three to five, though as few as two and as many as seven eggs have been recorded. The eggs have a matt surface and are greenish-blue, averaging 60&#160;mm ×&#160;43&#160;mm (2.36&#160;in ×&#160;1.69&#160;in). The eggs are normally laid at two-day intervals and incubation usually starts after the first or second egg has been laid. Both birds take part in incubation and the <a href="/wiki/Incubation_period" title="Incubation period">period</a> lasts  about 25 days. Both parents bring food for the young. At first, the chicks seize the adult's bill from the side and extract regurgitated food from it. Later, the adult disgorges the food at the nest and the chicks squabble for possession. They <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> at 7-8 weeks. Usually, a single brood is raised each year, but two broods have been recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_4-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The oldest recorded bird lived for 23 years, but the average life expectancy in the wild is about 5 years. Only about a third of juveniles survive into their second year, many falling victim to predation.<sup id="cite_ref-AvianWeb_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AvianWeb-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="City_life">City life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grey_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: City life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Spheniscus_humboldti_-Birdworld,_Farnham,_Surrey,_England_-zoo_keeper-8a.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Spheniscus_humboldti_-Birdworld%2C_Farnham%2C_Surrey%2C_England_-zoo_keeper-8a.jpg/150px-Spheniscus_humboldti_-Birdworld%2C_Farnham%2C_Surrey%2C_England_-zoo_keeper-8a.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Spheniscus_humboldti_-Birdworld%2C_Farnham%2C_Surrey%2C_England_-zoo_keeper-8a.jpg/225px-Spheniscus_humboldti_-Birdworld%2C_Farnham%2C_Surrey%2C_England_-zoo_keeper-8a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Spheniscus_humboldti_-Birdworld%2C_Farnham%2C_Surrey%2C_England_-zoo_keeper-8a.jpg/300px-Spheniscus_humboldti_-Birdworld%2C_Farnham%2C_Surrey%2C_England_-zoo_keeper-8a.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2406" data-file-height="3369" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Spheniscus_humboldti_-Birdworld,_Farnham,_Surrey,_England_-zoo_keeper-8a.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Seeking food from a zoo penguin enclosure</div></div></div>
<p>Grey herons have the ability to live in cities where habitats and nesting space are available. In the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, it has established itself over the past decades in great numbers in urban environments. In cities such as <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>, they are ever present and well adapted to modern city life.<sup id="cite_ref-Amsterdam_herons_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amsterdam_herons-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> They hunt as usual, but also visit street markets and snackbars. Some individuals make use of people feeding them at their homes or share the catch of recreational fishermen. Similar behaviour on a smaller scale has been reported in Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-Schoffies_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schoffies-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Garden ponds stocked with ornamental fish are attractive to herons, and may provide young birds with a learning opportunity on how to catch easy prey.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Herons have been observed visiting water enclosures in zoos, such as spaces for <a href="/wiki/Penguin" title="Penguin">penguins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Otter" title="Otter">otters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pelican" title="Pelican">pelicans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">seals</a>, and taking food meant for the animals on display.<sup id="cite_ref-Tiergarten_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tiergarten-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mallison_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mallison-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators_and_parasites">Predators and parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grey_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Predators and parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Being large birds with powerful beaks, grey herons have few predators as adults, but the eggs and young are more vulnerable. The adult birds do not usually leave the nest unattended, but may be lured away by marauding <a href="/wiki/Corvus_(genus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Corvus (genus)">crows</a> or <a href="/wiki/Kite_(bird)" title="Kite (bird)">kites</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> A dead grey heron found in the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a> is thought to have been killed by an <a href="/wiki/European_otter" class="mw-redirect" title="European otter">otter</a>. The bird may have been weakened by harsh winter weather causing scarcity of its prey.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A study  suggested that Central European grey herons host 29 species of <a href="/wiki/Helminths" class="mw-redirect" title="Helminths">parasitic worms</a>. The dominant species consisted of <i>Apharyngostrigea cornu</i> (67% prevalence), <i>Posthodiplostomum cuticola</i> (41% prevalence), <i>Echinochasmus beleocephalus</i> (39% prevalence), <i>Uroproctepisthmium bursicola</i> (36% prevalence), <i>Neogryporhynchus cheilancristrotus</i> (31% prevalence), <i>Desmidocercella numidica</i> (29% prevalence), and <i>Bilharziella polonica</i> (5% prevalence). Juvenile grey herons were shown to host fewer species, but the intensity of infection was higher in the juveniles than in the adult herons. Of the <a href="/wiki/Digenea" title="Digenea">digenean flatworms</a> found in Central European grey herons, 52% of the species likely infected their definitive hosts outside Central Europe itself, in the premigratory, migratory, or wintering quarters, despite the fact that a substantial proportion of grey herons does not migrate to the south.<sup id="cite_ref-Sitko_&amp;_Heneberg_2015_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sitko_&amp;_Heneberg_2015-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_human_culture">In human culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grey_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In human culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Heron_woodcut_Bewick%27s_British_Birds_1847.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Heron_woodcut_Bewick%27s_British_Birds_1847.jpg/170px-Heron_woodcut_Bewick%27s_British_Birds_1847.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="194" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Heron_woodcut_Bewick%27s_British_Birds_1847.jpg/255px-Heron_woodcut_Bewick%27s_British_Birds_1847.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Heron_woodcut_Bewick%27s_British_Birds_1847.jpg/340px-Heron_woodcut_Bewick%27s_British_Birds_1847.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1231" data-file-height="1403" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Heron_woodcut_Bewick%27s_British_Birds_1847.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"The Heron. Common Heron, Heronsewgh, or Heronshaw. (<i>Ardea cinerea</i>, <a href="/wiki/John_Latham_(ornithologist)" title="John Latham (ornithologist)">Lath</a>.—<i>Héron cendré</i>, <a href="/wiki/Temminck" class="mw-redirect" title="Temminck">Temm</a>.)" <a href="/wiki/Wood_engraving" title="Wood engraving">wood engraving</a> by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bewick" title="Thomas Bewick">Thomas Bewick</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/History_of_British_Birds" class="mw-redirect" title="History of British Birds">History of British Birds</a></i>, volume 2, 1804</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a>, the bird <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">deity</a> <a href="/wiki/Bennu" title="Bennu">Bennu</a>, associated with the sun, creation, and rebirth, was depicted as a heron in New Kingdom artwork.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Ancient Rome</a>, the heron was a bird of <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">divination</a> that gave an <a href="/wiki/Augury" title="Augury">augury</a> (sign of a coming event) by its call, like the raven, stork, and owl.<sup id="cite_ref-JonsonOrgel1969_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JonsonOrgel1969-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Roast heron was once a specially prized dish in Britain for special occasions such as state banquets. For the appointment of <a href="/wiki/George_Neville_(bishop)" class="mw-redirect" title="George Neville (bishop)">George Neville</a> as <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_York" title="Archbishop of York">Archbishop of York</a> in 1465, 400 herons were served to the guests. Young birds were still being shot and eaten in <a href="/wiki/Romney_Marsh" title="Romney Marsh">Romney Marsh</a> in 1896. Two grey herons feature in a <a href="/wiki/Stained-glass" class="mw-redirect" title="Stained-glass">stained-glass</a> window of the church in <a href="/wiki/Selborne" title="Selborne">Selborne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BB_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surnames</a> Earnshaw, Hernshaw, Herne, and Heron all derive from the heron, the suffix -shaw meaning a <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">wood</a>, referring to a place where herons nested.<sup id="cite_ref-Bardsley_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bardsley-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
