<p>The <b>blue rock thrush</b> (<i>Monticola solitarius</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Chat_(bird)" title="Chat (bird)">chat</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a>-like <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a> was formerly placed in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Turdidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Turdidae">Turdidae</a>. It breeds in southern Europe, northwest Africa, and from <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> to northern China and Malaysia. The blue rock thrush is the official national bird of <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a> and was shown on the <a href="/wiki/Maltese_lira" title="Maltese lira">Lm 1 coins</a> that were part of the country's former currency.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_rock_thrush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The blue rock thrush was described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> under the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Turdus solitarius</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The scientific name is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>. <i>Monticola</i> is from  <i>mons, montis</i> "mountain", and <i>colere</i>, "to dwell", and the <a href="/wiki/Specific_epithet_(zoology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Specific epithet (zoology)">specific epithet</a> <i>solitarius</i> means "solitary".<sup id="cite_ref-job_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The rock thrush genus <a href="/wiki/Monticola" class="mw-redirect" title="Monticola">Monticola</a> was formerly placed in the family <a href="/wiki/Turdidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Turdidae">Turdidae</a><sup id="cite_ref-h&amp;m3_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-h&amp;m3-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> but <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular phylogenetic">molecular phylogenetic</a> studies have shown that the species in the genus are more closely related to members of the Old World flycatcher family <a href="/wiki/Muscicapidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Muscicapidae">Muscicapidae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sangster2010_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sangster2010-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are five recognised <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbw_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>M. s. solitarius</i> (Linnaeus, 1758) – northwest Africa, south Europe, north Turkey to Georgia and Azerbaijan.</li>
<li><i>M. s. longirostris</i> (<a href="/wiki/Edward_Blyth" title="Edward Blyth">Blyth</a>, 1847) – Greece and west and south Turkey through the Middle East to the northwest Himalayas to northeast Africa and India</li>
<li><i>M. s. pandoo</i> (<a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Sykes" title="William Henry Sykes">Sykes</a>, 1832) – central Himalayas to east China and north Vietnam to <a href="/wiki/Greater_Sunda_Islands" title="Greater Sunda Islands">Greater Sunda Islands</a></li>
<li><i>M. s. philippensis</i> (<a href="/wiki/Philipp_Ludwig_Statius_M%C3%BCller" title="Philipp Ludwig Statius Müller">Statius Müller</a>, 1776) – east Mongolia to <a href="/wiki/Sakhalin" title="Sakhalin">Sakhalin</a> south to Japan, extreme north Philippines and northeast China to Indonesia</li>
<li><i>M. s. madoci</i> <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Nutter_Chasen" title="Frederick Nutter Chasen">Chasen</a>, 1940 – <a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">Malay Peninsula</a> and north <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a></li></ul>
<p>There is a proposal to split <i>Monticola solitarius</i> into two species: a western taxon comprising <i>M. s. solitarius</i> and <i>M. s. longirostris</i> and an eastern taxon with <i>M. s. philippensis</i>, <i>M. s. pandoo</i> and <i>M. s. madoci</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-zuccon2010_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zuccon2010-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_rock_thrush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The blue rock thrush is a <a href="/wiki/Starling" title="Starling">starling</a>-sized bird, 21–23&#160;cm (8.3–9.1&#160;in) in length with a long slim bill. The breeding male of the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> is unmistakable, with all blue-grey plumage apart from its darker wings.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Females and immatures are much less striking, with dark brown upperparts, and paler brown scaly underparts. The male of the subspecies <i>M. s. philippensis</i> has rufous-chestnut plumage from the mid-breast down to the undertail.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Both sexes lack the reddish outer tail feathers of rock thrush.
</p><p>The male blue rock thrush sings a clear, melodious call that is similar to, but louder than the call of the rock thrush.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_rock_thrush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Blue_Rock_Thrush_at_Kutch.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Blue_Rock_Thrush_at_Kutch.jpg/220px-Blue_Rock_Thrush_at_Kutch.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="190" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Blue_Rock_Thrush_at_Kutch.jpg/330px-Blue_Rock_Thrush_at_Kutch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Blue_Rock_Thrush_at_Kutch.jpg/440px-Blue_Rock_Thrush_at_Kutch.jpg 2x" data-file-width="889" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Blue_Rock_Thrush_at_Kutch.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>female at <a href="/wiki/Kutch" class="mw-redirect" title="Kutch">Kutch</a></div></div></div>
<p>The European, north African and southeast Asian birds are mainly resident, apart from altitudinal movements. Other Asian populations are more <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in sub-Saharan Africa, India and southeast Asia. This bird is a very uncommon visitor to northern and western Europe.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_rock_thrush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Blue rock thrush breeds in open mountainous areas. It nests in rock cavities and walls, and usually lays 3-5 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>. An <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivore</a>, the blue rock thrush eats a wide variety of <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and small <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a> in addition to berries and seeds.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
