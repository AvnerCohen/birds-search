<p>The <b>little gull</b> (<i>Hydrocoloeus minutus</i> or <i>Larus minutus</i>), is a small <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a> that breeds in northern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>. The genus name <i>Hydrocoloeus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i lang="el-Latn" title="Greek-language romanization">hydro</i>, "water", and <i lang="el-Latn" title="Greek-language romanization">koloios</i>, a sort of web-footed bird. The specific <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">minutus</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "small".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It also has small colonies in parts of southern Canada. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering on coasts in western Europe, the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> and (in small numbers) the northeast United States; in recent years non-breeding birds have summered in western Europe in increasing numbers and in 2016 they successfully nested for the first time in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Protection_of_Birds" title="Royal Society for the Protection of Birds">Royal Society for the Protection of Birds</a> reserve at <a href="/wiki/Loch_of_Strathbeg" title="Loch of Strathbeg">Loch of Strathbeg</a> reserve in <a href="/wiki/Aberdeenshire" title="Aberdeenshire">Aberdeenshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RBA_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RBA-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> As is the case with many gulls, it has traditionally been placed in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Larus" title="Larus">Larus</a></i>. It is the only member of the genus <i><b>Hydrocoloeus</b></i>, although it has been suggested that <a href="/wiki/Ross%27s_gull" title="Ross&#39;s gull">Ross's gull</a> also should be included in this genus.
</p><p>This species breeds colonially on freshwater marshes, making a lined nest on the ground amongst vegetation. Normally 2–6 eggs are laid.
</p><p>This is the smallest gull species, with a length of 25–30&#160;cm (9.8–11.8&#160;in), a wingspan of 61–78&#160;cm (24–31&#160;in), and a mass of 68–162&#160;g (2.4–5.7&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ARKive_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ARKive-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AAB_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAB-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It is pale grey in breeding plumage with a black hood, dark underwings and often a pinkish flush on the breast. In winter, the head goes white apart from a darker cap and eye-spot. The bill is thin and black and the legs dark red. The flight on rounded wings is somewhat <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">tern-like</a>.
</p><p>Young birds have black markings on the head and upperparts, and "W" pattern across the wings. They take three years to reach maturity.
</p><p>These gulls pick food off the water surface, and will also catch insects in the air like a <a href="/wiki/Black_tern" title="Black tern">black tern</a>.
</p>
