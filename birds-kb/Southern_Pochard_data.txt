<p>The <b>southern pochard</b> (<i>Netta erythrophthalma</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">duck</a>, and a member of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Netta" title="Netta">Netta</a></i>. There are two subspecies, the South American (southern) pochard <i>N. e. erythrophthalma</i> (Wied-Neuwied, 1833) and the African (southern) pochard <i>N. e. brunnea</i> (<a href="/wiki/Thomas_Campbell_Eyton" title="Thomas Campbell Eyton">Eyton</a>, 1838).
</p><p>The South American pochard has a fragmented range and is found from Colombia, Venezuela, Brazil, Ecuador, Peru, Bolivia and Argentina to Chile. Here it occurs in a wide variety of shallow fresh waters with submerged vegetation, from the lowlands up to 3,700 metres.
</p><p>The African pochard occurs from the Cape to the Ethiopian highlands on water bodies with or without emergent vegetation. They are suspected to have been strong migrants in the past but the construction of numerous farm dams seems to allow them a more sedentary lifestyle. They reach highest concentrations in Africa's central plateaus and in the south-western winter rainfall region.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_pochard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two <a href="/wiki/Type_(biology)" title="Type (biology)">syntype specimens</a> of <i>Nyroca brunnea</i> Eyton (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://doi.org/10.5962/bhl.title.51971">Monogr. Anat., 1838, p.161., pl.23.</a>), the African Southern Pochard, are held in the collections of <a href="/wiki/National_Museums_Liverpool" title="National Museums Liverpool">National Museums Liverpool</a> at <a href="/wiki/World_Museum" title="World Museum">World Museum</a>, with accession numbers D832 (male adult) and D832a (female adult). The specimens were collected in South Africa and came to the Liverpool national collection via <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Campbell_Eyton" title="Thomas Campbell Eyton">Thomas Campbell Eyton</a>’s collection and the <a href="/wiki/13th_Earl_of_Derby" class="mw-redirect" title="13th Earl of Derby">13th Earl of Derby</a>’s collection which was bequeathed to the city of Liverpool.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:D832_D832a_Description_of_Nyroca_brunnea_3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/D832_D832a_Description_of_Nyroca_brunnea_3.jpg/220px-D832_D832a_Description_of_Nyroca_brunnea_3.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/D832_D832a_Description_of_Nyroca_brunnea_3.jpg/330px-D832_D832a_Description_of_Nyroca_brunnea_3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/D832_D832a_Description_of_Nyroca_brunnea_3.jpg/440px-D832_D832a_Description_of_Nyroca_brunnea_3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="873" data-file-height="634" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:D832_D832a_Description_of_Nyroca_brunnea_3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nyroca brunnea</div></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habits">Habits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_pochard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Southern pochards are sociable and gregarious. They have been seen in groups of up to 5,000.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mating_and_nesting">Mating and nesting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_pochard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mating and nesting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The birds are solitary nesters, building nests out of leaves and stems on the banks of a river surrounded by vegetation.<sup id="cite_ref-biodiversityexplorer_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biodiversityexplorer-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The female southern pochard lays a clutch consisting of six to fifteen eggs. The female incubates the eggs for 20 to 28 days.<sup id="cite_ref-biodiversityexplorer_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biodiversityexplorer-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Once the eggs hatch, the mother leads them immediately to water.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eating_habits">Eating habits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_pochard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eating habits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The southern pochard eats mainly aquatic plants, which it finds when diving.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Also, the adults tend to feed on larvae, pupae, aquatic animals, and plant material.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
