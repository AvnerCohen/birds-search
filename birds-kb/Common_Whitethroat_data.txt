<p>The <b>common whitethroat</b> (<i>Curruca communis</i>) is a common and widespread <a href="/wiki/Typical_warbler" title="Typical warbler">typical warbler</a> which breeds throughout <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and across much of temperate western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Asia</a>. This small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, and winters in tropical <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>.
</p><p>This is one of several <i>Curruca</i> species that has distinct male and female <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumages</a>. Both sexes are mainly brown above and buff below, with chestnut fringes to the secondary <a href="/wiki/Remiges" class="mw-redirect" title="Remiges">remiges</a>. The adult male has a grey head and a white throat. The female lacks the grey head, and the throat is duller. The whitethroat's song is fast and scratchy, with a scolding tone.
</p><p>The hoarse, a little bit nasal call sounds like <i>wed-wed</i> or <i>woid-woid</i>. The warning cry is long-pulled, rough <i>tschehr</i> which resembles that of the <a href="/wiki/Dartford_warbler" title="Dartford warbler">Dartford warbler</a>.
</p><p>This species may appear to be closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_whitethroat" title="Lesser whitethroat">lesser whitethroat</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> having evolved only during the end of the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Last glacial period">last ice age</a> similar to the <a href="/wiki/Willow_warbler" title="Willow warbler">willow warbler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chiffchaff_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Chiffchaff (disambiguation)">chiffchaffs</a>. However, researchers found the presence of a white throat is an unreliable <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphological</a> marker for relationships in <i>Curruca</i>, and the greater and lesser whitethroats are not closely related.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Chestnut wing patches, like white throats, seem to be <a href="/wiki/Plesiomorph" class="mw-redirect" title="Plesiomorph">plesiomorphic</a>, but indicate <a href="/wiki/Phylogeny" class="mw-redirect" title="Phylogeny">phylogeny</a> better. Nonetheless, apart from the whitethroat not being closely related to the lesser whitethroat group, little can be resolved as it seems a fairly <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basal</a> <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>This is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of open country and cultivation, with bushes for nesting. The nest is built in low <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrub</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bramble" title="Bramble">brambles</a>, and 3–7 eggs are laid. Like most <a href="/wiki/Warbler" title="Warbler">warblers</a>, it is <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a>, but will also eat berries and other soft <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a>.
</p><p>In Europe, western and eastern populations of common whitethroats have contrasting moulting and pre-migratory fueling strategies to capitilise on food supplies before departing their breeding and non-breeding grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The specific <i>communis</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "common".<sup id="cite_ref-job_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An older scientific name for the whitethroat is <i>Sylvia cinerea</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-cinerea_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cinerea-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
