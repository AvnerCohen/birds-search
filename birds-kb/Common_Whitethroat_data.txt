<p>The <b>Common Whitethroat</b> (<i>Sylvia communis</i>) is a common and widespread <a href="/wiki/Typical_warbler" title="Typical warbler">typical warbler</a> which breeds throughout <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and across much of temperate western <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. This small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, and winters in tropical <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>.</p>
<p>This is one of several <i>Sylvia</i> species that has distinct male and female <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumages</a>. Both sexes are mainly brown above and buff below, with chestnut fringes to the secondary <a href="/wiki/Remiges" title="Remiges" class="mw-redirect">remiges</a>. The adult male has a grey head and a white throat. The female lacks the grey head, and the throat is duller. The Whitethroat's song is fast and scratchy, with a scolding tone.</p>
<p>The hoarse, a little bit nasal call sounds like <i>wed-wed</i> or <i>woid-woid</i>. The warning cry is long-pulled, rough <i>tschehr</i> which resembles that of the <a href="/wiki/Dartford_Warbler" title="Dartford Warbler">Dartford Warbler</a>.</p>
<p>This species was believed by some<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from April 2007">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> to be closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Whitethroat" title="Lesser Whitethroat">Lesser Whitethroat</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> having evolved only during the end of the <a href="/wiki/Last_ice_age" title="Last ice age" class="mw-redirect">last ice age</a> similar to the <a href="/wiki/Willow_Warbler" title="Willow Warbler">Willow Warbler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chiffchaff_(disambiguation)" title="Chiffchaff (disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect">Chiffchaffs</a>. But researchers learned soon that this is not correct, and now it is known that whitethroats are unreliable <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphological</a> markers for relationships in <i>Sylvia</i>. Chestnut wing patches, like whitethroats, seem to be <a href="/wiki/Plesiomorph" title="Plesiomorph" class="mw-redirect">plesiomorphic</a>, but indicate <a href="/wiki/Phylogeny" title="Phylogeny" class="mw-redirect">phylogeny</a> better. Nonetheless, apart from the Whitethroat not being closely related to the Lesser Whitethroat group, little can be resolved as it seems a fairly <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basal</a> <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxon</a>.(Helbig 2001, Jønsson &amp; Fjeldså 2006)</p>

<p>This is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of open country and cultivation, with bushes for nesting. The nest is built in low <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrub</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bramble" title="Bramble">brambles</a>, and 3-7 eggs are laid. Like most "<a href="/wiki/Warbler" title="Warbler">warblers</a>", it is <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" title="Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect">insectivorous</a>, but will also take berries and other soft <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a>.</p>
