<p>The <b>Masked Booby</b> (<i>Sula dactylatra</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Booby" title="Booby">booby</a> family, <a href="/wiki/Sulidae" title="Sulidae">Sulidae</a>. This species breeds on islands in tropical oceans, except in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic" class="mw-redirect">Atlantic</a>; in the eastern Pacific it is replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Nazca_Booby" title="Nazca Booby">Nazca Booby</a>, <i>Sula granti</i>, which was formerly regarded as a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of Masked Booby.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> It is also called the <b>Masked Gannet</b> or the <b>Blue-faced Booby</b>.</p>
<p>A conspicuous and distinct <a href="/wiki/Gannet" title="Gannet">gannet</a>-like species, it was proposed for separation to a <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" title="Monotypic" class="mw-redirect">monotypic</a> <a href="/wiki/Subgenus" title="Subgenus">subgenus</a> <i><b>Pseudosula</b></i>, but the <a href="/wiki/Nazca_Booby" title="Nazca Booby">Nazca Booby</a> and as it seems also the <a href="/wiki/Brown_Booby" title="Brown Booby">Brown Booby</a> (<i>S. leucogaster</i>) are quite close relatives.</p>

<h2> <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span></h2>
<p>First described by French naturalist <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9-Primev%C3%A8re_Lesson" title="René-Primevère Lesson" class="mw-redirect">René-Primevère Lesson</a> in 1831, the Masked Booby is one of six species of booby in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Sula_(genus)" title="Sula (genus)" class="mw-redirect">Sula</a></i>. The <a href="/wiki/Nazca_Booby" title="Nazca Booby">Nazca Booby</a> (<i>S. granti</i>) was formerly regarded as a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>. There are four <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>, none of which is separable at sea:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>S. d. personata</i> <small>van Tets, Meredith, Fullagar &amp; Davidson, 1988</small>: <b>Austropacific Masked Booby</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Breeds in the central and western <a href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific" class="mw-redirect">Pacific</a> and around <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, as well as off <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and on <a href="/wiki/Clipperton_Island" title="Clipperton Island">Clipperton Island</a>. Birds of the latter two locations have been separated as subspecies <i>granti</i>, and the NW Australian population has been named as subspecies <i>bedouti</i>, but neither is usually considered valid.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>S. d. dactylatra</i> <small>van Tets, Meredith, Fullagar &amp; Davidson, 1988</small>: <b>Atlantic Masked Booby</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> and some Atlantic islands including <a href="/wiki/Ascension_Island" title="Ascension Island">Ascension Island</a>. It has recently started breeding off <a href="/wiki/Tobago" title="Tobago">Tobago</a>, formerly being known in this area only from a single sight record from an <a href="/wiki/Oil_platform" title="Oil platform">oil rig</a> off <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>S. d. melanops</i> <small>van Tets, Meredith, Fullagar &amp; Davidson, 1988</small>: <b>Western Indian Ocean Masked Booby</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Breeds in the western <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>S. d. tasmani</i> (including <i>S. d. fullagari</i>) <small>van Tets, Meredith, Fullagar &amp; Davidson, 1988</small>: <b><a href="/wiki/Tasman_Booby" title="Tasman Booby">Tasman Booby</a></b> or Lord Howe Masked Booby</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>The form breeding on <a href="/wiki/Lord_Howe_Island" title="Lord Howe Island">Lord Howe</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kermadec_Islands" title="Kermadec Islands">Kermadec Islands</a>. Large prehistoric specimens known from the former and <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_Island" title="Norfolk Island">Norfolk Island</a> are sometimes considered a distinct "species" (properly: subspecies). If this is correct, the extant population's name would be <i>S. d. tasmani</i> as <i>S. d. fullagari</i> was described after <i>S. tasmani</i>. Comparison of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_DNA" title="Ancient DNA">ancient DNA</a> form <i>tasmani</i> specimens and living <i>fullagari</i> indicates that they are not distinct.</dd>
</dl>
<h2> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>This is the largest <a href="/wiki/Booby" title="Booby">booby</a>, at 74–91 cm (29–36 in) long, with a 137–165 cm (54–65 in) wingspan and 1.2–2.35 kg (2.6–5.2 lb) weight.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Adults are white with pointed black wings, a pointed black tail, and a dark grey facemask. The sexes are similar, but the male has a yellow bill, and the female's is greenish yellow; during the breeding season they have a patch of bare, bluish skin at the base of the bill. Juveniles are brownish on the head and upperparts, with a whitish rump and neck collar. The underparts are white. Adult plumage is acquired over two years.</p>
<p>The Masked Booby is silent at sea, but has a reedy whistling greeting call at the nesting colonies. While on the breeding grounds, these birds display a wide range of hissing and quacking notes.</p>
<h2> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<p>Masked Boobies are spectacular divers, plunging diagonally into the ocean at high speed. They mainly eat small <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Flying_fish" title="Flying fish">flying fish</a>. This is a fairly sedentary bird, <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">wintering</a> at sea, but rarely seen far away from the breeding colonies. However, Caribbean birds occasionally wander north to warm southern <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a> waters off the eastern seaboard of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. More remarkably, there have been three Western <a href="/wiki/Palaearctic" title="Palaearctic" class="mw-redirect">Palaearctic</a> records of Masked Booby, presumably <i>dactylatra</i>, all from <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> waters, although one of these also entered <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> territorial areas.</p>
<h3> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<p>The Masked Booby nests in small colonies, laying two chalky white eggs on sandy beaches in shallow depressions, which are incubated by both adults for 45 days. In most cases, the first chick will kill its smaller, weaker sibling after it hatches.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Siblicide" title="Siblicide">Siblicide</a> has been well studied in this species; researchers such as David Anderson have demonstrated that while the boobies can manage to feed two chicks if siblicide is prevented, they do so at a steep penalty to health and future reproductive success.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
