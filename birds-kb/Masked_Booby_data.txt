<p>The <b>masked booby</b> (<i>Sula dactylatra</i>), also called the <b>masked gannet</b> or the <b>blue-faced booby</b>, is a large <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Booby" title="Booby">booby</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gannet" title="Gannet">gannet</a> family, <a href="/wiki/Sulidae" title="Sulidae">Sulidae</a>. First described by the French naturalist <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9-Primev%C3%A8re_Lesson" class="mw-redirect" title="René-Primevère Lesson">René-Primevère Lesson</a> in 1831, the masked booby is one of six species of booby in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Sula_(genus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sula (genus)">Sula</a></i>. It has a typical sulid body shape, with a long pointed yellowish bill, long neck, aerodynamic body, long slender wings and pointed tail. The adult is bright white with black wings, a black tail and a dark face mask; at 75–85&#160;cm (30–33&#160;in) long, it is the largest species of booby. The sexes have similar plumage. This species ranges across tropical oceans, except in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> and eastern Pacific. In the latter, it is replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Nazca_booby" title="Nazca booby">Nazca booby</a> (<i>Sula granti</i>), which was formerly regarded as a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of masked booby.
</p><p>Nesting takes place in <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonies</a>, generally on islands and <a href="/wiki/Atoll" title="Atoll">atolls</a> far from the mainland and close to deep water required for foraging. Territorial when breeding, the masked booby performs <a href="/wiki/Agonistic_behaviour" title="Agonistic behaviour">agonistic displays</a> to defend its nest. Potential and mated pairs engage in courtship and greeting displays. The female lays two chalky white eggs in a shallow depression on flat ground away from vegetation. The chicks are born featherless, but are soon covered in white <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">down</a>. The second chick born generally does not survive and is killed by its elder sibling. These birds are spectacular plunge <a href="/wiki/Diving_bird" title="Diving bird">divers</a>, plunging into the ocean at high speed in search of prey—mainly <a href="/wiki/Flying_fish" title="Flying fish">flying fish</a>. The species faces few threats; although its population is declining, it is considered to be a <a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least-concern species</a> by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The French naturalist <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9-Primev%C3%A8re_Lesson" class="mw-redirect" title="René-Primevère Lesson">René Lesson</a> was a member of the crew on the <i><a href="/wiki/French_ship_Astrolabe_(1811)" title="French ship Astrolabe (1811)">La Coquille</a></i> captained by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Isidore_Duperrey" title="Louis Isidore Duperrey">Louis Isidore Duperrey</a> on its voyage around the world undertaken between August 1822 and March 1825.<sup id="cite_ref-cretella_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cretella-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In the multi-volume publication by Duperrey about the voyage, Lesson authored the ornithological sections. In his 1829 account of the visit to <a href="/wiki/Ascension_Island" title="Ascension Island">Ascension Island</a> in the <a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_Ocean" class="mw-redirect" title="South Atlantic Ocean">South Atlantic Ocean</a>, Lesson mentioned encountering masked boobies, and in a footnote proposed the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> <i>Sula dactylatra</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Lesson subsequently provided a <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">formal description</a> of the masked booby in 1831.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Specific_epithet" class="mw-redirect" title="Specific epithet">specific epithet</a> combines the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>dactyl</i>, meaning "finger", and the Latin <i>ater</i>, meaning "black". "Black fingers" refers to the splayed wingtips in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-fraser13_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fraser13-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The Swedish zoologist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Jakob_Sundevall" title="Carl Jakob Sundevall">Carl Jakob Sundevall</a> described the species as <i>Dysporus cyanops</i> in 1837<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> from a <a href="/wiki/Subadult" class="mw-redirect" title="Subadult">subadult</a> collected in the Atlantic Ocean on 6<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>September 1827.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The species name was derived from the Ancient Greek words <i>cyanos</i>, meaning "blue",<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELiddellScott1980397_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELiddellScott1980397-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and <i>ops</i>, meaning "face".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELiddellScott1980804_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELiddellScott1980804-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The English ornithologist and bird artist <a href="/wiki/John_Gould" title="John Gould">John Gould</a> described <i>Sula personata</i> in 1846 from Australia,<sup id="cite_ref-Gould_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gould-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> the species name being the Latin adjective <i>personata</i>, meaning "masked".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Gould adopted the name <i>Sula cyanops</i> in his 1865 <i><a href="/wiki/Handbook_to_the_Birds_of_Australia" title="Handbook to the Birds of Australia">Handbook to the Birds of Australia</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Sundevall's binomial name was followed as Lesson's 1829 record did not sufficiently describe the species; however, in 1911, the Australian amateur ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Mathews" title="Gregory Mathews">Gregory Mathews</a> pointed out that although Lesson's 1829 account did not describe the bird, his 1831 account did, and thus predated Sundevall by six years, and hence <i>Sula dactylactra</i> had <a href="/wiki/Principle_of_Priority" title="Principle of Priority">priority</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/American_Ornithological_Society" title="American Ornithological Society">American Ornithological Union</a> followed in the 17th supplement to their checklist in 1920.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Masked_Booby,_Grand_Turk,_TC.jpg" class="image"><img alt="predominantly white underside of bird in flight" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Masked_Booby%2C_Grand_Turk%2C_TC.jpg/220px-Masked_Booby%2C_Grand_Turk%2C_TC.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Masked_Booby%2C_Grand_Turk%2C_TC.jpg/330px-Masked_Booby%2C_Grand_Turk%2C_TC.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Masked_Booby%2C_Grand_Turk%2C_TC.jpg/440px-Masked_Booby%2C_Grand_Turk%2C_TC.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2948" data-file-height="2352" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Masked_Booby,_Grand_Turk,_TC.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Subsp. <i>dactylatra</i> In flight, <a href="/wiki/Grand_Turk_Island" title="Grand Turk Island">Grand Turk Island</a></div></div></div>
<p>"Masked booby" has been designated the official <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">common name</a> by the <a href="/wiki/International_Ornithologists%27_Union" title="International Ornithologists&#39; Union">International Ornithologists' Union</a> (IOC).<sup id="cite_ref-ioclist_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioclist-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The species has also been called the masked gannet, blue-faced booby, white booby (for its plumage), and whistling booby (for its distinctive call).<sup id="cite_ref-fraser13_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fraser13-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The Australian ornithologist Doug Dorward promoted the name "white booby" as he felt the blue coloration of its face was less prominent than that of the <a href="/wiki/Red-footed_booby" title="Red-footed booby">red-footed booby</a> (<i>Sula sula</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDorward1962174_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDorward1962174-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The masked booby is one of six species of booby in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Sula_(genus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sula (genus)">Sula</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ioclist_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioclist-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> A 2011 genetic study (<i>depicted below</i>) using both <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_DNA" title="Nuclear DNA">nuclear</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> showed the masked and <a href="/wiki/Nazca_booby" title="Nazca booby">Nazca boobies</a> (<i>Sula granti</i>) to be each other's closest relatives, their lineage diverging from a line that gave rise to the <a href="/wiki/Blue-footed_booby" title="Blue-footed booby">blue-footed</a> (<i>Sula nebouxii</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Peruvian_booby" title="Peruvian booby">Peruvian boobies</a> (<i>Sula variegata</i>). The masked and Nazca boobies were divergent enough to indicate that the latter, formerly regarded as a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of the former, should be classified as a separate species. Molecular evidence suggests they most likely diverged between 0.8 and 1.1&#160;million years ago. Complex water currents in the eastern Pacific may have established an environmental barrier leading to <a href="/wiki/Speciation" title="Speciation">speciation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Subfossil bones 14,000 years old belonging to the species have been found in deposits on <a href="/wiki/Saint_Helena" title="Saint Helena">St. Helena</a> Island.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intraspecific_variation_and_subspecies">Intraspecific variation and subspecies</span></h3>
<p>There is a <a href="/wiki/Cline_(biology)" title="Cline (biology)">clinal</a> change in size across the masked booby's range. Birds in the Atlantic are the smallest, with the size increasing westwards though the Pacific to the Indian Ocean, where the largest individuals are found.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Genetic analysis using <a href="/wiki/MtDNA_control_region" title="MtDNA control region">mtDNA control region</a> sequences shows that populations in the Indian and Pacific Oceans greatly expanded around 180,000 years ago, and that these became separated from Atlantic populations around 115,000 years ago. Furthermore, within each ocean, there is evidence of reduced <a href="/wiki/Gene_flow" title="Gene flow">gene flow</a> between populations that does not correspond with any physical barrier.<sup id="cite_ref-steeves05_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-steeves05-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Four subspecies are recognized by the International Ornithologists' Union.<sup id="cite_ref-ioclist_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioclist-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Suladactfam3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Two white birds and a fluffy white chick in long grass with sea in background" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Suladactfam3.jpg/220px-Suladactfam3.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Suladactfam3.jpg/330px-Suladactfam3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Suladactfam3.jpg/440px-Suladactfam3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4032" data-file-height="3024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Suladactfam3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A breeding pair of subsp. <i>tasmani</i> and their chick, <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_Island" title="Norfolk Island">Norfolk Island</a></div></div></div>
<ul><li><i>S. d. dactylatra</i> <small>Lesson, 1831</small></li></ul>
<dl><dd>Breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> and some Atlantic islands including <a href="/wiki/Ascension_Island" title="Ascension Island">Ascension Island</a>. There is significant genetic divergence between birds on <a href="/wiki/Boatswain_Bird_Island" title="Boatswain Bird Island">Boatswain Bird Island</a> off Ascension and those from <a href="/wiki/Monito_Island" title="Monito Island">Monito Island</a> off Puerto Rico.<sup id="cite_ref-steeves05_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-steeves05-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<ul><li><i>S. d. melanops</i> <small>Hartlaub, 1859</small></li></ul>
<dl><dd>Breeds in the western <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Redman_Stevenson_Fanshawe_2016_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Redman_Stevenson_Fanshawe_2016-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The German ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Hartlaub" title="Gustav Hartlaub">Gustav Hartlaub</a> described this <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxon</a> in 1859 from Maydh Island off the coast of Somalia near the <a href="/wiki/Maydh" title="Maydh">town of the same name</a>. He noted its black mask and blue-grey feet to be distinct from Sundevall's <i>cyanops</i> with a blue face, and Lesson's <i>dactylatra</i> with yellow feet.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The subspecies name is derived from the Ancient Greek words <i>mela(no)s</i>, meaning "black",<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELiddellScott1980431_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELiddellScott1980431-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and <i>ops</i>, meaning "face".</dd></dl>
<ul><li><i>S. d. tasmani</i> <small>van Tets, Meredith, Fullagar &amp; Davidson, 1988</small> (includes <i>S. d. fullagari</i> as a <a href="/wiki/Junior_synonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Junior synonym">junior synonym</a>): Tasman booby</li></ul>
<dl><dd>The form breeding on <a href="/wiki/Lord_Howe_Island" title="Lord Howe Island">Lord Howe</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kermadec_Islands" title="Kermadec Islands">Kermadec Islands</a>. The New Zealand naturalist <a href="/wiki/Walter_Oliver" title="Walter Oliver">Walter Oliver</a> had noted that this bird had dark brown rather than pale irises in 1930, but it was not until 1990 that it was formally investigated by R. M. O'Brien and J. Davies and found to also have longer wings than other populations. They classified it as a new subspecies: <i>S. d. fullagari</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Meanwhile, large prehistoric specimens known from the Lord Howe and <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_Island" title="Norfolk Island">Norfolk Island</a> had been classified as a separate species, <i>S. tasmani</i>, in 1988, thought to have become extinct due to Polynesian and then European seafarers and settlers.<sup id="cite_ref-vantets88_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vantets88-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> However, the paleoecologist Richard Holdaway and colleagues cast doubt on the distinctness of the fossil taxon in 2001,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> and a 2010 review by the New Zealand biologist Tammy Steeves and colleagues of the fossil material and DNA found the two overlapped considerably, and hence the extinct and living entities were found to be the same taxon, now known as <i>S. d. tasmani</i> as this name has priority over <i>S. d. fullagari</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Fieldwork in the Kermadec Islands indicates the bills of adults are bright yellow, and that adult males had brighter yellow feet than females.<sup id="cite_ref-ismar_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ismar-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<ul><li><i>S. d. personata</i> <small>Gould, 1846</small> (includes <i>S. d. californica</i> and <i>S. d. bedouti</i>)</li></ul>
<dl><dd>Breeds in the central and western <a href="/wiki/Pacific" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific">Pacific</a> and around <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, as well as off <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and on <a href="/wiki/Clipperton_Island" title="Clipperton Island">Clipperton Island</a> in the Pacific Ocean off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>. Birds of the latter two locations have been separated as subspecies <i>californica</i>, and the north west Australian population has been named as subspecies <i>bedouti</i>, but neither is usually considered distinct;<sup id="cite_ref-ioclist_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioclist-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> the American biologist Robert Pitman and colleagues found no consistent differences between these three subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-pitman1998_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitman1998-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kure_Masked_Booby_juveniles.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Seven brownish birds sitting on a large log on a beach" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Kure_Masked_Booby_juveniles.jpg/220px-Kure_Masked_Booby_juveniles.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Kure_Masked_Booby_juveniles.jpg/330px-Kure_Masked_Booby_juveniles.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Kure_Masked_Booby_juveniles.jpg/440px-Kure_Masked_Booby_juveniles.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kure_Masked_Booby_juveniles.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Juveniles of subsp <i>personata</i> on <a href="/wiki/Kure_Atoll" title="Kure Atoll">Kure Atoll</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian island</a> chain</div></div></div>
<p>The largest species of booby, the masked booby ranges from 75 to 85&#160;cm (30 to 33&#160;in) long, with a 160–170&#160;cm (63–67&#160;in) wingspan and 1.2–2.2&#160;kg (2.6–4.9&#160;lb) weight. It has a typical <a href="/wiki/Sulidae" title="Sulidae">sulid</a> body shape, with a long pointed bill, long neck, aerodynamic body, long slender wings and pointed tail. The adult is bright white with dark wings and a dark tail.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990763_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990763-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The sexes have similar plumage with no seasonal variation, but females are on average slightly heavier and larger than males.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990763,_771–772_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990763,_771–772-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The bare skin around the face, throat and <a href="/wiki/Lore_(anatomy)" title="Lore (anatomy)">lores</a> is described either as black or blue-black. It contrasts with the white plumage and gives a mask-like appearance.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990763,_771_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990763,_771-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The bill of the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> is pale yellow with a greenish tinge, sometimes greyish at the base.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Conical in shape, the bill is longer than the head and tapers to a slightly downcurved tip. Backward-pointing <a href="/wiki/Serration" title="Serration">serrations</a> line the mandibles.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990772_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990772-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Primaries" title="Flight feather">primaries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Secondaries" title="Flight feather">secondaries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Tertials" title="Flight feather">humerals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Rectrices" title="Flight feather">rectrices</a> are brown-black. The inner webs of the secondaries are white at the base. The underwing is white except for the brown-black flight-feathers that are not covered by the white <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Tectrices" title="Flight feather">coverts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990770_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990770-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The legs are yellow-orange or olive.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The iris is yellow.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990771_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990771-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The subspecies differ slightly in size and sometimes also in the colour of the irises, bill, legs and feet. The race <i>melanops</i> has an orange-yellow bill and olive-grey legs, the race <i>tasmani</i> has dark brown irises and dark grey-green legs and the race <i>personata</i> has olive to blueish-grey legs.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> For the subspecies <i>tasmani</i> and the nominate <i>dactylatra</i>, during the breeding season, the leg colour of male birds contains more yellow-red than those of the females.<sup id="cite_ref-ismar_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ismar-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The juvenile is a streaked or mottled grey-brown on the head and upperparts, with a whitish neck collar. The wings are dark brown and underparts are white. Its bill is yellowish, face is blue-grey and iris a dark brown. Older immature birds have a broader white collar and rump,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990763_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990763-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and more and more white feathers on the head until the head is wholly white by 14 to 15 months of age. Full adult plumage is acquired three to four months before the bird turns three years old.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990771_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990771-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The masked booby is usually silent at sea, but is noisy at the nesting colonies. The main call of male birds is a descending whistle; that of females is a loud honk.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990769_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990769-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The adult masked booby is distinguished from the related Nazca booby by its yellow rather than orange bill, larger size and less distinctive sexual dimorphism. The latter nests on steep cliffs rather than flat ground.<sup id="cite_ref-pitman1998_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitman1998-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The white <a href="/wiki/Morph_(zoology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Morph (zoology)">morph</a> of the red-footed booby is similar but smaller.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Abbott%27s_booby" title="Abbott&#39;s booby">Abbott's booby</a> (<i>Papasula abbotti</i>) has a more wholly black upperwing, and a longer neck and tail and larger head, while the <a href="/wiki/Cape_gannet" title="Cape gannet">Cape gannet</a> (<i>Morus capensis</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Australasian_gannet" title="Australasian gannet">Australasian gannet</a> (<i>Morus serrator</i>) have a buff-yellow crown, shorter tail, white <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#tertiaries" title="Glossary of bird terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of bird terms § tertiaries" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">humerals</span></a></dfn> and a grey rather than yellowish bill. The juvenile masked booby resembles the <a href="/wiki/Brown_booby" title="Brown booby">brown booby</a> (<i>Sula leucogaster</i>), though adults of that species have clearly demarcated brown and white plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990763_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990763-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>The masked booby is found across tropical oceans between the <a href="/wiki/30th_parallel_north" title="30th parallel north">30th parallel north</a> and <a href="/wiki/30th_parallel_south" title="30th parallel south">30th parallel south</a>. In the Indian Ocean it ranges from the coastlines of the Arabian Peninsula and East Africa across to Sumatra and Western Australia, though it is not found off the coast of the Indian subcontinent. Off the Western Australian coastline it is found as far south as the <a href="/wiki/Dampier_Archipelago" title="Dampier Archipelago">Dampier Archipelago</a>. In the Pacific, it ranges from Brisbane eastwards. It is found in the Caribbean and Atlantic Ocean south to Ascension Island.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990764_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990764-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> In the eastern Pacific off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, the masked booby is replaced by the Nazca booby.<sup id="cite_ref-pitman1998_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitman1998-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrant</a> was rescued in 2015 in <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Oregon" title="Newport, Oregon">Newport, Oregon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the Atlantic, Caribbean birds occasionally wander north to warm southern <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a> waters off the eastern seaboard of the United States, with single records from <a href="/wiki/Island_Beach_State_Park" title="Island Beach State Park">Island Beach</a> in New Jersey and New York.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> There are summer records from <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Bay" title="Delaware Bay">Delaware Bay</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Worcester_County,_Maryland" title="Worcester County, Maryland">Worcester County, Maryland</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> as well as waters off the coast of Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-Peterson1967_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peterson1967-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the monsoon season (midyear), the masked booby is an occasional vagrant along the western coast of India, with records from <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karnataka" title="Karnataka">Karnataka</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Maharashtra" title="Maharashtra">Maharashtra</a> states.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> It is a vagrant to the <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Islands" title="Caroline Islands">Caroline Islands</a> north of New Guinea.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding_colonies">Breeding colonies</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Starr_060228-6311_Chenopodium_oahuense.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Birds nesting across a flat rocky area near the sea" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Starr_060228-6311_Chenopodium_oahuense.jpg/220px-Starr_060228-6311_Chenopodium_oahuense.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Starr_060228-6311_Chenopodium_oahuense.jpg/330px-Starr_060228-6311_Chenopodium_oahuense.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Starr_060228-6311_Chenopodium_oahuense.jpg/440px-Starr_060228-6311_Chenopodium_oahuense.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Starr_060228-6311_Chenopodium_oahuense.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Breeding sites in areas relatively free of vegetation, Oahu</div></div></div>
<p>Breeding colonies are located on remote islands, atolls and cays.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990763_30-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990763-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Lord Howe Island is the southernmost colony.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990764_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990764-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Deep water nearby is important for feeding. As an example, waters around <a href="/wiki/Raine_Island" title="Raine Island">Raine Island</a>, at the edge of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Barrier_Reef" title="Great Barrier Reef">Great Barrier Reef</a>, are anywhere from 180 to 3,700&#160;m (590 to 12,140&#160;ft) deep.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990763_30-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990763-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> On these landforms, masked boobies select sites of generally flat, bare or exposed open ground that lie above the high-tide level with access to the ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990764_38-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990764-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> During the breeding season, the species remains near the colony. At other times, juveniles and some adults disperse widely, though some remain at the colony year-round. Most (but not all) birds return to breed at the colony of their birth; once they begin breeding at a site, they will return there annually.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990763_30-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990763-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The largest masked booby colony is on Clipperton Island in the eastern Pacific Ocean,<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_19-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> a desert atoll southwest of Mexico. In 2003, 112,000 birds were counted, having recovered from 150 individuals in 1958. The population had suffered from the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Feral_pigs" class="mw-redirect" title="Feral pigs">feral pigs</a> in the 1890s. These pigs preyed on the crabs that ate the vegetation. After the elimination of pigs in 1964, the crab population rose and vegetation largely disappeared. This was beneficial to the boobies, as they prefer open ground.<sup id="cite_ref-Pitman_et_al._2005_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pitman_et_al._2005-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Clipperton is on a narrow ridge surrounded by deep water.<sup id="cite_ref-Weimerskirch08_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weimerskirch08-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The colony on Lord Howe Island numbered in the thousands at the time of the island's discovery in 1788, but has declined to under 500 pairs—mostly on offshore islets with the remainder on two hard-to-access headlands—by 2005. Hunting by humans is thought to have played a role; although rats were introduced to the island in 1918, there has been no evidence they are able to kill chicks or eggs—possibly due to the size of the adult boobies.<sup id="cite_ref-Priddel05_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Priddel05-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> The masked booby was first recorded breeding on <a href="/wiki/Phillip_Island_(Norfolk_Island)" title="Phillip Island (Norfolk Island)">Philip Island</a> off Norfolk Island in 1908, with devegetation by feral animals creating the open ground preferred by the species. By 2007, an estimated 300 pairs were breeding over the island, though the island flora's regeneration after the removal of feral animals might begin to limit suitable nesting sites.<sup id="cite_ref-priddel10_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-priddel10-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> In 2006, two pairs nested in a brown booby colony on Morros del Potosí (White Friars Rocks) near <a href="/wiki/Zihuatanejo" title="Zihuatanejo">Zihuatanejo</a> in southern Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Major nesting areas in the Atlantic include <a href="/wiki/Rocas_Atoll" title="Rocas Atoll">Rocas Atoll</a> off the coast of Brazil,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Ascension Island in the south Atlantic,<sup id="cite_ref-ratcliffe_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ratcliffe-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> and five islands of the <a href="/wiki/Campeche_Bank" title="Campeche Bank">Campeche Bank</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> The species attempted to nest at <a href="/wiki/Dry_Tortugas" title="Dry Tortugas">Dry Tortugas</a> in the Gulf of Mexico over 1984 and 1985;<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> 19 pairs were recorded there in 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The masked booby generally flies at least 7&#160;m (23&#160;ft) in height, and at speeds of up to 70&#160;km/h (43&#160;mph). It alternates between gliding and active flying with strong periodic wingbeats. It is often encountered alone, or in a small group when returning to its colony.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990763_30-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990763-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Regarding the masked booby's longevity, a bird tagged at <a href="/wiki/Nepean_Island_(Norfolk_Island)" title="Nepean Island (Norfolk Island)">Nepean Island</a> (off Norfolk Island) in September 1979 was recovered and released after being caught in fishing gear 24 years and 9.9 months later some 713&#160;km (443&#160;mi) away off the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Pines_(New_Caledonia)" title="Isle of Pines (New Caledonia)">Isle of Pines</a>, New Caledonia in July 2004. The longest distance travelled is 3,152&#160;km (1,959&#160;mi); a bird tagged at Raine Island in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Barrier_Reef" title="Great Barrier Reef">Great Barrier Reef</a> in December 1981 was picked up and released at Phillip Island (off Norfolk Island) in December 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding_and_courtship">Breeding and courtship</span></h3>
<p>The masked booby begins breeding by around four or five years of age, though can occasionally do so at three years old.<sup id="cite_ref-florida_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-florida-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Adults form <a href="/wiki/Monogamy_in_animals" title="Monogamy in animals">monogamous</a> relationships with many pairs remaining together over multiple breeding seasons. Highly territorial when nesting, single males and mated pairs engage in <a href="/wiki/Agonistic_behaviour" title="Agonistic behaviour">agonistic displays</a> to mark their ground against neighbours and interlopers. The male advertises his territory to females by <i>flight circuiting</i>—making a short flight and holding his wings in a 'V' shape and making a call as he lands. The mated pair engages in <i>outposting</i> as other boobies fly overhead, stretching their necks out and forward. More direct trespassers are confronted with a <i>yes-no headshaking</i>, in which the booby shakes its head from side to side or up and down and ruffles its head feathers to make its head look bigger and facial markings more prominent. It may cock its tail and hold its wings up away from its body.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990767_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990767-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Neighbouring boobies may escalate by jabbing and lunging at each other. In the <i>pelican posture</i>, a bird tucks the tip of its bill into its chest, possibly positioned to avoid injury to others. This posture is used against intruders or as advertising for a mate.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990768_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990768-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are several displays related to the establishment and maintenance of pair-bonding. The male initiates <i>sky-pointing</i> when a female approaches or leaves his territory. In this display, he paces slowly with his neck and bill pointed upwards—between vertical and 45 degrees—with wings partly raised and whistling faintly with an open bill. In a <i>gazing</i> display, one bird stares at another of the opposite sex; this generally leads to other displays. Pairs engage in a (mostly) gentler form of jabbing display, and <a href="/wiki/Preening_(bird)#Allopreening" title="Preening (bird)">allopreening</a>. In an <i>oblique headshake</i>, a bird flings its head vigorously. The male may also parade in front of the female, walking with an exaggerated high-stepping gait and intermittently tucking his head in his breast, after collecting nesting material and before the pair begins laying. The male presents small sticks and debris as nesting material in a gesture of symbolic nest-building, which leads to copulation. Afterwards, the pair engages in more symbolic nest-building.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990768_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990768-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> The twigs and debris are cleared away later as none is actually used in adorning the nest while in use.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990769_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990769-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Breeding takes place at different times of year throughout its range. On the <a href="/wiki/Cocos_(Keeling)_Islands" title="Cocos (Keeling) Islands">Cocos (Keeling) Islands</a>, egg-laying takes place from January to July, peaking in June, with juvenile birds from April to December. On Moulter Cay in the <a href="/wiki/Coral_Sea" title="Coral Sea">Coral Sea</a>, breeding takes place year-round, with egg-laying peaking from September to early November, while on nearby Raine Island birds begin laying in or after August, likely peaking September to early November.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990769_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990769-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Eggs are laid between May and September on Lord Howe Island,<sup id="cite_ref-Priddel05_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Priddel05-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> and early July to early January (peaking in September) on Phillip Island.<sup id="cite_ref-priddel10_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-priddel10-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the northern hemisphere, egg-laying on <a href="/wiki/Kure_Atoll" title="Kure Atoll">Kure Atoll</a> can be any time from January to early July, peaking in February and March.<sup id="cite_ref-woodward72_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-woodward72-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> On Clipperton Island, egg-laying peaks in November to coordinate with peak fish productivity of the surrounding waters in January (for growing chicks).<sup id="cite_ref-Weimerskirch08_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weimerskirch08-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Masked boobies lay at any time in the Caribbean, peaking between March and September.<sup id="cite_ref-florida_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-florida-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The nest is a cleared area 0.75 to 1&#160;m (2&#160;ft 6&#160;in to 3&#160;ft 3&#160;in) in diameter, within which is a clearly demarcated 25 to 30&#160;cm (10 to 12&#160;in) shallow (1–2&#160;cm (0.4–0.8&#160;in) deep) depression. A clutch of two chalky white eggs is laid, with an interval of five to eight days between the laying of each egg. Occasionally nests with three eggs are reported; these are probably due to an egg from another nest rolling downhill into the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990769_37-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990769-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The eggs have an average size of 64&#160;mm ×&#160;45&#160;mm (2.5&#160;in ×&#160;1.8&#160;in) and weigh 75&#160;g (2.6&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990769_37-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990769-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> They are incubated by both adults for 45 days.<sup id="cite_ref-Priddel05_49-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Priddel05-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Parents incubate the eggs by resting on their <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#tarsi" title="Glossary of bird terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of bird terms § tarsi" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">tarsi</span></a></dfn> and wrapping their webbed feet over the eggs, with the outermost toes resting on the ground. Their feet are more vascular at this time.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDorward1962185–186_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDorward1962185–186-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> When first hatched, the chicks are about 10&#160;cm (3.9&#160;in) long and weigh around 40–60&#160;g (1.4–2.1&#160;oz), with a sparse covering of white down over their grey to pinkish-grey skin. <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">Altricial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nidicolous" title="Nidicolous">nidicolous</a>, their eyes are open at birth. Their down thickens as they age, and the chicks are quite fluffy by week 5–6. The primaries and rectrices appear by week 8, and <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#scapulars" title="Glossary of bird terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of bird terms § scapulars" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">scapulars</span></a></dfn> appear by week 10. They begin losing their down from week 12 onwards, until they are wholly covered by juvenile plumage by week 15 or 16, and <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> at around 120 days (17 weeks) of age.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990770_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990770-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> After leaving the nest, young birds are dependent on their parents for 3–4 weeks before dispersing out to sea.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDorward1962179_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDorward1962179-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although two eggs are often laid, the younger chick almost always perishes within a few days. This has been observed widely across the species' range. Dorward suspected <a href="/wiki/Siblicide" title="Siblicide">siblicide</a> on Ascension Island.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDorward1962194–197_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDorward1962194–197-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Siblicide has been observed in the Nazca booby on the <a href="/wiki/Galapagos_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Galapagos Islands">Galapagos Islands</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> and is assumed to occur in the masked booby as well.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDorward1962194–197_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDorward1962194–197-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Priddel05_49-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Priddel05-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cheilopogon_melanurusPCCA20070623-3956B.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Cheilopogon_melanurusPCCA20070623-3956B.jpg/220px-Cheilopogon_melanurusPCCA20070623-3956B.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Cheilopogon_melanurusPCCA20070623-3956B.jpg/330px-Cheilopogon_melanurusPCCA20070623-3956B.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Cheilopogon_melanurusPCCA20070623-3956B.jpg/440px-Cheilopogon_melanurusPCCA20070623-3956B.jpg 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="387" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cheilopogon_melanurusPCCA20070623-3956B.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Flying fish, such as the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_flyingfish" title="Atlantic flyingfish">Atlantic flyingfish</a>, are a common prey item.</div></div></div>
<p>The masked booby is a spectacular diver, plunging vertically or near-vertically from heights of anywhere from 12 to 100&#160;m (40 to 330&#160;ft)—but more commonly 15 to 35&#160;m (50 to 115&#160;ft)—above the water into the ocean at high speed, to depths of up to 3&#160;m (9.8&#160;ft) in search of fish.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990765_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990765-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> It generally swallows its catch underwater.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990766_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990766-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> Fieldwork at Clipperton Island showed that masked boobies flew on average to 103&#160;km (64&#160;mi) from their colony, with a maximum range of 242&#160;km (150&#160;mi), while feeding their chicks. They did not rest at sea at night, though part of their return trip was at night time for longer expeditions.<sup id="cite_ref-Weimerskirch08_48-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weimerskirch08-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The masked booby forages with the <a href="/wiki/White-bellied_storm_petrel" title="White-bellied storm petrel">white-bellied storm petrel</a> (<i>Fregetta grallaria</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Bulwer%27s_petrel" title="Bulwer&#39;s petrel">Bulwer's petrel</a> (<i>Bulweria bulwerii</i>) at times.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Frigatebird" title="Frigatebird">Frigatebirds</a> often harass the species until they disgorge their catch and steal their food.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_19-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">Fish</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Flying_fish" title="Flying fish">flying fish</a>, up to 28&#160;cm (11&#160;in) long (rarely up to 41&#160;cm (16&#160;in)) form the bulk of its diet, along with <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">cephalopods</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_19-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Species eaten include various species of flying fish such as <a href="/wiki/Blue_flyingfish" class="mw-redirect" title="Blue flyingfish">blue flyingfish</a> (<i>Exocoetus volitans</i>), <a href="/wiki/Mirrorwing_flyingfish" title="Mirrorwing flyingfish">mirrorwing flyingfish</a> (<i>Hirundichthys speculiger</i>), <a href="/wiki/Sailfin_flyingfish" class="mw-redirect" title="Sailfin flyingfish">sailfin flyingfish</a> (<i>Parexocoetus brachypterus</i>), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Glider_flyingfish&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Glider flyingfish (page does not exist)">glider flyingfish</a> (<i>Cheilopogon atrisignis</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_flyingfish" title="Atlantic flyingfish">Atlantic flyingfish</a> (<i>Cheilopogon melanurus</i>), other fish such as <a href="/wiki/Yellowtail_amberjack" title="Yellowtail amberjack">yellowtail amberjack</a> (<i>Seriola lalandi</i>), <a href="/wiki/Skipjack_tuna" title="Skipjack tuna">skipjack tuna</a> (<i>Katsuwonus pelamis</i>), <a href="/wiki/Mackerel_scad" title="Mackerel scad">mackerel scad</a> (<i>Decapterus macarellus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Pompano_dolphinfish" title="Pompano dolphinfish">pompano dolphinfish</a> (<i>Coryphaena equiselis</i>), <a href="/wiki/Mahi-mahi" title="Mahi-mahi">mahi-mahi</a> (<i>Coryphaena hippurus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Brown_chub" class="mw-redirect" title="Brown chub">brown chub</a> (<i>Kyphosus bigibbus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Redbarred_hawkfish" title="Redbarred hawkfish">redbarred hawkfish</a> (<i>Cirrhitops fasciatus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Snake_mackerel" title="Snake mackerel">snake mackerel</a> (<i>Gempylus serpens</i>), <a href="/wiki/Frigate_tuna" title="Frigate tuna">frigate tuna</a> (<i>Auxis thazard</i>), <a href="/wiki/Pacific_saury" title="Pacific saury">Pacific saury</a> (<i>Cololabis saira</i>), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Euleptorhamphus_viridis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Euleptorhamphus viridis (page does not exist)">ribbon halfbeak</a> (<i>Euleptorhamphus viridis</i>), <a href="/wiki/Flat_needlefish" title="Flat needlefish">flat needlefish</a> (<i>Ablennes hians</i>) and mullet of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Mugil" title="Mugil">Mugil</a></i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Sthenoteuthis_oualaniensis" title="Sthenoteuthis oualaniensis">purpleback flying squid</a> (<i>Sthenoteuthis oualaniensis</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990766_69-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarchantHiggins1990766-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators_and_parasites">Predators and parasites</span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Silver_gull" title="Silver gull">Silver gulls</a> (<i>Chroicocephalus novaehollandiae</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Buff-banded_rail" title="Buff-banded rail">buff-banded rails</a> (<i>Gallirallus philippensis</i>) prey on eggs and young. On some islands such as Ascension and Saint Helena, feral cats have been a threat to masked boobies.<sup id="cite_ref-ratcliffe_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ratcliffe-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> The tick species <i>Ornithodoros (Alectorobius) muesebecki</i> was described parasitising nesting blue-faced boobies off the Arabian coast.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Argasidae" title="Argasidae">argasid</a> tick <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ornithodoros_capensis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ornithodoros capensis (page does not exist)">Ornithodoros capensis</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/Ixodidae" title="Ixodidae">ixodid</a> tick <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amblyomma_loculosum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Amblyomma loculosum (page does not exist)">Amblyomma loculosum</a></i> have also been recorded as parasites, the latter possibly spreading <a href="/wiki/Babesiosis" title="Babesiosis">piroplasmosis</a> caused by <i><a href="/wiki/Babesia" title="Babesia">Babesia</a></i> among boobies.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> On Raine Island and Pandora Cay, nests have been destroyed by <a href="/wiki/Green_sea_turtle" title="Green sea turtle">green sea turtles</a> (<i>Chelonia mydas</i>) as they pass through booby colonies and dig their own nests in large numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> Rats prey on eggs and young of many seabirds, though the size of masked boobies probably prevents direct predation. On Clipperton Island, rats prey on the crab that eats vegetation.<sup id="cite_ref-Pitman_et_al._2005_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pitman_et_al._2005-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno" title="Taíno">Taíno</a> ate masked and red-footed boobies that nested on <a href="/wiki/Grand_Turk_Island" title="Grand Turk Island">Grand Turk Island</a> around 1000 years ago. The two species subsequently vanished from the Turks and Caicos Islands. A booby yielded around 1–2&#160;kg (2–5&#160;lb) of meat. European sailors in the area also caught and ate tame boobies.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> Masked booby young and eggs were eaten by the crew of <a href="/wiki/HMS_Supply_(1759)" title="HMS Supply (1759)">HMS&#160;<i>Supply</i></a> on Lord Howe Island.<sup id="cite_ref-vantets88_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vantets88-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_the_Conservation_of_Nature" class="mw-redirect" title="International Union for the Conservation of Nature">International Union for the Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN) lists the masked booby as a species of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>, though the population worldwide is decreasing.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> At Clipperton Island, the colony was benefitted by the presence of <a href="/wiki/Yellowfin_tuna" title="Yellowfin tuna">yellowfin tuna</a> (<i>Thunnus albacares</i>), which drove their prey item—flying fish—to the surface, facilitating predation by boobies. It may be that overfishing of tuna adversely impacts the availability of fish there.<sup id="cite_ref-Weimerskirch08_48-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weimerskirch08-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> In 2005, 508 young masked boobies at the colony suffered from "<a href="/wiki/Angel_wing" title="Angel wing">angel wing</a>", a congenital deformity of one or both wings resulting in flightlessness. This coincided with a season of high nestling mortality that was likely related to low numbers of yellowfin tuna due to possible overfishing at a crucial time in the breeding season.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> The warm phase (<a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a>) of the <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño–Southern Oscillation">El Niño–Southern Oscillation</a> in 1982 and 1983 negatively impacted breeding on Christmas Island as the higher water temperatures reduced food supply. Where usually 1500 pairs nested, no young were observed over this period; 50–60 pairs were observed breeding in October 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-Schreiber84_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schreiber84-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> The Australian government has rated both subspecies occurring in Australian waters as vulnerable to climate change. The low-lying colonies of subspecies <i>personata</i> are at risk from rising sea levels, and the rising sea temperatures are calculated to reduce food productivity, which may impact on breeding success of both subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
