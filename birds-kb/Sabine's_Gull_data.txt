<p>The <b>Sabine's Gull</b> <sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> is a small <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a>. Its generic placement is disputed; some authors treat it as the sole species in the genus <i>Xema</i> as <i><b>Xema sabini</b></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> while others retain it in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Larus" title="Larus">Larus</a></i> as <i><b>Larus sabini</b></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-bwp_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ebcc_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebcc-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> It breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">arctic</a> and has a circumpolar distribution through northernmost <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>. It <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrates</a> south in autumn; most of the population winters at sea in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific" class="mw-redirect">Pacific</a> off western <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> in the cold waters of the <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_Current" title="Humboldt Current">Humboldt Current</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and eastern Canadian <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> cross the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> by way of the westernmost fringes of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> to winter off southwest <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> in the cold waters of the <a href="/wiki/Benguela_Current" title="Benguela Current">Benguela Current</a>. Occasionally individual Sabine's Gulls can be seen off other coasts such as the northeastern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> or further east in Europe, typically following autumn storms.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species is easy to identify through its striking wing pattern. The adult has a pale grey back and wing coverts, black primary flight feathers and white secondaries. The white tail is forked. The male's hood darkens during breeding season. Their bills are black with a yellow tip. Young birds have a similar tricoloured wing pattern, but the grey is replaced by brown, and the tail has a black terminal band. The juveniles take two years to attain full adult plumage. They have a very high-pitched and squeaking call.<sup id="cite_ref-bwp_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Sabine's Gull breeds in colonies on coasts and <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>, laying two or three spotted olive-brown eggs in a ground nest lined with grass. It is very pelagic outside the breeding season. It takes a wide variety of mainly animal food, and will eat any suitable small prey. It also steals eggs from nesting colonies of <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Tern" title="Arctic Tern">Arctic Terns</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The specific epithet and common name honour the Irish scientist Sir <a href="/wiki/Edward_Sabine" title="Edward Sabine">Edward Sabine</a>, who had sent a specimen to his brother <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Sabine" title="Joseph Sabine">Joseph Sabine</a>; the first description was in 1819.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabine%27s_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and evolution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_evolution">Taxonomy and evolution</span></h2>
<p>The Sabine's Gull is usually treated as comprising a monotypic genus, it is only placed within the genus <i>Larus</i> when the genus is enlarged. The black bill and notched tail is almost unique within the gulls, characteristics shared only with the <a href="/wiki/Swallow-tailed_Gull" title="Swallow-tailed Gull">Swallow-tailed Gull</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Galapagos" title="Galapagos" class="mw-redirect">Galapagos</a>. On the basis of this the two species were often thought to be each other's closest relatives, a hypothesis ruled out by a number of behaviour and ecological differences. Mitochondrial DNA studies confirmed this, and the closest relative of the Sabine's Gull is now thought to be the <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Gull" title="Ivory Gull">Ivory Gull</a>, another Arctic species. The two species are thought to have separated a long time ago, around 2 million years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabine%27s_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Xema_sabini_-Iceland_-flying-8_(6).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Xema_sabini_-Iceland_-flying-8_%286%29.jpg/220px-Xema_sabini_-Iceland_-flying-8_%286%29.jpg" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Xema_sabini_-Iceland_-flying-8_(6).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Flying in Iceland</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Sabine's Gull is a small gull, 27–33 cm (11–13 in) in length and weighing 135–225 g (4.8–7.9 oz). The wings are long, thin and pointed with a span of between 81–87 cm (32–34 in). The bill, which is black with a yellow tip, is around 2.5&#160;cm (0.98&#160;in) long.</p>
