<p><b>Sabine's gull</b> <sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> (<i>Xema sabini</i>), also known as the <b>fork-tailed gull</b> or <b>xeme</b>, is a small <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a>. Its generic placement is disputed; some authors treat it as the <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monotypic">sole species</a> in the genus <i><b>Xema</b></i> as <i><b>Xema sabini</b></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> while others retain it in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Larus" title="Larus">Larus</a></i> as <i><b>Larus sabini</b></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-bwp_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ebcc_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebcc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Sabine's gull breeds in colonies on coasts and <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>, laying two or three spotted olive-brown <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> in a ground nest lined with <a href="/wiki/Grass" class="mw-redirect" title="Grass">grass</a>. It is very pelagic outside the breeding season. It takes a wide variety of mainly animal food, and will eat any suitable small <a href="/wiki/Predator" class="mw-redirect" title="Predator">prey</a>. It also steals eggs from nesting colonies of <a href="/wiki/Arctic_tern" title="Arctic tern">Arctic terns</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabine%27s_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Xema_sabini_-Iceland_-flying-8_(6).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Xema_sabini_-Iceland_-flying-8_%286%29.jpg/220px-Xema_sabini_-Iceland_-flying-8_%286%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Xema_sabini_-Iceland_-flying-8_%286%29.jpg/330px-Xema_sabini_-Iceland_-flying-8_%286%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Xema_sabini_-Iceland_-flying-8_%286%29.jpg/440px-Xema_sabini_-Iceland_-flying-8_%286%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="512" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Xema_sabini_-Iceland_-flying-8_(6).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult flying in Iceland</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:8I3A2002.CR2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/8I3A2002.CR2.jpg/220px-8I3A2002.CR2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/8I3A2002.CR2.jpg/330px-8I3A2002.CR2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/8I3A2002.CR2.jpg/440px-8I3A2002.CR2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1437" data-file-height="1077" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:8I3A2002.CR2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sabine's gull flying at the fjord <a href="/wiki/Trygghamna" title="Trygghamna">Trygghamna</a> in Spitsbergen. Photographed by Shlomo Yona.</div></div></div>
<p>The Sabine's gull is a small gull, 27&#160;to 33&#160;cm (11–13&#160;in) in length and weighing 135&#160;to 225&#160;g (4.8–7.9&#160;oz). The wings are long, thin and pointed with a span of between 81&#160;to 87&#160;cm (32–34&#160;in). The bill, which is black with a yellow tip, is around 2.5&#160;cm (0.98&#160;in) long.
</p><p>This species is easy to identify through its striking wing pattern. The adult has a pale grey back and wing coverts, black primary flight feathers and white secondaries. The white tail is forked. The male's hood darkens during breeding season. Young birds have a similar tricoloured wing pattern, but the grey is replaced by brown, and the tail has a black terminal band. Juveniles take two years to attain full adult <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>. Sabine's gulls have an unusual molt pattern for gulls. Fledged birds retain their juvenile plumage through the autumn and do not start molting into their first winter plumage until they have reached their wintering grounds. Adults have their complete molt in the spring prior to the spring migration, and have a partial molt in the autumn after returning to the wintering area, a reversal of the usual pattern for gulls.<sup id="cite_ref-Montevecchi_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Montevecchi-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> They have a very high-pitched and squeaking call.<sup id="cite_ref-bwp_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_evolution">Taxonomy and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabine%27s_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Sabine's gull is usually treated as comprising a monotypic genus; it is only placed within the genus <i>Larus</i> when the genus is enlarged. The black <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> and notched tail are almost unique within the gulls, as they are shared only with the <a href="/wiki/Swallow-tailed_gull" title="Swallow-tailed gull">swallow-tailed gull</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Galapagos" class="mw-redirect" title="Galapagos">Galapagos</a>. On the basis of this the two species were often thought to be each other's closest relatives, a hypothesis ruled out by a number of behaviour and ecological differences. <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">Mitochondrial DNA</a> studies confirmed that they are not closely related, and the closest relative of the Sabine's gull is now thought to be the <a href="/wiki/Ivory_gull" title="Ivory gull">ivory gull</a>, another Arctic species. The two species are thought to have separated around 2 million years ago, longer ago than most groups of gull species.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Geographical variation is slight; birds from <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> are slightly darker and perhaps bigger. Most authorities recognise no races, but a few recognise four based on size and mantle (back) colour.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Handbook_of_the_Birds_of_the_World" title="Handbook of the Birds of the World">Handbook of the Birds of the World</a> recognises four subspecies. The nominate subspecies, <i>X. s. sabini</i>, breeds from the Canadian Arctic to <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>. <i>X. s. palaearctica</i> (Stegman, 1934) breeds from <a href="/wiki/Spitsbergen" title="Spitsbergen">Spitsbergen</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Taymyr_Peninsula" title="Taymyr Peninsula">Taymyr Peninsula</a> in Russia, and <i>X. s. tschuktschorum</i> (Portenko, 1939) breeds on the <a href="/wiki/Chukotskiy_Peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Chukotskiy Peninsula">Chukotskiy Peninsula</a> of Russia, and <i>X. s. woznesenskii</i> (Portenko, 1939) is found from  the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Anadyr" title="Gulf of Anadyr">Gulf of Anadyr</a> to Alaska.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The specific epithet and common name honour the Irish scientist Sir <a href="/wiki/Edward_Sabine" title="Edward Sabine">Edward Sabine</a>, who sent a specimen to his brother <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Sabine" title="Joseph Sabine">Joseph Sabine</a>; Joseph referred to it as <i>Larus sabini</i> in 1818.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> (The first accepted description was by Leach in 1819.) The <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> gives the pronunciation <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="/æ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;bad&#39;">æ</span><span title="&#39;b&#39; in &#39;buy&#39;">b</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span style="font-size:90%">SAB</span>-eyen</i></a>,<sup id="cite_ref-OED_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> which is the pronunciation of the surname according to a history of the Sabine family.<sup id="cite_ref-Sabine_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sabine-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> However, some authorities give <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabine%27s_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> and has a circumpolar distribution through northernmost <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurasia</a>. It <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrates</a> south in autumn; most of the population winters at sea in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific">Pacific</a> off western <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> in the cold waters of the <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_Current" title="Humboldt Current">Humboldt Current</a>, while Greenland and eastern Canadian <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> cross the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> by way of the westernmost fringes of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> to winter off southwest <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> in the cold waters of the <a href="/wiki/Benguela_Current" title="Benguela Current">Benguela Current</a>. Occasionally individual Sabine's gulls can be seen off other coasts such as the northeastern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> or further east in Europe, typically following autumn storms.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-audubon_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-audubon-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> It is recorded often enough inland in North America, Europe, and even Siberia, that it has been said to exhibit "cross-continental migration" in addition to migration at sea.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet_and_feeding">Diet and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabine%27s_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diet and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The diet and feeding technique of the Sabine's gull varies by season and habitat. In the breeding season it takes a range of freshwater and terrestrial prey on the tundra. This includes insects and probably <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a>, aquatic insects and insect <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, fish and young birds and eggs. Young birds and eggs are taken opportunistically and rarely, but can include <a href="/wiki/Black_turnstone" title="Black turnstone">black turnstones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lapland_longspur" title="Lapland longspur">lapland longspurs</a> and even the eggs of other Sabine's gulls and <a href="/wiki/Goose" title="Goose">geese</a>. Insects and insect larvae taken include terrestrial and <a href="/wiki/Water_beetle" title="Water beetle">aquatic beetles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Springtail" title="Springtail">springtails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cranefly" class="mw-redirect" title="Cranefly">craneflies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">mosquitos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Midge" title="Midge">midges</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hoverfly" title="Hoverfly">flower flies</a> (<a href="/wiki/Syrphidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Syrphidae">Syrphidae</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
