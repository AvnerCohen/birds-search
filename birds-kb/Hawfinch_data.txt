<p>The <b>Hawfinch</b> (<i>Coccothraustes coccothraustes</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finch</a> family Fringillidae. Its closest living relatives are the <a href="/wiki/Evening_Grosbeak" title="Evening Grosbeak">Evening Grosbeak</a> (<i>C. vespertinus</i>) from <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hooded_Grosbeak" title="Hooded Grosbeak">Hooded Grosbeak</a> (<i>C. abeillei</i>) from <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> especially <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>.</p>
<p>This bird breeds across <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and temperate <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. It is mainly resident in Europe, but many Asian birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> further south in the winter. It is a rare vagrant to the western islands of <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>.</p>
<p>Deciduous or mixed woodland,including parkland, with large trees - especially <a href="/wiki/Hornbeam" title="Hornbeam">Hornbeam</a> - is favoured for breeding,. The Hawfinch builds its nest in a bush or tree, and lays 2-7 eggs. The food is mainly seeds and fruit kernels, especially those of <a href="/wiki/Cherry" title="Cherry">cherries</a>, which it cracks with its powerful bill. This large finch species is usually seen in a pair or small group.</p>
<p>The 16.5–18&#160;cm long Hawfinch is a bulky bull-headed bird, which appears very short-tailed in flight. Its head is orange-brown with a black eyestripe and bib, and a massive bill, which is black in summer but paler in winter. The upper parts are dark brown and the underparts orange.</p>
<p>The white wing bars and tail tip are striking in flight. The sexes are similar. The call is a hard <i>chick</i>. The song of this unobtrusive bird is quiet and mumbled.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The Hawfinch has a total length of 16.5–18&#160;cm, and its wingspan ranges from 29&#160;cm to 33&#160;cm. It weighs approximately 48–62&#160;cm.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text in the vicinity of this tag needs clarification or removal of jargon from October 2012">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Females are usually smaller and weigh less than males. It is a robust bird with a thick neck, large round head and a wide, strong conical beak with a metallic appearance. It has short pinkish legs with a light hue and it has a short tail. It has brown eyes. The males' plumage is much more appealing than the females'. The overall colour is light brown, its head having an orange hue to it. Its eyes have a black circle around them, extending to its beak and surrounding it at its edge. Its throat is also black. The sides of its neck, as well as the back of its neck, are gray. The upper side of its wings are a deep black colour. The wings also have three stripes from approximately the middle till their sides: a white, a brown and a blue stripe.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<p>The Hawfinch is distributed in the whole of Europe, Eastern Asia (including North Japan), the North of Africa (Morocco, Tunisia and Argelia). It has also been sighted in Alaska, but this is reported as an accidental presence. It is not found in Iceland, parts of the British Isles, Scandinavia nor certain Mediterranean islands. It is however found in the South, such as in Spain and Bulgaria, as well as in central Europe, including parts of Englans and the South of Sweden. In Asia it can be found in the Caucasus, the North of Iran, Afghanistan, Turkistan, Siberia, Manchuria and North Korea.</p>
<p>In the 18th century, the Hawfinch was recorded as only a rare winter visitor in Britain. The first breeding record was early in the 19th century; by the early 1830s, a well-documented colony was established at <a href="/wiki/Epping_Forest" title="Epping Forest">Epping Forest</a> in <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a>, and breeding was also recorded in other counties east and south of <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>. Further expansion of the range continued through the 19th and 20th centuries, with breeding occurring as far north as <a href="/wiki/Aberdeenshire" title="Aberdeenshire">Aberdeenshire</a> by 1968–1972. Peak numbers were in the period 1983–1990. Subsequently, there has been a significant decline of between 37% and 45% between 1990–1999.<sup id="cite_ref-holloway_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holloway-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sharrock_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sharrock-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gibbons_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gibbons-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-langston_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-langston-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Southeast England">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Southeast_England">Southeast England</span></h3>
<p>Southeast England is the stronghold of Hawfinch in Britain. One well-known site is <a href="/wiki/Bedgebury_Pinetum" title="Bedgebury Pinetum" class="mw-redirect">Bedgebury Pinetum</a>, where flocks gather to roost in winter. The species is also found in the <a href="/wiki/New_Forest" title="New Forest">New Forest</a>; a central roost site exists here, at the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackwater_Arboretum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Blackwater Arboretum (page does not exist)">Blackwater Arboretum</a>. The only Sussex stronghold is at Westdean Woods in <a href="/wiki/West_Sussex" title="West Sussex">West Sussex</a>. While in <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a> they are regularly seen at Bookham Common in Winter. Formerly Hawfinch were regularly encountered in the Windsor Great Park area in winter though no sizeable gatherings have been reported since the mid 1990s. The recent (2007–11) BTO Bird Atlas shows no evidence of Hawfinch breeding anywhere in this area, the reasons are unclear.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Southwest England">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Southwest_England">Southwest England</span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>, Hawfinch is largely confined to the upper <a href="/w/index.php?title=Teign_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Teign Valley (page does not exist)">Teign Valley</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Western England and Wales">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Western_England_and_Wales">Western England and Wales</span></h3>
<p>Two areas in which Hawfinch reliably occurs are the <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Dean" title="Forest of Dean">Forest of Dean</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Wyre_Forest" title="Wyre Forest">Wyre Forest</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Eastern England">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_England">Eastern England</span></h3>
<p>Hawfinch is present in the <a href="/wiki/Breckland" title="Breckland">Breckland</a> of East Anglia.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Northern England">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_England">Northern England</span></h3>
<p>Hawfinches are regularly found in a small number of locations in northern England. Prime sites include <a href="/wiki/Fountains_Abbey" title="Fountains Abbey">Fountains Abbey</a> in Yorkshire and <a href="/wiki/Hulne_Park" title="Hulne Park">Hulne Park</a> in Northumberland. Hawfinches can be seen at Cromford Derbyshire near the canal and at Clumber Park (Nottinghamshire) near the chapel.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Scone_Palace" title="Scone Palace">Scone Palace</a> near Perth is the most well-known site in Scotland for Hawfinch. Formerly, they also occurred in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Botanic_Garden_Edinburgh" title="Royal Botanic Garden Edinburgh">Royal Botanic Garden Edinburgh</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Migration">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Migration">Migration</span></h3>
<p>The Hawfinch is a partial migrator, with Northern flocks migrating towards the South during the winter, as shown by ringing techniques. These same studies showed that those Hawfinches inhabiting habitats with a temperate climate would often have sedentary behaviours.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span></h2>
<p>The Hawfinch typically inhabits deciduous forests during the spring to have offspring, often in trees that bare fruit, such as oak trees. They also incur into human areas, such as parks and gardens. They can also be found in pine woods, as long as there's a source of water in the vicinity. During autumn and winter they seek food-providing forests, especially those with cherry and plum trees. As for height, the Hawfinch is present in any altitude up to that which is limited by the size of the trees.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h2>
<p>The Hawfinch feeds primarily on hard seeds from trees, as well as fruit seeds, which it obtains with the help of its strong beak with accompanying jaw muscles. Its jaw muscles exert a force equivalent to a load of approximately 30–48&#160;kg. Thus it can break through the seeds of cherries and plums. Other common sources of food include pine seeds, berries, sprouts and the occasional caterpillar and beetle. They can also break through olive seeds. The bird is known to eat in groups, especially during the winter.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Flight">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Flight">Flight</span></h2>
<p>Its flight is fast and its trajectory is straight over short distances. During long flights periodical undulations can be observed in their flight pattern. While on the ground scavenging it hops, and they are quick to fly away at the slightest noise. They are observed to catch insects mid-flight. They fly up to a height of 200m and they are seen to fly in groups, as well as alone.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<p>The Hawfinch is a shy species, and therefore difficult to observe and study. It spends most of the day on top of high branches, above all during breeding season. During the course of the Hawfinch's life it can only be seen on the ground while looking for seeds or drinking water, always near trees. While drinking and eating it is fairly aggressive and dominant, towards both its same species or different ones, even bigger birds. It guards a quite small territory when chicks are born, however when not bearing any offspring it is known to guard entire woods. This is interpreted as an evolutionary advantage, given colony rearing is seen as safer against nest predators.</p>
