<p>The <b>hawfinch</b> (<i>Coccothraustes coccothraustes</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finch</a> family Fringillidae. Its closest living relatives are the <a href="/wiki/Evening_grosbeak" title="Evening grosbeak">evening grosbeak</a> (<i>Coccothraustes vespertinus</i>) from <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hooded_grosbeak" title="Hooded grosbeak">hooded grosbeak</a> (<i>Coccothraustes abeillei</i>) from Central America and Mexico.
</p><p>This bird breeds across Europe and temperate Asia (<a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>). It is mainly resident in Europe, but many Asian birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> further south in the winter. It is a rare vagrant to the western islands of Alaska.
</p><p>Deciduous or mixed woodland, including parkland, with large trees – especially <a href="/wiki/Carpinus_betulus" title="Carpinus betulus">hornbeam</a> – is favoured for breeding. The hawfinch builds its nest in a bush or tree, and lays 2–7 eggs. The food is mainly seeds and fruit kernels, especially those of <a href="/wiki/Cherry" title="Cherry">cherries</a>, which it cracks with its powerful bill. This large finch species is usually seen in a pair or small group.
</p><p>The 16.5–18&#160;cm long hawfinch is a bulky bull-headed bird, which appears very short-tailed in flight. Its head is orange-brown with a black eyestripe and bib, and a massive bill, which is black in summer but paler in winter. The upper parts are dark brown and the underparts orange.
</p><p>The white wing bars and tail tip are striking in flight. The sexes are similar. The call is a hard <i>chick</i>. The song of this unobtrusive bird is quiet and mumbled.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The hawfinch was described and illustrated by Swiss naturalist <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Gesner" class="mw-redirect" title="Conrad Gesner">Conrad Gesner</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Historiae_animalium_(Gesner)" class="mw-redirect" title="Historiae animalium (Gesner)">Historiae animalium</a></i> in 1555.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
He used the Latin name <i>Coccothraustes</i> which is derived from the Greek: <i>kokkos</i> is a seed or kernel and <i>thrauō</i> means to break or to shatter.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In 1758 <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> included the species in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> under the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Loxia coccothraustes</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-checklist_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-checklist-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The hawfinch was moved to a separate <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Coccothraustes" title="Coccothraustes">Coccothraustes</a></i> by the French zoologist <a href="/wiki/Mathurin_Jacques_Brisson" title="Mathurin Jacques Brisson">Mathurin Jacques Brisson</a> in 1760.<sup id="cite_ref-checklist_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-checklist-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The English name 'hawfinch' was used by the ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Francis_Willughby" title="Francis Willughby">Francis Willughby</a> in 1676.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Haws are the red berries of the <a href="/wiki/Common_hawthorn" class="mw-redirect" title="Common hawthorn">common hawthorn</a> (<i>Crataegus monogyna</i>).	
</p><p>Molecular phylogenetic studies have shown that the hawfinch is closely related to other <a href="/wiki/Grosbeak" title="Grosbeak">grosbeaks</a> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Eophona" title="Eophona">Eophona</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hesperiphona" class="mw-redirect" title="Hesperiphona">Hesperiphona</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mycerobas" title="Mycerobas">Mycerobas</a></i> genera. Finches with large beaks in the <i><a href="/wiki/Rhynchostruthus" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhynchostruthus">Rhynchostruthus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Rhodospiza" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhodospiza">Rhodospiza</a></i> genera are not closely related. The similar bill morphology is the result of <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">convergence</a> due to the similar feeding behaviour.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Fossil record:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Coccothraustes balcanicus</i> (Late Pliocene/Late Villafranchian of Slivnitsa, western Bulgaria)<sup id="cite_ref-Boev,_Z_1998_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boev,_Z_1998-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Coccothraustes simeonovi</i> (Late Pliocene/Middle Villafranchian of Varshets, western Bulgaria)<sup id="cite_ref-Boev,_Z_1998_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boev,_Z_1998-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>There are six recognised <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-iou_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iou-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>western hawfinch</b> (<i>C. c. coccothraustes</i>) (<a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">1758</a>) – Europe to central Siberia and northern Mongolia</li>
<li><b>Northwest African hawfinch</b> (<i>C. c. buvryi</i>) <a href="/wiki/Jean_Cabanis" title="Jean Cabanis">Cabanis</a>, 1862 – Northwest Africa</li>
<li><b>Caucasian hawfinch</b> (<i>C. c. nigricans</i>) <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Buturlin" title="Sergei Buturlin">Buturlin</a>, 1908 – southern Ukraine, the Caucasus, northeastern Turkey and northern Iran</li>
<li><i>C. c. humii</i> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bowdler_Sharpe" title="Richard Bowdler Sharpe">Sharpe</a>, 1886 – southern Kazakhstan, eastern Uzbekistan and northeastern Afghanistan</li>
<li><i>C. c. schulpini</i> <a href="/wiki/Hans_Johansen" title="Hans Johansen">Johansen, H</a>, 1944 – southeastern Siberia, northeastern China and Korea</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The hawfinch has an overall length of 18&#160;cm (7.1&#160;in), with a wingspan that ranges from 29 to 33&#160;cm (11 to 13&#160;in). It weighs  46–70&#160;g (1.6–2.5&#160;oz) with the male being on average slightly heavier than the female.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnowPerrins19981610–1613_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnowPerrins19981610–1613-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> It is a robust bird with a thick neck, large round head and a wide, strong conical beak with a metallic appearance. It has short pinkish legs with a light hue and it has a short tail. It has brown eyes.
The plumage of the female is slightly paler than that of the male. The overall colour is light brown, its head having an orange hue to it. Its eyes have a black circle around them, extending to its beak and surrounding it at its edge. Its throat is also black.
The sides of its neck, as well as the back of its neck, are gray. The upper side of its wings are a deep black colour. The wings also have three stripes from approximately the middle till their sides: a white, a brown and a blue stripe.
Adults moults between July and September.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Coccothraustes_coccothraustes_-_01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Coccothraustes_coccothraustes_-_01.jpg/250px-Coccothraustes_coccothraustes_-_01.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="187" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Coccothraustes_coccothraustes_-_01.jpg/375px-Coccothraustes_coccothraustes_-_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Coccothraustes_coccothraustes_-_01.jpg/500px-Coccothraustes_coccothraustes_-_01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1487" data-file-height="1115" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Coccothraustes_coccothraustes_-_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A hawfinch's head</div></div></div>
<p>The hawfinch is distributed in the whole of Europe, Eastern Asia (Palearctic including North Japan), and North Africa (Morocco, Tunisia and Algeria). It has also been sighted in Alaska, but this is reported as an accidental presence.
It is not found in Iceland, parts of the British Isles, Scandinavia or certain Mediterranean islands. It is however found in southern Europe, such as in Spain and Bulgaria, as well as in central Europe, including parts of England and southern Sweden.
In Asia it can be found in the Caucasus, northern Iran, Afghanistan, Turkistan, Siberia, Manchuria and North Korea.
</p><p>The hawfinch typically inhabits deciduous forests during the spring to have offspring, often in trees that bear fruit, such as oak trees. They also incur into human areas, such as parks and gardens. They can also be found in pine woods, as long as there is a source of water in the vicinity.
During autumn and winter they seek food-providing forests, especially those with cherry and plum trees. As for height, the hawfinch is present in any altitude up to that which is limited by the size of the trees.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_British_Isles">The British Isles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The British Isles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 18th century, the hawfinch was recorded as only a rare winter visitor in Britain. The first breeding record was early in the 19th century; by the early 1830s, a well-documented colony was established at <a href="/wiki/Epping_Forest" title="Epping Forest">Epping Forest</a> in <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a>, and breeding was also recorded in other counties east and south of <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>. Further expansion of the range continued through the 19th and 20th centuries, with breeding occurring as far north as <a href="/wiki/Aberdeenshire" title="Aberdeenshire">Aberdeenshire</a> by 1968–1972. Peak numbers were in the period 1983–1990. Subsequently, there has been a significant decline of between 37% and 45% between 1990–1999.<sup id="cite_ref-holloway_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holloway-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sharrock_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sharrock-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gibbons_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gibbons-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-langston_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-langston-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Southeastern England is the stronghold of the hawfinch in Britain. One well-known site is <a href="/wiki/Bedgebury_Pinetum" class="mw-redirect" title="Bedgebury Pinetum">Bedgebury Pinetum</a>, where flocks gather to roost in winter. The species is also found in the <a href="/wiki/New_Forest" title="New Forest">New Forest</a>; a central roost site exists here, at the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackwater_Arboretum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Blackwater Arboretum (page does not exist)">Blackwater Arboretum</a>. The only Sussex stronghold is at Westdean Woods in <a href="/wiki/West_Sussex" title="West Sussex">West Sussex</a>, while in <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a> they are regularly seen at Bookham Common in winter. Formerly, hawfinches were regularly encountered in the Windsor Great Park area in winter, though no sizeable gatherings have been reported since the mid-1990s. The recent (2007–11) BTO Bird Atlas shows no evidence of the hawfinch breeding anywhere in this area; the reasons why are unclear.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a> (southwestern England), the hawfinch is largely confined to the upper <a href="/w/index.php?title=Teign_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Teign Valley (page does not exist)">Teign Valley</a>. In western England and Wales, two areas in which hawfinches reliably occur are the <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Dean" title="Forest of Dean">Forest of Dean</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Wyre_Forest" title="Wyre Forest">Wyre Forest</a>. In Eastern England, the hawfinch is present in the <a href="/wiki/Breckland" title="Breckland">Breckland</a> of East Anglia. In northern England, hawfinches are regularly found in a small number of locations. Prime sites include <a href="/wiki/Fountains_Abbey" title="Fountains Abbey">Fountains Abbey</a> in Yorkshire and <a href="/wiki/Hulne_Park" title="Hulne Park">Hulne Park</a> in Northumberland.
Hawfinches can be seen at Cromford Derbyshire near the canal and at Clumber Park (Nottinghamshire) near the chapel. In Scotland, <a href="/wiki/Scone_Palace" title="Scone Palace">Scone Palace</a> near Perth is the most well-known site in Scotland for hawfinches. Formerly, they also occurred in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Botanic_Garden_Edinburgh" title="Royal Botanic Garden Edinburgh">Royal Botanic Garden Edinburgh</a>. In Ireland, they are an annual winter visitor in small numbers: they are most often seen at <a href="/wiki/Curraghchase_Forest_Park" title="Curraghchase Forest Park">Curraghchase Forest Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Limerick" title="County Limerick">County Limerick</a>, where a flock of between 15 and 30 birds occurs each winter.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The hawfinch is a shy species, and therefore difficult to observe and study. It spends most of the day on top of high branches, above all during breeding season. During the course of the hawfinch's life it can only be seen on the ground while looking for seeds or drinking water, always near trees.
While drinking and eating it is fairly aggressive and dominant, towards both its same species or different ones, even bigger birds.
It guards a quite small territory when its chicks are born; however, when not bearing any offspring it is known to guard entire woods. This is interpreted as an evolutionary advantage, given colony rearing is seen as safer against nest predators.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Coccothraustes_coccothraustes_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11_Gouvieux.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Coccothraustes_coccothraustes_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11_Gouvieux.jpg/220px-Coccothraustes_coccothraustes_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11_Gouvieux.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="212" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Coccothraustes_coccothraustes_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11_Gouvieux.jpg/330px-Coccothraustes_coccothraustes_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11_Gouvieux.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Coccothraustes_coccothraustes_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11_Gouvieux.jpg/440px-Coccothraustes_coccothraustes_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11_Gouvieux.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4244" data-file-height="4082" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Coccothraustes_coccothraustes_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11_Gouvieux.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Coccothraustes coccothraustes</i> eggs (<a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9um_de_Toulouse" title="Muséum de Toulouse">Muséum de Toulouse</a>)</div></div></div>
<p>Hawfinches first breed when they are 1 year old.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994843–844_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994843–844-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> They are monogamous, with a pair-bond that sometimes persists from one year to the next. Pair-formation takes place before the breakup of the wintering flocks.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwalive_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwalive-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The date for breeding is dependent on the spring temperature and is earlier in southwest Europe and later in the northeast. In Britain, most clutches are laid between late April and late June.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnowPerrins19981610–1613_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnowPerrins19981610–1613-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hawfinches engage in an elaborate series of courtship routines. The two birds stand apart facing one another and reach out to touch their bills. The male displays to the female by standing erect, puffing out the feathers on his head, neck and chest and allowing his wings to droop forward. He then makes a deep bow. The male will also lower a wing and move it in a semi-circular arc, revealing his wing bars and modified wing feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton1972167–168_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton1972167–168-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The breeding pairs are usually solitary, but they occasionally breed in loose groups.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwalive_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwalive-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The nest is normally located high in a tree on a horizontal branch with easy access from the air.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994843–844_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994843–844-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The male chooses the site of the nest and builds a layer of dry twigs. After a few days, the female takes over.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton1972166–167_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton1972166–167-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The nest is untidy and is formed of a bulky twig base and a shallow cup lined with roots, grasses and lichens.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton197263_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton197263-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The eggs are laid in early morning at daily intervals.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994843–844_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994843–844-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The clutch is normally 4–5 eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton197263_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton197263-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> There is considerable variable in the colour and shape of the eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994843–844_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994843–844-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> They have purple brown and pale grey squiggles on a background that can be buff, grey-green or pale blueish.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton197263_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton197263-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The average size is 24.1&#160;mm ×&#160;17.5&#160;mm (0.95&#160;in ×&#160;0.69&#160;in) with a calculated weight of 3.89&#160;g (0.137&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994843–844_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994843–844-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The eggs are incubated for 11–13 days by the female.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994843–844_17-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994843–844-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The nestlings are fed by both parents, who regurgitate seeds but also bring mouthfuls of caterpillars.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton1972167_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton1972167-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Initially, the male normally passes the food to the female who feeds the chicks, but as they grow bigger both adults feed them directly.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994843–844_17-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994843–844-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The female broods the chicks while they are in the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994843–844_17-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994843–844-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> They fledge after 12–14 days and the young birds become independent of their parents around 30 days later.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton197263_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton197263-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The parents generally only raise a single brood each year.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994843–844_17-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994843–844-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The hawfinch is highly unusual among cardueline finches in that the male bird chooses the nest site and starts the construction. In other species the female performs these roles.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwalivefinch_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwalivefinch-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The hawfinch is also unusual in that the nest is kept clean by the parents removing the <a href="/wiki/Faecal_sac" class="mw-redirect" title="Faecal sac">faecal sacs</a> of the nestlings right up to the time when the chicks fledge. This behaviour is shared by the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_bullfinch" title="Eurasian bullfinch">Eurasian bullfinch</a>, but most finches cease to remove the faecal material after the first few days.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton1972166,169_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton1972166,169-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The annual survival rate is not known.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The maximum age obtained from <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">ring-recovery</a> data is 12 years and 7 months for a bird in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The hawfinch feeds primarily on hard seeds from trees, as well as fruit seeds, which it obtains with the help of its strong beak with accompanying jaw muscles. Its jaw muscles exert a force equivalent to a load of approximately 30–48&#160;kg. Thus it can break through the seeds of cherries and plums. Other common sources of food include pine seeds, berries, sprouts and the occasional caterpillar and beetle. They can also break through olive seeds.
The bird is known to eat in groups, especially during the winter.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight">Flight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Flight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Its flight is quick and its trajectory is straight over short distances. During long flights periodical undulations can be observed in their flight pattern. While on the ground scavenging it hops, and they are quick to fly away at the slightest noise.
They are observed to catch insects mid-flight. They fly up to a height of 200&#160;m and they are seen to fly in groups, as well as alone.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration">Migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The hawfinch is a partial migrant, with northern flocks migrating towards the South during the winter, as shown by ringing techniques. These same studies showed that those hawfinches inhabiting habitats with a temperate climate would often have sedentary behaviour. A few migrants from northern Europe reach Britain in autumn and some are seen on the Northern Isles in spring.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European population of the hawfinch is estimated to be between 7,200,000 and 12,600,000 individuals. Assuming that the European range is between 25 percent and 49 percent of the global range, a tentative figure for the global population size is 14,700,000–50,400,000 individuals.  States with large populations include Romania (500,000–1,000,000 pairs), Croatia (250,000–500,000 pairs) and Germany (200,000–365,000 pairs). Although the global population appears to be stable,<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> the population within the United Kingdom underwent a 76 percent reduction between 1968 and 2011. In 2013 it was estimated that there were only 500–1000 breeding pairs. The reasons for this decline are not understood.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Given the high numbers and huge breeding area, the hawfinch is classified by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> as being of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
