<p>The <b>Arctic warbler</b> (<i>Phylloscopus borealis</i>) is a widespread <a href="/wiki/Leaf_warbler" title="Leaf warbler">leaf warbler</a> in <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a> or mixed birch forest near water throughout its breeding range in <a href="/wiki/Fennoscandia" title="Fennoscandia">Fennoscandia</a> and the northern <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>. It has established a foothold in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, breeding in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. This warbler is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>; the entire population winters in southeast Asia. It therefore has one of the longest migrations of any <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> insectivorous bird.
</p><p>It traditionally included populations that breed in <a href="/wiki/Kamchatka" class="mw-redirect" title="Kamchatka">Kamchatka</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kuril_Islands" title="Kuril Islands">Kuril Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> and vocal evidence strongly suggested these should be treated as separate species, and are all now considered distinct with the <a href="/wiki/Kamchatka_leaf_warbler" title="Kamchatka leaf warbler">Kamchatka leaf warbler</a> in Kamchatka, <a href="/wiki/Hokkaido" title="Hokkaido">Hokkaido</a> and the Kuril Islands, and the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_leaf_warbler" title="Japanese leaf warbler">Japanese leaf warbler</a> in Japan (except Hokkaido).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The nest is on the ground in a low shrub. Like most Old World warblers, this small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> is insectivorous.
</p><p>This is a typical leaf warbler in appearance, greyish-green above and off-white below. Its single wing bar distinguishes it from most similar <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> except the <a href="/wiki/Greenish_warbler" title="Greenish warbler">greenish warbler</a>, <i>Phylloscopus trochiloides</i>. It is larger than that species and has a heavier, dagger-like bill, with a dark tip to the lower mandible. Its song is a fast trill.
</p><p>This species occurs as an autumn vagrant in western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and is annual on <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>. There were 225 confirmed arctic warbler sightings in Britain between 1958 and 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-Stoddart_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stoddart-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The genus name <i>Phylloscopus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>phullon</i>, "leaf", and <i>skopos</i>, "seeker" (from <i>skopeo</i>, "to watch"). The specific <i>borealis</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means "northern".<sup id="cite_ref-job_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
