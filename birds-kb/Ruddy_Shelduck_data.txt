<p>The <b>ruddy shelduck</b> (<i>Tadorna ferruginea</i>), known in India as the <b>Brahminy duck</b>, is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Anatidae" title="Anatidae">Anatidae</a>. It is a distinctive waterfowl, 58 to 70&#160;cm (23 to 28&#160;in) in length with a wingspan of 110 to 135&#160;cm (43 to 53&#160;in). It has orange-brown body plumage with a paler head, while the tail and the flight feathers in the wings are black, contrasting with the white wing-coverts. It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory bird</a>, wintering in the Indian subcontinent and breeding in southeastern Europe and central Asia, though there are small resident populations in North Africa. It has a loud honking call.
</p><p>The ruddy shelduck mostly inhabits inland water-bodies such as lakes, reservoirs and rivers. The male and female form a lasting pair bond and the nest may be well away from water, in a crevice or hole in a cliff, tree or similar site. A clutch of about eight eggs is laid and is <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation" title="Egg incubation">incubated</a> solely by the female for about four weeks. The young are cared for by both parents and <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> about eight weeks after hatching.
</p><p>In central and eastern Asia, populations are steady or rising, but in Europe they are generally in decline. Altogether, the birds have a wide range and large total population, and the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> has assessed their conservation status as being of "<a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least concern</a>".
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruddy_shelduck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ruddy_Shelduck_or_Brahminy_Duck_.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Ruddy Shelduck" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Ruddy_Shelduck_or_Brahminy_Duck_.jpg/220px-Ruddy_Shelduck_or_Brahminy_Duck_.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Ruddy_Shelduck_or_Brahminy_Duck_.jpg/330px-Ruddy_Shelduck_or_Brahminy_Duck_.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Ruddy_Shelduck_or_Brahminy_Duck_.jpg/440px-Ruddy_Shelduck_or_Brahminy_Duck_.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1152" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ruddy_Shelduck_or_Brahminy_Duck_.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>At Chilika lake,  Mangalajodi, Odisha, India</div></div></div>
<p>The ruddy shelduck (<i>Tadorna ferruginea</i>) is a member of the shelduck <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Shelduck" title="Shelduck">Tadorna</a></i>; in the wildfowl <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Anatidae" title="Anatidae">Anatidae</a>. The bird was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">first described</a> in 1764 by the German zoologist and botanist <a href="/wiki/Peter_Simon_Pallas" title="Peter Simon Pallas">Peter Simon Pallas</a> who named it <i>Anas ferruginea</i>, but later it was transferred to the genus <i>Tadorna</i> with the other shelducks.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Some authorities place it in the genus <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Casarca&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Casarca (page does not exist)">Casarca</a></i> along with the <a href="/wiki/South_African_shelduck" title="South African shelduck">South African shelduck</a> (<i>T. cana</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Australian_shelduck" title="Australian shelduck">Australian shelduck</a> (<i>T. tadornoides</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Paradise_shelduck" title="Paradise shelduck">Paradise shelduck</a> (<i>T. variegata</i>). <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">Phylogenetic</a> analysis shows that it is most closely related to the South African shelduck. In captivity, the ruddy shelduck has been known to <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridise</a> with several other members of <i>Tadorna</i>, with several members of the dabbling duck genus <i><a href="/wiki/Anas" title="Anas">Anas</a></i>, and with the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_goose" title="Egyptian goose">Egyptian goose</a> (<i>Alopochen aegyptiaca</i>). No <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are recognised.<sup id="cite_ref-HBWA_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBWA-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The genus name <i>Tadorna</i> comes from the French "tadorne", the <a href="/wiki/Common_shelduck" title="Common shelduck">common shelduck</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-job377_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job377-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and may originally derive from a Celtic word meaning "pied waterfowl". The <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> name "sheld duck" dates from around 1700 and means the same.<sup id="cite_ref-Kear_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kear-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The species name <i>ferruginea</i> is Latin for "rusty" and refers to the colour of the plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-job159_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job159-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruddy_shelduck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ruddy shelduck grows to a length of 58 to 70&#160;cm (23 to 28&#160;in) and has a 110–135&#160;cm (43–53&#160;in) wingspan. The male has orange-brown body plumage and a paler, orange-brown head and neck, separated from the body by a narrow black collar. The rump, flight feathers, tail-coverts and tail feathers are black and there are iridescent green <a href="/wiki/Speculum_feathers" title="Speculum feathers">speculum feathers</a> on the inner surfaces of the wings. Both upper and lower wing-coverts are white, this feature being particularly noticeable in flight but hardly visible when the bird is at rest. The bill is black and the legs are dark grey. The female is similar but has a rather pale, whitish head and neck and lacks the black collar, and in both sexes, the colouring is variable and fades as the feathers age. The birds <a href="/wiki/Moulting" title="Moulting">moult</a> at the end of the breeding season and the male loses the black collar, but a further partial moult between December and April restores it. Juveniles are similar to the female but are a darker shade of brown.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The call is a series of loud, nasal honking notes, it being possible to discern the difference between those produced by the male and the female. The calls are made both on the ground and in the air, and the sounds are variable according to the circumstances in which they are uttered.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruddy_shelduck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are very small resident populations of this species in north west <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, but the main breeding area of the bird is from southeast Europe across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a>, Mongolia, and western China.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Eastern populations are mostly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stockley1923_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stockley1923-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  This species has colonised the island of <a href="/wiki/Fuerteventura" title="Fuerteventura">Fuerteventura</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, first breeding there in 1994, and reaching a population of almost fifty pairs by 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-Garcia_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Garcia-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The ruddy shelduck is a common winter visitor in India where it arrives by October and departs by April. Its typical breeding habitat is large wetlands and rivers with <a href="/wiki/Mudflat" title="Mudflat">mud flats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shingle_beach" title="Shingle beach">shingle banks</a>, and it is found in large numbers on lakes and reservoirs. It breeds in high altitude lakes and swamps in <a href="/wiki/Jammu_and_Kashmir_(state)" title="Jammu and Kashmir (state)">Jammu and Kashmir</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stockley1923_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stockley1923-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Outside the breeding season it prefers lowland streams, sluggish rivers, ponds, flooded grassland, <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brackish_water" title="Brackish water">brackish</a> lagoons.<sup id="cite_ref-BI_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BI-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although becoming quite rare in southeast <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, the ruddy shelduck is still common across much of its Asian range. It may be this population which gives rise to vagrants as far west as <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. However, since the European population is declining, it is likely that most occurrences in western Europe in recent decades are escapes or <a href="/wiki/Feral_animal" class="mw-redirect" title="Feral animal">feral</a> birds. Although this bird is observed in the wild from time to time in eastern <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, no evidence has been found that this is a genuine case of <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrancy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Feral ruddy shelduck have bred successfully in several European countries. In <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> the ruddy shelduck is considered an invasive species that threatens to displace native birds. Despite actions taken to reduce numbers, the population of ruddy shelduck in Switzerland increased from 211 to 1250 individuals in the period from 2006 to 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This shelduck mostly frequents open locations on inland bodies of water such as lakes, reservoirs and rivers. It is seldom seen in forested areas but does occur in brackish water and saline lagoons. Though more common in the lowlands, it also inhabits higher altitudes and in central Asia is one of the few waterbirds, along with the <a href="/wiki/Bar-headed_goose" title="Bar-headed goose">bar-headed goose</a> (<i>Anser indicus</i>), to be found on lakes at 5,000&#160;m (16,400&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-Kear_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kear-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruddy_shelduck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ruddy_shelduck_arp.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Ruddy_shelduck_arp.jpg/220px-Ruddy_shelduck_arp.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Ruddy_shelduck_arp.jpg/330px-Ruddy_shelduck_arp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Ruddy_shelduck_arp.jpg/440px-Ruddy_shelduck_arp.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1696" data-file-height="1211" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ruddy_shelduck_arp.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>At <a href="/wiki/WWT_Slimbridge" title="WWT Slimbridge">Slimbridge Wildfowl and Wetlands Centre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ruddy_Shelduck_flying_over_the_lake.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Ruddy_Shelduck_flying_over_the_lake.jpg/220px-Ruddy_Shelduck_flying_over_the_lake.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Ruddy_Shelduck_flying_over_the_lake.jpg/330px-Ruddy_Shelduck_flying_over_the_lake.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Ruddy_Shelduck_flying_over_the_lake.jpg/440px-Ruddy_Shelduck_flying_over_the_lake.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3529" data-file-height="2179" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ruddy_Shelduck_flying_over_the_lake.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ruddy Shelduck flying over the lake</div></div></div>
<p>The ruddy shelduck is a mainly <a href="/wiki/Nocturnality" title="Nocturnality">nocturnal</a> bird.<sup id="cite_ref-BI_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BI-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> It is <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivorous</a> and feeds on grasses, the young shoots of plants, grain and water plants as well as both aquatic and terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>. On land it grazes on the foliage, in the water it dabbles in the shallows, and at greater depths, it up-ends, but it does not dive.<sup id="cite_ref-Kear_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kear-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The ruddy shelduck is usually found in pairs or small groups and rarely forms large flocks. However, moulting and wintering gatherings on chosen lakes or slow rivers can be very large. Gatherings of over four thousand birds have been recorded on the <a href="/wiki/Koshi_Barrage" title="Koshi Barrage">Koshi Barrage</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Koshi_Tappu_Wildlife_Reserve" title="Koshi Tappu Wildlife Reserve">Koshi Tappu Wildlife Reserve</a> in Nepal, and over ten thousand at <a href="/w/index.php?title=D%C3%BCden_G%C3%B6l%C3%BC&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Düden Gölü (page does not exist)">Lake Gölü</a> in Turkey.<sup id="cite_ref-Kear_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kear-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The birds arrive at their main breeding locations in central Asia in March and April. There is a strong pair bond between the male and female and it is thought they pair for life. In their breeding quarters, the birds are very aggressive towards their own kind and towards other species. The female in particular approaches intruders with head lowered and neck outstretched, uttering anger calls. If the intruder stands its ground, the female returns to the male and runs round him, inciting him to attack. He may or may not do so.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Mating takes place on the water after a brief courtship ritual involving neck stretching, head dipping and tail raising.<sup id="cite_ref-Kear_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kear-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The nesting site is often far away from water in a hole in a tree or ruined building, a crevice in a cliff, among sand-dunes or in an animal burrow. The nest is constructed by the female using feathers and down and some grasses.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A clutch of about eight eggs (range six to twelve) is laid between late April and early June. These have a dull gloss and are creamy-white, averaging 68 by 47&#160;mm (2.68 by 1.85&#160;in). <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation" title="Egg incubation">Incubation</a> is done by the female while the male stands in attendance nearby. The eggs hatch after about twenty-eight days and both parents care for the young, which <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> in a further fifty-five days.<sup id="cite_ref-Kear_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kear-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> After breeding the adults <a href="/wiki/Moulting" title="Moulting">moult</a>, losing the power of flight for about a month while they do so. Before moulting they move to large water bodies where they can more easily avoid <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predation</a> while they are flightless.<sup id="cite_ref-BI_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BI-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The family may stay together as a group for some time; the autumn migration starts around September and the young may mature in their second year. North African birds breed about five weeks earlier, and their breeding success is greater in wet summers.<sup id="cite_ref-Kear_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kear-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruddy_shelduck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhists</a> regard the ruddy shelduck as sacred and this gives the birds some protection in central and eastern Asia, where the population is thought to be steady or even rising. The Pembo Black-necked Crane Reserve in Tibet is an important wintering area for ruddy ducks, and here they receive protection. In Europe on the other hand, populations are generally declining as wetlands are drained and the birds are hunted. However, they are less vulnerable than some other waterfowl because of their adaptability to new habitats such as reservoirs.<sup id="cite_ref-Kear_7-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kear-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The ruddy shelduck has a very wide range and an estimated total population size of 170,000 to 225,000 individuals. The overall population trend is unclear as some local populations are increasing while others are decreasing. The bird does not appear to meet the higher criteria necessary to be considered threatened, and the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> assesses that its conservation status is of "<a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least concern</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is one of the species to which the <a href="/wiki/Agreement_on_the_Conservation_of_African-Eurasian_Migratory_Waterbirds" title="Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a> (AEWA) applies.<sup id="cite_ref-AEWA_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AEWA-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
