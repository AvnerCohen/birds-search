<p>The <b>Greater Spotted Eagle</b> (<i>Aquila clanga</i>), occasionally just called the <b>spotted eagle</b>, is a large <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a>. Like all typical <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a>, it belongs to the family <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a>. The typical eagles are often united with the <a href="/wiki/Buteo" title="Buteo">buteos</a> (<i>Buteo</i>), <a href="/wiki/Sea_eagle" title="Sea eagle">sea eagles</a> (<i>Haliaetus</i>) and other more heaviset <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a>, but it appears as if they are less distinct from the more slender <a href="/wiki/Accipitrine" title="Accipitrine" class="mw-redirect">accipitrine</a> hawks than believed.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_Spotted_Eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Greater_Spotted_Eagle.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Greater_Spotted_Eagle.jpeg/170px-Greater_Spotted_Eagle.jpeg" width="170" height="261" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Greater_Spotted_Eagle.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Museum specimen of juvenile</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It is 59–71 cm (23–28 in) in length and has a wingspan of 157–179 cm (5.15–5.87 ft) and a typical body mass of 1.6–2.5 kg (3.5–5.5 lb), with an occasional big female weighing up to 3.2&#160;kg (7.1&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CRC_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> This medium-sized eagle is very similar in general appearance to its closest relative the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Spotted_Eagle" title="Lesser Spotted Eagle">Lesser Spotted Eagle</a> (<i>A. pomarina</i>), which shares part of its <a href="/wiki/Range_(biology)" title="Range (biology)">range</a>. Head and <a href="/wiki/Wing_covert" title="Wing covert" class="mw-redirect">wing coverts</a> are very dark brown and contrast with the generally medium brown <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>; the Lesser Spotted Eagle has a paler head and wing coverts. The head is small for an eagle. The similarities of the Greater Spotted to the Lesser Spotted often results in misidentification as being that species. This is further complicated by occasional <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrids</a> between the two species.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>There is often a less obvious white patch on the upperwings, but a light crescent on the primary <a href="/wiki/Remiges" title="Remiges" class="mw-redirect">remiges</a> is a good field mark. The white V mark on the rump is less clear-cut in adults than in the Lesser Spotted Eagle. The juvenile has white spots all over its wings and lacks a lighter nape patch.</p>
<p>The call is a dog-like <i>yip</i>.</p>
<p>In winter, it occurs in the range of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Spotted_Eagle" title="Indian Spotted Eagle">Indian Spotted Eagle</a> (<i>A. hastata</i>). From this recently-validated relative, it can be distinguished by the darker color and lighter eye (not darker than the body plumage at distance, lighter at close range), and in juveniles, the strong spotting. It is also a bit larger – though this cannot be reliably estimated in the field – and in the winter quarters prefers wetland <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_Spotted_Eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Systematics, taxonomy and evolution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Systematics.2C_taxonomy_and_evolution">Systematics, taxonomy and evolution</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Spotted_Eagle" title="Lesser Spotted Eagle">Lesser Spotted Eagle</a> (<i>A. pomarina</i>) is this species' closest living relative; their common ancestor seems to have diverged around the middle <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a>, perhaps some 3.6 million years ago (mya),<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> from the ancestors of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Spotted_Eagle" title="Indian Spotted Eagle">Indian Spotted Eagle</a> (<i>A. hastata</i>) that lives across Iran, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and India. The "proto-Spotted Eagle" probably lived in the general region of <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, being split into a northern and a southern lineage when both <a href="/wiki/Glaciers" title="Glaciers" class="mw-redirect">glaciers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">deserts</a> advanced in Central Asia as the <a href="/wiki/Last_ice_age" title="Last ice age" class="mw-redirect">last ice age</a> began. The northern lineage subsequently separated into the eastern (Greater) and western (Lesser) species of today, probably around the Pliocene-<a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> boundary not quite 2 mya.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The spotted eagles as a group are quite distinct from the typical members of <i><a href="/wiki/Aquila_(genus)" title="Aquila (genus)">Aquila</a></i>, the "true eagles". They will probably be included with their putative <a href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical" class="mw-redirect">tropical</a> relatives in <i><a href="/wiki/Lophaetus" title="Lophaetus" class="mw-redirect">Lophaetus</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Ictinaetus" title="Ictinaetus" class="mw-redirect">Ictinaetus</a></i>, or moved to a genus of their own in the near future.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_Spotted_Eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution, ecology and status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution.2C_ecology_and_status">Distribution, ecology and status</span></h2>
<p>This is a species of fairly wooded country, which hunts small <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> and similar, mainly terrestrial prey. It breeds from northern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> across <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">winters</a> in southeastern Europe, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>. Migration to breeding grounds takes place fairly late; in <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a> for example birds can be seen with some regularity until the end of March<sup id="cite_ref-bishop1999_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bishop1999-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup>. This eagle lays 1-3 <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> in a tree <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a>.</p>
<p>Generally <a href="/wiki/Territory_(biology)" title="Territory (biology)" class="mw-redirect">territorial</a>, juveniles spend some time with their parents after <a href="/wiki/Fledging" title="Fledging" class="mw-redirect">fledging</a>, until they reach <a href="/wiki/Sexual_maturity" title="Sexual maturity">sexual maturity</a> and seek out a territory and a mate of their own. In winter quarters, the species is more social. Small flocks of up to ten birds or so, of varying age, can be seen to patrol the land together. They also associate with other <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a> in winter quarters, like local and/or migrant <a href="/wiki/Black_Kite" title="Black Kite">Black Kites</a> (<i>Milvus migrans lineatus</i> and <i>govinda</i>) or <a href="/wiki/Steppe_Eagle" title="Steppe Eagle">Steppe Eagles</a> (<i>A. nipalensis</i>), distinctly smaller and larger raptors, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-bishop1999_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bishop1999-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species is prone to vagrancy. Its regular breeding range does not reach to <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> anymore these days, but still they are not rarely met with in that country, with a few birds seen every decade. Even young birds disperse widely; the <a href="/wiki/Staatliches_Museum_f%C3%BCr_Tierkunde_Dresden" title="Staatliches Museum für Tierkunde Dresden" class="mw-redirect">Staatliches Museum für Tierkunde Dresden</a> has a specimen (C 21845) shot in November 1914 at <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gro%C3%9Fgrabe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Großgrabe (page does not exist)">Großgrabe</a> near <a href="/wiki/Bernsdorf,_Bautzen" title="Bernsdorf, Bautzen">Bernsdorf</a> in <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a>. It is a juvenile, and though its exact age cannot be determined it is heavily spotted and probably less than 20 months old.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>An adult Greater Spotted was tagged with a satellite transponder in 1993 in order to track <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migration</a>. The tagged eagle migrated a total of 5,526 kilometers (3,434&#160;mi) from its wintering grounds in <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a> to it breeding grounds in western <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. It moved 150&#160;km (93&#160;mi) on average each day, but this increased to 280&#160;km (170&#160;mi) per day as the bird flew through <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is classified as Vulnerable to <a href="/wiki/Extinct" title="Extinct" class="mw-redirect">extinction</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN" class="mw-redirect">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> As of 2000, the world population of this eagle was estimated at less than 4,000 breeding pairs. The primary threats are habit degradation and habitat loss, as well as human disturbance during the mating season.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
