<p>The <b>mute swan</b> (<i>Cygnus olor</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Swan" title="Swan">swan</a> and a member of the <a href="/wiki/Waterfowl" class="mw-redirect" title="Waterfowl">waterfowl</a> family <a href="/wiki/Anatidae" title="Anatidae">Anatidae</a>. It is native to much of <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurosiberia</a>, and (as a rare winter visitor) the far north of Africa. It is an <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced species</a> in North America – home to the largest populations outside of its native range – with additional smaller introductions in <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a> and southern Africa. The name 'mute' derives from it being less vocal than other <a href="/wiki/Swan" title="Swan">swan</a> species.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Snow_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Madge_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Measuring 125 to 170&#160;cm (49 to 67&#160;in) in length, this large swan is wholly white in plumage with an orange beak bordered with black. It is recognisable by its pronounced knob atop the beak, which is larger in males.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The mute swan was first formally described by the German naturalist <a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Gmelin" title="Johann Friedrich Gmelin">Johann Friedrich Gmelin</a> as <i>Anas olor</i> in 1789, and was transferred by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Matth%C3%A4us_Bechstein" title="Johann Matthäus Bechstein">Johann Matthäus Bechstein</a> to the new genus <i>Cygnus</i> in 1803. Both <i>cygnus</i> and <i>olor</i> mean "swan" in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>; <i>cygnus</i> is a variant form of <i>cycnus</i>, a borrowing from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>  <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453) language text">κύκνος</span> <i>kyknos</i>, a word of the same meaning.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Liddell1980_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Liddell1980-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite its Eurasian origin, its closest relatives are the <a href="/wiki/Black_swan" title="Black swan">black swan</a> of Australia and the <a href="/wiki/Black-necked_swan" title="Black-necked swan">black-necked swan</a> of South America, not the other Northern Hemisphere swans of the genus <i>Cygnus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The species is <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monotypic">monotypic</a>, with no living <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Madge_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mute swan subfossils, 6,000 years old, have been found in post-glacial peat beds of <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a>, Great Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> They have been recorded from Ireland east to Portugal and Italy,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and from France, 13,000 <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">BP</a> (Desbrosse and Mourer-Chauvire 1972–1973).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed (November 2012)">full citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Paleosubspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleosubspecies">paleosubspecies</a> <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cygnus_olor_bergmanni&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cygnus olor bergmanni (page does not exist)">Cygnus olor bergmanni</a></i>, which differed only in size from the living bird, is known from fossils found in <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Fossils of swan ancestors more distantly allied to the mute swan have been found in four U.S. states: California, Arizona, Idaho and Oregon.<sup id="cite_ref-Jeff05_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jeff05-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The timeline runs from the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> to the late <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>, or 10,000 BP. The latest find was in Anza Borrego Desert, a state park in California.<sup id="cite_ref-Jeff05_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jeff05-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Fossils from the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> include <i><a href="/wiki/Cygnus_paloregonus" title="Cygnus paloregonus">Cygnus paloregonus</a></i> from Fossil Lake, Oregon, Froman's Ferry, Idaho, and Arizona, referred to by Howard in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Waterfowl_of_the_World" class="mw-redirect" title="The Waterfowl of the World">The Waterfowl of the World</a></i> as "probably the mute type swan".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adults of this large swan typically range from 140 to 160&#160;cm (55 to 63&#160;in) long, although can range in extreme cases from 125 to 170&#160;cm (49 to 67&#160;in), with a 200 to 240&#160;cm (79 to 94&#160;in) wingspan.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge1982_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge1982-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Males are larger than females and have a larger knob on their bill. On average, this is the second largest waterfowl species after the <a href="/wiki/Trumpeter_swan" title="Trumpeter swan">trumpeter swan</a>, although male mute swans can easily match or even exceed a male trumpeter in mass.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Among standard measurements of the mute swan, the wing chord measures 53–62.3&#160;cm (20.9–24.5&#160;in), the tarsus is 10–11.8&#160;cm (3.9–4.6&#160;in) and the bill is 6.9–9&#160;cm (2.7–3.5&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The mute swan is one of the heaviest flying birds. In several studies from Great Britain, males (known as <i>cobs</i>) were found to average from about 10.6 to 11.87&#160;kg (23.4 to 26.2&#160;lb), with a weight range of 9.2–14.3&#160;kg (20–32&#160;lb) while the slightly smaller females (known as <i>pens</i>) averaged about 8.5 to 9.67&#160;kg (18.7 to 21.3&#160;lb), with a weight range of 7.6–10.6&#160;kg (17–23&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CRC_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> While the top normal weight for a big cob is roughly 15&#160;kg (33&#160;lb), one unusually big Polish cob weighed almost 23&#160;kg (51&#160;lb) and this counts as the largest weight ever verified for a flying bird, although it has been questioned whether this heavyweight could still take flight.<sup id="cite_ref-Wood_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Young birds, called cygnets, are not the bright white of mature adults, and their bill is dull greyish-black, not orange, for the first year. The down may range from pure white to grey to buff, with grey/buff the most common. The white cygnets have a <a href="/wiki/Leucistic" class="mw-redirect" title="Leucistic">leucistic</a> gene. Cygnets grow quickly, reaching a size close to their adult size in approximately three months after hatching. Cygnets typically retain their grey feathers until they are at least one year old, with the down on their wings having been replaced by <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">flight feathers</a> earlier that year.
</p><p>All mute swans are white at maturity, though the feathers (particularly on the head and neck) are often stained orange-brown by iron and tannins in the water.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polish_swan">Polish swan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Polish swan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cygnus_olor_ML0002.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Cygnus_olor_ML0002.jpg/220px-Cygnus_olor_ML0002.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="137" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Cygnus_olor_ML0002.jpg/330px-Cygnus_olor_ML0002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Cygnus_olor_ML0002.jpg/440px-Cygnus_olor_ML0002.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3104" data-file-height="1937" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cygnus_olor_ML0002.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two mute swan cygnets a few weeks old. The cygnet on the right is of the "Polish swan" colour morph, and carries a gene responsible for <a href="/wiki/Leucism" title="Leucism">leucism</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The colour <a href="/wiki/Morph_(zoology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Morph (zoology)">morph</a> <i>C. o. </i> morpha <i>immutabilis</i> (<tt><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/immutabilis" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:immutabilis">immūtābilis</a></tt> is Latin for "immutable, unchangeable, unalterable"), also known as the "Polish swan", has pinkish (not dark grey) legs and dull white cygnets; as with white <a href="/wiki/Domestic_geese" class="mw-redirect" title="Domestic geese">domestic geese</a>, it is only found in populations with a history of domestication.<sup id="cite_ref-Cramp_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cramp-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Polish swans carry a copy of a gene responsible for <a href="/wiki/Leucism" title="Leucism">leucism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Drilon,_Pogradec_(17).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Drilon%2C_Pogradec_%2817%29.jpg/220px-Drilon%2C_Pogradec_%2817%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Drilon%2C_Pogradec_%2817%29.jpg/330px-Drilon%2C_Pogradec_%2817%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Drilon%2C_Pogradec_%2817%29.jpg/440px-Drilon%2C_Pogradec_%2817%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1728" data-file-height="2304" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Drilon,_Pogradec_(17).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nest in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Drilon_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Drilon National Park (page does not exist)">Drilon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pogradec" title="Pogradec">Pogradec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a>. The cob (male) is patrolling the area close to the nest to protect his mate.</div></div></div>
<p>Mute swans nest on <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest#Mound" title="Bird nest">large mounds</a> that they build with waterside vegetation in shallow water on islands in the middle or at the very edge of a lake. They are monogamous and often reuse the same nest each year, restoring or rebuilding it as needed. Male and female swans share the care of the nest, and once the cygnets are fledged it is not uncommon to see whole families looking for food. They feed on a wide range of vegetation, both submerged aquatic plants which they reach with their long necks, and by grazing on land. The food commonly includes agricultural crop plants such as <a href="/wiki/Oilseed_rape" class="mw-redirect" title="Oilseed rape">oilseed rape</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, and feeding flocks in the winter may cause significant crop damage, often as much through trampling with their large webbed feet, as through direct consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Unlike <a href="/wiki/Black_swan" title="Black swan">black swans</a>, mute swans are usually strongly territorial with just a single pair on smaller lakes, though in a few locations where a large area of suitable feeding habitat is found they can be colonial. The largest colonies have over 100 pairs, such as at the colony at <a href="/wiki/Abbotsbury_Swannery" title="Abbotsbury Swannery">Abbotsbury Swannery</a> in southern England, and at the southern tip of <a href="/wiki/%C3%96land" title="Öland">Öland</a> Island, <a href="/wiki/Ottenby" title="Ottenby">Ottenby</a> Preserve, in the coastal waters of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>, and can have nests spaced as little as 2&#160;m (7&#160;ft) apart.<sup id="cite_ref-Cramp_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cramp-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Non-mated juveniles up to 3–4 years old commonly form larger flocks, which can total several hundred birds, often at regular traditional sites.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> A notable flock of non-breeding birds is found on the <a href="/wiki/River_Tweed" title="River Tweed">River Tweed</a> estuary at <a href="/wiki/Berwick-upon-Tweed" title="Berwick-upon-Tweed">Berwick-upon-Tweed</a> in northeastern England, with a maximum count of 787 birds.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> A large population exists near the Swan Lifeline Station in <a href="/wiki/Windsor,_Berkshire" title="Windsor, Berkshire">Windsor</a>, and live on the Thames in the shadow of <a href="/wiki/Windsor_Castle" title="Windsor Castle">Windsor Castle</a>. Once the adults are mated they seek out their own territories and often live close to <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">ducks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, which may take advantage of the swan's ability to reach deep water weeds, which tend to spread out on the water surface.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The mute swan is less vocal than the noisy whooper and Bewick's swans; they do, however, make a variety of grunting, hoarse whistling, and snorting noises, especially in communicating with their cygnets, and usually hiss at competitors or intruders trying to enter their territory.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The most familiar sound associated with mute swans is the vibrant throbbing of the wings in flight which is unique to the species, and can be heard from a range of 1 to 2&#160;km (0.6 to 1&#160;mi), indicating its value as a contact sound between birds in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-Cramp_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cramp-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Cygnets are especially vocal, and communicate through a variety of whistling and chirping sounds when content, as well as a harsh squawking noise when distressed or lost.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hoeckerschwan_Bluecherpark_K%C3%B6ln_Gelege.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Hoeckerschwan_Bluecherpark_K%C3%B6ln_Gelege.jpg/220px-Hoeckerschwan_Bluecherpark_K%C3%B6ln_Gelege.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Hoeckerschwan_Bluecherpark_K%C3%B6ln_Gelege.jpg/330px-Hoeckerschwan_Bluecherpark_K%C3%B6ln_Gelege.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Hoeckerschwan_Bluecherpark_K%C3%B6ln_Gelege.jpg/440px-Hoeckerschwan_Bluecherpark_K%C3%B6ln_Gelege.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3994" data-file-height="2663" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hoeckerschwan_Bluecherpark_K%C3%B6ln_Gelege.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nesting in spring, <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>, Germany</div></div></div>
<p>Mute swans can be very aggressive in defence of their nests and are highly protective of their mate and offspring. Most defensive attacks from a mute swan begin with a loud hiss and, if this is not sufficient to drive off the predator, are followed by a physical attack. Swans attack by smashing at their enemy with bony spurs in the wings, accompanied by biting with their large bill, while smaller waterbirds such as ducks are normally grabbed with the swan's bill and dragged or thrown clear of the swan and its offspring. The wings of the swan are very powerful, though not strong enough to break an adult man's leg, as is commonly misquoted.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Large waterfowl, such as <a href="/wiki/Canada_goose" title="Canada goose">Canada geese</a>, (more likely out of competition than in response to potential predation) may be aggressively driven off, and mute swans regularly attack people who enter their territory.<sup id="cite_ref-NY_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NY-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The cob is responsible for defending the cygnets while on the water, and will sometimes attack small watercraft, such as canoes, that it feels are a threat to its young. The cob will additionally try and chase the predator out of his family territory, and will keep animals such as foxes and raptors at bay. In New York (outside its native range), the most common predators of cygnets are <a href="/wiki/Common_snapping_turtle" title="Common snapping turtle">common snapping turtles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NY_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NY-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Healthy adults are rarely preyed upon, though <a href="/wiki/Canid" class="mw-redirect" title="Canid">canids</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Coyote" title="Coyote">coyotes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Felid" class="mw-redirect" title="Felid">felids</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Lynx" title="Lynx">lynx</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bear" title="Bear">bears</a> can pose a threat to infirm ones (healthy adults can usually swim away from danger unless defending nests) and there are a few cases of healthy adults falling prey to <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">golden eagles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Watson_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> In England, there has been an increased rate of attacks on swans by out-of-control dogs, especially in parks where the birds are less territorial. This is considered criminal in British law, and the birds are placed under the highest protection due to their association with the monarch.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Mute swans will readily attack dogs to protect themselves and their cygnets from an attack, and an adult swan is capable of overwhelming and drowning<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> even large dog breeds.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The familiar pose with neck curved back and wings half raised, known as busking, is a threat display. Both feet are paddled in unison during this display, resulting in more jerky movement.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The swans may also use the busking posture for wind-assisted transportation over several hundred meters, so-called windsurfing.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Like other swans, mute swans are known for their ability to <a href="/wiki/Grief" title="Grief">grieve</a> for a lost or dead mate or cygnet.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Swans will go through a mourning process, and in the case of the loss of their mate, may either stay where its counterpart lived, or fly off to join a flock.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Should one of the pair die while there are cygnets present, the remaining parent will take up their partner's duties in raising the clutch.
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Birds_of_Sweden_2016_13.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Birds_of_Sweden_2016_13.jpg/220px-Birds_of_Sweden_2016_13.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Birds_of_Sweden_2016_13.jpg/330px-Birds_of_Sweden_2016_13.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Birds_of_Sweden_2016_13.jpg/440px-Birds_of_Sweden_2016_13.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1694" data-file-height="1271" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Birds_of_Sweden_2016_13.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nest of a mute swan, Sweden</div></div></div>

<p>Mute swans lay from 4 to 10 eggs. The female broods for around 36 days, with cygnets normally hatching between the months of May and July.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The young swans do not achieve the ability to fly before about 120 to 150 days old. This limits the distribution of the species at the northern edge of its range as the cygnets need to learn to fly before the ponds and lakes freeze over.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The mute swan is found naturally mainly in temperate areas of Europe then across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> as far east as <a href="/wiki/Primorsky_Krai" title="Primorsky Krai">Primorsky Krai</a>, near Sidemi.<sup id="cite_ref-Dementev67_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dementev67-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is partially <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> throughout northern latitudes in Europe and Asia, as far south as north Africa and the Mediterranean. It is known and recorded to have nested in Iceland and is a vagrant to that area, as well as to Bermuda, according to the <a href="/wiki/UN_Environment_Programme" class="mw-redirect" title="UN Environment Programme">UN Environment Programme</a> chart of international status chart of bird species, which places it in 70 countries, breeding in 49 countries, and vagrant in 16 countries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> While most of the current population in Japan is introduced, mute swans are depicted on scrolls more than 1,000 years old, and wild birds from the mainland Asian population still occur rarely in winter. Natural migrants to Japan usually occur along with whooper and sometimes Bewick's swans.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The mute swan is protected in most of its range, but this has not prevented illegal hunting and <a href="/wiki/Poaching" title="Poaching">poaching</a>. It is often kept in captivity outside its natural range, as a decoration for parks and ponds, and escapes have happened. The descendants of such birds have become naturalised in the eastern United States and <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>, much as the <a href="/wiki/Canada_goose" title="Canada goose">Canada goose</a> has done in Europe.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_population">World population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: World population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Native_populations">Native populations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Native populations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>The total native population of mute swans is about 500,000 birds at the end of the breeding season (adults plus young), of which 350,000 are in the <a href="/wiki/Former_Soviet_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="Former Soviet Union">former Soviet Union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The largest single breeding concentration is 11,000 pairs in the <a href="/wiki/Volga_Delta" title="Volga Delta">Volga Delta</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The population in the United Kingdom is about 22,000 birds, as of the 2006–2007 winter,<sup id="cite_ref-WeBS_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WeBS-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> a slight decline from the peak of about 26,000–27,000 birds in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This includes about 5,300 breeding pairs, the remainder being immatures.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Other significant populations in Europe include 6,800–8,300 breeding pairs in Germany, 4500 pairs in Denmark, 4000–4200 pairs in Poland, 3000–4000 pairs in the Netherlands, about 2500 pairs in Ireland, and 1200–1700 pairs in Ukraine.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For many centuries, mute swans in Great Britain were domesticated for food, with individuals being marked by nicks on their webs (feet) or beaks to indicate ownership. These marks were registered with the Crown and a Royal Swanherd was appointed. Any birds not so marked became Crown property, hence the swan becoming known as the "Royal Bird". It is quite possible that this domestication saved the swan from <a href="/wiki/Local_extinction" title="Local extinction">extirpation</a> through overhunting in Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thamesweb.co.uk_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thamesweb.co.uk-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thamesweb.co.uk_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thamesweb.co.uk-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Populations in western Europe were largely exterminated by hunting pressure in the 13th–19th centuries, with the exception of semi-domesticated birds maintained as <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">poultry</a> by large landowners. Better protection in the late 19th and early 20th centuries allowed birds to return to most or all of their former range.<sup id="cite_ref-Ticehurst_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ticehurst-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Holloway_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holloway-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> More recently in the period from about 1960 up to the early 1980s, numbers declined significantly again in many areas in England,<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> primarily due to <a href="/wiki/Lead_poisoning" title="Lead poisoning">lead poisoning</a> from birds swallowing discarded <a href="/wiki/Fishing_sinker" title="Fishing sinker">fishing sinkers</a> made from <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>. After lead weights were replaced by other less toxic alternatives, mute swan numbers increased again rapidly.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduced_populations">Introduced populations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Introduced populations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Since being introduced into North America, the mute swan has increased greatly in number, to the extent that it is considered as an <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> there. Populations introduced into other areas remain small, with around 200 in Japan, fewer than 200 in New Zealand and Australia, and about 120 in South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The mute swan was introduced to North America in the late 19th century. Recently, it has been widely viewed as an <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> because of its rapidly increasing numbers and its adverse effects on other <a href="/wiki/Waterfowl" class="mw-redirect" title="Waterfowl">waterfowl</a> and native <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>. For example, a study of population sizes in the lower <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> from 1971 to 2000 found that mute swan numbers were increasing at an average rate of at least 10% per year, doubling the population every seven to eight years.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Several studies have concluded that mute swans severely reduce densities of submerged vegetation where they occur.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2003, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Fish_and_Wildlife_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service">U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service</a> proposed to "minimize environmental damages attributed to Mute Swans" by reducing their numbers in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Flyway" title="Atlantic Flyway">Atlantic Flyway</a> to pre-1986 levels, a 67% reduction at the time. According to a report published in the <i><a href="/wiki/Federal_Register" title="Federal Register">Federal Register</a></i> of 2003<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> the proposal was supported by all thirteen state wildlife agencies which submitted comments, as well as by 43 bird conservation, wildlife conservation and wildlife management organisations. Ten <a href="/wiki/Animal_rights" title="Animal rights">animal rights</a> organisations and the vast majority of comments from individuals were opposed. At this time mute swans were protected under the <a href="/wiki/Migratory_Bird_Treaty_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Migratory Bird Treaty Act">Migratory Bird Treaty Act</a> due to a court order, but in 2005 the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Interior" title="United States Department of the Interior">United States Department of the Interior</a> officially declared them a non-native, unprotected species.<sup id="cite_ref-USDI_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USDI-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Mute swans are protected in some areas of the U.S. by local laws, as for example in <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The status of the mute swan as an introduced species in North America is disputed by the <a href="/wiki/Interest_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Interest group">interest group</a> "Save the Mute Swans".<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> They assert that mute swans are native in the region and therefore deserving of protection. They claim that mute swans had origins from Russia and cite historical sightings and fossil records. These claims have been rejected as specious by the U.S. Department of the Interior.<sup id="cite_ref-USDI_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USDI-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceania">Oceania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Oceania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The mute swan had absolute protection in New Zealand under the <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_Act_1953" title="Wildlife Act 1953">Wildlife Act 1953</a>, but this was changed in June 2010 to a lower level of protection. It still has protection, but is now allowed to be killed or held in captivity at the discretion of the Minister of Conservation.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A small feral population exists in the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Perth" title="Perth">Perth</a>, Australia; however, it is believed to number less than 100 individuals.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:De_bedreigde_zwaan_Rijksmuseum_SK-A-4.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/De_bedreigde_zwaan_Rijksmuseum_SK-A-4.jpeg/220px-De_bedreigde_zwaan_Rijksmuseum_SK-A-4.jpeg" decoding="async" width="220" height="186" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/De_bedreigde_zwaan_Rijksmuseum_SK-A-4.jpeg/330px-De_bedreigde_zwaan_Rijksmuseum_SK-A-4.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/De_bedreigde_zwaan_Rijksmuseum_SK-A-4.jpeg/440px-De_bedreigde_zwaan_Rijksmuseum_SK-A-4.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="5728" data-file-height="4830" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:De_bedreigde_zwaan_Rijksmuseum_SK-A-4.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/The_Threatened_Swan" title="The Threatened Swan">The Threatened Swan</a></i> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 1650) by <a href="/wiki/Jan_Asselijn" title="Jan Asselijn">Jan Asselijn</a></div></div></div>
<p>A mute swan was shown on the <a href="/wiki/Euro_gold_and_silver_commemorative_coins_(Ireland)#2004_coinage" title="Euro gold and silver commemorative coins (Ireland)">2004 commemorative Irish Euro coin</a> to mark <a href="/wiki/2004_enlargement_of_the_European_Union" title="2004 enlargement of the European Union">the accession of the 10 new member states</a> which occurred during the Irish <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_the_Council_of_the_European_Union" title="Presidency of the Council of the European Union">Presidency of the European Union</a>.
</p><p>The mute swan has been the <a href="/wiki/National_bird" class="mw-redirect" title="National bird">national bird</a> of Denmark since 1984. Prior to that, the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_skylark" title="Eurasian skylark">skylark</a> was considered Denmark's national bird (since 1960).
</p><p>The fairy tale "<a href="/wiki/The_Ugly_Duckling" title="The Ugly Duckling">The Ugly Duckling</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_Andersen" title="Hans Christian Andersen">Hans Christian Andersen</a> tells the story of a cygnet ostracised by his fellow barnyard fowl because of his perceived homeliness. To his delight (and to the surprise of others), he matures into a graceful swan, the most beautiful bird of all.
</p><p>In the famous ballet <i><a href="/wiki/Swan_Lake" title="Swan Lake">Swan Lake</a></i>, the main character, Princess Odette and her companions are turned into swans by a wicked spell.
</p><p>Today, the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Monarchy of the United Kingdom">British Monarch</a> retains the right to ownership of all unmarked mute swans in open water, but Queen <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Elizabeth II</a> only exercises her ownership on certain stretches of the Thames and its surrounding tributaries. This ownership is shared with the <a href="/wiki/Worshipful_Company_of_Vintners" title="Worshipful Company of Vintners">Vintners'</a> and <a href="/wiki/Worshipful_Company_of_Dyers" title="Worshipful Company of Dyers">Dyers'</a> Companies, who were granted rights of ownership by the Crown in the 15th century.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The mute swans in the <a href="/wiki/Moat" title="Moat">moat</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Bishop%27s_Palace,_Wells" title="Bishop&#39;s Palace, Wells">Bishops Palace</a> at <a href="/wiki/Wells_Cathedral" title="Wells Cathedral">Wells Cathedral</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wells,_Somerset" title="Wells, Somerset">Wells</a>, England have for centuries been trained to ring bells via strings attached to them to beg for food. Two swans are still able to ring for lunch.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The pair of swans in the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Public_Garden" title="Boston Public Garden">Boston Public Garden</a> are named Romeo and Juliet after the <a href="/wiki/Romeo_and_Juliet" title="Romeo and Juliet">Shakespearean couple</a>; however, it was found that both of them are females.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
