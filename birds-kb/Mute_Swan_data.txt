<p>The <b>Mute Swan</b> (<i>Cygnus olor</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Swan" title="Swan">swan</a>, and thus a member of the <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">duck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goose" title="Goose">goose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swan" title="Swan">swan</a> family <a href="/wiki/Anatidae" title="Anatidae">Anatidae</a>. It is native to much of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, and (as a rare winter visitor) the far north of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It is also an <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced species</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a> and southern Africa. The name 'mute' derives from it being less vocal than other <a href="/wiki/Swan" title="Swan">swan</a> species.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Snow_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Madge_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Measuring 125 to 170 centimetres (49 to 67 in) in length, this large swan is wholly white in plumage with an orange bill bordered with black. It is recognisable by its pronounced knob atop the bill.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The Mute Swan was first formally described by the German naturalist <a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Gmelin" title="Johann Friedrich Gmelin">Johann Friedrich Gmelin</a> as <i>Anas olor</i> in 1789, and was transferred by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Matth%C3%A4us_Bechstein" title="Johann Matthäus Bechstein">Johann Matthäus Bechstein</a> to the new genus <i>Cygnus</i> in 1803. It is the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a> of the genus <i>Cygnus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cheng_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cheng-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Both <i>cygnus</i> and <i>olor</i> mean "swan" in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>; <i>cygnus</i> is related to the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>kyknos</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Liddell1980_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Liddell1980-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The synonym <i>Sthenelides olor</i> has occasionally been used in the past.</p>
<p>Despite its Eurasian origin, its closest relatives are the <a href="/wiki/Black_Swan" title="Black Swan">Black Swan</a> of Australia and the <a href="/wiki/Black-necked_Swan" title="Black-necked Swan">Black-necked Swan</a> of South America, not the other Northern Hemisphere swans.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The species is <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" title="Monotypic" class="mw-redirect">monotypic</a> with no living <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Madge_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span></h3>
<p>Mute Swan subfossils, 6,000 years old, have been found in post-glacial peat beds of <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a>, Great Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> They have also been recorded from Ireland east to Portugal and Italy,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> and from France, 13,000 <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">BP</a> (Desbrosse and Mourer-Chauvire 1972–1973).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#Citation_style" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="Wikipedia:Citing_sources#Citation_style">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Paleosubspecies" title="Paleosubspecies" class="mw-redirect">paleosubspecies</a> <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cygnus_olor_bergmanni&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cygnus olor bergmanni（页面不存在）">Cygnus olor bergmanni</a></i>, which differed only in size from the living bird, is known from fossils found in <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from September 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Fossils of swan ancestors more distantly allied to the Mute Swan have been found in four U.S. states: California, Arizona, Idaho and Oregon.<sup id="cite_ref-Jeff05_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jeff05-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The timeline runs from the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> to the late <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>, or 10,000 BP. The latest find was in Anza Borrego Desert, a national park in California.<sup id="cite_ref-Jeff05_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jeff05-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Fossils from the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> include <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cygnus_paloregonus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cygnus paloregonus（页面不存在）">Cygnus paloregonus</a></i> from Fossil Lake, Oregon, Froman's Ferry, Idaho, and Arizona, referred to by Howard in <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Waterfowl_of_the_World&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Waterfowl of the World（页面不存在）">The Waterfowl of the World</a></i> as "probably the mute type swan".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The largest Mute Swans are found in the Caspian Sea area, on migration.<sup id="cite_ref-Latham1824_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Latham1824-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Adults of this large swan typically range from 140 to 160 cm (55 to 63 in) long, although can range in extreme cases from 125 to 170 cm (49 to 67 in), with a 200 to 240 cm (79 to 94 in) wingspan.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Madge_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Males are larger than females and have a larger knob on their bill. On average, this is the second largest waterfowl species after the <a href="/wiki/Trumpeter_Swan" title="Trumpeter Swan">Trumpeter Swan</a>, although male Mute Swans can easily match or even exceed a male Trumpeter in mass.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Among standard measurements of the Mute Swan, the wing chord measures 53–62.3 cm (21–24.5 in), the tarsus is 10–11.8 cm (3.9–4.6 in) and the bill is 6.9–9 cm (2.7–3.5 in).<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Mute Swan is one of the heaviest flying birds, with males (known as <i>cobs</i>) averaging about 11–12 kg (24–26 lb) and the slightly smaller females (known as <i>pens</i>) weighing about 8.5–9 kg (19–20 lb).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Madge_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> While the top normal weight for a big cob is 15&#160;kg (33&#160;lb), one unusually big Polish cob weighed almost 23&#160;kg (51&#160;lb) and this counts as the largest verified weight for a flying bird, although it has been questioned whether this heavyweight could still take flight.<sup id="cite_ref-Wood_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Its size, orange-reddish bill and white plumage make this swan almost unmistakable at close quarters. Compared with the other Northern white swans, the Mute Swan can easily be distinguished by its curved neck and orange, black-knobbed bill, which is larger in males. Unlike most other Northern swan species (who usually inhabit only pristine wetlands without regular human interference), the Mute Swan has, in some parts of the world, become habituated and fearless towards humans. Such swans are often seen at close range in urban areas with bodies of water.</p>
<p>Young birds, called cygnets, are not the bright white of mature adults, and their bill is dull greyish-black, not orange, for the first year. The down may range from pure white to grey to buff, with grey/buff the most common. The white cygnets have a <a href="/wiki/Leucistic" title="Leucistic" class="mw-redirect">leucistic</a> gene. All Mute Swans are white at maturity, though the feathers (particularly on the head and neck) are often stained orange-brown by iron and tannins in the water.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Morph_(zoology)" title="Morph (zoology)" class="mw-redirect">morph</a> <i>immutabilis</i> ("Polish Swan") has pinkish (not dark grey) legs and dull white cygnets; as with white <a href="/wiki/Domestic_geese" title="Domestic geese" class="mw-redirect">domestic geese</a>, it is only found in populations with a history of domestication.<sup id="cite_ref-Cramp_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cramp-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span></h2>
<p>Mute Swans nest on large mounds that they build with waterside vegetation in shallow water on islands in the middle or at the very edge of a lake. They are monogamous and often reuse the same nest each year, restoring or rebuilding it as needed. Male and female swans share the care of the nest, and once the cygnets are fledged it is not uncommon to see whole families looking for food.They feed on a wide range of vegetation, both submerged aquatic plants which they reach with their long necks, and by grazing on land. The food commonly includes agricultural crop plants such as <a href="/wiki/Oilseed_rape" title="Oilseed rape" class="mw-redirect">oilseed rape</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, and feeding flocks in the winter may cause significant crop damage, often as much through trampling with their large webbed feet, as through direct consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike <a href="/wiki/Black_Swan" title="Black Swan">Black Swans</a>, Mute Swans are usually strongly territorial with just a single pair on smaller lakes, though in a few locations where a large area of suitable feeding habitat is found they can be colonial. The largest colonies have over 100 pairs, such as at the colony at <a href="/wiki/Abbotsbury_Swannery" title="Abbotsbury Swannery">Abbotsbury Swannery</a> in southern England, and at the southern tip of <a href="/wiki/%C3%96land" title="Öland">Öland</a> Island, <a href="/wiki/Ottenby" title="Ottenby">Ottenby</a> Preserve, in the coastal waters of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>, and can have nests spaced as little as 2 metres (7&#160;ft) apart.<sup id="cite_ref-Cramp_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cramp-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Non-mated juveniles up to 3–4 years old also commonly form larger flocks, which can total several hundred birds, often at regular traditional sites.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> A notable flock of non-breeding birds is found on the <a href="/wiki/River_Tweed" title="River Tweed">River Tweed</a> estuary at <a href="/wiki/Berwick-upon-Tweed" title="Berwick-upon-Tweed">Berwick-upon-Tweed</a> in northeastern England, with a maximum count of 787 birds.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Once the adults are mated they seek out their own territories and often live close to ducks and gulls, which may take advantage of the swan's ability to reach deep water weeds, which tend to spread out on the water surface.</p>
<p>The Mute Swan is less vocal than the noisy Whooper and Bewick's Swans; the most familiar sound associated with Mute Swan is the vibrant throbbing of the wings in flight. This sound is unique to the species, and can be heard from a range of 1 to 2 kilometres (0.6 to 1 mi), indicating its value as a contact sound between birds in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-Cramp_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cramp-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> They do however make a variety of grunting, hoarse whistling, and snorting noises, especially in communicating with their cygnets, and usually hiss at predators trying to enter their territory.</p>

<p>Although this bird can be tame, especially to those who feed it daily, it is aggressive in defence of its nest, and its impressive size make it a formidable adversary for animals as large as a <a href="/wiki/Coyote" title="Coyote">coyote</a>. Various predators (including several types of predatory birds, mammals, reptiles or even fish) are potential predators of cygnets and adults must be regularly vigilant. Most defensive attacks from a Mute Swan begin with a loud hiss and, if this is not sufficient to drive off the predator, are followed by a physical attack. Swans attacks by smashing at their enemy with bony spurs in the wings, accompanied by biting with their large bill. The wings of the swan are very powerful, anecdotally reported to exert enough force to break an adult man's leg.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Large waterfowl, such as <a href="/wiki/Canada_Goose" title="Canada Goose">Canada Geese</a>, (more likely out of competition than in response to potential predation) may also be aggressively driven off, and Mute Swans regularly attack people who enter their territory.<sup id="cite_ref-NY_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NY-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> The cob is also responsible for defending the cygnets while on the water, and will sometimes attack small watercraft, such as canoes, that it feels are a threat to its young. The cob will additionally try and chase the predator out of his family territory, and will keep animals such as foxes and raptors at bay. In <a href="/wiki/New_York" title="New York">New York</a> (outside its native range), the most common predators of cygnets are <a href="/wiki/Common_snapping_turtle" title="Common snapping turtle">common snapping turtles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NY_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NY-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Healthy adults are rarely predated, though <a href="/wiki/Canid" title="Canid" class="mw-redirect">canids</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Coyote" title="Coyote">coyotes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Felid" title="Felid" class="mw-redirect">felids</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Lynx" title="Lynx">lynxes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bear" title="Bear">bears</a> can pose a threat to infirm ones (healthy adults can usually swim away from danger unless defending nests) and there are a few cases of healthy adults falling prey to <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle" class="mw-redirect">golden eagles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Watson_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The familiar pose with neck curved back and wings half raised, known as busking, is a threat display, mainly shown by males but also females to a small extent. <a href="/wiki/Black_Swan" title="Black Swan">Black Swans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Whooper_Swan" title="Whooper Swan">Whooper Swans</a> are less aggressive and are not as defensive against predators. Trumpeter Swans will sometimes leave their nests if threatened. Mute Swans will attack land animals in defense of their families, during the period before fledging of their offspring<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="You can help — from October 2009">vague</span></a></i>]</sup> (which, at six months, is longer than that of most other birds).<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from September 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>The Mute Swan is found naturally mainly in temperate areas of Europe across western Asia, as far east as the <a href="/wiki/Primorsky_Krai" title="Primorsky Krai">Russian Maritimes</a>, near Sidemi.<sup id="cite_ref-Dementev67_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dementev67-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Gmelin (1789) and John Latham (1824)<sup id="cite_ref-Latham1824_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Latham1824-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> reported Mute Swans present in Kamchatka in the 18th century.</p>
<p>It is partially <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> throughout northern latitudes in Europe and Asia, as far south as north Africa and the Mediterranean. It is known and recorded to have nested in Iceland and is a vagrant to that area, as well as to Bermuda, according to the U.N. Environmental Programme chart of international status chart of bird species, which places it in 70 countries, breeding in 49 countries, and vagrant in 16 countries. While most of the current population in Japan is introduced, Mute Swans are depicted on scrolls more than a thousand years old, and wild birds from the mainland Asian population still occur rarely in winter. Natural migrants to Japan usually occur along with Whooper and sometimes Bewick's Swans.</p>
<p>The Mute Swan is protected in most of its range, but this has not prevented illegal hunting and <a href="/wiki/Poaching" title="Poaching">poaching</a>. It is often kept in captivity outside its natural range, as a decoration for parks and ponds, and escapes have happened. The descendants of such birds have become naturalised in the eastern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>, much as the <a href="/wiki/Canada_Goose" title="Canada Goose">Canada Goose</a> has done in Europe.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: World population">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="World_population">World population</span></h3>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Native populations">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Native_populations">Native populations</span></h4>
<p>The total native population of Mute Swans is about 500,000 birds at the end of the breeding season (adults plus young), of which 350,000 are in the <a href="/wiki/Former_Soviet_Union" title="Former Soviet Union" class="mw-redirect">former Soviet Union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The largest single breeding concentration is 11,000 pairs in the <a href="/wiki/Volga_Delta" title="Volga Delta">Volga Delta</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The population in the United Kingdom is about 22,000 birds, as of the 2006-2007 winter,<sup id="cite_ref-WeBS_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WeBS-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> a slight decline from the peak of about 26,000-27,000 birds in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> This includes about 5,300 breeding pairs, the remainder being immatures.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Other significant populations in Europe include 6,800-8,300 breeding pairs in Germany, 4,500 pairs in Denmark, 4,000-4,200 pairs in Poland, 3,000-4,000 pairs in the Netherlands, about 2,500 pairs in Ireland, and 1,200-1,700 pairs in Ukraine.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Populations in western Europe were largely exterminated by hunting pressure in the 13th-19th centuries, with the exception of semi-domesticated birds maintained as <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">poultry</a> by large landowners. Better protection in the late 19th and early 20th centuries allowed birds to return to most or all of their former range.<sup id="cite_ref-Ticehurst_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ticehurst-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Holloway_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holloway-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> More recently in the period from about 1960 up to the early 1980s, numbers declined significantly again in many areas, primarily due to <a href="/wiki/Lead_poisoning" title="Lead poisoning">lead poisoning</a> from birds swallowing discarded <a href="/wiki/Fishing_sinker" title="Fishing sinker">fishing sinkers</a> made from <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>. After lead weights were replaced by other less toxic alternatives, Mute Swan numbers increased again rapidly.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Introduced populations">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Introduced_populations">Introduced populations</span></h4>
<p>Mute Swans have been introduced into North America and the increase in numbers has deemed it to be an <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a>. Other introduced populations are small, with around 200 in Japan, less than 200 in New Zealand and Australia, and about 120 in South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span></h5>
<p>The Mute Swan was introduced to North America in the late 19th century. Recently, it has been widely viewed as an <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> because of its rapidly increasing numbers and impacts on other <a href="/wiki/Waterfowl" title="Waterfowl">waterfowl</a> and native <a href="/wiki/Ecosystems" title="Ecosystems" class="mw-redirect">ecosystems</a>. For example, a study of population sizes in the lower <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> from 1971 to 2000 found that Mute Swan numbers were increasing at an average rate of at least 10% per year, doubling the population every seven to eight years.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Several studies have concluded that Mute Swans severely reduce densities of submerged vegetation where they occur.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Fish_and_Wildlife_Service" title="U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service" class="mw-redirect">U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service</a> proposed to "minimize environmental damages attributed to mute swans" by reducing their numbers in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Flyway" title="Atlantic Flyway">Atlantic Flyway</a> to pre-1986 levels, a 67% reduction at the time. According a report published in the <i><a href="/wiki/Federal_Register" title="Federal Register">Federal Register</a></i> of 2003<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> the proposal was supported by all thirteen state wildlife agencies which submitted comments as well as by 43 bird conservation, wildlife conservation and wildlife management organisations. Ten <a href="/wiki/Animal_rights" title="Animal rights">animal rights</a> organisations and the vast majority of comments from individuals were opposed. At this time Mute Swans were protected under the <a href="/wiki/Migratory_Bird_Treaty_Act" title="Migratory Bird Treaty Act" class="mw-redirect">Migratory Bird Treaty Act</a> due to a court order, but in 2005 the United States <a href="/wiki/Department_of_the_Interior" title="Department of the Interior" class="mw-redirect">Department of the Interior</a> officially declared them a non-native, unprotected species.<sup id="cite_ref-USDI_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USDI-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> Mute Swans are protected in some areas of the U.S. by local laws, as for example in <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The status of the Mute Swan as an introduced species in North America is disputed by the <a href="/wiki/Interest_group" title="Interest group" class="mw-redirect">interest group</a> "Save The Mute Swans".<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> They assert that Mute Swans are native in the region and therefore deserving of protection. They claim that Mute Swans had origins from Russia and cite historical sightings and fossil records. These claims are specifically rejected by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_the_Interior" title="U.S. Department of the Interior" class="mw-redirect">U.S. Department of the Interior</a> as specious.<sup id="cite_ref-USDI_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USDI-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span></h5>
<p>The Mute Swan had absolute protection in New Zealand under the <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_Act_1953" title="Wildlife Act 1953">Wildlife Act 1953</a> but this was changed in June 2010 to a lower level of protection. It still has protection but is now able to be killed or held in captivity at the discretion of the Minister of Conservation.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Mute_Swan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cultural references">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">Cultural references</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The term <a href="/wiki/Swan_song" title="Swan song">swan song</a> comes from the belief that the mute swan would live its whole life without singing, and would then sing one beautiful song just before death.</li>
<li>A Mute Swan was shown on the <a href="/wiki/Euro_gold_and_silver_commemorative_coins_(Ireland)#2004_coinage" title="Euro gold and silver commemorative coins (Ireland)">2004 commemorative Irish Euro coin</a> to mark <a href="/wiki/2004_enlargement_of_the_European_Union" title="2004 enlargement of the European Union">the accession of the 10 new member states</a> which occurred during the Irish <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_the_Council_of_the_European_Union" title="Presidency of the Council of the European Union">Presidency of the European Union</a>.</li>
<li>The Mute Swan has been the national bird of <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> since 1984. Prior to that, the <a href="/wiki/Skylark" title="Skylark" class="mw-redirect">Skylark</a> was considered Denmark's national bird (since 1960).</li>
<li>The fairy tale <a href="/wiki/The_Ugly_Duckling" title="The Ugly Duckling">The Ugly Duckling</a> by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_Andersen" title="Hans Christian Andersen">Hans Christian Andersen</a> tells the story of a cygnet ostracised by his fellow barnyard fowl because of his perceived homeliness. To his delight (and to the surprise of others), he matures into a graceful swan, the most beautiful bird of all.</li>
<li>Today, the Crown (the <a href="/wiki/British_monarch" title="British monarch" class="mw-redirect">British monarch</a>) retains the right to ownership of all unmarked mute swans in open water, but the Queen only exercises her ownership on certain stretches of the Thames and its surrounding tributaries. This ownership is shared with the <a href="/wiki/Worshipful_Company_of_Vintners" title="Worshipful Company of Vintners">Vintners'</a> and <a href="/wiki/Worshipful_Company_of_Dyers" title="Worshipful Company of Dyers">Dyers'</a> Companies, who were granted rights of ownership by the Crown in the 15th century.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> See <a href="/wiki/Swan_upping" title="Swan upping" class="mw-redirect">Swan upping</a>.</li>
<li>The Mute Swans in the <a href="/wiki/Moat" title="Moat">moat</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Bishop%27s_Palace,_Wells" title="Bishop's Palace, Wells">Bishops Palace</a> at <a href="/wiki/Wells_Cathedral" title="Wells Cathedral">Wells Cathedral</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wells" title="Wells">Wells</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> have for centuries been trained to ring <a href="/wiki/Bell_(instrument)" title="Bell (instrument)">bells</a> via strings attached to them to beg for food. Two swans are still able to ring for lunch.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The pair of swans in the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Public_Garden" title="Boston Public Garden" class="mw-redirect">Boston Public Garden</a> are named <b>Romeo</b> and <b>Juliet</b> after the <a href="/wiki/Romeo_and_Juliet" title="Romeo and Juliet">Shakespearean couple</a>; however, it was found that both are female.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Camille_Saint-Sa%C3%ABns" title="Camille Saint-Saëns">Camille Saint-Saëns</a> composed a movement called <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Cygne" title="Le Cygne" class="mw-redirect">Le Cygne</a></i> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Carnival_of_the_Animals" title="The Carnival of the Animals">The Carnival of the Animals</a></i>. It is played by solo cello and two pianos and represents a swan gliding over the water (cello) and the ripples it creates (pianos).</li>
</ul>
