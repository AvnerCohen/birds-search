<p>The <b>Red Kite</b> (<i>Milvus milvus</i>) is a medium-large <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a>, which also includes many other <a href="/wiki/Diurnality" title="Diurnality">diurnal</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptors</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buzzard" title="Buzzard">buzzards</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Harrier_(bird)" title="Harrier (bird)">harriers</a>. The species is currently <a href="/wiki/Endemism_in_birds" title="Endemism in birds" class="mw-redirect">endemic</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Western_Palearctic" title="Western Palearctic" class="mw-redirect">Western Palearctic</a> region in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and northwest <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, though formerly also occurred just outside in northern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> It is resident in the milder parts of its range in western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and northwest <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, but birds from northeastern and central <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> winter further south and west, reaching south to <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>. Vagrants have reached north to <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> and south to <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gambia" title="Gambia" class="mw-redirect">Gambia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gambia_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gambia-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> in 1758 as <i>Falco milvus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Red Kite has been known to successfully <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridize</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Black_Kite" title="Black Kite">Black Kite</a> in captivity where both species were kept together, and in the wild on the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_Islands" title="Cape Verde Islands" class="mw-redirect">Cape Verde Islands</a> and infrequently in other places.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The Red Kites on the Cape Verde Islands are (or rather were) quite distinct in <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>, being somewhat intermediate with Black Kites. The question whether the <b>Cape Verde Kite</b> should be considered a distinct <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> (<i>Milvus fasciicauda</i>) or a Red Kite <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> has not been settled. A recent <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA" class="mw-redirect">mtDNA</a> study on museum specimens suggests that Cape Verde birds did not form a <a href="/wiki/Monophyletic" title="Monophyletic" class="mw-redirect">monophyletic</a> lineage among or next to Red Kites.<sup id="cite_ref-Johnson_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This interpretation is problematic: mtDNA analysis is susceptible to hybridization events, the evolutionary history of the Cape Verde population is not known, and the <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> relationship of Red Kites is confusing, with geographical proximity being no indicator of genetic relatedness and the overall genetic similarity high,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> perhaps indicating a <a href="/wiki/Relict" title="Relict">relict</a> species.</p>
<p>Given the morphological distinctness of the Cape Verde birds and that the Cape Verde population was isolated from other populations of Red Kites, it cannot be conclusively resolved as to whether the Cape Verde population was not a distinct subspecies (as <i>M. migrans fasciicauda</i>) or even species that frequently absorbed stragglers from the migrating European populations into its gene pool. More research seems warranted, but at any rate the Cape Verde population is effectively <a href="/wiki/Extinct" title="Extinct" class="mw-redirect">extinct</a> since 2000, all surviving birds being hybrids with <a href="/wiki/Black_Kite" title="Black Kite">Black Kites</a> (which merely raises further questions about their taxonomic status).<sup id="cite_ref-Johnson_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>Red Kites are 60–66 cm (24–26 in) long with a 175–179 cm (69–70 in) wingspan; males weigh 800–1,200 g (28–42 oz), and females 1,000–1,300 g (35–46 oz).<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> It is an elegant bird, soaring on long wings held at a <a href="/wiki/Dihedral_(aircraft)" title="Dihedral (aircraft)">dihedral</a>, and long forked tail, twisting as it changes direction. The body, upper tail and wing coverts are <a href="/wiki/Rufous" title="Rufous">rufous</a>. The white primary flight feathers contrast with the black wing tips and dark secondaries. Apart from the weight difference, the sexes are similar, but juveniles have a buff breast and belly. Its call is a thin piping sound, similar to but less mewling than the <a href="/wiki/Common_Buzzard" title="Common Buzzard">Common Buzzard</a>. There is a rare white <a href="/wiki/Leucism" title="Leucism">leucistic</a> form accounting for approximately 1% of hatchlings in the Welsh population but is at a disadvantage in the survival stakes.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Differences between adults and juveniles">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Differences_between_adults_and_juveniles">Differences between adults and juveniles</span></h3>
<p>Adults differ from juveniles in a number of characteristics:</p>
<ul>
<li>Adults are overall more deeply rufous, compared with the more washed out colour of juveniles;</li>
<li>Adults have black breast-streaks whereas on juveniles these are pale;</li>
<li>Juveniles have a less deeply-forked tail, with a dark subterminal band;</li>
<li>Juveniles have pale tips to all of the greater-coverts (secondary and primary) on both the upper- and under-wings, forming a long narrow pale line; adults have pale fringes to upperwing secondary-coverts only.</li>
</ul>
<p>These differences hold throughout most of the first year of a bird's life.</p>

<p>The Red Kite's diet consists mainly of small mammals such as mice, voles, shrews, young hares and rabbits. It feeds on a wide variety of carrion including sheep carcasses and dead game birds. Live birds are also taken and occasionally <a href="/wiki/Reptiles" title="Reptiles" class="mw-redirect">reptiles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amphibians" title="Amphibians" class="mw-redirect">amphibians</a>. Earthworms form an important part of the diet, especially in spring.<sup id="cite_ref-Pugh_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pugh-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>As scavengers, red kites are particularly sensitive to poisoning.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Illegal poison baits set for <a href="/wiki/Foxes" title="Foxes" class="mw-redirect">foxes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Crows" title="Crows" class="mw-redirect">crows</a> are indiscriminate and kill protected <a href="/wiki/Birds" title="Birds" class="mw-redirect">birds</a> and other animals.</p>
<p>At signs of danger a mother will signal the young who will "play dead" to the extent that a <a href="/wiki/Red_Fox" title="Red Fox" class="mw-redirect">fox</a> will believe them to be dead and leave them, thinking it can return to eat them later.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<p>Adult red kites are sedentary birds, and occupy their breeding home range all year in the United Kingdom, though many global populations are migratory (particularly the Swedish population, which winters in Spain).<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_Scotland_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_Scotland-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Each nesting territory can contain up to five nest sites. Both birds build the nest on a main fork or a limb high in a tree, 12-20 metres above the ground. IThe nest is made of twigs and lined with grass or other vegetation and sheep’s wool.<sup id="cite_ref-Pugh_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pugh-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BBC_Scotland_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_Scotland-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Distribution and status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_status">Distribution and status</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bielik_kania.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Bielik_kania.jpg/170px-Bielik_kania.jpg" width="170" height="230" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Bielik_kania.jpg/255px-Bielik_kania.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Bielik_kania.jpg/340px-Bielik_kania.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bielik_kania.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Red Kite, top, harassing an adult <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_Eagle" title="White-tailed Eagle">White-tailed Eagle</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Red Kites inhabit broadleaf woodlands, valleys and wetland edges, to 800 metres. They are endemic to the western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" title="Palearctic" class="mw-redirect">Palearctic</a>, with the European population of 19,000-25,000 pairs encompassing 95% of its global breeding range. It breeds from Spain and Portugal east into central Europe and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, north to southern Sweden, <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a> and the UK, and south to southern Italy. There is a population in northern <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>. Northern birds move south in winter, mostly staying in the west of the breeding range, but also to eastern <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>. The three largest populations (in Germany, France and Spain, which together hold more than 75% of the global population) declined between 1990-2000, and overall the species declined by almost 20% over the ten years. The main threats to Red Kites are poisoning, through illegal direct poisoning and indirect poisoning from pesticides, particularly in the wintering ranges in France and Spain, and changes in agricultural practices causing a reduction in food resources. Other threats include electrocution, hunting and trapping, deforestation, egg-collection (on a local scale) and possibly competition with the generally more successful Black Kite <i>M. migrans</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Continental Europe">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Continental_Europe">Continental Europe</span></h3>
<p>German populations declined by 25%–30% between 1991 and 1997, but have remained stable since. The populations of the northern foothills of the <a href="/wiki/Harz_Mountains" title="Harz Mountains" class="mw-redirect">Harz Mountains</a> (the most densely populated part of its range) suffered an estimated 50% decline from 1991-2001. In Spain the species showed an overall decline in breeding population of up to 43% for the period 1994 to 2001–02, and surveys of wintering birds in 2003–04 suggest a similarly large decline in core wintering areas. The <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Island" title="Balearic Island" class="mw-redirect">Balearic Islands</a> population has declined from 41–47 breeding pairs in 1993 to just 10 in 2003. In France, breeding populations have decreased in the northeast, but seem to be stable in southwest and central France and <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>. Populations elsewhere are stable or undergoing increases. In Sweden the species has increased from 30–50 pairs in the 1970s to 1,200 breeding pairs in 2003. In Switzerland, populations increased during the 1990s, and have stabilised.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_0-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> According to a report by the Welsh Kite Trust, the UK is the only country in which the Red Kite population is increasing. Red Kites are decreasing in their strongholds of Spain, France and Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_Kite_in_Berkshire_2008.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Red_Kite_in_Berkshire_2008.jpg/170px-Red_Kite_in_Berkshire_2008.jpg" width="170" height="181" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Red_Kite_in_Berkshire_2008.jpg/255px-Red_Kite_in_Berkshire_2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Red_Kite_in_Berkshire_2008.jpg/340px-Red_Kite_in_Berkshire_2008.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_Kite_in_Berkshire_2008.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Red Kite in Berkshire, UK in 2008.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the United Kingdom Red Kites were ubiquitous scavengers that lived on carrion and garbage. Shakespeare's <a href="/wiki/King_Lear" title="King Lear">King Lear</a> describes his daughter <a href="/wiki/Goneril" title="Goneril">Goneril</a> as a <i>detested kite</i>, and he wrote "when the kite builds, look to your lesser linen" in reference to them stealing washing hung out to dry in the nesting season.<sup id="cite_ref-Radio4_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Radio4-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> In the mid-15th century <a href="/wiki/King_James_II_of_Scotland" title="King James II of Scotland" class="mw-redirect">King James II of Scotland</a> decreed that they should be "killed wherever possible", but they remained protected in England and Wales for the next 100 years as they kept the streets free of carrion and rotting food.<sup id="cite_ref-New_Kay_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Kay-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Under Tudor "vermin laws" many creatures were seen as competitors for the produce of the countryside and bounties were paid by the parish for their carcasses.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>By the 20th century the breeding population was restricted to a handful of pairs in <a href="/wiki/South_Wales" title="South Wales">South Wales</a>, but recently the Welsh population has been supplemented by re-introductions in England and Scotland. In 2004, from 375 occupied territories identified, at least 216 pairs were thought to have hatched eggs and 200 pairs reared at least 286 young.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_0-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1989 six Swedish birds were released at a site in north Scotland and four Swedish and a Welsh bird in <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_conservation_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB_conservation-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Altogether, 93 birds of Swedish and Spanish origin were released at each of the sites. In the second stage of reintroduction in 1995 and 1996, further birds were brought from Germany to populate areas of <a href="/wiki/Dumfries_and_Galloway" title="Dumfries and Galloway">Dumfries and Galloway</a>. 94 birds were brought from the Chilterns and introduced into the <a href="/wiki/River_Derwent,_North_East_England" title="River Derwent, North East England">Derwent Valley</a> in North East England between 2004 and 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_conservation_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB_conservation-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> In Northern Ireland 80 birds from wild stock in Wales were released over three years between 2008-2010 and 2010 the first successful breeding was recorded. The reintroductions in <a href="/wiki/The_Chilterns" title="The Chilterns" class="mw-redirect">the Chilterns</a> have been a success. Between 1989 and 1993 90 birds were released in the Chilterns <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> and by 2002, 139 pairs were breeding there.<sup id="cite_ref-Chilterns_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chilterns-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Another successful reintroduction has been in <a href="/wiki/Northamptonshire" title="Northamptonshire">Northamptonshire</a>, which has become a stronghold for the Red Kite.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Thirty Spanish birds were introduced into <a href="/wiki/Rockingham_Forest" title="Rockingham Forest">Rockingham Forest</a> near <a href="/wiki/Corby" title="Corby">Corby</a> in 2000,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> and by 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Protection_of_Birds" title="Royal Society for the Protection of Birds">RSPB</a> estimated that over 200 chicks had been reared from the initial release. So successful has the reintroduction been that 30 chicks have been transported from Rockingham Forest, for release in <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A sighting of the first Red Kite in London for 150 years was reported in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i> newspaper in January 2006<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> and in June of that year, the UK-based Northern Kites Project reported that kites had bred in the Derwent Valley in and around <a href="/wiki/Rowlands_Gill" title="Rowlands Gill">Rowlands Gill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tyne_and_Wear" title="Tyne and Wear">Tyne and Wear</a> for the first time since the re-introduction.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1999 the Red Kite was named 'Bird of the Century' by the <a href="/wiki/British_Trust_for_Ornithology" title="British Trust for Ornithology">British Trust for Ornithology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Radio4_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Radio4-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2010 the <a href="/wiki/Forestry_Commission" title="Forestry Commission">Forestry Commission</a> North West England announced a three-year project to release 90 Red Kites in <a href="/wiki/Grizedale_Forest" title="Grizedale Forest">Grizedale Forest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a> under a special licence issued by <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a>. The Grizedale programme will be the ninth reintroduction of Red Kites into different regions of the UK, and the final re-introduction phase in England.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The stated aims of the Grizedale project are:</p>
<ul>
<li>To establish a viable population of Red Kites in Grizedale, South Cumbria by 2015.</li>
<li>To increase the rate of Red Kite expansion into North West England and link up with existing populations in Wales, Yorkshire, North East England and South West Scotland and so increase the chances of a continuous geographical range.</li>
<li>To develop community involvement and create educational opportunities arising from the project.<sup id="cite_ref-FC_Poster_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FC_Poster-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>As of July 2011, non-breeding birds are regularly seen in all parts of Britain, and the number of breeding pairs is too large for the RSPB to continue to survey them on an annual basis.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span></h3>
<p>Red Kites were extinct in Ireland by the middle nineteenth century, due to persecution, poisoning and woodland clearance. In May 2007, <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_the_Environment,_Community_and_Local_Government" title="Minister for the Environment, Community and Local Government">Minister for the Environment, Heritage and Local Government</a> <a href="/wiki/Dick_Roche" title="Dick Roche">Dick Roche</a> announced an agreement to bring at least 100 birds from Wales to restock the population as part of a 5-year programme in the <a href="/wiki/Wicklow_Mountains" title="Wicklow Mountains">Wicklow Mountains</a>, similar to the earlier <a href="/wiki/Golden_Eagle" title="Golden Eagle">Golden Eagle</a> reintroduction programme.<sup id="cite_ref-NDP_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NDP-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> On the 19 July 2007 the first thirty red kites were released in Co. Wicklow.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> On 22 May 2010, 2 newly-hatched Red Kite chicks were discovered in the <a href="/wiki/County_Wicklow" title="County Wicklow">Wicklow mountains</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> bringing the number of chicks hatched since reintroduction to 7.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sweden">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">Sweden</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JNR_5246Wiki.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/JNR_5246Wiki.jpg/275px-JNR_5246Wiki.jpg" width="275" height="183" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/JNR_5246Wiki.jpg/413px-JNR_5246Wiki.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/JNR_5246Wiki.jpg/550px-JNR_5246Wiki.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JNR_5246Wiki.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Red Kite flying over Skåne, Sweden</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The red kite is the landscape bird of <a href="/wiki/Scania" title="Scania">Scania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and the coat of arms of the municipality of <a href="/wiki/Tomelilla" title="Tomelilla">Tomelilla</a>. Sweden is one location where the red kite seems to be in progress with around 2000 pairs in 2009 some of which are overwintering and some flying south to the Mideterranean landscapes for the winter. They return around March-April. The Kite is often seen roaming the open colourful patchwork quilt of wheat and rapeseed fields of Scania and if you go for a drive in the spring or summertime you are almost inevitably going to run into a number of them along the roadsides. Just north of <a href="/wiki/Ystad" title="Ystad">Ystad</a> you can even experience the kites flying along with <a href="/wiki/Golden_Eagles" title="Golden Eagles" class="mw-redirect">Golden Eagles</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Observation">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Observation">Observation</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Red-tailed_kite.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Red-tailed_kite.jpg/200px-Red-tailed_kite.jpg" width="200" height="139" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Red-tailed_kite.jpg/300px-Red-tailed_kite.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Red-tailed_kite.jpg/400px-Red-tailed_kite.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Red-tailed_kite.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Red kite in flight at Gigrin farm</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>One of the best places to see the Red Kite in Scandinavia is <a href="/wiki/Scania" title="Scania">Scania</a> in southern Sweden. It may be observed in one of its breeding locations such as the <a href="/wiki/Kullaberg" title="Kullaberg">Kullaberg</a> Nature Preserve near Molle.<sup id="cite_ref-Hogan2005_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hogan2005-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Some of the best places to see them in the UK are Gigrin Farm near <a href="/wiki/Rhayader" title="Rhayader">Rhayader</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mid_Wales" title="Mid Wales">mid Wales</a>, where hundreds are fed by the local farmer as a tourist attraction,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> and the nearby Nant-Yr-Arian forest recreation centre in <a href="/wiki/Ceredigion" title="Ceredigion">Ceredigion</a><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> where the rare <a href="/wiki/Leucism" title="Leucism">leucistic</a> variant can be seen.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> In the UK the <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">Oxfordshire</a> part of the <a href="/wiki/Chilterns" title="Chilterns" class="mw-redirect">Chilterns</a> has many Red Kites, especially near <a href="/wiki/Henley-on-Thames" title="Henley-on-Thames">Henley-on-Thames</a> and <a href="/wiki/Watlington,_Oxfordshire" title="Watlington, Oxfordshire">Watlington</a>, where they were introduced on <a href="/wiki/John_Paul_Getty,_Jr." title="John Paul Getty, Jr.">John Paul Getty</a>'s estate.<sup id="cite_ref-Chilterns_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chilterns-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> They can also be seen around <a href="/wiki/Harewood" title="Harewood">Harewood</a> near <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> where they were re-introduced in 1999, and in <a href="/wiki/Twyford" title="Twyford">Twyford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wokingham" title="Wokingham">Wokingham</a> in <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
