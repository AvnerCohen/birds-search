<p>The <b>red kite</b> (<i>Milvus milvus</i>) is a medium-large <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a>, which also includes many other <a href="/wiki/Diurnality" title="Diurnality">diurnal</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptors</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buzzard" title="Buzzard">buzzards</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Harrier_(bird)" title="Harrier (bird)">harriers</a>. The species currently breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Palearctic">Western Palearctic</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and northwest <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, though it formerly also occurred in northern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is resident in the milder parts of its range in western Europe and northwest <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, but birds from northeastern and <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a> winter further south and west, reaching south to <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>. <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">Vagrants</a> have reached north to <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> and south to <a href="/wiki/Palestine_(region)" title="Palestine (region)">Palestine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gambia" class="mw-redirect" title="Gambia">Gambia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gambia_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gambia-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The red kite was described by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> under the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Falco milvus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Linnaeus1758_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linnaeus1758-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The word <i>milvus</i> was the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> name for the bird.<sup id="cite_ref-Jobling_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jobling-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In 1799 the French naturalist <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Germain_de_Lac%C3%A9p%C3%A8de" title="Bernard Germain de Lacépède">Bernard Germain de Lacépède</a> moved the species to the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i>Milvus</i> creating the <a href="/wiki/Tautonym" title="Tautonym">tautonym</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lacepede_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lacepede-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are recognised:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>M. m. milvus</i> (Linnaeus, 1758) – Europe and northwest Africa to the Middle East</li>
<li><i>M. m. fasciicauda</i> <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a>, 1914 – <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde Islands</a></li></ul>
<p>The subspecies <i>M. m. fasciicauda</i> is almost certainly extinct.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The genus <i>Milvus</i> contains two other species: the <a href="/wiki/Black_kite" title="Black kite">black kite</a> (<i>M. migrans</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Yellow-billed_kite" title="Yellow-billed kite">yellow-billed kite</a> (<i>M. aegyptius</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The red kite has been known to successfully <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridize</a> with the black kite in captivity where both species were kept together, and in the wild on the Cape Verde Islands and infrequently in other places.<sup id="cite_ref-Hille2000_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hille2000-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The red kites on the Cape Verde Islands are (or rather were) quite distinct in <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>, being somewhat intermediate with black kites. The question whether the Cape Verde kite should be considered a distinct <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> (<i>Milvus fasciicauda</i>) or a red kite subspecies has not been settled. A <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> study on museum specimens suggested that Cape Verde birds did not form a <a href="/wiki/Monophyletic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monophyletic">monophyletic</a> lineage among or next to red kites.<sup id="cite_ref-Johnson_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> This interpretation is problematic: mtDNA analysis is susceptible to hybridization events, the evolutionary history of the Cape Verde population is not known, and the <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> relationship of red kites is confusing, with geographical proximity being no indicator of genetic relatedness and the overall genetic similarity high,<sup id="cite_ref-Schreiber2000_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schreiber2000-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> perhaps indicating a <a href="/wiki/Relict_(biology)" title="Relict (biology)">relict</a> species. Given the morphological distinctness of the Cape Verde birds and that the Cape Verde population was isolated from other populations of red kites, it cannot be conclusively resolved as to whether the Cape Verde population was not a distinct subspecies (as <i>M. migrans fasciicauda</i>) or even species that frequently absorbed stragglers from the migrating European populations into its gene pool. The Cape Verde population became effectively <a href="/wiki/Extinct" class="mw-redirect" title="Extinct">extinct</a> since 2000, all surviving birds being hybrids with <a href="/wiki/Black_kite" title="Black kite">black kites</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Johnson_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The English word "kite" is from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>cyta</i> which is of unknown origin. A kite is mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Geoffrey Chaucer</a>'s in his <i><a href="/wiki/The_Knight%27s_Tale" title="The Knight&#39;s Tale">Knight's Tale</a></i>. The early fifteenth century <a href="/wiki/Hengwrt_Chaucer" title="Hengwrt Chaucer">Hengwrt manuscript</a> contains the lines: "Ther cam a kyte, whil þt they were so wrothe That bar awey the boon bitwix hem bothe." The first recorded use of the word "kite" for a toy that is attached to a length of string and flown in the air dates from the seventeenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:White_Red_Kite_in_Flight_-_geograph.org.uk_-_687770.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/White_Red_Kite_in_Flight_-_geograph.org.uk_-_687770.jpg/220px-White_Red_Kite_in_Flight_-_geograph.org.uk_-_687770.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="168" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/White_Red_Kite_in_Flight_-_geograph.org.uk_-_687770.jpg/330px-White_Red_Kite_in_Flight_-_geograph.org.uk_-_687770.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/White_Red_Kite_in_Flight_-_geograph.org.uk_-_687770.jpg/440px-White_Red_Kite_in_Flight_-_geograph.org.uk_-_687770.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="490" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:White_Red_Kite_in_Flight_-_geograph.org.uk_-_687770.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Leucistic form</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_Kite_(Milvus_milvus)_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Red_Kite_%28Milvus_milvus%29_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG/220px-Red_Kite_%28Milvus_milvus%29_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Red_Kite_%28Milvus_milvus%29_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG/330px-Red_Kite_%28Milvus_milvus%29_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Red_Kite_%28Milvus_milvus%29_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG/440px-Red_Kite_%28Milvus_milvus%29_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2508" data-file-height="1968" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_Kite_(Milvus_milvus)_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A red kite skull</div></div></div>
<p>Red kites are 60 to 70&#160;cm (24 to 28&#160;in) long<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell2000_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell2000-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> with a 175–179&#160;cm (69–70&#160;in) wingspan; males weigh 800–1,200&#160;g (28–42&#160;oz), and females 1,000–1,300&#160;g (35–46&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is an elegant bird, soaring on long wings held at a <a href="/wiki/Dihedral_(aircraft)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dihedral (aircraft)">dihedral</a>, and long forked tail, twisting as it changes direction. The body, upper tail and <a href="/wiki/Covert_feather#wing_coverts" title="Covert feather">wing coverts</a> are <a href="/wiki/Rufous" title="Rufous">rufous</a>. The white primary flight feathers contrast with the black wing tips and dark secondaries. Apart from the weight difference, the sexes are similar, but juveniles have a buff breast and belly. Its call is a thin piping sound, similar to but less mewling than the <a href="/wiki/Common_buzzard" title="Common buzzard">common buzzard</a>. There is a rare white <a href="/wiki/Leucism" title="Leucism">leucistic</a> form accounting for approximately 1% of hatchlings in the Welsh population but is at a disadvantage in the survival stakes.<sup id="cite_ref-whiteredkite_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whiteredkite-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Differences_between_adults_and_juveniles">Differences between adults and juveniles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Differences between adults and juveniles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adults differ from juveniles in a number of characteristics:
</p>
<ul><li>Adults are overall more deeply <a href="/wiki/Rufous" title="Rufous">rufous</a>, compared with the more washed out colour of juveniles;</li>
<li>Adults have black breast-streaks whereas on juveniles these are pale;</li>
<li>Juveniles have a less deeply forked tail, with a dark subterminal band;</li>
<li>Juveniles have pale tips to all of the greater-coverts (secondary and primary) on both the upper- and under-wings, forming a long narrow pale line; adults have pale fringes to upperwing secondary-coverts only.</li></ul>
<p>These differences hold throughout most of the first year of a bird's life.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Milvus_milvus_MWNH_0750.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Milvus_milvus_MWNH_0750.JPG/220px-Milvus_milvus_MWNH_0750.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Milvus_milvus_MWNH_0750.JPG/330px-Milvus_milvus_MWNH_0750.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Milvus_milvus_MWNH_0750.JPG/440px-Milvus_milvus_MWNH_0750.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3718" data-file-height="2475" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Milvus_milvus_MWNH_0750.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs in the natural history collection of the <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a>, Germany</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Milmil_njg_980613.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Milmil_njg_980613.jpg/220px-Milmil_njg_980613.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Milmil_njg_980613.jpg/330px-Milmil_njg_980613.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Milmil_njg_980613.jpg/440px-Milmil_njg_980613.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1091" data-file-height="698" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Milmil_njg_980613.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Juveniles at nest, Berlin</div></div></div>
<p>Usually red kites first breed when they are two years old, although exceptionally they can successfully breed when they are only one year old.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp198043_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp198043-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> They are <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" class="mw-redirect" title="Monogamous">monogamous</a> and the pair-bond in <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#resident" title="Glossary of bird terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of bird terms § resident" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">resident</span></a></dfn> populations is probably maintained during the winter, particularly when the pair remain on their breeding territory. For migrant populations the fidelity to a particular nesting site means that the pair-bond is likely to be renewed each breeding season.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp198040_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp198040-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The nest is normally placed in a fork of a large hardwood tree at a height of between 12 and 15&#160;m (39 and 49&#160;ft) above the ground. A pair will sometimes use a nest from the previous year and can occasionally occupy an old nest of the <a href="/wiki/Common_buzzard" title="Common buzzard">common buzzard</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp198042_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp198042-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The nest is built by both sexes. The male brings dead twigs 30–50&#160;cm (12–20&#160;in) in length which are placed by the female. The nest is lined with grass and sometimes also with sheep's wool. Unlike the black kite, no greenery is added to the nest. Both sexes continue to add material to the nest during the incubation and nestling periods. Nests vary greatly in size and can become large when the same nest is occupied for several seasons.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp198043_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp198043-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The eggs are laid at three-day intervals. The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is usually between one and three eggs but four and even five eggs have occasionally been recorded. The eggs are non-glossy with a white ground and red-brown spots. The average size is 57&#160;mm ×&#160;45&#160;mm (2.2&#160;in ×&#160;1.8&#160;in) with a calculated weight of 63&#160;g (2.2&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp198043_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp198043-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In Britain and central Europe, laying begins at the end of March but in the Mediterranean area laying begins in early March.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp198042_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp198042-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The eggs are mainly incubated by the female, but the male will relieve her for short periods while she feeds. The male will also bring food for the female. Incubation starts as soon as the first egg is laid. Each egg hatches after 31 to 32 days but as they hatch asynchronously a clutch of three eggs requires 38 days of incubation. The chicks are cared for by both parents. The female <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#brood" title="Glossary of bird terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of bird terms § brood" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">broods</span></a></dfn> them for the first 14 days while the male brings food to the nest which the female feeds to the chicks. Later both parents bring items of food which are placed in the nest to allow the chicks to feed themselves. The nestlings begin climbing onto branches around their nest from 45 days but they rarely <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#fledge" title="Glossary of bird terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of bird terms § fledge" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">fledge</span></a></dfn> before 48–50 days and sometimes not until they are 60–70 days of age. The young spend a further 15–20 days in the neighbourhood of the nest being fed by their parents. Only a single brood is raised each year but if the eggs are lost the female will relay.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp198043_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp198043-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The maximum age recorded is 25 years and 8 months for a ringed bird in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-euring_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euring-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The longevity record for Britain and Ireland is 23 years and 10 months for a bird found dead in Wales in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_feeding">Food and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Food and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_Kite_-_Gigrin_Farm_(10359058775).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Red_Kite_-_Gigrin_Farm_%2810359058775%29.jpg/220px-Red_Kite_-_Gigrin_Farm_%2810359058775%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Red_Kite_-_Gigrin_Farm_%2810359058775%29.jpg/330px-Red_Kite_-_Gigrin_Farm_%2810359058775%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Red_Kite_-_Gigrin_Farm_%2810359058775%29.jpg/440px-Red_Kite_-_Gigrin_Farm_%2810359058775%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1365" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_Kite_-_Gigrin_Farm_(10359058775).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Side view of adult, Wales</div></div></div>
<p>The red kite's diet consists mainly of small <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">mice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vole" title="Vole">voles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shrew" title="Shrew">shrews</a>, young <a href="/wiki/Hare" title="Hare">hares</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a>. It feeds on a wide variety of carrion including sheep carcasses and dead game birds. Live <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> are also taken and occasionally <a href="/wiki/Reptiles" class="mw-redirect" title="Reptiles">reptiles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amphibians" class="mw-redirect" title="Amphibians">amphibians</a>. <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">Earthworms</a> form an important part of the diet, especially in <a href="/wiki/Spring_(season)" title="Spring (season)">spring</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pugh_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pugh-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In some parts of the United Kingdom, red kites are also deliberately fed in domestic <a href="/wiki/Garden" title="Garden">gardens</a>, explaining the presence of red kites in <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban</a> areas.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Here, up to 5% of householders have provided supplementary food for red kites, with <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">chicken</a> the predominant meat provided.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As scavengers, red kites are particularly susceptible to poisoning. Illegal poison baits set for foxes or crows are indiscriminate and kill protected birds and other animals.<sup id="cite_ref-yorkshireredkites_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yorkshireredkites-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> There have also been a number of incidents of red kites and other raptors being targeted by wildlife criminals.<sup id="cite_ref-Carrell2014_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carrell2014-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HeraldSeries_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HeraldSeries-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United Kingdom, there have been several unusual instances of red kites stealing food from people in a similar manner to gulls. One such occurrence took place in <a href="/wiki/Marlow,_Buckinghamshire" title="Marlow, Buckinghamshire">Marlow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a> (a town near a major reintroduction site for the species in the UK in the nearby village of <a href="/wiki/Stokenchurch" title="Stokenchurch">Stokenchurch</a>), in which Red Kites swooped down to steal sandwiches from people in one of the town's parks.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_status">Distribution and status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Distribution and status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_Kite,_Spain.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Red_Kite%2C_Spain.jpg/220px-Red_Kite%2C_Spain.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Red_Kite%2C_Spain.jpg/330px-Red_Kite%2C_Spain.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Red_Kite%2C_Spain.jpg/440px-Red_Kite%2C_Spain.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="667" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_Kite,_Spain.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Red kite in flight in Gredos Mountains, Avila, Spain</div></div></div>
<p>Red kites inhabit broadleaf woodlands, valleys and wetland edges, to 800 metres (2,600&#160;ft). They are native to the western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>, with the European population of 19,000–25,000 pairs encompassing 95% of its global breeding range. It breeds from Spain and Portugal east into central Europe and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, north to southern Sweden, <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>, and south to southern Italy. There is a population in northern <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>. Northern birds move south in winter, mostly staying in the west of the breeding range, but also to eastern <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>. The three largest populations (in Germany, France and Spain, which together hold more than 75% of the global population) declined between 1990 and 2000, and overall the species declined by almost 20% over the ten years. The main threats to red kites are poisoning, through illegal direct poisoning and indirect poisoning from pesticides, particularly in the wintering ranges in France and Spain, and changes in agricultural practices causing a reduction in food resources. Other threats include electrocution, hunting and trapping, deforestation, egg-collection (on a local scale) and possibly competition with the generally more successful black kite <i>M. migrans</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Continental_Europe">Continental Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Continental Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:P6150074.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/P6150074.JPG/220px-P6150074.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/P6150074.JPG/330px-P6150074.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/P6150074.JPG/440px-P6150074.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="2048" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:P6150074.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nestling red kites, <a href="/wiki/Barnim" title="Barnim">Barnim</a>, Germany</div></div></div>
<p>German populations declined by 25%–30% between 1991 and 1997, but have remained stable since. The populations of the northern foothills of the <a href="/wiki/Harz_Mountains" class="mw-redirect" title="Harz Mountains">Harz Mountains</a> (the most densely populated part of its range) suffered an estimated 50% decline from 1991 to 2001. In Spain, the species showed an overall decline in breeding population of up to 43% for the period 1994 to 2001–02, and surveys of wintering birds in 2003–04 suggest a similarly large decline in core wintering areas. The <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Balearic Island">Balearic Islands</a> population has declined from 41–47 breeding pairs in 1993 to just 10 in 2003. In France, breeding populations have decreased in the northeast, but seem to be stable in southwest and central France and <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>. Populations elsewhere are stable or undergoing increases. In Sweden, the species has increased from 30–50 pairs in the 1970s to 1,200 breeding pairs in 2003. In Switzerland, populations increased during the 1990s, and have stabilised.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> According to a report by the Welsh Kite Trust, the UK is the only country in which the red kite population is increasing. Red kites are decreasing in their strongholds of Spain, France and Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-WelshKiteTrust_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WelshKiteTrust-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_Kite_-_Gigrin_Farm_Wales_(10489884433).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Red_Kite_-_Gigrin_Farm_Wales_%2810489884433%29.jpg/220px-Red_Kite_-_Gigrin_Farm_Wales_%2810489884433%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Red_Kite_-_Gigrin_Farm_Wales_%2810489884433%29.jpg/330px-Red_Kite_-_Gigrin_Farm_Wales_%2810489884433%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Red_Kite_-_Gigrin_Farm_Wales_%2810489884433%29.jpg/440px-Red_Kite_-_Gigrin_Farm_Wales_%2810489884433%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1365" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_Kite_-_Gigrin_Farm_Wales_(10489884433).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Red kite, Gigrin Farm, Wales</div></div></div>

<p>In the United Kingdom, red kites were ubiquitous scavengers that lived on carrion and rubbish. Shakespeare's <a href="/wiki/King_Lear" title="King Lear">King Lear</a> describes his daughter <a href="/wiki/Goneril" title="Goneril">Goneril</a> as a <i>detested kite</i>, and he wrote "when the kite builds, look to your lesser linen" in reference to them stealing washing hung out to dry in the nesting season.<sup id="cite_ref-Radio4_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Radio4-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> In the mid-15th century, <a href="/wiki/King_James_II_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="King James II of Scotland">King James II of Scotland</a> decreed that they should be "killed wherever possible", but they remained protected in England and Wales for the next 100 years as they kept the streets free of carrion and rotting food.<sup id="cite_ref-NewKay_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NewKay-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Under Tudor "vermin laws" many creatures were seen as competitors for the produce of the countryside and bounties were paid by the parish for their carcasses.<sup id="cite_ref-McCarthy2007_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy2007-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the 20th century, the breeding population was restricted to a handful of pairs in <a href="/wiki/South_Wales" title="South Wales">South Wales</a>, but recently the Welsh population has been supplemented by re-introductions in England and Scotland. In 2004, from 375 occupied territories identified, at least 216 pairs were thought to have hatched eggs and 200 pairs reared at least 286 young.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 1989, six Swedish birds were released at a site in north Scotland and four Swedish and one Welsh bird in <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_conservation_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB_conservation-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Altogether, 93 birds of Swedish and Spanish origin were released at each of the sites. In the second stage of reintroduction in 1995 and 1996, further birds were brought from Germany to populate areas of <a href="/wiki/Dumfries_and_Galloway" title="Dumfries and Galloway">Dumfries and Galloway</a>. Between 2004 and 2006, 94 birds were brought from the Chilterns and introduced into the <a href="/wiki/River_Derwent,_North_East_England" title="River Derwent, North East England">Derwent Valley</a> in north East England.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_conservation_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB_conservation-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In Northern Ireland, 80 birds from wild stock in Wales were released between 2008 and 2010, and the first successful breeding was recorded in 2010. The reintroductions in <a href="/wiki/The_Chilterns" class="mw-redirect" title="The Chilterns">the Chilterns</a> <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> have been a success. Between 1989 and 1993, 90 birds were released there and by 2002, 139 pairs were breeding.<sup id="cite_ref-Chilterns_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chilterns-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> They can commonly be seen taking advantage of thermals from the <a href="/wiki/M40_motorway" title="M40 motorway">M40 motorway</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-McCarthy2_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy2-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Another successful reintroduction has been in <a href="/wiki/Northamptonshire" title="Northamptonshire">Northamptonshire</a>, which has become a stronghold for the red kite.<sup id="cite_ref-birdguides_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdguides-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Thirty Spanish birds were introduced into <a href="/wiki/Rockingham_Forest" title="Rockingham Forest">Rockingham Forest</a> near <a href="/wiki/Corby" title="Corby">Corby</a> in 2000,<sup id="cite_ref-northantset_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-northantset-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> and by 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Protection_of_Birds" title="Royal Society for the Protection of Birds">RSPB</a> estimated that over 200 chicks had been reared from the initial release. So successful has the reintroduction been that 30 chicks have been transported from Rockingham Forest for release in <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> From the Chilterns they have spread as far east as Essex and can be seen over the Town of Harlow
</p><p>A sighting of the first red kite in London for 150 years was reported in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i> newspaper in January 2006<sup id="cite_ref-McCarthy2006_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthy2006-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> and in June of that year, the UK-based Northern Kites Project reported that kites had bred in the Derwent Valley in and around <a href="/wiki/Rowlands_Gill" title="Rowlands Gill">Rowlands Gill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tyne_and_Wear" title="Tyne and Wear">Tyne and Wear</a> for the first time since the re-introduction.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC2006_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2006-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1999, the red kite was named 'Bird of the Century' by the <a href="/wiki/British_Trust_for_Ornithology" title="British Trust for Ornithology">British Trust for Ornithology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Radio4_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Radio4-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> According to the Welsh Kite Trust, it has been voted "Wales's favourite bird".<sup id="cite_ref-WalesFavBird_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WalesFavBird-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Forestry_Commission" title="Forestry Commission">Forestry Commission</a> North West England announced a three-year project to release 90 red kites in <a href="/wiki/Grizedale_Forest" title="Grizedale Forest">Grizedale Forest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a> under a special licence issued by <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a>. The Grizedale programme was the ninth reintroduction of red kites into different regions of the UK and the final re-introduction phase in England.<sup id="cite_ref-WestmorlandGazette_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WestmorlandGazette-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The stated aims of the Grizedale project were:
</p>
<ul><li>To establish a viable population of red kites in Grizedale, South Cumbria by 2015.</li>
<li>To increase the rate of red kite expansion into North West England and link up with existing populations in Wales, Yorkshire, North East England and South West Scotland and so increase the chances of a continuous geographical range.</li>
<li>To develop community involvement and create educational opportunities arising from the project.<sup id="cite_ref-FC_Poster_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FC_Poster-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>As of July 2011, non-breeding birds are regularly seen in all parts of Britain, and the number of breeding pairs is too large for the RSPB to continue to survey them on an annual basis.<sup id="cite_ref-rspb2_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb2-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Red kites were extinct in Ireland by the middle nineteenth century, due to persecution, poisoning and woodland clearance. In May 2007, <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_the_Environment,_Community_and_Local_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Minister for the Environment, Community and Local Government">Minister for the Environment, Heritage and Local Government</a> <a href="/wiki/Dick_Roche" title="Dick Roche">Dick Roche</a> announced an agreement to bring at least 100 birds from Wales to restock the population as part of a 5-year programme in the <a href="/wiki/Wicklow_Mountains" title="Wicklow Mountains">Wicklow Mountains</a>, similar to the earlier <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">golden eagle</a> reintroduction programme.<sup id="cite_ref-NDP_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NDP-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> On 19 July 2007, the first thirty red kites were released in <a href="/wiki/County_Wicklow" title="County Wicklow">County Wicklow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-environ.ie_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-environ.ie-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wildlifeextra_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wildlifeextra-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> On 22 May 2010, 2 newly hatched red kite chicks were discovered in the <a href="/wiki/County_Wicklow" title="County Wicklow">Wicklow mountains</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Melia2010_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Melia2010-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> bringing the number of chicks hatched since reintroduction to 7.<sup id="cite_ref-goldeneagle_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goldeneagle-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">Sweden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sweden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sweden is one location where the red kite seems to be increasing, with around 2,000 pairs in 2009, some of which are overwintering and some flying south to the Mediterranean for the winter. They return around March–April. The red kite is the landscape bird of <a href="/wiki/Scania" title="Scania">Scania</a>, and the coat of arms of the municipality of <a href="/wiki/Tomelilla" title="Tomelilla">Tomelilla</a>. The kite is often seen along the roadsides and roaming the open colourful wheat and rapeseed fields of Scania.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observation">Observation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Observation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the best places to see the red kite in <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> is <a href="/wiki/Scania" title="Scania">Scania</a> in southern Sweden. It may be observed in one of its breeding locations such as the <a href="/wiki/Kullaberg" title="Kullaberg">Kullaberg</a> Nature Preserve near <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6lle" title="Mölle">Mölle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hogan2005_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hogan2005-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> In Switzerland, they are a common sight in all rural areas, excluding the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> and its foothills.
</p><p>Some of the best places to see them in the United Kingdom are Gigrin Farm near <a href="/wiki/Rhayader" title="Rhayader">Rhayader</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mid_Wales" title="Mid Wales">mid Wales</a>, where hundreds are fed by the local farmer as a tourist attraction,<sup id="cite_ref-gigrin_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gigrin-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> a Red Kite Feeding Station at <a href="/wiki/Llanddeusant,_Carmarthenshire" title="Llanddeusant, Carmarthenshire">Llanddeusant</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Brecon_Beacons" title="Brecon Beacons">Brecon Beacons</a>, visited daily by over 50 birds,<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> and the Bwlch Nant yr Arian forest visitor centre in <a href="/wiki/Ceredigion" title="Ceredigion">Ceredigion</a><sup id="cite_ref-nantyrarian_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nantyrarian-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> where the rare <a href="/wiki/Leucism" title="Leucism">leucistic</a> variant can be seen.<sup id="cite_ref-Melton2008_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Melton2008-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> In the UK, the <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">Oxfordshire</a> part of the <a href="/wiki/Chilterns" class="mw-redirect" title="Chilterns">Chilterns</a> has many red kites, especially near <a href="/wiki/Henley-on-Thames" title="Henley-on-Thames">Henley-on-Thames</a> and <a href="/wiki/Watlington,_Oxfordshire" title="Watlington, Oxfordshire">Watlington</a>, where they were introduced on <a href="/wiki/John_Paul_Getty,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="John Paul Getty, Jr.">John Paul Getty</a>'s estate.<sup id="cite_ref-Chilterns_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chilterns-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Red Kites are also becoming common in <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a>, often being seen near <a href="/wiki/Stokenchurch" title="Stokenchurch">Stokenchurch</a>, where a population was released in the 1990s, and <a href="/wiki/Flackwell_Heath" title="Flackwell Heath">Flackwell Heath</a> near <a href="/wiki/High_Wycombe" title="High Wycombe">High Wycombe</a>. They can also be seen around <a href="/wiki/Harewood" title="Harewood">Harewood</a> near <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> where they were re-introduced in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-Harewood_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harewood-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> In Ireland they can be best observed at Redcross, near <a href="/wiki/Avoca,_County_Wicklow" title="Avoca, County Wicklow">Avoca, County Wicklow</a>.
