<p>The <b>calandra lark</b> (<i>Melanocorypha calandra</i>) or <b>European calandra-lark</b> breeds in warm <a href="/wiki/Temperate_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate countries">temperate countries</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> and eastwards through Turkey into northern Iran and southern Russia. It is replaced further east by its relative, the <a href="/wiki/Bimaculated_lark" title="Bimaculated lark">bimaculated lark</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calandra_lark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The calandra lark was originally placed in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Alauda" title="Alauda">Alauda</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The current genus name, <i>Melanocorypha</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>melas</i>, "black", and <i>koruphos</i> a term used by ancient writers for a now unknown bird, but here confused with <i>korudos</i>, "lark". "Calandra"' derives ultimately from <i>kalandros</i> the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> name for this bird.<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OED_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Bimaculated_lark" title="Bimaculated lark">bimaculated lark</a> is also sometimes termed as the calandra lark.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calandra_lark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Four <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are recognized:<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<ul><li><b>Western calandra lark</b> (<i>M. c. calandra</i>) - <small>(<a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, 1766)</small>: Found in southern Europe and north-western Africa to Turkey (except south-central and south-eastern Turkey), <a href="/wiki/Transcaucasia" title="Transcaucasia">Transcaucasia</a> and north-western Iran</li>
<li><b>Eastern calandra lark</b> (<i>M. c. psammochroa</i>) - <small><a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a>, 1904</small>: Found from northern Iraq and northern Iran to Turkmenistan and Kazakhstan</li>
<li><i>M. c. gaza</i> - <small><a href="/wiki/Richard_Meinertzhagen" title="Richard Meinertzhagen">Meinertzhagen, R</a>, 1919</small>: Originally described as a subspecies of the bimaculated lark. Found from eastern Syria and south-eastern Turkey to south-western Iran</li>
<li><b>Levant calandra lark</b> (<i>M. c. hebraica</i>) - <small>Meinertzhagen, R, 1920</small>: Found from south-central Turkey and north-western Syria to Israel and western Jordan</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calandra_lark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is a large, robust <a href="/wiki/Lark" title="Lark">lark</a>, 17.5–20&#160;cm long. It is an undistinguished-looking species on the ground, mainly streaked greyish brown above and white below, and with large black patches on the breast sides. It has a white supercilium.
</p><p>In flight it shows short broad wings, which are dark below, and a short white-edged tail. The wing and tail patterns are distinctions from its more easterly relatives.
</p><p>The song is like a slower version of that of the <a href="/wiki/Skylark" class="mw-redirect" title="Skylark">skylark</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calandra_lark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is mainly resident in the west of its range, but Russian populations of this <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> are more <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, moving further south in winter, as far as the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabian peninsula">Arabian peninsula</a> and Egypt. It is a very rare vagrant to western Europe.
</p><p>This is a bird of open cultivation and <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a>. Its nest is on the ground, with 4–5 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> being laid. Food is seeds supplemented with insects in the breeding season. It is gregarious outside the breeding season.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calandra_lark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species occupies open plains, from steppes and pastures to extensive dry cereal cultivations and true steppe with dense grass cover. In the Mediterranean Basin it is mainly found in dry pastures and dry cultivations. In cultivated areas, it prefers fallows, long-fallows and field edges and to a lesser extent sown fields, selecting unirrigated legumes and barley fields. The species is monogamous and lays eggs from early April to July. The nest is made from grass stems and small leaves, lined with softer material and built in a shallow depression on the ground, often under a tussock.  Clutches are usually three to six eggs (de Juana and Suárez 2004). Its diet is seasonal, feeding mostly on insects in the summers and seeds and grass shoots in the winter. Mediterranean populations are resident, forming large flocks in the autumn and winter (Snow and Perrins 1998, de Juana and Suárez 2004). Eastern populations are migratory or partially migratory (de Juana and Suárez 2004).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Parasites of the calandra lark include the <a href="/wiki/Mallophaga" title="Mallophaga">chewing louse</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Ricinus_vaderi" title="Ricinus vaderi">Ricinus vaderi</a></i>, described from specimens collected in Azerbaijan.<sup id="cite_ref-ValanSychra2016_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ValanSychra2016-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calandra_lark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The song is considered so musical to human ears that the calandra lark was formerly a popular <a href="/wiki/Aviculture" title="Aviculture">cagebird</a> in its range.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> It is mentioned in, for instance, the <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscan</a> <a href="/wiki/Proverb" title="Proverb">proverb</a> <i>"Canta come una calandra"</i>, he or she sings like a lark,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and the Spanish <a href="/wiki/Ballad" title="Ballad">ballad</a> <i>"Romance del prisionero"</i>, where its song is the only way the prisoner knows when day breaks.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
