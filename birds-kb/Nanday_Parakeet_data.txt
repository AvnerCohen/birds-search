<p>The <b>nanday parakeet</b> (<i>Aratinga nenday</i>), also known as the <b>black-hooded parakeet</b> or <b>nanday conure</b>, is a medium-small, mostly green, <a href="/wiki/Neotropical_parrot" title="Neotropical parrot">Neotropical parrot</a> native to continental South America.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanday_parakeet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The nanday parakeet was previously regarded as a member of the monotypic genus <i>Nandayus</i> <small>Bonaparte, 1854</small> one of the rough 16 genera of Neotropical parrots of tribe <a href="/wiki/Arini_(tribe)" title="Arini (tribe)">Arini</a> which includes the <a href="/wiki/Conure" title="Conure">conures</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macaw" title="Macaw">macaws</a>. However, phylogenetic evidence showed that it is positioned in one of the four groups in the genus <i>Aratinga</i>. When the genus <i>Aratinga</i> was revised and split in four separate genera, this species was included in the current genus <i>Aratinga</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-AratingaSplit_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AratingaSplit-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This revision was based on earlier studies. According to a <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" class="mw-redirect" title="MtDNA">mtDNA</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic_analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Phylogenetic analysis">phylogenetic analysis</a> of the genus <i>Aratinga</i> by Ribas &amp; Miyaki (2004), the nanday conure forms a monophyletic group with the <a href="/wiki/Sun_conure" class="mw-redirect" title="Sun conure">sun conure</a> (<i>A. solstitialis</i>), <a href="/wiki/Jenday_conure" class="mw-redirect" title="Jenday conure">jenday conure</a> (<i>A. jandaya</i>), and <a href="/wiki/Golden-capped_conure" class="mw-redirect" title="Golden-capped conure">golden-capped conure</a> (<i>A. auricapilla</i>). Tavares <i>et al.</i> (2005) in a mitochondrial and nuclear DNA analysis of 29 species representing 25 of 30 genera of Neotropical parrots found the nanday conure's closest relative to be <i>Aratinga solstitialis</i>, the sun conure, and the time of divergence of the species to have been 0.5 to 1.3 <a href="/wiki/Million_years_ago" class="mw-redirect" title="Million years ago">Mya</a>.
</p><p>Hybrids of the nanday and sun conures, nanday and jenday conures, and nanday and blue-crowned conures are known.
</p><p>A prehistoric relative, <i><a href="/wiki/Aratinga_vorohuensis" title="Aratinga vorohuensis">Aratinga vorohuensis</a></i>, was described from <a href="/wiki/Late_Pliocene" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Pliocene">Late Pliocene</a> fossils found in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanday_parakeet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The nanday parakeet is 27–30&#160;cm (11–12&#160;in) long, weighs 140&#160;g (4.9&#160;oz), and is mostly green in color. Its most distinguishing characteristic, for which it is named, is its black facial mask and beak. It also shows black, trailing flight feathers on its wings and has a long tail edged at the end in blue. The upper chest is bluish-green and the lower chest is a paler green. Feathers covering the thighs are red.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanday_parakeet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The species is native to <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> from southeast <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a> to southwest <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, central <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a>, and northern <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, from the region known as the <a href="/wiki/Pantanal" title="Pantanal">Pantanal</a>. Caged birds have been released in some areas, and the birds have established self-sustaining populations in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio,_Texas" class="mw-redirect" title="San Antonio, Texas">San Antonio, Texas</a>, and several areas of Florida (including <a href="/wiki/Pasco_County,_Florida" title="Pasco County, Florida">Pasco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sarasota_County,_Florida" title="Sarasota County, Florida">Sarasota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pinellas_County,_Florida" title="Pinellas County, Florida">Pinellas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manatee_County,_Florida" title="Manatee County, Florida">Manatee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Broward_County,_Florida" title="Broward County, Florida">Broward</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Miami-Dade_County,_Florida" title="Miami-Dade County, Florida">Miami-Dade</a> Counties).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanday_parakeet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_feeding">Food and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanday_parakeet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Food and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bird feeds on <a href="/wiki/Seed_predation" title="Seed predation">seeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frugivore" title="Frugivore">fruit</a>, palm nuts, <a href="/wiki/Berries" class="mw-redirect" title="Berries">berries</a>, flowers, and buds. <a href="/wiki/Feral" title="Feral">Feral</a> birds also come to bird feeders. Wild birds primarily use scrub forest and forest clearings around settlements. They frequent open <a href="/wiki/Savannah" class="mw-redirect" title="Savannah">savannah</a>, pastures, and stockyards in South America, where they are considered as pests in some areas.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanday_parakeet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nanday parakeets usually find holes in trees to nest. Females lay three or four eggs. After raising their young, all birds form rather large communal roosts until the next breeding season.
</p>
