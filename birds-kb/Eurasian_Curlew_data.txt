<p>The <b>Eurasian Curlew</b> (<i>Numenius arquata</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the large <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Scolopacidae" title="Scolopacidae" class="mw-redirect">Scolopacidae</a>. It is one of the most widespread of the <a href="/wiki/Curlew" title="Curlew">curlews</a>, breeding across temperate <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. In Europe, this <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> is often referred to just as <b>"the Curlew"</b>, and in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> known as the <b>"whaup"</b> in <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a>. This bird is also called क्रौञ्च(krauJca) in Sanskrit.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Numenius_arquata_male_flying.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Numenius_arquata_male_flying.jpg/220px-Numenius_arquata_male_flying.jpg" width="220" height="263" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Numenius_arquata_male_flying.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Male in flight. Note "pointed tail" formed by the feet.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This is the largest wader in its range, at 50–60 cm (20–24 in) in length, with a 89–106 cm (35–42 in) wingspan and a body weight of 410–1,360 g (0.90–3.0 lb).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> It is mainly greyish brown, with a white back, and a very long curved bill. Males and females look identical, but the bill is longest in the adult female. It is generally not possible to recognize the sex of a single Eurasian Curlew, or even several ones as there is much variation; telling male and female of a mated pair apart is usually possible however.</p>
<p>The familiar call, from which this <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> gets it name, is a loud <i>curloo-oo</i>.</p>
<p>The only similar species over most of the Curlew's range is the <a href="/wiki/Whimbrel" title="Whimbrel">Whimbrel</a> (<i>N. phaeopus</i>). This is smaller, and has a shorter <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> with a kink rather than a smooth curve. Flying birds may resemble a <a href="/wiki/Bar-tailed_Godwit" title="Bar-tailed Godwit">Bar-tailed Godwit</a> (<i>Limosa lapponica</i>) in winter plumage, but that species is smaller, has a slightly upturned bill, and its feet are barely longer than the tail tip; in the Eurasian Curlew the feet are longer, forming a conspicuous "point".</p>
<p>This is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> species over most of its range, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and south <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. Occasionally, a vagrant individual reaches places far away from its normal range, such as <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> or the <a href="/wiki/Marianas" title="Marianas" class="mw-redirect">Marianas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> It is present all year in the milder climate of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and the adjacent European coasts.</p>
<p>It is generally wary. Highly gregarious outside the breeding season, the Eurasian Curlew feeds by probing soft mud for small <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>, but will also pick small <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a> off the surface if the opportunity arises.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Eggs_Numenius_arquata.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Eggs_Numenius_arquata.jpg/220px-Eggs_Numenius_arquata.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Eggs_Numenius_arquata.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> of Eurasian Curlew's eggs</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a> is a bare scrape on <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a>, meadow or similar habitat. 3-6 eggs are laid in April/May, and incubated for about a month to hatching.</p>
<p>This is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>
<p>Formerly classified as a species of <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN" class="mw-redirect">IUCN</a>, it was suspected to be rarer than generally assumed. Following the evaluation of its population size, this was found to be incorrect, and it is consequently uplisted to <a href="/wiki/Near_Threatened" title="Near Threatened">Near Threatened</a> status in 2008; though it is still a rather common bird, its numbers are noticeably declining.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> In Ireland, for example, the breeding population is estimated to have declined by 86% in the last 30 years.</p>
