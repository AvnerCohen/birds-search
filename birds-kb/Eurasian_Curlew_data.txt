<p>The <b>Eurasian curlew</b> or <b>common curlew</b> (<i>Numenius arquata</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the large <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Scolopacidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Scolopacidae">Scolopacidae</a>. It is one of the most widespread of the <a href="/wiki/Curlew" title="Curlew">curlews</a>, breeding across temperate <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Asia</a>. In Europe, this <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> is often referred to just as the "curlew", and in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> known as the "whaup" in <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a>.
</p><p>This is the largest wader in its range, at 50–60&#160;cm (20–24&#160;in) in length, with an 89–106&#160;cm (35–42&#160;in) wingspan and a body weight of 410–1,360&#160;g (0.90–3.00&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is mainly greyish brown, with a white back, greyish-blue legs and a very long curved bill. Males and females look identical, but the bill is longest in the adult female. It is generally not possible to recognize the sex of a single Eurasian curlew, or even several ones, as there is much variation; telling male and female of a mated pair apart is usually possible however.
</p><p>The familiar call is a loud <i>curloo-oo</i>.
</p><p>The only similar species over most of the curlew's range is the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_whimbrel" title="Eurasian whimbrel">Eurasian whimbrel</a> (<i>N. phaeopus</i>). The whimbrel is smaller and has a shorter <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> with a kink rather than a smooth curve. Flying curlews may also resemble <a href="/wiki/Bar-tailed_godwit" title="Bar-tailed godwit">bar-tailed godwits</a> (<i>Limosa lapponica</i>) in their winter plumages; however, the latter have a smaller body, a slightly upturned beak, and legs that do not reach far beyond their tail tips. The Eurasian curlew's feet are longer, forming a conspicuous "point".
</p><p>The curlew exists as a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> species over most of its range, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and south <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. Occasionally a vagrant individual reaches places far from its normal range, such as <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a><sup id="cite_ref-NHMNS_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHMNS-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Marianas" class="mw-redirect" title="Marianas">Marianas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiles2000_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiles2000-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wiles2004_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiles2004-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It is present all year in the milder climates of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and the United Kingdom and its adjacent European coasts.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_curlew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The English name "curlew" is imitative of the Eurasian curlew's call, but may have been influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i>corliu</i>, "messenger", from <i>courir </i>, "to run".  It was first recorded in 1377 in <a href="/wiki/William_Langland" title="William Langland">Langland's</a> <a href="/wiki/Piers_Plowman" title="Piers Plowman">Piers Plowman</a> "<i>Fissch to lyue in þe flode..Þe corlue by kynde of þe eyre</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-OED_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The genus name <i>Numenius</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> νουμήνιος, <i>noumēnios</i>, a bird mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Hesychius_of_Miletus" title="Hesychius of Miletus">Hesychius</a>. It is associated with the curlew because it appears to be derived from <i>neos</i>, "new" and <i>mene</i> "moon", referring to the crescent-shaped bill. The species name <i>arquata</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> name for this bird, derived from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>arcuatus</i>, "bow-shaped", and again referring to the shape of the bill.<sup id="cite_ref-job_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_curlew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are three <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of the Eurasian curlew:
</p>
<ul><li><i>N. a. arquata</i>, <small>(<a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">1758</a>)</small>: breeds in west, north &amp; central <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.</li>
<li><i>N. a. orientalis</i>, <small><a href="/wiki/Christian_Ludwig_Brehm" title="Christian Ludwig Brehm">Brehm</a>, 1831</small>: breeds in west &amp; central <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> through to <a href="/wiki/Northeast_China" title="Northeast China">Northeast China</a>.</li>
<li><i>N. a. suschkini</i>, <small><a href="/wiki/Oscar_Neumann" title="Oscar Neumann">Neumann</a>, 1929</small>: breeds from western <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> to southwestern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>.</li></ul>
<p>It is generally wary. Highly gregarious outside the breeding season, the Eurasian curlew feeds by probing soft mud for small <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>, but will also pick small <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a> off the surface if the opportunity arises.
</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a> is a bare scrape on <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a>, meadow, and similar habitats. Each curlew lays between 3 and 6 eggs in April or May and incubates them for about a month until they begin to hatch. It has been observed that curlews tend to nest close to <a href="/wiki/Common_kestrel" title="Common kestrel">common kestrel</a>'s nests, as they can offer protection from other predators, such as <a href="/wiki/Corvidae" title="Corvidae">corvids</a>, even though kestrels also predate curlew nests.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The curlew is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p><p>Formerly classified as a species of <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>, it was suspected to be rarer than generally assumed. Following the evaluation of its population size, the classification was found to be incorrect, and it was consequently promoted to <a href="/wiki/Near_Threatened" class="mw-redirect" title="Near Threatened">Near Threatened</a> status in 2008. Though it is a common bird, its numbers are noticeably declining,<sup id="cite_ref-BLI2008_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BLI2008-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> particularly in the United Kingdom and Ireland, which have about a quarter of the global population. In the twenty years up to 2016, the population is estimated to have declined by more than 50% in England and Scotland, more than 80% in Wales, and more than 90% in Ireland. At the end of 2015 it was placed on the United Kingdom's red list of most endangered bird species.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><br />
</p>
