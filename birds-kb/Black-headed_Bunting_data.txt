<p>The <b>Black-headed Bunting</b> (<i>Emberiza melanocephala</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bunting_(bird)" title="Bunting (bird)">bunting</a> family Emberizidae. It breeds in southeast <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> east to <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrates</a> in winter mainly to India, with some individuals moving further into Southeast Asia. Like others in its family, it is found in open grassland habitats where they fly in flocks in search of grains and seed. Adult males are well marked with yellow underparts, chestnut back and a black head. Adult females in breeding plumage look like duller males. In other plumages, they can be hard to separate from the closely related <a href="/wiki/Red-headed_Bunting" title="Red-headed Bunting">Red-headed Bunting</a> and natural hybridization occurs between the two species in the zone of overlap of their breeding ranges in northern Iran.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-headed_Bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>This bird is 15&#160;cm long, larger than <a href="/wiki/Reed_Bunting" title="Reed Bunting">Reed Bunting</a>, and long-tailed. The breeding male has bright yellow underparts, chestnut upperparts and a black hood. The female is a washed-out version of the male, with paler underparts, a grey-brown back and a greyish head. The juvenile is similar but the vent is yellow, and both can be difficult to separate from the corresponding plumages of the closely related <a href="/wiki/Red-headed_Bunting" title="Red-headed Bunting">Red-headed Bunting</a> although the Black-headed tends to have the cheeks darker than the throat. First year males have a grey crown and the back has patches of chestnut and grey. First year females can be difficult to separate from female Red-headed Buntings although having more streaking on the crown than on the lower back. The vent is yellow.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Black-headed and Red-headed Bunting represent sister species which forms a clade along with the <a href="/wiki/Crested_Bunting" title="Crested Bunting">Crested Bunting</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-headed_Bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habitat and distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_distribution">Habitat and distribution</span></h2>
<p>Black-headed Bunting breeds in open scrubby areas including agricultural land. In winter they move to Asia and the large flocks are found in agricultural fields and grasslands. The longest migration noted from a ringed individual is about 7,000&#160;km. Another ringed bird was determined to have flown 1,000&#160;km in seven days. Males form pure flocks during migration and arrive in the winter quarters well before the females.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The winter range within India is mainly in western and northern India extending south to northern Karnataka.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> In winter they form large communal roosts in thorny acacia trees, often joining other species such as the <a href="/wiki/Yellow-throated_Sparrow" title="Yellow-throated Sparrow">Yellow-throated Sparrow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The main breeding zone extends from southeastern Europe to central Asia. The wintering grounds are mainly in India although vagrants have been found wintering as far east as Japan, China, Hong Kong, Thailand, Laos, South Korea and Malaysia.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Summer vagrants may occur as far north in Europe as Norway.</p>

<p>The Black-headed Bunting is found in flocks as it forages on grasslands for seeds. They breed in summer, building a nest in a low bush or on the ground. The nest is a cup made of dry grass and lined with hair.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The clutch consists of 4-6 eggs. The eggs hatch after about 13 days and the chicks fledge after about 10 days. Its natural food consists of insects when feeding young, and otherwise seeds. In Bulgaria, the collapse of the drying cotton thistle (<i><a href="/wiki/Onopordum_acanthium" title="Onopordum acanthium">Onopordum acanthium</a></i>) stems on which the birds build their nests has caused high mortality; this is thought to be an example of an <a href="/wiki/Ecological_trap" title="Ecological trap">ecological trap</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> In northern Iran, there is a region of range overlap with the Red-headed Bunting and natural hybrids are common<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> although molecular data indicates that there is considerable genetic divergence between the two species.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Like the Red-headed Bunting but unlike many other <i>Emberiza</i> buntings, it has two moults in a year. It undergoes one moult in the winter quarters prior to migrating back to the breeding region, and another after breeding. Young birds fledge with a soft plumage and then moult into a juvenile plumage before migrating and then assume an adult plumage after moulting in their winter quarters.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In winter their call is a single note tweet or soft <i>zrit</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The song consists of a loud series of strophes each made up a high harsh notes that accelerate into a jangling mix with some clear slurred notes interspersed before stopping abruptly.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
