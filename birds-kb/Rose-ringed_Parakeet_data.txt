<p>The <b>rose-ringed parakeet</b> (<i>Psittacula krameri</i>), also known as the <b>ring-necked parakeet</b>, is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Parrot" title="Parrot">parrot</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <a href="/wiki/Psittacula" title="Psittacula">Psittacula</a>, of the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Psittacidae" title="Psittacidae">Psittacidae</a>. It has disjunct native ranges in Africa and South Asia, and is now introduced into many other parts of the world where feral populations have established themselves and are bred for the exotic pet trade. 
</p><p>The rose-ringed parakeet is <a href="/wiki/Sexually_dimorphic" class="mw-redirect" title="Sexually dimorphic">sexually dimorphic</a>. The adult male sports a red and black neck ring, and the hen and immature birds of both sexes either show no neck rings, or display shadow-like pale to dark grey neck rings. Both sexes have a distinctive green colour in the wild, and captive bred ringnecks have multiple colour mutations including blue, violet and yellow. Rose-ringed parakeets measure on average 40&#160;cm (16&#160;in) in length, including the tail <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a>, a large portion of their total length. Their average single-wing length is about 15 to 17.5&#160;cm (5.9 to 6.9&#160;in). In the wild, this is a noisy species with an unmistakable squawking <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Bird vocalisation">call</a>. Captive individuals can be <a href="/wiki/Talking_bird" title="Talking bird">taught to speak</a>. They are a herbivorous and non-<a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> species.
</p><p>One of the few parrot species that have successfully adapted to living in disturbed habitats, it has withstood the onslaught of urbanisation and <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>. As a popular pet species, <a href="/wiki/Feral_parrot" title="Feral parrot">escaped birds</a> have colonised a number of cities around the world, including Northern and Western Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_How_do_parakeets_survive_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_How_do_parakeets_survive-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> These parakeets have also proven themselves capable of living in a variety of climates outside their native range, and are able to survive low winter temperatures in Northern Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The species is listed as <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a> by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN) because its population appears to be increasing, but its popularity as a pet and unpopularity with farmers have reduced its numbers in some parts of its native range.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose-ringed_parakeet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rose-ringed_Parakeet_-_Sri_Lanka_-_01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Rose-ringed_Parakeet_-_Sri_Lanka_-_01.jpg/220px-Rose-ringed_Parakeet_-_Sri_Lanka_-_01.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Rose-ringed_Parakeet_-_Sri_Lanka_-_01.jpg/330px-Rose-ringed_Parakeet_-_Sri_Lanka_-_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Rose-ringed_Parakeet_-_Sri_Lanka_-_01.jpg/440px-Rose-ringed_Parakeet_-_Sri_Lanka_-_01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4608" data-file-height="3456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rose-ringed_Parakeet_-_Sri_Lanka_-_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Head detail of <i>P. k. manillensis</i>.</div></div></div>
<p>Four <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are recognised, though they differ little:
</p>
<ul><li>African subspecies:</li></ul>
<dl><dd><b>African rose-ringed parakeet</b> (<i>P. k. krameri</i>): western Africa in <a href="/wiki/Guinea" title="Guinea">Guinea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>, and southern <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a>, east to western <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, north to <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>. Resident along the Nile valley and certainly <a href="/wiki/Giza" title="Giza">Giza</a>, it is sometimes seen on the north coast and in <a href="/wiki/Sinai_Peninsula" title="Sinai Peninsula">Sinai</a>. The African parakeet also started to breed in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> in the 1980s and is considered an <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a>.</dd>
<dd><b>Abyssinian rose-ringed parakeet</b> (<i>P. k. parvirostris</i>): northwest <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>, west across northern <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sennar_(state)" title="Sennar (state)">Sennar</a> state, <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a></dd></dl>
<ul><li>Asian subspecies:</li></ul>
<dl><dd><b>Indian rose-ringed parakeet</b> (<i>P. k. manillensis</i>) originates from the southern <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> and has feral and naturalised populations worldwide. In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> (mainly around <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>), the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, and other western countries, it is often referred to as the Indian ringneck parrot.<sup id="cite_ref-Determination_of_the_origin_of_British_feral_Rose-ringed_Parakeets_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Determination_of_the_origin_of_British_feral_Rose-ringed_Parakeets-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Morgan_Feral_Rose-ringed_Parakeets_in_Britain_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morgan_Feral_Rose-ringed_Parakeets_in_Britain-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></dd>
<dd><b>Boreal rose-ringed parakeet</b> (<i>P. k. borealis</i>) is distributed in <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a> to central <a href="/wiki/Burma" class="mw-redirect" title="Burma">Burma</a>; introduced populations are found worldwide.</dd></dl>
<p>The Indian subspecies are both larger than the African subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-Determination_of_the_origin_of_British_feral_Rose-ringed_Parakeets_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Determination_of_the_origin_of_British_feral_Rose-ringed_Parakeets-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><br />
The genus name <i>Psittacula</i> is a diminutive of <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>psittacus</i>, "parrot", and the specific <i>krameri</i> commemorates the Austrian <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">naturalist</a> <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Heinrich_Kramer" title="Wilhelm Heinrich Kramer">Wilhelm Heinrich Kramer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-job_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose-ringed_parakeet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rose_ringed_parakeet.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Rose_ringed_parakeet.JPG/220px-Rose_ringed_parakeet.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Rose_ringed_parakeet.JPG/330px-Rose_ringed_parakeet.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Rose_ringed_parakeet.JPG/440px-Rose_ringed_parakeet.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4481" data-file-height="2985" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rose_ringed_parakeet.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Parakeets in Garaboli National Park making a beak-lock – a common act in parakeet pairs</div></div></div>
<p>Since the 19th Century, the rose-ringed parakeet has successfully colonised many other countries. It breeds further north than any other parrot species. It has established itself on a large scale in Germany, France, Belgium, the Netherlands, Italy, and especially the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-Parau_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parau-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> See Feral Birds section below.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology_and_behaviour">Ecology and behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose-ringed_parakeet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology and behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose-ringed_parakeet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Babies_of_Rose-Ringed_Parakeet_in_hollow_trunk.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Babies_of_Rose-Ringed_Parakeet_in_hollow_trunk.JPG/220px-Babies_of_Rose-Ringed_Parakeet_in_hollow_trunk.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Babies_of_Rose-Ringed_Parakeet_in_hollow_trunk.JPG/330px-Babies_of_Rose-Ringed_Parakeet_in_hollow_trunk.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Babies_of_Rose-Ringed_Parakeet_in_hollow_trunk.JPG/440px-Babies_of_Rose-Ringed_Parakeet_in_hollow_trunk.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3469" data-file-height="2137" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Babies_of_Rose-Ringed_Parakeet_in_hollow_trunk.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chicks in tree hole</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rose-ringed_parakeet.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Rose-ringed_parakeet.JPG/220px-Rose-ringed_parakeet.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Rose-ringed_parakeet.JPG/330px-Rose-ringed_parakeet.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Rose-ringed_parakeet.JPG/440px-Rose-ringed_parakeet.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4896" data-file-height="3672" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rose-ringed_parakeet.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rose-ringed parakeets feeding on stored grain</div></div></div>
<p>In the wild, rose-ringed parakeets usually feed on buds, fruits, vegetables, <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nuts</a>, berries, and <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a>. Wild flocks also fly several miles to forage in farmlands and orchards, causing extensive damage.
</p><p>In India, they feed on <a href="/wiki/Cereal_grain" class="mw-redirect" title="Cereal grain">cereal grains</a>, and during winter also on <a href="/wiki/Pigeon_pea" title="Pigeon pea">pigeon peas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In Egypt during the spring, they feed on <a href="/wiki/Morus_(plant)" title="Morus (plant)">mulberry</a> and in summer they feed on <a href="/wiki/Date_palm#Dates" title="Date palm">dates</a> and nest inside palm trees and eat from sunflower and corn fields.
</p><p>In captivity, rose-ringed parakeets will take a large variety of food and can be fed on a number of fruits, vegetables, pellets, seeds, and even small amounts of cooked meat for protein. Oils, salts, chocolate, alcohol, and other preservatives should be avoided.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose-ringed_parakeet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In north-west India, Indian rose-ringed parakeets form pairs from September to December. They do not have life mates and often breed with another partner during the following breeding season. During this cold season, they select and defend <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a> sites, thus avoiding competition for sites with other birds. Feeding on winter <a href="/wiki/Pea" title="Pea">pea</a> crops provides the female with nutrients necessary for <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">egg</a> production. From April to June, they care for their young. <a href="/wiki/Fledgling_(birds)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fledgling (birds)">Fledglings</a> are ready to leave the nest before <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aviculture">Aviculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose-ringed_parakeet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aviculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rose-ringed_Parakeet_(Psittacula_krameri)_-blue_mutation_on_perch.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Rose-ringed_Parakeet_%28Psittacula_krameri%29_-blue_mutation_on_perch.jpg/220px-Rose-ringed_Parakeet_%28Psittacula_krameri%29_-blue_mutation_on_perch.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="187" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Rose-ringed_Parakeet_%28Psittacula_krameri%29_-blue_mutation_on_perch.jpg/330px-Rose-ringed_Parakeet_%28Psittacula_krameri%29_-blue_mutation_on_perch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Rose-ringed_Parakeet_%28Psittacula_krameri%29_-blue_mutation_on_perch.jpg/440px-Rose-ringed_Parakeet_%28Psittacula_krameri%29_-blue_mutation_on_perch.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2320" data-file-height="1968" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rose-ringed_Parakeet_(Psittacula_krameri)_-blue_mutation_on_perch.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A blue <a href="/wiki/Colour_morph" class="mw-redirect" title="Colour morph">colour morph</a> mutation parakeet kept as a pet.</div></div></div>
<p>Rose-ringed parakeets are popular as <a href="/wiki/Companion_parrot" title="Companion parrot">pets</a> and they have a long history in aviculture. The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greeks</a> kept the Indian subspecies <i>P. krameri manillensis</i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Romans</a> kept the African subspecies <i>P. krameri krameri</i>. Colour mutations of the Indian rose-ringed parakeet subspecies have become widely available in recent years.<sup id="cite_ref-DA_2003_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DA_2003-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> A blue <a href="/wiki/Colour_morph" class="mw-redirect" title="Colour morph">colour morph</a> mutation of the rose-ringed parakeet is also commonly kept in aviculture. Birds that display this mutation have solid light blue feathers instead of green, and lack the rings of their normal counterparts.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mimicry">Mimicry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose-ringed_parakeet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mimicry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>
Both males and females have the ability to <a href="/wiki/Talking_bird" title="Talking bird">mimic human speech</a>. First, the bird listens to its surroundings, and then it copies the voice of the human speaker. Some people hand-raise rose-ringed parakeet chicks for this purpose. Such parrots then become quite tame and receptive to learning.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> </p><div class="mediaContainer" style="width:220px"><audio id="mwe_player_1" controls="" preload="none" style="width:220px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="12.049183673469" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="Talking_Parrot_(Psittacula_krameri).ogg" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/7/7c/Talking_Parrot_%28Psittacula_krameri%29.ogg/Talking_Parrot_%28Psittacula_krameri%29.ogg.mp3" type="audio/mpeg" data-title="MP3" data-shorttitle="MP3" data-transcodekey="mp3" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="135776" /><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7c/Talking_Parrot_%28Psittacula_krameri%29.ogg" type="audio/ogg; codecs=&quot;vorbis&quot;" data-title="Original Ogg file (219 kbps)" data-shorttitle="Ogg source" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="218916" /></audio></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feral_birds">Feral birds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose-ringed_parakeet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Feral birds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Feral_parrots" class="mw-redirect" title="Feral parrots">Feral parrots</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ringnecked_Parakeet_-_Feral_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Ringnecked_Parakeet_-_Feral_1.jpg/220px-Ringnecked_Parakeet_-_Feral_1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="342" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Ringnecked_Parakeet_-_Feral_1.jpg/330px-Ringnecked_Parakeet_-_Feral_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Ringnecked_Parakeet_-_Feral_1.jpg/440px-Ringnecked_Parakeet_-_Feral_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="579" data-file-height="900" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ringnecked_Parakeet_-_Feral_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A feral female in a garden in Bromley, London</div></div></div>
<p>A popular pet, the rose-ringed parakeet has been released in a wide range of cities around the world, giving it an environment with few predators where their preferred diet of seeds, nuts, fruits, and berries is available from suburban gardens and <a href="/wiki/Bird_feeder" title="Bird feeder">bird feeders</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_How_do_parakeets_survive_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_How_do_parakeets_survive-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Its adaptations to cold winters in the Himalayan foothills allow it to easily withstand European winter conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_How_do_parakeets_survive_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_How_do_parakeets_survive-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It has established <a href="/wiki/Feral" title="Feral">feral</a> populations in a number of European cities, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. There are also apparently stable populations in the US (<a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>) and small self-sustaining populations in <a href="/wiki/Ankara" title="Ankara">Ankara</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0zmir" title="İzmir">İzmir</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0stanbul" class="mw-redirect" title="İstanbul">İstanbul</a> (concentrated in parks), <a href="/wiki/Tunis" title="Tunis">Tunis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tripoli" title="Tripoli">Tripoli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehran</a> (concentrated in the north side of the city). It is also found throughout Lebanon, Israel, Iran, the <a href="/wiki/UAE" class="mw-redirect" title="UAE">UAE</a>, Bahrain, Qatar, and Oman. A small number of escaped birds are present in Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The European populations became established during the mid-to-late 20th century. There is a burgeoning population of <a href="/wiki/Feral_parakeets_in_Great_Britain" title="Feral parakeets in Great Britain">feral parakeets in Great Britain</a> which is centred around suburban London and the <a href="/wiki/Home_Counties" class="mw-redirect" title="Home Counties">Home Counties</a> of <a href="/wiki/South-East_England" class="mw-redirect" title="South-East England">South-East England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Parakeet numbers have been highest in the south-west of London, although the population has since spread rapidly, and large flocks of birds can be observed in places such as <a href="/wiki/Crystal_Palace_Park" title="Crystal Palace Park">Crystal Palace Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Battersea_Park" title="Battersea Park">Battersea Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buckhurst_Hill" title="Buckhurst Hill">Buckhurst Hill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richmond_Park" title="Richmond Park">Richmond Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wimbledon_Common" title="Wimbledon Common">Wimbledon Common</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Park" title="Greenwich Park">Greenwich Park</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hampstead_Heath" title="Hampstead Heath">Hampstead Heath</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a>. Feral parakeets have also been observed in <a href="/wiki/Abbey_Wood" title="Abbey Wood">Abbey Wood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bostall_Heath" class="mw-redirect" title="Bostall Heath">Bostall Heath</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bostall_Woods" class="mw-redirect" title="Bostall Woods">Bostall Woods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plumstead_Common" title="Plumstead Common">Plumstead Common</a>. The winter of 2006 had three separate roosts of about 6000 birds around London.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> A smaller population occurs around <a href="/wiki/Margate" title="Margate">Margate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Broadstairs" title="Broadstairs">Broadstairs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ramsgate" title="Ramsgate">Ramsgate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>. There is also an established population to the North East of <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> in <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a> at <a href="/wiki/Loughton" title="Loughton">Loughton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theydon_Bois" title="Theydon Bois">Theydon Bois</a> by <a href="/wiki/Epping_Forest" title="Epping Forest">Epping Forest</a>. Elsewhere in Britain, smaller feral populations have become established from time to time (e.g., at <a href="/wiki/Sefton_Park" title="Sefton Park">Sefton Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenbank_Park" title="Greenbank Park">Greenbank Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>, <a href="/wiki/Studland" title="Studland">Studland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kensington_Gardens" title="Kensington Gardens">Kensington Gardens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a>, south <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> and even as far north as <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> It has been suggested that <a href="/wiki/Feral_parrots" class="mw-redirect" title="Feral parrots">feral parrots</a> could endanger populations of native British birds, and that the rose-ringed parakeet could even be culled as a result,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> although this is not currently recommended by conservation organisations.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> A major agricultural pest in locations such as India, as of 2011 the rose-ringed parakeet population was growing rapidly, but is generally limited to urban areas in southern England<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A Europe-wide count was held in 2015 and found 85,220 Rose-ringed parakeets in 10 European countries.<sup id="cite_ref-Parau_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parau-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In the Netherlands, the feral population in the four largest urban areas (<a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rotterdam" title="Rotterdam">Rotterdam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Utrecht_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Utrecht (city)">Utrecht</a> and especially in <a href="/wiki/The_Hague" title="The Hague">The Hague</a>) was estimated at 10,000 birds in 2010, almost double the number of birds estimated in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> There also exists a feral population in Belgium, with as many as 5,000 pairs estimated in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  These originate from an original population that was set free in 1974 by the owner of the Meli Zoo and Attraction Park near the <a href="/wiki/Atomium" title="Atomium">Atomium</a> who wanted to make Brussels more colourful.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In Germany, these birds are found along the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> in all major urban areas such as <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>, <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a> (about 800 birds),<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bonn" title="Bonn">Bonn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ludwigshafen" title="Ludwigshafen">Ludwigshafen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Speyer" title="Speyer">Speyer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wiesbaden" title="Wiesbaden">Wiesbaden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mainz" title="Mainz">Mainz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Worms,_Germany" title="Worms, Germany">Worms</a>. Other populations are found around <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> — notably in the gardens of the <a href="/wiki/Palatine_Hill" title="Palatine Hill">Palatine Hill</a>, the trees of the <a href="/wiki/Trastevere" title="Trastevere">Trastevere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Janiculum" title="Janiculum">Janiculum</a> and at <a href="/wiki/Villa_Borghese_gardens" title="Villa Borghese gardens">Villa Borghese</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Orto_Botanico_di_Palermo" class="mw-redirect" title="Orto Botanico di Palermo">Orto Botanico di Palermo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Palermo" title="Palermo">Palermo</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Público_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Público-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The specimens in these naturalised populations often represent intra-specific <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrids</a>, originally between varying numbers (according to locality) of the subspecies <i>manillensis</i>, <i>borealis</i><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (August 2007)">verification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, and/or (to a lesser extent) <i>krameri</i> along with some inter-specific hybrids with naturalised <i>Psittacula eupatria</i> (<a href="/wiki/Alexandrine_parakeet" title="Alexandrine parakeet">Alexandrine parakeet</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Ancillotto2015_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ancillotto2015-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Where introduced, rose-ringed parakeets may affect native <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> and human economy and wellness.<sup id="cite_ref-MenchettiMori2014_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MenchettiMori2014-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Menchetti2016_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Menchetti2016-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United Kingdom and especially within <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, parakeets face predation by native <a href="/wiki/Birds_of_prey" class="mw-redirect" title="Birds of prey">birds of prey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owls</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">Peregrine falcon</a> (<i>Falco peregrinus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_hobby" title="Eurasian hobby">Eurasian hobby</a> (<i>F. subbuteo</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Tawny_owl" title="Tawny owl">Tawny owl</a> (<i>Strix aluco</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is a breeding population on <a href="/wiki/Madeira_Island" title="Madeira Island">Madeira Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rocha-et-al-2020_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rocha-et-al-2020-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is a feral population of the birds in Japan. In the 1960s many Japanese people became pet owners for the first time and the parakeet was widely imported as a pet. Some escaped or were released and formed populations around the country. By the 1980s groups could be found in Tokyo, Osaka, Nagoya, Niigata and Kyushu. Some groups since died out, but as of 2009 there was a large population residing at the <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Institute_of_Technology" title="Tokyo Institute of Technology">Tokyo Institute of Technology</a>'s main campus at Ookayama, along with small groups in <a href="/wiki/Maebashi" title="Maebashi">Maebashi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chiba,_Chiba" class="mw-redirect" title="Chiba, Chiba">Chiba city</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
