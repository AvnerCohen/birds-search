<p>The <b>Blue-cheeked Bee-eater</b> (<i>Merops persicus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Near_passerine" title="Near passerine">near passerine</a> bird in the <a href="/wiki/Bee-eater" title="Bee-eater">bee-eater</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a>, Meropidae. It breeds in Northern Africa, and the Middle East from eastern <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. It is generally strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in tropical <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, although some populations breed and live year round in the <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> This species occurs as a rare vagrant north of its breeding range.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue-cheeked_Bee-eater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>This species, like other bee-eaters, is a richly-coloured, slender bird. It is predominantly green; its face has blue sides with a black eye stripe, and a yellow and brown throat; the beak is black. It can reach a length of 31&#160;cm (12&#160;in), with the two elongated central tail feathers adding another 7&#160;cm (2.8&#160;in). Sexes are mostly alike but the tail-streamers of the female are shorter.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This is a bird which breeds in sub-tropical semi-desert with a few trees, such as acacia. It winters in open woodland or grassland. As the name suggests, bee-eaters predominantly eat <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">wasps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hornet" title="Hornet">hornets</a>, which are caught in the air by sorties from an open perch. However, this species probably takes more <a href="/wiki/Dragonfly" title="Dragonfly">dragonflies</a> than any other food item. Its preferred hunting perch is telephone wires if available.</p>
<p>Blue-cheeked Bee-eaters may nest solitarily or in loose <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonies</a> of up to ten birds. They may also nest in colonies with <a href="/wiki/European_Bee-eater" title="European Bee-eater">European Bee-eaters</a>. The nests are located in sandy in sandy banks, embankments, low cliffs or on the shore of the Caspian Sea. They make a relatively long tunnel of 1–3 m (3.3–9.8 ft) in length in which the four to eight (usually six or seven), spherical white eggs are laid. Both the male and the female take care of the eggs, although the female alone incubates them at night. Incubation takes 23–26 days.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The call sounds 'flatter' and less 'fluty' than the <a href="/wiki/European_Bee-eater" title="European Bee-eater">European Bee-eater</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue-cheeked_Bee-eater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span></h2>
<p>It has two subspecies</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Merops persicus persicus</i> - Breeds in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, winters in East and Southern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a></li>
<li><i>Merops persicus chrysocercus</i> - Breeds in North Africa, winters in West Africa.</li>
</ul>
<p>This species is closely related to <a href="/wiki/Blue-tailed_Bee-eater" title="Blue-tailed Bee-eater">Blue-tailed Bee-eater</a>, <i>M. philippinus</i> of East Asia, and the <a href="/wiki/Olive_Bee-eater" title="Olive Bee-eater">Olive Bee-eater</a> of Africa, and has been treated as being the same species (conspecific).<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
