<p>The <b>little crake</b> (<i>Zapornia parva</i>) is a very small waterbird of the family <a href="/wiki/Rallidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Rallidae">Rallidae</a>.  <i>parva</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "small".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Its breeding habitat is <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed beds</a> in Europe, mainly in the east, and just into western Asia. This species is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in Africa.
</p><p>At 17–19&#160;cm (6.7–7.5&#160;in) in length, they are slightly smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Spotted_crake" title="Spotted crake">spotted crake</a>, from which they are readily distinguished by the lack of dark barring and white spots on the flanks. The little crake has a short straight bill, yellow with a red base. They have green legs with long toes, and a short tail which is barred black and white underneath. Unlike other <i><a href="/wiki/Porzana" title="Porzana">Porzana</a></i> crakes, this species has strong <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a>: Adult males have mainly brown upperparts and blue-grey face and underparts. They resemble the <a href="/wiki/Sympatric" class="mw-redirect" title="Sympatric">sympatric</a> <a href="/wiki/Baillon%27s_crake" title="Baillon&#39;s crake">Baillon's crake</a> (<i>P. pusilla</i>), which has strongly barred flanks and is a little smaller. Females have buff underparts, and are grey only on the face; they are more similar to the <a href="/wiki/Yellow-breasted_crake" title="Yellow-breasted crake">yellow-breasted crake</a> (<i>P. flaviventer</i>) of the American tropics. Immature little crakes are similar to the female but have a white face and breast. The downy chicks are black, as with all <a href="/wiki/Rallidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Rallidae">rails</a>.
</p><p>These birds probe with their bill in mud or shallow water, also picking up food by sight. They mainly eat <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and aquatic animals. Little crakes are very secretive in the breeding season, and are then mostly heard rather than seen. They can be easier to see on migration. They are then noisy <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, with a yapping <i>kua</i> call. They nest in a dry location in reed vegetation, laying 4–7 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>.
</p><p>The little crake is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>
