<p>The <b>Red-headed Bunting</b> (<i>Emberiza bruniceps</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bunting_(bird)" title="Bunting (bird)">bunting</a> family Emberizidae, a group now separated by most modern authors from the <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finches</a>, Fringillidae.</p>
<p>It breeds in central <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. Its status in western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, where it is a potential vagrant, is confused by escapes, especially as this species is more commonly recorded than the closely related <a href="/wiki/Black-headed_Bunting" title="Black-headed Bunting">Black-headed Bunting</a>, despite the latter have a more westerly breeding range. Reports in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a> have declined dramatically over recent years, co-inciding with the decline in Asiatic imports for the cage-bird trade.</p>

<p>Red-headed Bunting breeds in open scrubby areas including agricultural land. It lays 3-5 eggs in a nest in a tree or bush. Its natural food consists of insects when feeding young, and otherwise seeds.</p>
<p>This bird is 17&#160;cm long, larger than <a href="/wiki/Reed_Bunting" title="Reed Bunting">Reed Bunting</a>, and long-tailed. The breeding male has bright yellow underparts, green upperparts and a brownish-red face and breast.</p>
<p>The female is a washed-out version of the male, with paler underparts, a grey-brown back and a greyish head. The juvenile is similar, and both can be difficult to separate from the corresponding plumages of Black-headed Bunting.</p>
<p>The song, given from a high perch, is a jerky <i>sweet-sweet-churri-churri-churri</i>.</p>
