<p>The <b>Sandwich Tern</b> (<i><b>Thalasseus sandvicensis</b></i>, syn. <i>Sterna sandvicensis</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Bridge2005_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bridge2005-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">tern</a> family Sternidae. It is very closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Crested_Tern" title="Lesser Crested Tern">Lesser Crested Tern</a> <i>T. bengalensis</i>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Crested_Tern" title="Chinese Crested Tern">Chinese Crested Tern</a> <i>T. bernsteini</i>, and <a href="/wiki/Elegant_Tern" title="Elegant Tern">Elegant Tern</a> <i>T. elegans</i>, and has been known to interbreed with Lesser Crested.</p>
<p>The Sandwich Tern is a medium-large tern with grey upperparts, white underparts, a yellow-tipped black bill and a shaggy black crest which becomes less extensive in winter with a white crown. Young birds bear grey and brown scalloped plumage on their backs and wings. It is a vocal bird. It nests in a ground scrape and lays one to three eggs.</p>
<p>Like all <i>Thalasseus</i> terns, the Sandwich Tern feeds by plunge diving for fish, usually in marine environments, and the offering of fish by the male to the female is part of the courtship display.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sandwich_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The terns, family Sternidae, are small to medium-sized seabirds, <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull-like</a> in appearance, but usually with a more delicate, lighter build and shorter, weaker legs. They have long, pointed wings, which gives them a fast buoyant flight, and often a deeply forked tail. Most species are grey above and white below, and have a black cap which is reduced or flecked with white in the winter.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP764_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP764-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Sandwich Tern was originally described by ornithologist <a href="/wiki/John_Latham_(ornithologist)" title="John Latham (ornithologist)">John Latham</a> in 1787 as <i>Sterna sandvicensis</i>, but was recently moved to its current genus <i><a href="/wiki/Thalasseus" title="Thalasseus">Thalasseus</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Boie" title="Heinrich Boie">Boie</a>, 1822) following <a href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria" class="mw-redirect">mitochondrial</a> <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> studies which confirmed that the three types of head pattern (white crown, black cap, and black cap with a white blaze on the forehead) found amongst the terns corresponded to distinct clades.<sup id="cite_ref-Bridge2005_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bridge2005-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The current genus name is derived from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>Thalassa</i>, "sea", and <i>sandvicensis</i> refers to <a href="/wiki/Sandwich,_Kent" title="Sandwich, Kent">Sandwich, Kent</a>, Latham's <a href="/wiki/Type_locality_(biology)" title="Type locality (biology)" class="mw-redirect">type locality</a>. In birds, the <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific name</a> <i>sandvicensis</i> usually denotes that the species was first described from <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, formerly known as the "Sandwich Islands", but the Sandwich Tern does not occur there.</p>

<p>This <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> has three subspecies:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>T. s. sandvicensis</i> (Latham, 1787) breeds on the Atlantic and <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a> coasts of Europe, and <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">winters</a> off western <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabia</a>.</li>
<li>The marginally smaller <i>T. s. acuflavida</i> (Cabot, 1847) breeds on Atlantic coasts of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, wintering in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> and further south, and has wandered to Western Europe.</li>
<li>Yellow-billed <i>T. s. eurygnatha</i> (<a href="/wiki/Howard_Saunders" title="Howard Saunders">Saunders</a> 1876) (sometimes treated as a separate species, <b>Cayenne Tern</b>, <i>T. eurygnatha</i>) breeds on the Atlantic coast of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> from <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> north to the Caribbean, intergrading with <i>T. s. acuflavida</i> in the north of its range.</li>
</ul>
<p>The DNA analysis showed that Cayenne Tern differed genetically from <i>T. s. acuflavida</i>, but the difference not sufficient to confirm it as a definite separate species.<sup id="cite_ref-Bridge2005_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bridge2005-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sandwich_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cabot%27s_Tern_(Thalasseus_acuflavidus),_Venice,_Florida_-_20110311.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Cabot%27s_Tern_%28Thalasseus_acuflavidus%29%2C_Venice%2C_Florida_-_20110311.jpg/220px-Cabot%27s_Tern_%28Thalasseus_acuflavidus%29%2C_Venice%2C_Florida_-_20110311.jpg" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cabot%27s_Tern_(Thalasseus_acuflavidus),_Venice,_Florida_-_20110311.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>T. s. acuflavida</i> in non-breeding plumage, Venice beach, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This is a medium-large tern, 37–43 cm (15–17 in) long with an 85–97 cm (33–38 in) wingspan, which is unlikely to be confused within most of its range, although the South American race could be confused with the Elegant Tern.</p>
<p>The Sandwich Tern's thin sharp bill is black with a yellow tip, except in the yellow or orange billed South American race. Its short legs are black. Its upperwings are pale grey and its underparts white, and this tern looks very pale in flight, although the primary flight feathers darken during the summer.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Lesser Crested Tern and Elegant Tern differ in having all-orange bills; Lesser Crested also differs in having a grey rump and marginally stouter bill, and Elegant in having a slightly longer, slenderer bill. Chinese Crested Tern is the most similar to Sandwich, but has a reversal of the bill colour, yellow with a black tip; it does not overlap in range with Sandwich Tern so confusion is unlikely.</p>
<p>In winter, the adult Sandwich Tern's forehead becomes white. Juvenile Sandwich Terns have dark tips to their tails, and a scaly appearance on their back and wings, like juvenile <a href="/wiki/Roseate_Tern" title="Roseate Tern">Roseate Terns</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Sandwich Tern is a vocal bird; its call is a characteristic loud grating <i>kear-ik</i> or <i>kerr ink</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sandwich_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<p>This species breeds in very dense colonies on coasts and islands, and exceptionally inland on suitable large freshwater lakes close to the coast. It nests in a ground scrape and lays one to three eggs. Unlike some of the smaller white terns, it is not very aggressive toward potential predators, relying on the sheer density of the nests—often only 20–30 cm (7.9–12 in) apart and nesting close to other more aggressive species such as <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Tern" title="Arctic Tern">Arctic Terns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black-headed_Gull" title="Black-headed Gull">Black-headed Gulls</a> to avoid predation.</p>
<p>Like all <i>Thalasseus</i> terns, the Sandwich Tern feeds by plunge-diving for fish, almost invariably from the sea. It usually dives directly, and not from the "stepped-hover" favoured by <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Tern" title="Arctic Tern">Arctic Tern</a>. The offering of fish by the male to the female is part of the courtship display.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sandwich_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h2>
<p>The Sandwich Tern has an extensive global range estimated at 100,000–1,000,000&#160;million square kilometres km². (0.04–0.38&#160;million square miles). It has a population estimated at 460,000–500,000 individuals. Population trends have not been quantified, but the species is not believed to approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the IUCN Red List (i.e., declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations). For these reasons, the species is evaluated as Least Concern.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Sandwich Tern is among the taxa to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.<sup id="cite_ref-AEWA_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AEWA-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Parties to the Agreement are required to engage in a wide range of conservation actions which are describes in a detailed action plan. This plan should address key issues such as species and habitat conservation, management of human activities, research, education, and implementation.<sup id="cite_ref-AEWAintro_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AEWAintro-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
