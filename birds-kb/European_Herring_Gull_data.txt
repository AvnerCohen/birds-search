<p>The <b>European herring gull</b> (<i>Larus argentatus</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a>, up to 66&#160;cm (26&#160;in) long. One of the best-known of all gulls along the shores of <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a>, it was once abundant.<sup id="cite_ref-Gilliard1958_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gilliard1958-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It breeds across <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern Europe</a>, Western Europe, <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_states" title="Baltic states">Baltic states</a>. Some European herring gulls, especially those resident in colder areas, <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> further south in winter, but many are permanent residents, e.g. in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, or on the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> shores. They have a varied diet, including fish, crustaceans, and dead animals, as well as some plants.
</p><p>While herring gull numbers appear to have been harmed in recent years, possibly by fish population declines and competition, they have proved able to survive in human-adapted areas and can often be seen in towns acting as scavengers.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_herring_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Their scientific name is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>. <i>Larus</i> appears to have referred to a gull or other large seabird and <i>argentatus</i> means decorated with silver.<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The taxonomy of the herring gull/<a href="/wiki/Lesser_black-backed_gull" title="Lesser black-backed gull">lesser black-backed gull</a> complex is very complicated, with different authorities recognising between two and eight species.
</p><p>This group has a <a href="/wiki/Ring_species" title="Ring species">ring distribution</a> around the Northern Hemisphere. Differences between adjacent forms in this ring are fairly small, but by the time the circuit is completed, the end members, herring gull and lesser black-backed gull, are clearly different species. The terminal forms do not interbreed, though they coexist in the same localities.<sup id="cite_ref-Mayr1964_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mayr1964-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Association_of_European_Rarities_Committees" title="Association of European Rarities Committees">Association of European Rarities Committees</a> recognises six species:
</p>
<ul><li>European herring gull, <i>L. argentatus</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/American_herring_gull" title="American herring gull">American herring gull</a>, <i>L. smithsonianus</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Caspian_gull" title="Caspian gull">Caspian gull</a>, <i>L. cachinnans</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yellow-legged_gull" title="Yellow-legged gull">Yellow-legged gull</a>, <i>L. michahellis</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vega_gull" title="Vega gull">Vega gull</a>, <i>L. vegae</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Armenian_gull" title="Armenian gull">Armenian gull</a>, <i>L. armenicus</i></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_herring_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Herring_Gulls,_Coumeenoole_Bay,_Kerry,_Ireland.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Herring_Gulls%2C_Coumeenoole_Bay%2C_Kerry%2C_Ireland.jpg/220px-Herring_Gulls%2C_Coumeenoole_Bay%2C_Kerry%2C_Ireland.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Herring_Gulls%2C_Coumeenoole_Bay%2C_Kerry%2C_Ireland.jpg/330px-Herring_Gulls%2C_Coumeenoole_Bay%2C_Kerry%2C_Ireland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Herring_Gulls%2C_Coumeenoole_Bay%2C_Kerry%2C_Ireland.jpg/440px-Herring_Gulls%2C_Coumeenoole_Bay%2C_Kerry%2C_Ireland.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Herring_Gulls,_Coumeenoole_Bay,_Kerry,_Ireland.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two <i>L. a. argenteus</i> individuals on the shore of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coumeenoole_Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Coumeenoole Bay (page does not exist)">Coumeenoole Bay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a></div></div></div>
<ul><li><i>L. a. argentatus</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/Erik_Pontoppidan" title="Erik Pontoppidan">Pontoppidan</a>, 1763</small>, the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies#Nominotypical_subspecies_and_subspecies_autonyms" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate</a> form, sometimes known as the <b>Scandinavian herring gull</b>, breeds in Scandinavia and northwestern Russia. Northern and eastern populations migrate southwest in winter. It is a large, bulky gull with extensive white in the wingtips.</li>
<li><i>L. a. argenteus</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/Christian_Ludwig_Brehm" title="Christian Ludwig Brehm">Brehm</a> &amp; Schilling, 1822</small>, sometimes known as the <b>Western European herring gull</b> breeds in Western Europe in Iceland, the Faroes, Britain, Ireland, France, Belgium, the Netherlands and Germany. Many birds are resident while others make short-distance migratory journeys. It is smaller than <i>L. a. argentatus</i> with more black and less white in the wingtips and paler upper parts.</li></ul>

<p>These <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxa</a> are classified as <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of <i>Larus argentatus</i> by some authorities such as the <a href="/wiki/American_Ornithologists%27_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="American Ornithologists&#39; Union">American Ornithologists' Union</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Handbook_of_the_Birds_of_the_World" title="Handbook of the Birds of the World">Handbook of the Birds of the World</a></i>. Others such as the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_European_Rarities_Committees" title="Association of European Rarities Committees">Association of European Rarities Committees</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Ornithologists%27_Union" title="British Ornithologists&#39; Union">British Ornithologists' Union</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/International_Ornithologists%27_Union" title="International Ornithologists&#39; Union">International Ornithological Union</a> now regard them as one or two separate species.<sup id="cite_ref-AERC2003_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AERC2003-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sangster2007_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sangster2007-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>L. (a.) smithsonianus</i>, the <a href="/wiki/American_herring_gull" title="American herring gull">American herring gull</a>, breeds in Alaska, Canada, and the <a href="/wiki/Northeast_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Northeast United States">Northeast United States</a>. Many birds migrate southwards in winter, reaching as far as Central America and the West Indies. Immature birds tend to be darker and more uniformly brown than European herring gulls and have a dark tail.</li>
<li><i>L. (a.) vegae</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Vega_gull" title="Vega gull">Vega gull</a>, breeds in northeastern Siberia. It winters in Japan, Korea, eastern China, and Taiwan.</li></ul>
<p>Several other gulls have been included in this species in the past, but are now normally considered separate, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Yellow-legged_gull" title="Yellow-legged gull">yellow-legged gull</a> (<i>L. michahellis</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_gull" title="Caspian gull">Caspian gull</a> (<i>L. cachinnans</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_gull" title="Armenian gull">Armenian gull</a> (<i>L. armenicus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Heuglin%27s_gull" title="Heuglin&#39;s gull">Heuglin's gull</a> (<i>L. heuglini</i>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_herring_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><div class="mediaContainer" style="width:220px"><audio id="mwe_player_3" controls="" preload="none" style="width:220px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="182.81902494331" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="Herring_Gull_(Larus_argentatus)_(W1CDR0001420_BD12).ogg" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/f/ff/Herring_Gull_%28Larus_argentatus%29_%28W1CDR0001420_BD12%29.ogg/Herring_Gull_%28Larus_argentatus%29_%28W1CDR0001420_BD12%29.ogg.mp3" type="audio/mpeg" data-title="MP3" data-shorttitle="MP3" data-transcodekey="mp3" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="116384" /><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/ff/Herring_Gull_%28Larus_argentatus%29_%28W1CDR0001420_BD12%29.ogg" type="audio/ogg; codecs=&quot;vorbis&quot;" data-title="Original Ogg file (185 kbps)" data-shorttitle="Ogg source" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="185162" /></audio></div>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Herring_Gull_(Larus_argentatus)_(W1CDR0001420_BD12).ogg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bird Sound</div></div></div>
<p>The male European herring gull is 60–67&#160;cm (24–26&#160;in) long and weighs 1,050–1,525&#160;g (2.315–3.362&#160;lb), while the female is 55–62&#160;cm (22–24&#160;in) and weighs 710–1,100&#160;g (1.57–2.43&#160;lb). The wingspan can range from 125 to 155&#160;cm (49 to 61&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Pierotti2_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pierotti2-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CRC_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Among standard measurements, the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> is 38 to 48&#160;cm (15 to 19&#160;in), the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> is 4.4 to 6.5&#160;cm (1.7 to 2.6&#160;in) and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> is 5.3 to 7.5&#160;cm (2.1 to 3.0&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Adults in breeding plumage have a grey back and upper wings and white head and underparts. The wingtips are black with white spots known as "mirrors". The bill is yellow with a red spot and a ring of bare yellow skin is seen around the pale eye. The legs are normally pink at all ages, but can be yellowish, particularly in the Baltic population, which was formerly regarded as a separate subspecies "<i>L. a. omissus</i>". Non-breeding adults have brown streaks on their heads and necks. Male and female plumage are identical at all stages of development, but adult males are often larger.<sup id="cite_ref-AAB_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAB-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Juvenile_(organism)" title="Juvenile (organism)">Juvenile</a> and first-winter birds are mainly brown with darker streaks and have a dark bill and eyes. Second-winter birds have a whiter head and underparts with less streaking and the back is grey. Third-winter individuals are similar to adults, but retain some of the features of immature birds such as brown feathers in the wings and dark markings on the bill. The European herring gull attains adult plumage and reaches <a href="/wiki/Sexual_maturity" title="Sexual maturity">sexual maturity</a> at an average age of four years.<sup id="cite_ref-animaldiversityweb_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-animaldiversityweb-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yellow-legged_variety">Yellow-legged variety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_herring_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Yellow-legged variety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:LarusArgentatus_YellowLegs2.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/LarusArgentatus_YellowLegs2.JPG/220px-LarusArgentatus_YellowLegs2.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="106" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/LarusArgentatus_YellowLegs2.JPG/330px-LarusArgentatus_YellowLegs2.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/LarusArgentatus_YellowLegs2.JPG/440px-LarusArgentatus_YellowLegs2.JPG 2x" data-file-width="989" data-file-height="478" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:LarusArgentatus_YellowLegs2.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult <i>L. argentatus</i> with yellow legs to the right, its offspring has the normal pink colour. This bird is <i>not</i> to be confused with the always yellow-legged <i><a href="/wiki/Larus_michahellis" class="mw-redirect" title="Larus michahellis">Larus michahellis</a></i>. Photo from Warnemünde (harbour of <a href="/wiki/Rostock" title="Rostock">Rostock</a>), <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg-Vorpommern" title="Mecklenburg-Vorpommern">Mecklenburg-Vorpommern</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>At least in the south-west part of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> and surrounding areas, the European herring gull (<i>L. argentatus</i>) actually can be seen with yellow legs. They are not considered as a subspecies, since they regularly breed with grey/flesh-coloured legged herring gulls. The offspring may get yellow or normal-coloured legs. They must not be confused with the in general yellow-legged gull (<i>L. michahellis</i>), which are more common in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean area</a>, but single birds may reach more northern seas.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_species">Similar species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_herring_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Similar species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adult European herring gulls are similar to <a href="/wiki/Ring-billed_gull" title="Ring-billed gull">ring-billed gulls</a>, but are much larger, have pinkish legs, and a much thicker yellow bill with more pronounced <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gonys" class="extiw" title="wikt:gonys">gonys</a>. First-winter European herring gulls are much browner, but second- and third-winter birds can be confusing since soft part colours are variable and third-year herring gull often show a ring around the bill. Such birds are most easily distinguished by the larger size and larger bill of European herring gull.
</p><p>The European herring gull can be differentiated from the closely related, slightly smaller <a href="/wiki/Lesser_black-backed_gull" title="Lesser black-backed gull">lesser black-backed gull</a> by the latter's dark grey (not actually black) back and upper wing plumage and its yellow legs and feet.
</p><p>The smaller <a href="/wiki/Silver_gull" title="Silver gull">silver gull</a> is largely confined to Australia.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_herring_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Their loud, laughing call is well known in the Northern Hemisphere, and is often seen as a symbol of the <a href="/wiki/Seaside_resort" title="Seaside resort">seaside</a> in countries such as the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. The European herring gull also has a yelping alarm call and a low, barking anxiety call. The most distinct and best known call produced by European herring gulls – which is shared with their American relative – is the raucous territorial 'long call', used to signal boundaries to other birds; it is performed by the gull initially with its head bowed, then raised as the call continues.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>European herring gull chicks and fledglings emit a distinctive, repetitive, high-pitched 'peep', accompanied by a head-flicking gesture when <a href="/wiki/Begging_behavior_in_animals" class="mw-redirect" title="Begging behavior in animals">begging for food</a> from or calling to their parents. Adult gulls in urban areas also exhibit this behaviour when fed by humans.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_herring_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DefecatingSeagull.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/DefecatingSeagull.jpg/220px-DefecatingSeagull.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/DefecatingSeagull.jpg/330px-DefecatingSeagull.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/DefecatingSeagull.jpg/440px-DefecatingSeagull.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3257" data-file-height="2092" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DefecatingSeagull.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Herring gull expelling waste near <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele-de-Br%C3%A9hat" title="Île-de-Bréhat">Île-de-Bréhat</a></div></div></div>
<p>European herring gull flocks have a loose <a href="/wiki/Pecking_order" title="Pecking order">pecking order</a>, based on size, aggressiveness, and physical strength. Adult males are usually dominant over females and juveniles in feeding and boundary disputes, while adult females are typically dominant when selecting their nesting sites.<sup id="cite_ref-animaldiversityweb_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-animaldiversityweb-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Communication between these birds is complex and highly developed&#160;—employing both calls and body language. The warning sounds to chicks are the most obvious to interpret.
</p><p>The warning to their chicks sounds almost like a bark from a small dog. If the danger closes in, the bark is repeated, and when very close, the warning is three quick barks. If a chick is "grounded", the bird makes itself appear bigger to intimidate the threat. If other adult birds are present, they will help in the same way. For instance, a person with a dog (or someone who chases the chick) may be attacked by many adult birds, even if just one chick is in danger.
</p><p>The warning sound from a flying bird to a flock of fully fledged birds sounds very different. All kinds of gulls seemingly understand the "general alert warning sound" of all other gulls. Little doubt remains that the gull's screaming is a language for communication. It is limited to the present tense, but includes rather complex matters such as "follow me".
</p><p>Two identical vocalizations can have very different (sometimes opposite) meanings. For example, it depends on the position of the head, body, wings, and tail relative to each other and the ground.
</p><p>Unlike many flocking birds, European herring gulls do not engage in <a href="/wiki/Social_grooming" title="Social grooming">social grooming</a> and keep physical contact between individuals to a minimum. Outside the male/female and parent/chick relationships, each gull attempts to maintain a respectful 'safe distance' from others of its kind. However, the bird must be considered a social bird that dislikes being alone, and fights mainly occur over food or to protect their eggs and chicks. If a few birds discover a piece of food, the first one to land by the food piece often unfolds its wings (together with a sound) to proclaim "this food is mine". This is very often opposed by another gull, and during a short fight, a third bird may grab the food while the two other are arguing. However, if much food is found, especially at a "dangerous location" (such as in the backyard of a tall building), the bird that discovered the food will call to other gulls close by (of any species). The first bird may dare to land, but waits before eating; the others then feel safe to land, and they eat. If a large feast is found at a safer location, the gull that discovers it calls to other gulls, but starts eating immediately. The conclusion is that if more food is available than one bird can eat, it shares the food with other gulls.
</p><p>During the winter, large flocks can be seen at (snow-free) fields (agricultural or grass), especially if the ground has a high degree of moisture. At first sight, it the birds appear to be just standing there, but then on closer inspection, only their bodies are not moving; the birds actually are trampling the soil, most likely to trick worms to crawl closer to the surface of the soil.
</p><p>During early spring and late autumn, many herring gulls feed heavily on earthworms, but they are very opportunistic birds that seem to have many sources of food. For instance in southern Scandinavia and northern Germany, this species recently has become the most common of all gulls, and the increase has mostly occurred in urban or suburban environments.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_black-backed_gull" title="Great black-backed gull">great black-backed gull</a> (<i>L. marinus</i>) was around 1900 as common as the herring gull in the mentioned parts, but has not increased as much (if at all), though some signs indicate the bigger gull has learned (adopted) some of the herring gull's behaviour within urban environments. Where the herring gull is breeding in coastal urban environments, the great black-backed gull seems to do the same, but in a lesser scale.
</p><p>Herring gulls are good at producing all three eggs into flying birds. This means that at least one (often two) of the newly flying chicks loses both its parents within days after first flight. Some of these can later be seen in flocks of smaller gulls like the <a href="/wiki/Black-headed_gull" title="Black-headed gull">black-headed gull</a> (<i>Chroicocephalus ridibundus</i>) or the <a href="/wiki/Common_gull" title="Common gull">common gull</a> (<i>L. canus</i>). They are probably not welcomed in such flocks, but follow them for some months, anyway, and do thereby learn where to find food. Lonely juvenile herring gulls born in urban environments can also be seen staying for a some weeks close to outdoor restaurants and similar facilities, squeaking and begging for food from humans. By November or December, most juveniles have found other "flockmates", usually in areas near water.
</p><p>The herring gull does not need swimming, but seems to enjoy all kind of waters, especially on hot summer days. It can only catch slow creatures, like small crabs, which it often drops from some altitude to crack them open. The bird has little real power in its jaws while biting, but it may "stab" with better strength. Fish on land, eggs of other birds, and helpless chicks of smaller ducks (and similar birds where the female is the only caretaker of up to 9 eggs and chicks) are about as much predator the bird gets. It is then far more successful as a scavenger. Like vultures, an adult bird can dig its whole head and neck into a dead rabbit, for instance. Although not always appreciated by mankind due to their droppings and screaming, the herring gull must be regarded as a "natural cleaner", and just as with crows, they help by keeping rats away from the surface in urban environment, not by killing rats, but by eating the potential rat food before the rats get the chance. Unlike real scavengers, herring gulls also eat most other things than meat, like wasted food of all kinds, from bread to human vomit. They seldom eat fresh fruit, but windfalls and rotten fruit seem more desired.
</p><p>In cities, herring gulls have been witnessed attacking and killing <a href="/wiki/Feral_pigeon" title="Feral pigeon">feral pigeons</a>.
</p><p>The European herring gull has long been believed to have extremely keen vision in daylight and night vision equal or superior to that of humans;<sup id="cite_ref-strong_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-strong-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> however, this species is also capable of seeing <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> light.<sup id="cite_ref-ultraviolet_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ultraviolet-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This gull also appears to have excellent hearing and a sense of taste that is particularly responsive to salt and acidity.<sup id="cite_ref-strong_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-strong-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Parasite" class="mw-redirect" title="Parasite">Parasites</a> of European herring gulls include the <a href="/wiki/Trematode" class="mw-redirect" title="Trematode">fluke</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Microphallus_piriformes" title="Microphallus piriformes">Microphallus piriformes</a></i>.
</p>

<p>These are omnivores and opportunists like most <i>Larus</i> gulls, and scavenge from garbage dumps, landfill sites, and sewage outflows, with <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">refuse</a> comprising up to half of the bird's diet. It also steals the eggs and young of other birds (including those of other gulls), as well as seeking suitable small prey in fields, on the coast or in urban areas, or robbing <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">plovers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lapwing" title="Lapwing">lapwings</a> of their catches. European herring gulls may also dive from the surface of the water or engage in <a href="/wiki/Seabird#Plunge_diving" title="Seabird">plunge diving</a> in the pursuit of aquatic prey, though they are typically unable to reach depths greater than 1–2&#160;m (3.3–6.6&#160;ft) due to their natural buoyancy.<sup id="cite_ref-pierotti_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pierotti-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Despite their name, they have no special preference for <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herrings</a>&#160;— in fact, examinations have shown that <a href="/wiki/Echinoderm" title="Echinoderm">echinoderms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> comprised a greater portion of these gulls' stomach contents than fish, although fish is the principal element of regurgitations for nestlings.<sup id="cite_ref-cornellfoodhabits_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cornellfoodhabits-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> European herring gulls can frequently be seen to drop shelled prey from a height to break the shell. In addition, the European herring gull has been observed using pieces of bread as <a href="/wiki/Fishing_bait" title="Fishing bait">bait</a> with which to catch <a href="/wiki/Goldfish" title="Goldfish">goldfish</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-henry_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-henry-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Vegetable matter, such as roots, <a href="/wiki/Tuber" title="Tuber">tubers</a>, seeds, grains, nuts, and fruit, is also taken to an extent.<sup id="cite_ref-animaldiversityweb_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-animaldiversityweb-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Captive European herring gulls typically show aversion to spoiled meat or heavily salted food, unless they are very hungry. The gulls may also rinse food items in water in an attempt to clean them or render them more palatable before swallowing.<sup id="cite_ref-strong_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-strong-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>European herring gulls may be observed rhythmically drumming their feet upon the ground for prolonged periods of time in a behaviour that superficially resembles <a href="/wiki/Irish_stepdance" title="Irish stepdance">Irish stepdancing</a>, for the purpose of creating vibrations in the soil, driving <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a> to the surface, which are then consumed by the gull.<sup id="cite_ref-Tinbergen_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tinbergen-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> These vibrations are thought to mimic those of digging <a href="/wiki/Mole_(animal)" title="Mole (animal)">moles</a>, eliciting a surface-escape behaviour from the earthworm, beneficial in encounters with this particular predator, which the European herring gull then exploits to its own benefit in a similar manner to human <a href="/wiki/Worm_charming" title="Worm charming">worm charmers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Catania_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Catania-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Whilst the European herring gull is fully capable (unlike humans) of <a href="/wiki/Drinking_seawater" class="mw-redirect" title="Drinking seawater">consuming seawater</a>, using specialized glands located above the eyes to remove excess salt from the body (which is then excreted in solution through the nostrils and drips from the end of the bill), it drinks fresh water in preference, if available.<sup id="cite_ref-animaldiversityweb_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-animaldiversityweb-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cornellfoodhabits_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cornellfoodhabits-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Courtship_and_reproduction">Courtship and reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_herring_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Courtship and reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Larus_argentatus_MWNH_0351.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Larus_argentatus_MWNH_0351.JPG/220px-Larus_argentatus_MWNH_0351.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="126" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Larus_argentatus_MWNH_0351.JPG/330px-Larus_argentatus_MWNH_0351.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Larus_argentatus_MWNH_0351.JPG/440px-Larus_argentatus_MWNH_0351.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4240" data-file-height="2420" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Larus_argentatus_MWNH_0351.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a></div></div></div>
<p>When forming a pair bond, the hen approaches the cock on his territory with a hunched, submissive posture, while making begging calls (similar to those emitted by young gulls). If the cock chooses not to attack her and drive her away, he responds by assuming an upright posture and making a mewing call. This is followed by a period of synchronised head-tossing movements, after which the cock then <a href="/wiki/Regurgitation_(digestion)" title="Regurgitation (digestion)">regurgitates</a> some food for his prospective mate. If this is accepted, <a href="/wiki/Copulation_(zoology)" title="Copulation (zoology)">copulation</a> follows. A nesting site is then chosen by both birds which is returned to in successive years.<sup id="cite_ref-animaldiversityweb_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-animaldiversityweb-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> European herring gulls are almost exclusively sexually <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a> and may pair up for life, provided the couple is successful in hatching their eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-pierotti_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pierotti-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Two to four eggs, usually three, are laid on the ground or cliff ledges in colonies, and are defended vigorously by this large gull. The eggs are usually olive-brown in colour with dark speckles or blotches. They are <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" class="mw-redirect" title="Avian incubation">incubated</a> by both parents for 28–30 days. The chicks hatch with their eyes open, covered with fluffy down, and they are able to walk around within hours. Breeding colonies are preyed upon by <a href="/wiki/Great_black-backed_gull" title="Great black-backed gull">great black-backed gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harrier_(bird)" title="Harrier (bird)">harriers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corvid" class="mw-redirect" title="Corvid">corvids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">herons</a>.
</p><p>Juveniles use their beaks to peck at the red spot on the beaks of adults to indicate hunger. Parents then typically disgorge food for their offspring.<sup id="cite_ref-audubon_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-audubon-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The young birds are able to fly 35–40 days after hatching and fledge at five or six weeks of age. Chicks are generally fed by their parents until they are 11–12 weeks old, but the feeding may continue for more than six months of age if the young gulls continue to beg. The male feeds the chick more often than the female before fledging, with the female more often feeding after fledging.<sup id="cite_ref-animaldiversityweb_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-animaldiversityweb-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Like most gulls, European herring gulls are long-lived, with a maximum age of 49 years recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-AnAge_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AnAge-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Raptors (especially <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">peregrine falcons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gyrfalcon" title="Gyrfalcon">gyrfalcons</a>) and seals (especially <a href="/wiki/Grey_seal" title="Grey seal">grey seals</a>) occasionally prey on the non-nesting adults.<sup id="cite_ref-pierotti_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pierotti-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In the UK, the species, when taken as a whole, is declining significantly across the country, despite an increase in urban areas. The UK European herring gull population has decreased by 50% in 25 years<sup id="cite_ref-naturalengland_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-naturalengland-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and it is protected by law: since January 2010, <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a> has allowed lethal control only with a specific individual licence that is available only in limited circumstances.<sup id="cite_ref-natenglandgulladvice_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-natenglandgulladvice-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Natural England made the change following a public consultation<sup id="cite_ref-natenglandconsultation_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-natenglandconsultation-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> in response to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Protection_of_Birds" title="Royal Society for the Protection of Birds">Royal Society for the Protection of Birds</a> in 2009 placing the species on its 'Red List' of threatened bird species, affording it the highest possible conservation status.<sup id="cite_ref-rspb-redlist_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb-redlist-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> (Previously, killing the species was allowed under a general licence obtainable by authorised persons (e.g. landowners or occupiers) under certain circumstances (e.g. to prevent serious damage to crops or livestock, to prevent disease, or to preserve public health or safety) without requiring additional permission beyond the general licence.<sup id="cite_ref-naturalengland_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-naturalengland-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>)
</p><p>The European herring gull is an increasingly common roof-nesting bird in urban areas of the UK, and many individual birds show little fear of humans. The <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_1956" title="Clean Air Act 1956">Clean Air Act 1956</a> forbade the burning of refuse at <a href="/wiki/Landfill_site" class="mw-redirect" title="Landfill site">landfill sites</a>, providing the European herring gull with a regular and plentiful source of food. As a direct result, European herring gull populations in Britain skyrocketed. Faced with a lack of space at their traditional colonies, the gulls ventured inland in search of new breeding grounds. Dwindling fish stocks in the seas around Britain may also have been a significant factor in the gulls' move inland.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The gulls are found all year round in the streets and gardens of Britain, due to the presence of <a href="/wiki/Street_lighting" class="mw-redirect" title="Street lighting">street lighting</a> (which allows the gulls to forage at night), discarded food in streets, <a href="/wiki/Food_waste" title="Food waste">food waste</a> contained in easy-to-tear plastic <a href="/wiki/Bin_bag" title="Bin bag">bin bags</a>, food <a href="/wiki/Bird_feeding" title="Bird feeding">intentionally left out</a> for other birds (or the gulls themselves), the relative lack of predators, and readily available, convenient, warm and undisturbed rooftop nesting space in towns and cities. Particularly large urban gull colonies (composed primarily of European herring gulls and lesser black-backed gulls) are now present in <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gloucester" title="Gloucester">Gloucester</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rock_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rock-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> to name but a few.
</p><p>The survival rate for urban gulls is much higher than their counterparts in coastal areas, with an annual adult mortality rate less than 5%. Also, each European herring gull pair commonly rears three chicks per year. This, when combined with their long-lived nature, has resulted in a massive increase in numbers over a relatively short period of time and has brought urban-dwelling members of the species into conflict with humans.<sup id="cite_ref-rock_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rock-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Once familiar with humans, urban European herring gulls show little hesitation in swooping down to steal food from the hands of humans. During the breeding season, the gulls also aggressively 'dive bomb' and attempt to strike with claws and wings (sometimes spraying faeces or vomit at the same time) at humans that they perceive to be a threat to their eggs and chicks&#160;—often innocent passers-by or residents of the buildings on which they have constructed their nests. Large amounts of gull excrement deposited on property and the noise from courting pairs and begging chicks in the summer is also considered to be a nuisance by humans living alongside the European herring gull.<sup id="cite_ref-rock_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rock-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Nonlethal attempts to deter the gulls from nesting in urban areas have been largely unsuccessful. The European herring gull is intelligent and will completely ignore most '<a href="/wiki/Bird_scarer" title="Bird scarer">bird-scaring</a>' technology after determining that it poses no threat. Rooftop spikes, tensioned wires, netting, and similar are also generally ineffective against this species, as it has large, wide feet with thick, leathery skin, which affords the seagull excellent weight distribution and protection from sharp objects (the bird may simply balance itself on top of these obstacles with little apparent concern). If nests are removed and eggs are taken, broken, or oiled, the gulls simply rebuild and/or relay, or choose another nest site in the same area and start again.<sup id="cite_ref-rock_31-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rock-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Man-made models of birds of prey placed on top of buildings are generally ignored by the gulls once they realise they are not real, and attempts to scare the gulls away using <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptors</a> are similarly ineffective. Although they are intimidated by birds of prey, European herring gulls, in addition to being social birds with strength in numbers, are large, powerful, and aggressive as individuals and are more than capable of fighting back against the potential predator, particularly if they consider their chicks to be at risk; in fact, the gulls may actually pose a greater threat to a raptor than vice versa.<sup id="cite_ref-picas1_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-picas1-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> European herring gulls are also naturally accustomed to predators (such as <a href="/wiki/Skua" title="Skua">skuas</a> and great black-backed gulls) living in the vicinity of their nest sites in the wild and are not particularly discouraged from breeding by their presence.<sup id="cite_ref-rock_31-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rock-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
