<p>The <b>Arctic Tern</b> (<i>Sterna paradisaea</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">tern</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Sternidae" title="Sternidae" class="mw-redirect">Sternidae</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> has a <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/circumpolar" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:circumpolar">circumpolar</a> breeding distribution covering the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> and sub-Arctic regions of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> (as far south as <a href="/wiki/Brittany_(administrative_region)" title="Brittany (administrative region)">Brittany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, seeing two summers each year as it migrates from its northern breeding grounds along a winding route to the oceans around <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> and back, a round trip of about 70,900&#160;km (c. 44,300 miles) each year.<sup id="cite_ref-Arctic_terns.27_flying_feat_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arctic_terns.27_flying_feat-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> This is by far the longest regular migration by any known animal. The Arctic Tern flies as well as glides through the air, performing almost all of its tasks in the air. It nests once every one to three years (depending on its mating cycle); once it has finished nesting it takes to the sky for another long southern migration.</p>
<p>Arctic Terns are medium-sized birds. They have a length of 33–39&#160;cm (13–15&#160;in) and a <a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">wingspan</a> of 76–85&#160;cm (26–30&#160;in). They are mainly grey and white plumaged, with a red beak (as long as the head, straight, with pronounced <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gonys" class="extiw" title="wikt:gonys">gonys</a>) and feet, white forehead, a black nape and crown (streaked white), and white cheeks. The grey mantle is 305&#160;mm, and the scapulae are fringed brown, some tipped white. The upper wing is grey with a white leading edge, and the collar is completely white, as is the rump. The deeply forked tail is whitish, with grey outer webs. The hindcrown to the ear-coverts is black.</p>
<p>Arctic Terns are long-lived birds, with many reaching thirty years of age. They eat mainly fish and small marine <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>. The species is abundant, with an estimated one million individuals. While the trend in the number of individuals in the species as a whole is not known, exploitation in the past has reduced this bird's numbers in the southern reaches of its range.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution and migration">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_migration">Distribution and migration</span></h2>
<p>The Arctic Tern has a worldwide, circumpolar breeding distribution which is continuous; there are no recognized <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>. It can be found in coastal regions in cooler temperate parts of North America and <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere" class="mw-redirect">northern</a> summer. While wintering during the <a href="/wiki/Southern_hemisphere" title="Southern hemisphere" class="mw-redirect">southern</a> summer, it can be found at sea, reaching the southern edge of the Antarctic ice.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Arctic Tern is famous for its migration; it flies from its Arctic breeding grounds to the Antarctic and back again each year. This 19,000&#160;km (12,000&#160;mi) journey each way ensures that this bird sees two summers per year and more daylight than any other creature on the planet.<sup id="cite_ref-Cramp_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cramp-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> One example of this bird's remarkable long-distance flying abilities involves an Arctic Tern <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">ringed</a> as an unfledged chick on the <a href="/wiki/Farne_Islands" title="Farne Islands">Farne Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>, <a href="/wiki/UK" title="UK" class="mw-redirect">UK</a>, in the northern summer of 1982, which reached <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, in October 1982, a sea journey of over 22,000&#160;km (14,000&#160;mi) in just three months from fledging.<sup id="cite_ref-Heavisides_et_al._6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heavisides_et_al.-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Another example is that of a chick ringed in <a href="/wiki/Labrador" title="Labrador">Labrador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, on 23 July 1928. It was found in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> four months later.<sup id="cite_ref-Birds_of_Nova_Scotia_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birds_of_Nova_Scotia-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Research using tracking devices attached to the birds was published in January 2010 and showed that the above examples are in fact not unusual for the species; eleven Arctic Terns that bred in Greenland or Iceland each covered 70,900&#160;km on average in a year, with a maximum of 81,600&#160;km. The difference from previous estimates was because the birds were found to take a meandering course to take advantage of prevailing winds.<sup id="cite_ref-Arctic_terns.27_flying_feat_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arctic_terns.27_flying_feat-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The average Arctic Tern lives about twenty years, and will travel about 2.4 million km (1.5 million mi).</p>
<p>Arctic Terns usually migrate far offshore.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Consequently, they are rarely seen from land outside the breeding season.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description and taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_taxonomy">Description and taxonomy</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Arctic_Tern_2006_06_08.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Arctic_Tern_2006_06_08.JPG/220px-Arctic_Tern_2006_06_08.JPG" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Arctic_Tern_2006_06_08.JPG/330px-Arctic_Tern_2006_06_08.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Arctic_Tern_2006_06_08.JPG/440px-Arctic_Tern_2006_06_08.JPG 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Arctic_Tern_2006_06_08.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf3/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
An Arctic Tern in <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Arctic Tern is a medium-sized bird around 33–36&#160;cm (13–15&#160;in) from the tip of its beak to the tip of its tail. The wingspan is 76–85&#160;cm.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The weight is 86–127&#160;g (3.0–4.5&#160;oz). The <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">beak</a> is dark red, as are the short legs and webbed feet. Like most terns, the Arctic Tern has <a href="/wiki/Bird_flight" title="Bird flight">high aspect ratio wings</a> and a tail with a deep fork.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The adult <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> is grey above, with a black nape and crown and white cheeks. The upperwings are pale grey, with the area near the wingtip being translucent. The tail is white, and the underparts pale grey. Both sexes are similar in appearance. The winter plumage is similar, but the crown is whiter and the bills are darker.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Havterne.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Havterne.jpg/220px-Havterne.jpg" width="220" height="138" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Havterne.jpg/330px-Havterne.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Havterne.jpg/440px-Havterne.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Havterne.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf3/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
An Arctic Tern in flight with wings spread</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Juveniles differ from adults in their black bill and legs, "scaly" appearing wings, and mantle with dark feather tips, dark carpal wing bar, and short tail streamers.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> During their first summer, juveniles also have a whiter forecrown.<sup id="cite_ref-National_Geographic_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National_Geographic-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The species has a variety of <a href="/wiki/Bird_call" title="Bird call" class="mw-redirect">calls</a>; the two most common being the <i>alarm call</i>, made when possible predators (such as humans or other <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>) enter the colonies, and the <i>advertising call</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hatch_et_al._10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hatch_et_al.-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The advertising call is social in nature, made when returning to the colony and during aggressive encounters between individuals. It is unique to each individual tern and as such it serves a similar role to the <a href="/wiki/Bird_song" title="Bird song" class="mw-redirect">bird song</a> of <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerines</a>, identifying individuals. Eight other calls have been described, from <i>begging calls</i> made by females during mating to <i>attack calls</i> made while swooping at intruders.</p>
<p>While the Arctic Tern is similar to the <a href="/wiki/Common_Tern" title="Common Tern">Common</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roseate_Tern" title="Roseate Tern">Roseate Terns</a>, its colouring, profile, and call are slightly different. Compared to the Common Tern, it has a longer tail and mono-coloured bill, while the main differences from the Roseate are its slightly darker colour and longer wings. The Arctic Tern's call is more nasal and rasping than that of the Common, and is easily distinguishable from that of the Roseate.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This bird's closest relatives are a group of South Polar species, the <a href="/wiki/South_American_Tern" title="South American Tern">South American</a> (<i>Sterna hirundinacea</i>), <a href="/wiki/Kerguelen_Tern" title="Kerguelen Tern">Kerguelen</a> (<i>S. virgata</i>), and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Tern" title="Antarctic Tern">Antarctic</a> (<i>S. vittata</i>) Terns.<sup id="cite_ref-Bridge_et_al._12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bridge_et_al.-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> On the wintering grounds, the Arctic Tern can be distinguished from these relatives; the six-month difference in <a href="/wiki/Moult" title="Moult" class="mw-redirect">moult</a> is the best clue here, with Arctic Terns being in winter plumage during the southern summer. The southern species also do not show darker wingtips in flight.</p>
<p>The immature plumages of Arctic Tern were originally described as separate species, <i>Sterna portlandica</i> and <i>Sterna pikei</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Breeding begins around the third or fourth year.<sup id="cite_ref-Hawksley_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hawksley-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Arctic Terns mate for life, and in most cases, return to the same colony each year.<sup id="cite_ref-Firefly_p_267_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Firefly_p_267-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Courtship#Courtship_in_the_animal_kingdom" title="Courtship">Courtship</a> is elaborate, especially in birds nesting for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-Firefly_p_268_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Firefly_p_268-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Courtship begins with a so-called "high flight", where a female will chase the male to a high altitude and then slowly descend. This display is followed by "fish flights", where the male will offer fish to the female. Courtship on the ground involves strutting with a raised tail and lowered wings. After this, both birds will usually fly and circle each other.<sup id="cite_ref-Firefly_p_268_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Firefly_p_268-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Arctic_tern_(Sterna_paradisaea)_chick.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Arctic_tern_%28Sterna_paradisaea%29_chick.jpg/220px-Arctic_tern_%28Sterna_paradisaea%29_chick.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Arctic_tern_%28Sterna_paradisaea%29_chick.jpg/330px-Arctic_tern_%28Sterna_paradisaea%29_chick.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Arctic_tern_%28Sterna_paradisaea%29_chick.jpg/440px-Arctic_tern_%28Sterna_paradisaea%29_chick.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Arctic_tern_(Sterna_paradisaea)_chick.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf3/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>Both sexes agree on a site for a nest, and both will defend the site. During this time, the male continues to feed the female. Mating occurs shortly after this.<sup id="cite_ref-Firefly_p_268_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Firefly_p_268-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Breeding takes place in <a href="/wiki/Seabird_colony" title="Seabird colony" class="mw-redirect">colonies</a> on coasts, islands and occasionally inland on <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> near water. It often forms mixed flocks with the <a href="/wiki/Common_Tern" title="Common Tern">Common Tern</a>. It lays from one to three <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> per clutch, most often two.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Artic_tern_8664.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Artic_tern_8664.JPG/220px-Artic_tern_8664.JPG" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Artic_tern_8664.JPG/330px-Artic_tern_8664.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Artic_tern_8664.JPG/440px-Artic_tern_8664.JPG 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Artic_tern_8664.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf3/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
An Arctic Tern protecting nest near Markarfljot river in south Iceland.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It is one of the most aggressive terns, fiercely defensive of its nest and young. It will attack humans and large predators, usually striking the top or back of the head. Although it is too small to cause serious injury to an animal of a human's size, it is still capable of drawing blood, and is capable of repelling many raptorial birds and smaller mammalian predators such as foxes and cats.<sup id="cite_ref-Cramp_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cramp-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Other nesting birds, such as <a href="/wiki/Alcid" title="Alcid" class="mw-redirect">alcids</a>, often incidentally benefit from the protection provided by nesting in an area defended by Arctic Terns.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Arctic_tern_nest_by_Bruce_McAdam.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Arctic_tern_nest_by_Bruce_McAdam.jpg/220px-Arctic_tern_nest_by_Bruce_McAdam.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Arctic_tern_nest_by_Bruce_McAdam.jpg/330px-Arctic_tern_nest_by_Bruce_McAdam.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Arctic_tern_nest_by_Bruce_McAdam.jpg/440px-Arctic_tern_nest_by_Bruce_McAdam.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Arctic_tern_nest_by_Bruce_McAdam.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf3/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Arctic Tern nest with two eggs, at Thingvellir National Park, Iceland</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The nest is usually a depression in the ground, which may or may not be lined with bits of grass or similar materials. The eggs are mottled and camouflaged.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Both sexes share incubation duties. The young hatch after 22–27 days and <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> after 21–24 days.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> If the parents are disturbed and flush from the nest frequently the incubation period could be extended to as long as 34 days.<sup id="cite_ref-Hatch_et_al._10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hatch_et_al.-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>When hatched, the chicks are <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">downy</a>. Neither <a href="/wiki/Altricial" title="Altricial">altricial</a> nor <a href="/wiki/Precocial" title="Precocial">precocial</a>, the chicks begin to move around and explore their surroundings within one to three days after hatching.<sup id="cite_ref-Kaufman_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaufman-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Usually, they do not stray far from the nest. Chicks are brooded by the adults for the first ten days after hatching.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Both parents care for hatchlings.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_8-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Chick diets always include fish, and parents selectively bring larger prey items to chicks than they eat themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-Hatch_et_al._10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hatch_et_al.-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Males bring more food than females. Feeding by the parents lasts for roughly a month before being weaned off slowly.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_8-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> After fledging, the juveniles learn to feed themselves, including the difficult method of plunge-diving.<sup id="cite_ref-Firefly_p_269_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Firefly_p_269-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> They will fly south to winter with the help of their parents.<sup id="cite_ref-Audubon_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Audubon-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Arctic Terns are long-lived birds that spend considerable time raising only a few young, and are thus said to be <a href="/wiki/R/K_selection_theory" title="R/K selection theory">K-selected</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Burger_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burger-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The maximum recorded <a href="/wiki/Maximum_life_span" title="Maximum life span">life span</a> for the species is 34 years,<sup id="cite_ref-Hatch_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hatch-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> although the average lifespan is about 20 years<sup id="cite_ref-Firefly_p_267_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Firefly_p_267-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> A study in the Farne Islands estimated an annual survival rate of 82%.<sup id="cite_ref-Cullen_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cullen-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecology and behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology_and_behaviour">Ecology and behaviour</span></h2>
<p>The diet of the Arctic Tern varies depending on location and time, but is usually <a href="/wiki/Carnivore" title="Carnivore">carnivorous</a>. In most cases, it eats small <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> or marine <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HBW_8-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Fish species comprise the most important part of the diet, and account for more of the <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> consumed than any other food. Prey species are immature (1–2 year old) shoaling species such as <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herring</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sandlance" title="Sandlance" class="mw-redirect">sandlances</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Capelin" title="Capelin">capelin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cramp_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cramp-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Among the marine crustaceans eaten are <a href="/wiki/Amphipod" title="Amphipod" class="mw-redirect">amphipods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Krill" title="Krill">krill</a>. Sometimes, these birds also eat <a href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc" class="mw-redirect">molluscs</a>, marine worms, or <a href="/wiki/Berry" title="Berry">berries</a>, and on their northern breeding grounds, <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kaufman_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaufman-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Arctic Terns sometimes dip down to the surface of the water to catch prey close to the surface. They may also chase insects in the air when breeding.<sup id="cite_ref-Kaufman_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaufman-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> It is also thought that Arctic Terns may, in spite of their small size, occasionally engage in <a href="/wiki/Kleptoparasitism" title="Kleptoparasitism">kleptoparasitism</a> by swooping at birds so as to startle them into releasing their catches.<sup id="cite_ref-Kaufman_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaufman-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Several species are targeted—conspecifics, other terns (like the Common Tern), and some <a href="/wiki/Auk" title="Auk">auk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grebe" title="Grebe">grebe</a> species.<sup id="cite_ref-Hatch_et_al._10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hatch_et_al.-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>While nesting, Arctic Terns are vulnerable to predation by cats and other animals.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Besides being a competitor for nesting sites, the larger <a href="/wiki/American_Herring_Gull" title="American Herring Gull">Herring Gull</a> steals eggs and hatchlings. Camouflaged eggs help prevent this, as do isolated nesting sites.<sup id="cite_ref-Firefly_p_269_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Firefly_p_269-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> While feeding, <a href="/wiki/Skua" title="Skua">skuas</a>, gulls, and other tern species will often harass the birds and steal their food.<sup id="cite_ref-Firefly_p_271_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Firefly_p_271-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> They often form mixed colonies with other terns, such as <a href="/wiki/Common_Tern" title="Common Tern">Common</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sandwich_Tern" title="Sandwich Tern">Sandwich Terns</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kuestenseeschwalbe-Kniepsand.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Kuestenseeschwalbe-Kniepsand.jpg/220px-Kuestenseeschwalbe-Kniepsand.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Kuestenseeschwalbe-Kniepsand.jpg/330px-Kuestenseeschwalbe-Kniepsand.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Kuestenseeschwalbe-Kniepsand.jpg/440px-Kuestenseeschwalbe-Kniepsand.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kuestenseeschwalbe-Kniepsand.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf3/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Close-up</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Arctic Terns are considered threatened or species of concern in certain states. They are also among the species to which the <i><a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a></i> applies.<sup id="cite_ref-AEWA_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AEWA-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> The species reduced population in <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> in the late nineteenth-century because of hunting for the millinery trade.<sup id="cite_ref-Hatch_et_al._10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hatch_et_al.-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Exploitation continues today in western <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, where the species has reduced population greatly since 1950.<sup id="cite_ref-Hansen_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hansen-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>At the southern part of their range, the Arctic Tern has been reducing in numbers. Much of this is due to lack of food.<sup id="cite_ref-National_Geographic_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National_Geographic-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> However, most of these birds' range is extremely remote, with no apparent trend in the species as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-Kaufman_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaufman-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>''<i>Birdlife International has considered the species to be at lower risk since 1988, believing that there are approximately one million individuals around the world.<sup id="cite_ref-Birdlife_International_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birdlife_International-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></i></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cultural depictions">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_depictions">Cultural depictions</span></h2>
<p>The Arctic Tern has appeared on the <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamps" title="Postage stamps" class="mw-redirect">postage stamps</a> of several countries and dependent territories. Territories include the <a href="/wiki/%C3%85land_Islands" title="Åland Islands">Åland Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alderney" title="Alderney">Alderney</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>. Countries include <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bird_Stamps_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bird_Stamps-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
