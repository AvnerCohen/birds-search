<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Birds_of_Sweden_2016_37.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Birds_of_Sweden_2016_37.jpg/220px-Birds_of_Sweden_2016_37.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Birds_of_Sweden_2016_37.jpg/330px-Birds_of_Sweden_2016_37.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Birds_of_Sweden_2016_37.jpg/440px-Birds_of_Sweden_2016_37.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1677" data-file-height="1677" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Birds_of_Sweden_2016_37.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Great tit in Sweden, winter 2016</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>great tit</b> (<i>Parus major</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Tit_(bird)" title="Tit (bird)">tit</a> family Paridae. It is a widespread and common species throughout Europe, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> and east across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Amur_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Amur River">Amur River</a>, south to parts of <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> where it is generally resident in any sort of woodland; most great tits do not <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> except in extremely harsh winters. Until 2005 this species was <a href="/wiki/Lumpers_and_splitters" title="Lumpers and splitters">lumped</a> with numerous other subspecies. <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> studies have shown these other subspecies to be distinctive from the great tit and these have now been separated as two distinct species, the <a href="/wiki/Cinereous_tit" title="Cinereous tit">cinereous tit</a> of southern Asia, and the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_tit" title="Japanese tit">Japanese tit</a> of East Asia. The great tit remains the most widespread species in the genus <i>Parus</i>.
</p><p>The great tit is a distinctive bird with a black head and neck, prominent white cheeks, olive upperparts and yellow underparts, with some variation amongst the numerous subspecies. It is predominantly <a href="/wiki/Insectivore" title="Insectivore">insectivorous</a> in the summer, but will consume a wider range of food items in the winter months, including small hibernating bats.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Like all tits it is a cavity nester, usually nesting in a hole in a tree. The female lays around 12 eggs and incubates them alone, although both parents raise the chicks. In most years the pair will raise two broods. The nests may be raided by <a href="/wiki/Great_spotted_woodpecker" title="Great spotted woodpecker">woodpeckers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_gray_squirrel" title="Eastern gray squirrel">squirrels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Least_weasel" title="Least weasel">weasels</a> and infested with <a href="/wiki/Flea" title="Flea">fleas</a>, and adults may be hunted by <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">sparrowhawks</a>. The great tit has adapted well to human changes in the environment and is a common and familiar bird in urban parks and gardens. The great tit is also an important study species in <a href="/wiki/Ornithology" title="Ornithology">ornithology</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The great tit was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> under its current binomial name by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Its scientific name is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>parus</i> "tit" and <i>maior</i> "larger".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Francis_Willughby" title="Francis Willughby">Francis Willughby</a> had used the name in the 17th century.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Parus_major_cinereus_Gujarat.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Bird with similar markings to great tit, but colours washed out and greyer, drinks from a leaking tap" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Parus_major_cinereus_Gujarat.jpg/220px-Parus_major_cinereus_Gujarat.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Parus_major_cinereus_Gujarat.jpg/330px-Parus_major_cinereus_Gujarat.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Parus_major_cinereus_Gujarat.jpg/440px-Parus_major_cinereus_Gujarat.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="333" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Parus_major_cinereus_Gujarat.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 11 subspecies of the <a href="/wiki/Cinereous_tit" title="Cinereous tit">cinereous tit</a> were once lumped with the great tit but recent genetic and bioacoustic studies now separate that group as a distinct species</div></div></div>
<p>The great tit was formerly treated as ranging from Britain to Japan and south to the islands of Indonesia, with 36 described subspecies ascribed to four main species groups. The <i>major</i> group had 13 subspecies across Europe, temperate Asia and north Africa, the <i>minor</i> group's nine subspecies occurred from southeast <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> into northern southeast Asia and the 11 subspecies in the <i>cinereus</i> group were found from <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> across south Asia to <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>. The three <i>bokharensis</i> subspecies were often treated as a separate species, <i>Parus bokharensis</i>, the Turkestan tit. This form was once thought to form a <a href="/wiki/Ring_species" title="Ring species">ring species</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Plateau" title="Tibetan Plateau">Tibetan Plateau</a>, with gene flow throughout the subspecies, but this theory was abandoned when sequences of <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> were examined, finding that the four groups were distinct (<a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">monophyletic</a>) and that the <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridisation</a> zones between the groups were the result of secondary contact after a temporary period of isolation.<sup id="cite_ref-Kvist_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kvist-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A study published in 2005 confirmed that the <i>major</i> group was distinct from the <i>cinereus</i> and <i>minor</i> groups and that along with <i>P.&#160;m. bokharensis</i> it diverged from these two groups around 1.5 million years ago. The divergence between the <i>bokharensis</i> and <i>major</i> groups was estimated to have been about half a million years ago. The study also examined hybrids between representatives of the <i>major</i> and <i>minor</i> groups in the <a href="/wiki/Amur_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Amur River">Amur Valley</a> where the two meet. Hybrids were rare, suggesting that there were some reproductive barriers between the two groups. The study recommended that the two eastern groups be split out as new species, the <a href="/wiki/Cinereous_tit" title="Cinereous tit">cinereous tit</a> (<i>Parus cinereus</i>), and the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_tit" title="Japanese tit">Japanese tit</a> (<i>Parus minor</i>), but that the Turkestan tit be <a href="/wiki/Lumpers_and_splitters" title="Lumpers and splitters">lumped</a> in with the great tit.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This taxonomy has been followed by some authorities, for example the <i><a href="/wiki/Birds_of_the_World:_Recommended_English_Names" title="Birds of the World: Recommended English Names">IOC World Bird List</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Handbook_of_the_Birds_of_the_World" title="Handbook of the Birds of the World">Handbook of the Birds of the World</a></i> volume treating the <i>Parus</i> species went for the more traditional classification, treating the Turkestan tit as a separate species but retaining the Japanese and cinereous tits with the great tit,<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> a move that has not been without criticism.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> of the great tit is the most widespread, its range stretching from the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> to the Amur Valley and from <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> to the Middle East. The other subspecies have much more restricted distributions, four being restricted to islands and the remainder of the <i>P. m. major</i> subspecies representing former <a href="/wiki/Refugium_(population_biology)" title="Refugium (population biology)">glacial refuge populations</a>. The dominance of a single, morphologically uniform subspecies over such a large area suggests that the nominate race rapidly recolonised a large area after the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">glacial epoch</a>. This hypothesis is supported by genetic studies which suggest a geologically recent <a href="/wiki/Population_bottleneck" title="Population bottleneck">genetic bottleneck</a> followed by a rapid population expansion.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The genus <i>Parus</i> once held most of the species of tit in the family <a href="/wiki/Paridae" class="mw-redirect" title="Paridae">Paridae</a>, but morphological and genetic studies led to the splitting of that large genus in 1998. The great tit was retained in <i>Parus</i>, which along with <i><a href="/wiki/Cyanistes" title="Cyanistes">Cyanistes</a></i> comprises a lineage of tits known as the "non-hoarders", with reference to the <a href="/wiki/Hoarding_(animal_behaviour)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hoarding (animal behaviour)">hoarding</a> behaviour of members of the other clade. The genus <i>Parus</i> is still the largest in the family, but may be split again.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Other than those species formerly considered to be subspecies, the great tit's closest relatives are the <a href="/wiki/White-naped_tit" title="White-naped tit">white-naped</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green-backed_tit" title="Green-backed tit">green-backed</a> tits of southern Asia. Hybrids with tits outside the genus <i>Parus</i> are very rare, but have been recorded with <a href="/wiki/Blue_tit" class="mw-redirect" title="Blue tit">blue tit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal_tit" title="Coal tit">coal tit</a>, and probably <a href="/wiki/Marsh_tit" title="Marsh tit">marsh tit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Harrap_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrap-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

</p>

<li><i>P. m. kapustini</i>, was described by <a href="/wiki/Portenko" class="mw-redirect" title="Portenko">Portenko</a> in 1954, and is found in north west <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> (north west <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a>) to <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Tit_(Parus_major)_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="duller-plumaged great tit with weak breast and belly stripe" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Great_Tit_%28Parus_major%29_1.jpg/220px-Great_Tit_%28Parus_major%29_1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="181" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Great_Tit_%28Parus_major%29_1.jpg/330px-Great_Tit_%28Parus_major%29_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Great_Tit_%28Parus_major%29_1.jpg/440px-Great_Tit_%28Parus_major%29_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="1054" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Tit_(Parus_major)_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In females and juveniles the mid-line stripe is narrower and sometimes discontinuous</div></div></div>
<p>The great tit is large for a tit at 12.5&#160;to 14.0&#160;cm (4.9–5.5&#160;in) in length, and has a distinctive appearance that makes it easy to recognise. The nominate race <i>P. major major</i> has a bluish-black <a href="/wiki/Crown_(anatomy)" title="Crown (anatomy)">crown</a>, black neck, throat, bib and head, and white cheeks and ear coverts. The breast is bright lemon-yellow and there is a broad black mid-line stripe running from the bib to vent. There is a dull white spot on the neck turning to greenish yellow on the upper <a href="/wiki/List_of_terms_used_in_bird_topography" title="List of terms used in bird topography">nape</a>. The rest of the nape and back are green tinged with olive. The <a href="/wiki/Covert_(feather)" class="mw-redirect" title="Covert (feather)">wing-coverts</a> are green, the rest of the wing is bluish-grey with a white wing-bar. The tail is bluish grey with white outer tips. The <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> of the female is similar to that of the male except that the colours are overall duller; the bib is less intensely black,<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> as is the line running down the belly, which is also narrower and sometimes broken.<sup id="cite_ref-ventral_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ventral-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Young birds are like the female, except that they have dull olive-brown napes and necks, greyish rumps, and greyer tails, with less defined white tips.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Parus_major_Luc_Viatour.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Great tit with strongly yellow sides perched on twig" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Parus_major_Luc_Viatour.jpg/220px-Parus_major_Luc_Viatour.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Parus_major_Luc_Viatour.jpg/330px-Parus_major_Luc_Viatour.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Parus_major_Luc_Viatour.jpg/440px-Parus_major_Luc_Viatour.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2226" data-file-height="1483" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Parus_major_Luc_Viatour.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The plumage of the male is typically bright, although this varies by subspecies</div></div></div>
<p>There is some variation in the subspecies. <i>P. m. newtoni</i> is like the nominate race but has a slightly longer bill, the mantle is slightly deeper green, there is less white on the tail tips, and the ventral mid-line stripe is broader on the belly. <i>P. m. corsus</i> also resembles the nominate form but has duller upperparts, less white in the tail and less yellow in the nape. <i>P. m. mallorcae</i> is like the nominate subspecies, but has a larger bill, greyer-blue upperparts and slightly paler underparts. <i>P. m. ecki</i> is like <i>P. m. mallorcae</i> except with bluer upperparts and paler underparts. <i>P. m. excelsus</i> is similar to the nominate race but has much brighter green upperparts, bright yellow underparts and no (or very little) white on the tail. <i>P. m. aphrodite</i> has darker, more olive-grey upperparts, and the underparts are more yellow to pale cream. <i>P. m. niethammeri</i> is similar to <i>P. m. aphrodite</i> but the upperparts are duller and less green, and the underparts are pale yellow. <i>P. m. terrasanctae</i> resembles the previous two subspecies but has slightly paler upperparts. <i>P. m. blandfordi</i> is like the nominate but with a greyer mantle and scapulars and pale yellow underparts, and <i>P. m. karelini</i> is intermediate between the nominate and <i>P. m. blandfordi</i>, and lacks white on the tail. The plumage of <i>P. m. bokharensis</i> is much greyer, pale creamy white to washed out grey underparts, a larger white cheep patch, a grey tail, wings, back and nape. It is also slightly smaller, with a smaller bill but longer tail. The situation is similar for the two related subspecies in the Turkestan tit group. <i>P. m. turkestanicus</i> is like <i>P. m. bokharensis</i> but with a larger bill and darker upperparts. <i>P. m. ferghanensis</i> is like <i>P. m. bokharensis</i> but with a smaller bill, darker grey on the flanks and a more yellow wash on the juvenile birds.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GreatTit002.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/GreatTit002.jpg/220px-GreatTit002.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/GreatTit002.jpg/330px-GreatTit002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/GreatTit002.jpg/440px-GreatTit002.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="384" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GreatTit002.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A pair of great tits; the one on the left is female, the second one is male.</div></div></div><p>The colour of the male bird's breast has been shown to correlate with stronger sperm, and is one way that the male demonstrates his reproductive superiority to females. Higher levels of <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">carotenoid</a> increase the intensity of the yellow of the breast its colour, and also enable the sperm to better withstand the onslaught of <a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">free radicals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DellAmore_Christine_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DellAmore_Christine-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Carotenoids cannot be synthesized by the bird and have to be obtained from food, so a bright colour in a male demonstrates his ability to obtain good nutrition.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> However, the saturation of the yellow colour is also influenced by environmental factors, such as weather conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The width of the male's ventral stripe, which varies with individual, is selected for by females, with higher quality females apparently selecting males with wider stripes.<sup id="cite_ref-ventral_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ventral-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span></h3>

<p>The great tit is, like other tits, a <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">vocal bird</a>, and has up to 40 types of calls and songs. The calls are generally the same between the sexes, but the male is much more vocal and the female rarely calls. Soft single notes such as "pit", "spick", or "chit" are used as contact calls. A loud "tink" is used by adult males as an alarm or in territorial disputes. One of the most familiar is a "teacher, teacher", often likened to a squeaky <a href="/wiki/Wheelbarrow" title="Wheelbarrow">wheelbarrow</a> wheel, which is used in proclaiming ownership of a territory.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In former times, English folk considered the "saw-sharpening" call to be a foretelling of rain.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> There is little geographic variation in calls, but tits from the two south Asian groups recently split from the great tit do not recognise or react to the calls of the temperate great tits.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One explanation for the great tit's wide repertoire is the <a href="/wiki/Beau_Geste_hypothesis" title="Beau Geste hypothesis">Beau Geste hypothesis</a>. The eponymous hero of the novel propped dead soldiers against the battlements to give the impression that his fort was better defended than was really the case. Similarly, the multiplicity of calls gives the impression that the tit's territory is more densely occupied than it actually is. Whether the theory is correct or not, those birds with large vocabularies are socially dominant and breed more successfully.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Distribution.2C_movements_and_habitat"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution,_movements_and_habitat">Distribution, movements and habitat</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mixed-forest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="forest clearing with leaf strewn floor, low plants and saplings, and tall trees partly obscuring the sky" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Mixed-forest.jpg/220px-Mixed-forest.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Mixed-forest.jpg/330px-Mixed-forest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Mixed-forest.jpg/440px-Mixed-forest.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2560" data-file-height="1920" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mixed-forest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mixed-forests are one of the habitats great tits use in Europe</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2009-07-12-Nistkasten_(3).JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/2009-07-12-Nistkasten_%283%29.JPG/220px-2009-07-12-Nistkasten_%283%29.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="143" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/2009-07-12-Nistkasten_%283%29.JPG/330px-2009-07-12-Nistkasten_%283%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/2009-07-12-Nistkasten_%283%29.JPG/440px-2009-07-12-Nistkasten_%283%29.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1680" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2009-07-12-Nistkasten_(3).JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>At <a href="/wiki/Nest_box" title="Nest box">nest box</a> in <a href="/wiki/Altenbeken" title="Altenbeken">Altenbeken</a>, Germany</div></div></div>
<p>The great tit has a wide distribution across much of Eurasia. It can be found across all of Europe except for <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, including numerous <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a> islands. In North Africa it lives in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>. It also occurs across the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, and parts of <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> from northern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> to <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, as well as across northern Asia from the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Urals</a> as far east as northern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Amur_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Amur River">Amur Valley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The great tit occupies a range of habitats. It is most commonly found in open deciduous <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Temperate_broadleaf_and_mixed_forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate broadleaf and mixed forest">mixed forests</a>, forest edges and gardens. In dense forests, including <a href="/wiki/Pinophyta" title="Pinophyta">conifer forests</a> it prefers forest clearings. In northern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> it lives in boreal <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a>. In North Africa it rather resides in <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> forests as well as stands of <a href="/wiki/Atlas_cedar" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlas cedar">Atlas cedar</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Arecaceae" title="Arecaceae">palm</a> groves. In the east of its range in Siberia, Mongolia and China it favours riverine <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a> forest. Riverine woodlands of willows, poplars are among the habitats of the Turkestan subspecies, as well as low <a href="/wiki/Shrubland" title="Shrubland">scrubland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oasis" title="Oasis">oases</a>; at higher altitudes it occupies habitats ranging from dense deciduous and coniferous forests to open areas with scattered trees.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The great tit is generally not <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>. Pairs will usually remain near or in their <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territory</a> year round, even in the northern parts of their range. Young birds will disperse from their parents' territory, but usually not far. Populations may become <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration#Irruptions_and_dispersal" title="Bird migration">irruptive</a> in poor or harsh winters, meaning that groups of up to a thousand birds may unpredictably move from northern Europe to the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_states" title="Baltic states">Baltic</a> and also to Netherlands, Britain, even as far as the southern <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The great tit was unsuccessfully introduced into the United States; birds were set free near <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati, Ohio</a> between 1872 and 1874 but failed to become established. Suggestions that they were an excellent control measure for <a href="/wiki/Codling_moth" title="Codling moth">codling moths</a> nearly led to their introduction to some new areas particularly in the United States of America, however this plan was not implemented.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Birds were later introduced to the <a href="/wiki/Almaty_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Almaty Province">Almaty Province</a> in what is now <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> in 1960–61 and became established, although their present status is unclear.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet_and_feeding">Diet and feeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_tit_feeding.JPG" class="image"><img alt="Male great tit on branch with sunflower seed" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Great_tit_feeding.JPG/220px-Great_tit_feeding.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="96" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Great_tit_feeding.JPG/330px-Great_tit_feeding.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Great_tit_feeding.JPG/440px-Great_tit_feeding.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4800" data-file-height="2100" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_tit_feeding.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Like other tits, great tits transport food with their beak, and then transfer it to their feet, where it is held while they eat</div></div></div>
<p>Great tits are primarily <a href="/wiki/Insectivore" title="Insectivore">insectivorous</a> in the summer, feeding on <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a> which they capture by <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">foliage</a> <a href="/wiki/Gleaning_(birds)" title="Gleaning (birds)">gleaning</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ehrlich_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrlich-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Their larger <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrate</a> prey include <a href="/wiki/Cockroach" title="Cockroach">cockroaches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cricket_(insect)" title="Cricket (insect)">crickets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neuroptera" title="Neuroptera">lacewings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earwig" title="Earwig">earwigs</a>, bugs (<a href="/wiki/Hemiptera" title="Hemiptera">Hemiptera</a>), ants, flies (Diptera), caddis flies, beetles, scorpion flies, harvestmen, bees and wasps, snails and woodlice.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_breeder" title="Seasonal breeder">breeding season</a>, the tits prefer to feed <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein-rich</a> <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a> to their young.<sup id="cite_ref-Royoma_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Royoma-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> A study published in 2007 found that great tits helped to reduce caterpillar damage in <a href="/wiki/Orchard" title="Orchard">apple orchards</a> by as much as 50%.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Nestlings also undergo a period in their early development where they are fed a number of spiders, possibly for nutritional reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-Royoma_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Royoma-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In autumn and winter, when insect prey becomes scarcer, great tits add <a href="/wiki/Berry" title="Berry">berries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a> to their diet. Seeds and <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> usually come from deciduous trees and shrubs, like for instance the seeds of <a href="/wiki/Beech" title="Beech">beech</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hazel" title="Hazel">hazel</a>. Where it is available they will readily take table scraps, <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanuts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helianthus" title="Helianthus">sunflower</a> seeds from bird tables. In particularly severe winters they may consume 44% of their body weight in sunflower seeds.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> They often forage on the ground, particularly in years with high <a href="/wiki/Beech" title="Beech">beech</a> <a href="/wiki/Mast_(botany)" title="Mast (botany)">mast</a> production.<sup id="cite_ref-Ehrlich_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrlich-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Great tits, along with other tits, will join winter <a href="/wiki/Mixed-species_foraging_flock" title="Mixed-species foraging flock">mixed-species foraging flocks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Harrap_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrap-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Koolmees_Parus_major_Jos_Zwarts_4.tif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Koolmees_Parus_major_Jos_Zwarts_4.tif/lossy-page1-260px-Koolmees_Parus_major_Jos_Zwarts_4.tif.jpg" decoding="async" width="260" height="199" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Koolmees_Parus_major_Jos_Zwarts_4.tif/lossy-page1-390px-Koolmees_Parus_major_Jos_Zwarts_4.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Koolmees_Parus_major_Jos_Zwarts_4.tif/lossy-page1-520px-Koolmees_Parus_major_Jos_Zwarts_4.tif.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1985" data-file-height="1520" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Koolmees_Parus_major_Jos_Zwarts_4.tif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Great tit feeding its young with an insect</div></div></div>
<p>Large food items, such as large seeds or prey, are dealt with by "hold-hammering", where the item is held with one or both feet and then struck with the bill until it is ready to eat. Using this method, a great tit can get into a <a href="/wiki/Hazelnut" title="Hazelnut">hazelnut</a> in about twenty minutes. When feeding young, adults will hammer off the heads off large insects to make them easier to consume, and remove the gut from caterpillars so that the <a href="/wiki/Tannin" title="Tannin">tannins</a> in the gut will not retard the chick's growth.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Great tits combine dietary versatility with a considerable amount of intelligence and the ability to solve problems with insight learning, that is to solve a problem through insight rather than trial and error.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In England, great tits learned to break the foil caps of milk bottles delivered at the doorstep of homes to obtain the cream at the top.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> This behaviour, first noted in 1921, spread rapidly in the next two decades.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> In 2009, great tits were reported killing, and eating the brains of roosting <a href="/wiki/Pipistrellus" title="Pipistrellus">pipistrelle</a> bats. This is the first time a songbird has been recorded preying on bats. The tits only do this during winter when the bats are hibernating and other food is scarce.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> They have also been recorded using <a href="/wiki/Tool_use_by_animals" title="Tool use by animals">tools</a>, using a <a href="/wiki/Conifer" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifer">conifer needle</a> in the bill to extract larvae from a hole in a tree.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<p>Great tits are <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a> breeders and establish breeding <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territories</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> These territories are established in late January and defence begins in late winter or early spring.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Territories are usually reoccupied in successive years, even if one of the pair dies, so long as the brood is raised successfully. Females are likely to disperse to new territories if their nest is predated the previous year. If the pair divorces for some reason then the birds will disperse, with females travelling further than males to establish new territories.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Although the great tit is socially monogamous, <a href="/wiki/Promiscuity" title="Promiscuity">extra-pair</a> copulations are frequent. One study in Germany found that 40% of nests contained some offspring fathered by parents other than the breeding male and that 8.5% of all chicks were the result of <a href="/wiki/Cuckold" title="Cuckold">cuckoldry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Adult males tend to have a higher reproductive success compared to sub-adults.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Parus_major_MWNH_2290.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Parus_major_MWNH_2290.JPG/220px-Parus_major_MWNH_2290.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Parus_major_MWNH_2290.JPG/330px-Parus_major_MWNH_2290.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Parus_major_MWNH_2290.JPG/440px-Parus_major_MWNH_2290.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3596" data-file-height="2688" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Parus_major_MWNH_2290.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Parus.JPG" class="image"><img alt="nest with seven chicks. These are covered with grey down, and have bright yellow gapes" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Parus.JPG/220px-Parus.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="203" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Parus.JPG/330px-Parus.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Parus.JPG/440px-Parus.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2068" data-file-height="1909" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Parus.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Young chicks in the nest</div></div></div>
<p>Great tits are seasonal breeders. The exact timing of breeding varies by a number of factors, most importantly location. Most breeding occurs between January and September; in Europe the breeding season usually begins after March. In Israel there are exceptional records of breeding during the months of October to December. The amount of sunlight and daytime temperatures will also affect breeding timing.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> One study found a strong correlation between the timing of laying and the peak abundance of caterpillar prey, which is in turn correlated to temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> On an individual level, younger females tend to start laying later than older females.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sykora_Budka_Great_Tit_Bird_house.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Great tit leaving its wooden nest box" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Sykora_Budka_Great_Tit_Bird_house.jpg/170px-Sykora_Budka_Great_Tit_Bird_house.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="235" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Sykora_Budka_Great_Tit_Bird_house.jpg/255px-Sykora_Budka_Great_Tit_Bird_house.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Sykora_Budka_Great_Tit_Bird_house.jpg/340px-Sykora_Budka_Great_Tit_Bird_house.jpg 2x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="996" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sykora_Budka_Great_Tit_Bird_house.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Leaving nest box</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Koolmees_op_nest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Koolmees_op_nest.jpg/220px-Koolmees_op_nest.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Koolmees_op_nest.jpg/330px-Koolmees_op_nest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Koolmees_op_nest.jpg/440px-Koolmees_op_nest.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5152" data-file-height="3864" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Koolmees_op_nest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Great tit nesting in nest box</div></div></div>
<p>Great tits are cavity nesters, breeding in a hole that is usually inside a tree, although occasionally in a wall or rock face, and they will readily take to <a href="/wiki/Nest_box" title="Nest box">nest boxes</a>. The nest inside the cavity is built by the female, and is made of plant fibres, grasses, moss, hair, wool and feathers. The number in the <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is often very large, as many as 18, but five to twelve is more common. <a href="/wiki/Avian_clutch_size" title="Avian clutch size">Clutch size</a> is smaller when birds start laying later, and is also lower when the density of competitors is higher.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Second broods tend to have smaller clutches. Insularity also affects clutch size, with great tits on offshore islands laying smaller clutches with larger eggs than mainland birds.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The eggs are white with red spots. The female undertakes all <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" class="mw-redirect" title="Avian incubation">incubation</a> duties, and is fed by the male during incubation.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The bird is a close sitter, hissing when disturbed. The timing of hatching, which is best synchronised with peak availability of prey, can be manipulated when environmental conditions change after the laying of the first egg by delaying the beginning of incubation, laying more eggs or pausing during incubation.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The incubation period is between 12 and 15 days.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Tit_fledgeling.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Young bird with ruffled adult-like plumage and yellow gape" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Great_Tit_fledgeling.jpg/220px-Great_Tit_fledgeling.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Great_Tit_fledgeling.jpg/330px-Great_Tit_fledgeling.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Great_Tit_fledgeling.jpg/440px-Great_Tit_fledgeling.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2032" data-file-height="1524" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Tit_fledgeling.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fledgeling</div></div></div>
<p>The chicks, like those of all <a href="/wiki/Tit_(bird)" title="Tit (bird)">tits</a>, are hatched unfeathered and blind. Once feathers begin to erupt, the nestlings are unusual for <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a> birds in having plumage coloured with <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">carotenoids</a> similar to their parents (in most species it is dun-coloured to avoid predation). The <a href="/wiki/Nape" title="Nape">nape</a> is yellow and attracts the attention of the parents by its <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> reflectance. This may be to make them easier to find in low light, or be a signal of <a href="/wiki/Fitness_(biology)" title="Fitness (biology)">fitness</a> to win the parents' attention. This patch turns white after the first <a href="/wiki/Moulting" title="Moulting">moult</a> at age two months, and diminishes in size as the bird grows.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chicks are fed by both parents, usually receiving 6&#160;to 7&#160;g (0.21–0.25&#160;oz) of food a day.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Both parents provision the chicks with food and aid in nest sanitation by removing faecal packets, with no difference in the feeding effort between the sexes.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The nestling period is between 16 and 22 days, with chicks being independent of the parents eight days after <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledging</a>. Feeding of the fledgeling may continue after independence, lasting up to 25 days in chicks from the first brood, but as long as 50 days in the second brood.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Nestlings from second broods have weaker immune systems and body condition than those from first broods, and hence have a lower juvenile survival rate.<sup id="cite_ref-cjz_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cjz-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Inbreeding_depression" title="Inbreeding depression">Inbreeding depression</a> occurs when the offspring produced as a result of a mating between close relatives show reduced fitness.  The reduced fitness is generally considered to be a consequence of the increased expression of deleterious recessive alleles in these offspring.  In natural populations of <i>P. major</i>, inbreeding is avoided by dispersal of individuals from their birthplace, which reduces the chance of mating with a close relative.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid18211876_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18211876-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">Eurasian sparrowhawk</a> is a predator of great tits, with the young from second broods being at higher risk partly because of the hawk's greater need for food for its own developing young.<sup id="cite_ref-gotmark1_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gotmark1-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gotmark2_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gotmark2-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> The nests of great tits are raided by <a href="/wiki/Great_spotted_woodpecker" title="Great spotted woodpecker">great spotted woodpeckers</a>, particularly when nesting in certain types of nest boxes.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Other nest predators include <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced</a> <a href="/wiki/Eastern_gray_squirrel" title="Eastern gray squirrel">grey squirrels</a> (in Britain) and <a href="/wiki/Least_weasel" title="Least weasel">least weasels</a>, which are able to take nesting adults as well.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> A species of biting louse (<a href="/wiki/Chewing_louse" class="mw-redirect" title="Chewing louse">Mallophaga</a>) described as <i>Rostrinirmus hudeci</i> was isolated and described in 1981 from great tits in central Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-balat_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-balat-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> The hen flea <i>Ceratophyllus gallinae</i> is exceedingly common in the nests of blue and great tits. It was originally a specialist tit flea, but the dry, crowded conditions of chicken runs enabled it to flourish with its new host.<sup id="cite_ref-Miriam_Rothschild_1953_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miriam_Rothschild_1953-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> This flea is preferentially predated by the <a href="/wiki/Histeridae" title="Histeridae">clown beetle</a> <i>Gnathoncus punctulatus</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Miriam_Rothschild_1953_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miriam_Rothschild_1953-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Rove_beetle" title="Rove beetle">rove beetle</a> <i>Microglotta pulla</i> also feeds on fleas and their larvae. Although these beetles often remain in deserted nests, they can only breed in the elevated temperatures produced by brooding birds, tits being the preferred hosts.<sup id="cite_ref-Miriam_Rothschild_1953_48-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miriam_Rothschild_1953-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Great tits compete with <a href="/wiki/Pied_flycatcher" title="Pied flycatcher">pied flycatchers</a> for nesting boxes, and can kill prospecting flycatcher males. Incidences of fatal competition are more frequent when nesting times overlap, and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> has led to greater synchrony of nesting between the two species and flycatcher deaths. Having killed the flycatchers, the great tits may consume their brains.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sykora_konadra1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="adult great tit perched on hand" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Sykora_konadra1.jpg/220px-Sykora_konadra1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Sykora_konadra1.jpg/330px-Sykora_konadra1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a5/Sykora_konadra1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="279" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sykora_konadra1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The great tit's willingness to use bird-feeders and nesting boxes makes it popular with the general public and useful to scientists</div></div></div>
<p>The great tit is a popular garden bird due to its acrobatic performances when feeding on nuts or seed. Its willingness to move into nest boxes has made it a valuable study subject in <a href="/wiki/Ornithology" title="Ornithology">ornithology</a>; it has been particularly useful as a model for the study of the evolution of various life-history traits, particularly clutch size.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> A study of a <a href="/wiki/Biological_Abstracts" title="Biological Abstracts">literature database search</a> found 1,349 articles relating to <i>Parus major</i> for the period between 1969 and 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-Kvist_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kvist-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The great tit has generally adjusted to human modifications of the environment. It is more common and has better breeding success in areas with undisturbed forest cover, but it has adapted to human modified habitats. It can be very common in urban areas.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> For example, the breeding population in the city of <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a> (a city of half a million people) has been estimated at some 17,000 individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> In adapting to human environments its song has been observed to change in noise-polluted urban environments. In areas with low frequency background noise pollution, the song has a higher frequency than in quieter areas.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> This tit has expanded its range, moving northwards into Scandinavia and Scotland, and south into Israel and Egypt.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The total population is estimated at between 300–1,100 million birds in a range of 32.4&#160;million&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (12.5&#160;million&#160;sq&#160;mi). While there have been some localised declines in population in areas with poorer quality habitats, its large range and high numbers mean that the great tit is not considered to be threatened, and it is classed as <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a> on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
