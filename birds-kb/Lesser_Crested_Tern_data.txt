<p>The <b>Lesser Crested Tern</b> (<i>Thalasseus bengalensis</i>, syn. <i>Sterna bengalensis</i> - see Bridge <i>et al.</i>, 2005) is a <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">tern</a> family Sternidae. It breeds in subtropical coastal parts of the world mainly from the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> across the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> to the western <a href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific" class="mw-redirect">Pacific</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, with a significant population on the southern coast of the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a> on two islands off the <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libyan</a> coast. Accidental breeding has also been reported in Italy and France. The Australian birds are probably sedentary, but other populations are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering south to <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>.</p>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> has a number of geographical <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>, differing mainly in size and minor plumage details:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>T. b. emigrata</i>: breeding in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a> on islands off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>, wintering <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>. Pale grey above (only marginally darker than <a href="/wiki/Sandwich_Tern" title="Sandwich Tern">Sandwich Tern</a>); slightly larger.</li>
<li><i>T. b. bengalensis</i>: northern <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, wintering to <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>. Medium-dark grey above; slightly smaller.</li>
<li><i>T. b. torresii</i>: <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> south to <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, wintering in the same area (birds breeding in the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> are also often given as this race). Dark grey above; slightly larger.</li>
</ul>
<p>The Mediterranean race is a rare vagrant to <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, and has bred in pure or mixed pairs (with <a href="/wiki/Sandwich_Tern" title="Sandwich Tern">Sandwich Tern</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.</p>
<p>This species breeds in dense colonies on coasts and islands. It nests in a ground scrape and lays one to two (rarely three) eggs. Nesting behaviour is very similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Sandwich_Tern" title="Sandwich Tern">Sandwich Terns</a>, with predator avoidance by nesting in very dense colonies, and also (in race <i>emigrata</i> at least) by nesting in the late summer when predatory <a href="/wiki/Yellow-legged_Gull" title="Yellow-legged Gull">Yellow-legged Gulls</a> have finished breeding and departed from the nesting area.</p>
<p>Like all <i>Thalasseus</i> terns, Lesser Crested Tern feeds by plunge-diving for fish, usually from saline environments. It usually dives directly, and not from the "stepped-hover" favoured by <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Tern" title="Arctic Tern">Arctic Tern</a>. The offering of fish by the male to the female is part of the courtship display.</p>

<p>This is a medium-large tern, very similar in size and general appearance to its three very close relatives <a href="/wiki/Sandwich_Tern" title="Sandwich Tern">Sandwich Tern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elegant_Tern" title="Elegant Tern">Elegant Tern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Crested_Tern" title="Chinese Crested Tern">Chinese Crested Tern</a>. The summer adult has a black cap, black legs and a long sharp orange bill. The upperwings, rump and central tail feathers are grey and the underparts white. The primary flight feathers darken during the summer. In winter, the forehead becomes white. The call is a loud grating noise like Sandwich Tern.</p>
<p>The grey rump is a useful flight identification feature distinguishing it from the related species. The Elegant Tern also differs in a slightly longer, slenderer bill, while Chinese Crested Tern differs in a black tip to the bill and Sandwich Tern a black bill with a yellow tip.</p>
<p>Juvenile Lesser Crested Terns resemble same-age Sandwich Terns, but with a yellow-orange bill, and paler overall, with only faint dark crescents on the mantle feathers.</p>
<p>There are two other orange-billed terns within the range of this species, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Tern" title="Royal Tern">Royal Tern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greater_Crested_Tern" title="Greater Crested Tern">Greater Crested Tern</a>. Both are much larger and stouter-billed; Royal also has a white rump and tail, while Crested (which shares the grey rump) is darker overall above and has a yellower bill. See also <a href="/wiki/Orange-billed_tern" title="Orange-billed tern">Orange-billed tern</a>.</p>
<p><i>T. bengalensis</i> is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies, and one of 10 marine bird species listed in Mediterranean marine birds Action Plan <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.rac-spa.org/index.php?option=com_content&amp;task=view&amp;id=97&amp;Itemid=154">http://www.rac-spa.org/index.php?option=com_content&amp;task=view&amp;id=97&amp;Itemid=154</a>.</p>
