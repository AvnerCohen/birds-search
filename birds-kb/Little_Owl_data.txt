<p>The <b>Little Owl</b> (<i>Athene noctua</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> which is resident in much of the temperate and warmer parts of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> east to <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, and north <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It is not native to <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, but was first introduced in 1842,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Powys,_4th_Baron_Lilford" title="Thomas Powys, 4th Baron Lilford">Thomas Powys</a> and is now naturalised there. It was also successfully introduced to the <a href="/wiki/South_Island" title="South Island">South Island</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> in the early 20th century.</p>
<p>This species is a part of the larger grouping of owls known as <a href="/wiki/Typical_owl" title="Typical owl" class="mw-redirect">typical owls</a>, Strigidae, which contains most species of owl. The other grouping is the <a href="/wiki/Barn_owl" title="Barn owl" class="mw-redirect">barn owls</a>, Tytonidae.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Upload?wpDestFile=%27%27Athene_Noctua%27%27_-captive-8a.jpg" class="new" title="File:''Athene Noctua'' -captive-8a.jpg">File:<i>Athene Noctua</i> -captive-8a.jpg</a>
<div class="thumbcaption">At a bird display</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Little Owl is a small <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owl</a>, 23-27.5&#160;cm in length. The adult Little Owl of the most widespread form, the nominate <i>A. n. noctua</i>, is white-speckled brown above, and brown-streaked white below. It has a large head, long legs, and yellow eyes, and its white “eyebrows” give it a stern expression. This species has a bounding flight like a <a href="/wiki/Woodpecker" title="Woodpecker">woodpecker</a>. Juveniles are duller, and lack the adult's white crown spots. The call is a querulous <i>kee-ik</i>.</p>
<p>There is a pale grey-brown <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle Eastern</a> type known as Syrian Little Owl <i>A. n. lilith</i>. Other forms include another pale race, the north African <i>A. n. desertae</i>, and three intermediate subspecies, <i>A. n. indigena</i> of southeast Europe and <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a>, <i>A. n. glaux</i> in north Africa and southwest Asia, and <i>A. n. bactriana</i> of central Asia. A recent paper in the ornithological journal <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Birding" title="Dutch Birding">Dutch Birding</a> (vol. 31: 35-37, 2009) has advocated splitting the southeastern races as a separate species Lilith's Owl <i>Athene glaux</i> (with subspecies <i>A. g. glaux</i>, <i>A. g. indigena</i>, and <i>A. g. lilith</i>).</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_status">Distribution and status</span></h2>
<p>There are 13 recognized races of Little owl spread across Europe and Asia. The Little Owl was sacred to the goddess <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Athena</a>, from whom it gets the generic name. This is one of the most distributed owls and, due to its adaptability to human settlements and small size, probably ranks among the world's most numerous owl species.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Athene_noctua_(Steenuilkuiken).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Athene_noctua_%28Steenuilkuiken%29.jpg/220px-Athene_noctua_%28Steenuilkuiken%29.jpg" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Athene_noctua_%28Steenuilkuiken%29.jpg/330px-Athene_noctua_%28Steenuilkuiken%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Athene_noctua_%28Steenuilkuiken%29.jpg/440px-Athene_noctua_%28Steenuilkuiken%29.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Athene_noctua_(Steenuilkuiken).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf3/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>This is a sedentary species which is found in open country such as mixed farmland and parkland. It takes prey such as <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amphibia" title="Amphibia" class="mw-redirect">amphibians</a>, but also small <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>. It can attack birds of considerable size like game birds. It is partly <a href="/wiki/Diurnality" title="Diurnality">diurnal</a> and often perches boldly and prominently during the day.<sup id="cite_ref-fbi2_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fbi2-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Goldcrest_eggs_-_Natural_History_Museum_of_London-2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Goldcrest_eggs_-_Natural_History_Museum_of_London-2.jpg/220px-Goldcrest_eggs_-_Natural_History_Museum_of_London-2.jpg" width="220" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Goldcrest_eggs_-_Natural_History_Museum_of_London-2.jpg/330px-Goldcrest_eggs_-_Natural_History_Museum_of_London-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ed/Goldcrest_eggs_-_Natural_History_Museum_of_London-2.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Goldcrest_eggs_-_Natural_History_Museum_of_London-2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf3/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Little Owl egg (middle) compared with eggs of a <a href="/wiki/Goldcrest" title="Goldcrest">Goldcrest</a> (right) and <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">chicken</a> (left)</div>

</div>
<p>It becomes more vocal in nights as the breeding season approaches. Nest location varies based on the habitat, nests being found in holes in trees, rocks, cliffs, river banks, walls, buildings etc.<sup id="cite_ref-fbi2_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fbi2-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> It lays 3-5 eggs which are incubated by the female for 28–29 days, with a further 26 days to fledging. Little Owls will also nest in buildings, both abandoned and those fitted with custom owl <a href="/wiki/Nest_box" title="Nest box">nest boxes</a>. If living in an area with a large amount of human activity, Little Owls may grow used to man and will remain on their perch, often in full view, while humans are around.</p>
