<p>The <b>Eurasian crag martin</b> or just <b>crag martin</b> (<i>Ptyonoprogne rupestris</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Swallow" title="Swallow">swallow</a> family. It is about 14&#160;cm (5.5&#160;in) long with ash-brown upperparts and paler underparts, and a short, square tail that has distinctive white patches on most of its feathers. It breeds in the mountains of southern Europe, northwestern Africa and across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>. It can be confused with the three other species in its genus, but is larger than both, with brighter tail spots and different plumage tone. Many European birds are resident, but some northern populations and most Asian breeders are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in northern Africa, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> or <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.
</p><p>The Eurasian crag martin builds a nest adherent to the rock under a <a href="/wiki/Cliff" title="Cliff">cliff</a> overhang or increasingly onto a man-made structure. It makes a neat half-cup <a href="/wiki/Mud" title="Mud">mud</a> nest with an inner soft lining of feathers and dry grass. Nests are often solitary, although a few pairs may breed relatively close together at good locations. Two to five brown-blotched white eggs are incubated mainly by the female, and both parents feed the chicks. This species does not form large breeding colonies, but is <a href="/wiki/Gregarious" class="mw-redirect" title="Gregarious">gregarious</a> outside the breeding season. It feeds on a wide variety of <a href="/wiki/Insects" class="mw-redirect" title="Insects">insects</a> that it catches with its beak as the martin flies near to cliff faces or over streams and alpine meadows. Adults and young may be hunted and eaten by <a href="/wiki/Birds_of_prey" class="mw-redirect" title="Birds of prey">birds of prey</a> or <a href="/wiki/Corvids" class="mw-redirect" title="Corvids">corvids</a>, and this species is a host of blood-sucking mites. With its large, expanding range and large population, there are no significant conservation concerns involving the species.
</p><p>This bird is closely related to the other three crag martins which share its genus, and has sometimes been considered to be the same species as one or both, although it appears that there are areas where two species' ranges overlap without <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridisation</a> occurring. All three <i>Ptyonoprogne</i> crag martins are quite similar in behaviour to other <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> swallows that build mud nests, and are sometimes subsumed into the larger genus <i><a href="/wiki/Hirundo" title="Hirundo">Hirundo</a></i>, but this approach leads to inconsistencies in classifying other genera, particularly the <a href="/wiki/House_martin" class="mw-redirect" title="House martin">house martins</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_crag_martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Eurasian crag martin was formally described as <i>Hirundo rupestris</i> by Italian naturalist <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Antonio_Scopoli" title="Giovanni Antonio Scopoli">Giovanni Antonio Scopoli</a> in 1769<sup id="cite_ref-scopoli_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scopoli-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and was moved to the new genus <i><a href="/wiki/Ptyonoprogne" class="mw-redirect" title="Ptyonoprogne">Ptyonoprogne</a></i> by German ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Gustav_Reichenbach" title="Heinrich Gustav Reichenbach">Heinrich Gustav Reichenbach</a> in 1850.<sup id="cite_ref-reichenbach_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reichenbach-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Its nearest relatives are the three other members of the genus, the <a href="/wiki/Pale_crag_martin" title="Pale crag martin">pale crag martin</a>, <i>P. obsoleta</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Rock_martin" title="Rock martin">rock martin</a>, <i>P. fuligula</i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Dusky_crag_martin" title="Dusky crag martin">dusky crag martin</a>, <i>P. concolor</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-turner160_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner160-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The genus name is derived from the Greek <i>ptuon</i> (πτύον), "a fan", referring to the shape of the opened tail, and <a href="/wiki/Procne" title="Procne">Procne</a> (Πρόκνη), a mythological girl who was turned into a swallow. The specific <i>rupestris</i> means "of rocks", from the Latin <i>rupes</i> "rock".<sup id="cite_ref-bto_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> There are no generally recognised subspecies. Two races, Central Asian <i>P. r. centralasica</i> and <i>P. r. theresae</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas Mountains</a> of Morocco, have been proposed, but the slight differences in size and colour show no consistent geographical pattern.<sup id="cite_ref-turner158_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner158-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Fossils of this species have been found in <a href="/wiki/Late_Pleistocene" title="Late Pleistocene">Late Pleistocene</a> deposits in Bulgaria,<sup id="cite_ref-boev_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boev-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and in central France in layers dated at 242,000 to 301,000 years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-mc1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mc1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The four <i>Ptyonoprogne</i> species are members of the swallow family, and are placed in the subfamily Hirundininae, which comprises all swallows and martins except the very distinctive <a href="/wiki/River_martin" title="River martin">river martins</a>. <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> studies suggest that there are three major groupings within the Hirundininae, broadly correlating with the type of nest built.<sup id="cite_ref-sheldon_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sheldon-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The groups are the "core martins" including burrowing species like the <a href="/wiki/Sand_martin" title="Sand martin">sand martin</a>, the "nest-adopters", which are birds like the <a href="/wiki/Tree_swallow" title="Tree swallow">tree swallow</a> that use natural cavities, and the "mud nest builders". The <i>Ptyonoprogne</i> species construct an open mud nest and therefore belong to the last group; <i><a href="/wiki/Hirundo" title="Hirundo">Hirundo</a></i> species also build open nests, <i><a href="/wiki/Delichon" title="Delichon">Delichon</a></i> house martins have a closed nest, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Cecropis" title="Cecropis">Cecropis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Petrochelidon" title="Petrochelidon">Petrochelidon</a></i> swallows have <a href="/wiki/Retort" title="Retort">retort-like</a> closed nests with an entrance tunnel.<sup id="cite_ref-sheldon2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sheldon2-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Ptyonoprogne</i> is closely related to the larger swallow genus <i>Hirundo</i> into which it is often subsumed, but a DNA analysis showed that an enlarged genus <i>Hirundo</i> should logically contain all the mud-builder genera, including the <i><a href="/wiki/Delichon" title="Delichon">Delichon</a></i> house martins, a practice which few authorities follow. Although the nests of the <i>Ptyonoprogne</i> crag martins resembles those of typical <i>Hirundo</i> species like the <a href="/wiki/Barn_swallow" title="Barn swallow">barn swallow</a>, the research showed that if <i>Delichon</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cecropis" title="Cecropis">Cecropis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Petrochelidon" title="Petrochelidon">Petrochelidon</a></i> are split from <i>Hirundo</i>, <i>Ptyonoprogne</i> should also be treated as a separate genus.<sup id="cite_ref-sheldon_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sheldon-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_crag_martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ptyonoprogne_rupestris_-Europe-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Mostly brownish martins perching on brownish rocky ground" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Ptyonoprogne_rupestris_-Europe-8.jpg/220px-Ptyonoprogne_rupestris_-Europe-8.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Ptyonoprogne_rupestris_-Europe-8.jpg/330px-Ptyonoprogne_rupestris_-Europe-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Ptyonoprogne_rupestris_-Europe-8.jpg/440px-Ptyonoprogne_rupestris_-Europe-8.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ptyonoprogne_rupestris_-Europe-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Group perched on a rocky surface</div></div></div>
<p>The Eurasian crag martin is 13–15&#160;cm (5.1–5.9&#160;in) long with a 32–34.5&#160;cm (12.6–13.6&#160;in) wingspan, and weighs an average 23&#160;g (0.81&#160;oz). It has ash-brown upperparts and paler underparts, and has a broader body, wings and tail than any other European swallow. The tail is short and square, with white patches near the tips of all but the central and outermost pairs of feathers. The underwing and undertail <a href="/wiki/Covert_(feather)" class="mw-redirect" title="Covert (feather)">coverts</a> are blackish, the eyes are brown, the small bill is mainly black, and the legs are brownish-pink. The sexes are alike, but juveniles have buff-brown tips to the plumage of the head, upperparts and wing coverts. This species can be distinguished from the <a href="/wiki/Sand_martin" title="Sand martin">sand martin</a> by its larger size, the white patches on the tail, and its lack of a brown breast band. Where the range overlaps with that of another <i>Ptyonoprogne</i> species, the Eurasian crag martin is darker, browner and 15% larger than the rock martin,<sup id="cite_ref-turner158_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner158-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwp_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and larger and paler, particularly on its underparts than the dusky crag martin.<sup id="cite_ref-india_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-india-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The white tail spots of the Eurasian crag martin are significantly larger than those of both its relatives.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The crag martin's flight appears relatively slow for a swallow. Rapid wing beats are interspersed with flat-winged glides, and its long flexible <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Primaries" title="Flight feather">primaries</a> give it the agility to manoeuvre near cliff faces.<sup id="cite_ref-fantur_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fantur-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The average migration flight speed has been measured at 9.9&#160;m/s (32.5&#160;ft/s), less than the roughly 11&#160;m/s (36&#160;ft/s) typical for hirundines, but the data is limited.<sup id="cite_ref-bruderer_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bruderer-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The bird often flies high, and shows the white spots as it spreads its tail. The vocalisations include short high <i>pli</i>, and <i>piieh</i> and <i>tshir</i> calls resembling those of the <a href="/wiki/Linnet" class="mw-redirect" title="Linnet">linnet</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Common_house_martin" title="Common house martin">house martin</a> respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-collins_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_crag_martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Picos393.JPG" class="image"><img alt="bare cliffs with meadow in the foreground" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Picos393.JPG/220px-Picos393.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Picos393.JPG/330px-Picos393.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Picos393.JPG/440px-Picos393.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Picos393.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Breeding habitat in Spain</div></div></div>
<p>The Eurasian crag martin breeds in mountains from <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberia</a> and northwesternmost Africa through southern Europe, the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> to southwestern and northeastern China. Northern populations are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, with European birds wintering in north Africa, Senegal, Ethiopia and the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile Valley</a>, and Asian breeders going to southern China, the Indian subcontinent and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bnhs_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bnhs-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Some European birds stay north of the Mediterranean, and, like martins in warmer areas such as India, Turkey and Cyprus, just move to lower ground after breeding.<sup id="cite_ref-turner158_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner158-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The breeding range is bounded by the 20&#160;°C (68&#160;°F) July isotherm,<sup id="cite_ref-boev_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boev-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and wintering areas need a temperature of about 15&#160;°C (59&#160;°F) for enough insect food to be available.<sup id="cite_ref-fantur_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fantur-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This is a rare species any distance north of its breeding areas. For example, there are only eight records from the UK,<sup id="cite_ref-BB_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> none from Ireland,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and the first record for Sweden was reported as recently as 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-mats_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mats-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> South of its normal wintering range, it has occurred as a vagrant in The Gambia.<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Crag martins breed on dry, warm and sheltered cliffs in mountainous areas with crags and gorges. The typical altitude is 2,000–2,700&#160;m (6,600–8,900&#160;ft) but breeding occurs up to 5,000&#160;m (16,000&#160;ft) in Central Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-turner158_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner158-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The Eurasian crag martin's choice of nest sites is very similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Savi%27s_pipistrelle" title="Savi&#39;s pipistrelle">Savi's pipistrelle</a>, <i>Hypsugo savii</i>; the bird and the bat often breed in the same locations and have almost identical ranges in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-smithsonianzoo_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smithsonianzoo-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In South Asia, migrant Eurasian birds sometimes join with flocks of the dusky crag martin and roost communally on ledges of cliffs or buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_crag_martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_crag_martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cragmartinnest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="mud cup nest containing three young" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Cragmartinnest.jpg/220px-Cragmartinnest.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Cragmartinnest.jpg/330px-Cragmartinnest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Cragmartinnest.jpg/440px-Cragmartinnest.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cragmartinnest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nest with young</div></div></div>
<p>Crag martin pairs nest alone or in small <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonies</a>, usually containing fewer than ten nests.<sup id="cite_ref-bwp_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Nests are on average 30&#160;m (98&#160;ft) apart and each pair aggressively defends its breeding territory against other crag martin and most other bird species. Nesting takes place from May to August, and usually two broods are raised. The nest, built by both adults, is an open half cup made of mud and lined with soft material such as feathers or dry grass.<sup id="cite_ref-hume_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hume-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> It is constructed under an overhang on a rock cliff face, in a crevice or cave, or on a man-made structure. It takes one to three weeks to build and is re-used for the second brood and in subsequent years. The clutch is two to five eggs with an average of three. The eggs are white with brownish blotches particularly at the wide end, and average 20.2&#160;mm ×&#160;14.0&#160;mm (0.80&#160;in ×&#160;0.55&#160;in) with a weight of 2.08&#160;g (0.073&#160;oz). The eggs are incubated mainly by the female for 13–17 days to hatching, and the chicks take another 24–27 days to fledge. Both parents feed the chicks bringing food every two to five minutes, and the young are fed for 14–21 days after fledging.<sup id="cite_ref-turner158_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner158-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> With such frequent feeding rates the adults mainly forage in the best hunting zones in the immediate vicinity of the nest, since the further they fly to forage the longer it would take to bring food to the chicks in the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-fantur_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fantur-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In an Italian study, the hatching rate was 80.2&#160;percent, and the average number of fledged young was 3.1.<sup id="cite_ref-acquarone_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acquarone-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ptyonoprogne_rupestris_MHNT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Ptyonoprogne_rupestris_MHNT.jpg/220px-Ptyonoprogne_rupestris_MHNT.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Ptyonoprogne_rupestris_MHNT.jpg/330px-Ptyonoprogne_rupestris_MHNT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Ptyonoprogne_rupestris_MHNT.jpg/440px-Ptyonoprogne_rupestris_MHNT.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4363" data-file-height="2783" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ptyonoprogne_rupestris_MHNT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i> Ptyonoprogne rupestris </i> – <a href="/wiki/MHNT" class="mw-redirect" title="MHNT">MHNT</a></div></div></div>
<p>The crag martin has over the last few decades increasingly used houses and other man-made sites to nest. This greater availability of breeding sites has enabled the species to expand its range, but it is possible that this will lead to competition with other hirundines, such as the barn swallow and <a href="/wiki/Common_house_martin" title="Common house martin">common house martin</a>, which also use artificial nest sites.<sup id="cite_ref-isenmann_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-isenmann-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An Italian study showed that, as with other aerial feeders, the start of breeding was delayed by cold or wet weather, but this had no influence on the <a href="/wiki/Avian_clutch_size" title="Avian clutch size">clutch size</a> nor on the number of fledged young. Unexpectedly, it was found that once the eggs had hatched there was a negative relationship between temperature and the number of fledged young. The authors suggested that hot weather dried up the small rivers where the parents found food. Colony size did not influence the laying date, the clutch size or the number of successfully fledged young, but this species does not form large colonies anyway.<sup id="cite_ref-acquarone_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acquarone-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_crag_martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_crag_martin_David_Raju_(cropped).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Eurasian_crag_martin_David_Raju_%28cropped%29.jpg/220px-Eurasian_crag_martin_David_Raju_%28cropped%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Eurasian_crag_martin_David_Raju_%28cropped%29.jpg/330px-Eurasian_crag_martin_David_Raju_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Eurasian_crag_martin_David_Raju_%28cropped%29.jpg/440px-Eurasian_crag_martin_David_Raju_%28cropped%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3649" data-file-height="2435" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_crag_martin_David_Raju_(cropped).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In flight</div></div></div>
<p>The Eurasian crag martin feeds mainly on insects caught in its beak in flight, although it will occasionally take prey items off rocks, the ground, or a water surface. When breeding, birds often fly back and forth near to a rock face hunting for insects, feeding both inside and outside the nesting territory. At other times, they may hunt flying above streams or alpine meadows. The insects taken depend on what is locally available and may include <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>, ants, aerial spiders, and <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>. Aquatic species such as <a href="/wiki/Plecoptera" title="Plecoptera">stoneflies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caddisfly" title="Caddisfly">caddisflies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gerridae" title="Gerridae">pond skaters</a> appear to be important in at least Spain<sup id="cite_ref-turner158_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner158-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and Italy. Unlike other hirundines, these birds feed close to their breeding sites, and may be locally vulnerable to fluctuations in insect availability.<sup id="cite_ref-acquarone_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acquarone-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> This martin is gregarious outside the breeding season, and may form sizeable flocks where food is abundant.<sup id="cite_ref-bwp_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Cliff faces generate <a href="/wiki/Standing_wave" title="Standing wave">standing waves</a> in the airflow which concentrate insects near vertical areas. The crag martin exploits the area close to the cliff when it hunts, relying on its high manoeuvrability and ability to perform tight turns.<sup id="cite_ref-fantur_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fantur-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators_and_parasites">Predators and parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_crag_martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Predators and parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species is occasionally hunted by the <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">peregrine falcon</a>, which shares its mountain habitat,<sup id="cite_ref-rizzolli_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rizzolli-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and during its migration over the Himalayas, it is reported to be subject to predation by crows.<sup id="cite_ref-bnhs_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bnhs-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Common_kestrel" title="Common kestrel">Common kestrels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">Eurasian sparrowhawks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_jay" title="Eurasian jay">Eurasian jays</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_raven" title="Common raven">common ravens</a> are also treated as predators and attacked by repeated dives if they approach nesting cliffs. Despite the general aggressiveness of the martin, it tolerates <a href="/wiki/Sympatric" class="mw-redirect" title="Sympatric">sympatric</a> common house martins, perhaps because the large numbers of that highly colonial species provide an early warning of predators.<sup id="cite_ref-fantur_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fantur-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The crag martin is a host of blood-sucking mites of the genus <i>Dermanyssus</i>, including <i>D. chelidonis</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-RoyChauve2007_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RoyChauve2007-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and of the nasal mite <i>Ptilonyssus ptyonoprognes</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-texas_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-texas-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Two new species of parasites were first discovered on this martin, the fly <i>Ornithomya rupes</i> in Gibraltar and the flea <i>Ceratophyllus nanshanensis</i> from China.<sup id="cite_ref-hutson_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hutson-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-tsai_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tsai-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The European population of the Eurasian crag martin is estimated to be 360,000–1,110,000 individuals, including 120,000–370,000 breeding pairs. A rough estimate of the worldwide population is 500,000–5,000,000 individuals, with Europe hosting between one-quarter and one-half of the total. The population is estimated to be increasing following a northward expansion, which may be partly due to increased use of man-made structures as nest sites. Expansions of the range have been reported in Austria (where motorway bridges are used as nest sites), Switzerland, the former Yugoslavia, Romania, and Bulgaria. With its very large range and high numbers, the Eurasian crag martin is not considered to be threatened, and it is classed as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwp_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
