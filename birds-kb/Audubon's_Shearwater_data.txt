<p><b>Audubon's Shearwater</b>, <i><b>Puffinus lherminieri</b></i>, is a common <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical</a> <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Procellariidae" title="Procellariidae">Procellariidae</a>. Sometimes called <b>Dusky-backed Shearwater</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-carboneras1992_0-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carboneras1992-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_name" title="Scientific name" class="mw-redirect">scientific name</a> of this <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> commemorates the French naturalist <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9lix_Louis_L%27Herminier" title="Félix Louis L'Herminier">Félix Louis L'Herminier</a>.</p>
<p>Certain populations are also known as <b>Baillon's Shearwater</b> or <b>Tropical Shearwater</b>, <b>Bannerman's Shearwater</b>, <b>Mascarene Shearwater</b> and <b>Persian Shearwater</b>; some of these are considered distinct species by various authors.<sup id="cite_ref-carboneras1992_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carboneras1992-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> If they are all placed in <i>P. lherminieri</i>, the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Little_Shearwater" title="North Atlantic Little Shearwater" class="mw-redirect">North Atlantic Little Shearwater</a> (otherwise often separated as <i>P. baroli</i>) is generally included here too. Thus, these small <a href="/wiki/Shearwater" title="Shearwater">shearwaters</a> form a <a href="/wiki/Cryptic_species_complex" title="Cryptic species complex">cryptic species complex</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Audubon%27s_Shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Reed-audubons-shearwater.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Reed-audubons-shearwater.png/170px-Reed-audubons-shearwater.png" width="170" height="249" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Reed-audubons-shearwater.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Illustration by Chester A. Reed</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Audubon's Shearwaters are on average 30&#160;cm (12&#160;in) in length—about half the size of the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Shearwater" title="Greater Shearwater" class="mw-redirect">Greater Shearwater</a> (<i>Puffinus gravis</i>)—and weigh 170 g. There is some variation between populations and the normal size and weight range is 27<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material in the vicinity of this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s) from January 2009">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>–33&#160;cm and 150–230 g. The wingspan is 64–72&#160;cm, the tail is around 8.5&#160;cm long, the exposed <a href="/wiki/Culmen_(bird)" title="Culmen (bird)" class="mw-redirect">culmen</a> measures 3&#160;cm or slightly less, and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> is around 4&#160;cm in length.<sup id="cite_ref-em2001_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-em2001-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> In general appearance, it is a small <a href="/wiki/Shearwater" title="Shearwater">shearwater</a>, black above and white below and hard to distinguish from its relatives at first glance.<sup id="cite_ref-carboneras1992_0-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carboneras1992-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The upperparts, <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Rectrices" title="Flight feather">rectrices</a> and undertail coverts are blackish-brown, as are at least the <a href="/wiki/Distal" title="Distal" class="mw-redirect">distal</a> undersides of the <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">remiges</a>, but sometimes the entire feathers. The rest of the underparts are white, as is the head below eye level. The <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">iris</a> is dark, the feet are dull pink with a black wash and black toenails,<sup id="cite_ref-em2001_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-em2001-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> and the bill is grey, darker towards the tip, and with a pinkish hue.<sup id="cite_ref-carboneras1992_0-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carboneras1992-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Males and females look alike. Immature birds do not have a distinct plumage, while the nestlings are covered with <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">down feathers</a>, grey above and whitish on the belly.<sup id="cite_ref-carboneras1992_0-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carboneras1992-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It can be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Manx_Shearwater" title="Manx Shearwater">Manx Shearwater</a> (<i>P. puffinus</i>), which has white undertail coverts and in direct comparison a longer bill. Other similar-looking species are usually completely <a href="/wiki/Allopatric_speciation" title="Allopatric speciation">allopatric</a>, though the largely <a href="/wiki/Subantarctic" title="Subantarctic">subantarctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Little_Shearwater" title="Little Shearwater">Little Shearwater</a> (<i>P. assimilis</i>) may occasionally range into waters where <i>P. lherminieri</i> is normally found. It has more white on the face and underwing, a smaller bill and greyish-blue feet.<sup id="cite_ref-carboneras1992_0-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carboneras1992-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Its twittering calls and mewing are often only heard at night in the breeding colonies.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Audubon%27s_Shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Range and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Range_and_ecology">Range and ecology</span></h2>
<p>If not split into several species, Audubon's Shearwater ranges across the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> north to the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a>, throughout the northwest and central <a href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific" class="mw-redirect">Pacific</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, and parts of the eastern <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic" class="mw-redirect">Atlantic</a>. It is a species of <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical</a> waters; only some Atlantic populations and Bannerman's Shearwater of the <a href="/wiki/Ogasawara_Islands" title="Ogasawara Islands" class="mw-redirect">Ogasawara Islands</a> occur farther north. Unlike the larger <a href="/wiki/Shearwater" title="Shearwater">shearwaters</a>, adult Audubon's Shearwaters are not thought to wander much or undertake great migrations, although their young birds do so before breeding, and western <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> birds may gather in large numbers at the <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwelling</a> zone in the Arabian Sea.<sup id="cite_ref-carboneras1992_0-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carboneras1992-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is adaptable as regards its preferred marine habitat; it can be found in <a href="/wiki/Pelagic" title="Pelagic" class="mw-redirect">pelagic</a>, offshore and inshore waters. It feeds in a variety of methods, mainly diving out of flight, plunging underwater from a swimming position, and picking up food less than a bill's length underwater while "pattering" as if it were walking across the waves. It eats small <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">planktonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>. Unlike other shearwaters, it is not commonly a ship-follower, though it may attend small fishing boats; it is also sometimes met with as part of a <a href="/wiki/Mixed-species_feeding_flock" title="Mixed-species feeding flock" class="mw-redirect">mixed-species feeding flock</a>.&#160;<sup id="cite_ref-carboneras1992_0-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carboneras1992-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Audubon%27s_Shearwater_Chick.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Audubon%27s_Shearwater_Chick.jpg/220px-Audubon%27s_Shearwater_Chick.jpg" width="220" height="133" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Audubon%27s_Shearwater_Chick.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>P. l. lherminieri</i> chick in nest burrow on <a href="/wiki/Little_Tobago" title="Little Tobago">Little Tobago</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The species is <a href="/wiki/Seabird#Seabird_colonies" title="Seabird">colonial</a>, nesting in small burrows and crevices in rocks and on earthy slopes on <a href="/wiki/Atoll" title="Atoll">atolls</a> and rocky islets. The breeding season varies according to location and subspecies, but how precisely is not very well-studied.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Both parents share the responsibility of incubating the single white egg (measurements of 52.5 by 36.2&#160;mm and a weight of 37 g have been recorded for one specimen of average size<sup id="cite_ref-em2001_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-em2001-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup>), each incubating for periods of 2 to 10 days until the egg hatches after 49–51 days of incubation. The nestlings are brooded for half a week to one week, after which time the parents will leave it mostly alone in the burrow and spend most of their time foraging and feeding their voracious offspring, which become very fat. Time from hatching to <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledging</a> is 62–75 days. Audubon's Shearwaters take about 8 years to reach breeding age. As typical for <a href="/wiki/Procellariiformes" title="Procellariiformes">Procellariiformes</a> they are long-lived for their size, one bird <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">ringed</a> as an adult was still alive 11 years later; it must have been more than 15 years old at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-carboneras1992_0-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carboneras1992-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>While some small populations are threatened, the species as a whole (in the present sense, i.e. unsplit) is not considered to be globally threatened.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Audubon%27s_Shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Systematics">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Systematics">Systematics</span></h2>
<p>Audubon's Shearwater belongs to the <i>Puffinus sensu stricto</i> group of mid-sized and small shearwaters, which is related to the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Calonectris" title="Calonectris">Calonectris</a></i>. The <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy" title="Taxonomy">taxonomy</a> of this species is extremely confusing. It is occasionally listed as a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of <i>P. assimilis</i> (the <a href="/wiki/Little_Shearwater" title="Little Shearwater">Little Shearwater</a>), but they do not appear to be that closely related. Rather, <i>P. lherminieri</i> seems to belong to an ill-resolved <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> also including such species as the Little Shearwater, the <a href="/wiki/Manx_Shearwater" title="Manx Shearwater">Manx Shearwater</a> (<i>P. puffinus</i>) or the <a href="/wiki/Black-vented_Shearwater" title="Black-vented Shearwater">Black-vented Shearwater</a> (<i>P. opisthomelas</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The little-known <a href="/wiki/Heinroth%27s_Shearwater" title="Heinroth's Shearwater">Heinroth's Shearwater</a> (<i>P. heinrothi</i>) is sometimes considered a subspecies of either Audubon's or the Little Shearwater. Though it is likely to be another member of that close-knit group, its actual relationships remain uncertain due to lack of specimens.</p>
<p>Audubon's Shearwater itself has around 10 subspecies. Several have at one time or another been suggested to constitute separate species. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_Islands" title="Galápagos Islands">Galápagos Islands</a> population has turned out to be a very distinct species, the <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_Shearwater" title="Galápagos Shearwater">Galápagos Shearwater</a> (<i>P. subalaris</i>); it is apparently related to the <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Shearwater" title="Christmas Shearwater">Christmas Shearwater</a> (<i>P. nativitatis</i>) and together with it constitutes an ancient lineage without other close relatives in the genus.<sup id="cite_ref-austinetal2004_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-austinetal2004-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Other taxa were initially assigned to the Little Shearwater and later moved to Audubon's. Analysis of <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA" class="mw-redirect">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome <i>b</i></a> <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">sequence</a> data<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> – which is of somewhat limited value in procellariiform birds however<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> – indicates that at least 3 major <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clades</a> can be distinguished:</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Audubon%27s_Shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The lherminieri clade (Atlantic Ocean, Caribbean)">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="The_lherminieri_clade_.28Atlantic_Ocean.2C_Caribbean.29">The <i>lherminieri</i> clade (Atlantic Ocean, Caribbean)</span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i>Puffinus lherminieri lherminieri</i> <small>Lesson, 1839</small> – breeds throughout the Caribbean, on the <a href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas" class="mw-redirect">Bahamas</a> and formerly on <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>; ranges throughout the Caribbean and up the North American Atlantic coast up to southern <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, with vagrants having been recorded off northeastern Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-carboneras1992_0-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carboneras1992-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> A small breeding colony found in 1993 in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Itatiaia_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Itatiaia Islands (page does not exist)">Itatiaia Islands</a> off <a href="/wiki/Vila_Velha" title="Vila Velha">Vila Velha</a> (<a href="/wiki/Esp%C3%ADrito_Santo" title="Espírito Santo">Espírito Santo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>) probably belongs to this subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-em2001_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-em2001-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Includes <i>loyemilleri</i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Barolo_Shearwater" title="Barolo Shearwater">Barolo Shearwater</a>, <i>P. baroli</i> <small>(Bonaparte, 1857)</small> – breeds on <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> (E Atlantic); ranges throughout E Atlantic around (but mostly north of) the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">Tropic of Cancer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-carboneras1992_0-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carboneras1992-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Boyd%27s_Shearwater" title="Boyd's Shearwater">Boyd's Shearwater</a>, <i>Puffinus boydi</i> <small>Mathews, 1912</small> – breeds on <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_Islands" title="Cape Verde Islands" class="mw-redirect">Cape Verde Islands</a> (E Atlantic); ranges throughout E Atlantic around (but mostly south of) the Tropic of Cancer.<sup id="cite_ref-carboneras1992_0-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carboneras1992-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The former two have more white on the face and bluish feet like the Little Shearwater, with which they were formerly placed<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Many taxonomists consider both Boyd's and Barolo Shearwater distinct species, depending on whether <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphological</a> differences or the genetic similarity are considered more significant, and what other lineages are considered distinct from <i>P. lherminieri</i> in a particular treatment. The southern Caribbean birds were separated as <i>P. l. loyemilleri</i>, but are not distinct.<sup id="cite_ref-austinetal2004_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-austinetal2004-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Audubon%27s_Shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The persicus clade (West Indian Ocean)">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="The_persicus_clade_.28West_Indian_Ocean.29">The <i>persicus</i> clade (West Indian Ocean)</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PuffinusPersicusSmith.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/PuffinusPersicusSmith.png/220px-PuffinusPersicusSmith.png" width="220" height="134" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PuffinusPersicusSmith.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>Puffinus lherminieri persicus</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li><i>Puffinus lherminieri persicus</i> <small>Hume, 1837</small> – breeds on <a href="/wiki/Khuriya_Muriya_Islands" title="Khuriya Muriya Islands">Khuriya Muriya Islands</a> (<a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a>); ranges throughout the Arabian Sea.<sup id="cite_ref-carboneras1992_0-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carboneras1992-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>Puffinus lherminieri temptator</i> <small>Louette &amp; Herremans, 1985</small> – breeds on <a href="/wiki/Moh%C3%A9li" title="Mohéli">Mohéli</a> (<a href="/wiki/Comoros" title="Comoros">Comoros</a>); ranges in W Indian Ocean around the northern end of <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-carboneras1992_0-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carboneras1992-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>These form another distinct clade as indicated by mtDNA sequences, and have for some time been proposed as a distinct species, <b>Persian Shearwater</b> (<i>P. persicus</i>). From the molecular data alone, this seems fairly warranted, but the ranges of the two taxa are quite far apart, separated by forms of the third clade. It is quite obvious that on the basis of such contradicting data as presently available, no decision can be taken regarding the taxonomic status of these birds. Possibly, they do form a distinct species separated from the third clade by a different <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/circannual" class="extiw" title="wikt:circannual">circannual</a> rhythm, as is known from other procellariiform birds. They are <a href="/wiki/Phenotypical" title="Phenotypical" class="mw-redirect">phenotypically</a> distinct, with a longer bill, a dark rump, and more extensive dark on the underwing, including some mottling in the normally white area.</p>
<p>If <i>P. bailloni</i> is accepted as a distinct species but <i>P. persicus</i> is not, then this latter group would have to be included in <i>P. bailloni</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-austinetal2004_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-austinetal2004-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Audubon%27s_Shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The bailloni clade (Indian and Pacific oceans)">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="The_bailloni_clade_.28Indian_and_Pacific_oceans.29">The <i>bailloni</i> clade (Indian and Pacific oceans)</span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i>Puffinus lherminieri bailloni</i> <small>(Bonaparte, 1857)</small> – breeds on <a href="/wiki/Mascarene_Islands" title="Mascarene Islands">Mascarene Islands</a> (SW Indian Ocean); ranges throughout the SW Indian Ocean to the north of the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">Tropic of Capricorn</a>, and vagrant birds seen off <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> probably belong to this subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-carboneras1992_0-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carboneras1992-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Includes <i>atrodorsalis</i>.</li>
<li><i>Puffinus lherminieri dichrous</i> <small>Finsch &amp; Hartlaub, 1867</small> – breeds throughout central <a href="/wiki/Polynesia" title="Polynesia">Polynesia</a> and possibly <a href="/wiki/Melanesia" title="Melanesia">Melanesia</a> (Pacific) and the NW Indian Ocean up to the Arabian Sea; ranges throughout the W Indian Ocean around the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">Equator</a>, and in the C Pacific from the equatorial region to the Tropic of Capricorn.<sup id="cite_ref-carboneras1992_0-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carboneras1992-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Includes <i>colstoni</i>, <i>nicolae</i>, <i>polynesiae</i> and maybe <i>gunax</i>; vagrants seen off <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> could belong to <i>dichrous</i> or <i>gunax</i> (if valid), while vagrants recorded from <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rota_(island)" title="Rota (island)">Rota</a> (<a href="/wiki/Marianas" title="Marianas" class="mw-redirect">Marianas</a>) might be <i>dichrous</i> or <i>bannermani</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-wilesetal2000_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wilesetal2000-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>This group is the most confusing of all. The subspecies <i>dichrous</i> occurs in two areas which appear to be separated by the whole of <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> and the seas surrounding it; the Pacific subpopulation includes the proposed subspecies <i>polynesiae</i> (<a href="/wiki/Ta%E2%80%98%C5%AB" title="Ta‘ū" class="mw-redirect">Ta‘ū</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Samoa" title="American Samoa">American Samoa</a>) and possibly <i>gunax</i> (see below), whereas the geographically separated Indian Ocean subpopulation contains the birds formerly separated as <i>nicolae</i> (NW Indian Ocean, from Aldabra to the <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a>) and <i>colstoni</i> (<a href="/wiki/Aldabra" title="Aldabra">Aldabra</a>, Arabian Sea).</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:P%C3%A9trel_de_Barau.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/P%C3%A9trel_de_Barau.jpg/220px-P%C3%A9trel_de_Barau.jpg" width="220" height="168" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:P%C3%A9trel_de_Barau.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Audubon's Shearwater <i>Puffinus lherminieri bailloni</i> of reunion island</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There appear to be no significant genetical or morphological differences between these birds, which is quite amazing given that the Pacific and Indian Ocean subpopulations must have been isolated for a fairly long time, and that no less than three unequivocally distinct subspecies (<i>bailloni</i>, <i>persicus</i> and <i>temptator</i>) occur within the range of Indian Ocean <i>dichrous</i>. On the other hand, the supposed species <b>Mascarene Shearwater</b> ("<i>P. atrodorsalis</i>") is inseparable morphologically and genetically from <i>bailloni</i>.</p>
<p>Clearly, some mechanism blocking <a href="/wiki/Gene_flow" title="Gene flow">gene flow</a> is at work, but what this is exactly remains unknown – though as remarked above, separate breeding seasons seem a reasonable assumption and are tentatively supported by the available field data.<sup id="cite_ref-carboneras1992_0-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carboneras1992-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition, it is entirely mysterious why such a mechanism should apply in the rather limited and ecologically homogenous northwestern Indian Ocean range, but not in the ecologically more diverse and by far larger Pacific range of <i>dichrous</i>.</p>
<p>These unresolved problems nonwithstanding, this clade – possibly including the preceding one – has been proposed to constitute a separate species, the <b>Tropical Shearwater</b> or <b>Baillon's Shearwater</b>, <i>Puffinus bailloni</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-austinetal2004_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-austinetal2004-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Audubon%27s_Shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Undetermined">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Undetermined">Undetermined</span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i>Puffinus lherminieri bannermani</i> <small>Mathews &amp; Iredale, 1915</small> – breeds on <a href="/wiki/Ogasawara_Islands" title="Ogasawara Islands" class="mw-redirect">Ogasawara Islands</a> (NW Pacific); ranges throughout the NW Pacific from <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> waters to the equatorial region.<sup id="cite_ref-carboneras1992_0-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carboneras1992-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Vagrants recorded from <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rota_(island)" title="Rota (island)">Rota</a> (<a href="/wiki/Marianas" title="Marianas" class="mw-redirect">Marianas</a>) might be <i>dichrous</i> or <i>bannermani</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-wilesetal2000_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wilesetal2000-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>Puffinus lherminieri gunax</i> <small>Mathews, 1930</small> – breeds on <a href="/wiki/Banks_Islands" title="Banks Islands">Banks Islands</a> of <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a> (SW Pacific); ranges throughout the SW Pacific between the equatorial region and the Tropic of Capricorn.<sup id="cite_ref-carboneras1992_0-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carboneras1992-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Might belong in <i>dichrous</i>; vagrants seen off <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> could belong to either <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxon</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>These taxa could not be included in the most recent studies due to lack of material. The case of <i>gunax</i> seems fairly straightforward – as certainly as this can possibly be said in the absence of new data, it belongs to the <i>bailloni</i> clade either as a distinct subspecies, or, more likely, as yet another synonym of <i>dichrous</i>.</p>
<p>The case of the more distinct <i>bannermani</i>, the range of which is <a href="/wiki/Parapatric" title="Parapatric" class="mw-redirect">parapatric</a> to that of the Pacific <i>dichrous</i>, is more complicated. It has for some time<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> been proposed as a distinct species, <b>Bannerman's Shearwater</b> (<i>P. bannermani</i>). In the absence of more recent data to investigate this claim, its status continues to be altogether unresolved, though the case for it being at least a distinct subspecies in the <i>bailloni</i> clade seems good.</p>
