<p>The <b>common grasshopper warbler</b> (<i>Locustella naevia</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">Old World warbler</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Grass_warbler" title="Grass warbler">grass warbler</a> genus <i>Locustella</i>. It breeds across much of temperate Europe and the western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in north and west Africa.
</p><p>This small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> is found in short dense vegetation, often close to water. It is a medium-sized warbler about 13&#160;cm (5&#160;in) long. The adult has a streaked brown back and whitish grey underparts which are unstreaked except on the undertail coverts. The sexes are identical, as with most warblers, but young birds are yellower below. Like most warblers, it is <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a>. Four to seven <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> are laid in a nest on or near the ground in thick vegetation or in a <a href="/wiki/Bunch_grass" class="mw-redirect" title="Bunch grass">tussock of grass</a>.
</p><p>This is a species which skulks in the undergrowth, creeping through bushes and low foliage, and which is very difficult to see except sometimes when singing from a prominent position. The song, which gives this species its name, is a monotonous mechanical <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insect-like</a> reeling, often given at dawn or dusk.
</p><p><br />
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_grasshopper_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Italian naturalist <a href="/wiki/Ulisse_Aldrovandi" title="Ulisse Aldrovandi">Ulisse Aldrovandi</a> included the common grasshopper warbler in the second volume of his <i>Ornithologiae</i>. Aldrovandi died in 1605 but the volume was not published until 1637.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In 1760 the French zoologist <a href="/wiki/Mathurin_Jacques_Brisson" title="Mathurin Jacques Brisson">Mathurin Jacques Brisson</a> included a detailed description of the common grasshopper warbler in his <i>Ornithologie</i>. He used the French name <i>La fauvette tachetée</i> and the Latin name <i>Curruca naevia</i> but although Brisson coined Latin names, these do not conform to the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial system</a> and are not recognised by the <a href="/wiki/International_Commission_on_Zoological_Nomenclature" title="International Commission on Zoological Nomenclature">International Commission on Zoological Nomenclature</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-allen_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allen-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The common grasshopper warbler was described by the French polymath <a href="/wiki/Georges-Louis_Leclerc,_Comte_de_Buffon" title="Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon">Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon</a> in 1779 in his <i><a href="/wiki/Histoire_Naturelle" title="Histoire Naturelle">Histoire Naturelle des Oiseaux</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The bird was also illustrated in a hand-coloured plate engraved by <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Nicolas_Martinet" title="François-Nicolas Martinet">François-Nicolas Martinet</a> in the <i>Planches Enluminées D'Histoire Naturelle</i> which was produced under the supervision of <a href="/wiki/Edme-Louis_Daubenton" title="Edme-Louis Daubenton">Edme-Louis Daubenton</a> to accompany Buffon's text.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Neither the plate caption nor Buffon's description included a scientific name but in 1783 the Dutch naturalist <a href="/wiki/Pieter_Boddaert" title="Pieter Boddaert">Pieter Boddaert</a> coined the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> <i>Motacilla naevia</i> in his catalogue of the <i>Planches Enluminées</i>; he used the word <i>naevia</i> that had earlier been used by Brisson.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> 
The <a href="/wiki/Type_locality_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Type locality (biology)">type locality</a> is <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a> in Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The common grasshopper warbler is now one of 20 species placed in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Locustella" class="mw-redirect" title="Locustella">Locustella</a></i> that was introduced by the German naturalist <a href="/wiki/Johann_Jakob_Kaup" title="Johann Jakob Kaup">Johann Jakob Kaup</a> in 1829 with the common grasshopper warbler as the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ioc_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The genus name <i>Locustella</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and is a diminutive of <i>locusta</i>,  "grasshopper". The specific <i>naevia</i> is Latin for "spotted ".<sup id="cite_ref-job_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Three <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are recognised.<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>L. n. naevia</i> (<a href="/wiki/Pieter_Boddaert" title="Pieter Boddaert">Boddaert</a>, 1783) – Europe to west European Russia and Ukraine; wintering in West Africa</li>
<li><i>L. n. straminea</i> <a href="/wiki/Henry_Seebohm" title="Henry Seebohm">Seebohm</a>, 1881 – east European Russia to southwest, south central Siberia, east Kazakhstan, west Mongolia and northwest China; wintering in south Asia</li>
<li><i>L. n. obscurior</i> <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Buturlin" title="Sergei Buturlin">Buturlin</a>, 1929 – east Turkey and the Caucasus</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_grasshopper_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The adult common grasshopper warbler (the name is the <a href="/wiki/International_Ornithological_Congress" class="mw-redirect" title="International Ornithological Congress">IOC</a> recommended English name<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>) has a length of about 12.5&#160;cm (5&#160;in). It is a very secretive bird and seldom seen, but its presence is easily detected because of its characteristic song.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The upper-parts are pale olive-brown, each feather having a central darker brown streak. The cheeks are greyish, the irises are brown and there is a faint eye streak behind the eye. The upper mandible of the beak is dark brown and the lower mandible yellowish-brown. The underparts are cream-coloured or yellowish-buff with a few dark brown spots and streaks on the breast and flanks. The wings are brown with the outer edge of the feathers rimmed with paler brown. The tail feathers are reddish-brown with faint transverse bars being visible in some individuals and the under-tail <a href="/wiki/Covert_feather" title="Covert feather">coverts</a> are streaked. The slender legs and the feet are pale yellowish-brown.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The song is an unmusical long, high-pitched reeling trill performed with beak held wide open and the whole body vibrating. It lasts for from a few seconds to two or three minutes with hardly a pause for breath. It varies in volume from a faint hum to a sound resembling a distant mowing machine. It is performed at any time of day from early morning until after the sun has set and is constantly to be heard from the arrival of the bird in spring until late July. The alarm call is a repeated ticking noise that has been rendered as "twkit-twkit-twkit".<sup id="cite_ref-Coward_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The song is similar to that of its congeners: the <a href="/wiki/Lanceolated_warbler" title="Lanceolated warbler">lanceolated warbler</a> (<i>Locustella lanceolata</i>), <a href="/wiki/Savi%27s_warbler" title="Savi&#39;s warbler">Savi's warbler</a> (<i>Locustella luscinioides</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/River_warbler" title="River warbler">river warbler</a> (<i>Locustella fluviatilis</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_grasshopper_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common grasshopper warbler breeds in north west <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and the western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>. The range includes <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, central <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltic States">Baltic States</a> and western parts of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. Further east it is replaced by related species. In late summer, it migrates to north west <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> where it overwinters.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the breeding season, the common grasshopper warbler is found in damp or dry places with rough grass and bushes such as the edges of fens, clearings, neglected hedgerows, heaths, upland moors, gorse-covered areas, young plantations and felled woodland. In the winter, it is usually found in similar locations but information is scarce on its behaviour and habitat at this time.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_grasshopper_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Grasshopper_Warbler_Locustella_naevia_by_Dr._Raju_Kasambe.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Grasshopper_Warbler_Locustella_naevia_by_Dr._Raju_Kasambe.jpg/220px-Grasshopper_Warbler_Locustella_naevia_by_Dr._Raju_Kasambe.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Grasshopper_Warbler_Locustella_naevia_by_Dr._Raju_Kasambe.jpg/330px-Grasshopper_Warbler_Locustella_naevia_by_Dr._Raju_Kasambe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Grasshopper_Warbler_Locustella_naevia_by_Dr._Raju_Kasambe.jpg/440px-Grasshopper_Warbler_Locustella_naevia_by_Dr._Raju_Kasambe.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1082" data-file-height="730" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Grasshopper_Warbler_Locustella_naevia_by_Dr._Raju_Kasambe.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Grasshopper Warbler (<i>Locustella naevia</i>)</div></div></div>
<p>This bird seldom takes to the wing but spends its time scurrying through dense vegetation, flitting from twig to twig or running along the ground. It has a peculiar high-stepping gait and long stride as it moves along horizontal stems, looking slender and tapering. It seldom flies, soon diving back into cover, and when it alights it often raises and flares its tail to show its streaked under-tail coverts. It has been known to feign injury in order to distract a potential <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predator</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_grasshopper_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Locustella_naevia_MWNH_1685.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Locustella_naevia_MWNH_1685.JPG/220px-Locustella_naevia_MWNH_1685.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="196" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Locustella_naevia_MWNH_1685.JPG/330px-Locustella_naevia_MWNH_1685.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Locustella_naevia_MWNH_1685.JPG/440px-Locustella_naevia_MWNH_1685.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2496" data-file-height="2220" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Locustella_naevia_MWNH_1685.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a>, Germany</div></div></div>
<p>Male common grasshopper warblers try to attract females by displaying to them. They walk or run along twigs with tail spread, fluttering their wings as they raise and lower them, often carrying a grass or leaf in their beak. In the air, with wings well extended and fluttering, they spread their tail and fluff their feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Both sexes take part in nest-building. The nest is well-concealed and built close to the ground in such places as grass tussocks, <a href="/wiki/Ulex_europaeus" title="Ulex europaeus">gorse</a> bushes, <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">osier</a> beds, <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed beds</a>, tangled hedgerows, scrub and among coarse <a href="/wiki/Calluna" title="Calluna">heather</a> plants on moorland. It varies in size and shape but is constructed of <a href="/wiki/Grass" class="mw-redirect" title="Grass">grasses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyperaceae" title="Cyperaceae">sedges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">mosses</a> and often lined with fine grasses. A clutch of four to six eggs is laid. These are creamy white speckled with fine reddish spots, usually randomly distributed but sometimes merged into blotches or zones.<sup id="cite_ref-Coward_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The eggs measure 18 by 14 millimetres (0.71 by 0.55&#160;in) and weigh about 1.7&#160;g (0.06&#160;oz). Both parents are involved in <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation" title="Egg incubation">incubating</a> the eggs which takes about fourteen days. The chicks are <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a> and are fed on insects. They <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> in twelve to thirteen days and there are usually two broods in the season. Young birds become mature at a year old and the highest recorded age for this species is five years.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_feeding">Food and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_grasshopper_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Food and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The common grasshopper warbler is <a href="/wiki/Insectivore" title="Insectivore">insectivorous</a>, feeding on a wide range of invertebrates. Its diet includes <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">aphids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dragonfly" title="Dragonfly">dragonflies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mayfly" title="Mayfly">mayflies</a> and their <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a>. <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">Spiders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woodlouse" title="Woodlouse">woodlice</a> are also eaten and the chicks are fed on aphids, green <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a>, woodlice and flies.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_12-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_grasshopper_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common grasshopper warbler is assessed by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> in their <a href="/wiki/Red_List_of_Threatened_Species" class="mw-redirect" title="Red List of Threatened Species">Red List of Threatened Species</a> as being of "<a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>". This is because it has a large total population and an extensive range. The population in Europe is estimated to be between 840 thousand and 2.2 million breeding pairs with a total of 2.52 to 6.6 million individuals. As Europe amounts to about two thirds of its total range, the world population is estimated to be in the region of 3.41 to 13.2 million individuals. The total number of birds may be on the decline because of habitat loss, but not to an extent that would warrant listing the bird under a higher risk category.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In a study examining the possible <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Effects of global warming">effects of global warming</a> on the range of various species of bird, it was estimated that the breeding range of the common grasshopper warbler would be displaced several hundred miles northwards and would cover the British Isles and the whole of Scandinavia but that it would cease to breed in much of its present range in mainland Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
