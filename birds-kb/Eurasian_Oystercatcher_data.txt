<p>The <b>Eurasian oystercatcher</b> (<i>Haematopus ostralegus</i>) also known as the <b>common pied oystercatcher</b>, or <b>palaearctic oystercatcher</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> or (in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>) just <b>oystercatcher</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Oystercatcher" title="Oystercatcher">oystercatcher</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> family Haematopodidae. It is the most widespread of the oystercatchers, with three races breeding in western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, central <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurosiberia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kamchatka_Peninsula" title="Kamchatka Peninsula">Kamchatka</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, and the western coast of <a href="/wiki/Korean_Peninsula" title="Korean Peninsula">Korea</a>. No other oystercatcher occurs within this area. The extinct <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands_oystercatcher" title="Canary Islands oystercatcher">Canary Islands oystercatcher</a> (<i>Haematopus meadewaldoi</i>), formerly considered a distinct species, may have actually been an isolated subspecies or distinct population of the Eurasian oystercatcher.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This oystercatcher is the national bird of the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_oystercatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Haematopus_ostralegus_1,_Isle_of_Skye,_Scotland_-_Diliff.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Haematopus_ostralegus_1%2C_Isle_of_Skye%2C_Scotland_-_Diliff.jpg/220px-Haematopus_ostralegus_1%2C_Isle_of_Skye%2C_Scotland_-_Diliff.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="209" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Haematopus_ostralegus_1%2C_Isle_of_Skye%2C_Scotland_-_Diliff.jpg/330px-Haematopus_ostralegus_1%2C_Isle_of_Skye%2C_Scotland_-_Diliff.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Haematopus_ostralegus_1%2C_Isle_of_Skye%2C_Scotland_-_Diliff.jpg/440px-Haematopus_ostralegus_1%2C_Isle_of_Skye%2C_Scotland_-_Diliff.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1800" data-file-height="1714" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Haematopus_ostralegus_1,_Isle_of_Skye,_Scotland_-_Diliff.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eurasian oystercatcher flying on Loch Sligachan on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Skye" title="Isle of Skye">Isle of Skye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a></div></div></div>
<p>The oystercatcher is one of the largest <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">waders</a> in the region. It is 40–45&#160;cm (16–18&#160;in) long, the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> accounting for 8–9&#160;cm (3–<span class="frac nowrap">3<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span>&#160;in), and has a wingspan of 80–85&#160;cm (31–33&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> They are obvious and noisy <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">plover-like</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, with black and white <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>, red legs and strong broad red bills used for smashing or prising open <a href="/wiki/Mollusc" class="mw-redirect" title="Mollusc">molluscs</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Mussel" title="Mussel">mussels</a> or for finding <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Despite its name, oysters do not form a large part of its diet. The bird still lives up to its name, as few if any other wading birds are capable of opening oysters at all.
</p><p>This oystercatcher is unmistakable in flight, with white patches on the wings and tail, otherwise black upperparts, and white underparts. Young birds are more brown, have a white neck collar and a duller bill. The call is a distinctive loud piping.
</p><p>The bill shape varies; oystercatchers with broad bill tips open molluscs by prising them apart or hammering through the shell, whereas pointed-bill birds dig up worms. Much of this is due to the wear resulting from feeding on the prey. Individual birds specialise in one technique or the other which they learn from their parents.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_oystercatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are three <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>: the nominate <i>ostralegus</i> found in Europe and the coasts of eastern Europe, <i>longipes</i> from Central Asia and Russia, and <i>osculans</i> found from Kamchatka in the Russian Far East and northern parts of China. The extinct Canarian oystercatcher from the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> may have represented a fourth subspecies, <i>meadewoldi</i>.
</p><p>Bill length shows clinal variation with an increase from west to east. The subspecies <i>longipes</i> has distinctly brownish upperparts and the nasal groove extends more than halfway along the bill. In the subspecies <i>ostralegus</i> the nasal groove stops short of the half-way mark. The <i>osculans</i> subspecies lacks white on the shafts of the outer 2–3 primaries and has no white on the outer webs of the outer five primaries.<sup id="cite_ref-Hayman1986_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hayman1986-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_oystercatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Haematopus_ostralegus_MHNT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Haematopus_ostralegus_MHNT.jpg/220px-Haematopus_ostralegus_MHNT.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="270" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Haematopus_ostralegus_MHNT.jpg/330px-Haematopus_ostralegus_MHNT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Haematopus_ostralegus_MHNT.jpg/440px-Haematopus_ostralegus_MHNT.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4159" data-file-height="5097" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Haematopus_ostralegus_MHNT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg - <a href="/wiki/MHNT" class="mw-redirect" title="MHNT">MHNT</a></div></div></div>
<p>The oystercatcher is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> species over most of its range. The European population breeds mainly in northern Europe, but in winter the birds can be found in north <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and southern parts of Europe. Although the species is present all year in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and the adjacent European coasts, there is still migratory movement: the large flocks that are found in the estuaries of south-west England in winter mainly breed in northern England or <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. Similar movements are shown by the Asian populations. The birds are highly gregarious outside the breeding season.
</p><p>The nest is a bare scrape on pebbles, on the coast or on inland gravelly islands. 2–4 eggs are laid. Both eggs and chicks are highly <a href="/wiki/Crypsis" title="Crypsis">cryptic</a>.
</p><p>Because of its large numbers and readily identified behaviour, the oystercatcher is an important <a href="/wiki/Indicator_species" class="mw-redirect" title="Indicator species">indicator species</a> for the health of the ecosystems where it congregates. Extensive long-term studies have been carried out on its <a href="/wiki/Foraging" title="Foraging">foraging</a> behaviour, in northern <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and particularly on the <a href="/wiki/River_Exe" title="River Exe">River Exe</a> estuary in south-west <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> These studies form an important part of the foundation for the modern discipline of <a href="/wiki/Behavioural_ecology" class="mw-redirect" title="Behavioural ecology">behavioural ecology</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_oystercatcher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Scientific_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific name">scientific name</a> <i>Haematopus ostralegus</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> <i>haima</i> <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B1%E1%BC%B3%CE%BC%CE%B1" class="extiw" title="wikt:αἳμα">αἳμα</a> (blood), <i>pous</i> <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%80%CE%BF%CF%8D%CF%82" class="extiw" title="wikt:πούς">πούς</a> (foot) and the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>ostrea</i> (oyster) and <i>legere</i> (to collect or pick).<sup id="cite_ref-job_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The name <i>oystercatcher</i> was coined by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Catesby" title="Mark Catesby">Mark Catesby</a> in 1731 as a <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">common name</a> for the <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> species <i>H. palliatus,</i> described as eating <a href="/wiki/Oyster" title="Oyster">oysters</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODBBN_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODBBN-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_Yarrell" title="William Yarrell">Yarrell</a> in 1843 established this as the preferred term, replacing the older name <i>Sea Pie.</i><sup id="cite_ref-ODBBN_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODBBN-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
