<p>The <b>yellowhammer</b> (<i>Emberiza citrinella</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bunting_(bird)" title="Bunting (bird)">bunting</a> family that is native to <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurasia</a> and has been <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced</a> to New Zealand and Australia. Most European birds remain in the breeding range year-round, but the eastern <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> is partially <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, with much of the population wintering further south. The male yellowhammer has a bright yellow head, streaked brown back, chestnut rump, and yellow under parts. Other plumages are duller versions of the same pattern. The yellowhammer is common in open areas with some shrubs or trees, and forms small flocks in winter. Its song has a rhythm like "A little bit of bread and no cheese". The song is very similar to that of its closest relative, the <a href="/wiki/Pine_bunting" title="Pine bunting">pine bunting</a>, with which it interbreeds.
</p><p>Breeding commences mainly in April and May, with the female building a lined <a href="/wiki/Cup_nest" class="mw-redirect" title="Cup nest">cup nest</a> in a concealed location on or near the ground. The three to five eggs are patterned with a mesh of fine dark lines, giving rise to the old name for the bird of "scribble lark" or "writing lark". The female incubates the eggs for 12–14&#160;days prior to hatching, and broods the <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a> downy chicks until they <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> 11–13&#160;days later. Both adults feed the chick in the nest and raise two or three broods each year. The nest may be raided by rodents or <a href="/wiki/Corvidae" title="Corvidae">corvids</a>, and the adults are hunted by <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">birds of prey</a>. Yellowhammers feed on the ground, usually in flocks outside the breeding season. Their diet is mainly seeds, supplemented by <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> in the breeding season. Changes to agricultural practices have led to population declines in western Europe, but its large numbers and huge range mean that the yellowhammer is classed as being of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a> by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a>.
</p><p>This conspicuous yellow bird has inspired poems by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Burns" title="Robert Burns">Robert Burns</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Clare" title="John Clare">John Clare</a>, and its characteristic song has influenced musical works by <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Beethoven</a> and <a href="/wiki/Olivier_Messiaen" title="Olivier Messiaen">Messiaen</a>. Children's writer <a href="/wiki/Enid_Blyton" title="Enid Blyton">Enid Blyton</a> helped to popularise the standard English representation of the song.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellowhammer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The bird family <a href="/wiki/Emberizidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Emberizidae">Emberizidae</a> contains around 300 seed-eating species, the majority of which are found in the Americas, although the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Emberiza" class="mw-redirect" title="Emberiza">Emberiza</a></i>, with more than 40 members, is confined to the Old World.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Within its genus, the yellowhammer is most closely related to the pine bunting, with which it forms a <a href="/wiki/Superspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Superspecies">superspecies</a>; they have at times been considered as one species. The <a href="/wiki/White-capped_bunting" title="White-capped bunting">white-capped</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cirl_bunting" title="Cirl bunting">cirl buntings</a> are also near relatives of the species pair.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Where their ranges meet, the yellowhammer and pine bunting interbreed; the yellowhammer is dominant, and the hybrid zone is moving further east.<sup id="cite_ref-tieze_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tieze-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The yellowhammer was described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his landmark 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a> under its current scientific name.<sup id="cite_ref-Linn_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linn-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <i>Emberiza</i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old German</a> <i>Embritz</i>, a bunting,<sup id="cite_ref-job145_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job145-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and <i>citrinella</i> is the Italian for a small yellow bird.<sup id="cite_ref-job110_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job110-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The English name is thought to have come from <i>Ammer</i>, another German word for a bunting, and was first recorded in 1553 as <i>yelambre</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellowhammer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are currently 3 recognised subspecies of yellowhammer:<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>E. c. citrinella</i> (Linnaeus, 1758), the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate subspecies</a>, which occurs in southeast England and most of Europe east to the northwestern corner of Russia and western Ukraine.</li>
<li><i>E. c. caliginosa</i> (<a href="/wiki/Phillip_Clancey" title="Phillip Clancey">Clancey</a>, 1940) is the form found in Ireland, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, and Great Britain (except southeast England).</li>
<li><i>E. c. erythrogenys</i> (<a href="/wiki/Christian_Ludwig_Brehm" title="Christian Ludwig Brehm">Brehm</a>, 1855) breeds from Russia, central Ukraine and the eastern <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a> eastwards to <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> and northwest Mongolia, and also has isolated populations to the east of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellowhammer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Emberiza_citrinella_-New_Zealand_-North_Island-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Emberiza_citrinella_-New_Zealand_-North_Island-8.jpg/220px-Emberiza_citrinella_-New_Zealand_-North_Island-8.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Emberiza_citrinella_-New_Zealand_-North_Island-8.jpg/330px-Emberiza_citrinella_-New_Zealand_-North_Island-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Emberiza_citrinella_-New_Zealand_-North_Island-8.jpg/440px-Emberiza_citrinella_-New_Zealand_-North_Island-8.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="683" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Emberiza_citrinella_-New_Zealand_-North_Island-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Male <i>E. c. caliginosa</i></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Emberiza_citrinella_LC229.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Emberiza_citrinella_LC229.jpg/170px-Emberiza_citrinella_LC229.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="204" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Emberiza_citrinella_LC229.jpg/255px-Emberiza_citrinella_LC229.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Emberiza_citrinella_LC229.jpg/340px-Emberiza_citrinella_LC229.jpg 2x" data-file-width="672" data-file-height="807" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Emberiza_citrinella_LC229.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Female</div></div></div>
<p>The yellowhammer is a large bunting, 16–16.5&#160;cm (6.3–6.5&#160;in) long, with a 23–29.5&#160;cm (9.1–11.6&#160;in) wingspan;<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> it weighs 20–36.5&#160;g (0.71–1.29&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The male of the nominate subspecies <i>E. c. citrinella</i> has a bright yellow head, heavily streaked brown back, <a href="/wiki/Rufous" title="Rufous">rufous</a> rump, yellow under parts, and white outer tail feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The female is less brightly coloured, and more streaked on the crown, breast, and flanks. Both sexes are less strongly marked outside the breeding season, when the dark fringes on new feathers obscure the yellow plumage. The juvenile is much duller and less yellow than the adults, and often has a paler rump.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsson_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsson-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After breeding, adults have a complete <a href="/wiki/Moulting" title="Moulting">moult</a>, which takes at least eight weeks; males acquire more yellow in the plumage each time they moult. Juveniles have a partial moult not long after fledging, replacing the head, body, and some <a href="/wiki/Covert_feather" title="Covert feather">covert feathers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsson_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsson-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Differences between the subspecies are small and <a href="/wiki/Cline_(biology)" title="Cline (biology)">geographically gradual</a>. On average, the male of <i>E. c. caliginosa </i> is slightly smaller and darker than the same sex of the nominate subspecies, and also has more streaking on its back, a greenish tint to the yellow of the head and more chestnut on the flanks. The male of the eastern form, <i>E. c. erythrogenys</i>, is paler and less streaked than <i>E. c. citrinella</i>. Its flanks, undertail and wing bars are usually whiter, and its crown and throat are brighter yellow.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsson_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsson-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Distinguishing females of the three subspecies using plumage features is not usually possible.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsson24_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsson24-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Females and juveniles, especially of the pale eastern subspecies, <i>E. c. erythrogenys</i>, may be confused with pine buntings, but they always have a yellow tint to their plumage, a paler rufous rump, and more uniform upperparts than that species.<sup id="cite_ref-Olssonpl11_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olssonpl11-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Young and female yellowhammers can be distinguished from cirl buntings by the grey-brown rump of the latter species.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsson_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsson-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Male hybrids with pine buntings are typically white-faced and have some yellow on the head, under parts or <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">flight feathers</a>, but females are usually indistinguishable from yellowhammers.<sup id="cite_ref-Porter_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Porter-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellowhammer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The song of the cock yellowhammer is a series of short notes, gradually increasing in volume and followed by one or two more protracted notes. It is often represented as "A little bit of bread and no cheese", and the full version can be confused with the almost identical song of the pine bunting. If the final notes are omitted, confusion with the cirl bunting is possible. Other vocalisations include a <i>zit</i> contact call, a <i>see</i> alarm, and a trilled <i>tirrr</i> given in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Yellowhammer males learn their songs from their fathers, and over time, regional dialects have developed,<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian2017_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian2017-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> with minor differences to the conclusion of the basic song; all are mutually recognised by birds from different areas.<sup id="cite_ref-tieze_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tieze-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Each male has an individual repertoire of song variants within its regional dialect;<sup id="cite_ref-caro_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-caro-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> females tend to mate with males that share their dialect, and prefer those with the largest repertoires.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The pine bunting and yellowhammer are so closely related that each responds to the other's song. The male yellowhammer's song is more attractive to females, and is one reason for the dominance of that species where the ranges overlap.<sup id="cite_ref-tieze_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tieze-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellowhammer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shavington_farmland.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Shavington_farmland.jpg/220px-Shavington_farmland.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Shavington_farmland.jpg/330px-Shavington_farmland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Shavington_farmland.jpg/440px-Shavington_farmland.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="807" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shavington_farmland.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Traditional farmland provides good habitat for nesting and feeding.</div></div></div>
<p>The yellowhammer breeds across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> between the 16–20&#160;°C (61–68&#160;°F) July <a href="/wiki/Isotherm_(contour_line)" class="mw-redirect" title="Isotherm (contour line)">isotherms</a>. It is the commonest and most widespread European bunting,<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> although it is absent from high mountains, <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> regions, the western Netherlands, most of <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberia</a> and Greece, and low-lying regions of other countries adjoining the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. It breeds in Russia east to <a href="/wiki/Irkutsk" title="Irkutsk">Irkutsk</a>, and in most of Ukraine. The Asian range extends into northwest Turkey, the Caucasus, and northern Kazakhstan.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsson_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsson-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most European yellowhammers winter within their breeding range, only the far north being vacated, although some birds move south of their breeding range in Spain, Italy, and other Mediterranean countries.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsson_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsson-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Distances travelled can be up to 500&#160;km (310&#160;mi) for northern birds.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Asian birds are more strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, deserting much of the north to winter in Iraq, Iran, and southern <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>. The yellowhammer has occurred as a vagrant in the United Arab Emirates, Egypt,<sup id="cite_ref-Olsson_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsson-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Kuwait, Morocco, Malta, the Himalayas (winter vagrant from northern Afghanistan to central Nepal<sup id="cite_ref-ras_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ras-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>), the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a>, Iceland, and the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Yellowhammers of the British and Irish race, <i>E. c. caliginosa</i>, were <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced</a> to New Zealand by local <a href="/wiki/Acclimatisation_society" title="Acclimatisation society">acclimatisation societies</a> in 1862,<sup id="cite_ref-tieze_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tieze-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Oliver_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oliver-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and soon spread over the main islands. They sometimes visit New Zealand's subantarctic islands, although rarely staying to breed, and have reached Australia's <a href="/wiki/Lord_Howe_Island" title="Lord Howe Island">Lord Howe Island</a> on a number of occasions. At the beginning of the 20th century, this bunting was seen as a serious agricultural pest in its adopted country.<sup id="cite_ref-Lever_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lever-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Populations of yellowhammer have also been introduced to the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a> and South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The yellowhammer is a bird of dry, open country, preferably with a range of vegetation types and some trees from which to sing. It is absent from urban areas, forests, and wetlands. Probably originally found at forest edges and large clearing, it has benefited from traditional agriculture, which created extensive open areas with hedges and clumps of trees.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellowhammer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellowhammer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Emberiza_citrinella_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.216_Le_Monetier05.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Emberiza_citrinella_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.216_Le_Monetier05.jpg/220px-Emberiza_citrinella_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.216_Le_Monetier05.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Emberiza_citrinella_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.216_Le_Monetier05.jpg/330px-Emberiza_citrinella_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.216_Le_Monetier05.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Emberiza_citrinella_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.216_Le_Monetier05.jpg/440px-Emberiza_citrinella_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.216_Le_Monetier05.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3372" data-file-height="3367" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Emberiza_citrinella_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.216_Le_Monetier05.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs at the <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9um_de_Toulouse" title="Muséum de Toulouse">Muséum de Toulouse</a>, France</div></div></div>
<p>Breeding normally starts in early May, but often in April in the south of the range. Yellowhammers are <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a> and breed when aged one year. The males establish territories along hedges or woodland fringes and sing from a tree or bush, often continuing well into July or August. The male displays to the female by raising his wings and running towards her.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The nest is built by the female on or near the ground, and is typically well hidden in tussocks, against a bank or low in a bush. It is constructed from nearby plant material, such as leaves, dry grass, and stalks, and is lined with fine grasses and sometimes animal hair.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsson_10-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsson-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It is 11.5–13&#160;cm (4.5–5.1&#160;in) across with a cup 4–4.5&#160;cm (1.6–1.8&#160;in) deep.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is usually three to five whitish eggs, typically patterned with a network of fine, dark lines. The eggs average 21&#160;mm ×&#160;16&#160;mm (0.83&#160;in ×&#160;0.63&#160;in) in size and weigh 2.9&#160;g (0.10&#160;oz), of which 6% is shell.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The female incubates the eggs for 12–14&#160;days to hatching, and broods the <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a>, downy chicks until they <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> 11–13&#160;days later. Both adults feed the chick in the nest and two or three broods are raised each year.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsson_10-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsson-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bto_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The adult annual survival rate in the UK is around 54%, and that for juveniles in their first year is 53%. The typical lifespan is three years,<sup id="cite_ref-bto_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> although records from Great Britain and Germany indicate birds surviving more than 13 years.<sup id="cite_ref-EURING_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EURING-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellowhammer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Foraging is mainly on the ground, and the bird's diet consists mainly of seeds. Oily seeds, such as those of <a href="/wiki/Brassicaceae" title="Brassicaceae">brassicas</a>, are ignored in favour of more starchy items. Typical food plants include <a href="/wiki/Urtica_dioica" title="Urtica dioica">common nettle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rumex" title="Rumex">docks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polygonum_aviculare" title="Polygonum aviculare">common knotgrass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chenopodium_album" title="Chenopodium album">fat hen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stellaria_media" title="Stellaria media">common chickweed</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Achillea_millefolium" title="Achillea millefolium">yarrow</a>. Grasses are also important, particularly <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereals</a>, and grain makes up a significant part of the food consumed in autumn and winter, wheat and oats being preferred to barley. When not breeding, yellowhammers forage in flocks that can occasionally number hundreds of birds, and often <a href="/wiki/Mixed-species_foraging_flock" title="Mixed-species foraging flock">contain</a> other buntings and <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finches</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The yellowhammer adds <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> to its diet in the breeding season, particularly as food for its growing chicks. A wide range of species is taken, including <a href="/wiki/Springtail" title="Springtail">springtails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spiders" class="mw-redirect" title="Spiders">spiders</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  During the first few days, chicks are exclusively fed invertebrate prey, but from day three they are also fed cereal grains, which the chicks can digest efficiently.  This is thought to be intentional by the parents to allow the nestlings to adjust their physiology to eating seed.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators_and_parasites">Predators and parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellowhammer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Predators and parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Accipiter_nisus_1_(Bohu%C5%A1_%C4%8C%C3%AD%C4%8Del).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Accipiter_nisus_1_%28Bohu%C5%A1_%C4%8C%C3%AD%C4%8Del%29.jpg/170px-Accipiter_nisus_1_%28Bohu%C5%A1_%C4%8C%C3%AD%C4%8Del%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Accipiter_nisus_1_%28Bohu%C5%A1_%C4%8C%C3%AD%C4%8Del%29.jpg/255px-Accipiter_nisus_1_%28Bohu%C5%A1_%C4%8C%C3%AD%C4%8Del%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Accipiter_nisus_1_%28Bohu%C5%A1_%C4%8C%C3%AD%C4%8Del%29.jpg/340px-Accipiter_nisus_1_%28Bohu%C5%A1_%C4%8C%C3%AD%C4%8Del%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="682" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Accipiter_nisus_1_(Bohu%C5%A1_%C4%8C%C3%AD%C4%8Del).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">Eurasian sparrowhawk</a> is a predator of the yellowhammer.</div></div></div>
<p>Predators of the yellowhammer include the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">sparrowhawk</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Jedrzejewska257_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jedrzejewska257-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Northern_goshawk" title="Northern goshawk">northern goshawk</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Jedrzejewska250_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jedrzejewska250-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lesser_spotted_eagle" title="Lesser spotted eagle">lesser spotted eagle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hobby_(bird)" title="Hobby (bird)">hobby</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jedrzejewska272_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jedrzejewska272-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jedrzejewska282_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jedrzejewska282-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> It is not a significant host of the <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common cuckoo</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasite</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-glue_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glue-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> although as a ground-nesting bird, its eggs and chicks are vulnerable to predation from small mammals such as <a href="/wiki/Muridae" title="Muridae">mice</a> and other rodents. Nests are also raided by <a href="/wiki/Corvus_(genus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Corvus (genus)">crows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_jay" title="Eurasian jay">Eurasian jays</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_magpie" title="Eurasian magpie">Eurasian magpies</a>. Predation accounted for more than 60% of nest failures in a 2012 survey in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ludwig_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ludwig-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Thirteen species of <a href="/wiki/Flea" title="Flea">fleas</a> in the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Ceratophyllus" title="Ceratophyllus">Ceratophyllus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Dasypsyllus" title="Dasypsyllus">Dasypsyllus</a></i> have been found on this bunting,<sup id="cite_ref-nhm_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhm-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and internal parasites include <i><a href="/wiki/Ascaridia_galli" title="Ascaridia galli">Ascaridia galli</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Atkinson_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Atkinson-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The yellowhammer may carry <a href="/wiki/Haematozoa" title="Haematozoa">haematozoan</a> blood parasites such as <i><a href="/wiki/Haemoproteus_coatneyi" title="Haemoproteus coatneyi">Haemoproteus coatneyi</a></i>. Males with high parasite levels produced fewer offspring (there is no such effect for females), and tend to be less brightly coloured. The striking plumage of the male may therefore have arisen as a signal of fitness to breed.<sup id="cite_ref-Sundberg_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sundberg-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Yellowhammers infected with <i><a href="/wiki/Haemoproteus" title="Haemoproteus">Haemoproteus</a></i> may have lower winter survival rates due to a tendency to having shorter wings.<sup id="cite_ref-dunn_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dunn-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellowhammer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) estimates the European population of the yellowhammer to be from 54–93&#160;million individuals, suggesting a Eurasian total of 73–186&#160;million birds. Although the population appears to be in a decline, the decrease is not rapid enough to trigger their vulnerability criteria. The large numbers and huge breeding range of about 12.9&#160;million km<sup>2</sup> (5&#160;million sq mi),<sup id="cite_ref-BirdLife_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLife-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> mean that this bunting is classified by the IUCN as being of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Populations have declined in recent decades in western Europe, including the British Isles, Belgium, the Netherlands, Austria, and Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The yellowhammer is a red-list (severely declining) species in Ireland and the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-kingston_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kingston-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> In eastern Europe, numbers appear to be stable, although the trend in Russia is unknown. Changes to agricultural practices are thought to be responsible for reduced breeding densities.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The introduced population in New Zealand has been very successful, with breeding densities much higher than in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-Lever_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lever-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-prins_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prins-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellowhammer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Clare.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/John_Clare.jpg/170px-John_Clare.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="208" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/John_Clare.jpg/255px-John_Clare.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/John_Clare.jpg/340px-John_Clare.jpg 2x" data-file-width="654" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Clare.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/John_Clare" title="John Clare">John Clare</a> wrote two poems about the yellowhammer.</div></div></div>
<p>The yellowhammer is a conspicuous, vocal, and formerly common country bird, and has attracted human interest. Yellowham Wood and Yellowham Hill, near <a href="/wiki/Dorchester,_Dorset" title="Dorchester, Dorset">Dorchester</a>, both derive their names from the bird. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Burns" title="Robert Burns">Robbie Burns'</a> poem "The Yellow, Yellow Yorlin'" gets its title from a Scottish name for the yellowhammer, which is given an obvious sexual connotation: "I met a pretty maid, an' unto her I said,/ 'I wad fain fin' your yellow, yellow yorlin'.' "<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> More factual descriptions of the bird and its behaviour can be found in John Clare's "The Yellowhammer's Nest" and "The Yellowhammer", whose final lines read:<sup id="cite_ref-Clare_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clare-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Haughton_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haughton-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><div class="poem">
<p>In early spring, when winds blow chilly cold,<br />
The yellowhammer, trailing grass, will come<br />
To fix a place and choose an early home,<br />
With yellow breast and head of solid gold.
</p>
</div></blockquote>
<p>Enid Blyton helped to popularise the bird's song as "little bit of bread and no cheese" in books such as <i>The Ship of Adventure</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Five_Go_Off_in_a_Caravan" title="Five Go Off in a Caravan">Five Go Off in a Caravan</a></i>, and wrote a poem called "The Yellow-hammer".<sup id="cite_ref-bto_22-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Blyton_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blyton-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Beethoven's student, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Czerny" title="Carl Czerny">Carl Czerny</a>, and biographer <a href="/wiki/Anton_Schindler" title="Anton Schindler">Anton Schindler</a>, both suggested that the composer got the idea for the first four notes of <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._5_(Beethoven)" title="Symphony No. 5 (Beethoven)">his 5th symphony</a> from the yellowhammer's call, although  more likely the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Piano_Concerto_No._4_(Beethoven)" title="Piano Concerto No. 4 (Beethoven)">4th Piano Concerto</a> was actually the work in question.<sup id="cite_ref-bowden_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bowden-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Thayer_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thayer-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Beethoven also used the yellowhammer theme in two <a href="/wiki/Piano_sonatas_(Beethoven)" title="Piano sonatas (Beethoven)">piano sonatas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piano_Sonata_No._21_(Beethoven)" title="Piano Sonata No. 21 (Beethoven)">no. 21 in C major</a> (the "Waldstein", Op.53) and <a href="/wiki/Piano_Sonata_No._23_(Beethoven)" title="Piano Sonata No. 23 (Beethoven)">No. 23 in F minor</a> (the "Appassionata", Op.57).<sup id="cite_ref-bowden_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bowden-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Olivier_Messiaen" title="Olivier Messiaen">Olivier Messiaen</a> often used birdsong as an inspiration for his music, and the yellowhammer features in <i><a href="/wiki/Chronochromie" title="Chronochromie">Chronochromie</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Dingle1_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dingle1-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Catalogue_d%27oiseaux" title="Catalogue d&#39;oiseaux">Catalogue d'oiseaux</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-shenton_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shenton-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/La_fauvette_des_jardins" class="mw-redirect" title="La fauvette des jardins">La fauvette des jardins</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9ditations_sur_le_myst%C3%A8re_de_la_Sainte_Trinit%C3%A9" class="mw-redirect" title="Méditations sur le mystère de la Sainte Trinité">Méditations sur le mystère de la Sainte Trinité</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Bruhn_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bruhn-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dingle2_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dingle2-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> appearing in four movements of the last piece.<sup id="cite_ref-shenton_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shenton-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An old legend links the yellowhammer to the devil. Its tongue was supposed to bear a drop of his blood, and the intricate pattern on the eggs was said to carry a concealed, possibly evil, message; these satanic associations sometimes led to the persecution of the bird. The unusual appearance of the eggs also led to "scribble lark", an old name for the bird.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
