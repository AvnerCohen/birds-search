<p>The <b>Eastern Imperial Eagle</b> (<i>Aquila heliaca</i>) is a large species of <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> that breeds from southeastern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> to western and central <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. Most populations are migratory and winter in northeastern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, and southern and eastern Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Imperial_Eagle" title="Spanish Imperial Eagle">Spanish Imperial Eagle</a>, found in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, was formerly <a href="/wiki/Lumpers_and_splitters#Lumping_and_splitting_in_biology" title="Lumpers and splitters">lumped</a> with this species, the name Imperial Eagle being used in both circumstances. However, the two are now regarded as separate species<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> due to significant differences in <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a><sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> and molecular characteristics.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The Eastern Imperial Eagle is a large <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagle</a> with a length of 72–90 cm (28–35 in), a wingspan of 1.8–2.16 m (5.9–7.1 ft) and a weight of 2.45–4.55 kilograms (5.4–10.0 lb). Females are about a quarter larger than males.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> It closely resembles the Spanish Imperial Eagle, but has far less white to the "shoulder" and it is slightly larger.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In Europe, the Eastern Imperial Eagle is threatened with extinction. It has nearly vanished from many areas of its former range, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Today, the only European populations are increasing in the <a href="/wiki/Carpathian_basin" title="Carpathian basin" class="mw-redirect">Carpathian basin</a>, mainly the northern mountains of Hungary and the southern region of <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>. The breeding population in Hungary consists of about 105 pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-HM_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HM-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>There are many Eastern Imperial Eagle nests in the Bulgaria/Greece section of the <a href="/wiki/European_Green_Belt#Ecological_Values" title="European Green Belt">European Green Belt</a> (the uncultivated belt along the former <a href="/wiki/Iron_Curtain" title="Iron Curtain">Iron Curtain</a>.)</p>
<p>The monarchy of <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a> once chose the Imperial Eagle to be its <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">heraldic</a> animal, but this did not help this bird. The eagle's preferred habitat is open country with small woods; unlike many other species of eagle, it does not generally live in mountains, large forests or treeless steppes.</p>
<p>Eastern Imperial Eagles generally prefer to construct a nest in a tree which is not surrounded by other trees, so that the nest is visible from a considerable distance, and so that the occupants may observe the surroundings unobstructed. Tree branches are taken in order to build the nest, which is upholstered with grass and feathers. Very rarely it nests on cliffs or the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In March or April the female lays two to three eggs. The chicks hatch after about 43 days and leave the nest after 60–77 days.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Often, however, only one will survive to leave the nest, with the others dying before becoming fully fledged. In at least a part of its range, more than a third of all nesting attempts are entirely unsuccessful.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Eastern Imperial Eagle feeds mainly on <a href="/wiki/European_Hare" title="European Hare" class="mw-redirect">European Hares</a>, <a href="/wiki/European_Hamster" title="European Hamster" class="mw-redirect">European Hamsters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_Pheasant" title="Common Pheasant">Common Pheasants</a> as well as a variety of other birds and mammals.<sup id="cite_ref-HM_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HM-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
