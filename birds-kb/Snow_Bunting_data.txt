<p>The <b>snow bunting</b> (<i>Plectrophenax nivalis</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Calcariidae" title="Calcariidae">Calcariidae</a>. It is an <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> specialist, with a <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">circumpolar</a> Arctic breeding range throughout the northern hemisphere. There are small isolated populations on a few high mountain tops south of the Arctic region, including the <a href="/wiki/Cairngorms" title="Cairngorms">Cairngorms</a> in central <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Elias_Mountains" title="Saint Elias Mountains">Saint Elias Mountains</a> on the southern <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>-<a href="/wiki/Yukon" title="Yukon">Yukon</a> border, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Breton_Highlands" title="Cape Breton Highlands">Cape Breton Highlands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-byers_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-byers-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The snow bunting is the most northerly recorded passerine in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snow_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SnowBunting-MtA.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/SnowBunting-MtA.jpg/220px-SnowBunting-MtA.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/SnowBunting-MtA.jpg/330px-SnowBunting-MtA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/SnowBunting-MtA.jpg/440px-SnowBunting-MtA.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SnowBunting-MtA.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A female snow bunting wintering atop Mount <a href="/wiki/Agamenticus" class="mw-redirect" title="Agamenticus">Agamenticus</a> in York, ME.</div></div></div>
<p>The snow bunting is a <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexually dimorphic</a>, medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This passerine is a ground-dwelling <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> that walks, runs and could potentially jump if needed.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It is fairly large and long-winged for a <a href="/wiki/Bunting_(bird)" title="Bunting (bird)">bunting</a>. It measures 15&#160;cm with a wingspan of 32–38&#160;cm (13–15&#160;in) and weights 30 to 40 grams.<sup id="cite_ref-byers_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-byers-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The bill is yellow with a black tip, and is all black in summer for males. The plumage is white in the underparts and the wings and back have black and white on them.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The female and male have a different plumage. During the <a href="/wiki/Breeding_season" class="mw-redirect" title="Breeding season">breeding season</a>, the male is white with black wingtips and a black back, while the female has black wingtips and a rufous back.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> During the winter, they will both have a rufous colouration in the back.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In the spring, the buntings will not go through a moult as other <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerines</a> birds do, instead the <a href="/wiki/Breeding_in_the_wild" title="Breeding in the wild">breeding</a> colouration comes with the wearing and abrasion of the feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Unlike most passerines, it has feathered tarsi, an adaptation to its harsh environment. No other <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> can winter as far north as this species apart from the <a href="/wiki/Common_raven" title="Common raven">common raven</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-byers_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-byers-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species is often confused with the <a href="/wiki/McKay%27s_bunting" title="McKay&#39;s bunting">McKay's bunting</a> (<i>Plectrophenax hyperboreus</i>) due to the similar colouration of their plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Even more, the challenge of identification becomes harder when this two species hybridize in the boundaries of their territories.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Another similar species is the <a href="/wiki/Horned_lark" title="Horned lark">horned lark</a>, although it has a larger black tail and it has a smaller white patch on the wings.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snow_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The snow bunting was formerly part of the family <a href="/wiki/Emberizidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Emberizidae">Emberizidae</a>, which includes <a href="/wiki/American_sparrow" class="mw-redirect" title="American sparrow">American sparrows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bunting_(bird)" title="Bunting (bird)">buntings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Towhee" title="Towhee">towhees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finches</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This family was formed after a broad geologically recent radiation of <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> birds.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> However, it is now part of the family Calcariidae which contains the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.allaboutbirds.org/search/?q=longspur">longspurs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Despite the wide distribution of this species there is no a significant difference that exists among different <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotypes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The genus name <i>Plectrophenax</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>plektron</i>, "cock’s spur", and <i>phenax</i> "imposter", and the specific <i>nivalis</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "snow-white".<sup id="cite_ref-job_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are four <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>, which differ slightly in the plumage pattern of breeding males:<sup id="cite_ref-byers_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-byers-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>Plectrophenax nivalis nivalis</i> <small>(Linnaeus, 1758)</small>: Arctic Europe, Arctic North America. Head white, rump mostly black with a small area of white.</li>
<li><i>Plectrophenax nivalis insulae</i> <small>(Salomonsen, 1931)</small>: Iceland, Faroe Islands, Scotland. Head white with a blackish collar, rump black.</li>
<li><i>Plectrophenax nivalis vlasowae</i> <small>(Portenko, 1937)</small>: Arctic Asia. Head white, rump mostly white.</li>
<li><i>Plectrophenax nivalis townsendi</i> <small>(Ridgway, 1887)</small>: Aleutian Islands, Kamchatka, coastal far eastern Siberia. As <i>vlasowae</i>, but slightly larger.</li></ul>
<p>It is very closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Beringia" title="Beringia">Beringian</a> <a href="/wiki/McKay%27s_bunting" title="McKay&#39;s bunting">McKay's bunting</a>, which differs in having even more white in the plumage. <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">Hybrids</a> between the two occur in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-byers_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-byers-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sibley_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sibley-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and they have been considered <a href="/wiki/Conspecific" class="mw-redirect" title="Conspecific">conspecific</a> by some authors,<sup id="cite_ref-voous_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-voous-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> though they are generally treated as separate species.<sup id="cite_ref-aou_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aou-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The species also mated with a <a href="/wiki/Lapland_longspur" title="Lapland longspur">Lapland Longspur</a> creating a <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrid.</a> The first photographs of this <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrid</a> were taken in April 2011, during its spring migration.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_distribution">Habitat and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snow_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitat and distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The snow bunting lives in very high latitudes in the <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">Arctic tundra</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> There is no apparent limit to its northern range, while the southern range is limited by the duration of daylight, which influences their reproductive activity.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> This species is found in the high Arctic tundra of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ellesmere_Island" title="Ellesmere Island">Ellesmere Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, higher mountains of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, North <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Novaya_Zemlya" title="Novaya Zemlya">Novaya Zemlya</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Josef_Land" title="Franz Josef Land">Franz Josef Land</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:6_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> During the winter, this bird migrates to the circumglobal northern temperate zone including the south of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, north of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, north of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, and east to <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">central Asia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Last glacial period">last ice age</a>, the snow bunting was widespread throughout continental <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>During the breeding period the snow bunting looks for rocky habitats in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Since the vegetation in the tundra is low growing, this bird and its nestlings are exposed to predators, and in order to ensure the survival of its <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">offspring</a>, the snow bunting nests in cavities in order to protect the nestlings from any threat.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> During this period, buntings also look for a habitat rich in vegetation such as wet <a href="/wiki/Sedge" class="mw-redirect" title="Sedge">sedge</a> meadows and areas rich in <a href="/wiki/Dryas_octopetala" title="Dryas octopetala">dryas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In the winter, they look for open habitats such as farms and fields where they feed on seeds in the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration">Migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snow_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The snow buntings <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> to the Arctic to reproduce and they are the first migrant species that arrives to these territories.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> They must gain at least 30% of body mass before migration.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The males will arrive first at the beginning of April, when temperature could reach -30 degrees Celsius.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This early migration could be explained by the fact that this species is highly territorial and the quality of the nesting area is crucial to their <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_success" title="Reproductive success">reproductive success</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Females will arrive four to six weeks later, when the snow starts to melt.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> They tend to migrate in small flocks and have an undulate flight at a moderate height.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This passerine bird overwinters in northern temperate zones in open fields forming moving <a href="/wiki/Flock_(birds)" title="Flock (birds)">flocks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-byers_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-byers-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> They will leave the Arctic at the middle and end of September, although some will start the migration at the beginning of November.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The female leaves first and tends to winter in more southern territories than the male, while the juveniles will leave the Arctic even later than the adults.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The migration of this bird is nocturnal and they are able to detect the <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_field" class="mw-redirect" title="Geomagnetic field">geomagnetic field of the earth</a> in order to guide themselves to their breeding and overwinter territory.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The orientation of the snow bunting during migration is independent of any type of visual cue.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, studies have shown that only those individuals with adequate energy storage will be able to select seasonally appropriate directions during their migration.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snow_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snow_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From the fall to the spring the snow bunting eats a variety of weeds such as <a href="/wiki/Polygonaceae" title="Polygonaceae">knotweed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ragweed" title="Ragweed">ragweed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amaranth" title="Amaranth">amaranth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amaranthaceae" title="Amaranthaceae">goosefoot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asteraceae" title="Asteraceae">aster</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Goldenrod" class="mw-redirect" title="Goldenrod">goldenrod</a> and also eats various types of <a href="/wiki/Grass" class="mw-redirect" title="Grass">grass</a> seeds.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> During this season it will forage in the snow collecting seeds from lower stems.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> During the summer their diet includes seeds of <a href="/wiki/Empetrum_nigrum" title="Empetrum nigrum">crowberry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bilberry" title="Bilberry">bilberry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bistorta" title="Bistorta">bistort</a>, dock, <a href="/wiki/Poppy_(flower)" class="mw-redirect" title="Poppy (flower)">poppy</a>, purple <a href="/wiki/Saxifragaceae" title="Saxifragaceae">saxifrage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterflies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemiptera" title="Hemiptera">true bugs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">wasps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The nestlings are fed exclusively on invertebrates.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Snow buntings also prey on basking spiders by throwing rocks around and less regularly they will try to catch invertebrates in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocalizations">Vocalizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snow_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Vocalizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The call is a distinctive rippling whistle, <i>per,r,r,rit</i> and the typical <i>Plectrophenax</i> warble <i>hudidi feet feet feew hudidi</i>.
</p><p>Snow buntings use vocalizations to communicate among each other and males will have a song to attract the female.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The communication calls are done by both the male and the female and they tend to be emitted in flight or in the ground, while the males will often emit the song from a perching position or in a flight display.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The males will start singing as soon as they will reach the breeding grounds, and will stop once they find a mate.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Studies have shown that the quality and the rate at which a song is emitted affect the <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_success" title="Reproductive success">reproductive success</a> of a male.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The rate of a song measured by the number of strophes per minute is limited by the foraging needs of the male; therefore, a male that is able to sing more frequently shows that he is more successful and effective in his <a href="/wiki/Foraging" title="Foraging">foraging behavior</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The song becomes an indicator of the parental care qualities of the male, since having an effective foraging behavior will provide a better probability of survival of the nestlings.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Females will then choose their mates based on their song rate.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Within snow buntings, vocalizations in males are unique to each individual, although there is certain syllable sharing between one another.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The uniqueness of each song reveals a capacity of recognition between individuals and has an effect in the <a href="/wiki/Fitness_(biology)" title="Fitness (biology)">individual fitness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_success" title="Reproductive success">reproductive success</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The songs have duration of 2 seconds and have a frequency of 2 to 6&#160;kHz.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_21-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Each song is composed of similar and dissimilar figures that create different motifs that will alternate and repeat, resulting in a unique pattern for every male individual.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_21-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snow_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Snow buntings have a <a href="/wiki/Monogamy_in_animals" title="Monogamy in animals">monogamous</a> behaviour in which the males have a positive impact in the reproductive success of the female, although they are not essential to the survival of the nestling.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The male will follow the female during her fertile period to make sure that she will not mate with any other male.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The nest sites provide safety but bring other challenges to snow buntings, since in rock cracks and fissures the microclimate could be harsh, the incubation time might be longer for this species and there is a risk that the
lower temperatures kill the <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> To overcome this challenge, the male will bring food to the female during the incubation time, in this way she will be able to constantly control the temperatures of the nest <a href="/wiki/Microclimate" title="Microclimate">microclimate</a> improving the hatching success and reducing the incubation time.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> lays eggs as soon as the ambient temperature is above 0 degrees Celsius<sup id="cite_ref-:8_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The eggs are blue-green, spotted brown, and hatch in 12–13 days, and the young are already ready to fly after a further 12–14 days.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Courtship_behaviour">Courtship behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snow_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Courtship behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The courtship behaviour of snow buntings varies in different parts of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In Greenland, the male will have a threat display to ensure his territory.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This display will consist of very loud calls, the male will lower its head down and will turn completely to face the newcomer.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The males will also have a ceremonial flight to attract the female, in which they will reach a height of 30 to 50 feet, then they will glide, they will sing the song very loudly and will then keep on singing from a perching position.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_climate_change_in_snow_bunting_populations">Effects of climate change in snow bunting populations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snow_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Effects of climate change in snow bunting populations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several index suggest that climate change could potentially have an important impact in the snow buntings populations.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Arctic_oscillation" title="Arctic oscillation">Arctic oscillation index</a> (AO) is a regional <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> index
that helps to predict ecological processes.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In the Arctic, when the AO index is in a positive phase there are higher winter temperatures and precipitations, there are earlier and warmer springs and in the summer it is cloudy, humid and there are lower temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Usually the AO index tends to oscillate from a positive to a negative phase, but during the last past 40 years, the AO index has remained in
the positive phase.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Studies have shown that warmer springs trigger an early breeding behavior in the snow buntings that mismatches the peak of their food sources, leading to a lower success rate of the hatchlings.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_23-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Even more, the higher temperatures will bring to the Arctic other species that will compete with the snow bunting,<sup id="cite_ref-:8_23-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> it is also thought that higher temperatures might allow a better survival of second broods in the snow bunting species.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_23-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
