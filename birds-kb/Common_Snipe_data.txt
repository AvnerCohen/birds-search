<p>The <b>Common Snipe</b> (<i>Gallinago gallinago</i>) is a small, stocky <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> native to the Old World. The breeding habitat is <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> and wet meadows throughout northern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, with European birds wintering in southern and western Europe and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> (south to the Equator), and Asian migrants moving to tropical southern Asia. The North American <a href="/wiki/Wilson%27s_Snipe" title="Wilson's Snipe">Wilson's Snipe</a> was previously considered the same species, and is listed as such in older field guides.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gallinago_gallinago_-Iceland_-two-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Gallinago_gallinago_-Iceland_-two-8.jpg/220px-Gallinago_gallinago_-Iceland_-two-8.jpg" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" /></a>

<i>G. g. faeroeensis</i> in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a></div>

</div>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gallinago_gallinago_MHNT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Gallinago_gallinago_MHNT.jpg/220px-Gallinago_gallinago_MHNT.jpg" width="220" height="204" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gallinago_gallinago_MHNT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

</div>

<p>Adults are 25–27&#160;cm in length with a 44–47&#160;cm wingspan and a weight of 80–140 g (up to 180 g pre-migration). They have short greenish-grey legs and a very long (5.5–7&#160;cm) straight dark bill. The body is mottled brown with straw-yellow stripes on top and pale underneath. They have a dark stripe through the eye, with light stripes above and below it. The wings are pointed.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Snow_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is the most widespread of several similar <a href="/wiki/Snipe" title="Snipe">snipes</a>. It most closely resembles the <a href="/wiki/Wilson%27s_Snipe" title="Wilson's Snipe">Wilson's Snipe</a> <i>G. delicata</i> of North America, which was until recently considered to be a subspecies <i>G. g. delicata</i> of Common Snipe. They differ in the number of tail feathers, with seven pairs in <i>G. gallinago</i> and eight pairs in <i>G. delicata</i>; the North American species also has a slightly thinner white trailing edge to the wings (the white is mostly on the tips of the secondaries).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Both species breed in the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Islands" title="Aleutian Islands">Aleutian Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> It is also very similar to the <a href="/wiki/Pin-tailed_Snipe" title="Pin-tailed Snipe">Pin-tailed Snipe</a> <i>G. stenura</i> and <a href="/wiki/Swinhoe%27s_Snipe" title="Swinhoe's Snipe">Swinhoe's Snipe</a> <i>G. megala</i> of eastern Asia; identification of these species there is complex.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>There are two subspecies of Common Snipe, <i>G. g. faeroeensis</i> in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shetland_Islands" title="Shetland Islands" class="mw-redirect">Shetland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> (wintering in Britain and Ireland), and <i>G. g. gallinago</i> in the rest of the Old World.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Common_Snipe_(Gallinago_gallinago)_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5750.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Common_Snipe_%28Gallinago_gallinago%29_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5750.jpg/220px-Common_Snipe_%28Gallinago_gallinago%29_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5750.jpg" width="220" height="178" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Common_Snipe_(Gallinago_gallinago)_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5750.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>G. g. gallinago</i> at <a href="/wiki/Keoladeo_National_Park" title="Keoladeo National Park">Keoladeo National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bharatpur,_India" title="Bharatpur, India" class="mw-redirect">Bharatpur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.</div>

<p>It is a well camouflaged bird, it is usually shy and conceals itself close to ground vegetation and flushes only when approached closely. When flushed, they utter a sharp note that sounds like <i>scape, scape</i> and fly off in a series of aerial zig-zags to confuse predators.<sup id="cite_ref-eb1911_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb1911-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> They forage in soft mud, probing or picking up food by sight. They mainly eat insects and earthworms, also some plant material.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The male performs "winnowing" display during courtship, flying high in circles and then taking shallow dives to produce a "<a href="/wiki/Drumming_(snipe)" title="Drumming (snipe)">drumming</a>" sound by vibrating its tail feathers. This sound has been compared by others to the bleating of a sheep or <a href="/wiki/Goat" title="Goat">goat</a>; hence in many languages the Snipe is known by names signifying “Flying Goat,” “Heaven's Ram,” as in Scotland by “Heather-bleater”<sup id="cite_ref-eb1911_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb1911-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a> the name <i>taivaanvuohi</i>, "sky goat". Common Snipe nest in a well-hidden location on the ground, laying four eggs of a dark olive colour, blotched and spotted with rich brown,<sup id="cite_ref-eb1911_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb1911-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> which are incubated by the female for 18–21 days. The young when freshly hatched are covered in down of a dark maroon, variegated with black, white and buff.<sup id="cite_ref-eb1911_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb1911-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The young are cared for by both parents, each parent looking after half the brood, with fledging in 10–20 days.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Snow_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span></h2>
<p>Overall, the species is not threatened. Populations on the southern fringes of the breeding range in Europe are however declining with local extinction in some areas (notably in parts of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>), mainly due to field drainage and agricultural intensification.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies to the species. It is still hunted as a <a href="/wiki/Gamebird" title="Gamebird" class="mw-redirect">gamebird</a> in much of its range.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>Old folk names include "mire snipe", "horse gowk", "heather bleat", and the variant spelling "snite".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> See also <a href="/wiki/Snipe" title="Snipe">snipe</a> for other aspects of the name.</p>
