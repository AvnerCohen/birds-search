<p>The <b>common snipe</b> (<i>Gallinago gallinago</i>) is a small, stocky <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> native to the Old World. The scientific name <i>gallinago</i> is <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">New Latin</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Woodcock" title="Woodcock">woodcock</a> or <a href="/wiki/Snipe" title="Snipe">snipe</a> from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>gallina</i>, "hen" and the suffix <i>-ago</i>, "resembling".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The breeding habitat is <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> and wet meadows throughout the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>.In the north, the distribution limit extends from Iceland over the north of the British Isles and northern Fennoscandia, where it occurs at around 70 ° N, as well as through European Russia and Siberia. Here it is mostly on the northern edge of the Taiga zone at 71 ° N, but reaches 74 ° N on the east coast of the <a href="/wiki/Taymyr_Peninsula" title="Taymyr Peninsula">Taymyr Peninsula</a>. In the east it extends to the Anadyr, Kamchatka, the Bering Island and the Kuril Islands, The southern boundary of the distribution area in Europe runs through northern Portugal, central France, northern Italy, Bulgaria and the Ukraine, with populations in the west being only very scattered. In Asia, the distribution extends south to northern Turkestan, locally to Afghanistan and the Middle East, through the Altai and further to Manchuria and Ussuri. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, with European birds wintering in southern and western Europe and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> (south to the Equator), and Asian migrants moving to tropical southern Asia. The North American <a href="/wiki/Wilson%27s_snipe" title="Wilson&#39;s snipe">Wilson's snipe</a> was previously considered the same species, and is listed as such in older field guides.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gallinago_gallinago_MHNT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Gallinago_gallinago_MHNT.jpg/220px-Gallinago_gallinago_MHNT.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="204" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Gallinago_gallinago_MHNT.jpg/330px-Gallinago_gallinago_MHNT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Gallinago_gallinago_MHNT.jpg/440px-Gallinago_gallinago_MHNT.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3302" data-file-height="3061" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gallinago_gallinago_MHNT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Gallinago gallinago</i> – <a href="/wiki/MHNT" class="mw-redirect" title="MHNT">MHNT</a></div></div></div>
<p>Adults are 25–27&#160;cm (9.8–10.6&#160;in) in length with a 44–47&#160;cm (17–19&#160;in) wingspan and a weight of 80–140&#160;g (2.8–4.9&#160;oz) (up to 180&#160;g (6.3&#160;oz) pre-migration). They have short greenish-grey legs and a very long (5.5–7&#160;cm (2.2–2.8&#160;in)) straight dark bill. The body is mottled brown with straw-yellow stripes on top and pale underneath. They have a dark stripe through the eye, with light stripes above and below it. The wings are pointed.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Snow_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is the most widespread of several similar <a href="/wiki/Snipe" title="Snipe">snipes</a>. It most closely resembles the <a href="/wiki/Wilson%27s_snipe" title="Wilson&#39;s snipe">Wilson's snipe</a> (<i>G. delicata</i>) of North America, which was until recently considered to be a subspecies — <i>G. g. delicata</i> — of common snipe. They differ in the number of tail feathers, with seven pairs in <i>G. gallinago</i> and eight pairs in <i>G. delicata</i>; the North American species also has a slightly thinner white trailing edge to the wings (the white is mostly on the tips of the secondaries).<sup id="cite_ref-Leader_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leader-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Reid_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reid-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Both species breed in the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Islands" title="Aleutian Islands">Aleutian Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is also very similar to the <a href="/wiki/Pin-tailed_snipe" title="Pin-tailed snipe">pin-tailed snipe</a> (<i>G. stenura</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Swinhoe%27s_snipe" title="Swinhoe&#39;s snipe">Swinhoe's snipe</a> (<i>G. megala</i>) of eastern Asia; identification of these species there is complex.<sup id="cite_ref-Carey_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carey-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are two subspecies of common snipe, <i>G. g. faeroeensis</i> in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shetland_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Shetland Islands">Shetland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> (wintering in Britain and Ireland), and <i>G. g. gallinago</i> in the rest of the Old World. The <i>faeroeensis</i> normally is more richly toned on the breast, its upperparts and the head than the <i>gallinago</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Common_Snipe_(Gallinago_gallinago)_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5750.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Common_Snipe_%28Gallinago_gallinago%29_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5750.jpg/220px-Common_Snipe_%28Gallinago_gallinago%29_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5750.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="178" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Common_Snipe_%28Gallinago_gallinago%29_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5750.jpg/330px-Common_Snipe_%28Gallinago_gallinago%29_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5750.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Common_Snipe_%28Gallinago_gallinago%29_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5750.jpg/440px-Common_Snipe_%28Gallinago_gallinago%29_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5750.jpg 2x" data-file-width="740" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Common_Snipe_(Gallinago_gallinago)_at_Bharatpur_I_IMG_5750.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>G. g. gallinago</i> at <a href="/wiki/Keoladeo_National_Park" title="Keoladeo National Park">Keoladeo National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bharatpur,_India" class="mw-redirect" title="Bharatpur, India">Bharatpur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a>, India.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bekassine.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Bekassine.jpg/220px-Bekassine.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Bekassine.jpg/330px-Bekassine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Bekassine.jpg/440px-Bekassine.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="1330" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bekassine.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>G. g. gallinago</i> at nature park <a href="/wiki/S%27Albufera" title="S&#39;Albufera">S'Albufera</a>, on the island of Mallorca.</div></div></div>
<p>It is a well camouflaged bird, it is usually shy and conceals itself close to ground vegetation and flushes only when approached closely. When flushed, they utter a sharp note that sounds like <i>scape, scape</i> and fly off in a series of aerial zig-zags to confuse predators.<sup id="cite_ref-eb1911_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb1911-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> They forage in soft mud, probing or picking up food by sight. They mainly eat insects and earthworms, also some plant material.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The male performs "winnowing" display during courtship, flying high in circles and then taking shallow dives to produce a "<a href="/wiki/Drumming_(snipe)" title="Drumming (snipe)">drumming</a>" sound by vibrating its tail feathers. This sound has been compared by others to the bleating of a sheep or <a href="/wiki/Goat" title="Goat">goat</a>; hence in many languages the snipe is known by names signifying “flying goat,” “heaven's ram,” as in Scotland by “heather-bleater”<sup id="cite_ref-eb1911_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb1911-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a> the name <i>taivaanvuohi</i>, "sky goat".
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Philip_Manson-Bahr" title="Philip Manson-Bahr">Philip Manson-Bahr</a> is credited with unravelling the mystery of how the snipe creates that unusual breathy sound which is unlike other birdsong. He worked out that the sound was created by placing out two tail feathers at 90 degrees to the direction of flight. When diving these feathers create this unusual sound. He demonstrated this in front of the <a href="/wiki/British_Ornithologists_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="British Ornithologists Union">British Ornithologists Union</a> by inserting two snipe feathers into a cork which he then whirled around his head on a string.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Wing shape does not differ between sedentary and migratory common snipe, suggesting that social selection influences wing shape given this species aerial displays during courtship.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Common snipe nest in a well-hidden location on the ground, laying four eggs of a dark olive colour, blotched and spotted with rich brown,<sup id="cite_ref-eb1911_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb1911-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> which are incubated by the female for 18–21 days. The freshly hatched young are covered in dark maroon down, variegated with black, white and buff.<sup id="cite_ref-eb1911_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb1911-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The young are cared for by both parents, each parent looking after half the brood, with fledging in 10–20 days.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Snow_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CommonSnipe.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/CommonSnipe.jpg/220px-CommonSnipe.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="205" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/CommonSnipe.jpg/330px-CommonSnipe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/CommonSnipe.jpg/440px-CommonSnipe.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2214" data-file-height="2064" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CommonSnipe.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Common Snipe at Chilika, Odisha</div></div></div>
<p>Overall, the species is not threatened. Populations on the southern fringes of the breeding range in Europe are however declining with local extinction in some areas (notably in parts of England and Germany), mainly due to field drainage and agricultural intensification.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds (AEWA)</a> applies to the species. It is still hunted as a <a href="/wiki/Gamebird" class="mw-redirect" title="Gamebird">gamebird</a> in much of its range.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Old folk names include "mire snipe", "horse gowk", "heather bleat", and the variant spelling "snite".<sup id="cite_ref-Lockwood_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lockwood-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> See <a href="/wiki/Snipe" title="Snipe">snipe</a> for other aspects of the name.
</p>
