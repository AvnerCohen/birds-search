<p>The <b>Eurasian woodcock</b> (<i>Scolopax rusticola</i>) is a medium-small <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wading</a> bird found in temperate and subarctic <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurasia</a>. It has <a href="/wiki/Camouflage#Cryptic_coloration_in_nature" title="Camouflage">cryptic camouflage</a> to suit its woodland habitat, with reddish-brown upperparts and buff-coloured underparts. Its eyes are set far back on its head to give it 360-degree vision and it probes in the ground for food with its long, sensitive bill, making it vulnerable to cold weather when the ground remains frozen.
</p><p>The male performs a courtship flight known as 'roding' at dusk in spring. When threatened, the female can carry chicks between her legs, in her claws or on her back while flying, though this is rarely witnessed. The world population is estimated to be 14 million to 16 million birds.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_woodcock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adults are 33–38&#160;cm (13–15&#160;in) in length, including the 6–8&#160;cm (2.4–3.1&#160;in) long straight bill, and have a 55–65&#160;cm (22–26&#160;in) wingspan.<sup id="cite_ref-collins_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The Eurasian woodcock has <a href="/wiki/Camouflage#Cryptic_coloration_in_nature" title="Camouflage">cryptic camouflage</a> to suit its woodland habitat, with intricately patterned reddish-brown upperparts and buff underparts. The head is barred with black, not striped like that of its close relatives, the <a href="/wiki/Snipe" title="Snipe">snipe</a>. It has large eyes located high on the sides of its head, giving it 360-degree monocular vision.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-brit_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brit-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The wings are rounded and the base of the bill is flesh-coloured with a dark tip. The legs vary from grey to pinkish.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The species is <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexually dimorphic</a>, with the male much larger than the female,<sup id="cite_ref-birdfacts_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdfacts-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> although the sexes cannot be separated in the field.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_woodcock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Eurasian woodcock was given its current <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">scientific name</a> by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a> in his <i>Systema naturae</i> in 1758.<sup id="cite_ref-Linnaeus1758_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linnaeus1758-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The binomial name is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>skolopax</i>, meaning 'woodcock', and <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>rusticus</i> ('rural') and <i>colere</i> ('to live').<sup id="cite_ref-birdfacts_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdfacts-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The species is <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monotypic">monotypic</a><sup id="cite_ref-birdfacts_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdfacts-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and its closest relatives are the other woodcocks in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Scolopax" class="mw-redirect" title="Scolopax">Scolopax</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Audubon2009_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Audubon2009-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The latin adjective "rusticulus" is a diminutive of the adjective "rusticus", is not a compositum of the adjective "rusticus" and of the verb "colere". 
<a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/morph?l=rusticulus&amp;la=la#lexicon">http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/morph?l=rusticulus&amp;la=la#lexicon</a>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_woodcock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>About one third of the world's Eurasian woodcock breed in Europe, with more than 90% of the continent's population breeding in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fennoscandia" title="Fennoscandia">Fennoscandia</a>. Their breeding range stretches from Fennoscandia to the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> and from western Europe to Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-bin_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bin-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species is the woodcock found through most of temperate and subarctic Eurasia. Northern and Asian populations <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> to southern Europe or the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Subcontinent" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian Subcontinent">Indian Subcontinent</a>, respectively. Birds in milder western European countries and on Atlantic islands<sup id="cite_ref-bli_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bli-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> are resident. The populations breeding in north-west and southern Europe are mostly sedentary.<sup id="cite_ref-bin_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bin-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The birds' spring migration commences in February; breeding territories are reached between March and May.<sup id="cite_ref-bli_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bli-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Spring migration movements of woodcock are influenced by weather conditions, however this does not have a major effect on their subsequent breeding success.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2005-09_Bia%C5%82owieski_Park_Narodowy_3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/2005-09_Bia%C5%82owieski_Park_Narodowy_3.jpg/220px-2005-09_Bia%C5%82owieski_Park_Narodowy_3.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/2005-09_Bia%C5%82owieski_Park_Narodowy_3.jpg/330px-2005-09_Bia%C5%82owieski_Park_Narodowy_3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/2005-09_Bia%C5%82owieski_Park_Narodowy_3.jpg/440px-2005-09_Bia%C5%82owieski_Park_Narodowy_3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2272" data-file-height="1704" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2005-09_Bia%C5%82owieski_Park_Narodowy_3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Habitat: <a href="/wiki/Bia%C5%82owieski_Park_Narodowy" class="mw-redirect" title="Białowieski Park Narodowy">Białowieski Park Narodowy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a></div></div></div>
<p>The Eurasian woodcock has a large range, with an estimated Global Extent of Occurrence of 10 million square kilometres and a population of an estimated 15 million to 16 million birds. Because of its large range, stable population trend and large population size, the species has been evaluated as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>. The most important threat to the population of the Eurasian woodcock in the breeding range is the increased fragmentation of its woodland habitat. At other times of year, a reduction in the amount of permanent grassland and an increase in the intensification of farming are also threats,<sup id="cite_ref-habitat_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-habitat-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and the species' susceptibility to <a href="/wiki/Avian_influenza" title="Avian influenza">avian influenza</a> may have an impact in future.<sup id="cite_ref-bli_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bli-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Eurasian woodcock's required breeding habitat is large, unfragmented areas of broadleaved deciduous or mixed broadleaved and coniferous forest, with dense undergrowth of plants such as <a href="/wiki/Brambles" class="mw-redirect" title="Brambles">brambles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holly" title="Holly">holly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hazel" title="Hazel">hazel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gorse" class="mw-redirect" title="Gorse">gorse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bracken" title="Bracken">bracken</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bilberry" title="Bilberry">bilberry</a>. Breeding territories must include a mix of dry, warm resting places, damp areas for feeding, and clearings for flight.<sup id="cite_ref-bli_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bli-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In larger woods, wide 'rides' (open tracks through the wood) and small clearings are important. In winter, Eurasian woodcock also use <a href="/wiki/Scrubland" class="mw-redirect" title="Scrubland">scrubland</a> during the day<sup id="cite_ref-bin_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bin-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> but in freezing weather they may use intertidal mud.<sup id="cite_ref-bli_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bli-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_woodcock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eurasian woodcock are <a href="/wiki/Crepuscular" class="mw-redirect" title="Crepuscular">crepuscular</a> (most active at dawn and dusk) and rarely active during the day unless flushed, when they fly off with a whirring wing noise.<sup id="cite_ref-collins_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The flight is somewhat owl- or bat-like; woodcock fly fast and directly while migrating or crossing open country, but fly erratically with twisting and fluttering once in woodland.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> They are usually solitary and migrate singly, but may congregate when weather or geographical conditions force them to do so.<sup id="cite_ref-bli_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bli-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> This species is parasitised by the <a href="/wiki/Moorhen_flea" title="Moorhen flea">moorhen flea</a>, <i>Dasypsyllus gallinulae</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rothschild_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rothschild-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_woodcock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Scolopax_rusticola_MWNH_0171.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Scolopax_rusticola_MWNH_0171.JPG/220px-Scolopax_rusticola_MWNH_0171.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="248" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Scolopax_rusticola_MWNH_0171.JPG/330px-Scolopax_rusticola_MWNH_0171.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Scolopax_rusticola_MWNH_0171.JPG/440px-Scolopax_rusticola_MWNH_0171.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2848" data-file-height="3212" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Scolopax_rusticola_MWNH_0171.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden,_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Museum Wiesbaden, Germany">Museum Wiesbaden, Germany</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Scolopax_rusticola_-_Doi_Inthanon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Scolopax_rusticola_-_Doi_Inthanon.jpg/220px-Scolopax_rusticola_-_Doi_Inthanon.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Scolopax_rusticola_-_Doi_Inthanon.jpg/330px-Scolopax_rusticola_-_Doi_Inthanon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Scolopax_rusticola_-_Doi_Inthanon.jpg/440px-Scolopax_rusticola_-_Doi_Inthanon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2250" data-file-height="1500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Scolopax_rusticola_-_Doi_Inthanon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eurasian woodcock at <a href="/wiki/Doi_Inthanon_National_Park" title="Doi Inthanon National Park">Doi Inthanon National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chiang_Mai" title="Chiang Mai">Chiang Mai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a></div></div></div>
<p>The male performs a courtship display flight called 'roding' at dusk between April and June. A <a href="/wiki/Mating_call" title="Mating call">mating call</a> is performed during courtship display flights: <i>orr, orr, orr, pist</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Constantine2006_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Constantine2006-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> A study of <a href="/wiki/Spectrogram" title="Spectrogram">spectrograms</a> of the vocalisations of male Eurasian woodcock showed that 95% of calls could be attributed correctly to individual birds, and concluded that counting roding males was a suitable method of monitoring the population.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoodless2008_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoodless2008-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Eurasian woodcock nest on the ground in low cover in woodland or tall <a href="/wiki/Calluna" title="Calluna">heather</a>. The nest is a lined cup<sup id="cite_ref-collins_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> or a slight hollow<sup id="cite_ref-obs_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obs-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cbb_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbb-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> lined with dead leaves and other plant material.<sup id="cite_ref-tracks_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tracks-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> A single brood of one or two white or creamy eggs with light brown and grey blotches<sup id="cite_ref-obs_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obs-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> is laid; once the clutch is complete,<sup id="cite_ref-cbb_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbb-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> incubation is performed by the female for between 21 and 24 days. The eggs measure 44&#160;mm ×&#160;34&#160;mm (1.7&#160;in ×&#160;1.3&#160;in) and weigh 26.5&#160;g (0.93&#160;oz), of which 5% is shell. The downy, <a href="/wiki/Precocial" class="mw-redirect" title="Precocial">precocial</a> young leave the nest immediately<sup id="cite_ref-cbb_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbb-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and fledge after 15–20 days,<sup id="cite_ref-birdfacts_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdfacts-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> although they can fly short distances after 10 days.<sup id="cite_ref-jonsson_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jonsson-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> When threatened, the mother bird can fly whilst carrying small chicks between her legs, body and tail,<sup id="cite_ref-cbb_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbb-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> in her claws or on her back. This behaviour is rarely witnessed.<sup id="cite_ref-brit_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brit-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_feeding">Food and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_woodcock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Food and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Eurasian woodcock forage in soft soil in thickets, usually well hidden from sight. They mainly eat earthworms, but also insects and their larvae, freshwater molluscs and some plant seeds.<sup id="cite_ref-cbb_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbb-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Because they rely on probing into the ground to find food, they are vulnerable to cold winter weather when the ground remains frozen.<sup id="cite_ref-bin_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bin-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> During the cold British winter of 1962–63, starving woodcock were found feeding in urban areas and some were even forced to eat bird seed.<sup id="cite_ref-brit_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brit-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nocturnal" class="mw-redirect" title="Nocturnal">Nocturnal</a> feeding occurs in frost-free <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pasture</a> or long-rotation grass <a href="/wiki/Meadow" title="Meadow">meadows</a>; fields with woodland within 1&#160;km (0.62&#160;mi) are preferred to allow roosting during the day. There are only low densities of Eurasian woodcock in predominantly <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable</a> areas.<sup id="cite_ref-bin_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bin-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In a three-year scientific study based in <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, 65 radio-tagged woodcock and their habitat preferences were monitored. During the day, the birds chose areas of forest with humus which held large numbers of earthworms and with a dense <a href="/wiki/Scrubland" class="mw-redirect" title="Scrubland">scrub</a> layer to provide protection, and hedges with scrub and trees were also found to be important. At night, the woodcock selected grazed pastures which had five times the <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> of earthworms compared to tilled land.<sup id="cite_ref-habitat_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-habitat-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_humans">Relationship to humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_woodcock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Relationship to humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hunting">Hunting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_woodcock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hunting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In many countries woodcock are hunted as game, and their size, speed and flight pattern makes them a very challenging shot. Anyone shooting a witnessed 'right and left'—in which two woodcock are killed with consecutive shots from a shotgun, without the gun being lowered or reloaded—can be admitted to the <i><a href="/wiki/Shooting_Times" title="Shooting Times">Shooting Times</a></i> Woodcock Club.<sup id="cite_ref-countrylife_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-countrylife-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1821 <a href="/wiki/William_Bingley" title="William Bingley">William Bingley</a> described a method of trapping Eurasian woodcock formerly used in northern England: "Long parallel rows of stones or stick, four or five inches high, were made in moonlight nights on the commons frequented by them. In these rows several intervals or gateways were left in which the traps were placed. When the birds, running about in search of food, came to one of these rows, they did not usually cross it, but ran along the side till they arrived at the gateways, which they entered, and in which they were caught."<sup id="cite_ref-bingley_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bingley-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The effect of hunting on the species' European breeding population is "little understood."<sup id="cite_ref-Baillie2008_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baillie2008-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> A study conducted in <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>, France, explored the factors affecting the populations of wintering Eurasian woodcock. The mortality and survival rates of 98 radio-tagged birds were monitored in a hunted area and in an adjoining hunting-free reserve. The scientists found rates of survival of 0.86 for adults and 0.67 for juveniles, with the probability of survival increasing as birds spent more time in the reserve. <a href="/wiki/Matrix_population_models" title="Matrix population models">Matrix population models</a> were used and the conclusion was that "such low survival probabilities cannot sustain long-term viable populations. These results call for caution in the harvesting of woodcock populations wintering in western France and could be a forewarning of a decline."<sup id="cite_ref-Duriez2004_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duriez2004-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United Kingdom, surveys are being conducted to improve knowledge of the number of breeding Eurasian woodcock and the structure of their habitat.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">Isotope</a> analysis of feathers from Eurasian woodcock shot in the UK is also being performed to establish the origins of the birds.<sup id="cite_ref-appeal_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appeal-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gastronomy">Gastronomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_woodcock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Gastronomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Eurasian woodcock are considered at their best for culinary purposes from October to December. After shooting, the birds are <a href="/wiki/Hanging_(meat)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hanging (meat)">hung</a> for 4–8 days to tenderise; the meat can be eaten as a starter, savoury, or for breakfast.<sup id="cite_ref-Green2005_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Green2005-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> A bird serves one person. They are not gutted before cooking; the head, skinned with eyes removed, is left on and the beak used to truss the bird.<sup id="cite_ref-BASC_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BASC-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A recipe for roast woodcock was featured in <i><a href="/wiki/Mrs_Beeton%27s_Book_of_Household_Management" title="Mrs Beeton&#39;s Book of Household Management">Mrs Beeton's Book of Household Management</a></i>, but despite its name, the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian</a> dish named <a href="/wiki/Scotch_woodcock" title="Scotch woodcock">Scotch woodcock</a> consists of <a href="/wiki/Scrambled_eggs" title="Scrambled eggs">scrambled eggs</a> on <a href="/wiki/Toast" title="Toast">toast</a> with <a href="/wiki/Anchovy" title="Anchovy">anchovies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Beeton2006_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beeton2006-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Philp1865_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Philp1865-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Bingley wrote that [in the United Kingdom] "Few birds are so much in esteem for the table as these", although he also reported that woodcock meat was not eaten in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> or <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> as it was thought unwholesome, but that the eggs were taken and sold in "great numbers" in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gothenburg" title="Gothenburg">Gothenburg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bingley_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bingley-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Freeling" title="Nicolas Freeling">Nicolas Freeling</a>'s <i>Kitchen Book</i> he narrates the lavish dinner described in <a href="/wiki/Kipling" class="mw-redirect" title="Kipling">Kipling</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Debits_and_Credits_(Kipling)" class="mw-redirect" title="Debits and Credits (Kipling)">Sea Constables</a></i>, which features woodcock as a savoury, and explains that 'the woodcock has no gall, and is not cleaned [...] the creamy inside parts are spread on toast [...] no Edwardian gentleman would have resisted this for one minute.'<sup id="cite_ref-Freeling1970_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freeling1970-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Folklore_and_culture">Folklore and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_woodcock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Folklore and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stamps_of_Germany_(BRD)_Jugendmarke_1965_10_Pf.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Stamps_of_Germany_%28BRD%29_Jugendmarke_1965_10_Pf.jpg/220px-Stamps_of_Germany_%28BRD%29_Jugendmarke_1965_10_Pf.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="189" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Stamps_of_Germany_%28BRD%29_Jugendmarke_1965_10_Pf.jpg/330px-Stamps_of_Germany_%28BRD%29_Jugendmarke_1965_10_Pf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Stamps_of_Germany_%28BRD%29_Jugendmarke_1965_10_Pf.jpg/440px-Stamps_of_Germany_%28BRD%29_Jugendmarke_1965_10_Pf.jpg 2x" data-file-width="840" data-file-height="720" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stamps_of_Germany_(BRD)_Jugendmarke_1965_10_Pf.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eurasian woodcock stamp from <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a></div></div></div>
<p>In the United Kingdom, the early arrival of migrant woodcock in autumn was said to mean a good harvest, especially if they stayed until spring. It used to be thought that Eurasian woodcock flew to the moon during the months when they were not seen<sup id="cite_ref-air_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-air-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and the first <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">full moon</a> in November, when large numbers arrive on the British coast, is sometimes described as the 'woodcock moon'.<sup id="cite_ref-appeal_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appeal-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cross2008_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cross2008-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Reaney and Wilson wrote that the English surname <a href="/wiki/Woodcock_(surname)" title="Woodcock (surname)">"Woodcock"</a> developed as a nickname from the bird, meaning 'a fool, simpleton or dupe'.<sup id="cite_ref-brit_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brit-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Reaney1997_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reaney1997-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>'s play <i><a href="/wiki/Love%27s_Labours_Lost" class="mw-redirect" title="Love&#39;s Labours Lost">Love's Labours Lost</a></i>, Berowne describes himself and his friends as 'four woodcocks in a dish', after discovering they have all fallen in love when they have sworn not to.<sup id="cite_ref-brit_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brit-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> An old folk name for the <a href="/wiki/Goldcrest" title="Goldcrest">goldcrest</a> was the "woodcock pilot" because of the mistaken belief that the warblers rode in the feathers of migrating woodcock.<sup id="cite_ref-bto2009_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto2009-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/Hamlet" title="Hamlet">Hamlet</a></i>, Polonius refers to Hamlet's vows as "springes to catch woodcocks".
</p><p>The 'pin feather' of the Eurasian woodcock (the <a href="/wiki/Covert_(feather)" class="mw-redirect" title="Covert (feather)">covert feather</a> of the outermost <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">primary</a>) can be used as a fine paintbrush for miniatures, or as a delicate tool for moving tiny objects (e.g. specks of dust).<sup id="cite_ref-birdfacts_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdfacts-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> As a painting tool, the feather has been described as "far from ideal, holding very little paint, resisting water and wearing down quickly at the tip."<sup id="cite_ref-wildart_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wildart-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The Eurasian woodcock has been the subject of <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamp" title="Postage stamp">postage stamps</a> from several countries.<sup id="cite_ref-stamps_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stamps-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A woodcock serves as a symbolic element in the novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Birds_(novel)" title="The Birds (novel)">The Birds</a></i> by writer <a href="/wiki/Tarjei_Vesaas" title="Tarjei Vesaas">Tarjei Vesaas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
