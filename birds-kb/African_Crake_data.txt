<p>The <b>African crake</b> (<i>Crecopsis egregia</i>) is a small to medium-size ground-living bird in the <a href="/wiki/Rallidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Rallidae">rail</a> family, found in most of central to southern Africa. It is seasonally common in most of its range other than the <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforests</a> and areas that have low annual rainfall. This crake is a partial <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>, moving away from the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> as soon as the rains provide sufficient grass cover to allow it to breed elsewhere. There have been a few records of <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrant</a> birds reaching Atlantic islands. This species nests in a wide variety of grassland types, and agricultural land with tall crops may also be used.
</p><p>A smallish crake, the African crake has brown-streaked blackish upperparts, bluish-grey underparts and black-and-white barring on the flanks and belly. It has a stubby red bill, red eyes, and a white line from the bill to above the eye. It is smaller than its closest relative, the <a href="/wiki/Corn_crake" title="Corn crake">corn crake</a>; that species is also <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">lighter-plumaged</a>, and has an eye stripe. The African crake has a range of calls, the most characteristic being a series of rapid grating <i>krrr</i> notes. It is active during the day, and is <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territorial</a> on both the breeding and non-breeding grounds; the male has a <a href="/wiki/Threat_display" class="mw-redirect" title="Threat display">threat display</a>, and may fight at territory boundaries. The nest is a shallow cup of grass leaves built in a depression under a grass tussock or small bush. The 3–11 eggs start hatching after about 14 days, and the black, downy <a href="/wiki/Precocial" class="mw-redirect" title="Precocial">precocial</a> chicks <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> after four to five weeks. The African crake feeds on a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>, along with some small frogs and fish, and plant material, especially grass seeds. It may itself be eaten by large <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">birds of prey</a>; snakes; or mammals, including humans, and can host <a href="/wiki/Parasitism" title="Parasitism">parasites</a>. Although it may be displaced temporarily by the burning of grassland, or permanently by agriculture, wetland drainage or urbanisation, its large range and population mean that it is not considered to be threatened.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African_crake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rallidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Rallidae">rails</a> are a bird <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> comprising nearly 150 species. Although the origins of the group are lost in antiquity, the largest number of species and the most primitive forms are found in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a>, suggesting that this family originated there. The taxonomy of the small crakes is complicated, but the closest relative of the African crake is the corn crake, <i>C. crex</i>, which breeds in Europe and Asia, but winters in Africa. The African crake was first described as <i>Ortygometra egregia</i> by <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Peters" title="Wilhelm Peters">Wilhelm Peters</a> in 1854 from a specimen collected in Mozambique,<sup id="cite_ref-taylor316_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor316-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> but the genus name failed to become established. For some time it was placed as the sole member of the genus <i>Crecopsis</i><sup id="cite_ref-petersjameslee_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-petersjameslee-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> but subsequently moved to <i><a href="/wiki/Crex" class="mw-redirect" title="Crex">Crex</a></i>, created for this species by German naturalist and ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Johann_Matth%C3%A4us_Bechstein" title="Johann Matthäus Bechstein">Johann Matthäus Bechstein</a> in 1803.<sup id="cite_ref-bechstein_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bechstein-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bowdler_Sharpe" title="Richard Bowdler Sharpe">Richard Bowdler Sharpe</a> considered that the African bird differed sufficiently from the corn crake to have its own genus <i>Crecopsis</i>, and later authors sometimes placed it in <i><a href="/wiki/Porzana" title="Porzana">Porzana</a></i>, based on a resemblance to the <a href="/wiki/Ash-throated_crake" title="Ash-throated crake">ash-throated crake</a>, <i>P. albicollis</i>. Structural differences rule out <i>Porzana</i>, and the placement in <i>Crex</i> is now the most common and best-supported treatment.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor29_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor29-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Livezey1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Livezey1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">Phylogeny</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a> confirm that the <i>Porzana</i> crakes are the closest relatives of the genus <i>Crex</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Livezey2_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Livezey2-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The genus name is <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeic</a>, referring to the repetitive grating call of the corn crake,<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and the species name <i>egregia</i> derives from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>egregius</i>, "outstanding, prominent".<sup id="cite_ref-brookes_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brookes-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African_crake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The African crake is a smallish crake, 20–23&#160;cm (7.9–9.1&#160;in) long with a 40–42&#160;cm (16–17&#160;in) wingspan. The male has blackish upperparts streaked with olive-brown, apart from the nape and hindneck which are plain pale brown; there is a white streak from the base of the bill to above the eye. The sides of the head, foreneck, throat and breast are bluish-grey, the <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">flight feathers</a> are dark brown, and the flanks and sides of the belly are barred black and white. The eye is red, the bill is reddish, and the legs and feet are light brown or grey. The sexes are similar in appearance, although the female is slightly smaller and duller than the male, with a less contrasting head pattern. Immature birds have darker and duller upperparts than the adult, a dark bill, grey eye, and less barring on the underparts. There are no <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecific</a> or other geographical variations in plumage. This crake has a complete <a href="/wiki/Moulting" title="Moulting">moult</a> after breeding, mainly prior to migration.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor316_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor316-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Although this species occurs in fairly open habitats, it lacks the pure white undertail used for signalling in open water or <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gregarious" class="extiw" title="wikt:gregarious">gregarious</a> species like the <a href="/wiki/Coot" title="Coot">coots</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moorhen" title="Moorhen">moorhens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stang_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stang-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The African crake is smaller than the corn crake, which also has darker upperparts, a plain grey face and different underparts barring pattern. In flight, the African species has shorter, blunter wings with a less prominent white leading edge, and deeper wingbeats than its relative. Other <a href="/wiki/Sympatry" title="Sympatry">sympatric</a> crakes are smaller with white markings on the upperparts, different underparts patterns and a shorter bill. The <a href="/wiki/African_rail" title="African rail">African rail</a> has dark brown upperparts, a long red bill and red legs and feet.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor316_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor316-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African_crake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like other rails, this species has a wide range of vocalisations. The male's territorial and advertising call is a series of rapid grating <i>krrr</i> notes repeated two or three times a second for several minutes. It is given most often in the breeding season, usually early or late in the day, but sometimes continues after dark or starts before dawn. The male stands upright with his neck extended when advertising, but will also call when chasing intruders on the ground or in flight. Both sexes give a sharp, loud <i>kip</i> call as an alarm or during territorial interactions, adapting a similar pose as for the advertising call. Once breeding starts, the birds become much quieter, but territorial birds commence the <i>kip</i> call again during the non-breeding season, especially when there is a high density of African crakes in the area. A wheezy <i>kraaa</i> is associated with threat displays and copulation; imitation of this call by a human can bring a rail to within 10&#160;m (33&#160;ft). Newly hatched chicks make a soft <i>wheeeez</i> call, and older chicks cheep.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor316_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor316-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The rasping advertising call is readily distinguished from the <i>hwitt-hwitt-hwitt</i> of <a href="/wiki/Spotted_crake" title="Spotted crake">spotted crake</a>, the monotonous clockwork <i>tak-tak-tak-tak-tak</i> of <a href="/wiki/Striped_crake" title="Striped crake">striped crake</a>, or the <i>quick-quick</i> of <a href="/wiki/Baillon%27s_crake" title="Baillon&#39;s crake">Baillon's crake</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-newman_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newman-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The corn crake is silent in Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-serle_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-serle-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African_crake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:F_Cl_de_Dinderesso_MS4055.JPG" class="image"><img alt="tall grass with trees in the distance" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/F_Cl_de_Dinderesso_MS4055.JPG/220px-F_Cl_de_Dinderesso_MS4055.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/F_Cl_de_Dinderesso_MS4055.JPG/330px-F_Cl_de_Dinderesso_MS4055.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/F_Cl_de_Dinderesso_MS4055.JPG/440px-F_Cl_de_Dinderesso_MS4055.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2272" data-file-height="1704" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:F_Cl_de_Dinderesso_MS4055.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>African grassland, ideal African crake habitat.</div></div></div>
<p>The African crake occurs throughout sub-Saharan Africa from Senegal east to Kenya, and south to <a href="/wiki/KwaZulu-Natal" title="KwaZulu-Natal">KwaZulu-Natal</a>, South Africa, except in arid areas of south and southwest Africa where the annual summer rainfall is less than 300&#160;mm (12&#160;in). It is widespread and locally common in most of its range, apart from the <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforests</a> and the drier regions. Nearly all the South Africa population of about 8,000 birds occur in KwaZulu-Natal and the former <a href="/wiki/Transvaal_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Transvaal Province">Transvaal Province</a>, and much good habitat is protected in the <a href="/wiki/Kruger_National_Park" title="Kruger National Park">Kruger National Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/ISimangaliso_Wetland_Park" title="ISimangaliso Wetland Park">iSimangaliso Wetland Park</a>. This crake is only a <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrant</a> to southern Mauritania, southwest Niger, Lesotho, South Africa's northern and eastern <a href="/wiki/Cape_Province" title="Cape Province">Cape Province</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_West_(South_African_province)" title="North West (South African province)">North West Province</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-taylor316_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor316-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and southern Botswana.<sup id="cite_ref-sabap2_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sabap2-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Further afield, it is rare on <a href="/wiki/Bioko" title="Bioko">Bioko Island</a> (Equatorial Guinea),<sup id="cite_ref-larison_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-larison-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and there have been two records each for <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Tom%C3%A9_and_Pr%C3%ADncipe" title="São Tomé and Príncipe">São Tomé</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tenerife" title="Tenerife">Tenerife</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> birds being the first records for the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Palaeartic&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Western Palaeartic (page does not exist)">Western Palaeartic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SEO_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SEO-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-amadon_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amadon-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> remains from <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> suggest that the species was more widespread when the climate was wetter in what is now the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-petersjoris_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-petersjoris-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gautier_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gautier-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This crake is a partial <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>, but although it is less skulking than many of its relatives, its movements are complex and poorly studied; the distribution map is therefore largely hypothetical. It is mainly a wet-season breeder, and many birds move away from the equator as soon as the rains provide sufficient grass cover to allow them to breed elsewhere. Southward movement is mainly from November to April, the return north beginning when burning or drought reduces the grass cover again. This species is present throughout the year in some West African countries, and in equatorial regions, but even in those areas numbers vary seasonally due to local movements; north-south migration has been noted within countries including Nigeria, Senegal, The Gambia, Ivory Coast and Cameroon.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor316_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor316-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Migration takes place at night and involves small groups of up to eight birds;<sup id="cite_ref-BirdLife_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLife-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It may be one or two months after the rains begin before the grass is sufficiently high for breeding birds to arrive. Even in southern Africa, some birds may stay after breeding if enough usable habitat remains.<sup id="cite_ref-sabap2_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sabap2-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The habitat is predominantly grassland, ranging from wetland edges and seasonal marshes to <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a>, lightly wooded dry grassland, and grassy forest clearings. The crake also frequents corn, rice and cotton fields, derelict farmland and sugarcane plantations close to water. A wide range of grass species are used, with a preferred height of 0.3–1&#160;m (0.98–3.28&#160;ft) tall but vegetation is acceptable up to 2&#160;m (6.6&#160;ft) tall. It normally prefers moister and shorter grassland habitats than does the corn crake, and its breeding territories often contain or are close to thickets or <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termite</a> mounds. It occurs from sea level to 2,000&#160;m (6,600&#160;ft) but is rare in the higher altitude grasslands. Its grassland habitat is frequently burned in the dry season, forcing the birds to move elsewhere.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor316_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor316-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In an East African study, the average area occupied by one bird was 2.6 hectares (6.4 acres) when breeding, and 1.97 to 2.73&#160;ha (4.9 to 6.7 acres) at other times.<sup id="cite_ref-Taylorpb_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylorpb-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The highest densities occur in lush or moist grassland such as the <a href="/wiki/Okavango_Delta" title="Okavango Delta">Okavango Delta</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sabap2_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sabap2-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African_crake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Crecopsis_egregia00.jpg" class="image"><img alt="brown bird with grey head, striped flanks and red legs, facing left" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Crecopsis_egregia00.jpg/220px-Crecopsis_egregia00.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="285" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Crecopsis_egregia00.jpg/330px-Crecopsis_egregia00.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Crecopsis_egregia00.jpg/440px-Crecopsis_egregia00.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1360" data-file-height="1760" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Crecopsis_egregia00.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration by <a href="/wiki/Claude_Gibney_Finch-Davies" class="mw-redirect" title="Claude Gibney Finch-Davies">Claude Gibney Finch-Davies</a>, 1912</div></div></div>
<p>The African crake is active during the day, especially at dusk, during light rain, or after heavier rain. It is less skulking and easier to flush from cover than other crakes, and is often seen at the edges of roads and tracks. An observer in a vehicle can approach to within 1&#160;m (3.3&#160;ft). When a bird is flushed it normally flies less than 50&#160;m (160&#160;ft), but new arrivals may occasionally fly twice as far. A flushed crake will frequently land in a wet area or behind a thicket, and crouch on landing. In short grass, it can escape from a dog using its speed and manoeuvrability, running with the body held almost horizontal. It may roost in a depression near grass tussock and it will bathe in puddles.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor316_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor316-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The African crake is territorial on both the breeding and non-breeding grounds; the male threat display involves the bird standing upright and spreading the feathers of the flanks and belly like a fan to show the barred underparts. He may march towards the intruder, or walk side by side with another displaying male. The female may accompany the male, but with feathers less widely fanned. Fighting at territorial boundaries involves the male birds jumping at each other and pecking. Paired females will attack other females in the territory, especially if the male has shown an interest in them.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor316_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor316-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African_crake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Breeding behavior commences with a courtship chase with the female running in a crouch, pursued by the male, who adopts a more upright stance and has his neck outstretched. The female may stop and lower her head and tail to allow copulation; this takes just a few seconds, but may be repeated several times in an hour. The nest is a shallow cup of grass leaves, sometimes with a loose canopy, built in a depression and hidden under a grass tussock or small bush; it may be on dry ground or slightly raised above standing water, or occasionally floating. The nest is about 20&#160;cm (7.9&#160;in) across with the internal cup 2–5&#160;cm (0.79–1.97&#160;in) deep, and 11–12&#160;cm (4.3–4.7&#160;in) wide. The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> size is from 3 to 11 pink-coloured<sup id="cite_ref-sclater_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sclater-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> eggs; the first is often laid when the nest is little more than a pad of grass, and a further egg is laid on each subsequent day. Both sexes incubate, and the eggs start hatching after about 14&#160;days; all hatch with 48&#160;hours despite the extended laying period. The black, downy <a href="/wiki/Precocial" class="mw-redirect" title="Precocial">precocial</a> chicks soon leave the nest but are fed and protected by the parents. <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">Fledging</a> occurs after four to five weeks, and the young can fly before they are fully grown. It is not known whether a second brood is raised.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor316_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor316-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African_crake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The African crake feeds on <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> including <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gastropod" class="mw-redirect" title="Gastropod">gastropods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mollusc" class="mw-redirect" title="Mollusc">molluscs</a> and the adults and <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> of insects, especially <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a>. <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">Vertebrate</a> prey such as small frogs or fish may also be taken. Plant material is eaten, especially grass seeds, but also green shoots, leaves and other seeds. The crake searches for food both within vegetation and in the open, picking insects and seeds from the ground, turning over leaf litter, or digging with its bill in soft or very dry ground. It will chase faster moving prey, reach up to take food from plants, and wade to pluck food items from the water.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor316_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor316-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Crop plants such as rice, maize and peas may sometimes be eaten, but this bird is not an agricultural pest species.<sup id="cite_ref-Manikowski_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Manikowski-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Funmilayo_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Funmilayo-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> It forages singly, in pairs or in family groups, sometimes in association with other grassland birds such as <a href="/wiki/Great_snipe" title="Great snipe">great snipes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blue_quail" title="Blue quail">blue quails</a> and corn crakes.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor316_2-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor316-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Chicks are fed mainly on animal food. As with other rails, grit is swallowed to help break up food in the stomach.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor39_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor39-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators_and_parasites">Predators and parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African_crake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Predators and parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Predators include the <a href="/wiki/Leopard" title="Leopard">leopard</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-hill_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hill-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Serval" title="Serval">serval</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Black-headed_heron" title="Black-headed heron">black-headed heron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dark_chanting_goshawk" title="Dark chanting goshawk">dark chanting goshawk</a>, <a href="/wiki/African_hawk-eagle" title="African hawk-eagle">African hawk-eagle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wahlberg%27s_eagle" title="Wahlberg&#39;s eagle">Wahlberg's eagle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor316_2-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor316-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In South Africa, newly hatched chicks were taken by a <a href="/wiki/Boomslang" title="Boomslang">boomslang</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Haagner_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haagner-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> If surprised, an African crake will leap vertically into the air before running away, a tactic believed to help it to evade snakes or terrestrial mammals.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor44_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor44-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Parasites of this species include <a href="/wiki/Tick" title="Tick">ticks</a> of the family <a href="/wiki/Ixodidae" title="Ixodidae">Ixodidae</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-elbl_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elbl-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and a <a href="/wiki/Feather_mite" title="Feather mite">feather mite</a>, <i>Metanalges elongatus</i>, of the subspecies <i>M. e. curtus</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Subspecies#Nomenclature" title="Subspecies">nominate</a> form of the mite occurs thousands of kilometres away in <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-zumpt_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zumpt-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African_crake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The African crake has a huge breeding range estimated at 11,700,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (4,500,000&#160;mi<sup>2</sup>). Its population is unknown, but it is common in most of its range, and its numbers appear to be stable. It is therefore classed as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BirdLife_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLife-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Overgrazing, agriculture and the loss of wetland and moist grassland have reduced the availability of suitable habitat in many areas, such as some parts of the southern KwaZulu-Natal coast which have been urbanised or planted with sugarcane. In other areas, grassland may have increased locally in recent years as woodland is cleared. This crake is considered to be good eating, and is killed for food in some regions. Despite these adverse factors, it appears to be under no real threat.<sup id="cite_ref-taylor316_2-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor316-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although most rails in the Old World are covered by the <a href="/wiki/Agreement_on_the_Conservation_of_African-Eurasian_Migratory_Waterbirds" title="Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a> (AEWA), the African crake is not listed even in Kenya, where it is considered "near-threatened". Like its relative, the corn crake, it is too terrestrial to be classed as a wetland species.<sup id="cite_ref-aewa_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aewa-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
