<p>The <b>Greylag Goose</b> (also spelled <b>Graylag</b> in the United States), <i>Anser anser</i>, is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> with a wide range in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a>. It is the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Goose" title="Goose">Anser</a></i>.</p>
<p>It was in pre-<a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnean</a> times known as the <b>Wild Goose</b> ("Anser ferus"). This species is the ancestor of <a href="/wiki/Domesticated_goose" title="Domesticated goose" class="mw-redirect">domesticated geese</a> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. Flocks of <a href="/wiki/Feral" title="Feral">feral</a> birds derived from domesticated birds are widespread.</p>
<p>The Greylag Goose is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>
<p>Within science, the greylag goose is most notable as being the bird with which the <a href="/wiki/Ethology" title="Ethology">ethologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Lorenz" title="Konrad Lorenz">Konrad Lorenz</a> first did his major studying into the behavioural phenomenon of <a href="/wiki/Imprinting_(psychology)" title="Imprinting (psychology)">imprinting</a>.</p>

<p>The Greylag is the largest and bulkiest of the grey geese of the genus <i>Anser</i>. It has a rotund, bulky body, a thick and long neck, and a large head and bill. It has pink legs and feet, and an orange or pink bill.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> It is 74 to 91 cm (29 to 36 in) long with a wing length of 41.2 to 48 cm (16.2 to 19 in). It has a tail 6.2 to 6.9 cm (2.4 to 2.7 in), a bill of 6.4 to 6.9 centimetres (2.5 to 2.7 in) long, and a tarsus of 7.1 to 9.3 centimetres (2.8 to 3.7 in). It weighs 2.16 to 4.56 kg (4.8 to 10.1 lb), with a mean weight of around 3.3&#160;kg (7.3&#160;lb). The wingspan is 147 to 180 cm (58 to 71 in).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Males are generally larger than females, with the sexual dimorphism more pronounced in the eastern subspecies <i>rubirostris</i>, which is larger than the nominate subspecies on average.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> of the Greylag Goose is greyish-brown, with a darker head and paler belly with variable black spots. Its plumage is patterned by the pale fringes of its feathers. It has a white line bordering its upper flanks. Its <a href="/wiki/Covert_(feather)" title="Covert (feather)" class="mw-redirect">coverts</a> are lightly coloured, contrasting with its darker flight feathers. Juveniles differ mostly in their lack of a black-speckled belly.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Johnsgard_60_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnsgard_60-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It has a loud cackling call, <i>HOOOOOONK!</i>, like the domestic goose.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Greylag_Goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>This species is found throughout the Old World, apparently breeding where suitable localities are to be found in many European countries, although it no longer breeds in southwestern Europe. Eastwards it extends across <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. In North America there are both feral domestic geese, which are similar to greylags, and occasional <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrants</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Johnsgard_60_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnsgard_60-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> their numbers have declined as a breeding bird, retreating north to breed wild only in the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Hebrides" title="Outer Hebrides">Outer Hebrides</a> and the northern mainland of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. However during the 20th century, feral populations have been established elsewhere, and they have now re-colonised much of England. The breeding habitat is a variety of wetlands including marshes, lakes, and damp heather moors.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, the number of greylag geese is estimated to have increased three- to fivefold during the last 15–20 years. As a consequence, farmers' problems caused by goose grazing on farmland has increased considerably. This problem is also evident for the <a href="/wiki/Pink-footed_goose" title="Pink-footed goose" class="mw-redirect">pink-footed goose</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Greylag_Goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<p>The geese are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, moving south or west in winter, but Scottish breeders, some other populations in northwestern Europe, and feral flocks are largely resident. This species is one of the last to migrate, and the "<a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lag" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:lag">lag</a>" portion of its name is said to derive from this lagging behind other geese.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
