<p><b>Cetti's warbler</b> <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/tʃ/: &#39;ch&#39; in &#39;China&#39;">tʃ</span><span title="/ɛ/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">ɛ</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/i/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;happy&#39;">i</span></span>/</a></span></span> (<i>Cettia cetti</i>) is a small, brown <a href="/wiki/Bush-warbler" title="Bush-warbler">bush-warbler</a> which breeds in southern and central <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, northwest <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and the east <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> as far as <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> and northwest <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>. The sexes are alike. The bird is named after the 18th century <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoologist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Cetti" title="Francesco Cetti">Francesco Cetti</a>. This species is very difficult to see because of its skulking habits.<sup id="cite_ref-Seago_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seago-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cetti%27s_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cetti's warbler was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> in 1820 by the Dutch zoologist <a href="/wiki/Coenraad_Jacob_Temminck" title="Coenraad Jacob Temminck">Coenraad Jacob Temminck</a> from specimens collected by <a href="/wiki/Alberto_della_Marmora" title="Alberto della Marmora">Alberto della Marmora</a> in Sardinia. Temminck coined the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> <i>Sylvia cetti</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The specific epithet was chosen to commemorate the Italian zoologist <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Cetti" title="Francesco Cetti">Francesco Cetti</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Cetti's warbler is now placed in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Cettia" title="Cettia">Cettia</a></i> that was erected in 1834 by the French ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lucien_Bonaparte" title="Charles Lucien Bonaparte">Charles Lucien Bonaparte</a> with Cetti's warbler as the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ioc_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Three subspecies are recognised:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>C. c. cetti</i> (Temminck, 1820) – west Europe to Greece and the Balkans, northwest Africa</li>
<li><i>C. c. orientalis</i> <a href="/wiki/Henry_Baker_Tristram" title="Henry Baker Tristram">Tristram</a>, 1867 – Turkey to Iran and Afghanistan</li>
<li><i>C. c. albiventris</i> <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Severtzov" title="Nikolai Severtzov">Severtsov</a>, 1873 – Kazakhstan and Turkmenistan to northwest China and north Afghanistan</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cetti%27s_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Cetti's warbler is approximately 13–14&#160;cm (5.1–5.5&#160;in) from bill to tail.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The male weighs 15&#160;g (0.53&#160;oz) and the female 12&#160;g (0.42&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-BTOfacts_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTOfacts-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Cetti's warbler has a rounded head with a narrow pale grey stripe arching over conspicuous black eyes, and short, rounded wings. The upperparts are a rich chestnut or dark reddish-brown; the throat and breast are pale grey. The tail is longer and broader than many other warblers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
The sexes have similar plumage, but males are 26% to 32% heavier than females with a wing-length 11.2% to 13.% longer. The male has a wing longer than 60&#160;mm; the female has a wing shorter than 55&#160;mm. The birds can be aged by their plumage; juveniles look similar to adults but have fresh plumage and two dark or dark grey spots on the tongue.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Cetti's warbler is unusual among <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> birds in having ten tail feathers (<a href="/wiki/Rectrices" class="mw-redirect" title="Rectrices">rectrices</a>) rather than the usual twelve.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cetti's warblers signal their presence with loud <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">song</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Their song is distinct, comes in loud bursts, and has a unique structure that allows the birds to avoid mating with other species.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cetti%27s_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Cetti's warbler usually inhabits damp areas including <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">ponds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a> and <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Insectivore" title="Insectivore">insectivorous</a>. 
</p><p>The number of Cetti's warblers has greatly increased across <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> since 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-EBCC_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EBCC-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BTO_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The current population of Cetti's warblers in Europe is estimated to be about <span class="nowrap">600,000-1,600,000 breeding</span> pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-BirdLife_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLife-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The populations in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> are known to be stable or increasing. The exception to the general positive European population trend is <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, where the population decreased slightly between 1990 and 2000. However, overall, Cetti's warblers are evaluated as secure.<sup id="cite_ref-BirdsEurope_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdsEurope-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The Cetti's warbler was first recorded in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> in 1961.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_report_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO_report-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The UK population of Cetti's warblers fell by over a third between 1984 and 1986. However, populations in milder regions continued to grow.<sup id="cite_ref-BreedingBirds_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BreedingBirds-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cetti%27s_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cetti%27s_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the summer, the males spend most of their time establishing their <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territories</a>. While doing this, they spend little amount of time caring for the <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)#Bird_eggs" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> or young. Afterwards, the males usually attract more than one female to their territory.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Their song plays an important role.
</p><p>In Europe the main period for egg laying is from the middle to the end of June. The nest is placed in thick dense vegetation usually at around 30 to 45&#160;cm (12 to 18&#160;in) above the ground. 
The untidy cup shaped nest is made from leaves and stems and is lined with feathers, hair and other finer material. It is built entirely by the female. The eggs are laid in the early morning at daily intervals and are chestnut red in colour. The clutch consists of 4 to 5 eggs which measure approximately 18.0&#160;mm ×&#160;13.9&#160;mm (0.71&#160;in ×&#160;0.55&#160;in). The eggs are incubated by the female beginning when the clutch is complete and hatch after 16 to 17 days. The young are mainly fed and brooded by the female. They fledge after 14 to 16 days but continue to be fed by the parents for at least another 15 days. The young become independent by 30 days. In Europe Cetti's warblers normally have two broods each year. They first breed when one year of age.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp199217_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp199217-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cetti%27s_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cetti's warbler preys on <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> such as small, soft-bodied <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a>. Cetti's warblers prefer tiny insects because they can digest them faster.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
