<p>The <b>alpine accentor</b> (<i>Prunella collaris</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird in the family <a href="/wiki/Prunellidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Prunellidae">Prunellidae</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpine_accentor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Alpine accentor was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> by the Austria naturalist <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Antonio_Scopoli" title="Giovanni Antonio Scopoli">Giovanni Antonio Scopoli</a> in 1769. He coined the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> <i>Sturnus collaris</i> and specified the <a href="/wiki/Type_locality_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Type locality (biology)">type locality</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Carinthia" title="Carinthia">Carinthia</a> region of southern Austria.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Specific_epithet" class="mw-redirect" title="Specific epithet">specific epithet</a> is from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>collaris</i> "of the neck".<sup id="cite_ref-hbwkey_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwkey-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This species is now placed in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Prunella_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Prunella (bird)">Prunella</a></i> that was introduced by the French ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Louis_Jean_Pierre_Vieillot" title="Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot">Louis Vieillot</a> in 1816.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The Alpine accentor, along with the <a href="/wiki/Altai_accentor" title="Altai accentor">Altai accentor</a> is sometimes separated from the other accentors, into the genus <i>Laiscopus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The word "accentor" is from post-classical <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means a person who sings with another.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The genus name <i>Prunella</i> is from the German <i>Braunelle</i>, "dunnock", a diminutive of <i>braun</i>, "brown".<sup id="cite_ref-job_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Nine <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are recognised:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>P. c. collaris</i> (<a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Antonio_Scopoli" title="Giovanni Antonio Scopoli">Scopoli</a>, 1769) – southwest Europe to Slovenia and the Carpathians, northwest Africa</li>
<li><i>P. c. subalpina</i> (<a href="/wiki/Christian_Ludwig_Brehm" title="Christian Ludwig Brehm">Brehm, CL</a>, 1831) – Croatia to Bulgaria and Greece, Crete and southwest Turkey</li>
<li><i>P. c. montana</i> (<a href="/wiki/Carl_Ludwig_Hablitz" title="Carl Ludwig Hablitz">Hablizl</a>, 1783) – north and east Turkey to the Caucasus and Iran</li>
<li><i>P. c. rufilata</i> (<a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Severtzov" title="Nikolai Severtzov">Severtzov</a>, 1879) – northeast Afghanistan and north Pakistan through the mountains of central Asia to west China</li>
<li><i>P. c. whymperi</i> (<a href="/wiki/E._C._Stuart_Baker" title="E. C. Stuart Baker">Baker, ECS</a>, 1915) – west Himalayas</li>
<li><i>P. c. nipalensis</i> (<a href="/wiki/Edward_Blyth" title="Edward Blyth">Blyth</a>, 1843) – central and east Himalayas to southcentral China and north Myanmar</li>
<li><i>P. c. tibetana</i> (<a href="/wiki/Valentin_Bianchi" title="Valentin Bianchi">Bianchi</a>, 1905) – east Tibet</li>
<li><i>P. c. erythropygia</i> (<a href="/wiki/Robert_Swinhoe" title="Robert Swinhoe">R. Swinhoe</a>, 1870) – east Kazakhstan and southcentral Siberia to northeast Siberia, Japan, Korea and northeast China</li>
<li><i>P. c. fennelli</i> <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Girton_Deignan" title="Herbert Girton Deignan">Deignan</a>, 1964 – Taiwan</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpine_accentor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alpine_Accentor_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_19.04.2016.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Alpine_Accentor_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_19.04.2016.jpg/220px-Alpine_Accentor_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_19.04.2016.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Alpine_Accentor_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_19.04.2016.jpg/330px-Alpine_Accentor_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_19.04.2016.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Alpine_Accentor_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_19.04.2016.jpg/440px-Alpine_Accentor_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_19.04.2016.jpg 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="506" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alpine_Accentor_Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary_East_Sikkim_India_19.04.2016.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>From <a href="/wiki/Pangolakha_Wildlife_Sanctuary" title="Pangolakha Wildlife Sanctuary">Pangolakha Wildlife Sanctuary</a> in <a href="/wiki/East_Sikkim" class="mw-redirect" title="East Sikkim">East Sikkim</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>This is a <a href="/wiki/European_robin" title="European robin">robin-sized</a> bird at 15–17.5&#160;cm in length, slightly larger than its relative, the <a href="/wiki/Dunnock" title="Dunnock">dunnock</a>. It has a streaked brown back, somewhat resembling a <a href="/wiki/House_sparrow" title="House sparrow">house sparrow</a>, but adults have a grey head and red-brown spotting on the underparts. It has an insectivore's fine pointed bill.
</p><p>Sexes are similar, although the male may be contrasted in appearance. Young birds have browner heads and underparts.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpine_accentor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is found throughout the mountains of southern temperate Europe, <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Asia</a> at heights above 2000&#160;m.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It is mainly resident, wintering more widely at lower latitudes, but some <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">wander</a> as rare vagrants as far as <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>.
</p><p>It is a bird of bare mountain areas with some low vegetation.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpine_accentor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It builds a neat nest low in a bush or rock crevice, laying 3-5 unspotted sky-blue eggs.
</p><p>The mating system is of particular interest.  Home ranges are occupied by breeding groups of 3 or 4 males with 3 or 4 females.  These are unrelated birds which have a socially <a href="/wiki/Polygynandry" title="Polygynandry">polygynandrous</a> mating system.  Males have a dominance hierarchy, with the alpha males being generally older than subordinates.  Females seek matings with all the males, although the alpha male may defend her against matings from lower ranking males.  In turn, males seek matings with all the females.  DNA fingerprinting has been used to show that, within broods, there is often mixed paternity, although the female is always the true mother of the nestlings raised within her nest.  Males will provide food to chicks at several nests within the group, depending on whether they have mated with the female or not - males only provide care when they are likely to be the true fathers of the chicks.
</p>
