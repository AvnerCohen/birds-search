<p>The <b>Brown-throated Martin</b> or <b>Brown-throated Sand Martin</b> (<i>Riparia paludicola</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Hirundinidae" title="Hirundinidae" class="mw-redirect">swallow family</a>. It was first formally described as <i>Hirundo paludicola</i> by <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Ornithologist" title="Ornithologist" class="mw-redirect">ornithologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Louis_Jean_Pierre_Vieillot" title="Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot">Louis Vieillot</a> in 1817 in his <i>Nouveau Dictionnaire d'Histoire Naturelle</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-viellot_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-viellot-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It has a wide range in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and across <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and other parts of southern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">partially migratory</a> species, with some populations making seasonal movements. It is usually associated closely with water.</p>
<p>The Brown-throated Martin is colonial in its nesting habits, with many pairs breeding close together, according to available space. The nests are at the end of tunnels of 30 to 60&#160;cm in length, bored in sandbanks. The actual nest is a litter of straw and feathers in a chamber at the end of the burrow. Two to four white eggs are the normal clutch, and are incubated by both parents.</p>
<p>Its brown back, small size and quicker, jerkier flight separate Brown-throated Martin at once from most other members of the swallow family. It is most similar to the <a href="/wiki/Sand_Martin" title="Sand Martin">Sand Martin</a>, <i>Riparia riparia</i> , which is its northern counterpart. Where the breeding ranges overlap in southern Asia, Brown-throated Sand Martins tend to breed at lower altitudes than their relative. In the northern winter, both species are found in the same wetland habitats.</p>
<p>The 12&#160;cm long Brown-throated Martin is brown above and white or pale brown below. It lacks the narrow brown band on the breast shown by the Sand Martin; the bill is black and the legs are brown. Sexes are similar, but the young have pale tips to the feathers on the rump and wings.</p>
<p>There are seven races differing in size and the plumage tones of the upperparts or <a href="/w/index.php?title=Underparts&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Underparts (page does not exist)">underparts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Turner_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Turner-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>R. p. paludicola</i>, southern Africa. White underparts.</li>
<li><i>R. p. paludibula</i>, western Africa. Smaller and darker above than the nominate form.</li>
<li><i>R. p. ducis</i>, eastern Africa. Smaller and darker above and below than the nominate subspecies.</li>
<li><i>R. p. mauretanica</i>, <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>. Small and pale</li>
<li><i>R. p. newtoni</i>, mountains of <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a> only. Darker above than the nominate form, brownish underparts.</li>
<li><i>R. p. cowani</i>, <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>. Small, greyish underparts.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>R. p. chinensis</i>, southern Asia. Pale underparts and throat.</li>
</ul>
<p>The food of this species consists of small <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, mostly gnats and other flies whose early stages are aquatic.</p>
<p>The twittering song of Brown-throated Martin is continuous when the birds are on the wing, and becomes a conversational undertone after they have settled in the roost. There is also a harsh alarm call.</p>
