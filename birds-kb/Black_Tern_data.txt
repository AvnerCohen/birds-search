<p>The <b>black tern</b> (<i>Chlidonias niger</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">tern</a> generally found in or near inland water in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Western Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. As its name suggests, it has predominantly dark plumage. In some lights it can appear blue in the breeding season, hence the old English name "blue darr".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The genus name is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>khelidonios</i>, "swallow-like", from <i>khelidon</i>, "<a href="/wiki/Swallow" title="Swallow">swallow</a>": another old English name  for the black tern is  "carr (i.e. lake)  swallow".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The species name is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>niger</i> "shining black".<sup id="cite_ref-job_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Black_Tern_from_the_Crossley_ID_Guide_Britain_and_Ireland.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Black_Tern_from_the_Crossley_ID_Guide_Britain_and_Ireland.jpg/220px-Black_Tern_from_the_Crossley_ID_Guide_Britain_and_Ireland.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="285" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Black_Tern_from_the_Crossley_ID_Guide_Britain_and_Ireland.jpg/330px-Black_Tern_from_the_Crossley_ID_Guide_Britain_and_Ireland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Black_Tern_from_the_Crossley_ID_Guide_Britain_and_Ireland.jpg/440px-Black_Tern_from_the_Crossley_ID_Guide_Britain_and_Ireland.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1647" data-file-height="2130" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Black_Tern_from_the_Crossley_ID_Guide_Britain_and_Ireland.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>ID composite</div></div></div>
<p>Adults are 25&#160;cm (9.8&#160;in) long, with a wingspan 61&#160;cm (24&#160;in), and weigh 62&#160;g (2.2&#160;oz). They have short dark legs and a short, weak-looking black bill, measuring 27&#160;mm (1.1&#160;in), nearly as long as the head. The bill is long, slender, and looks slightly decurved. They have a dark grey back, with a white forewing, black head, neck (occasionally suffused with grey in the adult) and belly, black or blackish-brown cap (which unites in color with the ear coverts, forming an almost complete hood), and a light brownish-grey, 'square' tail. The face is white. There is a big dark triangular patch in front of the eye, and a broadish white collar in juveniles. There are greyish-brown smudges on the ides of the white breast, a downwards extension of the plumage of the upperparts. These marks vary in size and are not conspicuous. In non-breeding plumage, most of the black, apart from the cap, is replaced by grey. The plumage of the upperparts is drab, with pale feather-edgings. The rump is brownish-grey.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> race, <i>C. n. surinamensis</i>, is distinguishable from the European form in all plumages, and is considered by some to be a separate species.
</p><p>In flight, the build appears slim. The wing-beats are full and dynamic, and flight is often erratic as it dives to the surface for food; similar to other tern species.
</p><p>Its call has been described as a high-pitched "kik";  the sound of a large flock has been called  "deafening".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hybridisation_with_white-winged_black_tern">Hybridisation with white-winged black tern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hybridisation with white-winged black tern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hybridisation between this species and <a href="/wiki/White-winged_black_tern" class="mw-redirect" title="White-winged black tern">white-winged black tern</a> has been recorded from Sweden<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and the Netherlands.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Two juvenile birds at <a href="/wiki/Chew_Valley_Lake" title="Chew Valley Lake">Chew Valley Lake</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, in September 1978 and September 1981, were also believed to be hybrids; they showed mixed characters of the two species, specifically a combination of a dark mantle (a feature of white-winged black) with dark patches on the breast-side (a feature of black tern, not shown by white-winged black).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Their breeding habitat is freshwater marshes across most of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, the northern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and much of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Asia</a>. They usually nest either on floating material in a marsh or on the ground very close to water, laying 2–4 eggs.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>  the black tern was abundant in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Fens" class="mw-redirect" title="Fens">fens</a>, especially in <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a>,  until the early nineteenth century.  The English naturalist  <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Pennant" title="Thomas Pennant">Thomas Pennant</a>   in 1769 referred to   "vast flocks"  of black terns  "whose calls are almost deafening."  Extensive drainage of its breeding grounds wiped out the English population by about 1840. Intermittent attempts by the black tern  to recolonise England have proved unsuccessful, with only a handful of English breeding records, and one in Ireland,  in the second half of the twentieth century.
</p><p>North American black terns <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> to the coasts of northern <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, some to the open ocean. Old World birds winter in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.
</p><p>Unlike the "white" <i>Sterna</i> terns, these birds do not dive for fish, but forage on the wing picking up items at or near the water's surface or catching insects in flight. They mainly eat insects and fish as well as amphibians.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vagrancy">Vagrancy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vagrancy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The American race has occurred as a <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_in_birds" class="mw-redirect" title="Vagrancy in birds">vagrant</a> in Britain<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and in Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The North American population has declined in recent times due to loss of habitat.
</p><p>The black tern is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>
