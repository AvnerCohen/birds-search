<p>The <b>Northern Gannet</b> (<i>Morus bassanus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> and is the largest member of the <a href="/wiki/Gannet" title="Gannet">gannet</a> family, <a href="/wiki/Sulidae" title="Sulidae">Sulidae</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Gannet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Northern_Gannet_juvenile_RWD.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Northern_Gannet_juvenile_RWD.jpg/220px-Northern_Gannet_juvenile_RWD.jpg" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Northern_Gannet_juvenile_RWD.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Young birds are dark brown</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Two_Gannets_edit_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Two_Gannets_edit_2.jpg/220px-Two_Gannets_edit_2.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Two_Gannets_edit_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Pair at <a href="/wiki/Gasp%C3%A9_Peninsula" title="Gaspé Peninsula">Gaspé Peninsula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Young birds are dark brown in their first year, and gradually acquire more white in subsequent seasons until they reach maturity after five years.</p>
<p>Adults are 81–110 cm (32–43 in) long, weigh 2.2–3.6 kg (4.9–7.9 lb) and have a 165–180 cm (65–71 in) wingspan. Before fledging, the immature birds (at about 10 weeks of age) can weigh more than 4&#160;kg (8.8&#160;lb). Their plumage is white with black wing tips. The bill is light bluish. The eye is light blue, and it is surrounded by bare, black skin. During breeding, the head and neck are brushed in a delicate yellow.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Gannet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:442px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Northern_Gannet_colonies.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Northern_Gannet_colonies.png/440px-Northern_Gannet_colonies.png" width="440" height="155" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Northern_Gannet_colonies.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Breeding colonies in the north Atlantic<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Their breeding range is the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">North Atlantic</a>. They normally <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a> in large colonies, on cliffs overlooking the ocean or on small rocky islands. The largest colony of this bird, with over 60,000 couples, is found on <a href="/wiki/Bonaventure_Island" title="Bonaventure Island">Bonaventure Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, but 68% of the world population breeds around the coasts of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, with the largest colonies on the <a href="/wiki/Bass_Rock" title="Bass Rock">Bass Rock</a> (whence the species' Latin name) and <a href="/wiki/Boreray,_St_Kilda" title="Boreray, St Kilda">Boreray, St Kilda</a>.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, Gannets are a protected species. However, a legal exception is made for the inhabitants of the district of <a href="/wiki/Ness,_Lewis" title="Ness, Lewis">Ness</a> (also known as Nis) of the <a href="/wiki/Lewis" title="Lewis">Isle of Lewis</a> who are allowed to kill up to 2000 gannets (locally known as <i>guga</i>) annually to serve as a traditional local delicacy—the taste is described as <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fishy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Many of these Gannets are taken from <a href="/wiki/Sula_Sgeir" title="Sula Sgeir">Sula Sgeir</a>, which is itself named after them.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Gannet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span></h2>
<p>Gannet pairs may remain together over several seasons. They perform elaborate greeting rituals at the nest, stretching their bills and necks skywards and gently tapping bills together.</p>
<p>They are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and most winter at sea, heading further south in the Atlantic.</p>
<p>These birds are spectacular divers, plunging into the ocean at high speed, with their bodies completely straightened out like an arrow before striking the water. If a fish is taken after diving, gannets swallow the fish underwater before surfacing. Although they are strong and agile fliers, they are clumsy in takeoffs and landings. They mainly eat small <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> (2.5–30.5&#160;cm in length) which gather in groups near the surface. Virtually any small fish (roughly 80–90% of the diet) or other small <a href="/wiki/Pelagic" title="Pelagic" class="mw-redirect">pelagic</a> species (largely <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a>) will be taken opportunistically. Various <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smelt_(fish)" title="Smelt (fish)">smelt</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herring</a> species are most frequently taken.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Although Northern Gannet populations are now stable, their numbers were once greatly reduced due to loss of <a href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" title="Habitat (ecology)" class="mw-redirect">habitat</a>, removal of eggs and killing of adults.</p>
<p>Predators of eggs and nestlings include <a href="/wiki/Great_black-backed_gull" title="Great black-backed gull" class="mw-redirect">Great Black-backed</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_Herring_Gull" title="American Herring Gull">Herring Gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_Raven" title="Common Raven">Common Ravens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ermine" title="Ermine" class="mw-redirect">ermine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Red_fox" title="Red fox">red fox</a>. The only known natural predator of adults is the <a href="/wiki/Bald_Eagle" title="Bald Eagle">Bald Eagle</a>, though large <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">seals</a> may rarely snatch a gannet out at sea.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Old names for the Northern Gannet include Solan, Solan Goose, and Solant Bird.</p>
