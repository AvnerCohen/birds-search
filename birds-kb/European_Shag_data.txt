<p>The <b>European Shag</b> or <b>Common Shag</b> (<i>Phalacrocorax aristotelis</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Cormorant" title="Cormorant">cormorant</a>. It breeds around the rocky coasts of western and southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, southwest <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and north <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, mainly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">wintering</a> in its breeding range except for northernmost birds. In <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a> this <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> is usually referred to as simply the <b>Shag</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Shag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>This is a medium-large black <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a>, 68–78&#160;cm long and with a 95���110&#160;cm wingspan. It has a longish tail and yellow throat-patch. Adults have a small crest in the breeding season. It is distinguished from the <a href="/wiki/Great_Cormorant" title="Great Cormorant">Great Cormorant</a> by its smaller size, lighter build, thinner bill, and, in breeding adults, by the crest and metallic green-tinged sheen on the <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a>. Among those differences are that a shag has a lighter, narrower beak; and the juvenile shag has darker underparts. The European Shag's tail has 12 feathers, the Great Cormorant's 14 feathers. The green sheen on the feathers results in the alternative name <b>"Green Cormorant"</b> sometimes being given to the European Shag.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Shag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:EuropeanShag.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/EuropeanShag.jpg/220px-EuropeanShag.jpg" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/EuropeanShag.jpg/330px-EuropeanShag.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/65/EuropeanShag.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:EuropeanShag.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Shag in flight</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It feeds in the sea, and, unlike the Great Cormorant, is rare inland. It will winter along any coast that is well-supplied with <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>.</p>
<p>The European Shag is one of the deepest divers among the cormorant family. Using <a href="/wiki/Depth_gauge" title="Depth gauge">depth gauges</a>, European Shags have been shown to dive to at least 45 metres. European Shags are preponderantly <a href="/wiki/Benthic" title="Benthic" class="mw-redirect">benthic</a> feeders, i.e. they find their prey on the sea bottom. They will eat a wide range of fish but their commonest prey is the <a href="/wiki/Sand_eel" title="Sand eel">sand eel</a>. Shags will travel many kilometres from their roosting sites in order to feed.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a> coastal waters, dive times are typically around 20–45 seconds, with a recovery time of around 15 seconds between dives; this is consistent with aerobic diving, i.e. the bird depends on the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> in its <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a> and dissolved in its <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">bloodstream</a> during the dive. When they dive, they jump out of the water first to give extra impetus to the dive.</p>
<p>It breeds on coasts, nesting on rocky ledges or in crevices or small caves. The nests are untidy heaps of rotting <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweed</a> or twigs cemented together by the bird's own <a href="/wiki/Guano" title="Guano">guano</a>. The nesting season is long, beginning in late February but some nests not starting until May or even later. Three <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> are laid. Their chicks hatch without down and so they rely totally on their parents for warmth, often for a period of two months before they can fly. Fledging may occur at any time from early June to late August, exceptionally to mid October.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Shag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span></h2>
<p>There are three <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Phalacrocorax aristotelis aristotelis</i> - northwestern Europe (<a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> coasts)</li>
<li><i>Phalacrocorax aristotelis desmarestii</i> - southern Europe, southwest Asia (<a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> coasts)</li>
<li><i>Phalacrocorax aristotelis riggenbachi</i> - northwest African coast</li>
</ul>
<p>The subspecies differ slightly in bill size and the breast and leg colour of young birds. Recent evidence suggests that birds on the Atlantic coast of southwest Europe are distinct from all three, and may be an as-yet undescribed subspecies (Yésou et al., <i><a href="/wiki/British_Birds_(magazine)" title="British Birds (magazine)">Brit. Birds</a></i> 98: 369-370, 2005).</p>
<p>The name shag is also used in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a> for several additional species of <a href="/wiki/Cormorant" title="Cormorant">cormorants</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Shag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Example locations">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Example_locations">Example locations</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:European_shags_in_mating_plume.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/European_shags_in_mating_plume.jpg/220px-European_shags_in_mating_plume.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/European_shags_in_mating_plume.jpg/330px-European_shags_in_mating_plume.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/European_shags_in_mating_plume.jpg/440px-European_shags_in_mating_plume.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:European_shags_in_mating_plume.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
European Shags in breeding plumage, Snæfellsnes, Iceland</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The European Shag can be readily be seen at the following breeding locations in the season (late April to mid July): <a href="/wiki/Farne_Islands" title="Farne Islands">Farne Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>; <a href="/wiki/Fowlsheugh" title="Fowlsheugh">Fowlsheugh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>; <a href="/wiki/Runde" title="Runde">Runde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>; <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a>.</p>
<p>The largest colony of European Shags is in the <a href="/wiki/Cies_Islands" title="Cies Islands" class="mw-redirect">Cies Islands</a>, with 2,500 pairs (25% of the world's population).</p>
