<p>The <b>European shag</b> or <b>common shag</b> (<i>Phalacrocorax aristotelis</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Cormorant" title="Cormorant">cormorant</a>. It breeds around the rocky coasts of western and southern Europe, southwest Asia and north Africa, mainly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">wintering</a> in its breeding range except for the northernmost birds. In <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a> this <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> is usually referred to as simply the <b>shag</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-oceanwanderers_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oceanwanderers-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The scientific <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> name is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latinised</a> <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>, from φαλακρός (<i>phalakros</i>, "bald") and κόραξ (<i>korax</i>, "raven"). The species name <i>aristotelis</i> commemorates the Greek <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">philosopher</a> <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-job90_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job90-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>This is a medium-large black <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a>, 68 to 78&#160;cm (27 to 31&#160;in) long and with a 95-to-110-centimetre (37 to 43&#160;in) wingspan. It has a longish tail and yellow throat-patch. Adults have a small crest in the breeding season. It is distinguished from the <a href="/wiki/Great_cormorant" title="Great cormorant">great cormorant</a> by its smaller size, lighter build, thinner bill, and, in breeding adults, by the crest and metallic green-tinged sheen on the <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a>. Among those differences are that a shag has a lighter, narrower beak; and the juvenile shag has darker underparts. The European shag's tail has 12 feathers, the great cormorant's 14 feathers. The green sheen on the feathers results in the alternative name <b>green cormorant</b> sometimes being given to the European shag.
</p>

<p>It feeds in the sea, and, unlike the great cormorant, is rare inland. It will winter along any coast that is well-supplied with <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Phalacrocorax_aristotelis_MWNH_0525.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Phalacrocorax_aristotelis_MWNH_0525.JPG/220px-Phalacrocorax_aristotelis_MWNH_0525.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Phalacrocorax_aristotelis_MWNH_0525.JPG/330px-Phalacrocorax_aristotelis_MWNH_0525.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Phalacrocorax_aristotelis_MWNH_0525.JPG/440px-Phalacrocorax_aristotelis_MWNH_0525.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3878" data-file-height="2618" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Phalacrocorax_aristotelis_MWNH_0525.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>The European shag is one of the deepest divers among the cormorant family. Using <a href="/wiki/Depth_gauge" title="Depth gauge">depth gauges</a>, European shags have been shown to dive to at least 45&#160;m (148&#160;ft). European shags are preponderantly <a href="/wiki/Benthic" class="mw-redirect" title="Benthic">benthic</a> feeders, i.e. they find their prey on the sea bottom. They will eat a wide range of fish but their commonest prey is the <a href="/wiki/Sand_eel" title="Sand eel">sand eel</a>. Shags will travel many kilometres from their roosting sites in order to feed.
</p><p>In UK coastal waters, dive times are typically around 20 to 45 seconds, with a recovery time of around 15 seconds between dives; this is consistent with aerobic diving, i.e. the bird depends on the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> in its <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a> and dissolved in its <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">bloodstream</a> during the dive. When they dive, they jump out of the water first to give extra impetus to the dive.
</p><p>It breeds on coasts, nesting on rocky ledges or in crevices or small caves. The nests are untidy heaps of rotting <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweed</a> or twigs cemented together by the bird's own <a href="/wiki/Guano" title="Guano">guano</a>. The nesting season is long, beginning in late February but some nests are not started until May or even later. Three <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> are laid. Their chicks hatch without <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">down</a> and so they rely totally on their parents for warmth, often for a period of two months before they can fly. Fledging may occur at any time from early June to late August, exceptionally to mid-October.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_shag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The shag is a pursuit-diving seabird that feeds predominantly in benthic habitats. Due to the relative ease with which diet samples can be collected from this species (regurgitated food or pellets) and the perceived conflict between the Phalacrocoracidae and fisheries, shag diet competition has been the subject of substantial scientific interest.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Evidence collected at one colony, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_May" title="Isle of May">Isle of May</a>, Scotland, between 1985 and 2014, suggests that shag chick diet composition in this population has diversified in response to ocean warming.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Shags also feed on fewer sandeel on windy days, presumably due to the strong effect of wind on flight in this species.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The year-round diet of full-grown shags at this colony has also changed over the past 3 decades, from sandeel specialists to an increasingly diverse prey base.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_shag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are three <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-IOC_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IOC-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>P. a. aristotelis</i> – <small>(<a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, 1761)</small>: <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies#Nominotypical_subspecies_and_subspecies_autonyms" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate</a>, found in northwestern Europe (Atlantic Ocean coasts)</li>
<li><i>P. a.  desmarestii</i> – <small>(<a href="/wiki/Charles_Payraudeau" title="Charles Payraudeau">Payraudeau</a>, 1826)</small>: found in southern Europe, southwest Asia (Mediterranean and <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> coasts)</li>
<li><i>P. a.  riggenbachi</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a>, 1923</small>: found in northwest African coast</li></ul>
<p>The subspecies differ slightly in bill size and the breast and leg colour of young birds. Recent evidence suggests that birds on the Atlantic coast of southwest Europe are distinct from all three, and may be an as-yet undescribed subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-Yesou_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yesou-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The name shag is also used in the Southern Hemisphere for several additional species of <a href="/wiki/Cormorant" title="Cormorant">cormorants</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Example_locations">Example locations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_shag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Example locations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:European_shags_in_mating_plume.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/European_shags_in_mating_plume.jpg/220px-European_shags_in_mating_plume.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/European_shags_in_mating_plume.jpg/330px-European_shags_in_mating_plume.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/European_shags_in_mating_plume.jpg/440px-European_shags_in_mating_plume.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4752" data-file-height="3168" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:European_shags_in_mating_plume.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>European shags in breeding plumage, Snæfellsnes, Iceland</div></div></div>
<p>The European shag can be readily be seen at the following breeding locations in the season (late April to mid July): <a href="/wiki/Saltee_Islands" title="Saltee Islands">Saltee Islands</a>, Ireland; <a href="/wiki/Farne_Islands" title="Farne Islands">Farne Islands</a>, England;  <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171002215340/http://www.nnr-scotland.org.uk/isle-of-may/">Isle of May</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deerness" title="Deerness">Deerness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fowlsheugh" title="Fowlsheugh">Fowlsheugh</a>, Scotland; <a href="/wiki/Runde" title="Runde">Runde</a>, Norway; <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, Denmark; <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, Spain; <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a>, Croatia; <a href="/wiki/Dalmatia" title="Dalmatia">Dalmatia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Istria" title="Istria">Istria</a>. In April 2017, eight new European shags were born in <a href="/wiki/Monaco" title="Monaco">Monaco</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-france3naissanceexceptionnelle_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-france3naissanceexceptionnelle-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The largest colony of European shags is in the <a href="/wiki/C%C3%ADes_Islands" title="Cíes Islands">Cíes Islands</a>, Spain, with 2,500 pairs (25% of the world's population).
</p>
