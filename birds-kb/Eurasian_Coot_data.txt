<p>The <b>Eurasian coot</b> (<i>Fulica atra</i>), also known as the <b>common coot</b>, or <b>Australian coot</b>, is a member of the rail and crake bird <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Rallidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Rallidae">Rallidae</a>. It is found in Europe, Asia, Australia, New Zealand and parts of North Africa. It has a slaty-black body, a glossy black head and a white bill with a white <a href="/wiki/Frontal_shield" title="Frontal shield">frontal shield</a>. The sexes are similar.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_coot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Eurasian coot was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">formally described</a> by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">tenth edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> under its current <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Fulica atra</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Linnaeus specified the <a href="/wiki/Type_locality_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Type locality (biology)">locality</a> as Europe but this is now restricted to Sweden.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The binomial name is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>: <i>Fulica</i> means "coot", and <i>atra</i> mean "black".<sup id="cite_ref-job_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Four <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are recognised:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>F. a. atra</i> Linnaeus, 1758 – Europe and north Africa to Japan, India, southeast Asia, Philippines and Borneo</li>
<li><i>F. a. lugubris</i> <a href="/wiki/Salomon_M%C3%BCller" title="Salomon Müller">Müller, S</a>, 1847 – Java, Bali, northwest New Guinea</li>
<li><i>F. a. novaeguineae</i> <a href="/wiki/Austin_L._Rand" title="Austin L. Rand">Rand</a>, 1940 – central New Guinea</li>
<li><i>F. a. australis</i> <a href="/wiki/John_Gould" title="John Gould">Gould</a>, 1845 – Australia and New Zealand</li></ul>
<p>An extinct subspecies <i>F. atra pontica</i>,  has been described from the <a href="/wiki/Chalcolithic" title="Chalcolithic">Chalcolithic</a> (around 4800-4400 BP) from the Bulgarian Black Sea Coast.<sup id="cite_ref-Boev1998_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boev1998-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_coot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Eurasian coot is 36–38&#160;cm (14–15&#160;in) in length with a wing-span of 70–80&#160;cm (28–31&#160;in); males weigh around 890&#160;g (31&#160;oz) and females 750&#160;g (26&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1980599,_610_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1980599,_610-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It largely black except for the white bill and <a href="/wiki/Frontal_shield" title="Frontal shield">frontal shield</a> (which gives rise to the phrase "as <a href="/wiki/Baldness" class="mw-redirect" title="Baldness">bald</a> as a coot", in use as early as 1430).<sup id="cite_ref-OED_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> As a swimming species, the coot has partial webbing on its long strong toes. The sexes are similar in appearance.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1980599_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1980599-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_coot_(Fulica_atra)_juvenile.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Eurasian_coot_%28Fulica_atra%29_juvenile.jpg/220px-Eurasian_coot_%28Fulica_atra%29_juvenile.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Eurasian_coot_%28Fulica_atra%29_juvenile.jpg/330px-Eurasian_coot_%28Fulica_atra%29_juvenile.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Eurasian_coot_%28Fulica_atra%29_juvenile.jpg/440px-Eurasian_coot_%28Fulica_atra%29_juvenile.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3644" data-file-height="2429" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_coot_(Fulica_atra)_juvenile.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Juvenile in <a href="/wiki/Marais_Audomarois" title="Marais Audomarois">Marais Audomarois</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a></div></div></div>
<p>The juvenile is paler than the adult, has a whitish breast, and lacks the facial shield; the adult black plumage develops when about 3–4 months old, but the white shield is only fully developed at about one year old.
</p><p>This is a noisy bird with a wide repertoire of crackling, explosive, or trumpeting calls, often given at night.
</p>
<div style="clear:left;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_coot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The coot breeds across much of the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> on freshwater lakes and ponds. It occurs and breeds in Europe, Asia, Australia, and Africa. The species has recently expanded its range into New Zealand. It is resident in the milder parts of its range, but <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrates</a> further south and west from much of Asia in winter as the waters freeze.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_coot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Eurasian coot is much less secretive than most of the rail family, and can be seen swimming on open water or walking across waterside grasslands. It is an aggressive species, and strongly territorial during the breeding season, and both parents are involved in territorial defence.<sup id="cite_ref-Cave1989_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cave1989-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> During the non-breeding season they may form large flocks, possibly related to predator avoidance.<sup id="cite_ref-vandenHout2006_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vandenHout2006-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is reluctant to fly and when taking off runs across the water surface with much splashing. It does the same, but without actually flying, when travelling a short distance at speed in territorial disputes or on land to escape from intruders. As with many rails, its weak flight does not inspire confidence, but on migration, usually at night, it can cover surprisingly large distances. It bobs its head as it swims, and makes short dives from a little jump.
</p><p>When fighting other waterbirds (especially other coots), the coot attacks by charging its opponent and striking them with its long legs.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_coot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fulica_atra_MWNH_0102.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Fulica_atra_MWNH_0102.JPG/220px-Fulica_atra_MWNH_0102.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Fulica_atra_MWNH_0102.JPG/330px-Fulica_atra_MWNH_0102.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Fulica_atra_MWNH_0102.JPG/440px-Fulica_atra_MWNH_0102.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3880" data-file-height="2800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fulica_atra_MWNH_0102.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden,_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Museum Wiesbaden, Germany">Museum Wiesbaden, Germany</a></div></div></div>
<p>The nest is a bulky structure that is placed on the bottom in shallow water. It is constructed of plant stems and leaves with a lining of finer material. Normally concealed in vegetation the nest can sometimes be placed in the open. It is built by both sexes with the male collecting most of the material which is incorporated by the female. The eggs are laid at daily intervals. The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> contains between six and ten smooth and slightly glossy buff coloured eggs that are covered with black or dark brown speckles. On average they are 53&#160;mm ×&#160;36&#160;mm (2.1&#160;in ×&#160;1.4&#160;in) and weigh 38&#160;g (1.3&#160;oz). The eggs are incubated by both sexes beginning after the second egg is laid and hatch asynchronously after 21 to 24 days. The chicks are <a href="/wiki/Precocial" class="mw-redirect" title="Precocial">precocial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nidifugous" title="Nidifugous">nidifugous</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1980608–609_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1980608–609-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The chicks are covered with a black down. On the body the down has yellow hair-like tips. On the sides of the head, nape and throat the hair-like tips are longer and orange-red. Between the eyes and on the lores, the tips are red. The shield is bright red and the bill is red with a white tip.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1980609_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1980609-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The young are brooded by the female for the first three to four days during which time food is brought by the male. The male also builds one or more platforms that is used for roosting and brooding the chicks. On leaving the nest, the brood is sometimes split up with each parent taking care of a separate group. The young can feed themselves when they are around 30 days and fledge at 55 to 60 days. Eurasian coots normally only have a single brood each year but in some areas such as Britain they will sometimes attempt a second brood. They first breed when they are one to two years old.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1980608–609_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1980608–609-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chick mortality occurs mainly due to starvation rather than predation.  Most chicks died in the first 10 days after hatching, when they are most dependent on adults for food.<sup id="cite_ref-natureoutside.com_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-natureoutside.com-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Coots can be very brutal to their own young under pressure such as the lack of food. They will bite young that are <a href="/wiki/Begging_behavior_in_animals" class="mw-redirect" title="Begging behavior in animals">begging</a> for food and repeatedly do this until it stops begging. If the begging continues, they may bite so hard that the chick is killed.<sup id="cite_ref-Attenborough1998_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Attenborough1998-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Coots will also lay their eggs in the nests of other coots when their environment or physical condition limits their ability to breed, or to lengthen their reproductive life.<sup id="cite_ref-natureoutside.com_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-natureoutside.com-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The coot is an omnivore, and will take a variety of small live prey including the eggs of other water birds, as well as <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, vegetation, seeds and fruit.<sup id="cite_ref-Perrow1997_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Perrow1997-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> It shows considerable variation in its feeding techniques, grazing on land or in the water. In the water it may upend in the fashion of a <a href="/wiki/Mallard" title="Mallard">mallard</a> or dive in search of food.<sup id="cite_ref-Bakker1993_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bakker1993-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_coot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Eurasian coot is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>
