<p>The <b>red-billed chough</b>, <b>Cornish chough</b> or simply <b>chough</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/tʃ/: &#39;ch&#39; in &#39;China&#39;">tʃ</span><span title="/ʌ/: &#39;u&#39; in &#39;cut&#39;">ʌ</span><span title="&#39;f&#39; in &#39;find&#39;">f</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span style="font-size:90%">CHUF</span></i></a>; <i>Pyrrhocorax pyrrhocorax</i>), is a bird in the <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crow</a> family, one of only two species in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Pyrrhocorax" class="mw-redirect" title="Pyrrhocorax">Pyrrhocorax</a></i>. Its eight <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> breed on mountains and coastal cliffs from the western coasts of Ireland and Britain east through southern Europe and North Africa to Central Asia, India and China.
</p><p>This bird has glossy black plumage, a long curved red bill, red legs, and a loud, ringing call. It has a buoyant acrobatic flight with widely spread <a href="/wiki/Primary_feather" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary feather">primaries</a>. The red-billed chough <a href="/wiki/Pair_bond" title="Pair bond">pairs</a> for life and displays <a href="/wiki/Philopatry" title="Philopatry">fidelity</a> to its breeding site, which is usually a cave or crevice in a cliff face. It builds a wool-lined stick nest and lays three eggs. It feeds, often in flocks, on short grazed grassland, taking mainly <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrate</a> prey.
</p><p>Although it is subject to predation and parasitism, the main threat to this species is changes in agricultural practices, which have led to population decline, some local <a href="/wiki/Local_extinction" title="Local extinction">extirpation</a>, and range fragmentation in Europe; however, it is not threatened globally. The red-billed chough, which derived its common name from the <a href="/wiki/Jackdaw" class="mw-redirect" title="Jackdaw">jackdaw</a>, was formerly associated with fire-raising, and has links with Saint <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Becket" title="Thomas Becket">Thomas Becket</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>. The red-billed chough has been depicted on postage stamps of a few countries, including the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, with four different stamps, and <a href="/wiki/The_Gambia" title="The Gambia">the Gambia</a>, where the bird does not occur.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The red-billed chough was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a> as <i>Upupa pyrrhocorax</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It was moved to its current genus, <i>Pyrrhocorax</i>, by <a href="/wiki/Marmaduke_Tunstall" title="Marmaduke Tunstall">Marmaduke Tunstall</a> in his 1771 <i>Ornithologia Britannica</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The genus name is derived from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>πυρρός (pyrrhos)</i>, "flame-coloured", and <i>κόραξ (korax)</i>, "raven".<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The only other member of the genus is the <a href="/wiki/Alpine_chough" title="Alpine chough">Alpine chough</a>, <i>Pyrrhocorax graculus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ITIS1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITIS1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The closest relatives of the choughs are the typical <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crows</a>, <i>Corvus</i>, especially the jackdaws in the subgenus <i><a href="/wiki/Coloeus" title="Coloeus">Coloeus</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>"Chough" was originally an alternative <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeic</a> name for the jackdaw, <i>Corvus monedula</i>, based on its call. The similar red-billed species, formerly particularly common in Cornwall, became known initially as "Cornish chough" and then just "chough", the name transferring from one species to the other.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The Australian <a href="/wiki/White-winged_chough" title="White-winged chough">white-winged chough</a>,  <i>Corcorax melanorhamphos</i>, despite its similar shape and habits, is only distantly related to the true choughs, and is an example of <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">convergent evolution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ITIS2_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITIS2-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span></h3>
<p>There are eight extant <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>, although differences between them are slight.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>P. p. pyrrhocorax</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Subspecies#Nomenclature" title="Subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> and smallest form, is <a href="/wiki/Endemism_in_birds" class="mw-redirect" title="Endemism in birds">endemic</a> to the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, where it is restricted to <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, and the far west of <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> although it recolonised <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> in 2001 after an absence of 50 years.<sup id="cite_ref-CCC_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CCC-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. p. erythropthalmus</i>, described by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Jean_Pierre_Vieillot" title="Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot">Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot</a> in 1817 as <i>Coracia erythrorhamphos</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Vieillot_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vieillot-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> occurs in the red-billed chough's continental European range, excluding <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>. It is larger and slightly greener than the nominate race.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. p. barbarus</i>, described by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Vaurie" title="Charles Vaurie">Charles Vaurie</a> under its current name in 1954, is resident in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> and on <a href="/wiki/La_Palma" title="La Palma">La Palma</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>. Compared to <i>P. p. erythropthalmus</i>, it is larger, has a longer tail and wings, and its plumage has a greener gloss. It is the longest-billed form, both absolutely and relatively.<sup id="cite_ref-Vaurie_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vaurie-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. p. baileyi</i> described by <a href="/wiki/Austin_L._Rand" title="Austin L. Rand">Austin Loomer Rand</a> and Charles Vaurie under its current name in 1955,<sup id="cite_ref-Rand_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rand-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> is a dull-plumaged subspecies endemic to <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, where it occurs in two separate areas. The two populations could possibly represent different subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. p. docilis</i>, described by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Gmelin" title="Johann Friedrich Gmelin">Johann Friedrich Gmelin</a> as <i>Corvus docilis</i> in 1774,<sup id="cite_ref-Gmelin_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gmelin-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> breeds from Greece to <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>. It is larger than the African subspecies, but it has a smaller bill and its plumage is very green-tinted, with little gloss.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Red-billed_Chough_I_IMG_7083.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Red-billed_Chough_I_IMG_7083.jpg/220px-Red-billed_Chough_I_IMG_7083.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Red-billed_Chough_I_IMG_7083.jpg/330px-Red-billed_Chough_I_IMG_7083.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Red-billed_Chough_I_IMG_7083.jpg/440px-Red-billed_Chough_I_IMG_7083.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="557" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Red-billed_Chough_I_IMG_7083.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Immature <i>P. p. himalayanus</i> at 3,800 metres (12,500 feet) at Tilla Lotani, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a></div></div></div>
<ul><li><i>P. p. himalayanus</i>, described by <a href="/wiki/John_Gould" title="John Gould">John Gould</a> in 1862 as <i>Fregilus himalayanus</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Gould_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gould-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> is found from the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> to western <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Intergrade" class="mw-redirect" title="Intergrade">intergrades</a> with  <i>P. p. docilis</i> in the west of its range. It is the largest subspecies, long-tailed, and with blue or purple-blue glossed feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. p. centralis</i>, described by <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Stresemann" title="Erwin Stresemann">Erwin Stresemann</a> in 1928 under its current name,<sup id="cite_ref-Stresemann_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stresemann-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> breeds in <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>. It is smaller and less strongly blue than <i>P. p. himalayanus</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> but its distinctness from the next subspecies has been questioned.<sup id="cite_ref-Vaurie_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vaurie-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. p. brachypus</i>, described by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Swinhoe" title="Robert Swinhoe">Robert Swinhoe</a> in 1871 as <i>Fregilus graculus</i> var. <i>brachypus</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Swinhoe_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Swinhoe-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> breeds in central and northern China, Mongolia and southern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. It is similar to <i>P. p. centralis</i> but with a weaker bill.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>There is one known prehistoric form of the red-billed chough. <i>P. p. primigenius</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> that lived in Europe during the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Last glacial period">last ice age</a>, which was described in 1875 by <a href="/wiki/Alphonse_Milne-Edwards" title="Alphonse Milne-Edwards">Alphonse Milne-Edwards</a> from finds in southwest France.<sup id="cite_ref-Milne_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Milne-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mourer_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mourer-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Detailed analysis of call similarity suggests that the Asiatic and Ethiopian races diverged from the western subspecies early in <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolutionary</a> history, and that Italian red-billed choughs are more closely allied to the North African subspecies than to those of the rest of Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Laiolo_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laiolo-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pyrrhocorax_pyrrhocorax_-Penwith_-Cornwall_-flying-8b.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Pyrrhocorax_pyrrhocorax_-Penwith_-Cornwall_-flying-8b.jpg/220px-Pyrrhocorax_pyrrhocorax_-Penwith_-Cornwall_-flying-8b.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Pyrrhocorax_pyrrhocorax_-Penwith_-Cornwall_-flying-8b.jpg/330px-Pyrrhocorax_pyrrhocorax_-Penwith_-Cornwall_-flying-8b.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Pyrrhocorax_pyrrhocorax_-Penwith_-Cornwall_-flying-8b.jpg/440px-Pyrrhocorax_pyrrhocorax_-Penwith_-Cornwall_-flying-8b.jpg 2x" data-file-width="796" data-file-height="597" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pyrrhocorax_pyrrhocorax_-Penwith_-Cornwall_-flying-8b.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>P. p. pyrrhocorax</i>; nominate subspecies flying in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, UK</div></div></div>
<p>The adult of the "nominate" subspecies of the red-billed chough, <i>P. p. pyrrhocorax</i>, is 39–40 centimetres (15–16 inches) in length, has a 73–90 centimetres (29–35 inches) wingspan,<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and weighs an average 310&#160;grammes (10.9&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Its plumage is velvet-black, green-glossed on the body, and it has a long curved red bill and red legs. The sexes are similar (although adults can be sexed in the hand using a formula involving <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> length and bill width)<sup id="cite_ref-Blanco6_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blanco6-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> but the juvenile has an orange bill and pink legs until its first autumn, and less glossy plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_9-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The red-billed chough is unlikely to be confused with any other species of bird. Although the jackdaw and Alpine chough share its range, the jackdaw is smaller and has unglossed grey plumage, and the Alpine chough has a short yellow bill. Even in flight, the two choughs can be distinguished by Alpine's less rectangular wings, and longer, less square-ended tail.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_9-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The red-billed chough's loud, ringing <i>chee-ow</i> call is clearer and louder than the similar vocalisation of the jackdaw, and always very different from that of its yellow-billed <a href="/wiki/Conspecificity" class="mw-redirect" title="Conspecificity">congener</a>, which has rippling <i>preep</i> and whistled <i>sweeeooo</i> calls.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_9-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Small subspecies of the red-billed chough have higher frequency calls than larger races, as predicted by the inverse relationship between body size and frequency.<sup id="cite_ref-Laiolo2001_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laiolo2001-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Berneray_Machair_(hazelisles).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Berneray_Machair_%28hazelisles%29.jpg/220px-Berneray_Machair_%28hazelisles%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Berneray_Machair_%28hazelisles%29.jpg/330px-Berneray_Machair_%28hazelisles%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Berneray_Machair_%28hazelisles%29.jpg/440px-Berneray_Machair_%28hazelisles%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2272" data-file-height="1704" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Berneray_Machair_(hazelisles).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Machair" title="Machair">Machair</a> habitat in Scotland</div></div></div>
<p>The red-billed chough breeds in Ireland, western Great Britain, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, southern Europe and the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a> basin, the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>, and in mountainous country across <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, India and China, with two separate populations in the Ethiopian Highlands. It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">non-migratory resident</a> throughout its range.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_9-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Its main habitat is high mountains; it is found between 2,000 and 2,500 metres (6,600 and 8,200&#160;ft) in North Africa, and mainly between 2,400 and 3,000 metres (7,900 and 9,800&#160;ft) in the Himalayas. In that mountain range it reaches 6,000 metres (20,000 feet) in the summer, and has been recorded at 7,950 metres (26,080 feet) altitude on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_9-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In the British Isles and <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> it also breeds on coastal sea cliffs, feeding on adjacent short grazed grassland or <a href="/wiki/Machair" title="Machair">machair</a>. It was formerly more widespread on coasts but has suffered from the loss of its specialised habitat.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RSPB2_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB2-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> It tends to breed at a lower elevation than the Alpine chough,<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> that species having a diet better adapted to high altitudes.<sup id="cite_ref-Rolando2_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rolando2-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<p>The red-billed chough breeds from three years of age, and normally raises only one brood a year,<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> although the age at first breeding is greater in large populations.<sup id="cite_ref-Reid_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reid-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> A pair exhibits strong mate and <a href="/wiki/Philopatry" title="Philopatry">site fidelity</a> once a <a href="/wiki/Pair_bond" title="Pair bond">bond</a> is established.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_1985_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_1985-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The bulky nest is composed of roots and stems of <a href="/wiki/Calluna" title="Calluna">heather</a>, <a href="/wiki/Furze" class="mw-redirect" title="Furze">furze</a> or other plants, and is lined with wool or hair;<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> in central Asia, the hair may be taken from live <a href="/wiki/Himalayan_tahr" title="Himalayan tahr">Himalayan tahr</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Baletto_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baletto-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The nest is constructed in a cave or similar fissure in a crag or cliff face.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In soft sandstone, the birds themselves excavate holes nearly a metre deep.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Old buildings may be used, and in <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a> working monasteries provide sites, as occasionally do modern buildings in <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolian</a> towns, including <a href="/wiki/Ulaanbaatar" title="Ulaanbaatar">Ulaanbaatar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_9-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The red-billed chough will utilise other artificial sites, such as quarries and mineshafts for nesting where they are available.<sup id="cite_ref-JNCC_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JNCC-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The chough lays three to five eggs 3.9 by 2.8 centimetres (1.5 by 1.1 inches) in size and weighing 15.7&#160;grammes (0.55&#160;oz), of which 6% is shell.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> They are spotted, not always densely, in various shades of brown and grey on a creamy or slightly tinted ground.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_21-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Red-billed_Chough.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Red-billed_Chough.jpg/170px-Red-billed_Chough.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="232" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Red-billed_Chough.jpg/255px-Red-billed_Chough.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Red-billed_Chough.jpg/340px-Red-billed_Chough.jpg 2x" data-file-width="587" data-file-height="802" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Red-billed_Chough.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pyrrhocorax_pyrrhocorax_MWNH_1378.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Pyrrhocorax_pyrrhocorax_MWNH_1378.JPG/220px-Pyrrhocorax_pyrrhocorax_MWNH_1378.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Pyrrhocorax_pyrrhocorax_MWNH_1378.JPG/330px-Pyrrhocorax_pyrrhocorax_MWNH_1378.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Pyrrhocorax_pyrrhocorax_MWNH_1378.JPG/440px-Pyrrhocorax_pyrrhocorax_MWNH_1378.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2988" data-file-height="1848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pyrrhocorax_pyrrhocorax_MWNH_1378.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>The egg size is independent of the clutch size and the nest site, but may vary between different females.<sup id="cite_ref-Stillman_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stillman-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The female <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" class="mw-redirect" title="Avian incubation">incubates</a> for 17–18&#160;days before the <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a> downy chicks are hatched, and is fed at the nest by the male. The female <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" class="mw-redirect" title="Avian incubation">broods</a> the newly hatched chicks for around ten days,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and then both parents share feeding and nest sanitation duties. The chicks <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> 31–41&#160;days after hatching.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Juveniles have a 43% chance of surviving their first year, and the annual survival rate of adults is about 80%. Choughs generally have a lifespan of about seven years,<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> although an age of 17&#160;years has been recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_1985_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_1985-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The temperature and rainfall in the months preceding breeding correlates with the number of young fledging each year and their survival rate. Chicks fledging under good conditions are more likely to survive to breeding age, and have longer breeding lives than those fledging under poor conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-Reid_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reid-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PyrrhocoraxPEremita.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Monochrome drawing of the head and bill" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/PyrrhocoraxPEremita.jpg/220px-PyrrhocoraxPEremita.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="97" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/PyrrhocoraxPEremita.jpg/330px-PyrrhocoraxPEremita.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/PyrrhocoraxPEremita.jpg/440px-PyrrhocoraxPEremita.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2010" data-file-height="883" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PyrrhocoraxPEremita.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The long curved bill is used to dig for invertebrates and bulbs</div></div></div>
<p>The red-billed chough's food consists largely of insects, spiders and other <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> taken from the ground, with <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ants</a> probably being the most significant item.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_9-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The Central Asian subspecies <i>P. p. centralis</i> will perch on the backs of wild or domesticated <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> to feed on parasites.<sup id="cite_ref-Baletto_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baletto-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Although invertebrates make up most of the chough's diet, it will eat vegetable matter including fallen grain, and in the Himalayas has been reported as damaging <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a> crops by breaking off the ripening heads to extract the corn.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_9-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In the Himalayas, they form large flocks in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The preferred feeding habitat is short grass produced by grazing, for example by sheep and rabbits, the numbers of which are linked to the chough's breeding success. Suitable feeding areas can also arise where plant growth is hindered by exposure to coastal salt spray or poor soils.<sup id="cite_ref-Mccanch_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mccanch-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Blanco4_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blanco4-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> It will use its long curved bill to pick ants, <a href="/wiki/Dung_beetle" title="Dung beetle">dung beetles</a> and emerging flies off the surface, or to dig for grubs and other invertebrates. The typical excavation depth of 2–3&#160;cm (1–1&#160;in) reflects the thin soils which it feeds on, and the depths at which many invertebrates occur, but it may dig to 10–20&#160;cm (4–8&#160;in) in appropriate conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-bardsey_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bardsey-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-morris_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morris-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Where the two chough species occur together, there is only limited competition for food. An Italian study showed that the vegetable part of the winter diet for the red-billed chough was almost exclusively <i><a href="/wiki/Gagea" title="Gagea">Gagea</a></i> bulbs, whilst the Alpine chough took berries and hips. In June, red-billed choughs fed on <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">Lepidoptera</a> <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> whereas Alpine choughs ate <a href="/wiki/Cranefly" class="mw-redirect" title="Cranefly">cranefly</a> <a href="/wiki/Pupa" title="Pupa">pupae</a>. Later in the summer, the Alpine chough mainly consumed <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a>, whilst the red-billed chough added cranefly pupae, <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">fly</a> larvae and beetles to its diet.<sup id="cite_ref-Rolando2_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rolando2-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Both choughs will hide food in cracks and fissures, concealing the cache with a few pebbles.<sup id="cite_ref-wall_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wall-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_threats">Natural threats</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ChoughsDiff.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/ChoughsDiff.svg/170px-ChoughsDiff.svg.png" decoding="async" width="170" height="204" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/ChoughsDiff.svg/255px-ChoughsDiff.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/ChoughsDiff.svg/340px-ChoughsDiff.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ChoughsDiff.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Red-billed chough (left) can be distinguished from Alpine chough in flight by its deeper primary "fingers" and tail wedge. Its wings extend further, to or beyond the tail tip, when it is standing.</div></div></div>
<p>The red-billed chough's predators include the <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">peregrine falcon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">golden eagle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_eagle-owl" title="Eurasian eagle-owl">Eurasian eagle-owl</a>, while the <a href="/wiki/Common_raven" title="Common raven">common raven</a> will take nestlings.<sup id="cite_ref-birdwatchireland_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdwatchireland-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OperationChough_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OperationChough-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rolando_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rolando-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Blanco_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blanco-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> In northern <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, red-billed choughs preferentially nest near <a href="/wiki/Lesser_kestrel" title="Lesser kestrel">lesser kestrel</a> colonies. This small <a href="/wiki/Insectivore" title="Insectivore">insectivorous</a> <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon</a> is better at detecting a predator and more vigorous in defence than its <a href="/wiki/Corvidae" title="Corvidae">corvid</a> neighbours. The breeding success of the red-billed chough in the vicinity of the kestrels was found to be much higher than that of birds elsewhere, with a lower percentage of nest failures (16% near the falcon, 65% elsewhere).<sup id="cite_ref-Blanco_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blanco-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species is occasionally <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">parasitised</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Great_spotted_cuckoo" title="Great spotted cuckoo">great spotted cuckoo</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasite</a> for which the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_magpie" title="Eurasian magpie">Eurasian magpie</a> is the primary host.<sup id="cite_ref-Soler_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Soler-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
Red-billed choughs can acquire blood parasites such as <i><a href="/wiki/Plasmodium" title="Plasmodium">Plasmodium</a></i>, but a study in Spain showed that the prevalence was less than one percent, and unlikely to affect the life history and conservation of this species.<sup id="cite_ref-Blanco2_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blanco2-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> These low levels of parasitism contrast with a much higher prevalence in some other passerine groups; for example a study of <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrushes</a> in Russia showed that <i>all</i> the <a href="/wiki/Fieldfare" title="Fieldfare">fieldfares</a>, <a href="/wiki/Redwing" title="Redwing">redwings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Song_thrush" title="Song thrush">song thrushes</a> sampled carried haematozoans, particularly <i>Haemoproteus</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Trypanosoma" title="Trypanosoma">Trypanosoma</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Markovets_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Markovets-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Red-billed choughs can also carry <a href="/wiki/Mite" title="Mite">mites</a>, but a study of the feather mite <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabucinia_delibata&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gabucinia delibata (page does not exist)">Gabucinia delibata</a></i>, acquired by young birds a few months after fledging when they join communal roosts, suggested that this parasite actually <i>improved</i> the body condition of its host. It is possible that the feather mites enhance feather cleaning and deter <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Blanco3_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blanco3-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> and may complement other feather care measures such as sunbathing, and <a href="/wiki/Anting_(bird_activity)" title="Anting (bird activity)">anting</a>—rubbing the plumage with ants (the <a href="/wiki/Formic_acid" title="Formic acid">formic acid</a> from the insects deters parasites).<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_9-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h2>
<p>The red-billed chough has an extensive range, estimated at 10&#160;million&#160;square kilometres (3.8&#160;million sq mi), and a large population, including an estimated 86,000 to 210,000 individuals in Europe. Over its range as a whole, the species is not believed to approach the thresholds for the global population decline criterion of the IUCN Red List (i.e., declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations), and is therefore evaluated as <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, the European range has declined and fragmented due to the loss of traditional pastoral farming, persecution and perhaps disturbance at breeding and nesting sites, although the numbers in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, Great Britain and Ireland may now have stabilised.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_21-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The European breeding population is between 12,265–17,370 pairs, but only in Spain is the species still widespread. Since in the rest of the continent breeding areas are fragmented and isolated, the red-billed chough has been categorised as "vulnerable" in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-JNCC_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JNCC-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Spain, the red-billed chough has recently expanded its range by utilising old buildings, with 1,175 breeding pairs in a 9,716 square kilometres (3,751 square miles)mi) study area. These new breeding areas usually surround the original montane core areas. However, the populations with nest sites on buildings are threatened by human disturbance, persecution and the loss of old buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-Blanco5_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blanco5-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Fossils of both chough species were found in the mountains of the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>. The local extinction of the Alpine chough and the reduced range of red-billed chough in the islands may have been due to climate change or human activity.<sup id="cite_ref-rando_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rando-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A small group of wild red-billed chough arrived naturally in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> in 2001, and nested in the following year. This was the first English breeding record since 1947, and a slowly expanding population has bred every subsequent year.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPB2_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPB2-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Durrell_Wildlife_Conservation_Trust" title="Durrell Wildlife Conservation Trust">Durrell Wildlife Conservation Trust</a>, in partnership with the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Jersey" class="mw-redirect" title="States of Jersey">States of Jersey</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Jersey" title="National Trust for Jersey">National Trust for Jersey</a> began a project in 2010, aimed at restoring selected areas of Jersey's coastline with the intention of returning those birds that had become locally extinct. The red-billed chough was chosen as a flagship species for this project, having been absent from Jersey since around 1900. Durrell initially received two pairs of choughs from <a href="/wiki/Paradise_Park,_Cornwall" title="Paradise Park, Cornwall">Paradise Park</a> in Cornwall and began a <a href="/wiki/Captive_breeding" title="Captive breeding">captive breeding</a> programme.<sup id="cite_ref-durrell_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-durrell-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> In 2013, juveniles were released onto the north coast of Jersey using soft-release methods developed at Durrell. Over the next five years, small cohorts of captive-bred choughs were released, monitored, and provided supplemental food.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greek_mythology">Greek mythology</span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, the red-billed chough, also known as 'sea-crow', was considered sacred to the <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Titan (mythology)">Titan</a> <a href="/wiki/Cronus" title="Cronus">Cronus</a> and dwelt on <a href="/wiki/Calypso_(mythology)" title="Calypso (mythology)">Calypso's</a> 'Blessed Island',<sup id="cite_ref-deVries76_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deVries76-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> where "The birds of broadest wing their mansions form/The chough, the sea-mew, the loquacious crow."<sup id="cite_ref-Pope_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pope-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuisance_species">Nuisance species</span></h3>
<p>Up to the eighteenth century, the red-billed chough was associated with <a href="/wiki/Arson" title="Arson">fire-raising</a>, and was described by <a href="/wiki/William_Camden" title="William Camden">William Camden</a> as <i>incendaria avis</i>, "oftentime it secretly conveieth fire sticks, setting their houses afire".<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Defoe" title="Daniel Defoe">Daniel Defoe</a> was also familiar with this story:
</p>
<blockquote><p>It is counted little better than a kite, for it is of ravenous quality, and is very mischievous; it will steal and carry away any thing it finds about the house, that is not too heavy, tho' not fit for its food; as knives, forks, spoons and linnen cloths, or whatever it can fly away with, sometimes they say it has stolen bits of firebrands, or lighted candles, and lodged them in the stacks of corn, and the thatch of barns and houses, and set them on fire; but this I only had by oral tradition.<sup id="cite_ref-Defoe_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Defoe-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Not all mentions of "chough" refer to this species. Because of the origins of its name, when <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a> writes of "the crows and choughs that wing the midway air" [<i><a href="/wiki/King_Lear" title="King Lear">King Lear</a></i>, act 4, scene 6] or <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a>'s Vermin Act of 1532 is "ordeyned to dystroye Choughes, Crowes and Rookes", they are clearly referring to the <a href="/wiki/Jackdaw" class="mw-redirect" title="Jackdaw">jackdaw</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornish_heraldry">Cornish heraldry</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Becket-arms.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Becket-arms.jpg/220px-Becket-arms.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="263" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/73/Becket-arms.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="358" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Becket-arms.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Attributed_arms" title="Attributed arms">Attributed arms</a> of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Becket" title="Thomas Becket">Saint Thomas Becket</a> (d.1170), <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a>: <i>Argent, three Cornish choughs proper</i></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Christ_Church_Oxford_Coat_Of_Arms.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Christ_Church_Oxford_Coat_Of_Arms.svg/220px-Christ_Church_Oxford_Coat_Of_Arms.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="277" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Christ_Church_Oxford_Coat_Of_Arms.svg/330px-Christ_Church_Oxford_Coat_Of_Arms.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Christ_Church_Oxford_Coat_Of_Arms.svg/440px-Christ_Church_Oxford_Coat_Of_Arms.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="524" data-file-height="659" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Christ_Church_Oxford_Coat_Of_Arms.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Arms of <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Thomas_Wolsey" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardinal Thomas Wolsey">Cardinal Thomas Wolsey</a> (d.1530), <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_York" title="Archbishop of York">Archbishop of York</a>: <i>Sable, on a cross engrailed argent a lion passant gules between four leopard's faces azure; on a chief or a rose gules barbed vert seeded or between two Cornish choughs proper</i></div></div></div>
<p>The red-billed chough has a long association with Cornwall, and appears on the Cornish <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CCC_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CCC-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> According to Cornish legend <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a> did not die after his last battle but rather his soul migrated into the body of a red-billed chough, the red colour of its bill and legs being derived from the blood of the last battle<sup id="cite_ref-Newlyn_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newlyn-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> and hence killing this bird was unlucky.<sup id="cite_ref-deVries76_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deVries76-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Legend also holds that after the last Cornish chough departs from Cornwall, then the return of the chough, as happened in 2001, will mark the return of King Arthur.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In English <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">heraldry</a> the bird is always <a href="/wiki/Blazon" title="Blazon">blazoned</a> as "a Cornish chough" and is usually shown "proper", with <a href="/wiki/Tincture" title="Tincture">tinctures</a> as in nature. Since the 14th century, St <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Becket" title="Thomas Becket">Thomas Becket</a> (d.1170), <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a>, has retrospectively acquired an <a href="/wiki/Attributed_arms" title="Attributed arms">attributed coat of arms</a> consisting of three Cornish choughs on a white field,<sup id="cite_ref-Becket_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Becket-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> although as he died 30 to 45 years before the start of the age of heraldry,<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> in reality he bore no arms. These attributed arms appear in many English churches dedicated to him. The symbolism behind the association is not known for certain. According to one legend, a chough strayed into  <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Cathedral" title="Canterbury Cathedral">Canterbury Cathedral</a> during Becket's murder, while another  suggests that the choughs are a <a href="/wiki/Canting_arms" title="Canting arms">canting</a> reference to Becket's name, as they were once known as "beckits".<sup id="cite_ref-HS_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HS-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> However the latter theory does not stand up to scrutiny, as the use of the term "beckit" to mean a chough is not found before the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-HS_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HS-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Regardless of its origin, the chough is still used in heraldry as a symbol of Becket, and appears in the arms of several persons and institutions associated with him, most notably in the arms of the city of <a href="/wiki/Canterbury" title="Canterbury">Canterbury</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Canterbury_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canterbury-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> In early heraldry Cornish choughs were borne in the arms only of Cornish persons, but during the reign of King Henry VIII (1509-1547) the practice of allocating them indiscriminately regardless of any connection of the grantee to Cornwall began under  <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Barker_(officer_of_arms)" title="Christopher Barker (officer of arms)">Christopher Barker</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Garter_Principal_King_of_Arms" title="Garter Principal King of Arms">Garter Principal King of Arms</a>, who granted a coat of arms displaying Cornish choughs to <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Wolsey" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardinal Wolsey">Cardinal Wolsey</a>, whose arms continue to be borne by <a href="/wiki/Christ_Church,_Oxford" title="Christ Church, Oxford">Christ Church, Oxford</a>, founded by him. In Britain, the Cornish chough was the emblem of the Royal Navy battlecruiser HMS <i>Hood</i> and has been depicted on the stamps of the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_nations">Other nations</span></h3>
<p>This species has been depicted on the stamps of <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>, The Gambia, <a href="/wiki/Turkmenistan" title="Turkmenistan">Turkmenistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-stamps_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stamps-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> It is the animal symbol of the island of <a href="/wiki/La_Palma" title="La Palma">La Palma</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-lapalma_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lapalma-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
