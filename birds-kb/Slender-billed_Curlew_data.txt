<p>The <b>Slender-billed Curlew</b> (<i>Numenius tenuirostris</i>) is (or was) a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> family <a href="/wiki/Scolopacidae" title="Scolopacidae" class="mw-redirect">Scolopacidae</a>. It breeds in marshes and peat bogs in the <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a> of <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, and is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, formerly wintering in shallow freshwater habitats around the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a>. This species has occurred as a vagrant in western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Slender-billed_Curlew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Slender-billed_Curlew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Appearance">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance">Appearance</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Slender-billed_curlew_(Naumann).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Slender-billed_curlew_%28Naumann%29.jpg/220px-Slender-billed_curlew_%28Naumann%29.jpg" width="220" height="289" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Slender-billed_curlew_(Naumann).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Slender-billed Curlew (centre) between <a href="/wiki/Whimbrel" title="Whimbrel">Whimbrels</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Slender-billed Curlew is a small <a href="/wiki/Curlew" title="Curlew">curlew</a>, 36–41&#160;cm in length with a 77–88&#160;cm wingspan. It is therefore about the same size as a <a href="/wiki/Whimbrel" title="Whimbrel">Whimbrel</a>, but it is more like the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Curlew" title="Eurasian Curlew">Eurasian Curlew</a> in plumage. The breeding adult is mainly greyish brown above, with a whitish rump and lower back. The underparts are whitish, heavily streaked with dark brown. The flanks have round or heart-shaped spots. The non-breeding plumage is similar, but with fewer flank spots. Male and female are alike in plumage, but females are longer-billed than males, an adaptation in curlew species that avoids direct competition for food between the sexes. The juvenile plumage is very similar to the adult, but the flank are marked with brown streaking, the heart-shaped spots only appearing towards the end of the first winter.</p>
<p>Compared to the Eurasian Curlew, the Slender-billed Curlew is whiter on the breast, tail and underwing, and the bill is shorter, more slender, and slightly straighter at the base. The arrowhead-shaped flank spots of the Eurasian Curlew are also different from the round or heart-shaped spots of the Slender-billed. The head pattern, with a dark cap and whitish <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a>, recalls that of the Whimbrel, but that species also has a central crown stripe and a more clearly marked pattern overall; the pattern of the Slender-billed Curlew would be hard to make out in the field.</p>
<p>This species shows more white than other curlews, and the white underwings, along with the distinctive flank markings, are key identification criteria.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Slender-billed_Curlew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vocalisations">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Vocalisations">Vocalisations</span></h3>
<p>The call is a <i>cour-lee</i>, similar to that of the Eurasian Curlew but higher-pitched, more melodic and shorter. The alarm call is a fast <i>cu-ee</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Slender-billed_Curlew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span></h2>
<p>Little is known about the breeding biology, but the few nests observed had on average four eggs.</p>
<p>Slender-billed Curlews feed by using their <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bills</a> to probe soft mud for small <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>, but will also pick other small items off the surface if the opportunity arises. It used to be highly gregarious outside the breeding season, associating with related species, particularly Eurasian Curlews.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Slender-billed_Curlew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Slender-billed_Curlew.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Slender-billed_Curlew.jpg/220px-Slender-billed_Curlew.jpg" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Slender-billed_Curlew.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Print from 1866 by C R Bree</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After a long period of steady decline, the Slender-billed Curlew is extremely rare, with only a minute and still declining population. This is thought to be under 50 adult birds, with no more than two or three verified sightings in any year in the last five (as of 2007<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Slender-billed_Curlew&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>). As a result it is now listed as <a href="/wiki/Critically_endangered" title="Critically endangered" class="mw-redirect">critically endangered</a>. It is actually the first European bird species highly likely to become entirely <a href="/wiki/Extinct" title="Extinct" class="mw-redirect">extinct</a> since the last <a href="/wiki/Great_Auk" title="Great Auk">Great Auk</a> died over 150 years ago.</p>
<p>The primary cause of the decline is thought to be excessive <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunting</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a> wintering grounds. <a href="/wiki/Habitat_loss" title="Habitat loss" class="mw-redirect">Habitat loss</a>, particularly in the wintering grounds, may also have played a part, but huge areas of forest bogs suitable for breeding still exist in Siberia. It is unknown to what extent the birds still reproduce successfully, and how much <a href="/wiki/Gene_flow" title="Gene flow">gene flow</a> still exists in what may once have been a large and widely dispersed population undergoing little purging of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deleterious_recessive&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Deleterious recessive (page does not exist)">deleterious recessive</a> <a href="/wiki/Allele" title="Allele">alleles</a> and consequently with a high <a href="/wiki/Minimum_viable_population" title="Minimum viable population">MVP</a>. Furthermore, although there is evidence that birds in winter quarters were more numerous once and in general not a very rare sight in <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a> in the 19th century and were hunted with some regularity - and later on also threatened by <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Oil_spill" title="Oil spill">oil spills</a> -, there is no data how these threats endanger the species today exist. Theoretically they might have retreated to all but inaccessible areas, but then, a single hunter might unwittingly wipe out enough of the few remaining birds to doom the species.</p>
<p>The only well documented nest was found in 1924, near <a href="/wiki/Tara,_Russia" title="Tara, Russia">Tara</a> in <a href="/wiki/Omsk" title="Omsk">Omsk</a> <a href="/wiki/Oblast" title="Oblast">oblast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://toolserver.org/~geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Slender-billed_Curlew&amp;params=57_N_74_E_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">57°N</span> <span class="longitude">74°E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">57°N 74°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">57; 74</span></span></span></a></span>).<sup id="cite_ref-BB95334_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB95334-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Its nesting grounds since then remain unknown, despite several intensive searches (not surprising, with over 100,000 square kilometres to search). The extent of its decline is also reflected in the absence of wintering birds at previously regular <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Moroccan</a> sites.</p>
<p>More recently, 20 birds were recorded in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> in 1995. Remarkably, there is also a single recent (4–7 May 1998) record of an immature (one year old) at <a href="/wiki/Druridge_Pools" title="Druridge Pools" class="mw-redirect">Druridge Pools</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-BB95272_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB95272-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> for details of which see <a href="/wiki/The_Druridge_Bay_curlew" title="The Druridge Bay curlew" class="mw-redirect">the Druridge Bay curlew</a>.</p>
<p>Slender-billed Curlews have been reported in various Western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" title="Palearctic" class="mw-redirect">Palearctic</a> locations on a number of occasions since the Druridge bird, including claimed, but unverified, sightings of single birds from Italy and <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>; none have been documented with conclusive photographs and at least one claimed bird, at <a href="/wiki/RSPB_Minsmere" title="RSPB Minsmere" class="mw-redirect">RSPB Minsmere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> in 2004, is now widely believed to have been a Eurasian Curlew.</p>
<p>Further sourced reports of the species were published in 2007, in <a href="/wiki/British_Birds_(magazine)" title="British Birds (magazine)">British Birds magazine</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-BB100_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB100-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> the article stated, quoting from Zhmud<sup id="cite_ref-Zhmud_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zhmud-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>During the last few years, small groups of birds have been found in the northern coastal areas [of the <a href="/wiki/Danube_Delta" title="Danube Delta">Danube Delta</a>], frequenting low-lying islands, bays and sand-spits covered with <a href="/wiki/Common_Glasswort" title="Common Glasswort" class="mw-redirect">Common Glasswort</a> <i>Salicornia europaea</i> [...] Four birds were present from 25th July to 21st August 2003, six were seen on 11th August 2004, and another on 12th August 2004.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>A sighting of a single bird was reported from <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a> in 2006 by a team including ornithologists from the environmental organization <a href="/w/index.php?title=EuroNatur&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="EuroNatur (page does not exist)">EuroNatur</a>.</p>
<p>Thus, though hard proof is lacking but given the extent of possible habitat and the <a href="/wiki/Precautionary_principle" title="Precautionary principle">precautionary principle</a>, it is believed to be extant for the time being. Apparently at least the wintering range has starkly contracted; it appears that the handful of family or neighbor groups that are left retreat to remote habitat in southeastern Europe in winter. The <a href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN" class="mw-redirect">IUCN</a> classifies it as <a href="/wiki/Critically_Endangered" title="Critically Endangered">Critically Endangered</a> (CR) <b>C2a(ii); D</b>. This means that an estimated 50 mature birds or less are believed to exist, with numbers declining, and that there is probably only one <a href="/wiki/Subpopulation" title="Subpopulation" class="mw-redirect">subpopulation</a>.</p>
