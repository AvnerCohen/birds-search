<p>The <b>Indian Silverbill</b> or <b>White-throated Munia</b> (<i>Lonchura malabarica</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> found in South Asia that was formerly considered to include the closely related <a href="/wiki/African_Silverbill" title="African Silverbill">African Silverbill</a> (<i>Lonchura cantans</i>). This <a href="/wiki/Estrildid_finch" title="Estrildid finch">estrildid finch</a> is a common resident breeding bird in the dry regions of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>. It has also been introduced into many other parts of the world and has become established in some areas. They forage in small flocks in grassland and scrub habitats.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_Silverbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Indian_Silverbill_I_IMG_8877.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Indian_Silverbill_I_IMG_8877.jpg/220px-Indian_Silverbill_I_IMG_8877.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Indian_Silverbill_I_IMG_8877.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Foraging on the ground (<a href="/wiki/Hodal" title="Hodal">Hodal</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The adult Indian Silverbill is 11–11.5&#160;cm long and has a conical silver-grey bill, buff-brown upperparts, white underparts, buffy flanks and dark wings. The tail is black and the wings are dark contrasting with a white rump. The sexes are similar, but immatures have buff underparts and a shorter tail. The tail appears pointed as the length of the feathers reduces from the centre outwards. It feeds mainly on <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a>, but also takes insects and has been known to visit nectar bearing flowers, such as those of <i><a href="/wiki/Erythrina" title="Erythrina">Erythrina</a></i> trees.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This munia was described as <i>Loxia malabarica</i> by Linnaeus who placed it along with the crossbills. Subsequently they were included in the genera <i>Uroloncha</i> and <i>Aidemosyne</i> and later in the genus <i>Lonchura</i> into which many of the Estrildid finches were included by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Delacour" title="Jean Delacour" class="mw-redirect">Jean Delacour</a> in his 1943 revision.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The species earlier included <i><a href="/wiki/Lonchura_cantans" title="Lonchura cantans" class="mw-redirect">Lonchura cantans</a></i>, the African Silverbill, which is found in the dry savannah habitats south of the Sahara Desert. In captivity the African birds were found to preferentially pair with mates within their own populations and did not recognize the Indian populations as conspecific. They are however known to produce fertile hybrids.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_Silverbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habitat and distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_distribution">Habitat and distribution</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Indian_Silverbill_(Lonchura_malabarica)_in_nest_on_Acacia_W_IMG_0023.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Indian_Silverbill_%28Lonchura_malabarica%29_in_nest_on_Acacia_W_IMG_0023.jpg/170px-Indian_Silverbill_%28Lonchura_malabarica%29_in_nest_on_Acacia_W_IMG_0023.jpg" width="170" height="144" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Indian_Silverbill_(Lonchura_malabarica)_in_nest_on_Acacia_W_IMG_0023.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Nest in a thorny <i>Acacia</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It frequents dry open scrub, fallow land and cultivation, sometimes near water. Although mainly found on the plains, they can be found up to about 1200 m in some sub-Himalayan regions.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> It occurs in <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>. It has been accidentally introduced into many other parts of the world and has established itself in <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> (introduced), <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, <a href="/wiki/USA" title="USA" class="mw-redirect">USA</a> (introduced) and <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Islands" title="Virgin Islands">Virgin Islands</a> (possibly extinct) and Nice (S. France).</p>
<p>Although largely sedentary, some populations make seasonal movements.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_Silverbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span></h2>
<p>The species was earlier placed in the genus <i>Lonchura</i> but some authors suggested their placement in the genus <i>Euodice</i>. The placement was much debated<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> but more recent studies suggest that it is closely related to the <a href="/wiki/African_Silverbill" title="African Silverbill">African Silverbill</a> (now <i>Euodice cantans</i>) and the two form a clade that is well separated from other <i>Lonchura</i> finches. A molecular phylogeny study identifies the separation of the species from the nearest common ancestors of <i><a href="/wiki/Euodice_cantans" title="Euodice cantans" class="mw-redirect">Euodice cantans</a></i> (of Africa) and <i><a href="/wiki/Stagonopleura_guttata" title="Stagonopleura guttata" class="mw-redirect">Stagonopleura guttata</a></i> (of Australia) around 11 million years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_Silverbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Indian_Silverbills_(Lonchura_malabarica)_at_Sindhrot_near_Vadodara,_Gujrat_Pix_134.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Indian_Silverbills_%28Lonchura_malabarica%29_at_Sindhrot_near_Vadodara%2C_Gujrat_Pix_134.jpg/220px-Indian_Silverbills_%28Lonchura_malabarica%29_at_Sindhrot_near_Vadodara%2C_Gujrat_Pix_134.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Indian_Silverbills_(Lonchura_malabarica)_at_Sindhrot_near_Vadodara,_Gujrat_Pix_134.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A group (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sindhrot&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sindhrot (page does not exist)">Sindhrot</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>These birds are gregarious and are found in flocks of as many as 60 birds. They feed on the ground or on low shrubs and grass stalks. They constantly utter a low cheeping or chirping contact call as they forage. They visit water and drink with a rapid sip and swallow action.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Their feed on a wide range of grass seeds and will also make use of crop species.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The breeding season is spread out and varies with region. They nest in winter in southern India and after summer in northern India. They nest, an untidy ball of grasses with an opening on the side, is placed in low shrubs, often on thorny <i>Acacia</i> and are known to make use of the old nests of <a href="/wiki/Baya_Weaver" title="Baya Weaver">Baya Weaver</a> sometimes even visiting those that are occupied by the weaver birds. They will sometimes build their nest below the platform nests of vultures or storks.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Old nests are used as dormitories through the year for roosting. Females are known to lay their eggs in the nests of other pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> The clutch varies from 4 to 8 white eggs and these are incubated by both parents for about 11 days.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Helpers_at_the_nest" title="Helpers at the nest">Helpers</a> may be involved in breeding as more than a pair are sometimes seen at a nest.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Several parasitic protozoans and <a href="/wiki/Coccidia" title="Coccidia">coccidia</a> (<i>Sivatoshella lonchurae</i>) have been described from the species.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
