<p>The <b>common ringed plover</b> or <b>ringed plover</b> (<i>Charadrius hiaticula</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">plover</a> that breeds in Arctic <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>. The genus name <i>Charadrius</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">Late Latin</a> word for a yellowish bird mentioned in the fourth-century <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a>. It derives from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>kharadrios</i> a bird found in ravines and river valleys (<i>kharadra</i>, "ravine"). The specific <i>hiaticula</i> is  <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and has a similar meaning to the Greek term, coming from  <i>hiatus</i>, "cleft" and <i>-cola</i>, "dweller" (<i>colere</i>, "to dwell").<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_ringed_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adults are 17–19.5&#160;cm (6.7–7.7&#160;in) in length with a 35–41&#160;cm (14–16&#160;in) wingspan. They have a grey-brown back and wings, a white belly, and a white breast with one black neckband. They have a brown cap, a white forehead, a black mask around the eyes and a short orange and black bill. The legs are orange and only the outer two toes are slightly webbed, unlike the slightly smaller but otherwise very similar <a href="/wiki/Semipalmated_plover" title="Semipalmated plover">semipalmated plover</a>, which has all three toes slightly webbed, and also a marginally narrower breast band; it was in former times included in the present species. Juvenile ringed plovers are duller than the adults in colour, with an often incomplete grey-brown breast band, a dark bill and dull yellowish-grey legs.
</p><p>This species differs from the smaller <a href="/wiki/Little_ringed_plover" title="Little ringed plover">little ringed plover</a> in leg colour, the head pattern, and the lack of an obvious yellow eye-ring.
</p>
<h2><span id="Breeding.2C_range_and_habitat"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding,_range_and_habitat">Breeding, range and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_ringed_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Breeding, range and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common ringed plover's breeding habitat is open ground on beaches or flats across northern <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurosiberia</a>  and in Arctic northeast <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Some birds breed inland, and in western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> they nest as far south as northern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. They nest on the ground in an open area with little or no plant growth.
</p><p>If a potential predator approaches the nest, the adult will walk away from the scrape, calling to attract the intruder and feigning a broken wing. Once the intruder is far enough from the nest, the plover flies off.
</p><p>Common ringed plovers are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and winter in coastal areas south to <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. In Norway, geolocators have revealed that adult breeding birds migrate to West Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Many birds in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and northern France are resident throughout the year.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_ringed_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>These birds forage for food on beaches, tidal flats and fields, usually by sight. They eat insects, crustaceans and worms.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_ringed_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are three weakly defined <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-IOC_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IOC-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> which vary slightly in size and mantle colour; they intergrade where their ranges meet:
</p>
<ul><li><i>C. h. psammodroma</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/Finn_Salomonsen" title="Finn Salomonsen">Salomonsen</a>, 1930</small>: breeds in Iceland, Greenland, northeast Canada; winters in west Africa. It is intermediate in size and colour.</li>
<li><i>C. h. hiaticula</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, 1758</small>: breeds from temperate western Europe north to central Scandinavia; resident or short-distance migrant to southwest Europe. It is the largest and palest subspecies.</li>
<li><i>C. h. tundrae</i> – <small>(<a href="/wiki/Percy_Lowe" title="Percy Lowe">Lowe</a>, 1915)</small>: breeds in Arctic northern Scandinavia and Asiatic Russia; winters in Africa and southwest Asia. It is the smallest and darkest subspecies.</li></ul>
<p><i>C. h. hiaticula</i> and <i>C. h. tundrae</i> are among the <a href="/wiki/Taxa" class="mw-redirect" title="Taxa">taxa</a> to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>
