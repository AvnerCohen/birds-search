<p>The <b>Common Linnet</b> (<i>Carduelis cannabina</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finch</a> family Fringillidae. It derives its scientific name from its fondness for <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a> and its <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English name</a> from its liking for seeds of <a href="/wiki/Flax" title="Flax">flax</a>, from which <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">linen</a> is made.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Linnet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The Common Linnet is a slim bird with a long tail. The upperparts are brown, the throat is sullied white and the bill is grey. The summer male has a grey nape, red head patch and red breast.</p>
<p>Females and young birds lack the red and have white underparts with the breast streaked buff. The Linnet's pleasant song contains fast trills and twitters.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Linnet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<p>The Common Linnet bird breeds in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, western <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and north <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It is partially resident, but many eastern and northern birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> further south in the breeding range or move to the coasts.</p>
<p>They are sometimes found several hundred miles at sea.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Linnet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<p>Open land with thick bushes is favoured for breeding, including heathland and garden. It builds its nest in a bush, laying 4-7 eggs.</p>
<p>This species can form large flocks outside the breeding season, sometimes mixed with other finches, such as <a href="/wiki/Twite" title="Twite">Twite</a>, on coasts and salt marshes. Its food mainly consists of seeds, which it also feeds to its chicks.</p>
<p>They feed on the ground, and low down in bushes.</p>
<p>They like small to medium sized seeds: most arable weeds, <a href="/wiki/Polygonum" title="Polygonum">Polygonums</a> (<a href="/wiki/Knotgrass" title="Knotgrass" class="mw-redirect">Knotgrass</a>, dock), <a href="/wiki/Crucifers" title="Crucifers" class="mw-redirect">Crucifers</a> (<a href="/wiki/Charlock" title="Charlock" class="mw-redirect">Charlock</a>, shepherds purse <a href="/wiki/Capsella_bursa-pastoris" title="Capsella bursa-pastoris">Capsella bursa-pastoris</a>), Chickweeds <a href="/wiki/Stellaria" title="Stellaria">Stellaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dandelion" title="Dandelion" class="mw-redirect">Dandelions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thistle" title="Thistle">Thistle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sow-thistle" title="Sow-thistle" class="mw-redirect">Sow-thistle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mayweed" title="Mayweed">Mayweed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_groundsel" title="Common groundsel" class="mw-redirect">Common groundsel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_Hawthorn" title="Common Hawthorn" class="mw-redirect">Common Hawthorn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">Birch</a>.</p>
<p>They have a small component of <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">Invertebrates</a> in their diet.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Linnet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span></h2>

<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">

<td class="mbox-text" style=""><span class="mbox-text-span">The examples and perspective in this section <b>deal primarily with the United Kingdom and do not represent a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:WikiProject_Countering_systemic_bias" title="Wikipedia:WikiProject Countering systemic bias">worldwide view</a> of the subject</b>. <span class="hide-when-compact">Please <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Common_Linnet&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> and discuss the issue on the <a href="/wiki/Talk:Common_Linnet" title="Talk:Common Linnet">talk page</a>.</span> <small><i>(November 2011)</i></small> </span></td>

<p>The Common Linnet is listed by the UK <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_Action_Plan" title="Biodiversity Action Plan" class="mw-redirect">Biodiversity Action Plan</a> as a priority species. It is protected in the UK by the <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_and_Countryside_Act_1981" title="Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981">Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981</a>.</p>
<p>In Britain, populations are declining, attributed to increasing use of herbicides, aggressive scrub removal and excessive hedge trimming; its population fell by 56% between 1968 to 1991. This was probably due to decrease in seed supply and increasing use of herbicide being two of the factors.</p>
<p>During the 1980-2009 period, its European population decreased by 62% according to the Pan-European Common Bird Monitoring Scheme<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Favourable management practices on agricultural land include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Set-aside" title="Set-aside">Set-aside</a></li>
<li>Overwinter Stubbles</li>
<li>Uncultivated margins, ditches, field corners</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Conservation_headland" title="Conservation headland">Conservation headlands</a></li>
<li>Wild bird cover of species that produce small, oil-rich seeds such as <a href="/wiki/Kale" title="Kale">Kale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quinoa" title="Quinoa">Quinoa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mustard_plant" title="Mustard plant">Mustard plant</a>, Oil-seed rape <a href="/wiki/Brassica_napus" title="Brassica napus" class="mw-redirect">Brassica napus</a></li>
<li>Restoration and creation of hay meadows</li>
<li>Short, thick, thorny hedgerows and scrub for nesting habitat</li>
<li>Restoration of <a href="/wiki/Meadow" title="Meadow">meadows</a></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Linnet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cultural references">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">Cultural references</span></h2>
<p>The bird was a popular pet in the late <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edwardian_era" title="Edwardian era">Edwardian eras</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tennyson" title="Tennyson" class="mw-redirect">Tennyson</a> mentions "the linnet born within the cage" in part 27 of the poem <i>In Memoriam A.H.H</i>, the same section that contains the famous lines "'Tis better to have loved and lost / Than never to have loved at all." A "cock linnet" features in the classic British <a href="/wiki/Music_hall" title="Music hall">music hall</a> song of that period <a href="/wiki/My_Old_Man_(Said_Follow_the_Van)" title="My Old Man (Said Follow the Van)">My Old Man</a>, and as a character in <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Wilde" title="Oscar Wilde">Oscar Wilde</a>'s children's story <a href="/wiki/The_Happy_Prince#.22The_Devoted_Friend.22" title="The Happy Prince" class="mw-redirect">The Devoted Friend</a>. <a href="/wiki/Wilde" title="Wilde">Wilde</a> also mentions how the call of the linnet awakens <a href="/wiki/The_Selfish_Giant" title="The Selfish Giant" class="mw-redirect">The Selfish Giant</a> to the one tree where it is springtime in his garden. <a href="/wiki/William_Butler_Yeats" title="William Butler Yeats" class="mw-redirect">William Butler Yeats</a> evokes the image of the linnet in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lake_Isle_of_Innisfree" title="The Lake Isle of Innisfree" class="mw-redirect">The Lake Isle of Innisfree</a></i> (1890) in line 8: "And evening full of the linnet's wings."</p>
<p>"The Linnets" has become the nickname of <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Lynn_F.C." title="King's Lynn F.C.">King's Lynn Football Club</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burscough_F.C." title="Burscough F.C.">Burscough Football Club</a> and <a href="/wiki/Runcorn_Linnets_F.C." title="Runcorn Linnets F.C.">Runcorn Linnets Football Club</a> (formerly known as 'Runcorn F.C.' and Runcorn F.C. Halton). <a href="/wiki/Barry_Town_F.C." title="Barry Town F.C.">Barry Town F.C.</a>, the South Wales-based football team, also used to be nicknamed 'The Linnets'.</p>
<p>William Blake invokes "the linnet's song" in one of the poems entitled "Song" in his "Poetical Sketches."</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/William_Wordsworth" title="William Wordsworth">William Wordsworth</a> argued that the song of the Linnet provides more wisdom than books in the third verse of The Tables Turned:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"Books! 'tis a dull and endless strife:</dd>
<dd>Come, hear the woodland linnet,</dd>
<dd>How sweet his music! on my life,</dd>
<dd>There's more of wisdom in it."</dd>
</dl>
<p>But the fellow English poet <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bridges" title="Robert Bridges">Robert Bridges</a> used the Linnet instead to express the limitations of poetry - concentrating on the difficulty in poetry of conveying the beauty of a bird's song. He wrote in the first verse:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"I heard a linnet courting</dd>
<dd>His lady in the spring:</dd>
<dd>His mates were idly sporting,</dd>
<dd>Nor stayed to hear him sing</dd>
<dd>His song of love.--</dd>
<dd>I fear my speech distorting</dd>
<dd>His tender love."</dd>
</dl>
<p>The musical <a href="/wiki/Sweeney_Todd:_The_Demon_Barber_of_Fleet_Street_(musical)" title="Sweeney Todd: The Demon Barber of Fleet Street (musical)" class="mw-redirect">Sweeney Todd</a> features the song "Green Finch and Linnet Bird," in which a young lady confined to her room wonders why caged birds sing:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"Green finch and linnet bird,</dd>
<dd>Nightingale, blackbird,</dd>
<dd>How is it you sing?</dd>
<dd>How can you jubilate,</dd>
<dd>Sitting in cages,</dd>
<dd>Never taking wing?"</dd>
</dl>
