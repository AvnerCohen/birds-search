<p>The <b>common linnet</b> (<i>Linaria cannabina</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finch</a> family, Fringillidae. It derives its scientific name from its fondness for <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a> seeds and <a href="/wiki/Flax" title="Flax">flax</a> seeds—flax being the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English name</a> for the plant from which <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">linen</a> is made.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_linnet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1758, the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> included the common linnet in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> under the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a>, <i>Acanthis cannabina</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-checklist_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-checklist-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The species was formerly placed in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Carduelis" title="Carduelis">Carduelis</a></i> but based on the results of a <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Phylogenetic">phylogenetic</a> analysis of <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitochondrial">mitochondrial</a> and nuclear DNA sequences published in 2012, it was moved to the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Linaria_(bird)" title="Linaria (bird)">Linaria</a></i> that had been introduced by the German naturalist <a href="/wiki/Johann_Matth%C3%A4us_Bechstein" title="Johann Matthäus Bechstein">Johann Matthäus Bechstein</a> in 1802.<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The genus name <i>linaria</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for a linen-weaver, from <i>linum</i>, "flax". The species name <i>cannabina</i> comes from the Latin for <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The English name has a similar root, being derived from <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i>linette</i>, from <i>lin</i>, "flax".<sup id="cite_ref-OED_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are seven recognised <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Scottish linnet</b> (<i>L. c. autochthona</i>) (<a href="/wiki/Phillip_Clancey" title="Phillip Clancey">Clancey</a>, 1946) &#8211; Scotland</li>
<li><b>Eurasian linnet</b> (<i>L. c. cannabina</i>) (<a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">1758</a>) &#8211; western, central and northern Europe, western and central Siberia. Non-breeding in North Africa and Southwest Asia</li>
<li><b>eastern linnet</b> (<i>L. c. bella</i>) (<a href="/wiki/Christian_Ludwig_Brehm" title="Christian Ludwig Brehm">Brehm, CL</a>, 1845) &#8211; the Middle East to Mongolia and northwestern China</li>
<li><b>Mediterranean linnet</b> (<i>L. c. mediterranea</i>) (<a href="/wiki/Viktor_von_Tschusi_zu_Schmidhoffen" title="Viktor von Tschusi zu Schmidhoffen">Tschusi</a>, 1903) &#8211; the Iberian Peninsula, Italy, Greece, Northwest Africa and the Mediterranean islands</li>
<li><b>Madeiran linnet</b> (<i>L. c. guentheri</i>) (<a href="/wiki/Hans_Edmund_Wolters" title="Hans Edmund Wolters">Wolters</a>, 1953) &#8211; Madeira</li>
<li><b>West Canary Islands linnet</b> (<i>L. c. meadewaldoi</i>) (<a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a>, 1901) &#8211; the western and central Canary Islands (El Hierro and Gran Canaria)</li>
<li><b>East Canary Islands linnet</b> (<i>L. c. harterti</i>) (<a href="/wiki/David_Armitage_Bannerman" title="David Armitage Bannerman">Bannerman</a>, 1913) &#8211; the eastern Canary Islands (Alegranza, Lanzarote and Fuerteventura)</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_linnet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common linnet is a slim bird with a long tail. The upper parts are brown, the throat is sullied white and the bill is grey. The summer male has a grey nape, red head-patch and red breast. Females and young birds lack the red and have white underparts, the breast streaked buff.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_linnet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common linnet breeds in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>. It is partially resident, but many eastern and northern birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> farther south in the breeding range or move to the coasts. They are sometimes found several hundred miles off-shore.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_linnet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Carduelis_cannabina_cannabina_MHNT_223_St_Mor%C3%A9.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Carduelis_cannabina_cannabina_MHNT_223_St_Mor%C3%A9.jpg/220px-Carduelis_cannabina_cannabina_MHNT_223_St_Mor%C3%A9.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="202" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Carduelis_cannabina_cannabina_MHNT_223_St_Mor%C3%A9.jpg/330px-Carduelis_cannabina_cannabina_MHNT_223_St_Mor%C3%A9.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Carduelis_cannabina_cannabina_MHNT_223_St_Mor%C3%A9.jpg/440px-Carduelis_cannabina_cannabina_MHNT_223_St_Mor%C3%A9.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3970" data-file-height="3638" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Carduelis_cannabina_cannabina_MHNT_223_St_Mor%C3%A9.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs</div></div></div>
<p>Open land with thick bushes is favoured for breeding, including heathland and garden. It builds its nest in a bush, laying four to seven eggs.
</p><p>This species can form large flocks outside the breeding season, sometimes mixed with other finches, such as <a href="/wiki/Twite" title="Twite">twite</a>, on coasts and salt marshes.
</p><p>The common linnet's pleasant song contains fast trills and twitters.
</p><p>It feeds on the ground, and low down in bushes, its food mainly consisting of seeds, which it also feeds to its chicks. It likes small to medium-sized seeds from most arable weeds, <a href="/wiki/Polygonum" title="Polygonum">knotgrass</a>, dock), <a href="/wiki/Crucifers" class="mw-redirect" title="Crucifers">crucifers</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Charlock" class="mw-redirect" title="Charlock">charlock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capsella_bursa-pastoris" title="Capsella bursa-pastoris">shepherd's purse</a>), chickweeds, <a href="/wiki/Dandelion" class="mw-redirect" title="Dandelion">dandelions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thistle" title="Thistle">thistle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sow-thistle" class="mw-redirect" title="Sow-thistle">sow-thistle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mayweed" title="Mayweed">mayweed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Senecio_vulgaris" title="Senecio vulgaris">common groundsel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_hawthorn" class="mw-redirect" title="Common hawthorn">common hawthorn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a>. They have a small component of <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">Invertebrates</a> in their diet.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_linnet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-Globalize plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-globalize" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="Globe icon." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ambox_globe_content.svg/48px-Ambox_globe_content.svg.png" decoding="async" width="48" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ambox_globe_content.svg/73px-Ambox_globe_content.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ambox_globe_content.svg/97px-Ambox_globe_content.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="350" data-file-height="290" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">The examples and perspective in this section <b>deal primarily with the United Kingdom and do not represent a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:WikiProject_Countering_systemic_bias" title="Wikipedia:WikiProject Countering systemic bias">worldwide view</a> of the subject</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> You may <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Common_linnet&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a>, discuss the issue on the <a href="/wiki/Talk:Common_linnet" title="Talk:Common linnet">talk page</a>, or create a new section, as appropriate.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">November 2011</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The common linnet is listed by the UK <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_Action_Plan" class="mw-redirect" title="Biodiversity Action Plan">Biodiversity Action Plan</a> as a priority species. It is protected in the UK by the <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_and_Countryside_Act_1981" title="Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981">Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981</a>.
</p><p>In Britain, populations are declining, attributed to increasing use of herbicides, aggressive scrub removal and excessive hedge trimming; its population fell by 56% between 1968 and 1991, probably due to a decrease in seed supply and the increasing use of herbicide. From 1980–2009, according to the Pan-European Common Bird Monitoring Scheme, the European population decreased by 62%<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Favourable management practices on agricultural land include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Set-aside" title="Set-aside">Set-aside</a></li>
<li>Overwinter stubbles</li>
<li>Uncultivated margins, ditches, field corners</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Conservation_headland" title="Conservation headland">Conservation headlands</a></li>
<li>Wild bird cover, using plants that produce small, oil-rich seeds, such as <a href="/wiki/Kale" title="Kale">kale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quinoa" title="Quinoa">quinoa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mustard_plant" title="Mustard plant">mustard plant</a> and oil-seed rape <i><a href="/wiki/Brassica_napus" class="mw-redirect" title="Brassica napus">Brassica napus</a></i></li>
<li>Restoration of <a href="/wiki/Meadow" title="Meadow">meadows</a>: restoration and creation of hay-meadows</li>
<li>Short, thick, thorny hedgerows and scrub for nesting habitat</li></ul>
<p>Robert Burns's 1788 poem "A Mother's Lament for the Death of Her Son" also tells of a linnet bird bewailing her ravished young.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">Cultural references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_linnet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cultural references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The bird was a popular pet in the late <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edwardian_era" title="Edwardian era">Edwardian eras</a>. <a href="/wiki/Alfred,_Lord_Tennyson" title="Alfred, Lord Tennyson">Alfred, Lord Tennyson</a> mentions "the linnet born within the cage" in Canto 27 of his 1849 poem "<a href="/wiki/In_Memoriam_A.H.H." title="In Memoriam A.H.H.">In Memoriam A.H.H.</a>", the same section that contains the famous lines "'Tis better to have loved and lost / Than never to have loved at all." A "cock linnet" features in the classic British <a href="/wiki/Music_hall" title="Music hall">music hall</a> song of that period <a href="/wiki/My_Old_Man_(Said_Follow_the_Van)" class="mw-redirect" title="My Old Man (Said Follow the Van)">My Old Man</a>, and as a character in <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Wilde" title="Oscar Wilde">Oscar Wilde</a>'s children's story <a href="/wiki/The_Happy_Prince_and_Other_Tales#&quot;The_Devoted_Friend&quot;" title="The Happy Prince and Other Tales">"The Devoted Friend"</a>; Wilde also mentions how the call of the linnet awakens <a href="/wiki/The_Happy_Prince_and_Other_Tales#&quot;The_Selfish_Giant&quot;" title="The Happy Prince and Other Tales">"The Selfish Giant"</a> to the one tree where it is springtime in his garden. <a href="/wiki/William_Butler_Yeats" class="mw-redirect" title="William Butler Yeats">William Butler Yeats</a> evokes the image of the common linnet in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lake_Isle_of_Innisfree" class="mw-redirect" title="The Lake Isle of Innisfree">The Lake Isle of Innisfree</a></i> (1890) in line 8: "And evening full of the linnet's wings." In the novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Old_Curiosity_Shop" title="The Old Curiosity Shop">The Old Curiosity Shop</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>, the heroine Nell keeps "only a poor linnet" in a cage, which she leaves for Kit as a sign of her gratefulness to him.
</p><p>The English Baroque composer <a href="/wiki/John_Blow" title="John Blow">John Blow</a> composed an ode on the occasion of the death of his colleague <a href="/wiki/Henry_Purcell" title="Henry Purcell">Henry Purcell</a>, "An Ode on the Death of Mr. Purcell" set to the poem "Mark how the lark and linnet sing" by the poet <a href="/wiki/John_Dryden" title="John Dryden">John Dryden</a>.
</p><p>"The Linnets" has become the nickname of <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Lynn_F.C." title="King&#39;s Lynn F.C.">King's Lynn Football Club</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burscough_F.C." title="Burscough F.C.">Burscough Football Club</a> and <a href="/wiki/Runcorn_Linnets_F.C." title="Runcorn Linnets F.C.">Runcorn Linnets Football Club</a> (formerly known as 'Runcorn F.C.' and Runcorn F.C. Halton). <a href="/wiki/Barry_Town_F.C." class="mw-redirect" title="Barry Town F.C.">Barry Town F.C.</a>, the South Wales-based football team, also used to be nicknamed 'The Linnets'.
</p><p>William Blake invokes "the linnet's song" in one of the poems entitled "Song" in his <i>Poetical Sketches</i>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Walter_de_la_Mare" title="Walter de la Mare">Walter de la Mare</a>'s poem "The Linnet", published in 1918 in the collection <i>Motley and Other Poems</i>, has been set to music by a number of composers including Cecil <a href="/wiki/Armstrong_Gibbs" title="Armstrong Gibbs">Armstrong Gibbs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Leighton" title="Kenneth Leighton">Kenneth Leighton</a><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Gibbons" title="Jack Gibbons">Jack Gibbons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Eurovision_Song_Contest_2014" title="Eurovision Song Contest 2014">Eurovision Song Contest 2014</a> entry for the Netherlands "<a href="/wiki/The_Common_Linnets" title="The Common Linnets">The Common Linnets</a>" is a direct reference to the bird.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/William_Wordsworth" title="William Wordsworth">William Wordsworth</a> argued that the song of the common linnet provides more wisdom than books in the third verse of "The Tables Turned":
</p>
<blockquote><div class="poem">
<p>Books! 'tis a dull and endless strife:<br />
Come, hear the woodland linnet,<br />
How sweet his music! on my life,<br />
There's more of wisdom in it.
</p>
</div></blockquote>
<p>But the fellow English poet <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bridges" title="Robert Bridges">Robert Bridges</a> used the common linnet instead to express the limitations of poetry—concentrating on the difficulty in poetry of conveying the beauty of a bird's song. He wrote in the first verse:
</p>
<blockquote><div class="poem">
<p>I heard a linnet courting<br />
His lady in the spring:<br />
His mates were idly sporting,<br />
Nor stayed to hear him sing<br />
His song of love.—<br />
I fear my speech distorting<br />
His tender love.
</p>
</div></blockquote>
<p>The musical <a href="/wiki/Sweeney_Todd:_The_Demon_Barber_of_Fleet_Street_(musical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sweeney Todd: The Demon Barber of Fleet Street (musical)">Sweeney Todd</a> features the song "Green Finch and Linnet Bird", in which a young lady confined to her room wonders why caged birds sing:
</p>
<blockquote><div class="poem">
<p>Green finch and linnet bird,<br />
Nightingale, blackbird,<br />
How is it you sing?<br />
How can you jubilate,<br />
Sitting in cages,<br />
Never taking wing?
</p>
</div></blockquote>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Emily_Dickinson" title="Emily Dickinson">Emily Dickinson</a>'s poem "Morns like these—we parted—" the last line is: "And this linnet flew!"
</p>
