<p>The <b>South Polar Skua</b> (<i>Stercorarius maccormicki</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Skua" title="Skua">skua</a> family Stercorariidae. An older name for the bird is <b>MacCormick’s Skua</b>, after explorer and naval surgeon <a href="/wiki/Robert_McCormick_(explorer)" title="Robert McCormick (explorer)">Robert McCormick</a>, who first collected the <a href="/wiki/Type_specimen" title="Type specimen" class="mw-redirect">type specimen</a>. This species and the other large southern hemisphere skuas, together with <a href="/wiki/Great_Skua" title="Great Skua">Great Skua</a>, are sometimes placed in a separate genus <i>Catharacta</i>.</p>
<p>The South Polar Skua is a large <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> (though small compared to other skuas sometimes placed in <i>Catharacta</i>) that measures approximately 53 centimetres (21&#160;in) in length. It breeds on <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a> coasts, usually laying two eggs in November and December. Like other skuas, it will fly at the head of a human or other intruder approaching its nest. It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>, wintering at sea in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Oceans</a>. In the eastern North Atlantic it is replaced by the Great Skua.</p>
<p>The South Polar Skua eats mainly fish, which it often obtains by robbing <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">terns</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Gannet" title="Gannet">gannets</a> of their catches. It will also directly attack and kill other seabirds and their chicks. It also eats scraps and <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">carrion</a>.</p>
<p>Like most other skua <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, it continues this piratical behaviour throughout the year, showing less agility and more brute force than the smaller skuas (jaegers) when it harasses its victims.</p>
<p>Distinguishing this skua from the northern hemisphere <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Skua" title="Arctic Skua" class="mw-redirect">Arctic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pomarine_Skua" title="Pomarine Skua">Pomarine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Long-tailed_Skua" title="Long-tailed Skua" class="mw-redirect">Long-tailed Skuas</a> is relatively straightforward. The large size, massive barrel chest, and white wing flashes of this bird are distinctive even at a distance. The flight is direct and powerful.</p>
<p>Identification of this skua is more complicated when it is necessary to distinguish it from the closely related Great Skua of the North Atlantic, and the other large southern hemisphere skuas.</p>
<p>Adult South Polar Skuas are greyish brown above, and have a whitish (pale morph) or straw-brown (intermediate morph) head and underparts, and the contrast between head and body makes it easy to separate from similar species with good views.</p>
<p>Juveniles and adult dark morphs are harder to distinguish from their relatives, and more subjective or difficult-to-observe criteria, such as the colder brown plumage and blue bill base, must be used.</p>
<p>Identification problems make claims of any southern hemisphere skua in the eastern North Atlantic problematic, and few records of South Polar Skua have been accepted in Western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. Similar problems occur, of course, with extra-limital claims of Great Skua.</p>
<p>South Polar Skuas <a href="/wiki/The_world%27s_southernmost" title="The world's southernmost" class="mw-redirect">have been seen</a> at the actual <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a> itself.</p>
