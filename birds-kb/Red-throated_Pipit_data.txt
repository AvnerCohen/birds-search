<p>The <b>red-throated pipit</b> (<i>Anthus cervinus</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> which breeds in the far north of Europe and the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>, with a foothold in northern <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. It is a long-distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a> moving in winter to Africa, south and east Asia and west coast United States. It is a vagrant to western Europe.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-throated_pipit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The scientific name is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>. <i>Anthus</i> is the name for a small bird of grasslands, and the specific <i>cervinus</i> means "stag-coloured", from <i>cervus</i>,  "<a href="/wiki/Stag" class="mw-redirect" title="Stag">stag</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-throated_pipit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is a small <a href="/wiki/Pipit" title="Pipit">pipit</a>, with adults easily identified in the breeding season by their brick red face and throat. In other plumages this is an undistinguished looking species, heavily streaked brown above, with whitish mantle stripes, and with black markings on a white background below. It is very similar in appearance to the <a href="/wiki/Meadow_pipit" title="Meadow pipit">meadow pipit</a> and in the autumn it much resembles the <a href="/wiki/Tree_pipit" title="Tree pipit">tree pipit</a> but has an altogether more striped appearance because of the larger numbers of streaks on the cap, back, flank, rump and chest. The flight of the red-throated pipit is strong and direct, and it gives a characteristic <i>psii</i> call as it flies.<sup id="cite_ref-NatureGate_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NatureGate-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-throated_pipit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The red-throated pipit is native to the boreal regions of northern Europe and Asia. The breeding habitat is open country including mountains, marshland and tundra. The nest is built on the ground, often beside a tussock of grass, on rough grassland or on a hummock in a marsh. It is made of dry grasses and sedges with a soft lining of reindeer hair or down. Four to six eggs are laid and incubated by the female for nearly two weeks. The young are fledged and ready to leave the nest about twelve days later. The red-throated pipit is insectivorous, like its relatives, but also eats seeds.<sup id="cite_ref-NatureGate_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NatureGate-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The red-throated pipit has a very large range and the global population has been estimated to be about two million individuals. It is rated as being of "<a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>" by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> as its population is believed to be stable and it faces no particular threats.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
