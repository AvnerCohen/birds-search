<p>The <b>European roller</b> (<i>Coracias garrulus</i>) is the only member of the <a href="/wiki/Coraciidae" title="Coraciidae">roller</a> family of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> to breed in Europe. Its overall range extends into the Middle East, Central Asia and Morocco.
</p><p>The European roller is found in a wide variety of habitats, avoiding only treeless plains. It winters primarily in dry wooded savanna and bushy plains, where it typically nests in tree holes.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_roller&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Coraciidae" title="Coraciidae">rollers</a> are medium-sized Old World birds of open woodland habitats. They have brightly coloured plumage and a hooked bill. Most are found south of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>. The genus <i><a href="/wiki/Coracias" title="Coracias">Coracias</a></i> contains eight species of sit-and-wait hunters. The European roller is similar in appearance and behaviour to the <a href="/wiki/Abyssinian_roller" title="Abyssinian roller">Abyssinian roller</a>, which appears to be its closest relative. These two birds and the <a href="/wiki/Lilac-breasted_roller" title="Lilac-breasted roller">lilac-breasted roller</a> seem to share a common ancestry and could possibly be considered to form <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The European roller was described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a> under its current name.<sup id="cite_ref-Linn_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linn-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The generic name derives from Greek <i>korakias</i> referring to a type of crow, perhaps the <a href="/wiki/Red-billed_chough" title="Red-billed chough">red-billed chough</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-job117_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job117-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific epithet</a> <i>garrulus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means 'chattering' or 'noisy'.<sup id="cite_ref-job171_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job171-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Alternate names include the <b>blue roller</b>, <b>common roller</b>, <b>Eurasian roller</b>, or simply <b>roller</b>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_roller&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> recognised:<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Western European roller</b> (<i>C. g. garrulus</i>) - <small><a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, 1758</small>: The <a href="/wiki/Subspecies#Nominotypical_subspecies_and_subspecies_autonyms" title="Subspecies">nominate subspecies</a>. Found in north-western Africa, southern Europe and east through north-western Iran to south-western <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Fry_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fry-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Eastern European roller</b> (<i>C. g. semenowi</i>) - <small><a href="/wiki/Harald_von_Loudon" title="Harald von Loudon">Loudon</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Viktor_von_Tschusi_zu_Schmidhoffen" title="Viktor von Tschusi zu Schmidhoffen">Tschusi</a>, 1902</small>:<sup id="cite_ref-loudon_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-loudon-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Also named the <b>Kashmir roller</b>. Found in Iraq and southern Iran east through <a href="/wiki/Kashmir" title="Kashmir">Kashmir</a> and southern Kazakhstan to <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a> in western China.<sup id="cite_ref-Fry_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fry-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>

<p>The European roller is a stocky bird, the size of a <a href="/wiki/Jackdaw" class="mw-redirect" title="Jackdaw">jackdaw</a> at 29–32&#160;cm in length with a 52–58&#160;cm wingspan; it is mainly blue with an orange-brown back. Rollers often perch prominently on trees, posts or overhead wires, like giant <a href="/wiki/Shrike" title="Shrike">shrikes</a>, whilst watching for the large insects, small reptiles, rodents and frogs that they eat. The diet of adult rollers is dominated by <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">Coleoptera</a>, whereas nestlings mostly eat <a href="/wiki/Orthoptera" title="Orthoptera">Orthoptera</a>, such as grasshoppers and bush crickets.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species is striking in its strong direct flight, with the brilliant blue contrasting with black flight feathers. Sexes are similar, but the juvenile is a drabber version of the adult.
</p><p>The display of this bird is like that of a <a href="/wiki/Northern_lapwing" title="Northern lapwing">lapwing</a>, with the twists and turns that give this species its English name. It nests in an unlined tree or cliff hole, and lays up to six eggs.
</p><p>The call is a harsh <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crow</a>-like sound. It gives a raucous series of calls when nervous.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_roller&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European roller is a bird of warmer regions. The nominate subspecies breeds in northern Africa from Morocco to Tunisia, in southern and east-central Europe, and eastwards through northwestern Iran to southwestern Siberia. The subspecies <i>C. g. semenowi</i> breeds from Iraq and southern Iran east through <a href="/wiki/Kashmir" title="Kashmir">Kashmir</a> and southern Kazakhstan to <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a>. The European range was formerly more extensive, but there has been a long-term decline in the north and west, with extinction as a nesting bird in Sweden and Germany. The European roller is a long-distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Africa_south_of_the_Sahara" class="mw-redirect" title="Africa south of the Sahara">Africa south of the Sahara</a> in two distinct regions, from Senegal east to Cameroon and from Ethiopia west (with observations in the <a href="/wiki/Degua_Tembien" class="mw-redirect" title="Degua Tembien">Degua Tembien</a> mountains)<sup id="cite_ref-ALN2019_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ALN2019-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> to Congo and south to South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Fry_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fry-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Some populations migrate to Africa through India. A collision with an aircraft over the Arabian Sea has been recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-satheesan_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-satheesan-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Catry2014_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Catry2014-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Coracias_garrulus_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_161_Seville_HdB.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Coracias_garrulus_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_161_Seville_HdB.jpg/220px-Coracias_garrulus_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_161_Seville_HdB.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Coracias_garrulus_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_161_Seville_HdB.jpg/330px-Coracias_garrulus_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_161_Seville_HdB.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Coracias_garrulus_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_161_Seville_HdB.jpg/440px-Coracias_garrulus_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_161_Seville_HdB.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4555" data-file-height="4138" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Coracias_garrulus_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_161_Seville_HdB.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs of <i>Coracias garrulus</i></div></div></div>
<p>It is a bird of warm, dry, open country with scattered trees, preferring lowlands, but occurs up to 1000&#160;m (3300&#160;ft) in Europe and 2000&#160;m (6600&#160;ft) in Morocco. Oak and pine woodlands with open areas are prime breeding habitat, but farms, orchards and similar areas with mixed vegetation are also used. In Africa, a similarly wide range of dry, open land with trees is used.<sup id="cite_ref-Fry_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fry-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It winters primarily in dry, wooded savanna and bushy plains, where it typically nests in tree holes.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Defence">Defence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_roller&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Defence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European roller chick will vomit a foul-smelling orange <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a> onto itself to deter a predator. The smell also warns the parents on their return to the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NascimentoParejo2013_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NascimentoParejo2013-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration">Migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_roller&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The advent of sufficiently lightweight tracking technology has facilitated several recent studies of roller <a href="/wiki/Animal_migration" title="Animal migration">migration</a>, providing new information on the non-breeding sites used by rollers from different breeding populations. Individuals from south-west European populations migrate to south-west Africa (Angola, Namibia, and Botswana), with French and north-Spanish birds taking a direct southerly route across the Sahara, while Portuguese and south-Spanish birds take a more westerly route around the west African coast.<sup id="cite_ref-Emmenegger2013_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emmenegger2013-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Rollers from eastern European populations also spend the winter period in southern Africa, but further east in Zambia, Zimbabwe, and Botswana. The <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel savannah</a> region immediately south of the Sahara Desert (particularly the area around Lake Chad) appears to be important for rollers from many populations as an autumn re-fuelling site, and Latvian and other north and north-eastern populations migrate northwards via the Arabian Peninsula in spring.<sup id="cite_ref-Finch_1051–1062_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Finch_1051–1062-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Individuals from different breeding populations use distinct but overlapping winter sites; there is a good correlation between the longitude of individual breeding and non-breeding sites, suggesting parallel migration. In the east, the northernmost breeders (from Latvia) tend to winter south of the southernmost breeders (from Cyprus) - this suggests a pattern of 'leap-frog' migration.<sup id="cite_ref-Finch_1051–1062_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Finch_1051–1062-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_roller&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Eurasian roller has an extensive distribution in Europe and western Asia, and its European breeding population is estimated at 159,000 to 330,000 birds. When Asian breeders are added, this gives a global total population of 277,000 to 660,000 individuals. There have been fairly rapid population declines across much of its range, so it was formerly classified as <a href="/wiki/Near_threatened" class="mw-redirect" title="Near threatened">near threatened</a> by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> in 2005; it has, however, been downlisted to <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a> in 2015, as population development was judged to not meet the criteria for a more urgent rating at this time.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The European population declined by 25 percent between 1990 and 2000. The northern areas of the breeding range have fared worst, with numbers in the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_states" title="Baltic states">Baltic states</a> and northern Russia collapsing, and no birds left breeding in Estonia.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Threats include hunting while on migration around the Mediterranean, and large numbers, possibly in the thousands, are killed for food in Oman. Agricultural practices have led to the loss of trees and hedges which provide potential nest sites and perches for hunting, and <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a> have reduced the availability of insect food.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> At locations where foraging resources are abundant, providing nest boxes can be a useful short-term conservation solution; however, in other locations foraging habitat restoration is more important. <sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The European roller is a host of the <a href="/wiki/Acanthocephala" title="Acanthocephala">Acanthocephalan</a> intestinal parasite <i><a href="/wiki/Moniliformis_gracilis" class="mw-redirect" title="Moniliformis gracilis">Moniliformis gracilis</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-AminHeckmann2016_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AminHeckmann2016-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
