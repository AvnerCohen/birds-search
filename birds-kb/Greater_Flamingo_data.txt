<p>The <b>greater flamingo</b> (<i>Phoenicopterus roseus</i>) is the most widespread and largest <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Flamingo" title="Flamingo">flamingo</a> family. It is found in Africa, on the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>, in the Middle East, and in southern Europe.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_flamingo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The greater flamingo was described by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Simon_Pallas" title="Peter Simon Pallas">Peter Simon Pallas</a> in 1811. It was previously thought to be the same species as the <a href="/wiki/American_flamingo" title="American flamingo">American flamingo</a> (<i>Phoenicopterus ruber</i>), but because of coloring differences of its head, neck, body, and bill, the two flamingos are now most commonly considered separate species. The greater flamingo has no subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_flamingo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The greater flamingo is the largest living species of flamingo,<sup id="cite_ref-origin_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-origin-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> averaging 110–150&#160;cm (43–59&#160;in) tall and weighing 2–4&#160;kg (4.4–8.8&#160;lb). The largest male flamingos have been recorded at up to 187&#160;cm (74&#160;in) tall and 4.5&#160;kg (9.9&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sub-adult_Phoenicopterus_roseus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Sub-adult_Phoenicopterus_roseus.jpg/220px-Sub-adult_Phoenicopterus_roseus.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="275" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Sub-adult_Phoenicopterus_roseus.jpg/330px-Sub-adult_Phoenicopterus_roseus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Sub-adult_Phoenicopterus_roseus.jpg/440px-Sub-adult_Phoenicopterus_roseus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2400" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sub-adult_Phoenicopterus_roseus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chick with gray down</div></div></div>
<p>Most of the <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> is pinkish-white, but the wing coverts are red and the primary and secondary flight <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a> are black. The <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> is pink with a restricted black tip, and the legs are entirely pink. The call is a goose-like honking.
</p><p>Chicks are covered in gray fluffy down. Subadult flamingos are paler with dark legs. Adults feeding chicks also become paler, but retain the bright pink legs. The coloration comes from the <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">carotenoid pigments</a> in the organisms that live in their feeding grounds. 
Secretions of the <a href="/wiki/Uropygial_gland" title="Uropygial gland">uropygial gland</a> also contain carotenoids.  During the breeding season, greater flamingos increase the frequency of their spreading uropygial secretions over their feathers and thereby enhance their color.  This cosmetic use of uropygial secretions has been described as applying "make-up".<sup id="cite_ref-Amat_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amat-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_flamingo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Greater_flamingos_at_Kutch.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Greater_flamingos_at_Kutch.jpg/220px-Greater_flamingos_at_Kutch.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Greater_flamingos_at_Kutch.jpg/330px-Greater_flamingos_at_Kutch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Greater_flamingos_at_Kutch.jpg/440px-Greater_flamingos_at_Kutch.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1366" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Greater_flamingos_at_Kutch.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Greater flamingos at Kutch</div></div></div>
<p>It is found in parts of Africa, southern Asia (<a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> and coastal regions of <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>), the Middle East  (<a href="/wiki/Bahrain" title="Bahrain">Bahrain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>) and southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montenegro" title="Montenegro">Montenegro</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Macedonia" title="North Macedonia">North Macedonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, and South of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>). The most northern breeding spot is the <a href="/wiki/Vreden" title="Vreden">Zwillbrocker Venn</a> in western Germany, close to the border with the Netherlands. They have been recorded breeding in the United Arab Emirates at three different locations in the Abu Dhabi Emirate.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
In <a href="/wiki/Gujarat" title="Gujarat">Gujarat</a>, a state of India, flamingos can be observed at the <a href="/wiki/Nal_Sarovar_Bird_Sanctuary" title="Nal Sarovar Bird Sanctuary">Nal Sarovar Bird Sanctuary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Khijadiya_Bird_Sanctuary" title="Khijadiya Bird Sanctuary">Khijadiya Bird Sanctuary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kutch_Desert_Wildlife_Sanctuary#Flamingo_City" title="Kutch Desert Wildlife Sanctuary">Flamingo City</a>, and in the <a href="/wiki/Thol_Bird_Sanctuary" class="mw-redirect" title="Thol Bird Sanctuary">Thol Bird Sanctuary</a>. They remain there during the entire winter season.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_flamingo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The greater flamingo resides in <a href="/wiki/Mudflat" title="Mudflat">mudflats</a> and shallow coastal <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoons</a> with salt water. Using its feet, the bird stirs up the mud, then sucks water through its bill and filters out small <a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a>, seeds, <a href="/wiki/Blue-green_algae" class="mw-redirect" title="Blue-green algae">blue-green algae</a>, microscopic organisms, and <a href="/wiki/Mollusk" class="mw-redirect" title="Mollusk">mollusks</a>. The greater flamingo feeds with its head down, and its upper jaw is movable and not rigidly fixed to its skull.<sup id="cite_ref-stanford-flamingo_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stanford-flamingo-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Like all flamingos, this species lays a single chalky-white <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">egg</a> on a mud mound.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lifespan">Lifespan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_flamingo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lifespan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The typical lifespan in captivity, according to <a href="/wiki/Basel_Zoo" title="Basel Zoo">Basel Zoo</a>, is over 60 years.<sup id="cite_ref-vasel_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vasel-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In the wild, the average lifespan is 30 – 40 years.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats_and_predators">Threats and predators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_flamingo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Threats and predators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural">Natural</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_flamingo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Natural">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adult greater flamingos have few natural predators. Eggs and chicks may be eaten by raptors, crows, gulls, and the <a href="/wiki/Marabou_stork" title="Marabou stork">marabou stork</a> (<i>Leptoptilos crumenifer</i>); an estimated half of the predation of greater flamingo eggs and chicks is from the <a href="/wiki/Yellow-legged_gull" title="Yellow-legged gull">yellow-legged gull</a> (<i>Larus michahellis</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-marwell_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marwell-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human">Human</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_flamingo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Human">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The primary threats to flamingo populations are bacteria, toxins, and pollution in  water supplies, which is usually run-off from manufacturing companies, and encroachment on their habitat.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_human_captivity">In human captivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_flamingo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In human captivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first recorded zoo hatch was in 1959 at Zoo Basel. In <a href="/wiki/Breeding_programs_at_Zoo_Basel" title="Breeding programs at Zoo Basel">Zoo Basel's breeding program</a>, over 400 birds have been hatched with between 20 and 27 per year since 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Greater_(flamingo)" title="Greater (flamingo)">oldest known greater flamingo</a> was a bird at the <a href="/wiki/Adelaide_Zoo" title="Adelaide Zoo">Adelaide Zoo</a> in Australia that died aged at least 83 years. The bird's exact age is not known; he was already a mature adult when he arrived in Adelaide in 1933. He was euthanized in January 2014 due to complications of old age.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
