<p>The <b>barnacle goose</b> (<i>Branta leucopsis</i>) belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i>Branta</i> of black <a href="/wiki/Goose" title="Goose">geese</a>, which contains <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> with largely black <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>, distinguishing them from the grey <i><a href="/wiki/Anser_(genus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Anser (genus)">Anser</a></i> species. Despite its superficial similarity to the <a href="/wiki/Brant_goose" class="mw-redirect" title="Brant goose">brant goose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> analysis has shown it is an eastern derivative of the <a href="/wiki/Cackling_goose" title="Cackling goose">cackling goose</a> lineage.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_naming">Taxonomy and naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnacle_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The barnacle goose was first <a href="/wiki/Biological_classification" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological classification">classified taxonomically</a> by Johann Matthäus Bechstein in 1803. <i>Branta</i> is a Latinised form of <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> <i>Brandgás</i>, "burnt (black) goose" and the specific epithet is from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>leukos</i> "white", and <i>opsis</i> "faced".<sup id="cite_ref-job90_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job90-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The barnacle goose and the similar <a href="/wiki/Brant_goose" class="mw-redirect" title="Brant goose">brant goose</a> were previously considered one species, and <a href="/wiki/Barnacle_Geese_Myth" title="Barnacle Geese Myth">were formerly believed to spawn</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Goose_barnacle" title="Goose barnacle">goose barnacle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This gave rise to the English name of the barnacle goose and the scientific name of the brant.<sup id="cite_ref-job90_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job90-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is sometimes claimed that the word comes from a <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic</a> word for "<a href="/wiki/Limpet" title="Limpet">limpet</a>", but the sense-history seems to go in the opposite direction.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The barnacle myth can be dated back to at least the 12th century. <a href="/wiki/Gerald_of_Wales" title="Gerald of Wales">Gerald of Wales</a> claimed to have seen these birds hanging down from pieces of timber, <a href="/wiki/William_Turner_(naturalist)" title="William Turner (naturalist)">William Turner</a> accepted the theory, and <a href="/wiki/John_Gerard" title="John Gerard">John Gerard</a> claimed to have seen the birds emerging from their shells. The legend persisted until the end of the 18th century. In <a href="/wiki/County_Kerry" title="County Kerry">County Kerry</a>, until relatively recently, Catholics abstaining from meat during <a href="/wiki/Lent" title="Lent">Lent</a> could still eat this bird because it was considered as fish.<sup id="cite_ref-cocker_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cocker-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnacle_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The barnacle goose is a medium-sized goose, 55–70&#160;cm (22–28&#160;in) long,<sup id="cite_ref-Soothill1978_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Soothill1978-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> with a wingspan of 130–145&#160;cm (51–57&#160;in) and a body mass of 1.21–2.23&#160;kg (2.7–4.9&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ADW_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ADW-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It has a white face and black head, neck, and upper breast. Its belly is white. The wings and its back are silver-gray with black-and-white bars that look like they are shining when the light reflects on it. During flight, a V-shaped white rump patch and the silver-gray underwing linings are visible.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnacle_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Branta_leucopsis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.14.1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Branta_leucopsis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.14.1.jpg/220px-Branta_leucopsis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.14.1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Branta_leucopsis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.14.1.jpg/330px-Branta_leucopsis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.14.1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Branta_leucopsis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.14.1.jpg/440px-Branta_leucopsis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.14.1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4090" data-file-height="2637" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Branta_leucopsis_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.14.1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Branta leucopsis</i> - <a href="/wiki/MHNT" class="mw-redirect" title="MHNT">MHNT</a></div></div></div>
<p>Barnacle geese breed mainly on the Arctic islands of the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a>. The three main populations, with separate breeding and wintering ranges, from west to east, are:
</p>
<ul><li>Breeding in eastern <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, wintering on the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a> of western <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and in western <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, population about 40,000</li>
<li>Breeding on <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">Svalbard</a>, wintering on the <a href="/wiki/Solway_Firth" title="Solway Firth">Solway Firth</a> on the England/Scotland border, population about 24,000</li>
<li>Breeding on <a href="/wiki/Novaya_Zemlya" title="Novaya Zemlya">Novaya Zemlya</a>, wintering in the Netherlands, population about 130,000</li>
<li>A new fourth population, derived from the Novaya Zemlya population, has become established since 1975 breeding on the islands and coasts of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> (<a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>), and wintering in the Netherlands. Its population numbers about 8,000.</li></ul>
<p>Small numbers of feral birds, derived from escapes from <a href="/wiki/Zoo" title="Zoo">zoo</a> collections, also breed in other Northern European countries. Occasionally, a wild bird will appear in the Northeastern United States or <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, but care must be taken to separate out wild birds from escaped individuals, as barnacle geese are popular waterfowl with collectors.
</p>

<p>Barnacle geese frequently build their nests high on mountain <a href="/wiki/Cliff" title="Cliff">cliffs</a>, away from predators (primarily <a href="/wiki/Arctic_fox" title="Arctic fox">Arctic foxes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">polar bears</a>), but also away from their feeding grounds such as lakes, rivers. Like all geese, the goslings are not fed by the adults. Instead of bringing food to the newly hatched goslings, the goslings are brought to the ground. The parents show them the way to jump from the cliff and the goslings follow them by instincts and take the plunge. Unable to fly, the goslings, in their first days of life, jump off the cliff and fall; their small size, feathery down, and very light weight helps to protect some of them from serious injury when they hit the rocks below, but many die from the impact. Arctic foxes are attracted by the noise made by the parent geese during this time, and capture many dead or injured goslings. The foxes also stalk the young as they are led by the parents to wetland feeding areas.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Due to these hardships only 50% of the chicks survive the first month.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnacle_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BrantaLeucopsisFilghtB.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/BrantaLeucopsisFilghtB.jpg/220px-BrantaLeucopsisFilghtB.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/BrantaLeucopsisFilghtB.jpg/330px-BrantaLeucopsisFilghtB.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/BrantaLeucopsisFilghtB.jpg/440px-BrantaLeucopsisFilghtB.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2793" data-file-height="1862" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BrantaLeucopsisFilghtB.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In flight</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Branta_leucopsis.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Branta_leucopsis.jpg/220px-Branta_leucopsis.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Branta_leucopsis.jpg/330px-Branta_leucopsis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Branta_leucopsis.jpg/440px-Branta_leucopsis.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1544" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Branta_leucopsis.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>At the <a href="/wiki/Korkeasaari" title="Korkeasaari">Helsinki Zoo</a>, Finland</div></div></div>
<p>The barnacle goose is one of the species to which the <a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a>  applies.<sup id="cite_ref-aewa_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aewa-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Ornithological_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="Swedish Ornithological Society">Sveriges ornitologiska förening</a>, the geese began breeding in Sweden in 1971, and according to <a href="/wiki/Skansen" title="Skansen">Skansen</a>, it was 40 years ago, more or less, when the entire population of barnacle geese left in the autumn to return in spring, soon after they began breeding in the wild.  The number of barnacle geese is stable to increasing.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Folklore">Folklore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnacle_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Folklore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Barnacle_Geese_Fac_simile_of_an_Engraving_on_Wood_from_the_Cosmographie_Universelle_of_Munster_folio_Basle_1552.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Barnacle_Geese_Fac_simile_of_an_Engraving_on_Wood_from_the_Cosmographie_Universelle_of_Munster_folio_Basle_1552.png/220px-Barnacle_Geese_Fac_simile_of_an_Engraving_on_Wood_from_the_Cosmographie_Universelle_of_Munster_folio_Basle_1552.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="178" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Barnacle_Geese_Fac_simile_of_an_Engraving_on_Wood_from_the_Cosmographie_Universelle_of_Munster_folio_Basle_1552.png/330px-Barnacle_Geese_Fac_simile_of_an_Engraving_on_Wood_from_the_Cosmographie_Universelle_of_Munster_folio_Basle_1552.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Barnacle_Geese_Fac_simile_of_an_Engraving_on_Wood_from_the_Cosmographie_Universelle_of_Munster_folio_Basle_1552.png/440px-Barnacle_Geese_Fac_simile_of_an_Engraving_on_Wood_from_the_Cosmographie_Universelle_of_Munster_folio_Basle_1552.png 2x" data-file-width="884" data-file-height="714" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Barnacle_Geese_Fac_simile_of_an_Engraving_on_Wood_from_the_Cosmographie_Universelle_of_Munster_folio_Basle_1552.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>From the 1552 <i><a href="/wiki/Cosmographia_(Sebastian_M%C3%BCnster)" title="Cosmographia (Sebastian Münster)">Cosmographia</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_M%C3%BCnster" title="Sebastian Münster">Sebastian Münster</a> with <a href="/wiki/Goose_barnacle" title="Goose barnacle">goose barnacles</a> ready to turn into barnacle geese</div></div></div>
<p>The natural history of the barnacle goose was long surrounded with a legend claiming that they were born of driftwood:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Nature produces [Bernacae] against Nature in the most extraordinary way. They are like marsh geese but somewhat smaller. They are produced from fir timber tossed along the sea, and are at first like gum. Afterwards they hang down by their beaks as if they were a seaweed attached to the timber, and are surrounded by shells in order to grow more freely. Having thus in process of time been clothed with a strong coat of feathers, they either fall into the water or fly freely away into the air. They derived their food and growth from the sap of the wood or from the sea, by a secret and most wonderful process of alimentation. I have frequently seen, with my own eyes, more than a thousand of these small bodies of birds, hanging down on the sea-shore from one piece of timber, enclosed in their shells, and already formed. They do not breed and lay eggs like other birds, nor do they ever hatch any eggs, nor do they seem to build nests in any corner of the earth.<sup id="cite_ref-Heron-Allen_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heron-Allen-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:A_flock_of_barnacle_geese_in_Helsinki,_Finland.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/A_flock_of_barnacle_geese_in_Helsinki%2C_Finland.jpg/220px-A_flock_of_barnacle_geese_in_Helsinki%2C_Finland.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/A_flock_of_barnacle_geese_in_Helsinki%2C_Finland.jpg/330px-A_flock_of_barnacle_geese_in_Helsinki%2C_Finland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/A_flock_of_barnacle_geese_in_Helsinki%2C_Finland.jpg/440px-A_flock_of_barnacle_geese_in_Helsinki%2C_Finland.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:A_flock_of_barnacle_geese_in_Helsinki,_Finland.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A flock feeding at <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a>, Finland</div></div></div>

<p>The legend was widely repeated in, for example, <a href="/wiki/Vincent_of_Beauvais" title="Vincent of Beauvais">Vincent of Beauvais</a>'s great encyclopedia. However, it was also criticized by other medieval authors, including <a href="/wiki/Albertus_Magnus" title="Albertus Magnus">Albertus Magnus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Heron-Allen_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heron-Allen-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This belief may be related to the fact that these geese were never seen in summer, when they were supposedly developing underwater (they were actually breeding in remote Arctic regions) in the form of <a href="/wiki/Barnacle" title="Barnacle">barnacles</a>—which came to have the name "barnacle" because of this legend.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Based on these legends—indeed, the legends may have been invented for this purpose<sup id="cite_ref-Lankester_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lankester-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>—some Irish clerics considered barnacle goose flesh to be acceptable <a href="/wiki/Fasting" title="Fasting">fast day</a> food, a practice that was criticized by <a href="/wiki/Giraldus_Cambrensis" class="mw-redirect" title="Giraldus Cambrensis">Giraldus Cambrensis</a>, a Welsh author:
</p>
<blockquote><p>...Bishops and religious men (<i>viri religiosi</i>) in some parts of Ireland do not scruple to dine off these birds at the time of fasting, because they are not flesh nor born of flesh... But in so doing they are led into sin. For if anyone were to eat of the leg of our first parent (Adam) although he was not born of flesh, that person could not be adjudged innocent of eating meat.<sup id="cite_ref-Heron-Allen_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heron-Allen-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Council_of_the_Lateran" title="Fourth Council of the Lateran">Fourth Council of the Lateran</a> (1215), <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_III" title="Pope Innocent III">Pope Innocent III</a> explicitly prohibited the eating of these geese during Lent, arguing that despite their unusual reproduction, they lived and fed like ducks and so were of the same nature as other birds.<sup id="cite_ref-Lankester2_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lankester2-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The question of the nature of barnacle geese also came up as a matter of Jewish dietary law in the <a href="/wiki/Halakha" title="Halakha">Halakha</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rabbeinu_Tam" title="Rabbeinu Tam">Rabbeinu Tam</a> (1100–71) determined that they were <a href="/wiki/Kosher" class="mw-redirect" title="Kosher">kosher</a> (even if born of trees) and should be slaughtered following <a href="/wiki/Shechita" title="Shechita">the normal prescriptions for birds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Heron-Allen_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heron-Allen-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In one Jewish legend, the barnacle goose is purported to have its beak forever attached to the tree from which it grew just as the <a href="/wiki/Adne_Sadeh" title="Adne Sadeh">Adne Sadeh</a> is fixed to the earth by its navel cord.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The mythical barnacle tree, believed in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> to have barnacles that opened to reveal geese, may have a similar origin to the other legends already mentioned.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
