<p>The <b>Little Stint</b> (<i>Calidris minuta</i>) (or <i>Erolia minuta</i>), is a very small <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a>. It breeds in <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">arctic</a> Europe and Asia, and is a long-distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>, wintering south to Africa and south Asia. It occasionally is a vagrant to North America and to Australia.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Stint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Appearance">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance">Appearance</span></h2>
<p>Its small size, fine dark bill, dark legs and quicker movements distinguish this species from all waders except the other dark-legged <a href="/wiki/Stint" title="Stint">stints</a>. It can be distinguished from these in all plumages by its combination of a fine bill tip, unwebbed toes and long primary projection. The call is a sharp "stit".</p>

<p>The breeding adult has an orange wash to the breast, a white throat and a strong white V on its back. In winter plumage identification is difficult. Juveniles have pale crown stripes and a pinkish breast.</p>
<p>An apparent <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrid</a> between this species and <a href="/wiki/Temminck%27s_Stint" title="Temminck's Stint">Temminck's Stint</a> has been reported from the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Stint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Population dynamics">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Population_dynamics">Population dynamics</span></h2>
<p>The numbers of this species (and of <a href="/wiki/Curlew_Sandpiper" title="Curlew Sandpiper">Curlew Sandpiper</a>) depend on the population of <a href="/wiki/Lemming" title="Lemming">lemmings</a>. In poor lemming years, predatory species such as <a href="/wiki/Skua" title="Skua">skuas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snowy_Owl" title="Snowy Owl">Snowy Owls</a> take Arctic-breeding waders instead.</p>
<p>It is gregarious in winter, sometimes forming large flocks with other <i>Calidris</i> waders, particularly <a href="/wiki/Dunlin" title="Dunlin">Dunlin</a>, on coastal mudflats or the edges of inland pools.</p>
<p>The Little Stint is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Stint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nesting">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Nesting">Nesting</span></h2>
<p>This bird nests on a scrape in bare ground, laying 3–5 eggs. It is polygamous, and male and female may incubate separate clutches.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Stint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Food">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Food">Food</span></h2>
<p>Food is small <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> picked off the mud.</p>
