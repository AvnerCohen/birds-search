<p>The <b>Moustached Warbler</b> (<i>Acrocephalus melanopogon</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">Old World warbler</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Acrocephalus" title="Acrocephalus">Acrocephalus</a></i>. It breeds in southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and southern temperate <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> with a few in north-west <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It is partially <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>. South west European birds are resident, south east European birds winter in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a> breeding range, and the Asiatic race migrates to <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>.</p>
<p>It is scarce north of its range, but has occurred as a very rare vagrant as far as <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>. There are a few reports from <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, including a pair breeding in <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a> in 1946, but these records have recently been removed from the official list of British birds.</p>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> is a species found in upright aquatic vegetation such as reeds and <a href="/wiki/Cyperaceae" title="Cyperaceae">sedge</a>. 3-6 <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> are laid from mid-April and incubated for 14–15 days. The nest is built over water among reeds or rushes or in a bush. This species is usually <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" title="Monogamous" class="mw-redirect">monogamous</a> (Leisler &amp; Wink 2000).</p>
<p>This is a medium-sized warbler, 12-13.5&#160;cm long, slightly larger than the similar <a href="/wiki/Sedge_Warbler" title="Sedge Warbler">Sedge Warbler</a>, <i>Acrocephalus schoenobaenus</i>. The adult has a finely streaked brown back and white underparts. The forehead is flattened, there is a prominent whitish supercilium, grey ear coverts, and the bill is strong and pointed.</p>
<p>The sexes are identical, as with most warblers, but young birds are more heavily streaked and have markings on the breast. Like most warblers, it is <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" title="Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect">insectivorous</a> and also feeds on water snails.</p>
<p>The song is fast and similar to the Sedge Warbler and <a href="/wiki/Reed_Warbler" title="Reed Warbler" class="mw-redirect">Reed Warbler</a>, with some mimicry and typically acrocephaline whistles added. Its song is softer and more melodious than those of its relatives, and includes phrases reminiscent of the <a href="/wiki/Nightingale" title="Nightingale" class="mw-redirect">Nightingale</a>. Unlike the Sedge Warbler, it does not sing in flight.</p>
