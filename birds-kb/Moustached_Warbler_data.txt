<p>The <b>moustached warbler</b> (<i>Acrocephalus melanopogon</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">Old World warbler</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Acrocephalus_(bird)" title="Acrocephalus (bird)">Acrocephalus</a></i>. It breeds in southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">temperate Asia</a> with a few in north-west <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It is partially <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>. South west European birds are resident, south east European birds winter in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> breeding range, and the Asiatic race migrates to <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabia</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>.
</p><p>It is scarce north of its range, but has occurred as a very rare vagrant as far as <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>. There have been a few reports from <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, including a pair breeding in <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a> in 1946, but these records have recently been removed from the official list of British birds, being unconvincingly distinguished from sedge warblers or <a href="/wiki/Paddyfield_warbler" title="Paddyfield warbler">paddyfield warblers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> is a species found in upright aquatic vegetation such as reeds and <a href="/wiki/Cyperaceae" title="Cyperaceae">sedge</a>. 3 to 6 <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> are laid from mid-April and incubated for 14 to 15 days. The nest is built over water among reeds or rushes or in a bush. This species is usually <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" class="mw-redirect" title="Monogamous">monogamous</a> (Leisler &amp; Wink 2000).
</p><p>This is a medium-sized warbler, 12&#160;to 13.5 centimetres (4.7–5.3&#160;in) long, slightly larger than the similar <a href="/wiki/Sedge_warbler" title="Sedge warbler">sedge warbler</a>, <i>Acrocephalus schoenobaenus</i>. The adult has a finely streaked brown back and white underparts. The forehead is flattened, there is a prominent whitish supercilium, grey ear coverts, and the bill is strong and pointed.
</p><p>The sexes are identical, as with most warblers, but young birds are more heavily streaked and have markings on the breast. Like most warblers, it is <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a> and also feeds on water snails.
</p><p>The song is fast and similar to the sedge warbler and <a href="/wiki/Reed_warbler" class="mw-redirect" title="Reed warbler">reed warbler</a>, with some mimicry and typically acrocephaline whistles added. Its song is softer and more melodious than those of its relatives, and includes phrases reminiscent of the <a href="/wiki/Nightingale" class="mw-redirect" title="Nightingale">nightingale</a>. Unlike the sedge warbler, it does not sing in flight.
</p>
