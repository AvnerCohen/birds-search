<p>The <b>European Goldfinch</b> or <b>Goldfinch</b> (<i>Carduelis carduelis</i>), is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird in the <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finch</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Goldfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Habitat and range">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_range">Habitat and range</span></h2>
<p>The goldfinch breeds across <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, and western and central <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, in open, partially wooded lowlands. It is resident in the milder west of its range, but <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrates</a> from colder regions. It will also make local movements, even in the west, to escape bad weather. It has been introduced to many areas of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Goldfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The average Goldfinch is 12–13&#160;cm long with a wingspan of 21–25&#160;cm and a weight of 14 to 19&#160;grams. The sexes are broadly similar, with a red face, black and white head, warm brown upperparts, white underparts with buff flanks and breast patches, and black and yellow wings. On closer inspection male Goldfinches can often be distinguished by a larger, darker red mask that extends just behind the eye. In females, the red face does not reach the eye. The ivory-coloured bill is long and pointed, and the tail is forked. Goldfinches in breeding condition have a white bill, with a greyish or blackish mark at the tip for the rest of the year. Juveniles have a plain head and a greyer back but are unmistakable due to the yellow wing stripe. Birds in central Asia (<i>caniceps</i> group) have a plain grey head behind the red face, lacking the black and white head pattern of European and western Asian birds.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The species is divided into two major groups, each comprising several races. The two groups intergrade at their boundary, so the <i>caniceps</i> group is not recognised as a distinct species despite its readily distinguishable plumage.</p>
<dl>
<dt><i>Carduelis carduelis carduelis</i> group.</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>Carduelis carduelis balcanica</i>. Southeastern European</li>
<li><i>Carduelis carduelis brevirostris</i>. Crimea, north Caucasus.</li>
<li><i>Carduelis carduelis britannica</i>. British Isles.</li>
<li><i>Carduelis carduelis carduelis</i>. Most of European mainland, Scandinavia.</li>
<li><i>Carduelis carduelis loudoni</i>. South Caucasus, Iran.</li>
<li><i>Carduelis carduelis major</i>. Western Siberia.</li>
<li><i>Carduelis carduelis niediecki</i>. Southwest Asia, northeast Africa.</li>
<li><i>Carduelis carduelis parva</i>. Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Macaronesia" title="Macaronesia">Macaronesic</a> Islands (<a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary I.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>), Iberia<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from February 2010">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, northwest Africa.</li>
<li><i>Carduelis carduelis tschusii</i>. Corsica, Sardinia, Sicily.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dt><i>Carduelis carduelis caniceps</i> group.</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>Carduelis carduelis caniceps</i>. Southern central Asia.</li>
<li><i>Carduelis carduelis paropanisi</i>. Afghanistan to western Himalaya and Tien Shan.</li>
<li><i>Carduelis carduelis subulata</i>. South-central Siberia.</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> classified the bird as <i>Fringilla carduelis</i>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Phylogeny" title="Phylogeny" class="mw-redirect">phylogeny</a> has been obtained by Antonio Arnaiz-Villena et al.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Goldfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>

<p>The goldfinch's preferred food is small seeds such as those from <a href="/wiki/Thistle" title="Thistle">thistles</a> (the Latin name is from <i><a href="/wiki/Carduus" title="Carduus">Carduus</a></i>, a genus of thistles) and <a href="/wiki/Dipsacus" title="Dipsacus">teasels</a>, but insects are also taken when feeding young. It also regularly visits bird feeders in winter. Goldfinches nest in the outer twigs of tall leafy trees, or even in bamboo, laying four to six <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>, which hatch in 11–14 days.</p>
<p>In the winter goldfinches group together to form flocks of up to forty, occasionally more.</p>
<p>The song is a pleasant silvery twittering. The call is a melodic <i>tickeLIT</i>, and the song is a pleasant tinkling medley of trills and twitters, but always including the trisyllabic call phrase or a <i>teLLIT-teLLIT-teLLIT</i>.</p>
<p>In earlier times, the Goldfinch was kept as a cagebird for its song. Escapes from captivity and deliberate releases have colonised southeastern <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Carduelis_carduelis_-United_Kingdom_-two_at_garden_bird_feeder-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Carduelis_carduelis_-United_Kingdom_-two_at_garden_bird_feeder-8.jpg/220px-Carduelis_carduelis_-United_Kingdom_-two_at_garden_bird_feeder-8.jpg" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Carduelis_carduelis_-United_Kingdom_-two_at_garden_bird_feeder-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>Goldfinches are attracted to <a href="/wiki/Back_garden" title="Back garden">back gardens</a> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> by <a href="/wiki/Birdfeeder" title="Birdfeeder" class="mw-redirect">birdfeeders</a> containing <a href="/wiki/Nyjer" title="Nyjer" class="mw-redirect">niger</a> (commercially described as nyjer) seed. This seed of an annual from <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> is small, and high in oils. Special <a href="/wiki/Polycarbonate" title="Polycarbonate">polycarbonate</a> feeders with small oval slits at which the Goldfinches feed are sometimes used.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Goldfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relationships with humans">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships_with_humans">Relationships with humans</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Carduelis_caniceps_F.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Carduelis_caniceps_F.JPG/220px-Carduelis_caniceps_F.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Carduelis_caniceps_F.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Captive <i>Carduelis carduelis caniceps</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Goldfinches are commonly kept and bred in captivity around the world because of their distinctive appearance and pleasant song. The Goldfinch males are sometimes crossed with Canary females with the intention to produce male mules with beautiful singing voices, that often capture the best singing attributes of both breeds.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Raffael_031.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Raffael_031.jpg/170px-Raffael_031.jpg" width="170" height="244" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Raffael_031.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>Madonna of the Goldfinch</i> by Raphael, (c. 1505-1506)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Because of the thistle seeds it eats, in Christian symbolism the Goldfinch is associated with the Passion and <a href="/wiki/Christ" title="Christ">Christ</a>'s Crown of Thorns. The Goldfinch, appearing in pictures of the Madonna and the Christ Child, represents the foreknowledge Jesus and Mary had of the Crucifixion. Examples include the <i><a href="/wiki/Madonna_del_cardellino" title="Madonna del cardellino">Madonna del cardellino</a></i> or <i>Madonna of the Goldfinch</i>, painted (c. 1505-1506) by the Italian renaissance artist <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">Raphael</a>, in which <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a> offers the goldfinch to Christ in warning of his future. In <a href="/wiki/Federico_Barocci" title="Federico Barocci">Barocci</a>'s <i>Holy Family</i> a goldfinch is held in the hand of <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a> who holds it high out of reach of an interested cat. In <a href="/wiki/Cima_da_Conegliano" title="Cima da Conegliano">Cima da Conegliano</a>'s <i>Madonna and Child</i>, a goldfinch flutters in the hand of the Christ Child. It is also an emblem of endurance, fruitfulness, and persistence. Because it symbolizes the Passion, the goldfinch is considered a "saviour" bird and may be pictured with the common fly (which represents sin and disease).<sup id="cite_ref-Werness_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Werness-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The Goldfinch is also associated with <a href="/wiki/Saint_Jerome" title="Saint Jerome" class="mw-redirect">Saint Jerome</a> and appears in some of his depictions.<sup id="cite_ref-Werness_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Werness-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Antonio_Vivaldi" title="Antonio Vivaldi">Antonio Vivaldi</a> composed a Concerto in D major for Flute "Il Gardellino" (RV&#160;428,&#160;Op.&#160;10&#160;No.&#160;3), where the singing of the Goldfinch is imitated by a flute.</p>
<p>Goldfinches, with their "wanton freak" and "yellow flutterings", are among the many natural "luxuries" that delight the speaker of <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">John Keats</a>' 'I stood tip-toe upon a little hill...' (1816).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the much admired poem <i>The Great Hunger</i> by <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Kavanagh" title="Patrick Kavanagh">Patrick Kavanagh</a> the goldfinch is one of the rare glimpses of beauty in the life of an elderly Irish farmer-</p>
<p><i>a man might imagine.... these birds the birds of paradise</i>.</p>
