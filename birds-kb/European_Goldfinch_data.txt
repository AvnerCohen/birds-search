<p>The <b>European goldfinch</b> or simply the <b>goldfinch</b> (<i>Carduelis carduelis</i>), is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird in the <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finch</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> that is native to Europe, North Africa and <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">western and central Asia</a>. It has been introduced to other areas, including Australia, New Zealand and Uruguay.
</p><p>The breeding male has a red face and a black-and-white head. The back and flanks are buff or chestnut brown. The black wings have a broad yellow bar. The tail is black and the rump is white. Males and females are very similar, but females have a slightly smaller red area on the face.
</p><p>The goldfinch is often depicted in <a href="/wiki/Italian_renaissance" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian renaissance">Italian renaissance</a> paintings of the <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(art)" title="Madonna (art)">Madonna and Child</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_goldfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European goldfinch was one of the birds described and illustrated by Swiss naturalist <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Gessner" title="Conrad Gessner">Conrad Gessner</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Historiae_animalium_(Gessner)" class="mw-redirect" title="Historiae animalium (Gessner)">Historiae animalium</a></i> of 1555.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The first <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">formal description</a> was by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> published in 1758. He introduced the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a>, <i>Fringilla carduelis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-checklist_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-checklist-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  <i>Carduelis</i> is the Latin word for 'goldfinch'.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The European goldfinch is now placed in the genus <i>Carduelis</i> that was introduced by the French zoologist <a href="/wiki/Mathurin_Jacques_Brisson" title="Mathurin Jacques Brisson">Mathurin Jacques Brisson</a> in 1760 by <a href="/wiki/Tautonym" title="Tautonym">tautonomy</a> based on Linneus's <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific epithet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Modern molecular genetic studies have shown that the European goldfinch is closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Citril_finch" title="Citril finch">citril finch</a> (<i>Carduelis citrinella</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Corsican_finch" title="Corsican finch">Corsican finch</a> (<i>Carduelis corsicana</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The English word 'goldfinch' was used in the second half of the 14th century by <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Geoffrey Chaucer</a> in his unfinished <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cook%27s_Tale" title="The Cook&#39;s Tale">The Cook's Tale</a></i>: "Gaillard he was as goldfynch in the shawe (Gaily dressed he was as is a goldfinch in the woods)".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_goldfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of the European goldfinch are divided into two major groups. These <a href="/wiki/Intergrade" class="mw-redirect" title="Intergrade">intergrade</a> at their boundary, so the groups are not recognized as distinct species despite their readily distinguishable plumage. Subspecies in the <i>carduelis</i> group occupy the western part of the range and have black crowns; subspecies in the <i>caniceps</i> group occupy the eastern part of the range and have grey heads.<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbwgoldfinch_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwgoldfinch-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phylogeny">Phylogeny</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_goldfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Phylogeny">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European goldfinch originated in the late <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a>-<a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a> and belongs to the clade of <a href="/wiki/Carduelinae" title="Carduelinae">cardueline</a> finches. The <a href="/wiki/Citril_finch" title="Citril finch">citril finch</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Corsican_finch" title="Corsican finch">Corsican finch</a> are its sister <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxa</a>. Their closest relatives are the <a href="/wiki/Greenfinches" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenfinches">greenfinches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crossbill" title="Crossbill">crossbills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Redpoll" title="Redpoll">redpolls</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The monophyly of the subfamily <a href="/wiki/Carduelinae" title="Carduelinae">Carduelinae</a> is suggested in previous studies.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_goldfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:European_goldfinch_(Carduelis_carduelis_parva).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/European_goldfinch_%28Carduelis_carduelis_parva%29.jpg/220px-European_goldfinch_%28Carduelis_carduelis_parva%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/European_goldfinch_%28Carduelis_carduelis_parva%29.jpg/330px-European_goldfinch_%28Carduelis_carduelis_parva%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/European_goldfinch_%28Carduelis_carduelis_parva%29.jpg/440px-European_goldfinch_%28Carduelis_carduelis_parva%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2728" data-file-height="2728" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:European_goldfinch_(Carduelis_carduelis_parva).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>C. c. parva</i>, <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a></div></div></div>
<p>The average European goldfinch is 12–13&#160;cm (4.7–5.1&#160;in) long with a wingspan of 21–25&#160;cm (8.3–9.8&#160;in) and a weight of 14 to 19&#160;g (0.49 to 0.67&#160;oz). The sexes are broadly similar, with a red face, black and white head, warm brown upper parts, white underparts with buff flanks and breast patches, and black and yellow wings.
</p><p>On closer inspection, male European goldfinches can often be distinguished by a larger, darker red mask that extends just behind the eye. The shoulder feathers are black, whereas they are brown on the female. In females, the red face does not extend past the eye. The ivory-coloured bill is long and pointed, and the tail is forked. Goldfinches in breeding condition have a white bill, with a greyish or blackish mark at the tip for the rest of the year. Juveniles have a plain head and a greyer back but are unmistakable due to the yellow wing stripe. Birds in central Asia (the <i>caniceps</i> group) have a plain grey head behind the red face, lacking the black and white head pattern of European and western Asian birds.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Adults moult after the breeding season, with some individuals beginning in July and others not completing their moult until November. After moult birds appear less colourful, until the tips of the newly grown feathers wear away.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The song is a pleasant silvery twittering. The call is a melodic <i>tickeLIT</i>, and the song is a pleasant tinkling medley of trills and twitters, but always including the tri-syllabic call phrase or a <i>teLLIT-teLLIT-teLLIT</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_goldfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European goldfinch is native to <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">western and central Asia</a>. It is found in open, partially wooded lowlands and is a resident in the milder west of its range, but <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrates</a> from colder regions. It will also make local movements, even in the west, to escape bad weather. 
It has been introduced to many areas of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
It was introduced to <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> in the 19th century, and their populations quickly increased and their range expanded greatly. In Australia, They now occur from <a href="/wiki/Brisbane" title="Brisbane">Brisbane</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Eyre_Peninsula" title="Eyre Peninsula">Eyre Peninsula</a>, and are also spread throughout New Zealand.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_goldfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gold_Finch_eggs.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Gold_Finch_eggs.jpg/220px-Gold_Finch_eggs.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Gold_Finch_eggs.jpg/330px-Gold_Finch_eggs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Gold_Finch_eggs.jpg/440px-Gold_Finch_eggs.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1333" data-file-height="1000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gold_Finch_eggs.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A European goldfinch nest and eggs</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_goldfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The nest is built entirely by the female and is generally completed within a week. The male accompanies the female, but does not contribute.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994582-583_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994582-583-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The nest is neat and compact and is generally located several meters above the ground, hidden by leaves in the twigs at the end of a swaying branch.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton197237_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton197237-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> It is constructed of mosses and lichens and lined with plant down such as that from thistles. It is attached to the twigs of the tree with spider silk. A deep cup prevents the loss of eggs in windy weather.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton1972175_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton1972175-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Beginning within a couple of days after the completion of the nest, the eggs are laid in early morning at daily intervals.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994582-583_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994582-583-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is typically 4-6 eggs, which are whitish with reddish-brown speckles.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton197237_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton197237-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> They have a smooth surface and are slightly glossy.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994582-583_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994582-583-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The average size is 17.3&#160;mm ×&#160;13.0&#160;mm (0.68&#160;in ×&#160;0.51&#160;in) with a calculated weight of 1.53&#160;g (0.054&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994582-583_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994582-583-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The eggs are incubated for 11–13 days by the female, who is fed by the male. The chicks are fed by both parents. Initially they receive a mixture of seeds and insects, but as they grow the proportion of insect material decreases.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton1972178_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton1972178-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> For the first 7–9 days the young are brooded by the female. The nestlings fledge 13–18 days after hatching. The young birds are fed by both parents for a further 7–9 days. The parents typically raise two broods each year and occasionally three.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994582-583_20-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994582-583-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_goldfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The European goldfinch's preferred food is small seeds such as those from <a href="/wiki/Thistle" title="Thistle">thistles</a> (the Latin name is from <i><a href="/wiki/Carduus" title="Carduus">Carduus</a></i>, a genus of thistles) and <a href="/wiki/Dipsacus" title="Dipsacus">teasels</a>, but insects are also taken when feeding young. It also regularly visits bird feeders in winter. In the winter, European goldfinches group together to form flocks of up to 40, occasionally more. European goldfinches are attracted to <a href="/wiki/Back_garden" title="Back garden">back gardens</a> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> by <a href="/wiki/Birdfeeder" class="mw-redirect" title="Birdfeeder">birdfeeders</a> containing <a href="/wiki/Nyjer" class="mw-redirect" title="Nyjer">niger</a> (commercially described as nyjer) seed.  This seed of an annual from Africa is small, and high in oils.  Special <a href="/wiki/Polycarbonate" title="Polycarbonate">polycarbonate</a> feeders with small oval slits at which the European goldfinches feed are sometimes used. They adore beige sunflower hearts seeds.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships_with_humans">Relationships with humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_goldfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Relationships with humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fabritius-vink.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Fabritius-vink.jpg/170px-Fabritius-vink.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="260" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Fabritius-vink.jpg/255px-Fabritius-vink.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Fabritius-vink.jpg/340px-Fabritius-vink.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1965" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fabritius-vink.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/The_Goldfinch_(painting)" title="The Goldfinch (painting)">The Goldfinch</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Carel_Fabritius" title="Carel Fabritius">Carel Fabritius</a>, 1654</div></div></div>
<p>European goldfinches are commonly kept and bred in captivity around the world because of their distinctive appearance and pleasant song. If European goldfinches are kept with <a href="/wiki/Domestic_canary" title="Domestic canary">domestic canaries</a>, they tend to lose their native song and call in favour of their cagemates' songs. This is considered undesirable, as it detracts from the allure of keeping European goldfinches. In Great Britain during the 19th century, many thousands of European goldfinches were trapped each year to be sold as cage birds. One of the earliest campaigns of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Protection_of_Birds" title="Royal Society for the Protection of Birds">Royal Society for the Protection of Birds</a> was directed against this trade.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton197236-37_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton197236-37-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_conservation" title="Wildlife conservation">Wildlife conservation</a> attempts to limit <a href="/wiki/Bird_trapping" title="Bird trapping">bird trapping</a> and the destruction of the open space habitats of European goldfinches.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Steglitz" title="Steglitz">Steglitz</a>, a borough of the German city of <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> is named after the European goldfinch.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The surname Goldspink is based on the <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a> word for the European goldfinch.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_symbolism">Christian symbolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_goldfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Christian symbolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because of the thistle seeds it eats, in Christian symbolism the European goldfinch is associated with <a href="/wiki/Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Christ">Christ</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Passion_(Christianity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Passion (Christianity)">Passion</a> and his <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_thorns" title="Crown of thorns">crown of thorns</a>. The European goldfinch, appearing in pictures of the <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(art)" title="Madonna (art)">Madonna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Child_Jesus" class="mw-redirect" title="Child Jesus">Christ child</a>, represents the foreknowledge Jesus and Mary had of the <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion_of_Jesus" title="Crucifixion of Jesus">Crucifixion</a>.  Examples include the <i><a href="/wiki/Madonna_del_cardellino" title="Madonna del cardellino">Madonna del cardellino</a></i> or <i>Madonna of the Goldfinch</i>, painted by the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance_art" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian Renaissance art">Italian Renaissance</a> artist <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">Raphael</a> in about 1505–6, in which <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a> offers a European goldfinch to Christ in a warning of his future. In <a href="/wiki/Federico_Barocci" title="Federico Barocci">Barocci</a>'s <i>Holy Family</i>, a European goldfinch is held in the hand of John the Baptist, who holds it high out of reach of an interested <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cat</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Cima_da_Conegliano" title="Cima da Conegliano">Cima da Conegliano</a>'s <i>Madonna and Child</i>, a European goldfinch flutters in the hand of the Christ child. It is also an emblem of endurance, fruitfulness, and persistence. Because it symbolizes the Passion, the European goldfinch is considered a "saviour" bird and may be pictured with the <a href="/wiki/Housefly" title="Housefly">common housefly</a> (which represents sin and disease).<sup id="cite_ref-Werness_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Werness-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The European goldfinch is also associated with <a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">Saint Jerome</a> and appears in some depictions of him.<sup id="cite_ref-Werness_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Werness-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Depictions_in_art">Depictions in art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_goldfinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Depictions in art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Antonio_Vivaldi" title="Antonio Vivaldi">Antonio Vivaldi</a> composed a Concerto in D major for Flute "Il Gardellino" (RV&#160;428,&#160;Op.&#160;10&#160;No.&#160;3), where the singing of the European goldfinch is imitated by a flute. An anonymous <a href="/wiki/Neapolitan_language" title="Neapolitan language">Italian Neapolitan</a> poem titled <i>Il Cardellino</i><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> was put to music by <a href="/wiki/Saverio_Mercadante" title="Saverio Mercadante">Saverio Mercadante</a> and sung by <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Carreras" title="José Carreras">Jose Carreras</a>.
</p><p>European goldfinches, with their "wanton freak" and "yellow flutterings", are among the many natural "luxuries" that delight the speaker of <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">John Keats</a>' poem 'I stood tip-toe upon a little hill...' (1816).<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the poem <i>The Great Hunger</i> by <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Kavanagh" title="Patrick Kavanagh">Patrick Kavanagh</a>, the European goldfinch is one of the rare glimpses of beauty in the life of an elderly Irish farmer:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r960796168">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><div class="poem">
<p>The goldfinches on the railway paling were worth looking at<br />
A man might imagine then<br />
Himself in Brazil and these birds the birds of paradise
</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Donna_Tartt" title="Donna Tartt">Donna Tartt</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Goldfinch_(novel)" title="The Goldfinch (novel)">The Goldfinch</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> won the 2014 <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize_for_Fiction" title="Pulitzer Prize for Fiction">Pulitzer Prize for Fiction</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> A turning point in the plot occurs when the narrator, Theo, sees his mother's favourite painting, <a href="/wiki/Carel_Fabritius" title="Carel Fabritius">Carel Fabritius</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Goldfinch_(painting)" title="The Goldfinch (painting)">The Goldfinch</a></i>, in the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a>.
</p>
