<p>The <b>yellow-billed stork</b> (<i>Mycteria ibis</i>), sometimes also called the <b>wood stork</b> or <b>wood <a href="/wiki/Ibis" title="Ibis">ibis</a></b>, is a large <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a> wading <a href="/wiki/Stork" title="Stork">stork</a> species in the family <a href="/wiki/Ciconiidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Ciconiidae">Ciconiidae</a>. It is widespread in regions south of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> and also occurs in <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_evolution">Taxonomy and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-billed_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The yellow-billed stork is closely related to 3 other species in the genus <i>Mycteria</i>: the <a href="/wiki/Wood_stork" title="Wood stork">American woodstork</a> (<i>Mycteria americana</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Milky_stork" title="Milky stork">milky stork</a> (<i>Mycteria cinerea</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Painted_stork" title="Painted stork">painted stork</a> (<i>Mycteria leucocephala</i>).  It is classified as belonging to one <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> with these 3 other species<sup id="cite_ref-Peters_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peters-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  because they all display remarkable <a href="/wiki/Homologies" class="mw-redirect" title="Homologies">homologies</a> in <a href="/wiki/Behavior" title="Behavior">behavior</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In one analytical study of feeding and <a href="/wiki/Courtship" title="Courtship">courtship</a> behaviours of the wood-stork family, M.P. Kahl attributed the same general <a href="/wiki/Ethology" title="Ethology">ethology</a> to all members of the genus <i>Mycteria</i>, with few species-specific variations.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> These four species are collectively referred to as the wood-storks, which should not be confused with one alternative common name (wood-stork) for the yellow-billed stork.
</p><p>Before it was established that the yellow-billed stork was closely related to the American woodstork, the former was classified as belonging to the genus <i>Ibis</i>, together with the milky stork and painted stork.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> However, the yellow-billed stork has actually long been recognised as a true stork<sup id="cite_ref-Garrod1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Garrod1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Garrod2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Garrod2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and along with the other 3 related stork species, it should not strictly be called an <a href="/wiki/Ibis" title="Ibis">ibis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-billed_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is a medium-sized stork standing 90–105&#160;cm (35–41&#160;in) tall.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The body is white with a short black tail that is glossed green and purple when freshly <a href="/wiki/Moulted" class="mw-redirect" title="Moulted">moulted</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> is deep yellow, slightly decurved at the end and has a rounder cross-section than in other stork species outside the <i>Mycteria</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Feathers extend onto the head and neck just behind the eyes, with the face and forehead being covered by deep red skin. Both sexes are similar in appearance, but the male is larger and has a slightly longer heavier bill.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Males and females weigh approximately 2.3&#160;kg (5.1&#160;lb) and 1.9&#160;kg (4.2&#160;lb) respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Animal_colouration" class="mw-redirect" title="Animal colouration">Colouration</a> becomes more vivid during the breeding season. In the breeding season, the <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> is coloured pink on the <a href="/wiki/Covert_feather" title="Covert feather">upperwings</a> and back;<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> the ordinarily brown legs also turn bright pink; the bill becomes a deeper yellow and the face becomes a deeper red.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Juvenile_(organism)" title="Juvenile (organism)">Juveniles</a> are greyish-brown with a dull, partially bare, orange face and a dull yellowish bill.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The legs and feet are brown and feathers all over the body are blackish-brown. At <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledging</a>, salmon-pink colouration in the <a href="/wiki/Covert_feather" title="Covert feather">underwings</a> begins to develop and after about one year, the plumage is greyish-white. <a href="/wiki/Flight_feathers" class="mw-redirect" title="Flight feathers">Flight feathers</a> on the tail and wing also become black. Later, the pink colouration typical of adult plumage begins to appear.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>These storks walk with a high-stepped stalking <a href="/wiki/Gait" title="Gait">gait</a><sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> on the ground of shallow water and their approximate walking rate has been recorded as 70 steps per minute.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> They fly with alternating flaps and glides, with the speed of their flaps averaging 177–205 beats per minute.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> They usually flap only for short journeys and often fly in a soaring and gliding motion over several kilometres for <a href="/wiki/Animal_locomotion" title="Animal locomotion">locomotion</a> between breeding <a href="/wiki/Colonies" class="mw-redirect" title="Colonies">colonies</a> or roosts and feeding sites.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> By soaring on <a href="/wiki/Thermals" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermals">thermals</a> and gliding by turns, they can cover large distances without wasting much energy.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> On descending from high <a href="/wiki/Altitudes" class="mw-redirect" title="Altitudes">altitudes</a>, this stork has been observed to dive deeply at high speeds and flip over and over from side to side,<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> hence showing impressive <a href="/wiki/Aerobatics" title="Aerobatics">aerobatics</a>. It even appears to enjoy these aerial stunts.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species is generally non-vocal, but utters hissing <a href="/wiki/Falsetto" title="Falsetto">falsetto</a> screams during social displays in the breeding season.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> These storks also engage in bill clattering and an audible “woofing” wing beat at breeding <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonies</a><sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Nestlings make a loud continual monotonous <a href="/wiki/Braying" class="mw-redirect" title="Braying">braying</a> call to beg parental adults for food.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-billed_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The yellow-billed stork occurs primarily in Eastern Africa,<sup id="cite_ref-Kasoma_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kasoma-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> but is widely distributed in areas extending from <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a> down to <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a><sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and in some regions of western Madagascar.<sup id="cite_ref-Rand_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rand-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> During one observation of a mixed species bird colony on the <a href="/wiki/Tana_River_(Kenya)" title="Tana River (Kenya)">Tana River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, it was found to be the commonest species there, with 2000 <a href="/wiki/Individuals" class="mw-redirect" title="Individuals">individuals</a> being counted at once.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It does not generally <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> far, at least not out of its breeding range; but usually makes short migratory movements which are influenced by rainfall. It makes local movements in Kenya and has also been found to migrate from North to South <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> with the rainy season.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It may also migrate regularly to and from South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> However, little is actually known about this bird's general migratory movements. Due to apparent observed variation in migratory patterns throughout Africa, the yellow-billed stork has been termed a facultative nomad.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It may migrate simply to avoid areas where water or rainfall conditions are too high or too low for feeding on prey.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Some populations migrate considerable distances between feeding or breeding sites; usually by using thermals to soar and glide.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Other local populations have been found to be <a href="/wiki/Sedentism" title="Sedentism">sedentary</a> and remain in their respective <a href="/wiki/Habitats" class="mw-redirect" title="Habitats">habitats</a> all year round.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Its preferred habitats include <a href="/wiki/Wetlands" class="mw-redirect" title="Wetlands">wetlands</a>, shallow lakes and <a href="/wiki/Mudflats" class="mw-redirect" title="Mudflats">mudflats</a>, usually 10–40&#160;cm deep<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> but it usually avoids heavily forested regions in central Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It also avoids flooded regions and deep expansive bodies of water because feeding conditions there are unsuitable for their typical grope and stir feeding techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species breeds especially in <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>. Although it is known to breed in <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>, breeding sites have not been recorded there.<sup id="cite_ref-Kasoma_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kasoma-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It has been found to breed also in Malakol in Sudan<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and often inside walled cities in West Africa from <a href="/wiki/Gambia" class="mw-redirect" title="Gambia">Gambia</a> down to northern <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bannerman_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bannerman-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Still other breeding sites include <a href="/wiki/KwaZulu-Natal" title="KwaZulu-Natal">Zululand</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a><sup id="cite_ref-Clancey_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clancey-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and northern Botswana,<sup id="cite_ref-Fraser_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fraser-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> but are rarer below northern <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a> where sites are well-watered.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Although there is no direct evidence of current breeding in <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, young birds unable to fly have been observed near <a href="/wiki/Lake_Kinkony" title="Lake Kinkony">Lake Kinkony</a> during October.<sup id="cite_ref-Langrand_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Langrand-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-billed_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_feeding">Food and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-billed_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Food and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Their diet comprises mainly small, freshwater <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> of about 60-100mm length<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and maximally 150g,<sup id="cite_ref-Bell-Cross_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bell-Cross-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> which they swallow whole. They also feed on <a href="/wiki/Crustaceans" class="mw-redirect" title="Crustaceans">crustaceans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worm" title="Worm">worms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_insects" class="mw-redirect" title="Aquatic insects">aquatic insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frogs" class="mw-redirect" title="Frogs">frogs</a> and occasionally small <a href="/wiki/Mammals" class="mw-redirect" title="Mammals">mammals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Birds" class="mw-redirect" title="Birds">birds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species appears to rely mainly on sense of touch to detect and capture <a href="/wiki/Prey" class="mw-redirect" title="Prey">prey</a>, rather than by <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">vision</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> They feed patiently by walking through the water with partially open bills and probe the water for prey. Contact of the bill with a prey item is followed by a rapid snap-bill <a href="/wiki/Reflex" title="Reflex">reflex</a>, whereby the bird snaps shut its <a href="/wiki/Mandibles" class="mw-redirect" title="Mandibles">mandibles</a>, raises its head and swallows the prey whole.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The speed of this reflex in the closely related <a href="/wiki/Wood_stork" title="Wood stork">American woodstork</a> (<i>Mycteria americana</i>) has been recorded as 25 milliseconds<sup id="cite_ref-Peacock_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peacock-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and although the corresponding reflex in the yellow-billed stork has not been quantitatively measured,<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> the yellow-billed stork's feeding mechanism appears to be at least qualitatively identical to that of the American woodstork.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to the snap-bill reflex, the yellow-billed stork also uses a systematic foot stirring technique to sound out evasive prey.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It prods and churns up the bottom of the water as part of a “herding mechanism”<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> to force prey out of the bottom vegetation and into the bird's bill. The bird does this several times with one foot before bringing it forwards and repeating with the other foot.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Although they are normally active <a href="/wiki/Predator" class="mw-redirect" title="Predator">predators</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Kasoma_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kasoma-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> they have also been observed to scavenge fish regurgitated by <a href="/wiki/Cormorants" class="mw-redirect" title="Cormorants">cormorants</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kasoma_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kasoma-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The yellow-billed stork has been observed to follow moving <a href="/wiki/Crocodiles" class="mw-redirect" title="Crocodiles">crocodiles</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hippopotami" class="mw-redirect" title="Hippopotami">hippopotami</a> through the water and feed behind them, appearing to take advantage of organisms churned up by their quarry.<sup id="cite_ref-Pooley_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pooley-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Feeding lasts for only a short time before the bird obtains its requirements and proceeds to rest again.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Parents feed their young by regurgitating fish onto the nest floor, whereupon it is picked up and consumed by the <a href="/wiki/Nestlings" class="mw-redirect" title="Nestlings">nestlings</a>.  The young eat voraciously and an individual nestling increases its body weight from 50 grams to 600 grams during the first ten days of its life.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Hence, this species has earned the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> colloquial common name “Nimmersatt”;<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> meaning “never full”.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding_behaviour">Breeding behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-billed_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Breeding is seasonal and appears to be stimulated by the peak of long heavy rainfall<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl2_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl2-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BrownBritton_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BrownBritton-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and resultant flooding of shallow <a href="/wiki/Marshes" class="mw-redirect" title="Marshes">marshes</a>, usually near Lake Victoria.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl2_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl2-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> This flooding is linked to an increase in prey fish availability;<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl2_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl2-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and reproduction is therefore synchronised with this peak in food availability. In such observations near <a href="/wiki/Kisumu" title="Kisumu">Kisumu</a>, M.P. Kahl's explanation for this trend was that in the dry season, most prey fish are forced to leave the dried-up, deoxygenated marshes that cannot support them and retreat to the deep waters of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Victoria" title="Lake Victoria">Lake Victoria</a> where the storks cannot reach them.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl2_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl2-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> However, fish move back up the streams on the onset of rain and spread out over the marshes to breed, where they become accessible to the storks. By nesting at this time and providing that the rains do not end pre-maturely, the storks are guaranteed a plentiful food supply for their young.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl2_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl2-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The yellow-billed stork may also begin <a href="/wiki/Nest" title="Nest">nesting</a> and breeding at the end of long rains. This occurs especially on flat extensive marshlands as water levels gradually decrease and concentrate fish sufficiently for the storks to feed on.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> However, unseasonal rainfall has also been reported to induce off-season breeding in northern Botswana<sup id="cite_ref-Tree_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tree-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and western and eastern Kenya. Rainfall may cause local flooding and hence ideal feeding conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This stork appears to breed simply when rainfall and local flooding are optimal and hence seems to be flexible in its temporal breeding pattern, which varies with rainfall pattern throughout the African continent.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As with all stork species, male yellow-billed storks select and occupy potential nest sites in trees, whereupon females attempt to approach the males.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The yellow-billed stork has an extensive repertoire of courtship behaviours near and at the nest that may lead to pair formation and <a href="/wiki/Animal_sexual_behavior" class="mw-redirect" title="Animal sexual behavior">copulation</a>. Generally, these courtship behaviours are also assumed to be common to all <i>Mycteria</i> species and show remarkable homology within the genus <i>Mycteria</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> After the male has initially established at the nesting-site and the female begins to approach, he displays behaviours that advertise himself to her. One of these is the Display Preening, whereby the male pretends to strip down each of his extended wings with the bill several times each side<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and the bill does not effectively close around the feathers. Another observed display among males is the Swaying-Twig Grasping. Here, the male stands on the potential nesting-site and bends over to gently grasp and release underlying twigs at regular intervals.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This is sometimes accompanied by side-to-side oscillations of the neck and head and he continues to pick at twigs in between such movements.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Reciprocally, approaching females display their own distinct behaviours. One such behaviour is the Balancing Posture,<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> whereby she walks with a horizontal body axis and extended wings toward the male occupying the nesting-site. Later, when the female continues to approach or already stands near an established male, she may also engage in Gaping. Here, the bill is gaped open slightly with the neck inclined upward at about 45o .<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and often occurs in conjunction with the Balancing-Posture. This behaviour ordinarily continues if the male accepts the female and has allowed her to enter the nest, but the female usually closes her wings by this time.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The male may also continue his Display-Preening when standing next to the female in the nest<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During copulation, the male steps onto the female's back from the side, hooks his feet over her shoulders, holds out his wings for balance and finally bends his legs to lower himself for <a href="/wiki/Cloacal" class="mw-redirect" title="Cloacal">cloacal</a> contact, as happens in most birds. In turn, the female holds out her wings almost horizontally. The process is accompanied by bill clattering from the male as he regularly opens and closes his mandibles and vigorously shakes his head to beat his bill against the female's.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In turn, the female keeps her bill horizontal with the male's or inclined downward at approximately 45 degrees.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Average copulation time in this species has been calculated as 15.7 seconds.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The male and female build the nest together either in high trees on dry land away from predators, or in small trees over water.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Nest building takes up to 10 days.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The nest may be 80–100&#160;cm in diameter and 20–30&#160;cm thick.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The female typically lays 2-4 <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> (usually 3)<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> on alternate days<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and average <a href="/wiki/Clutch_size" class="mw-redirect" title="Clutch size">clutch size</a> has been recorded as 2.5.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The male and female share duties to <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation" title="Egg incubation">incubate</a> the eggs,  which takes up to 30 days.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> As in many other stork species,<sup id="cite_ref-Schuez_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schuez-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kahl3_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl3-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> hatching is asynchronous (usually at 1- to 2-day intervals),<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> so that the young in the brood differ considerably in body size at any one time.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> During food shortage, the smaller young are at risk of being outcompeted for food by their larger nest-mates.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Both parents share duties of guarding and feeding the young until the latter are about 21 days old.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Thereafter, both parents forage to attend to the young's intense food demands.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Alongside parental feeding by regurgitation of fish, parents have also been observed to regurgitate water into the open bills of their nestlings,<sup id="cite_ref-Stronach_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stronach-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> especially on hot days. This may aid the typical thermoregulatory strategy of the young (common to all stork species)<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> to excrete dilute urine down their legs in response to hot weather.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Water regurgitated over the young serves as a water supplement in addition to fluid in their food, so that they have sufficient water to continue urinating down their legs to avoid hyperventilation.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, parents sometimes help keep the young cool by shading them with their open wings.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The nestlings usually <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> after 50–55 days of hatching<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and fly away from the nest. However, after leaving the nest for the first time, the offspring often return there to be fed by their parents and roost with them for another 1–3 weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is also thought that individuals are not fully adult until 3 years old<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and despite lack of data, new adults are thought to not breed until much later than this.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fledgling_(birds)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fledgling (birds)">Fledglings</a> have also been observed to not differ considerably in their foraging and feeding strategies from adults. In one investigation, four adult, hand-reared yellow-billed storks kept in captivity showed typical grope-feeding and foot stirring shortly after they were introduced to bodies of water.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Hence, this suggests that such feeding techniques in this species are innate.
</p><p>These birds breed colonially, often alongside other species;<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> but the yellow-billed stork is sometimes the only occupant species of a nesting site.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> A subset of up to 20 individuals may nest close together in any one part of a colony;<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> with several males occupying potential nest sites all in the same place.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> If many of these males do not acquire mates, the whole group moves on with the unpaired females to another tree. These “bachelor parties”<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> are a noticeable feature of colonies of this species and usually consist of 12 or more males and at least as many females.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> As many as 50 nests have been counted all at once in a single breeding area.<sup id="cite_ref-Bannerman_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bannerman-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_behaviours">Other behaviours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-billed_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other behaviours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite their <a href="/wiki/Gregariousness" class="mw-redirect" title="Gregariousness">gregariousness</a> during breeding, most individuals generally ignore each other outside nesting-sites;<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> although some hostile encounters may occur. Some of these encounters involve one individual showing an unambiguous attack or escape response if there is a large difference in social status between the two individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> However, if two individuals are equally matched, they slowly approach each other and show a ritualised display called the Forward Threat.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Here, one individual holds its body forward horizontally and retracts the neck so that it touches the crown, with the tail cocked at 45 degrees and all feathers erect.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It approaches the opponent and points its bill at it, sometimes gaping.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> If the opponent does not capitulate, the attacker may grab at it with its bill and the two may briefly spar with their bills until one retreats in an erect stance with compressed plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hostility can also arise between opposite sexes when a female approaches a male on a potential nest site. Both sexes may display a similar aforementioned Forward Threat, but clatter their bills after grabbing with them at the other stork and extend their wings to maintain balance.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Another hostile behaviour between sexes is the Snap Display,<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> whereby they snap horizontally with their bills while standing upright. This may occur during and immediately after pair formation, but subsides later in the breeding cycle as the male and female become familiar with each other and it eventually disappears.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nestlings" class="mw-redirect" title="Nestlings">Nestlings</a> show remarkable behavioural transformations at 3 weeks of age. During the constant parental attendance before this time, the young show little fear or aggression in response to intruders (such as a human observer), but are found to merely crouch low and quietly in the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> After this time, when both parents go foraging and leave the young in the nest, a nestling shows strong fear in response to an intruder. It either attempts to climb out of the nest to escape or acts aggressively toward the intruder.<sup id="cite_ref-Kahl_3-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kahl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats_and_survival">Threats and survival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-billed_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Threats and survival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As well as being abundant and widespread, the yellow-billed stork also appears tolerant of short-term natural habitat changes.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> However, in East Africa, it is known to be at risk from <a href="/wiki/Poaching" title="Poaching">poaching</a> and habitat reduction despite abundance and population stability<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  and is listed under the <i>African-Eurasian Waterbird Agreement</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, the overall population is not currently considered to be threatened with serious decline,<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> especially since breeding success is relatively high.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_4-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In East Africa where it is most abundant, <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">broods</a> of 1-3 per nest have been recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Alongside human activities, natural enemies include <a href="/wiki/Cheetahs" class="mw-redirect" title="Cheetahs">cheetahs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leopards" class="mw-redirect" title="Leopards">leopards</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lions" class="mw-redirect" title="Lions">lions</a>, which all sometimes prey on this species.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Eggs may be also at risk of predation by <a href="/wiki/African_fish_eagles" class="mw-redirect" title="African fish eagles">African fish eagles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Parsons_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parsons-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> At one colony in Kisumu, Kenya, approximately 61% of eggs counted between all nests hatched and 38% were predated by fish eagles.<sup id="cite_ref-Parsons_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parsons-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The success rate of fledglings was only 0.33 young per nest.<sup id="cite_ref-Parsons_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parsons-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> However, increased <a href="/wiki/Egg_predation" title="Egg predation">egg predation</a> by fish eagles has been reported to be linked to decline in fish stocks in the <a href="/wiki/Winam_Gulf" title="Winam Gulf">Winam Gulf</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Parsons_27-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parsons-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture_or_relationships_to_humans">In culture or relationships to humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-billed_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In culture or relationships to humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The yellow-billed stork has appeared on postage stamps in several African countries.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow-billed_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species is evaluated as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> for several reasons. First, population trend appears to be decreasing<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> but this decrease is not believed to rapidly approach thresholds for <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">Vulnerable</a> under the population trend criterion.<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Its range is also very large and does not approach threshold for Vulnerable under range size criterion.<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Finally, although there have been no official population size estimates,<sup id="cite_ref-Kasoma_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kasoma-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> the population is known to be very large and so does not approach thresholds for Vulnerable under population size criterion.<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_29-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
