<p>The <b>northern wheatear</b> or <b>wheatear</b> (<i>Oenanthe oenanthe</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> that was formerly classed as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family Turdidae, but is now more generally considered to be an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a>, Muscicapidae. It is the most widespread member of the <a href="/wiki/Wheatear" title="Wheatear">wheatear</a> genus <i>Oenanthe</i> in Europe and <a href="/wiki/North_Asia" title="North Asia">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>.
</p><p>The northern wheatear is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> insectivorous species breeding in open stony country in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and east across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>  with footholds in northeastern <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> as well as in northwestern Canada and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. It nests in rock crevices and <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbit</a> burrows. All birds spend most of their winter in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_wheatear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The northern wheatear was first <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">formally described</a> by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> as <i>Motacilla oenanthe</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  The species is now placed in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Oenanthe_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Oenanthe (bird)">Oenanthe</a></i> that was introduced by the French ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Louis_Jean_Pierre_Vieillot" title="Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot">Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot</a> in 1816.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The generic name, <i>Oenanthe</i>, is also the name of a <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> genus, the <a href="/wiki/Water_dropwort" title="Water dropwort">water dropworts</a>, and is derived from the Greek <i>ainos</i> "wine" and <i>anthos</i> "flower", from the wine-like scent of the flowers.<sup id="cite_ref-Botanical_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Botanical-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In the case of the wheatear, it refers to the birds' return to Greece in the spring at the time that the <a href="/wiki/Vitis" title="Vitis">grapevines</a> blossom.<sup id="cite_ref-enature_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enature-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Its <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> name has nothing to do with <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a> or with <a href="/wiki/Ear" title="Ear">ear</a>, but is an altered (perhaps <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bowdler" title="Thomas Bowdler">bowdlerised</a>) form of <b>white-arse</b>, which refers to its prominent white rump.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The four <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of the Northern Wheatear with their breeding range are as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>O. o. leucorhoa</i> (<a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Gmelin" title="Johann Friedrich Gmelin">Gmelin, JF</a>, 1789) – northeast Canada, Greenland and Iceland (the '<b>Greenland wheatear'</b>)</li>
<li><i>O. o. oenanthe</i> (<a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, 1758) – north and central Europe through north Asia to east Siberia and northwest North America</li>
<li><i>O. o. libanotica</i> (<a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Hemprich" title="Wilhelm Hemprich">Hemprich</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Christian_Gottfried_Ehrenberg" title="Christian Gottfried Ehrenberg">Ehrenberg</a>, 1833) – southern Europe through the Middle East and southwest Asia to Mongolia and northwest China</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Seebohm%27s_wheatear" title="Seebohm&#39;s wheatear"><i>O. o. seebohmi</i></a> (<a href="/wiki/Charles_Dixon_(ornithologist)" title="Charles Dixon (ornithologist)">Dixon</a>, 1882) – northwest Africa</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_wheatear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Oenanthe_oenanthe_MWNH_1870.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Oenanthe_oenanthe_MWNH_1870.JPG/220px-Oenanthe_oenanthe_MWNH_1870.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="361" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Oenanthe_oenanthe_MWNH_1870.JPG/330px-Oenanthe_oenanthe_MWNH_1870.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Oenanthe_oenanthe_MWNH_1870.JPG/440px-Oenanthe_oenanthe_MWNH_1870.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2529" data-file-height="4147" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Oenanthe_oenanthe_MWNH_1870.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>The northern wheatear is larger than the <a href="/wiki/European_robin" title="European robin">European robin</a> at 14.5–16&#160;cm (5.7–6.3&#160;in) in length. The northern wheatear also has a wingspan of 26- 32 cm and weighs 17-30 g<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>.
</p><p>Both sexes have a white rump and tail, with a black inverted T-pattern at the end of the tail.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> of the summer male has grey upperparts, buff throat and black wings and face mask. In autumn it resembles the female apart from the black wings. The female is pale brown above and buff below with darker brown wings. The male has a whistling, crackly <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">song</a>. Its call is a typical chat <i>chack</i> noise, and the flight call is the same.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_wheatear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration">Migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_wheatear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The northern wheatear makes one of the longest journeys of any small bird, crossing ocean, ice, and desert.<sup id="cite_ref-JE-1995_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JE-1995-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It migrates from <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a> in spring over a vast area of the Northern Hemisphere that includes northern and central Asia, Europe, Greenland, Alaska, and parts of Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-JE-1995_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JE-1995-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In autumn all return to Africa, where their ancestors had wintered.<sup id="cite_ref-JE-1995_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JE-1995-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Arguably, some of the birds that breed in north Asia could take a shorter route and winter in south Asia; however, their inherited inclination to migrate takes them back to Africa,<sup id="cite_ref-JE-1995_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JE-1995-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> completing one of the longest migrations for its body size in the animal kingdom<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Birds of the large, bright Greenland race, <i>leucorhoa</i>, makes one of the longest transoceanic crossings of any passerine. In spring most migrate along a route (commonly used by <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">waders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Waterfowl" class="mw-redirect" title="Waterfowl">waterfowl</a>) from Africa via <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">continental Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> to <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>. However, autumn sightings from ships suggest that some birds cross the North Atlantic directly from Canada and Greenland to southwest Europe, a distance of up to 2,500 kilometres (1,600&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Birds breeding in <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Canada" title="Eastern Canada">eastern Canada</a> are thought to fly from <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Island" title="Baffin Island">Baffin Island</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a> via Greenland, Ireland, and Portugal to the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, crossing 3,500 kilometres (2,200&#160;mi) of the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a>) before flying onwards to Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  Other populations from western Canada and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> migrate by flying over much of Eurasia to Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-JE-1995_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JE-1995-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Miniature tracking devices have recently shown that the northern wheatear has one of the longest migratory flights known - 30,000&#160;km (18,640 miles), from sub-Saharan Africa to their Arctic breeding grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>"The Alaskan birds travelled almost 15,000km (9,000 miles) each way - crossing Siberia and the Arabian Desert, and travelling, on average, 290km per day. "This is the longest recorded migration for a songbird as far as we know," said Dr Schmaljohann.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_wheatear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Northern wheatears first breed when they are one year old.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1988788_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1988788-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The nest is built entirely by the female while the male perches nearby, sings and sometimes performs song-flights.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1988784_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1988784-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The nest is placed in a cavity such as a rabbit burrow, a crevice among rocks or in a man-made object such as a wall or pipe. The nest typically has a foundation of untidy plant material. The nest cup is constructed of finer grasses, leaves, moss and lichen. The female lays eggs at daily intervals. The clutch is 4-7 smooth but not glossy eggs that are around 21.0&#160;mm ×&#160;15.8&#160;mm (0.83&#160;in ×&#160;0.62&#160;in) in size with an average weight of 2.83&#160;g (0.100&#160;oz). The eggs are very pale blue in colour and sometimes have a few red-brown marks at the larger end. They are incubated almost entirely by the female beginning after the penultimate or final egg has been laid. The eggs hatch after approximately 13 days. The chicks are fed by both parents and are brooded by the female for the first five or six days. They fledge after 15 days and become independent of their parents when they are between 28 and 32 days old. Normally only a single brood is raised each year but when a clutch of eggs is lost, the female will lay a second clutch.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1988787-788_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1988787-788-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_and_conservation">Status and conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_wheatear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Status and conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The northern wheatear has an extensive range, estimated at 2.3&#160;million&#160;square kilometres (0.87&#160;million square miles), and a large population estimated at 2.9 million individuals in the Old World and the Americas combined. The species is not believed to approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the IUCN Red List (i.e., declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations), and is therefore evaluated as <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_wheatear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 18th and 19th centuries wheatears were considered a delicacy in England, called "the English <a href="/wiki/Ortolan_bunting" title="Ortolan bunting">ortolan</a>" and Sussex shepherds supplemented their income by selling the birds they trapped.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
