<p>The <b>Northern Wheatear</b> or <b>Wheatear</b> (<i>Oenanthe oenanthe</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> that was formerly classed as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family Turdidae, but is now more generally considered to be an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a>, Muscicapidae. It is the most widespread member of the <a href="/wiki/Wheatear" title="Wheatear">wheatear</a> genus <i>Oenanthe</i> in Europe and Asia.</p>
<p>The Northern Wheatear is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> insectivorous species breeding in open stony country in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> with footholds in northeastern <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> as well as in northwestern Canada and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. It nests in rock crevices and <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbit</a> burrows. All birds winter in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Wheatear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Oenanthe_oenanthe_01_II.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Oenanthe_oenanthe_01_II.jpg/220px-Oenanthe_oenanthe_01_II.jpg" width="220" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Oenanthe_oenanthe_01_II.jpg/330px-Oenanthe_oenanthe_01_II.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Oenanthe_oenanthe_01_II.jpg/440px-Oenanthe_oenanthe_01_II.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Oenanthe_oenanthe_01_II.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Breeding male</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Northern Wheatear is larger than the <a href="/wiki/European_Robin" title="European Robin">European Robin</a> at 14½–16&#160;cm length. Both sexes have a white rump and tail, with a black inverted T-pattern at the end of the tail.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> of the summer male has grey upperparts, buff throat and black wings and face mask. In autumn it resembles the female apart from the black wings. The female is pale brown above and buff below with darker brown wings. The male has a whistling, crackly <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">song</a>. Its call is a typical chat <i>chack</i> noise.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Wheatear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in his <i>Systema naturae</i> in 1758 as <i>Motacilla oenanthe</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The generic name, <i>Oenanthe</i>, is also the name of a <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> genus, the <a href="/wiki/Water_dropwort" title="Water dropwort">water dropworts</a>, and is derived from the Greek <i>ainos</i> "wine" and <i>anthos</i> "flower", from the wine-like scent of the flowers.<sup id="cite_ref-Botanical_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Botanical-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> In the case of the wheatear, it refers to these birds' return to Greece in the spring just as the <a href="/wiki/Vitis" title="Vitis">grapevines</a> blossom.<sup id="cite_ref-enature_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enature-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Its <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> name has nothing to do with <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ear" title="Ear">ears</a>, but is an altered (perhaps <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bowdler" title="Thomas Bowdler">bowdlerised</a>) form of <i>white-arse</i>, which refers to its prominent white rump.</p>
<p>The following are the subspecies of Northern Wheatear<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from September 2012">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>:</p>
<ul>
<li>nominate <i>oenanthe</i></li>
<li>Greenland <i>leucorhoa</i></li>
<li>Seebohm's <i>seebohmi</i></li>
<li>southern <i>libanotica</i></li>
<li>Cretan <i>virago</i></li>
<li>Egyptian <i>rostrata</i></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Wheatear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Migration">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Migration">Migration</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Oenanthe_oenanthe_-Iceland-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Oenanthe_oenanthe_-Iceland-8.jpg/220px-Oenanthe_oenanthe_-Iceland-8.jpg" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Oenanthe_oenanthe_-Iceland-8.jpg/330px-Oenanthe_oenanthe_-Iceland-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Oenanthe_oenanthe_-Iceland-8.jpg/440px-Oenanthe_oenanthe_-Iceland-8.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Oenanthe_oenanthe_-Iceland-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Male in Iceland</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Northern Wheatear makes one of the longest journeys of any small bird, crossing ocean, ice, and desert.<sup id="cite_ref-JE-1995_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JE-1995-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> It migrates from Sub-Saharan Africa in Spring over a vast area of the northern hemisphere that includes northern and central Asia, Europe, Greenland, Alaska, and parts of Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-JE-1995_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JE-1995-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> In Autumn all return to Africa, where their ancestors had wintered.<sup id="cite_ref-JE-1995_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JE-1995-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Arguably, some of the birds that breed in north Asia could take a shorter route and winter in south Asia; however, their inherited inclination to migrate takes them back to Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-JE-1995_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JE-1995-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Birds of the large, bright Greenland race, <i>leucorhoa</i>, makes one of the longest transoceanic crossings of any passerine. In spring most migrate along a route (commonly used by <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">waders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Waterfowl" title="Waterfowl">waterfowl</a>) from Africa via continental Europe, the British Isles, and Iceland to Greenland. However, autumn sightings from ships suggest that some birds cross the North Atlantic directly from Canada and Greenland to southwest Europe (a distance of up to 2500&#160;km).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Birds breeding in eastern <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> are thought to fly from <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Island" title="Baffin Island">Baffin Island</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a> via Greenland, Ireland, and Portugal to the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> (crossing 3500&#160;km of the North Atlantic) before flying onwards to Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Other populations from western Canada and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> migrate by flying over much of Eurasia to Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-JE-1995_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JE-1995-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Miniature tracking devices have recently shown that the Northern Wheatear has one of the longest migratory flights known - 30,000&#160;km (18,640 miles), from from sub-Saharan Africa to their Arctic breeding grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>"The Alaskan birds travelled almost 15,000km (9,000 miles) each way - crossing Siberia and the Arabian Desert, and travelling, on average, 290km per day. "This is the longest recorded migration for a songbird as far as we know," said Dr Schmaljohann.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Wheatear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h2>
<p>The Northern Wheatear has an extensive range, estimated at 2.3&#160;million&#160;square kilometres (0.87&#160;million square miles), and a large population estimated at 2.9 million individuals in the Old World and the Americas combined. The species is not believed to approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the IUCN Red List (i.e., declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations), and is therefore evaluated as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
