<p>The <b>marsh sandpiper</b> (<i>Tringa stagnatilis</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a>. It is a rather small <a href="/wiki/Tringa" title="Tringa">shank</a>, and breeds in open grassy <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a> <a href="/wiki/Wetlands" class="mw-redirect" title="Wetlands">wetlands</a> from easternmost <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Far_East" title="Russian Far East">Russian Far East</a>. The genus name <i>Tringa</i> is the <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">New Latin</a> name given to the <a href="/wiki/Green_sandpiper" title="Green sandpiper">green sandpiper</a> by <a href="/wiki/Ulisse_Aldrovandi" title="Ulisse Aldrovandi">Aldrovandus</a> in 1599 based on <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>trungas</i>, a thrush-sized, white-rumped, tail-bobbing wading bird mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>. The specific <i>stagnatilis</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>stagnum</i>, "swamp".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marsh_sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It resembles a small elegant <a href="/wiki/Greenshank" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenshank">greenshank</a>, with a long fine <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> and very long yellowish legs. Like the greenshank, it is greyish brown in breeding <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>, paler in winter, and has a white wedge up its back that is visible in flight. However, it is more closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Common_redshank" title="Common redshank">common redshank</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Wood_sandpiper" title="Wood sandpiper">wood sandpiper</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pereira2005_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pereira2005-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Together, they form a group of smallish shanks which tend to have red or reddish legs, and in breeding plumage are generally a subdued, light brown above with some darker mottling, with a pattern of somewhat diffuse small brownish spots on the breast and neck. The length is 22–26&#160;cm (8.7–10.2&#160;in), wingspan is 55–59&#160;cm (22–23&#160;in) and weight is 45–120&#160;g (1.6–4.2&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-oiseaux_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oiseaux-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marsh_sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>.It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> species, with majority of birds wintering in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> with fewer migrating to <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. They prefer to winter on fresh water wetlands such as <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a> and lakes and are usually seen singly or in small groups.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tringa_stagnatilis_MWNH_0206.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Tringa_stagnatilis_MWNH_0206.JPG/220px-Tringa_stagnatilis_MWNH_0206.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Tringa_stagnatilis_MWNH_0206.JPG/330px-Tringa_stagnatilis_MWNH_0206.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Tringa_stagnatilis_MWNH_0206.JPG/440px-Tringa_stagnatilis_MWNH_0206.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3056" data-file-height="2320" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tringa_stagnatilis_MWNH_0206.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>These birds forage by probing in shallow water or on wet mud. They mainly eat insects, and similar small <a href="/wiki/Prey" class="mw-redirect" title="Prey">prey</a>.
</p><p>The marsh sandpiper is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>
