<p>The <b>collared flycatcher</b> (<i>Ficedula albicollis</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a> family, one of the four <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Western_Palearctic_black-and-white_flycatchers" title="Western Palearctic black-and-white flycatchers">Western Palearctic black-and-white flycatchers</a>.  It breeds in southeast <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> (isolated populations are present in the islands of <a href="/wiki/Gotland" title="Gotland">Gotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%96land" title="Öland">Öland</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>) and Eastern France to the <a href="/wiki/Balkan_Peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Balkan Peninsula">Balkan Peninsula</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> and is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in sub Sahara <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is a rare vagrant in western Europe.
</p><p>This is a 12–13.5&#160;cm long bird. The breeding male is mainly black above and white below, with a white collar, large white wing patch, black tail (although some males have white tail sides) and a large white forehead patch. It has a pale rump. The bill is black and has the broad but pointed shape typical of aerial insectivores. As well as taking insects in flight, this species hunts <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a> amongst the oak foliage, and will take berries.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ficedula_albicollis_MWNH_1878.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Ficedula_albicollis_MWNH_1878.JPG/220px-Ficedula_albicollis_MWNH_1878.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="248" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Ficedula_albicollis_MWNH_1878.JPG/330px-Ficedula_albicollis_MWNH_1878.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Ficedula_albicollis_MWNH_1878.JPG/440px-Ficedula_albicollis_MWNH_1878.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2194" data-file-height="2475" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ficedula_albicollis_MWNH_1878.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>Non-breeding males, females and juveniles have the black replaced by a pale brown, and may be very difficult to distinguish from other <i>Ficedula</i> flycatchers, particularly the <a href="/wiki/European_pied_flycatcher" title="European pied flycatcher">European pied flycatcher</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Semicollared_flycatcher" title="Semicollared flycatcher">semicollared flycatcher</a>, with which this species <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridizes</a> to a limited extent.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>They are birds of deciduous woodlands, parks and gardens, with a preference for old trees with cavities in which it nests. They build an open nest in a tree hole, or man-made nest-boxes. Normally 5-7 eggs are laid. The song is slow strained whistles, quite unlike the pied flycatcher. Pied flycatchers can mimic the song of the collared flycatcher in sympatric populations.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The genus name is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and refers to a small fig-eating bird (<i>ficus</i>, "fig") supposed to change into the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_blackcap" title="Eurasian blackcap">blackcap</a> in winter. The specific <i>albicollis</i> is from Latin <i>albus</i>, white, and  <i>collum</i>,  "neck".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The collared flycatcher is used as a model species in both <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a> and it was one of the first birds that had its full <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> sequenced.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Repeated spectrometric data taken from male Collared Flycatchers has revealed that plumage reflectance should be measured during courtship, the primary period of sexual signalling, with spectral traits declining over the breeding season. <sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
