<p>The <b>lesser whitethroat</b> (<i>Curruca curruca</i>) is a common and widespread <a href="/wiki/Typical_warbler" title="Typical warbler">typical warbler</a> which breeds in temperate <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, except the southwest, and in the western and central <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>. This small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> just south of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabia</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.
</p>

<p>Unlike many typical warblers, the sexes are almost identical. This is a small <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> with a grey back, whitish underparts, a grey head with a darker "bandit mask" through the eyes and a white throat. It is slightly smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Whitethroat" class="mw-redirect" title="Whitethroat">whitethroat</a>, and lacks the chestnut wings and uniform head-face color of that species. The lesser whitethroat's song is a fast and rattling sequence of <i>tet</i> or <i>che</i> calls, quite different from the whitethroat's scolding song.
</p><p>Like most "warblers", it is <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a>, but will also take <a href="/wiki/Berry" title="Berry">berries</a> and other soft <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a>. This is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of fairly open country and cultivation, with large bushes for nesting and some trees. The nest is built in low shrub or brambles, and 3 to 7 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> are laid.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesser_whitethroat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his 1758 edition of <i><a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>, from a specimen collected in southern <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>. The specific <i>curruca</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> term for a bird mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Juvenal" title="Juvenal">Juvenal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species has been commonly assumed to be closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Whitethroat" class="mw-redirect" title="Whitethroat">whitethroat</a>, as their common names imply. It was suggested<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> that the two species separated in the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Last glacial period">last ice age</a> similar to the pattern found in the <a href="/wiki/Common_chiffchaff" title="Common chiffchaff">chiffchaff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Willow_warbler" title="Willow warbler">willow warbler</a>, with their ancestor being forced into two enclaves, one in the southeast and one in the southwest of Europe. When the ice sheets retreated, the two forms supposedly no longer recognised each other as the same species. However, scientists researching this question have for quite some time realized that these two <a href="/wiki/Taxa" class="mw-redirect" title="Taxa">taxa</a> are not particularly close relatives.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Rather, the lesser whitethroat and its closest relatives <a href="/wiki/Hume%27s_whitethroat" title="Hume&#39;s whitethroat">Hume's whitethroat</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Small_whitethroat" class="mw-redirect" title="Small whitethroat">small whitethroat</a> appear more related to a group of <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphologically</a> quite dissimilar species. These vary much in size and color pattern, but also lack chestnut wing patches and have a strong contrast between the usually dark head sides and the white or whitish throat. The latter group occurs from the southern parts of the lesser whitethroat complex' range into Africa and include the <a href="/wiki/Orphean_warbler" title="Orphean warbler">Orphean warbler</a> group, the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_warbler" title="Arabian warbler">Arabian warbler</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Brown_warbler" class="mw-redirect" title="Brown warbler">brown</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yemen_warbler" title="Yemen warbler">Yemen warblers</a> sometimes placed in <i>Parisoma</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Thus, it rather appears as if the divergence of the lesser whitethroat complex and its closest living relatives is more ancient than assumed, and that it did not involve separation by ice sheets building up in Europe, but by <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">aridification</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabia">Arabian</a> region (which also occurred throughout the Ice Ages).
</p><p>The lesser whitethroat complex has been split up into the present species, Hume's whitethroat, and the small whitethroat from which the Margelanic whitethroat may also be specifically distinct. In this <a href="/wiki/Superspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Superspecies">superspecies</a>, the lesser whitethroat seems to form the <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basal</a> European lineage. Only two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are nowadays unequivocally recognized for the lesser whitethroat, and they intergrade throughout Central Europe:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Curruca curruca curruca</i> – <b>Western lesser whitethroat</b> – western parts of range</li>
<li><i>Curruca curruca blythi</i> – <b>Northeastern lesser whitethroat</b> – eastern parts of range. Has somewhat paler top of head, separated from face sides by white <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Two more taxa occur in the intergradation zone with the small whitethroat, stretching from the northern <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a> area into <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Phylogeny" class="mw-redirect" title="Phylogeny">phylogeny</a> of these is not well-researched, and they might eventually turn out to belong to either species or be stereotyped <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrids</a>:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Curruca curruca/minula halimodendri</i></li>
<li><i>Curruca curruca/minula telengitica</i></li></ul>
<p>Similarly, <i>Curruca curruca caucasica</i> is intermediate between the lesser and Hume's whitethroats.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A bird which wintered at <a href="/wiki/Landsort" title="Landsort">Landsort</a> Bird Observatory, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, differed in the analyzed <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" class="mw-redirect" title="MtDNA">mtDNA</a> sequences from all known taxa in the complex, although it most closely resembled <i>halimodendri</i> in the field. As mtDNA is inherited from the mother only, were this bird a hybrid this should have been recognizable.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some taxonomic authorities lump the <a href="/wiki/Hume%27s_whitethroat" title="Hume&#39;s whitethroat">Hume's whitethroat</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Desert_whitethroat" title="Desert whitethroat">desert whitethroat</a> into this species.
</p>
