<p>The <b>greater crested tern</b><sup id="cite_ref-gill_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gill-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> (<i>Thalasseus bergii</i>), also called <b>crested tern</b> or <b>swift tern</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">tern</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Laridae" title="Laridae">Laridae</a> that nests in dense colonies on coastlines and islands in the tropical and subtropical <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a>. Its five <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> breed in the area from <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> to the central <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, all populations <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">dispersing</a> widely from the breeding range after nesting. This large tern is closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_tern" title="Royal tern">royal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lesser_crested_tern" title="Lesser crested tern">lesser crested terns</a>, but can be distinguished by its size and bill colour.
</p><p>The greater crested tern has grey upperparts, white underparts, a yellow bill, and a shaggy black crest that recedes in winter. Its young have a distinctive appearance, with strongly patterned grey, brown and white plumage, and rely on their parents for food for several months after they have <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledged</a>. Like all members of the genus <i>Thalasseus</i>, the greater crested tern feeds by plunge diving for fish, usually in marine environments; the male offers fish to the female as part of the courtship ritual.
</p><p>This is an adaptable species that has learned to follow fishing boats for jettisoned  <a href="/wiki/Bycatch" title="Bycatch">bycatch</a>, and to use unusual nest sites such as the roofs of buildings and artificial islands in <a href="/wiki/Salt_evaporation_pond" title="Salt evaporation pond">salt pans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">sewage works</a>. Its eggs and young are taken by <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ibis" title="Ibis">ibises</a>, and human activities such as fishing, shooting and egg harvesting have caused local population declines. There are no global conservation concerns for this bird, which has a stable total population of more than 500,000 individuals.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_crested_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The terns, family Sternidae, are small to medium-sized seabirds closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skimmer_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Skimmer (bird)">skimmers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skua" title="Skua">skuas</a>. They are gull-like in appearance, but typically have a lighter build, long pointed wings (which give them a fast, buoyant flight), a deeply forked tail and short legs. Most species are grey above and white below, and have a black cap that is reduced or flecked with white in the winter.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP764_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP764-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>

<td style="text-align: left; font-size: 88%; border: 0; padding: 0;">Relationships in the genus <i>Thalasseus</i><sup id="cite_ref-ref3_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ref3-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>

<p>The greater crested tern was originally described as <i>Sterna bergii</i> by German naturalist <a href="/wiki/Martin_Lichtenstein" class="mw-redirect" title="Martin Lichtenstein">Martin Lichtenstein</a> in 1823, but was moved to its current genus, <i><a href="/wiki/Thalasseus" title="Thalasseus">Thalasseus</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Boie_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boie-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> after <a href="/wiki/Mitochondria" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitochondria">mitochondrial</a> <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> studies confirmed that the three main head patterns shown by terns (no black cap, black cap, black cap with a white forehead) corresponded to distinct <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clades</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bridge2005_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bridge2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The greater crested tern's closest relatives within its genus appear to be the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_crested_tern" title="Lesser crested tern">lesser crested tern</a> (<i>T.&#160;bengalensis</i>), and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_tern" title="Royal tern">royal tern</a> (<i>T. maximus</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Bridge2005_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bridge2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The DNA study did not include the <a href="/wiki/Critically_endangered_species" class="mw-redirect" title="Critically endangered species">critically endangered</a> <a href="/wiki/Chinese_crested_tern" title="Chinese crested tern">Chinese crested tern</a> (<i>T. bernsteini</i>) but, as that bird was formerly considered to be <a href="/wiki/Conspecificity" class="mw-redirect" title="Conspecificity">conspecific</a> with the greater crested tern as a <a href="/wiki/Synonym_(taxonomy)" title="Synonym (taxonomy)">synonym</a> of the subspecies <i>T. b. cristatus</i>, it is presumably also very closely related.<sup id="cite_ref-Mees_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mees-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">generic name</a> of the greater crested tern is derived from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>Thalassa</i>, "sea", and the species <a href="/wiki/Epithet" title="Epithet">epithet</a> <i>bergii</i> commemorates <a href="/wiki/Karl_Heinrich_Bergius" title="Karl Heinrich Bergius">Carl Heinrich Bergius</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> pharmacist and botanist who collected the first specimens of this tern near <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HANZAB_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HANZAB-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The greater crested tern has about five geographical races, differing mainly in the colour of the upperparts and bill. These are listed below in <a href="/wiki/Taxonomic_sequence" title="Taxonomic sequence">taxonomic sequence</a>. A similar number of other potential subspecies have been proposed, but are not considered valid.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_crested_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Crested_Tern.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Crested_Tern.jpg/220px-Crested_Tern.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Crested_Tern.jpg/330px-Crested_Tern.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Crested_Tern.jpg/440px-Crested_Tern.jpg 2x" data-file-width="557" data-file-height="371" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Crested_Tern.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Non-breeding adult <i>T. b. cristata</i></div></div></div>
<p>The greater crested tern is a large tern with a long (5.4–6.5&#160;cm or 2.1–2.6&#160;in) yellow bill, black legs, and a glossy black crest that is noticeably shaggy at its rear. The breeding adult of the <a href="/wiki/Subspecies#Nomenclature" title="Subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> <i>T. b. bergii</i> is 46–49&#160;cm (18–19.5&#160;in) long, with a 125–130&#160;cm (49–51&#160;in) wing-span; this subspecies weighs 325–397&#160;<a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">g</a> (11.4–14.0&#160;<a href="/wiki/Ounce" title="Ounce">oz</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The forehead and the underparts are white, the back and inner wings are dusky-grey. In winter, the upperparts plumage wears to a paler grey, and the crown of the head becomes white, merging at the rear into a peppered black crest and mask.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The adults of both sexes are identical in appearance, but juvenile birds are distinctive, with a head pattern like the winter adult, and upperparts strongly patterned in grey, brown, and white; the closed wings appear to have dark bars. After <a href="/wiki/Moult" class="mw-redirect" title="Moult">moulting</a>, the young terns resemble the adult, but still have a variegated wing pattern with a dark bar on the inner <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">flight feathers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_13-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Crested_tern444_edit.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Crested_tern444_edit.jpg/220px-Crested_tern444_edit.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="126" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Crested_tern444_edit.jpg/330px-Crested_tern444_edit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Crested_tern444_edit.jpg/440px-Crested_tern444_edit.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2184" data-file-height="1250" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Crested_tern444_edit.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>First year <i>T. b. cristata</i></div></div></div>
<p>The northern subspecies <i>T. b. velox</i> and <i>T. b. thalassina</i> are in breeding plumage from May to September or October, whereas the relevant period for the two southern African races is from December to April. For <i>T. b. cristata</i>, the moult timing depends on location; birds from Australia and <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Oceania</a> are in breeding plumage from September to about April, but those in <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, China and <a href="/wiki/Sulawesi" title="Sulawesi">Sulawesi</a> have this appearance from February to June or July.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_13-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The royal tern is similar in size to this species, but has a heavier build, broader wings, a paler back and a blunter, more orange bill. The greater crested often associates with the lesser crested tern, but is 25%&#160;larger than the latter, with a proportionately longer bill, longer and heavier head, and bulkier body.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Lesser crested tern has an orange-tinted bill, and in immature plumage it is much less variegated than greater crested.<sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The greater crested tern is highly <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">vocal</a>, especially at its breeding grounds. The territorial advertising call is a loud, raucous, <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crow-like</a> <i>kerrak</i>. Other calls include a <i>korrkorrkorr</i> given at the nest by anxious or excited birds, and a hard <i>wep wep</i> in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_crested_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Little_Tern_with_Crested_Terns-2008-30-07.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Little_Tern_with_Crested_Terns-2008-30-07.jpg/220px-Little_Tern_with_Crested_Terns-2008-30-07.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="125" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Little_Tern_with_Crested_Terns-2008-30-07.jpg/330px-Little_Tern_with_Crested_Terns-2008-30-07.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Little_Tern_with_Crested_Terns-2008-30-07.jpg/440px-Little_Tern_with_Crested_Terns-2008-30-07.jpg 2x" data-file-width="526" data-file-height="300" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Little_Tern_with_Crested_Terns-2008-30-07.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Roosting with <a href="/wiki/Little_tern" title="Little tern">little terns</a>, note size difference</div></div></div>
<p>The greater crested tern occurs in tropical and warm temperate coastal parts of the Old World from South Africa around the Indian Ocean to the Pacific and Australia. The subspecies <i>T. b. bergii</i> and <i>T. b. enigma</i> breed in Southern Africa from Namibia to <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>, and possibly on islands around Madagascar. There is then a break in the breeding distribution of this species until <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a> and the Red Sea, and another discontinuity further east in southern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The greater crested tern breeds on many islands in the Indian Ocean including <a href="/wiki/Aldabra" title="Aldabra">Aldabra</a> and Etoile in the Seychelles, the <a href="/wiki/Chagos_Archipelago" title="Chagos Archipelago">Chagos Archipelago</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rodrigues_(island)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rodrigues (island)">Rodrigues.</a><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> There are colonies on numerous Pacific islands, including <a href="/wiki/Kiribati" title="Kiribati">Kiribati</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Society_Islands" title="Society Islands">Society Islands</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tuamotus" title="Tuamotus">Tuamotus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The nests are located on low‑lying sandy, rocky, or coral islands, sometimes amongst stunted shrubs, often without any shelter at all.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> When not breeding, the greater crested tern will roost or rest on open shores, less often on boats, pilings, harbour buildings and raised salt mounds in lagoons. It is rarely seen on tidal creeks or inland waters.<sup id="cite_ref-COOPER_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-COOPER-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Thalasseus_bergii_in_flight_-_Boat_Harbour.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Thalasseus_bergii_in_flight_-_Boat_Harbour.jpg/220px-Thalasseus_bergii_in_flight_-_Boat_Harbour.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Thalasseus_bergii_in_flight_-_Boat_Harbour.jpg/330px-Thalasseus_bergii_in_flight_-_Boat_Harbour.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Thalasseus_bergii_in_flight_-_Boat_Harbour.jpg/440px-Thalasseus_bergii_in_flight_-_Boat_Harbour.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4647" data-file-height="3098" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Thalasseus_bergii_in_flight_-_Boat_Harbour.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In flight</div></div></div>
<p>All populations of greater crested tern disperse after breeding. When Southern African birds leave colonies in Namibia and <a href="/wiki/Western_Cape_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Cape Province">Western Cape Province</a>, most adults move east to the Indian Ocean coastline of South Africa. Many young birds also travel east, sometimes more than 2,000&#160;km (1,200&#160;mi), but others move northwards along the western coast. <i>T. b. thalassina</i> winters on the east African coast north to <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a> and Somalia and may move as far south as Durban. Populations of <i>T. b. velox</i> breeding from the Persian Gulf eastwards appear to be sedentary or dispersive rather than truly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, but those breeding in the Red Sea winter south along the east African coast to Kenya.<sup id="cite_ref-COOPER_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-COOPER-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <i>T. b. cristata</i> mostly stays within 400&#160;km (250&#160;mi) of its colonies, but some birds wander up to around 1,000&#160;km (620&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-Carrick_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carrick-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> This species has occurred as a vagrant to <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-AOU_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOU-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OSNZ_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OSNZ-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<p>In India, the Greater crested tern is protected in the <a href="/wiki/PM_Sayeed_Marine_Birds_Conservation_Reserve" title="PM Sayeed Marine Birds Conservation Reserve">PM Sayeed Marine Birds Conservation Reserve</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_crested_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_crested_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Crested_Tern_courtship.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Crested_Tern_courtship.jpg/220px-Crested_Tern_courtship.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="170" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Crested_Tern_courtship.jpg/330px-Crested_Tern_courtship.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Crested_Tern_courtship.jpg/440px-Crested_Tern_courtship.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1911" data-file-height="1477" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Crested_Tern_courtship.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>T. b. cristata</i> displaying</div></div></div>
<p>The greater crested tern breeds in <a href="/wiki/Seabird_colony" class="mw-redirect" title="Seabird colony">colonies</a>, often in association with other seabirds. It is <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a> and the pair bond is maintained through the year and sometimes in consecutive breeding seasons.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The colony size is related to the abundance of <a href="/wiki/Pelagic_fish" title="Pelagic fish">pelagic fish</a> prey,<sup id="cite_ref-COOPER_14-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-COOPER-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and the largest documented colony, with 13,000 to 15,000 pairs, is in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Carpentaria" title="Gulf of Carpentaria">Gulf of Carpentaria</a> in northern Australia, a region which also supports major colonies of other seabirds. Since nesting in this area follows the summer <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoonal</a> flooding, it is presumably a response to fish stocks rising, probably due to river run-off providing extra nutrient to the Gulf.<sup id="cite_ref-Walker_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walker-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> This tern does not show <a href="/wiki/Philopatry" title="Philopatry">site fidelity</a>, frequently changing its nest site from year to year,<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_16-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> sometimes by more than 200&#160;km (120&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-Carrick_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carrick-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Thailandsand_Laem_Pak_Bia.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Thailandsand_Laem_Pak_Bia.jpg/220px-Thailandsand_Laem_Pak_Bia.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Thailandsand_Laem_Pak_Bia.jpg/330px-Thailandsand_Laem_Pak_Bia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Thailandsand_Laem_Pak_Bia.jpg/440px-Thailandsand_Laem_Pak_Bia.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2880" data-file-height="2160" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Thailandsand_Laem_Pak_Bia.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tropical sandy islands are used for breeding and roosting, but may be disturbed by human activities.</div></div></div>
<p>A male greater crested tern establishes a small area of the colony in preparation for nesting, and initially pecks at any other tern entering his territory. If the intruder is another male, it retaliates in kind, and is normally vigorously repelled by the incumbent. A female entering the nest area reacts passively to the male's aggression, enabling him to recognise her sex and initiate pair formation by display, including head raising and bowing; this behaviour is frequently repeated during nesting to reinforce the bond between the pair. Terns also use fish as part of the courtship ritual. One bird flies around the colony with a fish in its beak, calling loudly; its partner may also fly, but the pair eventually settle and the gift is exchanged.<sup id="cite_ref-Fisher_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fisher-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Crested_Tern_breeding_plumage.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Crested_Tern_breeding_plumage.jpg/220px-Crested_Tern_breeding_plumage.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="133" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Crested_Tern_breeding_plumage.jpg/330px-Crested_Tern_breeding_plumage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Crested_Tern_breeding_plumage.jpg/440px-Crested_Tern_breeding_plumage.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1535" data-file-height="931" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Crested_Tern_breeding_plumage.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Breeding plumage in <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a></div></div></div>
<p>The nest is a shallow scrape in the sand on open, flat or occasionally sloping ground. It is often unlined, but sometimes includes stones or <a href="/wiki/Cuttlefish" title="Cuttlefish">cuttlefish</a> bones. One, sometimes two, eggs are laid and <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" class="mw-redirect" title="Avian incubation">incubated</a> by both parents for 25 to 30&#160;days prior to hatching.<sup id="cite_ref-COOPER_14-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-COOPER-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The eggs are cream with blackish streaks.<sup id="cite_ref-Ausmus_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ausmus-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Egg laying is synchronised within a breeding colony<sup id="cite_ref-Dunlop_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunlop-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> and more tightly so within sub-colonies.<sup id="cite_ref-emu_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emu-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Parents do not recognize their own eggs or newly hatched chicks, but are able to distinguish their chicks by the time they are two days-old, shortly before they begin to wander from the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-Davies_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Precocial" class="mw-redirect" title="Precocial">precocial</a> chicks, which are very pale with black speckling, are brooded and fed by both parents, but may gather in crèches when older. The young terns fledge after 38 to 40&#160;days, but remain dependent on the parents after leaving the colony until they are about four months old.<sup id="cite_ref-COOPER_14-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-COOPER-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Thalasseus_bergii_by_Gregg_Yan_01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Thalasseus_bergii_by_Gregg_Yan_01.jpg/220px-Thalasseus_bergii_by_Gregg_Yan_01.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Thalasseus_bergii_by_Gregg_Yan_01.jpg/330px-Thalasseus_bergii_by_Gregg_Yan_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Thalasseus_bergii_by_Gregg_Yan_01.jpg/440px-Thalasseus_bergii_by_Gregg_Yan_01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1508" data-file-height="1000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Thalasseus_bergii_by_Gregg_Yan_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A nesting colony in <a href="/wiki/Tubbataha_Reef" title="Tubbataha Reef">Tubbataha Reef</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Thalasseus_bergii_MWNH_0382.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Thalasseus_bergii_MWNH_0382.JPG/220px-Thalasseus_bergii_MWNH_0382.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Thalasseus_bergii_MWNH_0382.JPG/330px-Thalasseus_bergii_MWNH_0382.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Thalasseus_bergii_MWNH_0382.JPG/440px-Thalasseus_bergii_MWNH_0382.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2865" data-file-height="1952" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Thalasseus_bergii_MWNH_0382.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>In South Africa, this species has adapted to breeding on the roofs of building, sometimes with <a href="/wiki/Hartlaub%27s_gull" title="Hartlaub&#39;s gull">Hartlaub's gull</a>, which also shares the more typical nesting sites of the nominate race. In 2000, 7.5%&#160;of the population of this subspecies bred on roofs.<sup id="cite_ref-Crawford_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Artificial islands in salt pans and sewage works have also recently been colonised by this adaptable seabird.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Adult terns have few predators, but in Namibia immature birds are often robbed of their food by <a href="/wiki/Kelp_gull" title="Kelp gull">kelp gulls</a>, and that species, along with Hartlaub's gull, <a href="/wiki/Silver_gull" title="Silver gull">silver gull</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sacred_ibis" class="mw-redirect" title="Sacred ibis">sacred ibis</a>, has been observed feeding on eggs or nestlings, especially when colonies are disturbed.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-leroux_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leroux-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Smaller subcolonies with a relatively larger numbers of nests located on the perimeter are subject to more predation.<sup id="cite_ref-emu_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emu-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> In Australia, predation by cats and dogs, and occasional deaths by shooting or collisions with cars, wires or light-towers have been documented.<sup id="cite_ref-HANZAB_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HANZAB-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sternabergiisouthafrica.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Sternabergiisouthafrica.jpg/220px-Sternabergiisouthafrica.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="137" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Sternabergiisouthafrica.jpg/330px-Sternabergiisouthafrica.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Sternabergiisouthafrica.jpg/440px-Sternabergiisouthafrica.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="639" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sternabergiisouthafrica.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nominate subspecies roosting with <a href="/wiki/Sandwich_tern" title="Sandwich tern">Sandwich terns</a> in South Africa</div></div></div>
<p>Commercial fisheries can have both positive and negative effects on the greater crested tern. Juvenile survival rates are improved where trawler discards provide extra food, and huge population increases in the southeastern Gulf of Carpentaria are thought to have been due to the development of a large prawn trawl fishery.<sup id="cite_ref-Blaber_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blaber-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Conversely, <a href="/wiki/Seine_fishing#Purse_seine" title="Seine fishing">purse-seine</a> fishing reduces the available food supply, and sizeable fluctuations in the numbers of great crested terns breeding in the Western Cape of South Africa are significantly related to changes in the abundance of pelagic fish, which are intensively exploited by purse-seine fishing.<sup id="cite_ref-leroux_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leroux-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Terns may be killed or injured by collisions with trawl warps, trapped in trawls or discarded gear, or hooked by <a href="/wiki/Longline_fishing" title="Longline fishing">longline fishing</a>, but, unlike <a href="/wiki/Albatross" title="Albatross">albatrosses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petrel" title="Petrel">petrels</a>, there is little evidence that overall numbers are significantly affected.<sup id="cite_ref-COOPER_14-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-COOPER-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An unusual incident was the incapacitation of 103 terns off <a href="/wiki/Robben_Island" title="Robben Island">Robben Island</a>, South Africa by marine foam, generated by a combination of wave action, <a href="/wiki/Kelp" title="Kelp">kelp</a> <a href="/wiki/Mucilage" title="Mucilage">mucilage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a>. After treatment, 90%&#160;of the birds were fit to be released.<sup id="cite_ref-Parsons_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parsons-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_crested_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pacific_sardine002.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Pacific_sardine002.jpg/220px-Pacific_sardine002.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b1/Pacific_sardine002.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pacific_sardine002.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sardine" title="Sardine">Sardines</a> are a common food item.</div></div></div>
<p>Fish are the main food of the greater crested tern, found to make up nearly 90% of all prey items with the remainder including <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">cephalopods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacea" class="mw-redirect" title="Crustacea">crustaceans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-COOPER_14-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-COOPER-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Unusual <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrate</a> prey included <a href="/wiki/Agamids" class="mw-redirect" title="Agamids">agamid lizards</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green_turtle" class="mw-redirect" title="Green turtle">green turtle</a> hatchlings.<sup id="cite_ref-Blaber_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blaber-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The great crested tern feeds mostly at sea by plunge diving to a depth of up to 1&#160;m (3.3&#160;ft), or by dipping from the surface, and food is usually swallowed in mid-air. Birds may forage up to 10&#160;km (6.2&#160;mi) from land in the breeding season. Prey size ranges from 7–138&#160;mm (0.28–5.43&#160;in) in length and up to 30&#160;g (1.1&#160;oz) in weight. Shoaling <a href="/wiki/Pelagic" class="mw-redirect" title="Pelagic">pelagic</a> fish such as <a href="/wiki/Anchovy" title="Anchovy">anchovy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sardine" title="Sardine">sardine</a> are typical prey,<sup id="cite_ref-COOPER_14-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-COOPER-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> but bottom-living species are taken as discards from commercial fishing. This tern actively follows trawlers, including at night, and during the fishing season trawl discards can constitute 70% of its diet.<sup id="cite_ref-Blaber_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blaber-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Prawn fishing is particularly productive in providing extra food, since prawns usually represent only 10–20% of the catch, the remaining being bycatch, mainly fish such as <a href="/wiki/Apogonidae" title="Apogonidae">cardinalfish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Goby" title="Goby">gobies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Blaber_33-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blaber-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shrimp_bycatch.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Shrimp_bycatch.jpg/220px-Shrimp_bycatch.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Shrimp_bycatch.jpg/330px-Shrimp_bycatch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Shrimp_bycatch.jpg/440px-Shrimp_bycatch.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1776" data-file-height="1168" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shrimp_bycatch.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bycatch from <a href="/wiki/Prawn" title="Prawn">prawn</a> fishing can provide extra food</div></div></div>
<p>A study of an area of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Barrier_Reef" title="Great Barrier Reef">Great Barrier Reef</a> where the number of breeding great crested terns has grown ten-fold, probably due to extra food from trawl <a href="/wiki/Bycatch" title="Bycatch">by-catch</a>, suggested that lesser crested and <a href="/wiki/Sooty_tern" title="Sooty tern">sooty terns</a> have moved away and now breed on a part of the reef where fishing is banned. It is possible that the large increase in the number of greater crested terns may have affected other species through competition for food and nesting sites.<sup id="cite_ref-Blaber2_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blaber2-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Terns have red oil droplets in the <a href="/wiki/Cone_cell" title="Cone cell">cone cells</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">retinas</a> of their eyes. This improves contrast and sharpens distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_vision" title="Bird vision">vision</a>, especially in hazy conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Birds that have to see through an air/water interface, such as terns and gulls, have more strongly coloured <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">carotenoid</a> <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigments</a> in the cone oil drops than other avian species.<sup id="cite_ref-Varela_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Varela-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The improved eyesight helps terns to locate shoals of fish, although it is uncertain whether they are sighting the <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> on which the fish feed, or observing other terns diving for food.<sup id="cite_ref-Lythgoe_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lythgoe-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Tern's eyes are not particularly <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> sensitive, an adaptation more suited to terrestrial feeders like the gulls.<sup id="cite_ref-Hastad_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hastad-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_crested_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The greater crested tern has a widespread distribution range, estimated at 1–10&#160;million square kilometres (0.4–3.8&#160;million square miles). The population has not been quantified, but it is not believed to approach the thresholds for either the size criterion (fewer than 10,000 mature individuals) or the population decline criterion (declining more than 30%&#160;in ten years or three generations) of the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>. For these reasons, the species is evaluated as being of Least Concern at the global level.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> However, there are concerns for populations in some areas such as the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Thailand" title="Gulf of Thailand">Gulf of Thailand</a> where the species no longer breeds, and in <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> where egg harvesting has caused declines.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_10-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>All subspecies except <i>T. b. cristata</i> are covered under the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-AEWA_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AEWA-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Parties to the Agreement are required to engage in a wide range of conservation strategies described in a detailed action plan. The plan is intended to address key issues such as species and habitat conservation, management of human activities, research, education, and implementation.<sup id="cite_ref-AEWAintro_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AEWAintro-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
