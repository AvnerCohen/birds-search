<p>The <b>parasitic jaeger</b> (<i>Stercorarius parasiticus</i>), also known as the <b>Arctic skua</b>, <b>Arctic jaeger</b> or <b>parasitic skua</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Skua" title="Skua">skua</a> family Stercorariidae. The word "jaeger" is derived from the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> word <i>Jäger</i>, meaning "hunter".<sup id="cite_ref-OEDjaeger_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OEDjaeger-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The English "skua" comes from the <a href="/wiki/Faroese_language" title="Faroese language">Faroese</a> name <i>skúgvur</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[ˈskɪkvʊər]</a></span> for the <a href="/wiki/Great_skua" title="Great skua">great skua</a>, with the island of <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%BAvoy" title="Skúvoy">Skúvoy</a> known for its colony of that bird. The general Faroese term for skuas is <i>kjógvi</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[ˈtʃɛkvə]</a></span>.<sup id="cite_ref-OEDskua_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OEDskua-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The genus name <i>Stercorarius</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means "of dung"; the food disgorged by other birds when pursued by skuas was once thought to be excrement. The specific <i>parasiticus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means "parasitic".<sup id="cite_ref-job_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parasitic_jaeger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stercorarius_parasiticus-pjt.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Stercorarius_parasiticus-pjt.jpg/220px-Stercorarius_parasiticus-pjt.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Stercorarius_parasiticus-pjt.jpg/330px-Stercorarius_parasiticus-pjt.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Stercorarius_parasiticus-pjt.jpg/440px-Stercorarius_parasiticus-pjt.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2943" data-file-height="2080" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stercorarius_parasiticus-pjt.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dark morph (Iceland)</div></div></div>
<p>Identification is complicated by similarities to <a href="/wiki/Long-tailed_jaeger" title="Long-tailed jaeger">long-tailed jaeger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pomarine_jaeger" title="Pomarine jaeger">pomarine jaeger</a>, and the existence of three colour <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(biology)" title="Polymorphism (biology)">morphs</a>. Small for a skua, the parasitic jaeger measures 41–48&#160;cm (16–19&#160;in) in length, 107–125&#160;cm (42–49&#160;in) in wingspan and weighs 300–650&#160;g (0.66–1.43&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-arkive_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arkive-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CRC_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The tail streamer of the breeding adult accounts for about 7&#160;cm (2.8&#160;in) of their length. Light-morph adults have a brown back, mainly white underparts and dark primary wing feathers with a white "flash". The head and neck are yellowish-white with a black cap and there is a pointed central tail projection. Dark-morph adults are dark brown, and intermediate-phase birds are dark with somewhat paler underparts, head and neck. All morphs have the white wing flash.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ArcticSkua3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/ArcticSkua3.jpg/220px-ArcticSkua3.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="204" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/ArcticSkua3.jpg/330px-ArcticSkua3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/ArcticSkua3.jpg/440px-ArcticSkua3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2718" data-file-height="2519" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ArcticSkua3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An immature parasitic jaeger</div></div></div>
<p>Identification of juveniles is even more problematic, and it is difficult to separate parasitic jaegers from long-tailed jaegers. Parasitic jaegers are bulkier, shorter-winged, and less tern-like than long-tailed jaegers. They are usually warmer toned, with browner shades, rather than grey. However, they show the same wide range of plumage variation. The flight is more <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon</a>-like.
</p><p>The typical call of these birds is a nasal mewing sound, repeated a few times in display. Their alarm call is a shorter sound.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parasitic_jaeger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parasitic_jaeger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stercorarius_parasiticus_MWNH_0303.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Stercorarius_parasiticus_MWNH_0303.JPG/220px-Stercorarius_parasiticus_MWNH_0303.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Stercorarius_parasiticus_MWNH_0303.JPG/330px-Stercorarius_parasiticus_MWNH_0303.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Stercorarius_parasiticus_MWNH_0303.JPG/440px-Stercorarius_parasiticus_MWNH_0303.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stercorarius_parasiticus_MWNH_0303.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>This species breeds in the north of <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurasia</a> and North America, with significant populations as far south as northern <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. It nests on dry <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>, higher fells and islands, laying up to four olive-brown eggs. It is usually silent except for mewing and wailing notes while on the breeding grounds. Like other skuas, it will fly at the head of a human or fox approaching its nest. Although it cannot inflict serious damage, it is a frightening and painful experience. It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>, wintering at sea in the tropics and southern oceans.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, they breed in <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Hebrides" title="Outer Hebrides">Outer Hebrides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sutherland" title="Sutherland">Sutherland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caithness" title="Caithness">Caithness</a>, and some islands in <a href="/wiki/Argyll_and_Bute" title="Argyll and Bute">Argyll</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parasitic_jaeger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This bird will feed on rodents, insects, eggs, chicks and small birds in the breeding season, but the majority of its diet (especially in winter and on migration) is made up of food that it acquires by robbing other <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> (primarily <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">terns</a>) of their catches in an act called <a href="/wiki/Kleptoparasitism" title="Kleptoparasitism">kleptoparasitism</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parasitic_jaeger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2018, <i>Stercorarius parasiticus</i> was regionally uplisted to Endangered in Iceland, from Least Concern in 2000, after their numbers declined drastically in the early 2000s.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
