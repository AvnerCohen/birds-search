<p>The <b>Little Swift</b> (<i>Apus affinis</i>), is a small <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a>, superficially similar to a <a href="/wiki/Barn_Swallow" title="Barn Swallow">Barn Swallow</a> or <a href="/wiki/House_Martin" title="House Martin" class="mw-redirect">House Martin</a>. It is, however, completely unrelated to those <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> species, since <a href="/wiki/Swift" title="Swift">swifts</a> are in the order Apodiformes. The resemblances between the groups are due to <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">convergent evolution</a> reflecting similar life styles. The former eastern race, <a href="/wiki/House_Swift" title="House Swift">House Swift</a>, is usually considered a separate species, <i>Apus nipalensis</i>.</p>
<p>These birds have very short legs which they use only for clinging to vertical surfaces. The scientific name comes from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> απους, <i>apous</i>, meaning "without feet". They never settle voluntarily on the ground.</p>
<p>Little Swifts breed around habitation and cliffs from <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> northeastwards through southern <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>. Unlike the more northerly <a href="/wiki/Common_Swift" title="Common Swift">Common Swift</a>, many birds are resident, but some populations are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, and winter further south than their breeding areas. They wander widely on migration, and are seen as rare vagrants in much of Europe and Asia.</p>

<p>Little Swifts build their nests in hole in buildings or sometimes on cliffs, laying 1-4 eggs. A swift will return to the same site year after year, rebuilding its nest when necessary. A species of bedbug <i>Cimex hemipterus</i> has been recorded from its nest in India.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Little Swifts spend most of their lives in the air, living on the insects they catch in their beaks. They drink on the wing, but roost on vertical cliffs or walls. They are notoriously slow risers in the mornings.</p>
<p>Little Swifts are readily identified by their small size. Their wingspan is 33&#160;cm compared to the 42&#160;cm of Common Swift. They are black except for a white rump, the white extending on to the flanks. They have a short square tail. The flight is fluttering like a House Martin.</p>
<p>The call is a high twittering.</p>
