<p>The <b>wood warbler</b> (<i>Phylloscopus sibilatrix</i>) is a common and widespread <a href="/wiki/Leaf_warbler" title="Leaf warbler">leaf warbler</a> which breeds throughout northern and temperate <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, and just into the extreme west of <a href="/wiki/North_Asia" title="North Asia">Asian Russia</a> in the southern <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural Mountains</a>. The genus name <i>Phylloscopus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>phullon</i>, "leaf", and <i>skopos</i>, "seeker" (from <i>skopeo</i>, "to watch"). The specific <i>sibilatrix</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "whistler".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This <a href="/wiki/Warbler" title="Warbler">warbler</a> is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and the entire population winters in tropical <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wood_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Phylloscopus_sibilatrix_MWNH_1671.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Phylloscopus_sibilatrix_MWNH_1671.JPG/220px-Phylloscopus_sibilatrix_MWNH_1671.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Phylloscopus_sibilatrix_MWNH_1671.JPG/330px-Phylloscopus_sibilatrix_MWNH_1671.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Phylloscopus_sibilatrix_MWNH_1671.JPG/440px-Phylloscopus_sibilatrix_MWNH_1671.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3460" data-file-height="2232" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Phylloscopus_sibilatrix_MWNH_1671.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>This is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of open but shady mature woodlands, such as <a href="/wiki/Beech" title="Beech">beech</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sessile_oak" class="mw-redirect" title="Sessile oak">sessile oak</a>, with some sparse ground cover for nesting. The dome-shaped nest is built near the ground in low shrub. 6 or 7 eggs are laid in May; there may be a second brood. Like most <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> <a href="/wiki/Warbler" title="Warbler">warblers</a>, this small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> is insectivorous.
</p><p>The main nest predators of wood warblers breeding in the primeval habitat of <a href="/wiki/Bia%C5%82owie%C5%BCa" title="Bia≈Çowie≈ºa">Bia≈Çowie≈ºa</a> Forest, Poland, are mammals, especially medium-sized carnivores, which mostly predate nests at night using sound or olfaction. Therefore, nest survival declined with nest progression likely due to increased predator detection of older and louder chicks.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wood_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The wood warbler is 11‚Äì12.5&#160;cm long, and a typical leaf warbler in appearance, green above and white below with a lemon-yellow breast. It can be distinguished from similar <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, like the <a href="/wiki/Common_chiffchaff" title="Common chiffchaff">chiffchaff</a> <i>P. collybita</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Willow_warbler" title="Willow warbler">willow warbler</a>, <i>P. trochilus</i> by its yellow supercilium, throat and upper breast, pale tertial edges, longer primary projection, and by its shorter but broader tail.
</p><p>It is a summer visitor to the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, seen from April until August. It has declined there in recent years. It  is  rare in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, where there is a very small  but apparently stable breeding population in <a href="/wiki/County_Wicklow" title="County Wicklow">County Wicklow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Various factors associated with forest structure, including slope, <a href="/wiki/Forest_cover" title="Forest cover">forest cover</a>, proportion of broad-leaf forest, canopy height and forest edge length, all influenced the occupancy rates of this declining forest species. Conservation measures are therefore required that provide and maintain the wood warblers preferred forest structure.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> There is also a preference for forest in the non-breeding season, however this habitat is declining in wintering areas such as Ghana. Despite the decline in forest habitats, there has been no change in number of wood warblers as it appears that this species can use degraded habitats, such as well-wooded farms. However, further loss of trees will likely have a negative impact on this species in the future.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Songs">Songs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wood_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Songs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It has two song types, often (but not always) given alternately; a high-pitched fluid metallic trill of increasing tempo <span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Phylloscopus_sibilatrix.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" decoding="async" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a></span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/90/Phylloscopus_sibilatrix.ogg" class="internal" title="Phylloscopus sibilatrix.ogg"><i>pit-pit-pitpitpitpt-t-t-ttt</i></a></span>&#160;<small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>¬∑<a href="/wiki/File:Phylloscopus_sibilatrix.ogg" title="File:Phylloscopus sibilatrix.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span>  lasting 2‚Äì3 seconds, and a series of 3 to 5 descending piping notes of lower pitch <i>pi√º√º-pi√º√º-pi√º√º</i>. The contact call is a soft piping note, similar to the second song type, but shorter and given singly, "<i>pi√º</i>".
</p>
