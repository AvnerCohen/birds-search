<p>The <b>Pacific golden plover</b> (<i>Pluvialis fulva</i>) is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">plover</a>. The genus name is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means relating to rain, from <i>pluvia</i>, "rain". It was believed that golden plovers flocked when rain was imminent. The species name <i>fulva</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and refers to a tawny colour.<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 23–26&#160;cm long breeding adult is spotted gold and black on the crown, and back on the wings. Its face and neck are black with a white border, and it has a black breast and a dark rump. The legs are black. In winter, the black is lost and the plover then has a yellowish face and breast, and white underparts.
</p><p>It is similar to two other golden plovers: the Eurasian and American plovers. The Pacific golden plover is smaller, slimmer and relatively longer-legged than the <a href="/wiki/European_golden_plover" title="European golden plover">European golden plover</a>, <i>Pluvialis apricaria</i>, which also has white axillary (armpit) feathers. Overall, the Pacific golden plover is found to be more similar to the <a href="/wiki/American_golden_plover" title="American golden plover">American golden plover</a>, <i>Pluvialis dominica</i>, with which it was once considered conspecific as "lesser golden plover".<sup id="cite_ref-sang_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sang-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Pacific golden plover is slimmer than the American species, has a shorter primary projection, longer legs, and is usually found to have more yellow on the back.
</p><p>This <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> forages for food on tundra, fields, beaches and <a href="/wiki/Tidal_flats" class="mw-redirect" title="Tidal flats">tidal flats</a>, usually by sight. It eats insects and <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> and some berries.
</p><p>The breeding habitat of Pacific golden plover is the Arctic <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> from northernmost <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurosiberia</a> into western Alaska. It nests on the ground in a dry open area.
</p><p>It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and winters in south Asia and Australasia. A few winter in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, USA.  In Hawaii, the bird is known as the <i>kōlea</i>, and in New Zealand it is known to Māori as <i>kuriri</i>. It is very rare <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrant</a> to western Europe. They return to the same wintering territory each year, which allowed scientists in Hawaii to attach tiny <a href="/wiki/Light_level_geolocator" title="Light level geolocator">light level geolocator</a> devices to the birds and then retrieve them the following year in the same location. This research revealed that these birds make the 4800&#160;km non-stop flight between Alaska and Hawaii in 3–4 days.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
