<p>The <b>thrush nightingale</b> (<i>Luscinia luscinia</i>), also known as the <b>sprosser</b>, is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> that was formerly classed as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family Turdidae, but is now more generally considered to be an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a>, Muscicapidae.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It, and similar small <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> species, are often called <a href="/wiki/Chat_(bird)" title="Chat (bird)">chats</a>.
</p><p>It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> insectivorous species breeding in forests in Europe and the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> and overwintering in Africa. The distribution is more northerly than the very closely related <a href="/wiki/Common_nightingale" title="Common nightingale">common nightingale</a>, <i>Luscinia megarhynchos</i>, which it closely resembles in appearance. It nests near the ground in dense undergrowth.
</p><p>The thrush nightingale is similar in size to the <a href="/wiki/European_robin" title="European robin">European robin</a>. It is plain greyish-brown above and white and greyish-brown below. Its greyer tones, giving a cloudy appearance to the underside, and lack of the <a href="/wiki/Common_nightingale" title="Common nightingale">common nightingale</a>'s obvious rufous tail side patches are the clearest plumage differences from that species. Sexes are similar. It has a similar but more powerful song than that of the nightingale.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thrush_nightingale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Nightingale" is derived from "night", and the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>galan</i>, "to sing".<sup id="cite_ref-OED_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OEDgale_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OEDgale-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The genus name <i>Luscinia</i> is  <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for the common nightingale.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thrush_nightingale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Luscinia_luscinia_-adult_and_juvenile_-2008.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Luscinia_luscinia_-adult_and_juvenile_-2008.jpg/220px-Luscinia_luscinia_-adult_and_juvenile_-2008.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="234" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Luscinia_luscinia_-adult_and_juvenile_-2008.jpg/330px-Luscinia_luscinia_-adult_and_juvenile_-2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Luscinia_luscinia_-adult_and_juvenile_-2008.jpg/440px-Luscinia_luscinia_-adult_and_juvenile_-2008.jpg 2x" data-file-width="738" data-file-height="784" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Luscinia_luscinia_-adult_and_juvenile_-2008.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A singing male above and a juvenile below</div></div></div>
<p>An adult thrush nightingale is about 16 centimetres (6.3&#160;in) long with a wingspan of approximately 18 centimetres (7.1&#160;in). The head, nape and the whole of the upper parts of the thrush nightingale are dark brown with a slight olive tinge. The colour is much deeper than that of the nightingale and is not at all rufous. The upper <a href="/wiki/Covert_feather" title="Covert feather">tail-coverts</a> are less olivaceous and the tail feathers are dark rufous-brown. The lores and ear-coverts are brownish-black and the chin and throat are pale buff or whitish, mottled with brown, and are paler in colour than the nightingale. The sides of the throat are spotted brown and the pale feathers of the breast have brown central bands giving the breast a mottled appearance. The under tail-coverts are buff, sometimes barred or marked with brown. The wing feathers and wing-coverts are dark brown and less rufous than the nightingale. The beak, legs and feet are brown and the irises are dark brown. The sexes are similar to each other in appearance and the juveniles are darker and more mottled. There is a single <a href="/wiki/Moulting" title="Moulting">moult</a> in July and August at the end of the breeding season.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thrush_nightingale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sonogram_L_luscinia_L_megarhynchos.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Sonogram_L_luscinia_L_megarhynchos.png/220px-Sonogram_L_luscinia_L_megarhynchos.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="240" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Sonogram_L_luscinia_L_megarhynchos.png/330px-Sonogram_L_luscinia_L_megarhynchos.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Sonogram_L_luscinia_L_megarhynchos.png/440px-Sonogram_L_luscinia_L_megarhynchos.png 2x" data-file-width="2200" data-file-height="2400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sonogram_L_luscinia_L_megarhynchos.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The sonograms of <a href="/wiki/Luscinia_luscinia" class="mw-redirect" title="Luscinia luscinia">Luscinia luscinia</a> and  <a href="/wiki/Luscinia_megarhynchos" class="mw-redirect" title="Luscinia megarhynchos">Luscinia megarhynchos</a> singing help to distinguish these two species by voice definitely.</div></div></div>
<p>The male's <span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Luscinia_luscinia.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" decoding="async" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a></span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/13/Luscinia_luscinia.ogg" class="internal" title="Luscinia luscinia.ogg">song</a></span>&#160;<small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:Luscinia_luscinia.ogg" title="File:Luscinia luscinia.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span> is loud, with a range of whistles, trills and clicks and includes a flute-like "<i>pioo</i>" with a pure bell-like tone. It is sometimes interrupted by a rasping "<i>dserr</i>" sound and is rather solemn as compared to that of the nightingale. The song does not have that bird's loud whistling crescendo and is quite distinctive. It is also sometimes sung in the bird's winter quarters. The call-note "<i>whit</i>" resembles that of the nightingale but is higher pitched and more abrupt.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thrush_nightingale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The thrush nightingale is a migrant species. It breeds in eastern Europe and the western part of temperate Asia. Its northern limit of its summer range extends to <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltic States">Baltic States</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Karelia" title="Republic of Karelia">Republic of Karelia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kostroma" title="Kostroma">Kostroma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vologda" title="Vologda">Vologda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perm" title="Perm">Perm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkestan" title="Turkestan">Turkestan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Altai_Mountains" title="Altai Mountains">Altai</a>. The southern limit extends from <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>, through <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>. It overwinters in Africa south of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It is an occasional visitor to the British Isles.
<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In its breeding range, the thrush nightingale is found in damp deciduous woodland typically with <a href="/wiki/Alder" title="Alder">alder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a>. It favours thick undergrowth with <a href="/wiki/Bramble" title="Bramble">brambles</a>, dense shrubs and tangled vegetation in swampy places and near water. In its winter quarters it is found in dense patches of thorn bush, especially in valley bottoms near water courses, and sometimes in thick vegetation at the edge of woodland.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The bird is a host of the <a href="/wiki/Acanthocephala" title="Acanthocephala">acanthocephalan</a> intestinal parasite <i><a href="/wiki/Apororhynchus_silesiacus" class="mw-redirect" title="Apororhynchus silesiacus">Apororhynchus silesiacus</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dimitrova_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dimitrova-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thrush_nightingale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Luscinia_luscinia_-Gugny,_Biebrza_National_Park,_Poland-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Luscinia_luscinia_-Gugny%2C_Biebrza_National_Park%2C_Poland-8.jpg/220px-Luscinia_luscinia_-Gugny%2C_Biebrza_National_Park%2C_Poland-8.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Luscinia_luscinia_-Gugny%2C_Biebrza_National_Park%2C_Poland-8.jpg/330px-Luscinia_luscinia_-Gugny%2C_Biebrza_National_Park%2C_Poland-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Luscinia_luscinia_-Gugny%2C_Biebrza_National_Park%2C_Poland-8.jpg/440px-Luscinia_luscinia_-Gugny%2C_Biebrza_National_Park%2C_Poland-8.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4224" data-file-height="3168" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Luscinia_luscinia_-Gugny,_Biebrza_National_Park,_Poland-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In Poland</div></div></div>

<p>The thrush nightingale feeds chiefly on the ground taking <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a> and the adults, <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pupa" title="Pupa">pupae</a> of insects such as <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>. In the autumn, the berries of <a href="/wiki/Ribes" title="Ribes">currants</a> (<i>Ribes</i> spp.) and <a href="/wiki/Sambucus" title="Sambucus">elders</a> (<i>Sambucus</i> spp.) are also eaten.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Before crossing the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> on its migration, thrush nightingales build up their fat reserves. It has been found experimentally that <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth&#39;s magnetic field">magnetic cues</a> may stimulate the birds to do this. A simulation of the magnetic field found in northern Egypt encouraged birds preparing to migrate from Sweden to further build up their body fat.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The thrush nightingale breeds in damp forests, nesting on the ground, often in the middle of a bed of <a href="/wiki/Stinging_nettle" class="mw-redirect" title="Stinging nettle">stinging nettles</a> (<i>Urtica dioica</i>). The nest rests on a platform of dead leaves and is composed of dead grass stalks, bents (<i><a href="/wiki/Agrostis" title="Agrostis">Agrostis</a></i> spp.), sedges and stems, lined with finer material. It is built by the female which lays four or five (occasionally six) eggs. These are a milky-blue colour, usually plain but sometimes with a slight speckling of rusty-brown and measure an average of 21.7 by 16.2 millimetres (0.85&#160;in ×&#160;0.64&#160;in). The hen <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" class="mw-redirect" title="Avian incubation">incubates</a> the eggs which hatch in about thirteen days. The young are fed by both parents and <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> when about eleven days old, but are not fully independent for another twelve days or so.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thrush_nightingale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/BirdLife_International" title="BirdLife International">BirdLife International</a> estimates that there are between 11 and 20 million thrush nightingales in Europe and that, as Europe forms somewhere between 50% and 74% of the bird's global range, the total world population may be between 15 and 41 million individuals. In Europe, the population seems to be increasingly slightly. The bird is considered to be of <i><a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a></i> by the International Union for Conservation of Nature <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BI_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BI-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
