<p><b>Kittlitz's plover</b> (<i>Charadrius pecuarius</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Shorebird" class="mw-redirect" title="Shorebird">shorebird</a> (35-40 g) in the family <a href="/wiki/Charadriidae" title="Charadriidae">Charadriidae</a> that breeds near coastal and inland saltmarshes, sandy or muddy riverbanks or alkaline grasslands with short vegetation. It is native to much of <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> Delta and <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>. It is thought to be mainly monogamous and has <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(biology)" title="Polymorphism (biology)">monomorphic</a> plumage.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kittlitz%27s_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Charadrius_pecuarius_1832.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Charadrius_pecuarius_1832.jpg/220px-Charadrius_pecuarius_1832.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="246" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Charadrius_pecuarius_1832.jpg/330px-Charadrius_pecuarius_1832.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Charadrius_pecuarius_1832.jpg/440px-Charadrius_pecuarius_1832.jpg 2x" data-file-width="626" data-file-height="700" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Charadrius_pecuarius_1832.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration by <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_von_Kittlitz" title="Heinrich von Kittlitz">Heinrich von Kittlitz</a>, after whom the species is named</div></div></div>
<p>Kittlitz's plover is a small <a href="/wiki/Shorebird" class="mw-redirect" title="Shorebird">shorebird</a> weighing between 35-40 grams.<sup id="cite_ref-hockey_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hockey-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Both male and female have a black bill, dark brown eyes with black eyelids and black legs, although sometimes the legs can appear greenish or grey. During the breeding season, the male has a white forehead, a blackish bar followed by a narrow white bar on the forecrown, whereas the rest of the crown is brown with sandy tips to the feathers. A black stripe - separated from the crown by a white superciliary stripe - runs from the bill through the eye and extends to the side of the neck forming a collar across the upper mantle. The mantle is dark grey-brown, the other upperparts are sooty brown with feathers having sandy rufous margins. The face, the chin and the upper throat are white and the rest of the underparts are yellowish, the belly is pale. Kittlitz's Plover has blackish central tail feathers that get continuously lighter towards the side of the tail; the outer one or two pairs are completely white. 
The female <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> is similar to the male's, only the black band across the forecrown is narrower. 
The non-breeding plumage does not differ much from the breeding one, the eye stripe is browner, the frontal bar is missing and the underparts are a lot paler. In general, adult wing length lies between 100–110&#160;mm, adult bill length between 15–23&#160;mm and adult <a href="/wiki/Bird_anatomy" title="Bird anatomy">tarsus</a> length between 26–33&#160;mm.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> 
Juvenile Kittlitz's plovers appear similar to adults, however the black face marks are lacking, the upperparts are brown, the hindneck collar is buff and the underparts are white.<sup id="cite_ref-zef_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zef-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Distribution.2C_movement_and_habitat"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution,_movement_and_habitat">Distribution, movement and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kittlitz%27s_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution, movement and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kittlitz%27s_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kittlitz's plover is distributed throughout most of sub-Saharan Africa, but is also native to <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nile_River_Delta" class="mw-redirect" title="Nile River Delta">Nile River Delta</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-urban_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urban-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> A genetic study reported genetic differentiation between Madagascar and the mainland population.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It is common in South Africa, however rarer in arid regions of <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a>. In Namibia, it mostly inhabits coastal regions, highlands and Ovamboland.<sup id="cite_ref-tree2_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tree2-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It is more scarce in the southern lowveld of South Africa and patchy in the interior of <a href="/wiki/KwaZulu-Natal" title="KwaZulu-Natal">KwaZulu-Natal</a> and the eastern Cape. Usually it avoids mountains or densely wooded areas.<sup id="cite_ref-tree2_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tree2-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hockey_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hockey-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The Malagasy population might result from a relatively recent immigration from mainland Africa, whereas the <a href="/wiki/Madagascar_plover" class="mw-redirect" title="Madagascar plover">Madagascar plover</a> (<i>Charadrius thoracius</i>) might have evolved from an earlier Kittlitz's plover population. The two species are clearly distinct and can not interbreed. 
Birds from Madagascar are in general smaller than birds from continental Africa. Kittlitz's plovers are heavier and have longer wings in South Africa compared to Madagascar, whereas Egyptian specimens have longer wings and shorter tarsi than in Madagascar.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-zef_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zef-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> However, they are still regarded as the same species in the absence of apparent plumage difference.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
In Madagascar, Kittlitz's plover is both a breeding resident and intra-island migrant<sup id="cite_ref-zef_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zef-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and has been recorded mostly below 950 m,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> although it has been recorded at up to 1,400 m too.<sup id="cite_ref-zef_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zef-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Movements">Movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kittlitz%27s_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Movements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kittlitz%27s_Plover_-_Mara_-_Kenya_S4E9279_(15456500187).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Kittlitz%27s_Plover_-_Mara_-_Kenya_S4E9279_%2815456500187%29.jpg/220px-Kittlitz%27s_Plover_-_Mara_-_Kenya_S4E9279_%2815456500187%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Kittlitz%27s_Plover_-_Mara_-_Kenya_S4E9279_%2815456500187%29.jpg/330px-Kittlitz%27s_Plover_-_Mara_-_Kenya_S4E9279_%2815456500187%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Kittlitz%27s_Plover_-_Mara_-_Kenya_S4E9279_%2815456500187%29.jpg/440px-Kittlitz%27s_Plover_-_Mara_-_Kenya_S4E9279_%2815456500187%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="683" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kittlitz%27s_Plover_-_Mara_-_Kenya_S4E9279_(15456500187).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In the <a href="/wiki/Maasai_Mara" title="Maasai Mara">Maasai Mara</a>, Kenya</div></div></div>
<p>The African populations are partially sedentary in coastal areas, but mostly <a href="/wiki/Nomadic" class="mw-redirect" title="Nomadic">nomadic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> with their movements varying between years in response to rainfall.<sup id="cite_ref-delany_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-delany-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> There is limited evidence for comparable variation in <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>. In the lower Mangoky basin for instance, they are thought to be resident, whereas at the Lake Tsimanampetsotsa, ringed adults have been recorded to have moved 113&#160;km to Ifaty.<sup id="cite_ref-zef_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zef-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kittlitz%27s_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kittlitz's plover can be both found in inland and coastal regions. It favours open habitats at low elevation, often dry ground (open dry mud) with very short grass, mostly in close proximity to water. It breeds at lakes, <a href="/wiki/Alkaline" class="mw-redirect" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a> grasslands, <a href="/wiki/Lagoons" class="mw-redirect" title="Lagoons">lagoons</a>, rivers, tidal mudflats or inland <a href="/wiki/Saltmarsh" class="mw-redirect" title="Saltmarsh">saltmarshes</a> and artificial water bodies including commercial salt pans,<sup id="cite_ref-urban_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urban-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-zef_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zef-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hockey_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hockey-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> but generally avoids sandy or rocky beaches. In the eastern and western Cape, some populations move to <a href="/wiki/Estuarine" class="mw-redirect" title="Estuarine">estuarine</a> mudflats during the winter, probably because of reduced competition from <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrants</a> but also to avoid food <a href="/wiki/Shortage" title="Shortage">shortage</a> due to harsh interior winters.<sup id="cite_ref-hockey_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hockey-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> 
They are strictly terrestrial, feeding, nesting, <a href="/wiki/Preening_(bird)" title="Preening (bird)">preening</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roosting" class="mw-redirect" title="Roosting">roosting</a> solely on the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-zef_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zef-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kittlitz%27s_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Kittlitz's Plover's calls include ‘pipip’, ‘towhit’, ‘tit-peep’, ‘trit-tritritritrit’, ‘perrup’ and ‘kich-kich-kich’. They give alarm calls when in danger or when <a href="/wiki/Flushed" class="mw-redirect" title="Flushed">flushed</a>, which include ‘chirrt’,<sup id="cite_ref-urban_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urban-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> hard ‘trip’, ‘tric’, ‘prrrt’ or plaintive ‘pip-ip’.<sup id="cite_ref-hockey_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hockey-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  
During fights, the males give a buzzy call in aggressive or <a href="/wiki/Courtship" title="Courtship">courtship</a> chases,<sup id="cite_ref-hockey_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hockey-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> 
When male or female are feigning injury, they give a ‘cheep-cheep’ and when a parent is inviting the young to brood, it gives a ‘chip-chip’ and the young are being warned with a ‘trr-trr’.<sup id="cite_ref-urban_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urban-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The call of a young is a thin ‘peep’.<sup id="cite_ref-hockey_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hockey-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kittlitz%27s_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kittlitz's plover forages in a typical run-stop-search fashion, meaning it runs around, stops suddenly to peck at an item and then continues running.<sup id="cite_ref-hockey2_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hockey2-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Sometimes, an individual stands erect with one foot vibrating on the surface (foot-trembling), then lunges forward to catch any prey that has been attracted to the surface.<sup id="cite_ref-tree_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tree-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hockey_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hockey-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This behaviour has not been reported in <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>.
They feed day and night (on moonlit nights until 11PM) often in groups of 2-5 individuals or in small mixed <a href="/wiki/Flocks" class="mw-redirect" title="Flocks">flocks</a> together with other small <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">waders</a> like the <i><a href="/wiki/Calidris" title="Calidris">Calidris</a></i> species. When foraging, they can show an aggressive behaviour, especially towards <a href="/wiki/Conspecifics" class="mw-redirect" title="Conspecifics">conspecifics</a> and species with similar diets, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Curlew_sandpiper" title="Curlew sandpiper">curlew sandpiper</a> (<i>Calidris ferruginea</i>) or <a href="/wiki/Common_ringed_plover" title="Common ringed plover">common ringed plover</a> (<i>Charadrius hiaticula</i>). The aggression increases with density and is particularly high in the winter months before breeding.<sup id="cite_ref-hockey2_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hockey2-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> 
They mainly feed on <a href="/wiki/Insects" class="mw-redirect" title="Insects">insects</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Beetles" class="mw-redirect" title="Beetles">beetles</a>, insect <a href="/wiki/Larvae" class="mw-redirect" title="Larvae">larvae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spiders" class="mw-redirect" title="Spiders">spiders</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Crustaceans" class="mw-redirect" title="Crustaceans">crustaceans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Molluscs" class="mw-redirect" title="Molluscs">molluscs</a>. Most of their food is small, but they were recorded to have taken a <a href="/wiki/Cricket_(insect)" title="Cricket (insect)">cricket</a> that was about 40&#160;mm long.<sup id="cite_ref-tree_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tree-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-urban_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urban-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kittlitz%27s_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The species is social in the non-breeding season and stays in small flocks of about 20 individuals, however it has also been reported in larger flocks of 100-300 individuals during migratory movements.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-urban_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urban-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hockey_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hockey-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding_and_territories">Breeding and territories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kittlitz%27s_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Breeding and territories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kittlitz's Plover has a flexible breeding system and shorter duration of pair bond than other plover species (ZS and TS, unpublished data). It is thought to be mainly <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" class="mw-redirect" title="Monogamous">monogamous</a> and the pairing usually occurs around 2–4 weeks before the couple occupies their territory. <a href="/wiki/Copulation_(zoology)" title="Copulation (zoology)">Copulation</a> and feeding both take place in the territories, which can occupy 3600-4200m². The parents are highly defensive of their territories until the chicks hatch. When intruders invade their territory representing a threat, the parents run after them, stopping shortly in front of them, taking on an upright posture with their legs almost straight and their head up.<sup id="cite_ref-urban_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urban-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Courtship">Courtship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kittlitz%27s_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Courtship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The initial <a href="/wiki/Courtship" title="Courtship">courtship</a> takes place on ‘neutral’ ground.<sup id="cite_ref-tree_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tree-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and consists of a scrape-ceremony, where one bird is placing its breast on the sand, then rotating around on the breast, whilst having its tail raised and kicking out sand with back- and forward movements of its legs. 
Usually, the male takes the initiative of making several <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest#Scrape" title="Bird nest">scrapes</a> (although sometimes both parents take turns in making them)<sup id="cite_ref-urban_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urban-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and then the female probably chooses the final nest site.<sup id="cite_ref-clark_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clark-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Both parents pick up pebbles or break off bits of dead vegetation as nest material to line the nest with.<sup id="cite_ref-zef2_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zef2-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nesting_and_incubation">Nesting and incubation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kittlitz%27s_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Nesting and incubation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Kittlitz's Plover is a ground-nesting <a href="/wiki/Shorebird" class="mw-redirect" title="Shorebird">shorebird</a>, that breeds throughout the whole year, although there are varying peak seasons in different geographic locations. Copulation usually occurs 6–11 days before egg-laying on or near the nest scrape.<sup id="cite_ref-clark_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clark-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The simple nest scrape is usually made 50–100 m away from water on open, dry ground at low elevation, from where it is easy to spot intruders. It is about 10–15&#160;cm in diameter and is lined with shells, pebbles, animal dung and fragments of vegetation. The Kittlitz's Plovers usually nest solitary or in loose flocks with their nests being mostly more than 40 m apart, however they can be sometimes as close as 8 m.<sup id="cite_ref-tree_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tree-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hockey2_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hockey2-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Sometimes the old scrape may be reused, probably by the same pair.
</p><p>Kittlitz's Plovers lays 1-3 eggs (usually 2) at 1-2 day intervals.<sup id="cite_ref-urban_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urban-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> They eggs are oval, dark grey-maroon with black speckles giving and overall sand-coloured or light brown background.<sup id="cite_ref-zef_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zef-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Incubation starts once the clutch is completed <sup id="cite_ref-tree_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tree-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and is carried out by both parents - usually by the male at night and by the female during the day - for a period of 21–27 days.<sup id="cite_ref-zef2_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zef2-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> When a parent leaves the nest during the day or when the nest is approached by a predator, the parent usually covers the eggs up to two thirds or completely with sand within 3-90 s by moving several times around the nest kicking its feet alternately. Newly or partly hatched chicks also get covered. Sometimes the eggs might be left unattended up to 5–7 hours. When the parent returns to the nest, it uncovers the nest before pursuing incubation.<sup id="cite_ref-urban_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urban-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parental_care">Parental care</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kittlitz%27s_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Parental care">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Once the young have hatched, both parents remove eggshells from nest site. Newly hatched young are <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">downy</a>, their upper parts are grey to white, their back is mottled with a dark median stripe and their underparts are white.<sup id="cite_ref-zef2_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zef2-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The chicks leave the nest within a few hours of <a href="/wiki/Hatching" title="Hatching">hatching</a> or may be <a href="/wiki/Brooded" class="mw-redirect" title="Brooded">brooded</a> on the nest for up to 24 hours. The chicks are <a href="/wiki/Precocial" class="mw-redirect" title="Precocial">precocial</a> and can feed for themselves from 24 hours after hatching; one parent usually leads them to foraging areas up to 1&#160;km away from the nest. Kittlitz's plovers exhibit uniparental care, where only one parent (either male or female) stays with the young and broods them in frequent intervals until they can fly at 26–32 days. The parent calls the chicks when inviting them to brood or when danger is present. The parents actively defend their young against conspecifics or intruders by a) feigning injury to attract more attention on themselves by lying flat on the ground, flapping their wings in a helpless manner, fanning their tail or running away or towards intruder, b) false brooding or c) running with head held low, tail drooped and spread wings.   
Chicks and juveniles obtain adult plumage after one year, and some may start breeding at that age.<sup id="cite_ref-zef2_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zef2-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-urban_5-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urban-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kittlitz%27s_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_and_trends">Status and trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kittlitz%27s_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Status and trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> classifies Kittlitz's plover as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>. The species is abundant in Africa and locally common in Madagascar, and it has a very wide range. Although not much is known about population trends due to difficulties in assessing the impact of habitat modifications, any declines appear to be below the threshold that would be identified as threatening.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kittlitz's Plover is locally very abundant in East, South and Central Africa, with a population size estimated at 100,000-400,000 individuals. West Africa is estimated to sustain 20,000-50,000 individuals. In Madagascar, the population is estimated at 10,000-20,000 individuals, making the species locally common.<sup id="cite_ref-delany_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-delany-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats">Threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kittlitz%27s_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kittlitz's plover is mainly threatened by habitat loss due to <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetland</a> degradation.<sup id="cite_ref-ntiamoa_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ntiamoa-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> For example, one of the key wetland sites in southern Africa, <a href="/wiki/Walvis_Bay" title="Walvis Bay">Walvis Bay</a> in <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a>, has been subject to degradation and destruction due to the building of roads, disturbance from tourists and wetland reclamation for the development of suburbs and ports.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In Ghana, wetlands are threatened by coastal erosion and developments that include drainage and wetland reclamation <sup id="cite_ref-ntiamoa_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ntiamoa-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The fraction of the population migrating via the east Atlantic is vulnerable to <a href="/wiki/Avian_malaria" title="Avian malaria">avian malaria</a> and would be greatly threatened in case of an outbreak of this disease. The same applies to <a href="/wiki/Avian_botulism" title="Avian botulism">avian botulism</a>, which the Kittlitz's plover is susceptible to.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
