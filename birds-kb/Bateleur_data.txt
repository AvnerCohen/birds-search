<p>The <b>Bateleur</b> (<i>Terathopius ecaudatus</i>) is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagle</a> in the bird family <i>Accipitridae</i> which also includes many other diurnal <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptors</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Buzzard" title="Buzzard">buzzards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kite_(bird)" title="Kite (bird)">kites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harrier_(bird)" title="Harrier (bird)">harriers</a>. It is the only member of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i>Terathopius</i> and probably the origin of the "<a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe_bird" title="Zimbabwe bird" class="mw-redirect">Zimbabwe bird</a>", national emblem of <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Bateleur eagle is a common resident species of the open <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a> country in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>, though it also occurs in south-west <a href="/wiki/Arabia" title="Arabia" class="mw-redirect">Arabia</a>. Total distribution size is estimated at 28,000,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>. It nests in <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">trees</a>, laying a single <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">egg</a> which is incubated by the female for 42 to 43 days, with a further 90 to 125 days until fledging. Bateleurs pair for life, and will use the same nest for a number of years. Unpaired birds, presumably from a previous clutch, will sometimes help at the nest.</p>

<p>Global population is estimated at 10,000 - 100,000 individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Bateleur is a colourful <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> with a very short tail (<i>ecaudatus</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for tailless) which makes it unmistakable in flight. The adult is 55 to 70&#160;cm (21.5 to 27.5 in) long with a 175&#160;cm (5.75&#160;ft) wingspan. Weight is 1.8-2.9&#160;kg (4-6.5&#160;lbs).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The male has black plumage except for the <a href="/wiki/Chestnut_(color)" title="Chestnut (color)">chestnut</a> mantle and tail, grey shoulders, and red facial skin, bill and legs.</p>
<p>The female is similar to the male except that she is slightly bigger and has grey rather than black secondary flight <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a>. Immature birds are brown with white dappling and have greenish facial skin. It takes them seven or eight years to reach full maturity.</p>
<p>The eagle hunts over a territory of 250 square miles (650&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) a day. The prey of this raptor is mostly <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Dove" title="Dove" class="mw-redirect">pigeons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sandgrouse" title="Sandgrouse">sandgrouse</a>, and also small <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>; it also takes carrion.</p>
<p>The Bateleur is generally silent, but on occasions it produces a variety of <a href="/wiki/Bird_song" title="Bird song" class="mw-redirect">barks and screams</a>.</p>

<p>Bateleur eagles are among a group of raptors that secrete a clear, salty fluid from their <a href="/wiki/Nares" title="Nares" class="mw-redirect">nares</a> whilst eating. According to Schmidt-Nielson's (1964) hypothesis, this is due to the general necessity for birds to use an extrarenal mechanism of salt secretion to aid water reabsorption.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"<i>Bateleur</i>" is <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> for "<a href="/wiki/Tightrope_walking" title="Tightrope walking">tight-rope walker</a>". This name describes the bird’s characteristic habit of tipping the ends of its <a href="/wiki/Wing" title="Wing">wings</a> when flying, as if catching its balance.</p>
<p>In some countries, outside of its natural distribution, the Bateleur is occasionally known as the "Conifer Eagle" or even "Pine Eagle", since its feathers somewhat resemble a <a href="/wiki/Conifer_cone" title="Conifer cone">conifer cone</a> when it fluffs itself up.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from November 2009">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 2009, the Bateleur was placed in the <a href="/wiki/Near_Threatened" title="Near Threatened">Near-Threatened</a> <a href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN" class="mw-redirect">IUCN</a> Red List Category due to loss of <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pesticides" title="Pesticides" class="mw-redirect">pesticides</a>, capture for international trade and nest disturbance.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Decline of the species is suspected to have been moderately rapid over the past three generations. Current <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_management" title="Wildlife management">conservation</a> efforts are unknown.</p>
