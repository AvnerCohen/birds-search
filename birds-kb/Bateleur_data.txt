<p>The <b>Bateleur</b> (<i>Terathopius ecaudatus</i>) is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagle</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a>. Its closest relatives are the snake eagles. It is the only member of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i>Terathopius</i> and may be the origin of the "<a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe_Bird" title="Zimbabwe Bird">Zimbabwe Bird</a>", the national emblem of <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is endemic to Africa and small parts of Arabia. "<i>Bateleur</i>" is <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> for "street performer".<sup id="cite_ref-dictionary.reverso.net_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dictionary.reverso.net-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bateleur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Terathopius-ecaudatus-01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Terathopius-ecaudatus-01.jpg/220px-Terathopius-ecaudatus-01.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Terathopius-ecaudatus-01.jpg/330px-Terathopius-ecaudatus-01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Terathopius-ecaudatus-01.jpg/440px-Terathopius-ecaudatus-01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Terathopius-ecaudatus-01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Close-up of head</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Terathopius_ecaudatus_Flickr1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Terathopius_ecaudatus_Flickr1.jpg/220px-Terathopius_ecaudatus_Flickr1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="130" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Terathopius_ecaudatus_Flickr1.jpg/330px-Terathopius_ecaudatus_Flickr1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Terathopius_ecaudatus_Flickr1.jpg/440px-Terathopius_ecaudatus_Flickr1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2075" data-file-height="1228" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Terathopius_ecaudatus_Flickr1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A captive immature bateleur</div></div></div>
<p>The average adult is 55 to 70&#160;cm (22 to 28&#160;in) long with a 186&#160;cm (6&#160;ft 1&#160;in) wingspan. The wing chord averages approximately 51&#160;cm (20&#160;in). Adult weight is typically 2 to 2.6&#160;kg (4&#160;lb 7&#160;oz to 5&#160;lb 12&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Bateleur is a colourful <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> with a bushy head and very short tail (<i>ecaudatus</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for tail-less) which, together with its white underwing coverts, makes it unmistakable in flight. The tail is so small the bird's legs protrude slightly beyond the tail during flight. The Bateleur is sexually dimorphic; both adults have black plumage, a <a href="/wiki/Chestnut_(color)" title="Chestnut (color)">chestnut</a> mantle and tail, grey shoulders, tawny wing coverts, and red facial skin, bill and legs. The female additionally has tawny secondary wing feathers. Less commonly, the mantle may be white.<sup id="cite_ref-Newmans_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newmans-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Immature birds are brown with white dappling and have greenish blue-grey facial skin.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It takes them seven or eight years to reach full maturity.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bateleur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bateleur_des_savanes.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Bateleur_des_savanes.jpg/220px-Bateleur_des_savanes.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Bateleur_des_savanes.jpg/330px-Bateleur_des_savanes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Bateleur_des_savanes.jpg/440px-Bateleur_des_savanes.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5184" data-file-height="3888" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bateleur_des_savanes.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>savannah bateleur in bénin</div></div></div><p>The range of the Bateleur spreads across Mauritania, Senegal, Mali, Guinea, Sudan, Ethiopia, Somalia, Namibia, Botswana, South Africa, Saudi Arabia, Yemen, Côte d'Ivoire, Kenya, Nigeria, Zambia, Zimbabwe, Tanzania.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The bird's range has diminished significantly in recent decades, possibly due to poisoning,<sup id="cite_ref-Allan_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allan-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and as such has been confined mostly to conservation areas such as national wildlife parks.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 2012, a juvenile Bateleur was seen in Algeciras in southern Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> This was the first European record for the Bateleur.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bateleur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This eagle is a solitary, tree-nesting species with a large home range.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bateleur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Bateleur is a common to fairly common resident or nomadic<sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> bird of the open <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a> country and woodland (<a href="/wiki/Thornveld" class="mw-redirect" title="Thornveld">thornveld</a>) within <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>; it also occurs in south-western <a href="/wiki/Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabia">Arabia</a>. Found in closed-canopy savannah woodland habitats, including Acacia savannah, Mopane and miombo woodlands. It is commonest in broad-leaved woodland in the Okavango Delta. It occurs rarely in heavily forested, mountainous or largely treeless habitats. In Namibia, it is often found over tall woodland near drainage lines, and ephemeral rivers in north-eastern Namibia and within the more arid Etosha National Park.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bateleur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Bateleur is diurnal, foraging over a huge range (55–200&#160;km²)<sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and hunting over a territory of approximately 250 square miles (650&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) a day. Bateleurs are hunters and scavengers, they will attack other species for food and will scavenge carrion. The bird is adept at finding smaller carcasses before most other scavengers. The Bateleur will hunt birds and their eggs (mainly doves and pigeons), small reptiles, small mammals (like rodents, genets and mongooses) and insects.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Its prey is often stolen by the <a href="/wiki/Tawny_eagle" title="Tawny eagle">tawny eagle</a> (<i>Aquila rapax</i>), and the Bateleur may attempt kleptoparasitism of white-backed vultures.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bateleur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Bateleur is generally silent, but can produce a variety of <a href="/wiki/Bird_song" class="mw-redirect" title="Bird song">barks and screams</a>. The bird spends a considerable amount of time on the wing, particularly in low-altitude glides.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> "<i>Bateleur</i>" is <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> for "tumbler".  This name implies the bird's characteristic habit of rocking its <a href="/wiki/Wing" title="Wing">wings</a> or tilting action from side to side when gliding, as if catching its balance.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The wings are held in a slightly bent, deep 'V', position and fast flight with. Bateleurs hunt from swift, direct gliding flight across country, or in wide sweeping circles.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The Bateleur is a territorial bird and will defend its territory by means of an aggressive attack flight pattern shown to intruding conspecifics.<sup id="cite_ref-Watson_1989_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson_1989-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Intruders to whom this behavior is displayed always submit and submission is shown by retreating to a safe upper boundary (elevation). Males and females both display this behavior in all stages of the breeding cycle. This behavior is mainly shown to members of the same sex and particularly to non-adults, as it is thought that they may have a greater ability to take over another bird's territory (having greater competitive ability for limited food resources).<sup id="cite_ref-Watson_1989_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson_1989-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In the wild Bateleurs are shy of man and sensitive to disturbance at the nest, easily abandoning the structure.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In captivity, however, they becomes unusually tame.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Bateleur eagles are among a group of raptors that secrete a clear, salty fluid from their <a href="/wiki/Nares" class="mw-redirect" title="Nares">nares</a> whilst eating. According to Schmidt-Nielson's (1964) hypothesis, this is due to the general necessity for birds to use an extrarenal mechanism of salt secretion to aid water reabsorption.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sunning">Sunning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bateleur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sunning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bateleur_(Terathopius_ecaudatus)_male_(6854281398).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Bateleur_%28Terathopius_ecaudatus%29_male_%286854281398%29.jpg/220px-Bateleur_%28Terathopius_ecaudatus%29_male_%286854281398%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Bateleur_%28Terathopius_ecaudatus%29_male_%286854281398%29.jpg/330px-Bateleur_%28Terathopius_ecaudatus%29_male_%286854281398%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Bateleur_%28Terathopius_ecaudatus%29_male_%286854281398%29.jpg/440px-Bateleur_%28Terathopius_ecaudatus%29_male_%286854281398%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4320" data-file-height="3240" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bateleur_(Terathopius_ecaudatus)_male_(6854281398).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bateleur sunbathing by a waterhole</div></div></div>
<p>Bateleurs frequently enter water-bodies for a bath and then open their wings to often sunbathe. Standing upright and holding their wings straight out to the sides and tipped vertically, a classic 'phoenix' pose as they turn to follow the sun.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Bateleurs will lie on the ground with their wings spread, exposing the feathers to direct sunlight, warming the oils in the feathers. The bird will then spread the oils with its beak to improve its aerodynamics. Bateleurs may also be seen "praying" allowing ants to crawl over the wings and feathers, collecting bits of food, dead feather and skin material. When covered in ants, the Bateleur then ruffles its feathers, startling the ants, which react by secreting formic acid as self-defense. This in turn kills the ticks and fleas, ridding the host of its parasites.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bateleur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bateleurs are monogamous and breed from September–May in West Africa, throughout the year in East Africa and December–August in southern Africa, with a peak from January to April.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Bateleurs are long-lived species, slow-maturing, slow-breeding species,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> laying only one egg at a time<sup id="cite_ref-Watson_1990_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson_1990-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> (this is because more parental care can be invested per offspring resulting in greater survival).<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Nests are stick platforms placed below the canopy of large trees such as <a href="/wiki/Senegalia_nigrescens" title="Senegalia nigrescens">Senegalia nigrescens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Both parents put equal amounts of care into the young and breeding failures are due to predation or a failure to lay the egg. Incubation lasts for 55 days and annual mean production is 0.47 chicks per breeding pair per year.<sup id="cite_ref-Watson_1990_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson_1990-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bateleur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Bateleur is considered to have a lifespan about 27 years. The annual adult survival rate is estimated at 95%, while the annual juvenile survival rate is estimated at 75%. Average sex ratios remain 1:1.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Global population is estimated in the tens of thousands at 10,000 to 100,000 individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Like the <a href="/wiki/Martial_eagle" title="Martial eagle">martial eagle</a>, the Bateleur is reasonably common in conservation areas and scarce elsewhere.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In 2009, the Bateleur was placed in the <a href="/wiki/Near_threatened" class="mw-redirect" title="Near threatened">near threatened</a> <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> Red List category due to loss of <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pesticides" class="mw-redirect" title="Pesticides">pesticides</a>, capture for international trade and nest disturbance.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>  Decline of the species and the reduction in range is suspected to have been moderately rapid over the past three generations. In South Africa and Namibia the Bateleur has been labelled as a "Vulnerable/Endangered" species, most likely from being trapped, for its feathers to be used in medicine by traditional healers for predicting future events.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The population has also decreased due to it feasting on poisoned animal carcasses being left out for other species. The Bateleur's wide foraging areas and their ability to locate very small pieces of carrion, makes them highly susceptible to poison-laced carcasses even from a small proportion of farmers who use poisons.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> No large scale actions are underway but they are possibly protected in Yemen as an endangered species.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It is proposed to implement education and awareness campaigns across its range to reduce the use of poisoned baits. Regular population monitoring is being carried out.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
