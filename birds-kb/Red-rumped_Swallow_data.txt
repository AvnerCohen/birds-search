<p>The <b>red-rumped swallow</b> (<i>Cecropis daurica</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Swallow" title="Swallow">swallow</a> family. It breeds in open hilly country of temperate southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and Asia from <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> and tropical <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. The Indian and African birds are resident, but European and other Asian birds are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>. They winter in Africa or <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and are vagrants to <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Island" title="Christmas Island">Christmas Island</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>.
</p><p>Red-rumped swallows are somewhat similar in habits and appearance to the other aerial <a href="/wiki/Insectivore" title="Insectivore">insectivores</a>, such as the related swallows and the unrelated <a href="/wiki/Swift" title="Swift">swifts</a> (order Apodiformes). They have blue upperparts and dusky underparts.
</p><p>They resemble <a href="/wiki/Barn_swallow" title="Barn swallow">barn swallows</a>, but are darker below and have pale or reddish rumps, face and neck collar. They lack a breast band, but have black undertails. They are fast fliers and they swoop on <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> while airborne. They have broad but pointed wings.
</p><p>Red-rumped swallows build quarter-sphere nests with a tunnel entrance lined with mud collected in their beaks, and lay 3 to 6 eggs. They normally nest under cliff overhangs in their mountain homes, but will readily adapt to buildings such as mosques and bridges.
</p><p>They do not normally form large breeding colonies, but are gregarious outside the breeding season. Many hundreds can be seen at a time on the plains of India.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-rumped_swallow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The red-rumped swallow was formally described by Finnish-Swedish clergyman, explorer and natural scientist <a href="/wiki/Erik_Laxmann" title="Erik Laxmann">Erik Laxmann</a> in 1769 as <i>Hirundo daurica</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-laxman_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-laxman-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> using a specimen from Mount Schlangen near <a href="/wiki/Zmeinogorsk" title="Zmeinogorsk">Zmeinogorsk</a> Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-asianbirds32_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asianbirds32-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-china_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-china-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It is now usually placed in the genus <i>Cecropis</i> created by German scientist <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Boie" title="Friedrich Boie">Friedrich Boie</a> in 1826,<sup id="cite_ref-boie_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boie-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> although it is arguable how distinct this genus is from <i>Hirundo</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-turner7_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner7-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and some authorities retain it in that genus.<sup id="cite_ref-BirdLife_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLife-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Boie's genus name <i>Cecropis</i> is from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> for an Athenian woman.<sup id="cite_ref-job_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  The specific <i>daurica</i> is derived from <a href="/wiki/Transbaikal" title="Transbaikal">Dauria</a>, a mountainous region to the east of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a> in Russia. the alternative genus <i>Hirundo</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word for "swallow".<sup id="cite_ref-name_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-name-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Some authorities consider the <a href="/wiki/West_African_swallow" title="West African swallow">West African swallow</a> to be a subspecies of the red-rumped swallow.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species is believed to form a superspecies complex with <i><a href="/wiki/Hirundo_striolata" class="mw-redirect" title="Hirundo striolata">Hirundo striolata</a></i>. The widely distributed population shows a lot of variation and several have been named as subspecies.  Many of these are migratory and overlap in their wintering ranges and field identification of these forms is not reliable. The Sri Lankan breeding population <i>hyperythra</i> is a resident, and are now usually considered a distinct species, the <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka_swallow" title="Sri Lanka swallow">Sri Lanka swallow</a>. The underparts are deep chestnut and the nuchal collar is not well marked.
</p><p>The populations in mainland India, <i>erythropygia</i> has the rump patch uniform dark chestnut without any dark shaft-streaks. The tail fork is shallow and the white patch on the inner web of the outer-tail feathers is indistinct. Populations of <i>japonica</i> breed in eastern Asia and winter in Thailand, Burma, India and northern Australia. They are heavily streaked on the underside and have faint streaks on the rump. The populations along the Himalayas <i>nipalensis</i> migrate to peninsular India in winter and breed from Kulu in the west to Bhutan and Arunachal Pradesh in the east. This population has the rump paler with dark shaft streaks. Subspecies <i>rufula</i> of Southern Europe, the Iberian Peninsula east to Baluchistan and Kashmir is resident and winters further south. The chestnut of rump fades to white towards the tail base. The nominate population breeds in Mongolia and Trans-Baikailia wintering in South and Southeast Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Subspecies <i>gephyra</i> of inner Mongolia is considered indistinguishable from the nominate subspecies. The African populations include <i>kumboensis</i> from the highlands of Sierra Leone and Cameroon; <i>melanocrissus</i> of the Ethiopian highlands and <i>emini</i> of Sudan, Uganda, Kenya and Zambia. Many of the variations are separable only on tail and wing lengths and these vary with overlap across populations.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-rumped_swallow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The red-rumped swallow breeds across southern Europe and Asia east to southern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> and Japan, These populations, along with Moroccan birds,  are migratory, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">sub-Saharan</a> Africa or south Asia. There are resident races in Africa in a broad belt from West Africa east to Ethiopia and then south to Tanzania, and most Indian and Sri Lanka breeders are also year-round residents. The African and Asian subspecies may undertake local seasonal movements. This species is a regular vagrant outside its breeding range.<sup id="cite_ref-Turner201_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Turner201-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-rumped_swallow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>These swallows are usually found over grassland where they hawk insects. They may sometimes take advantage of grass fires and grazing cattle that flush insects into the air.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-rumped_swallow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Red-rumped_Swallow_(Hirundo_daurica)_collecting_mud_for_nest_W_IMG_7964.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Red-rumped_Swallow_%28Hirundo_daurica%29_collecting_mud_for_nest_W_IMG_7964.jpg/220px-Red-rumped_Swallow_%28Hirundo_daurica%29_collecting_mud_for_nest_W_IMG_7964.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Red-rumped_Swallow_%28Hirundo_daurica%29_collecting_mud_for_nest_W_IMG_7964.jpg/330px-Red-rumped_Swallow_%28Hirundo_daurica%29_collecting_mud_for_nest_W_IMG_7964.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Red-rumped_Swallow_%28Hirundo_daurica%29_collecting_mud_for_nest_W_IMG_7964.jpg/440px-Red-rumped_Swallow_%28Hirundo_daurica%29_collecting_mud_for_nest_W_IMG_7964.jpg 2x" data-file-width="774" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Red-rumped_Swallow_(Hirundo_daurica)_collecting_mud_for_nest_W_IMG_7964.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Collecting mud for nest in India</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cecropis_daurica_MHNT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Cecropis_daurica_MHNT.jpg/220px-Cecropis_daurica_MHNT.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="135" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Cecropis_daurica_MHNT.jpg/330px-Cecropis_daurica_MHNT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Cecropis_daurica_MHNT.jpg/440px-Cecropis_daurica_MHNT.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4375" data-file-height="2693" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cecropis_daurica_MHNT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs of <i> Cecropis daurica </i>- <a href="/wiki/MHNT" class="mw-redirect" title="MHNT">MHNT</a></div></div></div>
<p>It is thought that the sequence "open-nest" to "closed nest" to "retort nest" represents the evolutionary development in the mud-building swallows, and individual species follow this order of construction. A retort builder like red-rumped swallow starts with an open cup, closes it, and then builds the entrance tunnel. It has been proposed that the development of closed nests reduced competition between males for copulations with the females. Since mating occurs inside the nest, the difficulty of access means other males are excluded. This reduction in competition permits the dense breeding colonies typical of the <i><a href="/wiki/Delichon" title="Delichon">Delichon</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Petrochelidon" title="Petrochelidon">Petrochelidon</a></i> genera, but colonial breeding is not inevitable; most <i>Cecropis</i> species are solitary nesters.<sup id="cite_ref-sheldon2_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sheldon2-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red-rumped_swallow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN) is the organisation responsible for assessing the conservation status of species. A species is assessed as subject to varying levels of threat if it has a small, fragmented or declining range, or if the total population is less than 10,000 mature individuals, or numbers have dropped by more than 10% in ten years or with a continuing decline generations. Red-rumped swallow has a huge range and a population numbered in millions. It is not known to be seriously declining in range or numbers, so it is classed as Least Concern.<sup id="cite_ref-BirdLife_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLife-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The red-rumped swallow is extending its range northward in Europe, colonising France and Romania in recent decades.<sup id="cite_ref-Turner201_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Turner201-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The European population is estimated as 100,000 to 430,000 breeding pairs or 300,000 to 1,290,000 individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-BirdLife_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLife-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
