<p>The <b>Northern Lapwing</b> (<i>Vanellus vanellus</i>), also known as the <b>Peewit</b>, <b>Green Plover</b> or (in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>) just <b>Lapwing</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">plover</a> family. It is common through temperate <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>. It is highly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> over most of its extensive range, wintering further south as far as north <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, and parts of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. It migrates mainly by day, often in large flocks. Lowland breeders in westernmost areas of Europe are resident. It occasionally is a vagrant to <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, especially after storms, as in the <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> sightings after storms in December 1927 and in January 1966.<sup id="cite_ref-Godfrey_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Godfrey-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is a <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> which breeds on cultivated land and other short vegetation habitats. 3&#160;– 4 eggs are laid in a ground scrape. The nest and young are defended noisily and aggressively against all intruders, up to and including <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>.</p>
<p>In winter it forms huge flocks on open land, particularly <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable land</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mudflat" title="Mudflat">mud-flats</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The Northern <a href="/wiki/Lapwing" title="Lapwing">lapwing</a> is a 28–33 cm (11–13 in) long bird with a 67–87 cm (26–34 in) wingspan and a body mass of 128–330 g (4.5–12 oz).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> It has rounded wings and a crest. It is the shortest-legged of the lapwings. It is mainly black and white, but the back is tinted green. The male has a long crest and a black crown, throat and breast contrasting with an otherwise white face. Females and young birds have shorter crests, and have less strongly marked heads, but plumages are otherwise quite similar.</p>
<p>The name <i>lapwing</i> has been variously attributed to the "lapping" sound its wings make in flight, from the irregular progress in flight due to its large wings (<a href="/wiki/OED" title="OED" class="mw-redirect">OED</a> derives this from an Old English word meaning "to totter"), or from its habit of drawing potential predators away from its nest by trailing a wing as if broken. This is a vocal bird in the breeding season, with constant calling as the crazed tumbling display flight is performed by the male. The typical contact call is a loud, shrill "pee-wit" from which they get their other name of <b>Peewit</b>. Displaying males usually make a wheezy "pee-wit, wit wit, eeze wit" during their display flight, these birds also make squeaking or mewing sounds.</p>
<p>It feeds primarily on insects and other small invertebrates. This species often feeds in mixed flocks with <a href="/wiki/European_Golden_Plover" title="European Golden Plover">Golden Plovers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black-headed_Gull" title="Black-headed Gull">Black-headed Gulls</a>, the latter often robbing the two plovers, but providing a degree of protection against predators.</p>
<p>Like the Golden Plovers, this species prefers to feed at night when there is moonlight.</p>
<p>The Northern Lapwing is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>

<p>National surveys of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> have shown a population decline between 1987 and 1998. The numbers of this species have been adversely affected by intensive agricultural techniques. In the <a href="/wiki/Lowland" title="Lowland">lowlands</a> this includes the loss of rough grassland, conversion to <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable</a> or improved grassland, loss of mixed farms, and switch from spring to autumn sown crops. In the uplands the losses may have been due to increases in <a href="/wiki/Grazing" title="Grazing">grazing</a> density.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a> gives grant aid to help restore lapwing habitat within its <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Stewardship" title="Environmental Stewardship" class="mw-redirect">Environmental Stewardship</a> Scheme. The organisation suggests an option within this scheme called 'Fallow plots for ground-nesting birds'. Uncropped plots at least 2 ha in size provide nesting habitat, and are located in suitable arable fields, which provide additional foraging habitat. Locating the plots within 2&#160;km of extensively grazed grassland will provide additional foraging habitat. The plots is cultivated in the spring to produce a rough fallow, which is retained without the input of fertiliser or pesticides.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cultural significance">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_significance">Cultural significance</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Harvesting eggs">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Harvesting_eggs">Harvesting eggs</span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> there is a cultural-historical competition to finds the first peewit egg of the year (<i>het eerste kievitsei</i>). It is especially popular in <a href="/wiki/Friesland" title="Friesland">Friesland</a> province, but there are also regional competitions. The gathering of peewit eggs is prohibited by the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, but Friesland was granted an exception for cultural-historical reasons. The Frisian exception was removed in 2005 by a court, which determined that the Frisian <a href="/wiki/Gedeputeerde_Staten" title="Gedeputeerde Staten" class="mw-redirect">executive councillors</a> had not properly followed procedure.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> As of 2006 it is again allowed to look for peewit eggs between 1 March and 9 April, though the actual harvest of those eggs is now forbidden. The first egg of 2008 was found on 3 March, in <a href="/wiki/Eemnes" title="Eemnes">Eemnes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Utrecht_(province)" title="Utrecht (province)">Utrecht</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> as was the first egg of 2009 found on 8 March.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Over the last century, the first peewit egg is found earlier and earlier. This is caused both by increase use of <a href="/wiki/Fertiliser" title="Fertiliser" class="mw-redirect">fertiliser</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mythology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Mythology">Mythology</span></h3>
<p>The bird referred to in English translations of <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Metamorphoses" title="Metamorphoses">Metamorphoses</a></i>, book 6 (the story of King <a href="/wiki/Tereus" title="Tereus">Tereus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a>, who rapes his wife's sister, <a href="/wiki/Philomela_(princess_of_Athens)" title="Philomela (princess of Athens)">Philomela</a>, and cuts out her tongue), as <i><a href="/wiki/Lapwing" title="Lapwing">lapwing</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> is probably the Northern lapwing. Tereus is turned into an <i>epops</i> (6.674); Ovid presumably had the <a href="/wiki/Hoopoe" title="Hoopoe">hoopoe</a> in mind, whose crest indicates his royal status and whose long, sharp beak is a symbol of his violent nature.</p>
