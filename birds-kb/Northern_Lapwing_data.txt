<p>The <b>northern lapwing</b> (<i>Vanellus vanellus</i>), also known as the <b>peewit</b> or <b>pewit</b>, <b>tuit</b> or <b>tew-it</b>, <b>green plover</b>, or (in  Britain and Ireland) <b>pyewipe</b> or just <b>lapwing</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Lapwing" title="Lapwing">lapwing</a> family. It is common through temperate <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurosiberia</a>.
</p><p>It is highly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> over most of its extensive range, wintering further south as far as <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, and parts of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. It migrates mainly by day, often in large flocks. Lowland breeders in westernmost areas of Europe are resident. It occasionally is a vagrant to <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, especially after storms, as in the <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> sightings after storms in December 1927 and in January 1966.<sup id="cite_ref-Godfrey_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Godfrey-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is a <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> that breeds on cultivated land and other short vegetation habitats. 3–4 eggs are laid in a ground scrape. The nest and young are defended noisily and aggressively against all intruders, up to and including <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>.
</p><p>In winter, it forms huge flocks on open land, particularly <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable land</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mudflat" title="Mudflat">mud-flats</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name <i>lapwing</i> has been variously attributed to the "lapping" sound its wings make in flight, from the irregular progress in flight due to its large wings (the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a> derives this from an Old English word meaning "to totter"),<sup id="cite_ref-OED_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> or from its habit of drawing potential predators away from its nest by trailing a wing as if broken. The names <i>peewit</i>, <i>pewit</i>, <i>tuit</i> or <i>tew-it</i> are <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopaeic</a> and refer to the bird's characteristic call.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The scientific name <i>Vanellus</i> is <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> for the northern lapwing and derives from <i>vannus</i> a <a href="/wiki/Winnowing#In_Greek_culture" title="Winnowing">winnowing fan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-job_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The northern lapwing is a 28–33&#160;cm (11–13&#160;in) long bird with a 67–87&#160;cm (26–34&#160;in) wingspan and a body mass of 128–330&#160;g (4.5–11.6&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-oiseaux-birds_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oiseaux-birds-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It has rounded wings and a crest. It is also the shortest-legged of the lapwings. It is mainly black and white, but the back is tinted green. The male has a long crest and a black crown, throat and breast contrasting with an otherwise white face. Females and young birds have shorter crests, and have less strongly marked heads, but plumages are otherwise quite similar.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><div class="mediaContainer" style="width:220px"><audio id="mwe_player_2" controls="" preload="none" style="width:220px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="35.253310657596" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="Northern_Lapwing_(Vanellus_vanellus)_(W1CDR0001471_BD4).ogg" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/7/77/Northern_Lapwing_%28Vanellus_vanellus%29_%28W1CDR0001471_BD4%29.ogg/Northern_Lapwing_%28Vanellus_vanellus%29_%28W1CDR0001471_BD4%29.ogg.mp3" type="audio/mpeg" data-title="MP3" data-shorttitle="MP3" data-transcodekey="mp3" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="130104" /><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/77/Northern_Lapwing_%28Vanellus_vanellus%29_%28W1CDR0001471_BD4%29.ogg" type="audio/ogg; codecs=&quot;vorbis&quot;" data-title="Original Ogg file (214 kbps)" data-shorttitle="Ogg source" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="213678" /></audio></div>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Northern_Lapwing_(Vanellus_vanellus)_(W1CDR0001471_BD4).ogg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Display calls, Surrey, England</div></div></div>
<p>This is a vocal bird in the breeding season, with constant calling as the crazed tumbling display flight is performed by the male. The typical contact call is a loud, shrill "pee-wit" from which they get their other name of <b>peewit</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Displaying males usually make a wheezy "pee-wit, wit wit, eeze wit" during their display flight, these birds also make squeaking or mewing sounds.
</p><p>It feeds primarily on insects and other small invertebrates. This species often feeds in mixed flocks with <a href="/wiki/European_golden_plover" title="European golden plover">golden plovers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black-headed_gull" title="Black-headed gull">black-headed gulls</a>, the latter often robbing the two plovers, but providing a degree of protection against predators.
</p><p>Like the golden plovers, this species prefers to feed at night when there is moonlight.
</p><p>The northern lapwing is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_decline">Population decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Population decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vanellus_vanellus_-Rotterdam_Zoo,_Netherlands_-chick-8a.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Vanellus_vanellus_-Rotterdam_Zoo%2C_Netherlands_-chick-8a.jpg/220px-Vanellus_vanellus_-Rotterdam_Zoo%2C_Netherlands_-chick-8a.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Vanellus_vanellus_-Rotterdam_Zoo%2C_Netherlands_-chick-8a.jpg/330px-Vanellus_vanellus_-Rotterdam_Zoo%2C_Netherlands_-chick-8a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Vanellus_vanellus_-Rotterdam_Zoo%2C_Netherlands_-chick-8a.jpg/440px-Vanellus_vanellus_-Rotterdam_Zoo%2C_Netherlands_-chick-8a.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3237" data-file-height="2158" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vanellus_vanellus_-Rotterdam_Zoo,_Netherlands_-chick-8a.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chick in the Netherlands</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vanellus_vanellus_MHNT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Vanellus_vanellus_MHNT.jpg/220px-Vanellus_vanellus_MHNT.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="215" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Vanellus_vanellus_MHNT.jpg/330px-Vanellus_vanellus_MHNT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Vanellus_vanellus_MHNT.jpg/440px-Vanellus_vanellus_MHNT.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3662" data-file-height="3578" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vanellus_vanellus_MHNT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg – <a href="/wiki/MHNT" class="mw-redirect" title="MHNT">MHNT</a></div></div></div>
<p>National surveys of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> have shown a population decline between 1987 and 1998, and since 2009 the northern lapwing has had red list conservation status in the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The numbers of this species have been adversely affected by intensive agricultural techniques. In the <a href="/wiki/Lowland" class="mw-redirect" title="Lowland">lowlands</a> this includes the loss of rough grassland, conversion to <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable</a> or improved grassland, loss of mixed farms, and switch from spring- to autumn-sown crops. In the uplands, the losses may have been due to increases in <a href="/wiki/Grazing" title="Grazing">grazing</a> density. <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a> gives grant aid to help restore lapwing habitat within its <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Stewardship" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental Stewardship">Environmental Stewardship</a> Scheme. The organisation suggests an option within this scheme called 'Fallow plots for ground-nesting birds'. Uncropped plots at least 2&#160;ha (4.9 acres) in size provide nesting habitat and are located in suitable arable fields, which provide additional foraging habitat. Locating the plots within 2&#160;km (1.2&#160;mi) of extensively grazed grassland will provide additional foraging habitat. The plots are cultivated in the spring to produce a rough fallow, which is retained without the input of fertiliser or pesticides.<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In addition to agricultural intensification and land-use change, predation of nests and chicks contributes to wader declines, including of lapwing. By radio-tagging lapwing chicks, and using automatic radio tracking systems, the timing of chick predation can be revealed, which provides additional insights in to the importance of different predators. Lapwing chicks are predated both in the day and at night, with mammalian predators having the greatest impact.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a>, the population decline and loss of breeding habitats was also documented; the threats are thought to be intensification of land use and hunting, but further investigations for threat clarification are required.<sup id="cite_ref-ABCC_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABCC-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_significance">Cultural significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cultural significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Harvesting_eggs">Harvesting eggs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Harvesting eggs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Plover's eggs" were an expensive <a href="/wiki/Delicacy" title="Delicacy">delicacy</a> in Victorian Europe, mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Evelyn_Waugh" title="Evelyn Waugh">Evelyn Waugh</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Brideshead_Revisited" title="Brideshead Revisited">Brideshead Revisited</a>,</i> about aristocratic British society in 1920–40. In the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, there is a cultural-historical competition to find the first peewit egg of the year (<i>het eerste kievietsei</i>). It is especially popular in the province <a href="/wiki/Friesland" title="Friesland">Friesland</a>, but there are also regional competitions. Gathering peewit eggs is prohibited by the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, but Friesland was granted an exception for cultural-historical reasons. The Frisian exception was removed in 2005 by a court, which determined that the Frisian <a href="/wiki/Gedeputeerde_Staten" class="mw-redirect" title="Gedeputeerde Staten">executive councillors</a> had not properly followed procedure.<sup id="cite_ref-Walinga2005_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walinga2005-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-court_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-court-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> As of 2006 looking for peewit eggs is permitted between 1 March and 9 April, though harvesting the eggs is now forbidden. In 2008 the first egg was found on 3 March, in <a href="/wiki/Eemnes" title="Eemnes">Eemnes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Utrecht_(province)" title="Utrecht (province)">Utrecht</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-NederlandseOmroepStichting_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NederlandseOmroepStichting-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and the first egg of 2009 was found on 8 March in <a href="/wiki/Krabbendijke" title="Krabbendijke">Krabbendijke</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RadioNetherlands_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RadioNetherlands-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Over the last century, the first peewit egg has been found earlier and earlier in the year. This is ascribed to both increased use of <a href="/wiki/Fertiliser" class="mw-redirect" title="Fertiliser">fertiliser</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, causing the growth of grass needed for egg laying to occur earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-Natuurcompendium_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Natuurcompendium-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mythology">Mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bird referred to in English translations of <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Metamorphoses" title="Metamorphoses">Metamorphoses</a></i>, book 6, as <i>lapwing</i><sup id="cite_ref-Garth_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Garth-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> is probably the northern lapwing. Tereus is turned into an <i>epops</i> (6.674); Ovid presumably had the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_hoopoe" title="Eurasian hoopoe">hoopoe</a> in mind, whose crest indicates his royal status and whose long, sharp beak is a symbol of his violent nature.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Ireland">In Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_lapwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The northern lapwing was declared the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>'s <a href="/wiki/National_bird" class="mw-redirect" title="National bird">national bird</a> by a committee of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Wildlife_Conservancy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Irish Wildlife Conservancy (page does not exist)">Irish Wildlife Conservancy</a> in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish language</a> it is called <i>pilibín</i>, "little Philip", supposedly a reference to <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II of Spain</a> (<a href="/wiki/King_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Ireland">King of Ireland</a> 1554–58), who often wore a feather in his cap.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
