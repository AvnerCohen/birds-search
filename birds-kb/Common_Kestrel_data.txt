<p>The <b>Common Kestrel</b> (<i>Falco tinnunculus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Kestrel" title="Kestrel">kestrel</a> group of the <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Falconidae" title="Falconidae">Falconidae</a>. It is also known as the <b>European Kestrel</b>, <b>Eurasian Kestrel</b>, or <b>Old World Kestrel</b>. In Britain, where no other brown falcon occurs, it is generally just called "the <b>kestrel</b>".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species occurs over a large range. It is widespread in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, as well as occasionally reaching the east coast of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a><sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from January 2009">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. But although it has colonized a few <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceanic</a> islands, vagrant individuals are generally rare; in the whole of <a href="/wiki/Micronesia" title="Micronesia">Micronesia</a> for example, the species was only recorded twice each on <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saipan" title="Saipan">Saipan</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Marianas" title="Marianas" class="mw-redirect">Marianas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Kestrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mysiar.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Mysiar.JPG/220px-Mysiar.JPG" width="220" height="295" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Mysiar.JPG/330px-Mysiar.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Mysiar.JPG/440px-Mysiar.JPG 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mysiar.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Typical in-flight appearance of a Common Kestrel</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Common Kestrels measure 32–39&#160;cm (13–15&#160;in) from head to tail, with a wingspan of 65–82&#160;cm (26–32&#160;in). Females are noticeably larger, with the adult male weighing 136-252 g (c,5-9 oz), around 155 g (around 5.5 oz) on average; the adult female weighs 154-314 g (about 5.5-11 oz), around 184 g (around 6.5 oz) on average. They are thus small compared with other birds of prey, but larger than most <a href="/wiki/Songbird" title="Songbird">songbirds</a>. Like the other <i><a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">Falco</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, they have long wings as well as a distinctive long tail.<sup id="cite_ref-orta1994_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-orta1994-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Their plumage is mainly light <a href="/wiki/Chestnut_(colour)" title="Chestnut (colour)" class="mw-redirect">chestnut</a> brown with blackish spots on the upperside and <a href="/wiki/Buff_(colour)" title="Buff (colour)">buff</a> with narrow blackish streaks on the underside; the <a href="/wiki/Remiges" title="Remiges" class="mw-redirect">remiges</a> are also blackish. Unlike most <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptors</a>, they display <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual colour dimorphism</a> with the male having fewer black spots and streaks, as well as a blue-grey cap and tail. The tail is brown with black bars in females, and has a black tip with a narrow white rim in both sexes. All Common Kestrels have a prominent black <a href="/wiki/Cheek" title="Cheek">malar</a> stripe like their closest relatives.<sup id="cite_ref-orta1994_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-orta1994-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cere" title="Cere" class="mw-redirect">cere</a>, feet, and a narrow ring around the eye are bright yellow; the toenails, bill and <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">iris</a> are dark. Juveniles look like adult females, but the underside streaks are wider; the yellow of their bare parts is paler. Hatchlings are covered in white <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">down feathers</a>, changing to a buff-grey second down coat before they grow their first true plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-orta1994_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-orta1994-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Kestrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span></h2>
<p>In the cool-<a href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate" class="mw-redirect">temperate</a> parts of its range, the Common Kestrel <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrates</a> south in winter; otherwise it is sedentary, though juveniles may wander around in search for a good place to settle down as they become mature. It is a <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_animal" title="Diurnal animal" class="mw-redirect">diurnal animal</a> of the lowlands and prefers open <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Field_(agriculture)" title="Field (agriculture)">fields</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heath_(habitat)" title="Heath (habitat)" class="mw-redirect">heaths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shrubland" title="Shrubland">shrubland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marshland" title="Marshland" class="mw-redirect">marshland</a>. It does not require woodland to be present as long as there are alternate perching and nesting sites like rocks or buildings. It will thrive in treeless <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a> where there are abundant <a href="/wiki/Herbaceous_plant" title="Herbaceous plant">herbaceous plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrubs</a> to support a population of prey animals. The Common Kestrel readily adapts to human settlement, as long as sufficient swathes of vegetation are available, and may even be found in <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moorlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a>. It is found from the sea to the lower mountain ranges, reaching up to 4,500 m (15,000&#160;ft) <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" title="Above mean sea level">ASL</a> in the hottest <a href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical" class="mw-redirect">tropical</a> parts of its range but only to about 1,750 meters (5,700&#160;ft) in the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical" class="mw-redirect">subtropical</a> climate of the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayan</a> <a href="/wiki/Foothills" title="Foothills">foothills</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Globally, this species is not considered threatened by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN" class="mw-redirect">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Its stocks were affected by the indiscriminate use of <a href="/wiki/Organochlorine" title="Organochlorine" class="mw-redirect">organochlorines</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a> in the mid-20th century, but being something of an <a href="/wiki/R-strategist" title="R-strategist" class="mw-redirect">r-strategist</a> able to multiply quickly under good conditions it was less affected than other birds of prey. The global population is fluctuating considerably over the years but remains generally stable; it is roughly estimated at 1-2 million pairs or so, about 20% of which are found in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. There has been a recent decline in parts of Western Europe such as <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. Subspecies <i>dacotiae</i> is quite rare, numbering less than 1000 adult birds in 1990, when the ancient western Canarian subspecies <i>canariensis</i> numbered about ten times as many birds.<sup id="cite_ref-orta1994_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-orta1994-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Kestrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Food and feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_feeding">Food and feeding</span></h3>
<div class="floatleft"><a href="/wiki/File:Common-Kestrel-4.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Common-Kestrel-4.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Common-Kestrel-4.jpg/125px-Common-Kestrel-4.jpg" width="125" height="87" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Common-Kestrel-4.jpg/188px-Common-Kestrel-4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Common-Kestrel-4.jpg/250px-Common-Kestrel-4.jpg 2x" /></a></div>
<div class="floatleft"><a href="/wiki/File:Common-Kestrel-2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Common-Kestrel-2.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Common-Kestrel-2.jpg/125px-Common-Kestrel-2.jpg" width="125" height="87" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Common-Kestrel-2.jpg/188px-Common-Kestrel-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Common-Kestrel-2.jpg/250px-Common-Kestrel-2.jpg 2x" /></a></div>
<p>When hunting, the Common Kestrel characteristically hovers about 10–20 m (c.30–70&#160;ft) above the ground, searching for prey, either by flying into the wind or by soaring using <a href="/wiki/Ridge_lift" title="Ridge lift">ridge lift</a>. Like most <a href="/wiki/Birds_of_prey" title="Birds of prey" class="mw-redirect">birds of prey</a>, Common Kestrels have keen eyesight enabling them to spot small prey from a distance. Once prey is sighted, the bird makes a short, steep dive toward the target. It can often be found hunting along the sides of roads and motorways. This <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> is able to see <a href="/wiki/Near_ultraviolet" title="Near ultraviolet" class="mw-redirect">near ultraviolet</a> light, allowing the birds to detect the <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">urine</a> trails around <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodent</a> burrows as they shine in an ultraviolet colour in the sunlight.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Another favourite (but less conspicuous) hunting technique is to perch a bit above the ground cover, surveying the area. When the birds spot prey animals moving by, they will pounce on them. They also prowl a patch of hunting ground in a ground-hugging flight, ambushing prey as they happen across it.<sup id="cite_ref-orta1994_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-orta1994-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<div class="floatleft"><a href="/wiki/File:Common-Kestrel-5.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Common-Kestrel-5.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Common-Kestrel-5.jpg/125px-Common-Kestrel-5.jpg" width="125" height="87" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Common-Kestrel-5.jpg/188px-Common-Kestrel-5.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Common-Kestrel-5.jpg/250px-Common-Kestrel-5.jpg 2x" /></a></div>
<p>Common Kestrels eat almost exclusively mouse-sized <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>: typically <a href="/wiki/Vole" title="Vole">voles</a>, but also <a href="/wiki/Shrew" title="Shrew">shrews</a> and true <a href="/wiki/Mice" title="Mice" class="mw-redirect">mice</a> supply up to three-quarters or more of the <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> most individuals ingest. On oceanic islands (where <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> are often scarce), small <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> – mainly <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> – may make up the bulk of its diet<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> while elsewhere birds are only important food during a few weeks each summer when unexperienced <a href="/wiki/Fledgling_(birds)" title="Fledgling (birds)" class="mw-redirect">fledglings</a> abound. Other suitably sized <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a> like <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a><sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from January 2009">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a> are eaten only on rare occasions. However, kestrels may more often prey on lizards at southern latitudes, in northern latitudes the kestrel is found to more often deliver lizards to their nestlings during midday and also with increasing ambient temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Seasonally, <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> may be a main prey item. Generally, <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> like <a href="/wiki/Camel_spider" title="Camel spider" class="mw-redirect">camel spiders</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a>, but mainly sizeable <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orthoptera" title="Orthoptera">orthopterans</a> and winged <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termites</a> are eaten with delight whenever the birds happen across them.<sup id="cite_ref-orta1994_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-orta1994-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>F. tinnunculus</i> requires the equivalent of 4-8 voles a day, depending on energy expenditure (time of the year, amount of hovering, etc.). They have been known to catch several voles in succession and <a href="/wiki/Hoarding_(animal_behavior)" title="Hoarding (animal behavior)">cache</a> some for later consumption. An individual nestling consume on average 4.2 g/h, this is equivalent to 67.8 g/d (3-4 voles per day).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Kestrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span></h3>
<p>The Common Kestrel starts breeding in spring (or the start of the <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">dry season</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>), i.e. April/May in <a href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate" class="mw-redirect">temperate</a> <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> and some time between August and December in the tropics and southern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It is a cavity nester, preferring holes in cliffs, trees or buildings; in built-up areas, Common Kestrels will often nest on buildings, and generally they often reuse the old nests of <a href="/wiki/Corvid" title="Corvid" class="mw-redirect">corvids</a> if are available. The diminutive <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> <i>dacotiae</i>, the <i>sarnicolo</i> of the eastern <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> is peculiar for nesting occasionally in the dried fronds below the top of <a href="/wiki/Palm_tree" title="Palm tree" class="mw-redirect">palm trees</a>, apparently coexisting rather peacefully with small <a href="/wiki/Songbird" title="Songbird">songbirds</a> which also make their home there.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> In general, Common Kestrels will usually tolerate conspecifics nesting nearby, and sometimes a few dozen pairs may be found nesting in a loose colony.<sup id="cite_ref-orta1994_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-orta1994-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is normally 3-6 eggs, but may contain any number of eggs up to seven; even more eggs may be laid in total when some are removed during the laying time, which lasts about 2 days per egg laid. The eggs are abundantly patterned with brown spots, from a wash that tinges the entire surface <a href="/wiki/Buff_(colour)" title="Buff (colour)">buffish</a> white to large almost-black blotches. Incubation lasts some 4 weeks to one month, and only the female hatches the eggs. The male is responsible for provisioning her with food, and for some time after hatching this remains the same. Later, both parents share brooding and hunting duties until the young fledge, after 4–5 weeks. The family stays close together for a few weeks, up to a month or so, during which time the young learn how to fend for themselves and hunt prey. The young become sexually mature the next breeding season.<sup id="cite_ref-orta1994_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-orta1994-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Data from <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a> shows nesting pairs bringing up about 2-3 chicks on average, though this includes a considerable rate of total brood failures; actually, few pairs that do manage to fledge offspring raise less than 3 or 4. <a href="/wiki/Population_cycle" title="Population cycle">Population cycles</a> of prey, particularly voles, have a considerable influence on breeding success. Most Common Kestrels die before they reach 2 years of age; mortality til the first birthday may be as high as 70%. At least females generally breed at one year of age;<sup id="cite_ref-anage2010_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anage2010-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> possibly, some males take a year longer to maturity as they do in related species. The biological lifespan to death from <a href="/wiki/Senescence" title="Senescence">senescence</a> can be 16 years or more, however; one was recorded to have lived almost 24 years.<sup id="cite_ref-anage2010_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anage2010-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Kestrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Evolution and systematics">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_and_systematics">Evolution and systematics</span></h2>
<p>This species is part of a <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> that contains the kestrel species with black <a href="/wiki/Cheek" title="Cheek">malar</a> stripes, a feature which apparently was not present in the most ancestral kestrels. They seem to have <a href="/wiki/Radiation_(biology)" title="Radiation (biology)" class="mw-redirect">radiated</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Gelasian" title="Gelasian">Gelasian</a> (<a href="/wiki/Late_Pliocene" title="Late Pliocene" class="mw-redirect">Late Pliocene</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> roughly 2.5-2 <a href="/wiki/Mya_(unit)" title="Mya (unit)" class="mw-redirect">mya</a>, probably starting in tropical East Africa, as indicated by <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA" class="mw-redirect">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome <i>b</i></a> <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">sequence</a> data analysis and considerations of <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a>. The Common Kestrel's closest living relative is apparently the <a href="/wiki/Nankeen_Kestrel" title="Nankeen Kestrel">Nankeen</a> or Australian Kestrel (<i>F. cenchroides</i>), which probably derived from ancestral Common Kestrels settling in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adaptation_(biology)" title="Adaptation (biology)" class="mw-redirect">adapting</a> to local conditions less than one million years ago, during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Pleistocene" title="Middle Pleistocene">Middle Pleistocene</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Rock Kestrel may be a distinct species <i>F. rupicolus</i>, more distantly related to the Common Kestrel proper than the Nankeen Kestrel; its relationship to the other African and South Asian <a href="/wiki/Kestrel" title="Kestrel">kestrel</a> <a href="/wiki/Taxa" title="Taxa" class="mw-redirect">taxa</a> remains insufficiently studied. The <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> subspecies are apparently independently derived from Continental birds.<sup id="cite_ref-groombridgeetal2002_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-groombridgeetal2002-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Kestrel" title="Lesser Kestrel">Lesser Kestrel</a> (<i>F. naumanni</i>), which much resembles a small Common Kestrel with no black on the upperside except wing and tail tips, is probably not very closely related to the present species, and the <a href="/wiki/American_Kestrel" title="American Kestrel">American Kestrel</a> (<i>F. sparverius</i>) is apparently not a true <a href="/wiki/Kestrel" title="Kestrel">kestrel</a> at all.<sup id="cite_ref-groombridgeetal2002_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-groombridgeetal2002-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Both species have much grey in their wings in males, which does not occur in the Common Kestrel or its close living relatives but does in almost all other falcons.</p>

<p>A number of <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of the Common Kestrel are known, though some are hardly distinct and may be invalid. Most of them differ little, and mainly in accordance with <a href="/wiki/Bergmann%27s_Rule" title="Bergmann's Rule" class="mw-redirect">Bergmann's</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gloger%27s_Rule" title="Gloger's Rule" class="mw-redirect">Gloger's Rules</a>. Tropical African forms have less grey in the male plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-orta1994_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-orta1994-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>Falco tinnunculus tinnunculus</i> <small>Linnaeus, 1758</small></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate" class="mw-redirect">Temperate</a> areas of Europe, North Africa, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, and Asia north of the <a href="/wiki/Hindu_Kush" title="Hindu Kush">Hindu Kush</a>-<a href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya" class="mw-redirect">Himalaya</a> mountain ranges to the NW <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Okhotsk" title="Sea of Okhotsk">Sea of Okhotsk</a> region. Northern Asian populations <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> south in winter, apparently not crossing the Himalayas but diverting to the west.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>Falco (tinnunculus) rupicolus</i> <small><a href="/wiki/Francois-Marie_Daudin" title="Francois-Marie Daudin" class="mw-redirect">Daudin</a>, 1800</small> – <b>Rock Kestrel</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>NW Angola and S <a href="/wiki/Zaire" title="Zaire">Zaire</a> to S Tanzania, and south to <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>. Probably a distinct species, but its limits with <i>rufescens</i> require further study. It differs markedly from the other subspecies of the <i>F. tinnunculus</i> complex. In particular, the females have what in other subspecies are typically male characteristics such as a grey head and tail, and spotted rather than barred upperparts. The Rock Kestrel has less heavily marked, brighter chestnut upperparts and its underparts are also a bright chestnut that contrasts with the nearly unmarked white underwings. Females tend to have more black bands in the central tail feathers than males. The open mountain habitat also differs from that its relatives.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>Falco tinnunculus rufescens</i> <small><a href="/wiki/William_John_Swainson" title="William John Swainson">Swainson</a>, 1837</small></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a> east to <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, southwards around <a href="/wiki/Congo_basin" title="Congo basin" class="mw-redirect">Congo basin</a> to S <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a> and NE <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>Falco tinnunculus interstictus</i> <small><a href="/wiki/John_McClelland_(doctor)" title="John McClelland (doctor)">McClelland</a>, 1840</small></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Breeds <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a> from <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a> to <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, south into <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">Indochina</a>. Winters to the south of its breeding range, from <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> (where it is localized, e.g. from <a href="/wiki/Mindanao" title="Mindanao">Mindanao</a> only two records exist).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Birds in the <a href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya" class="mw-redirect">Himalayan</a> <a href="/wiki/Foothills" title="Foothills">foothills</a> (e.g. of <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> might be all-year residents</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>Falco tinnunculus rupicolaeformis</i> <small>(<a href="/wiki/Christian_Ludwig_Brehm" title="Christian Ludwig Brehm">C. L. Brehm</a>, 1855)</small></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a> except in the desert and across the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> into Africa.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>Falco tinnunculus neglectus</i> <small><a href="/wiki/Hermann_Schlegel" title="Hermann Schlegel">Schlegel</a>, 1873</small></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Northern <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_Islands" title="Cape Verde Islands" class="mw-redirect">Cape Verde Islands</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>Falco tinnunculus canariensis</i> <small>(Koenig, 1890)</small></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a> and western Canary Islands. The more ancient Canaries subspecies.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>Falco tinnunculus dacotiae</i> <small><a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a>, 1913</small> – Local name: <i><b>cernícalo</b></i></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Eastern Canary Islands: <a href="/wiki/Fuerteventura" title="Fuerteventura">Fuerteventura</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lanzarote" title="Lanzarote">Lanzarote</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinijo_Archipelago" title="Chinijo Archipelago">Chinijo Archipelago</a>. A more recently evolved subspecies than <i>canariensis</i>.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>Falco tinnunculus objurgatus</i> <small>(<a href="/wiki/Edward_Charles_Stuart_Baker" title="Edward Charles Stuart Baker" class="mw-redirect">Baker</a>, 1929)</small></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Western_Ghats" title="Western Ghats">Western</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nilgiris" title="Nilgiris" class="mw-redirect">Nilgiris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Ghats" title="Eastern Ghats">Eastern Ghats</a> of India; <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>. Heavily marked, has rufous thighs with dark grey head in males.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>Falco tinnunculus archerii</i> <small>(Hartert &amp; Neumann, 1932)</small></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>, coastal <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Socotra" title="Socotra">Socotra</a></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>Falco tinnunculus alexandri</i> <small>Bourne, 1955</small></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Southwestern Cape Verde Islands.</dd>
</dl>
<p>The Common Kestrels of Europe living during cold periods of the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">Quaternary glaciation</a> differed slightly in size from the current population; they are sometimes referred to as <a href="/wiki/Paleosubspecies" title="Paleosubspecies" class="mw-redirect">paleosubspecies</a> <i>F. t. atavus</i> (<i>see also</i> <a href="/wiki/Bergmann%27s_Rule" title="Bergmann's Rule" class="mw-redirect">Bergmann's Rule</a>). The remains of these birds, which presumably were the direct ancestors of the living <i>F. t. tinnunculus</i> (and perhaps other subspecies), are found throughout the then-unglaciated parts of Europe, from the <a href="/wiki/Late_Pliocene" title="Late Pliocene" class="mw-redirect">Late Pliocene</a> (<a href="/wiki/European_Land_Mammal_Age" title="European Land Mammal Age" class="mw-redirect">ELMA</a> <a href="/wiki/Villanyian" title="Villanyian">Villanyian</a>/<a href="/wiki/International_Commission_on_Stratigraphy" title="International Commission on Stratigraphy">ICS</a> <a href="/wiki/Piacenzian" title="Piacenzian">Piacenzian</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=MN16&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="MN16 (page does not exist)">MN16</a>) about 3 <a href="/wiki/Million_years_ago" title="Million years ago" class="mw-redirect">million years ago</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Pleistocene" title="Middle Pleistocene">Middle Pleistocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Saalian" title="Saalian" class="mw-redirect">Saalian</a> glaciation which ended about 130.000 years ago, when they finally gave way to birds indistinguishable from those living today. Some of the <a href="/wiki/Vole" title="Vole">voles</a> the Ice Age Common Kestrels ate – such as <a href="/wiki/European_Pine_Vole" title="European Pine Vole">European Pine Voles</a> (<i>Microtus subterraneus</i>) – were indistinguishable from those alive today. Other prey species of that time <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolved</a> more rapidly (like <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microtus_malei&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Microtus malei (page does not exist)">M. malei</a></i>, the presumed ancestor of today's <a href="/wiki/Tundra_Vole" title="Tundra Vole" class="mw-redirect">Tundra Vole</a> <i>M. oeconomus</i>), while yet again others seem to have gone entirely <a href="/wiki/Extinct" title="Extinct" class="mw-redirect">extinct</a> without leaving any living descendants – for example <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pliomys_lenki&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pliomys lenki (page does not exist)">Pliomys lenki</a></i>, which apparently fell victim to the <a href="/wiki/Weichselian_glaciation" title="Weichselian glaciation" class="mw-redirect">Weichselian glaciation</a> about 100.000 years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Kestrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span></h2>
<p>The Kestrel is sometimes seen, like other birds of prey, as a symbol of the power and vitality of nature. In "Into Battle" (1915), the war poet <a href="/wiki/Julian_Grenfell" title="Julian Grenfell">Julian Grenfell</a> invokes the superhuman characteristics of the Kestrel among several birds, when hoping for prowess in battle:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"The kestrel hovering by day,<br />
And the <a href="/wiki/Little_owl" title="Little owl" class="mw-redirect">little owl</a> that call at night,<br />
Bid him be swift and keen as they,<br />
As keen of ear, as swift of sight."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Gerard Manley Hopkins (1844–1889) writes on the kestrel in his poem The Windhover, exalting in their mastery of flight and their majesty in the sky.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"I caught this morning morning's minion, king-<br />
dom of daylight's dauphin, dapple-dawn-drawn Falcon, in his riding"</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Archaic names for the kestrel include <i><a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/windhover" class="extiw" title="wikt:windhover">windhover</a></i> and <i><a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/windfucker" class="extiw" title="wikt:windfucker">windfucker</a>,</i> due to its habit of beating the wind (hovering in air).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A kestrel is also one of the main characters in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Animals_of_Farthing_Wood_(book)" title="The Animals of Farthing Wood (book)">The Animals of Farthing Wood</a></i>.</p>
