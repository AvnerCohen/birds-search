<p>The <b>White-eyed Gull</b> (<i>Ichthyaetus leucophthalmus</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a> which is <a href="/wiki/Endemism_in_birds" title="Endemism in birds" class="mw-redirect">endemic</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>. Its closest relative is the <a href="/wiki/Sooty_Gull" title="Sooty Gull">Sooty Gull</a>. It is one of the world's rarest gulls, with a population of just 4,000 - 6,500 pairs. The species is classed as <a href="/wiki/Near_Threatened" title="Near Threatened">Near Threatened</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN" class="mw-redirect">IUCN</a>; <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> pressure and <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">oil</a> <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> are deemed the major threats. As is the case with many gulls, it has traditionally been placed in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Larus" title="Larus">Larus</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=White-eyed_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Morphology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span></h2>
<p>Adult White-eyed Gulls have a <a href="/wiki/Black" title="Black">black</a> hood in breeding <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>, which extends down onto the upper throat, and on the neck-sides is bordered below by a narrow <a href="/wiki/White" title="White">white</a> bar. The upperparts and inner upperwings are medium-dark <a href="/wiki/Grey" title="Grey">grey</a>; the breast is mid-grey but the rest of the underparts are white. The secondaries are black with a white trailing edge, and the primaries are black. The underwing is dark, and the tail white. Adults in non-breeding plumage are similar, but the hood is flecked white small white spots.</p>
<p>The White-eyed Gull acquires adult plumage at two to three years of age. Juvenile birds have a very different plumage - chocolate <a href="/wiki/Brown" title="Brown">brown</a> on the head, neck and breast, and with brown, broadly pale-fringed, <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a> to the upperparts and upperwings, and a black tail. In their first Winter, birds acquire greyer feathering on their head, breast and upperparts; the second-winter plumage is closer to that of the adult, but lacking the hood.</p>
<p>A distinctive feature of White-eyed Gull at all ages is its long slender <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a>. This is black in younger birds, but in adults it is deep <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a> with a black tip. The legs are <a href="/wiki/Yellow" title="Yellow">yellow</a> – dullest in younger birds, brightest in breeding plumaged adults. The eye itself is not white; the bird takes its name from white eye-crescents, which are present at all ages.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=White-eyed_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<p>The White-eyed Gull breeds on inshore islands with rocks and sandy beaches, such as the <a href="/wiki/Siyal_Islands" title="Siyal Islands">Siyal Islands</a>, in the Red Sea from July to September. For the rest of the year it occurs throughout the Red Sea, with some birds travelling to <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>.</p>
