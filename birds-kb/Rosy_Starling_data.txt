<p>The <b>rosy starling</b> (<i>Pastor roseus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Starling" title="Starling">starling</a> family, <a href="/wiki/Sturnidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Sturnidae">Sturnidae</a>, also known as the <b>rose-coloured starling</b> or <b>rose-coloured pastor</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-nie_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nie-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The species was recently placed in its own <a href="/wiki/Monotypic_genus" class="mw-redirect" title="Monotypic genus">monotypic genus</a>, <i><b>Pastor</b></i>, and split from <i><a href="/wiki/Sturnus" title="Sturnus">Sturnus</a></i>. This split is supported by recent studies, though other related species within its new genus are not yet known for certain.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosy_starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The genus name <i>Pastor</i>, and the old English name come from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>pastor</i>, "shepherd",<sup id="cite_ref-job_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and by extension a <a href="/wiki/Pastor" title="Pastor">pastor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The specific <i>roseus</i> is Latin for "rose-coloured".<sup id="cite_ref-job_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Formerly, some authorities also considered the <a href="/wiki/Maroon_oriole" title="Maroon oriole">maroon oriole</a> to be a species within the genus <i>Pastor</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosy_starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sturnus_roseus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Sturnus_roseus.jpg/200px-Sturnus_roseus.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="286" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Sturnus_roseus.jpg/300px-Sturnus_roseus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Sturnus_roseus.jpg/400px-Sturnus_roseus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="977" data-file-height="1395" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sturnus_roseus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Summer plumages:<br />Adult male (center). female (below), and juvenile (behind)</div></div></div>
<p>The adult rosy starling is highly distinctive, with its pink body, pale orange legs and bill, and glossy black head, wings and tail. Males in the breeding season have elongated head feathers which form a wispy crest that is fluffed and more prominent when the bird gets excited. In winter, the crest is shorter, and the edges of black feathers within the plumage become paler as the edges of these feather erode. Winter plumage in males is comparatively dull.
</p><p>Females in contrast have a short crest and lack the sharp separation between pink and black.
</p><p>The juvenile birds can be distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Common_starling" title="Common starling">common starling</a> (<i>Sturnus vulgaris</i>) by its obviously paler plumage and short yellow bill. Young birds moult into a subdued version of the adult plumage in autumn, yet these lack the crest. They do not acquire their adult plumage until they are nearly one year old in females, and nearly two years in males. The latter grow plumage very similar to adult females in their second year, but are distinguished by  longer crests and noticeably pale feather edges than female juvenile birds.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosy_starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rosy_starling_04.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Rosy_starling_04.jpg/220px-Rosy_starling_04.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Rosy_starling_04.jpg/330px-Rosy_starling_04.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Rosy_starling_04.jpg/440px-Rosy_starling_04.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6000" data-file-height="4000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rosy_starling_04.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rosy starling flock (Armenia)</div></div></div>
<p>The breeding range of this bird is in steppes, semi-deserts and deserts of Central Asia and Southeast Europe. It can be found from northwestern Mongolia via Dzungarei, Xinjiang, Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, Uzbekistan and Turkmenistan to southern Russia, Ukraine, Azerbaijan and Armenia . Its southern range extends to the north of Afghanistan and Iran . Irregular and rare broods are also observed outside of this area (Romania) <sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is a strong <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>, and winters in India and tropical Asia. In India in winter, it often appears to outnumber the local starlings and <a href="/wiki/Myna" title="Myna">mynas</a>. The rosy starling is a bird of steppe and open agricultural land. In years when <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a> and other insects are abundant, it will erupt well beyond its core range, with significant numbers reaching France,  the United Kingdom and Ireland. The starling is a summer visitor for northwestern Afghanistan, passage migrants in the rest of the Afghanistan and a regular winter visitor in most of Pakistan and India.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosy_starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rosy starlings are highly gregarious birds, and often form large, noisy flocks, which can on occasion be a pest for growers of cereal crops or orchards; the birds are strongly attracted to flowering trees. However, they are also greatly beneficial to farmers as they prey on pests such as locusts and grasshoppers, thereby limiting their numbers. The birds breed in tight colonies in a very short breeding season timed to take advantage of peak abundance of grasshoppers during May to June.<sup id="cite_ref-BOSA_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BOSA-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosy_starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The rosy starling is a colonial breeder, and like other starlings, is highly gregarious, forming large winter flocks. It also shares other species' omnivorous diet, although it prefers insects. 
The song is a typical starling mixture of squeaks and rattles, given with much wing trembling. 
In <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a>, China, farmers used to use <a href="/wiki/Insecticide" title="Insecticide">insecticide</a> to eliminate locust, which is costly and polluting. In the 1980s, experts found that rosy starlings which fly to Xinjiang farms and feed on locusts could be used for control instead. The experts begin to build artificial nests to attract rosy starlings, an effort reported to be so successful that the number of locusts was insufficient to feed the birds, causing many juveniles die for hunger. By the 2000s many Xinjiang farms greatly decreased the usage of insecticide.<sup id="cite_ref-BOSA_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BOSA-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_feeding">Food and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosy_starling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Food and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Chiefly fruits, berries, flower-nectar, cereal grains and insects. 
Specific observations of preferred food types made on the feeding habits of rosy starling are listed as:
Fruits and berries: <i><a href="/wiki/Ficus" title="Ficus">Ficus</a></i> (many species), <i><a href="/wiki/Lantana" title="Lantana">Lantana</a></i> spp., <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zizyphus_oenoplia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Zizyphus oenoplia (page does not exist)">Zizyphus oenoplia</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridelia_hamistoniana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bridelia hamistoniana (page does not exist)">Bridelia hamistoniana</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Streblus_asper" title="Streblus asper">Streblus asper</a></i>, grapes, mulberries (<i><a href="/wiki/Morus_(plant)" title="Morus (plant)">Morus</a></i>), dates, <i><a href="/wiki/Salvadora_persica" title="Salvadora persica">Salvadora persica</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Capparis_aphylla" class="mw-redirect" title="Capparis aphylla">Capparis aphylla</a></i> and chillies.
Flower-nectar: <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salmalia_persica&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Salmalia persica (page does not exist)">Salmalia persica</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bombax_insigne&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bombax insigne (page does not exist)">Bombax insigne</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Erythrina_indica" class="mw-redirect" title="Erythrina indica">Erythrina indica</a></i> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erythrina_suberosa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Erythrina suberosa (page does not exist)">Erythrina suberosa</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Butea_monosperma" title="Butea monosperma">Butea monosperma</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Careya_arborea" title="Careya arborea">Careya arborea</a></i>.
Cereal grains: Jowar and bajra.
Insects: largely locusts and grasshoppers, beetles of the families Lucanidae, Elateridae, Tenebrionidae, Buprestidae, Scarabaeidae and Curculionidae.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
