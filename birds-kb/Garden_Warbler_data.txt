<p>The <b>garden warbler</b> (<i>Sylvia borin</i>) is a common and widespread small <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> that breeds in most of Europe and in the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> to western Siberia. It is a plain, long-winged and long-tailed <a href="/wiki/Typical_warbler" title="Typical warbler">typical warbler</a> with brown upperparts and dull white underparts; the sexes are similar and juveniles resemble the adults. Its two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> differ only slightly and interbreed where their ranges overlap. Due to its lack of distinguishing features, this species can be confused with a number of other unstreaked warblers. The garden warbler's rich melodic song is similar to that of the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_blackcap" title="Eurasian blackcap">blackcap</a>, its closest relative, which competes with it for territory when nesting in the same woodland.
</p><p>The preferred breeding habitat in Eurasia is open woodland with dense low cover for nesting; despite its name, gardens are rarely occupied by this small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird. The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> of four or five blotched cream or white eggs is laid in a robust cup-shaped nest built near the ground and concealed by dense vegetation. The eggs are <a href="/wiki/Incubate_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Incubate (bird)">incubated</a> for 11–12&#160;days. The chicks are <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a>, hatching naked and with closed eyes, and are fed by both parents. They <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> about 10&#160;days after hatching. Only about a quarter of young birds survive their first year. The garden warbler is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a>. A wide range of habitats are used in Africa, but closed forest and treeless <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a> are both shunned. Insects are the main food in the breeding season, although fruit predominates when birds are fattening prior to migration, <a href="/wiki/Common_fig" title="Common fig">figs</a> being a particular favourite where available. These warblers have a mixed diet of insects and fruit in their African wintering grounds.
</p><p>The garden warbler is hunted by <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">Eurasian sparrowhawks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Domestic_cat" class="mw-redirect" title="Domestic cat">domestic cats</a>, and its eggs and nestlings are taken by a variety of mammalian and avian predators. It may be host to various <a href="/wiki/Flea" title="Flea">fleas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mite" title="Mite">mites</a> and internal <a href="/wiki/Parasitism" title="Parasitism">parasites</a>, and it is a host of the <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common cuckoo</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasite</a>. The large and fairly stable numbers and huge range of the garden warbler mean that it is classed as <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a> by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a>. Despite a small population decline in much of its European range, the bird's breeding distribution is expanding northwards in <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The genus <i>Sylvia</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Typical_warbler" title="Typical warbler">typical warblers</a>, forms part of a large family of Old World warblers, the <a href="/wiki/Sylviidae" title="Sylviidae">Sylviidae</a>. Fossils from France show that the genus dates back at least 20&#160;million years.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason11_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason11-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The garden warbler and its nearest relative, the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_blackcap" title="Eurasian blackcap">blackcap</a>, are an ancient <a href="/wiki/Superspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Superspecies">species pair</a> which diverged very early from the rest of the genus, between 12 and 16&#160;million years ago. In the course of time, these two species have become sufficiently distinctive that they have been placed in separate subgenera, with the blackcap in subgenus <i>Sylvia</i> and the garden warbler in <i>Epilais</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-shir_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> These <a href="/wiki/Sister_group" title="Sister group">sister species</a> have a breeding range which extends farther northeast than all other <i>Sylvia</i> species except the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_whitethroat" title="Lesser whitethroat">lesser whitethroat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_whitethroat" title="Common whitethroat">common whitethroat</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-zoolscripta_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zoolscripta-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The nearest relatives of the garden warbler outside the sister group are believed to be the <a href="/wiki/African_hill_babbler" title="African hill babbler">African hill babbler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dohrn%27s_thrush-babbler" class="mw-redirect" title="Dohrn&#39;s thrush-babbler">Dohrn's thrush-babbler</a>, both of which should probably be placed in <i>Sylvia</i> rather than their current genera, <i>Pseudoalcippe</i> and <i>Horizorhinus</i> respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-malaria_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-malaria-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BMC_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMC-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The garden warbler was given the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> <i>Motacilla borin</i> by the Dutch naturalist <a href="/wiki/Pieter_Boddaert" title="Pieter Boddaert">Pieter Boddaert</a> in 1783.<sup id="cite_ref-Boddaert_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boddaert-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The current genus name is from <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">Modern Latin</a> <i>silvia</i>, a woodland <a href="/wiki/Nymph" title="Nymph">nymph</a>, related to <i>silva</i>, a wood.<sup id="cite_ref-job59_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job59-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The specific <i>borin</i> is derived from a local name for the bird in the <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a> area of Italy; it derives from the Latin <i>bos</i>, ox, because the warbler was believed to accompany oxen.<sup id="cite_ref-job75_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job75-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are two recognised <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker339_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker339-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>Sylvia borin borin</i> (Boddaert, 1783), the <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">nominate subspecies</a>, breeds in western, northern and central Europe to Finland, central Poland, western Hungary and Bosnia.</li>
<li><i>S. b. woodwardi</i> (<a href="/wiki/Richard_Bowdler_Sharpe" title="Richard Bowdler Sharpe">Sharpe</a>, 1877), named for Sharpe's collaborator <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Barham_Woodward" title="Bernard Barham Woodward">Bernard Barham Woodward</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Sharpe_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sharpe-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> breeds in eastern Europe and temperate Asia east to western <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Intermediate birds occur where the recognised forms meet and interbreed, and have sometimes been given subspecies status, including <i>S. b. kreczmeri</i> in Poland and <i>S. b. pateffi</i> in Bulgaria, but these are not generally accepted as valid <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker339_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker339-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The garden warbler is 14&#160;cm (5.5&#160;in) long with a 7.6–8.4&#160;cm (3.0–3.3&#160;in) wing length.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker339_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker339-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The weight is typically 16–22&#160;g (0.56–0.78&#160;oz), but can be up to 35.5&#160;g (1.25&#160;oz) for birds preparing to migrate.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> It is a plain, long-winged and long-tailed bird with unstreaked olive-brown upperparts and dull white underparts.<sup id="cite_ref-shir73_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir73-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> It has a whitish eyering and a faint pale <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a>, and there is a buff wash to the throat and flanks. The eye is black, the legs are bluish-grey and the strong bill has a grey upper and paler grey lower <a href="/wiki/Mandible" title="Mandible">mandible</a>. The male and female are indistinguishable by external appearance including size. Juveniles have a looser plumage than an adult, with paler and greyer upperparts and a buff tone to the underparts. The eastern subspecies <i>S. b. woodwardi</i> is slightly larger and paler than the nominate form with a greyer tone to the upperparts and whiter underparts.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker339_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker339-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The subspecies are hard to distinguish visually where they occur together in Africa, but a wing length greater than 80&#160;mm (3.1&#160;in) confirms <i>S. b. woodwardi</i> when birds are trapped.<sup id="cite_ref-shir73_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir73-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The plain appearance of the garden warbler means that it can be confused with several other species. The <a href="/wiki/Melodious_warbler" title="Melodious warbler">melodious</a> and <a href="/wiki/Icterine_warbler" title="Icterine warbler">icterine warblers</a> usually have long bills and a yellowish tint to their plumage. The <a href="/wiki/Booted_warbler" title="Booted warbler">booted warbler</a> is similar in colour, although it is smaller, more delicately built and has a flesh-coloured bill. <a href="/wiki/Western_olivaceous_warbler" title="Western olivaceous warbler">Western</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_olivaceous_warbler" title="Eastern olivaceous warbler">eastern olivaceous warblers</a> are also relatively small, and have white outer tail feathers as well as a pinkish bill. Juvenile <a href="/wiki/Barred_warbler" title="Barred warbler">barred warblers</a>, which lack the obvious barring of adults, are much larger than garden warblers and have a pale double wingbar.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker339_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker339-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Juvenile garden warblers have a partial <a href="/wiki/Moulting" title="Moulting">moult</a> mainly involving the body plumage between June and September prior to <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migration</a>. Adults also have a similar, but sometimes more extensive, partial moult in late summer, and a complete moult in their African wintering areas before the return migration.<sup id="cite_ref-shir73_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir73-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><div class="mediaContainer" style="width:220px"><audio id="mwe_player_0" controls="" preload="none" style="width:220px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="33.163514739229" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="Garden_Warbler_(Sylvia_borin)_(W1CDR0001463_BD12).ogg" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/f/fc/Garden_Warbler_%28Sylvia_borin%29_%28W1CDR0001463_BD12%29.ogg/Garden_Warbler_%28Sylvia_borin%29_%28W1CDR0001463_BD12%29.ogg.mp3" type="audio/mpeg" data-title="MP3" data-shorttitle="MP3" data-transcodekey="mp3" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="137088" /><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fc/Garden_Warbler_%28Sylvia_borin%29_%28W1CDR0001463_BD12%29.ogg" type="audio/ogg; codecs=&quot;vorbis&quot;" data-title="Original Ogg file (211 kbps)" data-shorttitle="Ogg source" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="210899" /></audio></div>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Garden_Warbler_(Sylvia_borin)_(W1CDR0001463_BD12).ogg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Male singing, recorded in Surrey, England</div></div></div>
<p>The male's song, usually delivered by birds in dense cover,<sup id="cite_ref-shir73_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir73-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> is a rich musical warbling usually delivered in bursts of a few seconds duration, but sometimes for longer periods. The song is confusable with that of the <a href="/wiki/Blackcap" class="mw-redirect" title="Blackcap">blackcap</a>, although compared to that species it is slightly lower-pitched, less broken into discrete song segments and more mellow. Both species have a quiet subsong, a muted version of the full song, which is much more difficult to separate.<sup id="cite_ref-simms56_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-simms56-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The most frequent call of the garden warbler is a sharp <i>kek-kek</i>, which is repeated rapidly when the bird is alarmed.<sup id="cite_ref-Simms68_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simms68-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> A quiet rasping <i>tchurr-r-r-r</i> resembling the main call of the <a href="/wiki/Common_whitethroat" title="Common whitethroat">common whitethroat</a> is also sometimes heard.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker339_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker339-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The juvenile has a <i>quia</i> alarm vocalisation.<sup id="cite_ref-shir73_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir73-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Subsong may be heard on the wintering grounds in Africa, developing into the full song in March and April prior to the return to Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-simms56_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-simms56-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Holies_-_geograph.org.uk_-_915202.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A woodland glade" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/The_Holies_-_geograph.org.uk_-_915202.jpg/220px-The_Holies_-_geograph.org.uk_-_915202.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/The_Holies_-_geograph.org.uk_-_915202.jpg/330px-The_Holies_-_geograph.org.uk_-_915202.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/The_Holies_-_geograph.org.uk_-_915202.jpg/440px-The_Holies_-_geograph.org.uk_-_915202.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Holies_-_geograph.org.uk_-_915202.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Deciduous woodland is the preferred breeding habitat.</div></div></div>
<p>The garden warbler breeds in most of Europe between the 12–28&#160;°C (54–82&#160;°F) <a href="/wiki/Isotherm_(contour_line)" class="mw-redirect" title="Isotherm (contour line)">isotherms</a> and east across temperate Asia to the <a href="/wiki/Yenisei_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Yenisei River">Yenisei River</a> in Siberia. Its range extends further north than any other <i>Sylvia</i> warbler. All populations are migratory, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a> as far south as South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Birds from central Europe initially migrate to the southwest, reorientating to the south or southeast once in Africa, although Scandinavian migrants may head south through the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> and across the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. <i>S. b. woodwardi</i> reaches Africa by a more easterly route, many birds passing through the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a>. When garden warblers cross the Sahara, they fly at night, resting motionless and without feeding in suitable shade during the day.<sup id="cite_ref-Newton158_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newton158-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> During their journey, they can metabolise not only <a href="/wiki/Body_fat" class="mw-redirect" title="Body fat">body fat</a> but also up to 19% of their breast and leg muscles and 39% of their digestive tract. Many birds pause for a few days to feed after the desert crossing before continuing further south.<sup id="cite_ref-shir84_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir84-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The nominate subspecies occurs in the western and central parts of the winter range,<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> although some birds occur as far east as Kenya.<sup id="cite_ref-ZTP0_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZTP0-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <i>S. b. woodwardi</i> winters in eastern and southern Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker339_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker339-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Movements in Africa are poorly known, although at least some birds return to the same location in subsequent years.<sup id="cite_ref-shir84_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir84-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> There are only a handful of records of birds recorded in Europe in winter, from <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>, the UK and Ireland. Spring migration routes are poorly known, but appear to lie more directly across the Mediterranean.<sup id="cite_ref-shir84_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir84-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> This warbler has occurred as a vagrant in Afghanistan, Djibouti, Iceland, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Tom%C3%A9_and_Pr%C3%ADncipe" title="São Tomé and Príncipe">São Tomé and Príncipe</a>, Somalia, Yemen, <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">Svalbard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jan_Mayen" title="Jan Mayen">Jan Mayen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The breeding habitat of the garden warbler is open areas with dense bushes, including thickets and woodland edges. Shady areas and a bushy or <a href="/wiki/Herbaceous_plant" title="Herbaceous plant">herbaceous</a> undergrowth are preferred, as are woods adjacent to rivers or <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed beds</a>; in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> it favours thickets on the shores of small lakes. A tolerance of <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alder" title="Alder">alder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a> allows it to breed farther north and at higher altitudes than any other European <i>Sylvia</i> warbler. Mature conifers and dense plantations are avoided, although young conifer plantations with thick undergrowth are suitable for nesting. Despite its name, it is not a bird of gardens. In Africa, a wide range of habitats with trees are used, although closed forests and arid areas are again avoided. This warbler occurs at altitudes of up to 2,600&#160;m (8,500&#160;ft) in suitable mountain woodland, although in East Africa it is usually found at a lower altitude than the blackcap, and in moister areas than the common whitethroat.<sup id="cite_ref-shir84_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir84-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Garden_Warbler.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Garden_Warbler.jpg/220px-Garden_Warbler.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Garden_Warbler.jpg/330px-Garden_Warbler.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c5/Garden_Warbler.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="342" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Garden_Warbler.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Singing male in England</div></div></div>
<p>Garden warblers first breed when they are one year old, and are mainly <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a>, although the male at least may sometimes deviate from this.<sup id="cite_ref-simms56_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-simms56-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> When males return to their breeding areas, they establish a <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territory</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-simms56_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-simms56-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <i>Sylvia</i> warblers are unusual in that they vigorously defend their territories against other species in their genus as well as <a href="/wiki/Conspecificity" class="mw-redirect" title="Conspecificity">conspecifics</a>. Blackcaps and garden warblers use identical habits in the same woods, yet aggressive interactions mean that their territories never overlap.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason42_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason42-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-garcia_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-garcia-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Similar songs are a feature of the <i>Sylvia</i> warblers as a group, and it has been suggested that this promotes <a href="/wiki/Interspecific_competition" title="Interspecific competition">interspecific competition</a> and helps to segregate territories between related species.<sup id="cite_ref-mason85_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mason85-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cody_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cody-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> However, it appears more likely from later studies that segregation of <a href="/wiki/Sympatry" title="Sympatry">sympatric</a> species, other than the blackcap and garden warbler, is due to subtle differences in habitat preferences rather than interspecies aggression.<sup id="cite_ref-martin_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-martin-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> There are typically 3–9 territories per hectare (1.2–3.6 per acre), but in prime habitat, such as moist willow or birch woodland or young <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> regrowth, there may be more than 10 pairs per hectare (4 per acre).<sup id="cite_ref-shir84_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir84-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Individual territories are similar in size to those of blackcaps at 0.2–0.76&#160;ha (0.49–1.88 acres).<sup id="cite_ref-Mason39_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason39-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A male attracts a female to his territory through song and a display which involves rapid wing beating while perched. He will also build a number of simple nests (cock's nests) to show to his mate, although only rarely will she complete the structure, usually starting afresh.<sup id="cite_ref-simms56_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-simms56-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The nest is concealed in vegetation, the nature of which depends on local availability. <i><a href="/wiki/Rubus" title="Rubus">Rubus</a></i> species are commonly used in temperate regions, with willow predominating in alpine valleys. Sometimes, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Stinging_nettle" class="mw-redirect" title="Stinging nettle">stinging nettles</a>, the nest may be built around a number of vertical stems, in the manner of a <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_reed_warbler" title="Eurasian reed warbler">reed warbler</a>. The nest is normally between 0.3 and 1.2&#160;m (0.98 and 3.94&#160;ft) above the ground, and very rarely higher than 2&#160;m (6.6&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-shir84_17-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir84-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The nest is a cup of dry grass, moss and twigs, with a soft lining of finer plant material or hair.<sup id="cite_ref-simms56_14-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-simms56-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It is larger and heavier than a blackcap's nest, averaging 8.3&#160;cm (3.3&#160;in) high and 11.2&#160;cm (4.4&#160;in) wide with a cup 5.5&#160;cm (2.2&#160;in) deep and 6.3&#160;cm (2.5&#160;in) wide.<sup id="cite_ref-vlad_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vlad-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Seebohm_Garden_Warbler_egg.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Seebohm_Garden_Warbler_egg.jpg/170px-Seebohm_Garden_Warbler_egg.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="193" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Seebohm_Garden_Warbler_egg.jpg/255px-Seebohm_Garden_Warbler_egg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Seebohm_Garden_Warbler_egg.jpg/340px-Seebohm_Garden_Warbler_egg.jpg 2x" data-file-width="368" data-file-height="417" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Seebohm_Garden_Warbler_egg.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Painting of an egg</div></div></div>
<p>The first eggs are laid in late April in southern Germany, early May in northwest Europe, and late May in Finland. The season is prolonged with some birds nesting as late as July.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is typically four or five eggs (range two to six), which are usually whitish or buff with grey, purple and brown blotches.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The average size of the egg is 20&#160;mm ×&#160;15&#160;mm (0.79&#160;in ×&#160;0.59&#160;in), and it weighs 2.2&#160;g (0.078&#160;oz), of which 6% is shell.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The eggs are <a href="/wiki/Incubate_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Incubate (bird)">incubated</a> for 11–12&#160;days by both adults, although only the female stays on the nest at night. The chicks are <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a>, hatching naked and with closed eyes, and are fed by both parents. They <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> about 10&#160;days after hatching (range 9–12), leaving the nest shortly before they are able to fly.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_12-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> They are assisted with feeding for a further two weeks, and the family may stay together for a few days after that.<sup id="cite_ref-shir84_17-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir84-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The short incubation and fledging times may be a result of predation pressure, the rapid development of the chicks enabling them to leave the vulnerable nest as early as possible, even while still flightless. This avoids the possible loss of an entire brood, but means that the adults must provide food to the young for a significant period until they can fend for themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-shir34_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir34-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> One brood per year is normal,<sup id="cite_ref-rspb_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> although a few second broods are known.<sup id="cite_ref-shir84_17-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir84-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The southward migration starts in mid-July, with larger numbers departing in August and peaking in early September. Most adults have gone by mid-September, although juveniles may linger for another month.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_12-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On average, just over 50% of breeding pairs are successful in producing at least one fledged young from a nest, with early breeding and low population density being factors increasing success. In the UK, more than 50% of the failures are due to predation and about 30% result from food shortages in bad weather.<sup id="cite_ref-shir84_17-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir84-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The annual survival rate is about 50% for adults and 26% for juveniles in their first year. The typical lifespan is two years,<sup id="cite_ref-bto_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> but a bird in Sweden lived to ten&#160;years and two&#160;months<sup id="cite_ref-euring_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euring-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Much greater ages, up to 24&#160;years, have been recorded in captive garden warblers.<sup id="cite_ref-shir84_17-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir84-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ficus_carica0.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Ficus_carica0.jpg/220px-Ficus_carica0.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Ficus_carica0.jpg/330px-Ficus_carica0.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Ficus_carica0.jpg/440px-Ficus_carica0.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ficus_carica0.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Common_fig" title="Common fig">common fig</a> is a popular food prior to migration.</div></div></div>
<p>The garden warbler feeds mainly on insects in the breeding season, although other small <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> such as spiders are also eaten. It picks its prey off leaves and twigs, sometimes hovering to do so. It normally forages at less than 6&#160;m (20&#160;ft) above the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_12-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> After nesting, there is a genetically controlled switch to a fruit diet, although insects are still consumed while the birds fatten prior to migration;<sup id="cite_ref-shir84_17-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir84-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> birds gain weight more rapidly from a diet containing both fruit and insects than either alone.<sup id="cite_ref-Newton116_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newton116-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Berries and other soft fruit are preferred,<sup id="cite_ref-shir84_17-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir84-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Common_fig" title="Common fig">figs</a> are particularly important for birds preparing to migrate.<sup id="cite_ref-thomas_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thomas-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> This predilection gives rise to the Italian <i>beccafico</i> (fig pecker) and Portuguese <i>felosa-das-figueiras</i> (fig-tree warbler) as names for this species.<sup id="cite_ref-simms56_14-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-simms56-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> On this diet a bird can gain weight quickly and the liver increases the rate at which it produces <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid" title="Fatty acid">fatty acids</a> for storage in <a href="/wiki/Adipose_tissue" title="Adipose tissue">adipose tissue</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Newton116_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newton116-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Biebach_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Biebach-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Africa, the warbler eats insects as well as berries, and the fruits of the introduced <a href="/wiki/Lantana_camara" title="Lantana camara">Spanish flag</a> is a favourite where present.<sup id="cite_ref-simms56_14-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-simms56-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> An increase in body mass occurs again before the northward migration, birds fattening even more rapidly than prior to their southward journey.<sup id="cite_ref-Ottosson_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ottosson-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Most <a href="/wiki/Internal_organ" class="mw-redirect" title="Internal organ">internal organs</a> (including the liver, spleen, intestines, kidneys and heart) and the flight muscles lose weight during the journey over the Sahara, although the <a href="/wiki/Testes" class="mw-redirect" title="Testes">testes</a> quadruple in mass in preparation for the breeding season.<sup id="cite_ref-Bauchinger_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bauchinger-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Unlike drier-habitat species like the common whitethroat, they leave from <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a> rather than the treeless <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a> further north.<sup id="cite_ref-Ottosson_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ottosson-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Fruit is normally picked by a perched bird, although there is a record of a <a href="/wiki/Morus_(plant)" title="Morus (plant)">mulberry</a> fruit being taken in flight. Garden warblers often feed with <a href="/wiki/Conspecificity" class="mw-redirect" title="Conspecificity">conspecifics</a> and other fruit-eating <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerines</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Over 35 plant species have been recorded as food for this warbler just in central Europe, with many additional species being consumed in the Mediterranean region and on the African wintering grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-shir84_17-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir84-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators_and_parasites">Predators and parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Predators and parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo_chris_romeiks_CHR0431.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A bird with a grey back, pale underparts and long tail perched on a post" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo_chris_romeiks_CHR0431.jpg/220px-Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo_chris_romeiks_CHR0431.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="162" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo_chris_romeiks_CHR0431.jpg/330px-Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo_chris_romeiks_CHR0431.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo_chris_romeiks_CHR0431.jpg/440px-Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo_chris_romeiks_CHR0431.jpg 2x" data-file-width="903" data-file-height="666" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo_chris_romeiks_CHR0431.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common cuckoo</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasite</a> of the garden warbler.</div></div></div>
<p>The main predators of the garden warbler are <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">Eurasian sparrowhawks</a> and domestic cats. Some <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcons</a> will also take adults, and the <a href="/wiki/Eleonora%27s_falcon" title="Eleonora&#39;s falcon">Eleonora's falcon</a> hunts the garden warbler and many other small passerines as they migrate across the Mediterranean.<sup id="cite_ref-walter_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-walter-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hogstedt_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hogstedt-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dierschke_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dierschke-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_jay" title="Eurasian jay">Eurasian jays</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_magpie" title="Eurasian magpie">Eurasian magpies</a> take the eggs and young of warblers, as do mammals such as <a href="/wiki/Stoat" title="Stoat">stoats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Least_weasel" title="Least weasel">weasels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Squirrel" title="Squirrel">squirrels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason49_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason49-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gibbons_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gibbons-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The garden warbler is a host of the common cuckoo, a brood parasite.<sup id="cite_ref-mann_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mann-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The blackcap has a much lower level of parasitism than its relative because the cuckoo's eggs are often rejected.<sup id="cite_ref-honza_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-honza-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>External parasites of the garden warbler include the <a href="/wiki/Flea" title="Flea">fleas</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Ceratophyllus_gallinae" title="Ceratophyllus gallinae">Ceratophyllus gallinae</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Moorhen_flea" title="Moorhen flea">Dasypsyllus gallinulae</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/Mite" title="Mite">mite</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Syringophilosis_borini" title="Syringophilosis borini">Syringophilosis borini</a></i>, named after its host.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bochkov_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bochkov-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Two species of <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoan</a> parasites in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Isospora" title="Isospora">Isospora</a></i> occur in garden warblers, <i><a href="/wiki/Isospora_sylvianthina" title="Isospora sylvianthina">I. sylvianthina</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Isospora_sylviae" title="Isospora sylviae">I. sylviae</a></i>. Samples from two sites showed infection levels above 74% and 28% respectively for the two species. The extent of infection does not impact on the bird's body mass or the amount of body fat.<sup id="cite_ref-dolnik_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dolnik-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Three strains of another protozoan, <i><a href="/wiki/Haemoproteus_parabelopolskyi" title="Haemoproteus parabelopolskyi">Haemoproteus parabelopolskyi</a></i> are found only in the garden warbler, and form a <a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">monophyletic</a> group. Seventeen further members of that group are found only in the blackcap, and another three occur in the African hill babbler, supporting the shared ancestry of the three bird species.<sup id="cite_ref-malaria_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-malaria-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The garden warbler has a very large range of 9,650,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (3,700,000&#160;mi<sup>2</sup>), and its population in Europe is estimated at 17–31&#160;million breeding pairs. Allowing for birds breeding in Asia, the total population is between 54–124&#160;million individuals. There is no evidence of any serious decline in numbers, so it is classified by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> as being of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There has been a slight decline in numbers in Europe since 1980,<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> although the Scandinavian population is growing.<sup id="cite_ref-shir84_17-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shir84-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Climate change appears to be affecting the migration pattern of the garden warbler and blackcap. Both are arriving in Europe earlier than previously, and blackcaps and juvenile (but not adult) garden warblers are departing nearly two weeks later than in the 1980s. Birds of both species are longer-winged and lighter than in the past, suggesting a longer migration as the breeding range expands northwards.<sup id="cite_ref-kovacs_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kovacs-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garden_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his <i><a href="/wiki/History_of_Animals" title="History of Animals">History of Animals</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> considered that the garden warbler eventually <a href="/wiki/Metamorphosis" title="Metamorphosis">metamorphosed</a> into a <a href="/wiki/Blackcap" class="mw-redirect" title="Blackcap">blackcap</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-arnason_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arnason-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The composer <a href="/wiki/Olivier_Messiaen" title="Olivier Messiaen">Olivier Messiaen</a> used the song of the garden warbler as the basis for his 1971 solo piano piece <i><a href="/wiki/La_fauvette_des_jardins" class="mw-redirect" title="La fauvette des jardins">La fauvette des jardins</a></i>, the title being the French name of the species.<sup id="cite_ref-fauvette_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fauvette-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> His <a href="/wiki/Turangal%C3%AEla-Symphonie" title="Turangalîla-Symphonie">Turangalîla-Symphonie</a>, a major work inspired by the legend of <a href="/wiki/Tristan_and_Iseult" title="Tristan and Iseult">Tristan and Iseult</a>, has a summer garden scene as its sixth movement. This features the song of the warbler, along with those of the nightingale and <a href="/wiki/Common_blackbird" title="Common blackbird">blackbird</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Johnson_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The garden warbler is prized as a gastronomic delicacy in Mediterranean countries. French epicure <a href="/wiki/Jean_Anthelme_Brillat-Savarin" title="Jean Anthelme Brillat-Savarin">Jean Anthelme Brillat-Savarin</a> said of the warbler when cooked like <a href="/wiki/Ortolan_bunting" title="Ortolan bunting">ortolan bunting</a> "if it were the size of a pheasant, it would be worth an acre of land".<sup id="cite_ref-larrouse_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-larrouse-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> An Italian stuffed sardine dish <i>sarde a beccafico</i> derives its name from its supposed resemblance to the cooked birds, known in that country as <i>beccafico</i>, fig-pecker.<sup id="cite_ref-Locatelli_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Locatelli-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Old names for the garden warbler, such as strawsmear, small straw and haychat, are often derived from its choice of nesting material, although the commonest of the English folk names was "pettychaps". These names were often shared with other warblers including the blackcap, common whitethroat and <a href="/wiki/Common_chiffchaff" title="Common chiffchaff">common chiffchaff</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason13_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason13-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cocker_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cocker-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
