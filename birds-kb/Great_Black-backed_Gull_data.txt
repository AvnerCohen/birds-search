<p>The <b>Great Black-backed Gull</b> (<i>Larus marinus</i>) is the largest member of the <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a> family. It breeds on the <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> coasts and islands of the North Atlantic and is fairly sedentary, though some Black-backs move farther south or inland to large lakes or reservoirs.</p>
<p>The Great Black-backed Gull was one of the many species originally described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in his 18th-century work, <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>, and it still bears its original name of <i>Larus marinus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Black-backed_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Black-backed_Gull_juvenile_2012RWD.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Great_Black-backed_Gull_juvenile_2012RWD.jpg/220px-Great_Black-backed_Gull_juvenile_2012RWD.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Great_Black-backed_Gull_juvenile_2012RWD.jpg/330px-Great_Black-backed_Gull_juvenile_2012RWD.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Great_Black-backed_Gull_juvenile_2012RWD.jpg/440px-Great_Black-backed_Gull_juvenile_2012RWD.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Black-backed_Gull_juvenile_2012RWD.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Juvenile</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This is the largest gull in the world, noticeably outsizing a <a href="/wiki/Herring_Gull" title="Herring Gull">Herring Gull</a>. Only a few other gulls, including <a href="/wiki/Pallas%27s_Gull" title="Pallas's Gull">Pallas's Gull</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glaucous_Gull" title="Glaucous Gull">Glaucous Gull</a>, come close to matching this species' size. It is 64–79 cm (25–31 in) long with a 1.5–1.7 m (4&#160;ft 10&#160;in–5&#160;ft 7&#160;in) wingspan and a body weight of 0.75–2.3 kg (1.7–5.1 lb).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> In 209 adults from the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" title="North Atlantic" class="mw-redirect">North Atlantic</a>, males were found to average 1,830&#160;g (4.0&#160;lb) and females were found to average 1,488&#160;g (3.28&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> An exceptionally large Glaucous Gull was found outweigh any known Great Black-backed Gull, although usually that species is slightly smaller.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> It is bulky and imposing in appearance with a large, powerful bill. The standard measurements are as follows: the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> is 5.4 to 7.25 cm (2.1 to 2.85 in), the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> is 44.5 to 53 cm (17.5 to 21 in) and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> is 6.6 to 8.8 cm (2.6 to 3.5 in).<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The adult Great Black-backed Gull is fairly distinctive, as no other very large gull with blackish coloration on its upper-wings generally occurs in the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" title="North Atlantic" class="mw-redirect">North Atlantic</a>. It is grayish-black on the wings and back, with conspicuous, contrasting white "mirrors" at the wing tips. The legs are pinkish, and the bill is yellow or yellow-pink with some orange or red near tip of lower bill. The adult <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Black-backed_Gull" title="Lesser Black-backed Gull">Lesser Black-backed Gull</a> has a mantle that can range from slate-gray to brownish-colored but it is never as dark as the larger species. The Lesser Black-backed is also distinctly smaller, with different bare part coloration. A few superficially similar dark-backed, fairly large gulls occur in the Pacific ocean or in the tropics, such as the <a href="/wiki/Slaty-backed_Gull" title="Slaty-backed Gull">Slaty-backed</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Western_Gull" title="Western Gull">Western</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kelp_Gull" title="Kelp Gull">Kelp Gull</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Juvenile birds of under a year old have scaly, checkered black-brown upper parts, the head and underparts streaked with gray brown, and a neat wing pattern. The face and nape are paler and the wing flight feathers are blackish-brown. The juvenile's tail is white with zigzag bars and spots at base and a broken blackish band near the tip. The bill of the juvenile is brownish-black with white tip and the legs dark bluish-gray with some pink tones. As the young gull ages, the gray-brown coloration gradually fades to more contrasting plumage and the bill darkens to black before growing paler. By the third year, the young gulls resemble a streakier, dirtier-looking version of the adult. They take at least four years to reach maturity, development in this species being somewhat slower than that of other large gulls. The call is a deep "laughing" cry.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HBW_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Black-backed_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Range">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Range">Range</span></h2>
<p>This species can be found breeding in coastal areas from the extreme northwest portion of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, through much of coastal <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> coasts, to the coasts of northwestern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. Across the northern portion of the Atlantic, this gull is distributed in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and on the Atlantic coasts of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Though formerly mainly just a non-breeding visitor south of Canada in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, the species has spread to include several colonies in the <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> states and now extends as far south as a breeding bird to <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>. Individuals breeding in harsher environments will migrate south, wintering on northern coasts of Europe from the Baltic Sea to southern <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, and down North America to as far south as the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> and off the coast of northern <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Black-backed_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span></h2>
<p>The Great Black-backed Gull is found in a variety of coastal habitats, including rocky and sandy coasts and estuaries, as well as inland wetland habitats, such as lakes, ponds, rivers, wet fields and moorland. They are generally found within striking distance of large bodies of water while ranging inland. Today, it is a common fixture at refuse dumps both along coasts and relatively far into land. It generally breeds in areas free of or largely inaccessible to terrestrial predators, such as vegetated islands, sand dunes, flat-topped stacks, building roofs and sometimes amongst bushes on salt-marsh islands. During the winter, the Great Black-backed Gull often travels far out to sea to feed.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Black-backed_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GBBeatsDuckling.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/GBBeatsDuckling.jpg/220px-GBBeatsDuckling.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/GBBeatsDuckling.jpg/330px-GBBeatsDuckling.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/GBBeatsDuckling.jpg/440px-GBBeatsDuckling.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GBBeatsDuckling.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Great Black-Backed Gull grabs an Eider Duckling.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Great Black-backed Gulls are opportunistic and will eat almost any small organism that they access. Like most gulls, they will eat almost anything that they can swallow. They get most of their food from scavenging, with <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">refuse</a> locally comprising more than half of their diet. Like most gulls, they also capture fish with some regularity and will readily capture any fish smaller than itself found close to the surface of the water.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HBW_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike most other <i>Larus</i> gulls, they are highly predatory and frequently <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">hunt</a> and kill any prey smaller than themselves, behaving more like a <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptor</a> than a typical larid gull. Lacking the razor-sharp <a href="/wiki/Talon_(anatomy)" title="Talon (anatomy)" class="mw-redirect">talons</a> and curved, tearing beak of a raptor, the Great Black-backed Gull relies on aggression, physical strength and endurance when hunting. When attacking other animals, they usually attack <a href="/wiki/Sea_bird" title="Sea bird" class="mw-redirect">sea bird</a> eggs, nestlings or fledgings at the nest, including smaller gull species as well as <a href="/wiki/Eider" title="Eider">eiders</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup>, <a href="/wiki/Gannet" title="Gannet">gannets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">terns</a><sup id="cite_ref-Tern_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tern-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> and various <a href="/wiki/Alcidae" title="Alcidae" class="mw-redirect">alcids</a>. Adult or fledged juveniles of various bird species have also been predaceously attacked, including <i><a href="/wiki/Anas" title="Anas">Anas</a></i> ducks, <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">terns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glossy_ibis" title="Glossy ibis" class="mw-redirect">glossy ibises</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Rock_pigeon" title="Rock pigeon" class="mw-redirect">rock pigeons</a>. When attacking other flying birds, the Great Black-backed Gulls often pursue them and attack them on the wing by jabbing with their bill and hoping to exhaust the other bird.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> They will also catch flying <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerines</a>, which they typically target while the small birds are exhausted from migration and swallow them immediately.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Great Black-backed Gull also feed on land animals, including <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a> at garbage dumps and even sickly <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">lambs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HBW_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Most foods are swallowed whole, including most fish and even other gulls. When foods are too large to be swallowed at once, they will sometimes be shaken in the bill until they fall apart into pieces. Like some other gulls, when capturing <a href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc" class="mw-redirect">molluscs</a> or other hard-surfaced foods such as <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>, they will fly into the air with it and drop it on rocks or hard earth to crack it open. Alternate foods, including <a href="/wiki/Berries" title="Berries" class="mw-redirect">berries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, are eaten when available. They will readily exploit easy food sources, including <a href="/wiki/Chum_salmon" title="Chum salmon">chum</a> lines made by boats at sea. They are skilled <a href="/wiki/Kleptoparasitism" title="Kleptoparasitism">kleptoparasites</a> who will readily pirate fish and other prey captured by other birds and dominate over other gulls when they encounter them. At tern colonies, <a href="/wiki/American_Herring_Gull" title="American Herring Gull">American Herring Gulls</a> occasionally also attack nestling and fledging terns but in a great majority of cases were immediately pirated of their catch by Great Black-backs.<sup id="cite_ref-Tern_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tern-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Due to their method of using intimidation while encountering other water birds, the species has been referred to as a "merciless tyrant".<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Naturally, these gulls attracted to surface activity of large marine animals, from <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_bluefin_tuna" title="Atlantic bluefin tuna">Atlantic bluefin tuna</a> to <a href="/wiki/Humpback_whale" title="Humpback whale">Humpback whales</a>, in order to capture fish driven to the surface by such creatures.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HBW_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Black-backed_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h2>
<p>This species breeds singly or in small colonies, with pair formation occurring in March or April. They make a lined nest on the ground often on top of a rocky stack, fallen log or other obstructing object which can block the contents from the elements. Usually, several nest scrapes are made before the one deemed best by the parents is selected and is then lined with grass, seaweed or moss or objects such as rope or plastic. The female lays one to three eggs sometime between late April and late June. The eggs are greenish-brown with dark speckles and blotches. Both parents participate in the incubation stage, which lasts for approximately 28 days. The breeding pair are devoted parents who both take shifts brooding the young, defending the nest and gathering food. Young Great Black-backed Gulls leave the nest area at 50 days of age and may remain with their parents for an overall period of around six months, though most fledglings choose to congregate with other immature gulls in the search for food by fall. These gulls reach breeding maturity when they obtain adult plumage at four years, though may not successfully breed until they are six years old.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Black-backed_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Longevity and mortality">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Longevity_and_mortality">Longevity and mortality</span></h2>
<p>This is a relatively long-living bird. The maximum recorded age for a wild Great Black-backed Gull is 27.1 years.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Mortality typically occurs in the early stages of life, when harsh weather conditions (including flooding) and starvation can threaten them, as well as predators. Chicks and eggs are preyed on by <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a>, other <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">raccoons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Bald_Eagle" title="Bald Eagle">Bald Eagle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Golden_Eagle" title="Golden Eagle">Golden Eagle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_Eagle" title="White-tailed Eagle">White-tailed Eagle</a> are the only birds known to habitually predate healthy, fully grown Great Black-backed Gulls. A <a href="/wiki/Great_Skua" title="Great Skua">Great Skua</a> was filmed in Scotland unsuccessfully attempting to kill a second or third year Great Black-backed Gull.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The slightly smaller <a href="/wiki/Pomarine_Skua" title="Pomarine Skua">Pomarine Skua</a> has been observed to have been predated by Great Black-backed Gulls.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Killer_whale" title="Killer whale">Killer whales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a> also reportedly prey upon adult and juvenile birds at sea. In some biomes, where large eagles are absent the Great Black-backed Gull may be considered the <a href="/wiki/Apex_predator" title="Apex predator">apex predator</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Black-backed_Gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h2>
<p>Historically, the Great Black-backed Gull was harvested for its feathers, which were used in the hat-making trade, and this species was removed from large parts of its range as a result of this exploitation.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Today however, its adaptability to human presence and the use of urban environments as artificial nesting sites has resulted in the Great Black-backed Gull rapidly increasing in number and range. It is now a widespread and abundant species in its range and its numbers have increased to such high levels in some areas that it is often seen as a pest species, especially near airports where it risks collisions with airplanes, and in some coastal areas where it predates upon and competes with rarer seabirds such as <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_puffin" title="Atlantic puffin" class="mw-redirect">Atlantic puffins</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_10-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Although there are no known major threats to the Great Black-backed Gull, high levels of toxic pollutants, which are ingested with contaminated prey, are often found in individuals and eggs, reducing reproductive success. Breeding is also interrupted by human disturbance, which can lead to eggs being abandoned, leaving them vulnerable to exposure and predation. The great black-backed gull is additionally hunted for sport in Denmark.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_10-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HBW_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
