<p>The <b>great black-backed gull</b> (<i>Larus marinus</i>), mistakenly called <i>greater black-backed gull</i> by some, is the largest member of the <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a> family. A very aggressive hunter, pirate and scavenger, it breeds on the European and North American coasts and islands of the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a> and is fairly sedentary, though some move farther south or inland to large lakes or reservoirs. The adult great black-backed gull has a white head, neck and underparts, dark grey wings and back, pink legs and yellow bill.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_black-backed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The great black-backed gull was one of the many species originally described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a>, and it still bears its original name of <i>Larus marinus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Linnaeus1758_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linnaeus1758-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The scientific name is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>. <i>Larus</i> appears to have referred to a <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a> or other large seabird. The specific name <i>marinus</i> means "marine",<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> or when taken together, "sea gull". This predates Linnean taxonomy, as it had been called <i>Larus ingens marinus</i> by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Clusius" title="Carolus Clusius">Carolus Clusius</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-willughby1681_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-willughby1681-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The terms <i>swaabie</i> (from <a href="/wiki/Norn_language" title="Norn language">Norn</a>: <i lang="nrn">swartbak</i>, <small><a href="/wiki/Literal_translation" title="Literal translation">lit.</a>&#160;</small>&#39;black back&#39;), <i>baagie</i> and <i>baigie</i> (<a href="/wiki/Norn_language" title="Norn language">Norn</a>: <i lang="nrn">bagi</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Norse language">Old Norse</a>: <i lang="non">bak</i>, <small><a href="/wiki/Literal_translation" title="Literal translation">lit.</a>&#160;</small>&#39;back&#39;) are local names from the Shetland Islands.<sup id="cite_ref-Jakobsen1928_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jakobsen1928-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Swann1913_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Swann1913-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Francis_Willughby" title="Francis Willughby">Francis Willughby</a> called it the <i>great black and white gull</i> in the 17th century, noting that it was called the <i>swarth-back</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-willughby1681_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-willughby1681-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_black-backed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Black-backed_Gull_juvenile_2012RWD.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Great_Black-backed_Gull_juvenile_2012RWD.jpg/220px-Great_Black-backed_Gull_juvenile_2012RWD.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Great_Black-backed_Gull_juvenile_2012RWD.jpg/330px-Great_Black-backed_Gull_juvenile_2012RWD.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Great_Black-backed_Gull_juvenile_2012RWD.jpg/440px-Great_Black-backed_Gull_juvenile_2012RWD.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4079" data-file-height="3059" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Black-backed_Gull_juvenile_2012RWD.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Juvenile</div></div></div>
<p>This is the largest gull in the world,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> considerably larger than a <a href="/wiki/European_herring_gull" title="European herring gull">herring gull</a> (<i>Larus argentatus</i>). Only a few other gulls, including <a href="/wiki/Pallas%27s_gull" title="Pallas&#39;s gull">Pallas's gull</a> (<i>Ichthyaetus ichthyaetus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Glaucous_gull" title="Glaucous gull">glaucous gull</a> (<i>Larus hyperboreus</i>), come close to matching this species' size.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It is 64–79&#160;cm (25–31&#160;in) long with a 1.5–1.7&#160;m (4&#160;ft 11&#160;in–5&#160;ft 7&#160;in) wingspan and a body weight of 0.75–2.3&#160;kg (1.7–5.1&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BNA_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-npolar_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-npolar-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-arkive_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arkive-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In a sample of 2009 adults from the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a>, males were found to average 1,830&#160;g (4.03&#160;lb) and females were found to average 1,488&#160;g (3.280&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Some adult gulls with access to fisheries in the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> can weigh up to roughly 2.5&#160;kg (5.5&#160;lb) and averaged 1.96&#160;kg (4.3&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-Huppop2000_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huppop2000-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> An exceptionally large glaucous gull was found to outweigh any known great black-backed gull, although usually that species is slightly smaller.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The great black-backed gull is bulky and imposing in appearance with a large, powerful bill. The standard measurements are: the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> is 5.4 to 7.25&#160;cm (2.13 to 2.85&#160;in), the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> is 44.5 to 53&#160;cm (17.5 to 20.9&#160;in) and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> is 6.6 to 8.8&#160;cm (2.6 to 3.5&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Coues1903_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coues1903-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BNA_Measure_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA_Measure-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Spinus-greater-black-backed-gull-2015-05-n035442-w.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Spinus-greater-black-backed-gull-2015-05-n035442-w.jpg/220px-Spinus-greater-black-backed-gull-2015-05-n035442-w.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="122" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Spinus-greater-black-backed-gull-2015-05-n035442-w.jpg/330px-Spinus-greater-black-backed-gull-2015-05-n035442-w.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Spinus-greater-black-backed-gull-2015-05-n035442-w.jpg/440px-Spinus-greater-black-backed-gull-2015-05-n035442-w.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1888" data-file-height="1048" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Spinus-greater-black-backed-gull-2015-05-n035442-w.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult <i>Larus marinus</i> picking on fish, Sandy Hook, New Jersey, United States.</div></div></div>
<p>The adult great black-backed gull is fairly distinctive, as no other very large gull with blackish coloration on its upper-wings generally occurs in the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a>. In other white-headed North Atlantic gulls, the mantle is generally a lighter gray color and, in some species, it is a light powdery color or even pinkish.<sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> It is grayish-black on the wings and back, with conspicuous, contrasting white "mirrors" at the wing tips. The legs are pinkish, and the bill is yellow or yellow-pink with some orange or red near tip of lower bill. The adult <a href="/wiki/Lesser_black-backed_gull" title="Lesser black-backed gull">lesser black-backed gull</a> (<i>L. fuscus</i>) is distinctly smaller, typically weighing about half as much as a great black-back. The lesser black-back has yellowish legs and a mantle that can range from slate-gray to brownish-colored but it is never as dark as the larger species. A few superficially similar dark-backed, fairly large gulls occur in the Pacific Ocean or in the tropics, all generally far outside this species' range, such as the <a href="/wiki/Slaty-backed_gull" title="Slaty-backed gull">slaty-backed</a> (<i>L. schistisagus</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Western_gull" title="Western gull">western</a> (<i>L. occidentalis</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Kelp_gull" title="Kelp gull">kelp gull</a> (<i>L. dominicanus</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Juvenile birds of under a year old have scaly, checkered black-brown upper parts, the head and underparts streaked with gray brown, and a neat wing pattern. The face and nape are paler and the wing flight feathers are blackish-brown. The juvenile's tail is white with zigzag bars and spots at base and a broken blackish band near the tip. The bill of the juvenile is brownish-black with white tip and the legs dark bluish-gray with some pink tones. As the young gull ages, the gray-brown coloration gradually fades to more contrasting plumage and the bill darkens to black before growing paler. By the third year, the young gulls resemble a streakier, dirtier-looking version of the adult. They take at least four years to reach maturity, development in this species being somewhat slower than that of other large gulls. The call is a deep "laughing" cry, <i>kaa-ga-ga</i>, with the first note sometimes drawn out in an almost <a href="/wiki/Bovidae" title="Bovidae">bovid</a>-like sound. The voice is distinctly deeper than most other gull species.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HBW_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_black-backed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species can be found breeding in coastal areas from the extreme northwest portion of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, through much of coastal <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> coasts, to the coasts of northwestern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. Across the northern portion of the Atlantic, this gull is distributed in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and on the Atlantic coasts of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and the United States. Though formerly mainly just a non-breeding visitor south of Canada in North America, the species has spread to include several colonies in the <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> states and now breeds as far south as <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>. Individuals breeding in harsher environments will migrate south, wintering on northern coasts of Europe from the Baltic Sea to southern <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, and regularly down to coastal <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> in North America.<sup id="cite_ref-Duncan1981_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duncan1981-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> During the winter in the Baltic Sea, the bird usually stays close to the ice boundary. North of the <a href="/wiki/%C3%85land" class="mw-redirect" title="Åland">Åland</a> islands, the sea often freezes all the way from <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> to <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, and then the bird migrates to open waters. Exceptionally, the species can range as far south as the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> and off the coast of northern South America.<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The great black-backed gull is found in a variety of coastal habitats, including rocky and sandy <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coasts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuaries</a>, as well as inland wetland habitats, such as <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">ponds</a>, <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meadow" title="Meadow">wet fields</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moorland</a>. They are generally found within striking distance of large bodies of water while ranging inland. Today, it is a common fixture at refuse dumps both along coasts and relatively far inland. The species also makes extensive use of <a href="/wiki/Dredging" title="Dredging">dredge spoils</a>, which, in the state of <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, comprise their most prevalent nesting sites.<sup id="cite_ref-Sutton_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutton-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> It generally breeds in areas free of or largely inaccessible to terrestrial predators, such as vegetated islands, sand <a href="/wiki/Dune" title="Dune">dunes</a>, flat-topped stacks, building <a href="/wiki/Roof" title="Roof">roofs</a> and sometimes amongst bushes on <a href="/wiki/Salt_marsh" title="Salt marsh">salt marsh</a> islands. During the winter, the great black-backed gull often travels far out to sea to feed.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_black-backed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GBBeatsDuckling.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/GBBeatsDuckling.jpg/220px-GBBeatsDuckling.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/GBBeatsDuckling.jpg/330px-GBBeatsDuckling.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/GBBeatsDuckling.jpg/440px-GBBeatsDuckling.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GBBeatsDuckling.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Great black-backed gull grabs an eider duckling.</div></div></div>
<p>Great black-backed gulls are opportunistic feeders, apex predators, and are very <a href="/wiki/Curious" class="mw-redirect" title="Curious">curious</a>. They will investigate any small organism they encounter and will readily eat almost anything that they can swallow. They get much of their dietary energy from scavenging, with <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">refuse</a>, most provided directly by humans, locally comprising more than half of their diet. The proliferation of garbage or refuse dumps has become a major attractant to this and all other non-specialized gull species in its range.<sup id="cite_ref-Wells1994_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wells1994-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Zufelt2009_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zufelt2009-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> However, apparently, in attempt to observe how much time they spend foraging at refuse dumps in <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, great black-backed gulls were only observed actively foraging 19% of their time there, eating less garbage than other common gulls, and spent most of their time roosting or loafing.<sup id="cite_ref-Cavanagh1992_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavanagh1992-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Like most gulls, they also capture fish with some regularity and will readily capture any fish smaller than itself found close to the surface of the water.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HBW_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Whether caught or eaten after death or injury from other sources, stomach contents of great black-backed gulls usually show fish to be the primary food.<sup id="cite_ref-Threlfall_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Threlfall-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pierotti_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pierotti-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Sable_Island" title="Sable Island">Sable Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>, 25% of the stomach contents were comprised by fish but 96% of the regurgitations given to young was made up fish.<sup id="cite_ref-Lock_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lock-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, on <a href="/wiki/Ramea_Island,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" class="mw-redirect" title="Ramea Island, Newfoundland and Labrador">Great Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland" class="mw-redirect" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a>, 25% of the stomach contents were fish but 68% of regurgitants were fish.<sup id="cite_ref-Pierotti_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pierotti-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The most regularly reported fish eaten in <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland" class="mw-redirect" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a> were <a href="/wiki/Capelin" title="Capelin">capelin</a> (<i>Mallotus villosus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_cod" title="Atlantic cod">Atlantic cod</a> (<i>Gadus morrhua</i>), <a href="/wiki/Microgadus_tomcod" title="Microgadus tomcod">Atlantic tomcod</a> (<i>Microgadus tomcod</i>), <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_mackerel" title="Atlantic mackerel">Atlantic mackerel</a> (<i>Scomber scombrus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_herring" title="Atlantic herring">Atlantic herring</a> (<i>Clupea harengus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Ammodytes" title="Ammodytes">sand lance</a> (<i>Ammodytes hexapterus</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Pierotti_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pierotti-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lock_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lock-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Other prey often includes various <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jonah_crab" title="Jonah crab">Jonah crabs</a> (<i>Cancer borealis</i>), <a href="/wiki/Cancer_irroratus" title="Cancer irroratus">rock crabs</a> (<i>Cancer irroratus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Sea_urchin" title="Sea urchin">sea urchins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carcinus_maenas" title="Carcinus maenas">green crabs</a> (<i>Carcinus maenas</i>), <a href="/wiki/Starfish" title="Starfish">starfish</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Asterias_forbesi" title="Asterias forbesi">Asterias forbesi</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Common_starfish" title="Common starfish">Asterias rubens</a></i>) and other <a href="/wiki/Echinoderm" title="Echinoderm">echinoderms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mollusca" title="Mollusca">mollusks</a> when they come across the opportunity.<sup id="cite_ref-Good_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Good-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dumas1996_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumas1996-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> From observations in northern <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>, 23% of observed prey was echinoderms and 63% was <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Good_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Good-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Unlike most other <i>Larus</i> gulls, they are highly predatory and frequently <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">hunt</a> and kill any prey smaller than themselves, behaving more like a <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptor</a> than a typical larid gull. Lacking the razor-sharp <a href="/wiki/Talon_(anatomy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Talon (anatomy)">talons</a> and curved, tearing beak of a raptor, the great black-backed gull relies on aggression, physical strength and endurance when hunting. When attacking other animals, they usually attack <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> eggs, nestlings or fledglings at the nest, perhaps most numerously <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">terns</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Tern_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tern-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> but also including smaller gull species as well as <a href="/wiki/Eider" title="Eider">eiders</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Donehower2008_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donehower2008-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gannet" title="Gannet">gannets</a> and various <a href="/wiki/Alcidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Alcidae">alcids</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland" class="mw-redirect" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>, 10% of the stomach contents of great black-backed gulls was made up of birds, while a further 17% of stomach contents was made up of <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">tern</a> eggs alone.<sup id="cite_ref-Pierotti_26-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pierotti-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lock_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lock-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Adult or fledged juveniles of various bird species have also been predaceously attacked. Some fully-fledged or adult birds observed to be hunted in flight or on the ground by great black-backed gulls have included <i><a href="/wiki/Anas" title="Anas">Anas</a></i> ducks, <a href="/wiki/Ruddy_duck" title="Ruddy duck">ruddy ducks</a> (<i>Oxyura jamaicensis</i>), <a href="/wiki/Bufflehead" title="Bufflehead">buffleheads</a> (<i>Bucephala albeola</i>), <a href="/wiki/Manx_shearwater" title="Manx shearwater">Manx shearwaters</a> (<i>Puffinus puffinus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Pied-billed_grebe" title="Pied-billed grebe">pied-billed grebes</a> (<i>Podilymbus podiceps</i>), <a href="/wiki/Common_moorhen" title="Common moorhen">common moorhens</a> (<i>Gallinula chloropus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">terns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_puffin" title="Atlantic puffin">Atlantic puffins</a> (<i>Fratercula arctica</i>), <a href="/wiki/Coot" title="Coot">coots</a> (<i>Fulica</i> ssp.), <a href="/wiki/Glossy_ibis" title="Glossy ibis">glossy ibises</a> (<i>Plegadis falcinellus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Rock_pigeon" class="mw-redirect" title="Rock pigeon">rock pigeons</a> (<i>Columba livia</i>) and even predatory birds such as <a href="/wiki/Hen_harrier" title="Hen harrier">hen harriers</a> (<i>Circus cyaneus</i>). When attacking other flying birds, the great black-backed gulls often pursue them on the wing and attack them by jabbing with their bill, hoping to bring down the other bird either by creating an open wound or simply via exhaustion. They may kill healthy adult birds weighing up to at least 750&#160;g (1.65&#160;lb) but take exclusively the small young of larger birds such as <a href="/wiki/Common_eider" title="Common eider">common eider</a> (<i>Somateria mollissima</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Cormorant" title="Cormorant">cormorants</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sutton_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutton-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Donehower2006_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donehower2006-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Yates_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yates-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-neseabirds_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-neseabirds-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> They will also catch flying <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerines</a>, which they typically target while the small birds are exhausted from migration and swallow them immediately.<sup id="cite_ref-Sutton_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutton-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Great black-backed gull also feed on land animals, including <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a> (<i>Rattus</i> ssp.) at garbage dumps and even sickly <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">lambs</a> (<i>Ovis aries</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HBW_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Most foods are swallowed whole, including most fish and even other gulls. When foods are too large to be swallowed at once, they will sometimes be shaken in the bill until they fall apart into pieces. Like some other gulls, when capturing <a href="/wiki/Mollusc" class="mw-redirect" title="Mollusc">molluscs</a> or other hard-surfaced foods such as <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>, they will fly into the air with it and drop it on rocks or hard earth to crack it open. Alternate foods, including <a href="/wiki/Berries" class="mw-redirect" title="Berries">berries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, are eaten when available. They will readily exploit easy food sources, including <a href="/wiki/Chum_salmon" title="Chum salmon">chum</a> lines made by boats at sea. They are skilled <a href="/wiki/Kleptoparasitism" title="Kleptoparasitism">kleptoparasites</a> who will readily pirate fish and other prey captured by other birds and dominate over other gulls when they encounter them. At tern colonies in coastal <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_herring_gull" title="American herring gull">American herring gulls</a> (<i>L. smithsonianus</i>) occasionally also attack nestling and fledgling terns but in a great majority of cases were immediately pirated of their catch by great black-backs.<sup id="cite_ref-Tern_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tern-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In one observation, an adult great black-back was seen to rob a female <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">peregrine falcon</a> (<i>Falco peregrinus</i>) of a freshly caught <a href="/wiki/Gadwall" title="Gadwall">gadwall</a> (<i>Anas strepera</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Sutton_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutton-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In another case, a third-year great black-back was observed fighting an adult female <a href="/wiki/Northern_goshawk" title="Northern goshawk">northern goshawk</a> (<i>Accipiter gentilis</i>) off its kill, although the goshawk attempted to strike the gull before leaving.<sup id="cite_ref-Karlsen_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Karlsen-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Due to their method of using intimidation while encountering other water and raptorial birds, the species has been referred to as a "merciless tyrant".<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Naturally, these gulls are attracted to the surface activity of large marine animals, from <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_bluefin_tuna" title="Atlantic bluefin tuna">Atlantic bluefin tuna</a> (<i>Thunnus thynnus</i>) to <a href="/wiki/Humpback_whale" title="Humpback whale">humpback whales</a> (<i>Megaptera novaeangliae</i>), to capture fish driven to the surface by such creatures.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HBW_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_black-backed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Birds_of_Sweden_2016_19.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Birds_of_Sweden_2016_19.jpg/220px-Birds_of_Sweden_2016_19.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="110" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Birds_of_Sweden_2016_19.jpg/330px-Birds_of_Sweden_2016_19.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Birds_of_Sweden_2016_19.jpg/440px-Birds_of_Sweden_2016_19.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2477" data-file-height="1239" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Birds_of_Sweden_2016_19.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Great black-backed gulls nesting in Sweden 2016</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Larus_marinus_MWNH_0320.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Larus_marinus_MWNH_0320.JPG/220px-Larus_marinus_MWNH_0320.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="93" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Larus_marinus_MWNH_0320.JPG/330px-Larus_marinus_MWNH_0320.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Larus_marinus_MWNH_0320.JPG/440px-Larus_marinus_MWNH_0320.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="1820" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Larus_marinus_MWNH_0320.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>This species breeds singly or in small colonies, sometimes in the middle of a <i>Larus argentatus</i> colony. Young adult pair formation occurs in March or April. The following spring the same birds usually form a pair again, meeting at the previous year's nest. If one of the birds doesn't appear, the other bird begins looking for a new mate. Usually a single bird does not breed in that season.
</p><p>They make a lined nest on the ground often on top of a rocky stack, fallen log or other obstructing object which can protect the eggs from the elements. Usually, several nest scrapes are made before the one deemed best by the parents is selected and then lined with grass, seaweed or moss or objects such as rope or plastic. When nesting on roofs in urban environments, previous year's nests are often reused over and over again. The female lays usually three eggs sometime between late April and late June. When only two eggs are found in a nest, the reason is almost always that one egg, for one reason or another, has been destroyed. It takes around one week for the female to produce the three eggs, and the incubation doesn't begin until all three eggs are laid. Hence all three chicks are hatched the same day. The birds are usually successful in bringing up all the three chicks.
</p><p>The eggs are greenish-brown with dark speckles and blotches. Both parents participate in the incubation stage, which lasts for approximately 28 days. During this time, the birds attempt to avoid being noticed and stay silent. The breeding pair are devoted parents who both take shifts brooding the young, defending the nest and gathering food. Young great black-backed gulls leave the nest area at 50 days of age and may remain with their parents for an overall period of around six months, though most fledglings choose to congregate with other immature gulls in the search for food by fall. These gulls reach breeding maturity when they obtain adult plumage at four years, though may not successfully breed until they are six years old.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_17-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Longevity_and_mortality">Longevity and mortality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_black-backed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Longevity and mortality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is a relatively long-living bird. The maximum recorded age for a wild great black-backed gull is 27.1 years.<sup id="cite_ref-senescence_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-senescence-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> This species is rarely kept in captivity, but domestically kept <a href="/wiki/European_herring_gull" title="European herring gull">European herring gulls</a> have been known to live for over 44 years and generally larger birds can outlive smaller ones.<sup id="cite_ref-Wood_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Mortality typically occurs in the early stages of life, when harsh weather conditions (including flooding) and starvation can threaten them, as well as predators. Chicks and eggs are preyed on by <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crows</a> (<i>Corvus</i> ssp.), <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a> (<i>Felis catus</i>), other <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">raccoons</a> (<i>Procyon lotor</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a> (<i>Rattus</i> ssp.). The <a href="/wiki/Bald_eagle" title="Bald eagle">bald eagle</a> (<i>Haliaeetus leucocephalus</i>),<sup id="cite_ref-Marcotte2011_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marcotte2011-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_eagle" title="White-tailed eagle">white-tailed eagle</a> (<i>H. albicilla</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">golden eagle</a> (<i>Aquila chrysaetos</i>) are the only birds known to habitually predate healthy, fully grown great black-backed gulls.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> In one study from <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, great black-backed gulls were the fifth highest frequency prey item for white-tailed eagles and gulls are prone to <a href="/wiki/Mobbing_(animal_behavior)" title="Mobbing (animal behavior)">mobbing</a> these huge eagles.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Great_skua" title="Great skua">great skua</a> (<i>Stercorarius skua</i>) was filmed in Scotland unsuccessfully attempting to kill a second or third year great black-backed gull.<sup id="cite_ref-youtube_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-youtube-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, great black-backed gulls have also been reported to fall prey to <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_eagle-owl" title="Eurasian eagle-owl">Eurasian eagle-owls</a> (<i>Bubo bubo</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Hagen1950_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hagen1950-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Killer_whale" title="Killer whale">Killer whales</a> (<i>Orcinus orca</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a> also reportedly prey upon adult and juvenile birds at sea. In some biomes, where large eagles are absent the great black-backed gull may be considered the <a href="/wiki/Apex_predator" title="Apex predator">apex predator</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_18-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_black-backed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Historically, the great black-backed gull was harvested for its feathers, which were used in the hat-making trade, and this species was extirpated from large parts of its range as a result of this exploitation.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_17-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Today, however, its adaptability to human presence and the use of urban environments as artificial nesting sites has resulted in the great black-backed gull rapidly increasing in number and range. It is now a widespread and abundant species in its range and its numbers have increased to such high levels in some areas that it is often seen as a pest species, especially near airports where it risks collisions with airplanes, and in some coastal areas where it sometimes outcompetes or hunts rarer seabirds, such as <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_puffin" title="Atlantic puffin">Atlantic puffins</a>, possibly resulting in conservationist intervention.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_17-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The increase and expansion of great black-backed gulls has been attributed to increasing winter fishery activities in the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Huppop2000_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huppop2000-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Although there are no known major threats to the great black-backed gull, high levels of toxic pollutants, which are ingested with contaminated prey, are often found in individuals and eggs, reducing reproductive success. Breeding is also interrupted by human disturbance, which can lead to eggs being abandoned, leaving them vulnerable to exposure and predation.
</p>
