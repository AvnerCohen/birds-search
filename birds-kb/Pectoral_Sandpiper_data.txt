<p>The <b>pectoral sandpiper</b> (<i>Calidris melanotos</i>) is a small, <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> that breeds in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wintering" class="mw-redirect" title="Wintering">wintering</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Oceania</a>. It eats small <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>. Its <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a>, a hole scraped in the ground and with a thick lining, is deep enough to protect its four <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> from the cool breezes of its breeding grounds. The pectoral sandpiper is 21&#160;cm (8.3&#160;in) long, with a wingspan of 46&#160;cm (18&#160;in).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pectoral_sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The pectoral sandpiper is sometimes separated with the "<a href="/wiki/Stint" title="Stint">stint</a>" sandpipers in <i>Erolia</i>. This may or may not represent a good <a href="/wiki/Monophyletic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monophyletic">monophyletic</a> group, depending on the placement of the <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Phylogenetic">phylogenetically</a> enigmatic <a href="/wiki/Curlew_sandpiper" title="Curlew sandpiper">curlew sandpiper</a> (<i>"C." ferruginea</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a> of <i>Erolia</i>. In any case, the genus name <i>Ereunetes</i>—formerly used for the <a href="/wiki/Western_sandpiper" title="Western sandpiper">western sandpiper</a> (<i>"C." mauri</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Semipalmated_sandpiper" title="Semipalmated sandpiper">semipalmated sandpiper</a> (<i>"C." pusilla</i>), which are also members of the stint <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a>—was established before <i>Erolia</i>.
</p><p>"<a href="/wiki/Cox%27s_sandpiper" title="Cox&#39;s sandpiper">Cox's sandpiper</a>" (<i>"Calidris"</i> × <i>paramelanotos</i>) is a stereotyped <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrid</a> between this species and the curlew sandpiper. This does not prove a particularly close relationship between these two species, as far more distantly related waders <a href="/wiki/Hybridisation_in_shorebirds" title="Hybridisation in shorebirds">have successfully hybridized</a>. In any case, among the stint clade the <a href="/wiki/Red-necked_stint" title="Red-necked stint">red-necked stint</a> (<i>"C." ruficollis</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Long-toed_stint" title="Long-toed stint">long-toed stint</a> (<i>"C." subminuta</i>) are particularly close relatives of the pectoral sandpiper.<sup id="cite_ref-Thomas_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The scientific name is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>. The genus name <i>kalidris</i> or <i>skalidris</i>, is a term used by <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> for some grey-coloured waterside birds. The specific <i>melanotos</i> is from <i>melas</i>,  "black" and  <i>notos</i>, "backed ".<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pectoral_sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pectoral-Sandpiper017.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Pectoral-Sandpiper017.jpg/220px-Pectoral-Sandpiper017.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="186" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Pectoral-Sandpiper017.jpg/330px-Pectoral-Sandpiper017.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Pectoral-Sandpiper017.jpg/440px-Pectoral-Sandpiper017.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="1692" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pectoral-Sandpiper017.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult in breeding plumage; note sharp margin of breast colour</div></div></div>
<p>This bird looks similar to the widely <a href="/wiki/Sympatric" class="mw-redirect" title="Sympatric">sympatric</a> <a href="/wiki/Sharp-tailed_sandpiper" title="Sharp-tailed sandpiper">sharp-tailed sandpiper</a> (<i>"C." acuminata</i>), which is not a member of the stint clade however. The pectoral sandpiper is a largish <a href="/wiki/Calidrid" class="mw-redirect" title="Calidrid">calidrid</a> (21&#160;cm (8.3&#160;in) in length, with a wingspan of 46&#160;cm (18&#160;in))<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> with a grey-brown back, brownest in the summer male, and greyest in winter. The pectoral sandpiper has a grey breast, sharply demarcated at its lower edge, which gives this species its <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> name; this clear dividing line is particularly conspicuous if the birds are turned towards the observer. The legs are yellowish, and the bill is olive with a darker tip.
</p><p>The juveniles are more brightly patterned above with rufous colouration and white mantle stripes.
</p><p>This species differs from the sharp-tailed sandpiper in its breast pattern, weaker <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a> and greyer crown.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_ecology">Distribution and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pectoral_sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PectoralSandpiper-1OCT2016.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Pectoral Sandpiper" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/PectoralSandpiper-1OCT2016.jpg/275px-PectoralSandpiper-1OCT2016.jpg" decoding="async" width="275" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/PectoralSandpiper-1OCT2016.jpg/413px-PectoralSandpiper-1OCT2016.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/PectoralSandpiper-1OCT2016.jpg/550px-PectoralSandpiper-1OCT2016.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3823" data-file-height="2167" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PectoralSandpiper-1OCT2016.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>At <a href="/wiki/Cosumnes_River_Preserve" title="Cosumnes River Preserve">Cosumnes River Preserve</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_County,_California" title="Sacramento County, California">Sacramento County</a>, California.</div></div></div>
<p>It is a very long-distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>, and about half of the species breeds in the boggy <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> of northeast <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Asia</a>, the rest nesting in a range from <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> to central <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-USGS_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The American and most of the Asian birds winter in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, but some Asian breeders winter in southern and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. On migration and in winter, the pectoral sandpiper is typically found in <a href="/wiki/Freshwater" class="mw-redirect" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitats</a>.
</p><p>This species also occurs as a regular migrant to western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, and is seen most years in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> or <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NBotWM_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NBotWM-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> While the pectoral sandpiper has not been recorded as breeding species in Europe, vagrant individuals were found in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> in suitable breeding habitat during summer.<sup id="cite_ref-Vittery_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vittery-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Many of the birds occurring in Western Europe may be on a regular migration from Asian breeding grounds to winter in Southern Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Lees_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lees-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> September 2003 saw a record influx to those two countries, with 40 found in Ireland and 150 in Great Britain. On the US Pacific coast, such stagings of migrant flocks appear to be rarer.<sup id="cite_ref-Littlejohn_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Littlejohn-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Vagrant individuals are sometimes seen elsewhere off the usual migration routes, e.g. on the <a href="/wiki/Marianas" class="mw-redirect" title="Marianas">Marianas</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Islands" title="Marshall Islands">Marshall Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">Palau</a> in <a href="/wiki/Micronesia" title="Micronesia">Micronesia</a>; they are somewhat more frequently encountered on the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiles_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiles-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-VanderWerf2006a_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VanderWerf2006a-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-VanderWerf2006b_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VanderWerf2006b-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The pectoral sandpiper's migration might be affected by <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, as is suspected for many Arctic-breeding birds: 100 years ago, migrating pectoral sandpipers were observed to pass through northern <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> in early-mid May and again in late August; today, the bulk of the northward migration takes place in April already, and most birds do not return until mid-September.<sup id="cite_ref-Henninger_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Henninger-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OOS_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OOS-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>These birds forage on grasslands and mudflats, picking up food by sight, sometimes by probing. They mainly eat <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>. The male has a courtship display which involves puffing up his breast, which has a fat sac in the breeding season to enhance his performance.
</p><p>The pectoral sandpiper builds a steep-sided <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">scrape nest</a> with a considerable volume of lining material. The nest is deep enough that the <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> sit about 3&#160;cm (1.2&#160;in) below ground level, which helps to minimize heat loss from the cool breezes which occur at the latitudes where the species nests.<sup id="cite_ref-Reid2002_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reid2002-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The female lays four eggs.
</p><p>Pectoral sandpipers have decreased in number 50% since 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
