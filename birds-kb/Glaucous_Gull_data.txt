<p>The <b>glaucous gull</b> (<i>Larus hyperboreus</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a>, the second-largest gull in the world.  It breeds in <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> regions of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> and winters south to shores of the <a href="/wiki/Holarctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Holarctic">Holarctic</a>. The genus name is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>larus</i>, which appears to have referred to a gull or other large seabird. The specific name <i>hyperboreus</i> is Latin for "northern" from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>Huperboreoi</i> people from the far north <sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Glaucous" title="Glaucous">Glaucous</a>" is from Latin <i>glaucus</i> and denotes the grey colour of the gull.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This gull is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering from in the North <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> and North <a href="/wiki/Pacific" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific">Pacific</a> Oceans as far south as the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> and northernmost states of the United States, also on the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>. A few birds sometimes reach the southern USA and northern Mexico.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glaucous_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki-shirokamome.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Wiki-shirokamome.jpg/220px-Wiki-shirokamome.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Wiki-shirokamome.jpg/330px-Wiki-shirokamome.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Wiki-shirokamome.jpg/440px-Wiki-shirokamome.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki-shirokamome.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult plumage</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Larus_hyperboreus_Immature.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Larus_hyperboreus_Immature.jpg/220px-Larus_hyperboreus_Immature.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="185" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Larus_hyperboreus_Immature.jpg/330px-Larus_hyperboreus_Immature.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Larus_hyperboreus_Immature.jpg/440px-Larus_hyperboreus_Immature.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1042" data-file-height="875" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Larus_hyperboreus_Immature.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Immature plumage</div></div></div>
<p>This is a large and powerful gull, second-largest of all gull species and very pale in all plumage, with no black on either the wings or the tail. Adults are pale grey above, with a thick, yellow bill. Immatures are very pale grey with a pink and black bill. This species is considerably larger, bulkier, and thicker-billed than the similar <a href="/wiki/Iceland_gull" title="Iceland gull">Iceland gull</a>, and can sometimes equal the size of the <a href="/wiki/Great_black-backed_gull" title="Great black-backed gull">great black-backed gull</a>, the oft-titled largest gull species. In some areas, glaucous gulls are about the same weight as great black-backed gulls or even heavier, and their maximum weight is greater.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC2_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> They can weigh from 960 to 2,700&#160;g (2.12 to 5.95&#160;lb), with the sexes previously reported to average 1.55&#160;kg (3.4&#160;lb) in males and 1.35&#160;kg (3.0&#160;lb) in females.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> At the colony on <a href="/wiki/Coats_Island" title="Coats Island">Coats Island</a> in Canada, the gulls are nearly 15% heavier than some other known populations, with a mean weight 1.86&#160;kg (4.1&#160;lb) in five males and 1.49&#160;kg (3.3&#160;lb) in seven females. One other study claimed even higher weights for glaucous gulls, as on <a href="/wiki/Wrangel_Island" title="Wrangel Island">Wrangel Island</a>, 9 males reportedly averaged 2.32&#160;kg (5.1&#160;lb) and 2.1&#160;kg (4.6&#160;lb) in six females, which if accurate, would make the glaucous gull the heaviest gull and shorebird in the world if not (as far as is known) the largest in length on average.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Weiser_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weiser-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> These gulls range from 55 to 77&#160;cm (22 to 30&#160;in) in length and can span 132 to 170&#160;cm (52 to 67&#160;in), with some specimens possibly attaining 182&#160;cm (72&#160;in), across the wings.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AAB_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAB-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Weiser_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weiser-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Among standard measurements, the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> is 40.8 to 50.1&#160;cm (16.1 to 19.7&#160;in), the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> is 4.9 to 6.9&#160;cm (1.9 to 2.7&#160;in) and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> is 6 to 7.7&#160;cm (2.4 to 3.0&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> They take four years to reach maturity.
</p><p>The call is a "laughing" cry similar to that of the herring gull, but deeper.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glaucous_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The four recognized <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are:
</p>
<ul><li><i>L. h. hyperboreus</i>, <small><a href="/wiki/Johann_Ernst_Gunnerus" class="mw-redirect" title="Johann Ernst Gunnerus">Gunnerus</a>, 1767</small>:  <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies#Nominotypical_subspecies_and_subspecies_autonyms" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate</a>, found from northern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> to north-western <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a></li>
<li><i>L. h. pallidissimus</i>, <small>Portenko, 1939</small>: found from north-western Siberia to the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Sea" title="Bering Sea">Bering Sea</a></li>
<li><i>L. h. barrovianus</i>, <small><a href="/wiki/Robert_Ridgway" title="Robert Ridgway">Ridgway</a>, 1886</small>: found from <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> to north-west Canada</li>
<li><i>L. h. leuceretes</i>, <small>Schleep, 1819</small>: found from north-central Canada to <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glaucous_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species breeds colonially or singly on coasts and cliffs, making a lined nest on the ground or cliff. Normally, two to four light brown eggs with dark brown splotches are laid.
</p><p>These are omnivores like most <i>Larus</i> gulls, and they eat fish, insects, molluscs, starfish, offal, scraps, eggs, small birds, small mammals, and carrion, as well as seeds, berries, and grains.
</p>
