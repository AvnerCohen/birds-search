Glaucous Gull</div>

<p>The <b>Glaucous Gull</b> (<i>Larus hyperboreus</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a> which breeds in the Arctic regions of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere" class="mw-redirect">northern hemisphere</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> coasts of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering from in the North <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> and North <a href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific" class="mw-redirect">Pacific</a> oceans as far south as the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> and northernmost states of the <a href="/wiki/USA" title="USA" class="mw-redirect">USA</a>, also on the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>. A few birds sometimes reach the southern USA and northern <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>.</p>
<p>This species breeds colonially or singly on coasts and cliffs, making a lined nest on the ground or cliff. Normally, 2–4 light brown eggs with dark chocolate splotches are laid.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Glaucous_Gull_flying_with_Great_Black-backed_Gulls.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Glaucous_Gull_flying_with_Great_Black-backed_Gulls.jpg/220px-Glaucous_Gull_flying_with_Great_Black-backed_Gulls.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Glaucous_Gull_flying_with_Great_Black-backed_Gulls.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
In flight with Great Black-backed Gulls, Ottawa, Ontario</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This is a large and powerful gull, very pale in all plumages, with no black in the wings or tail. The term <i><a href="/wiki/Glaucous" title="Glaucous">glaucous</a></i> describes its colouration. Adults are pale grey above, with a thick yellow bill. Immatures are very pale grey with a pink and black bill. This species is considerably larger, bulkier and thicker-billed than the similar <a href="/wiki/Iceland_Gull" title="Iceland Gull">Iceland Gull</a>, and can sometimes equal the size of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Black-backed_Gull" title="Great Black-backed Gull">Great Black-backed Gull</a>, the largest gull species. They can weigh anywhere from 960 to 2,700 g (2.1 to 6.0 lb), averaging 1.55&#160;kg (3.4&#160;lb) in males and 1.35&#160;kg (3.0&#160;lb) in females.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> These gulls range from 55 to 77 cm (22 to 30 in) in length and can span 132 to 170 cm (52 to 67 in) across the wings.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Among standard measurements, the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> is 40.8 to 50.1 cm (16.1 to 19.7 in), the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> is 4.9 to 6.9 cm (1.9 to 2.7 in) and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> is 6 to 7.7 cm (2.4 to 3.0 in).<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> They take four years to reach maturity. The call is a "laughing" cry similar to that of the <a href="/wiki/Herring_Gull" title="Herring Gull">Herring Gull</a> but deeper.</p>
<p>These are omnivores like most <i>Larus</i> gulls, and they will eat fish, insects, molluscs, starfish, offal, scraps, eggs, small birds, small mammals and carrion as well as seeds, berries and grain.<br style="clear: left;" /></p>
