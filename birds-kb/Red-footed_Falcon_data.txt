<p>The <b>Red-footed Falcon</b> (<i>Falco vespertinus</i>), formerly <b>Western Red-footed Falcon</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a>. It belongs to the family <a href="/wiki/Falconidae" title="Falconidae">Falconidae</a>, the falcons. This <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> is found in eastern Europe and Asia although its numbers are dwindling rapidly due to habitat loss and hunting. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It is a regular wanderer to western Europe, and in August 2004 a Red-footed Falcon was found in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> for the first time on the island of <a href="/wiki/Martha%27s_Vineyard" title="Martha's Vineyard">Martha's Vineyard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Amur_Falcon" title="Amur Falcon">Amur Falcon</a> was formerly included herein as a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> but it is nowadays considered well distinct. Nonetheless, it is the present species' closest relative; their relationship to other falcons is more enigmatic. They appear <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphologically</a> somewhat intermediate between <a href="/wiki/Kestrel" title="Kestrel">kestrels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hobby_(bird)" title="Hobby (bird)">hobbies</a> and <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">DNA sequence</a> data has been unable to further resolve this question, mainly due to lack of comprehensive sampling. They might be closer to the <a href="/wiki/Merlin_(bird)" title="Merlin (bird)">Merlin</a> than to most other living falcons, or more generally related to this species and American falcons such as the <a href="/wiki/American_Kestrel" title="American Kestrel">American Kestrel</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Aplomado_Falcon" title="Aplomado Falcon">Aplomado Falcon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is a medium-small, long-winged species. The adult male is all blue-grey, except for his red undertail and legs; its underwings are uniformly grey. The female has a grey back and wings, orange head and underparts, and a white face with black eye stripe and moustaches.</p>
<p>Young birds are brown above and buff below with dark streaks, and a face pattern like the female. Red-footed Falcons are 28-34 <a href="/wiki/Centimetre" title="Centimetre">centimetres</a> (11-13½ <a href="/wiki/Inch" title="Inch">in</a>) in length with a wingspan of 65-75 centimetres (25½-29½ in).</p>
<p>This is a diurnal bird of open country with some trees, often near water. Its distinctive method of hunting is shared by the <a href="/wiki/Common_Kestrel" title="Common Kestrel">Common Kestrel</a>. It regularly hovers, searching the ground below, then makes a short steep dive towards the target. The Red-footed Falcon's main prey is large insects, but it will also take small <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> and birds.</p>
<p>This falcon is a colonial breeder, reusing the old nests of <a href="/wiki/Corvid" title="Corvid" class="mw-redirect">corvids</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Rook_(bird)" title="Rook (bird)">Rooks</a>. It lays two to four eggs.</p>
