<p>The <b>dunlin</b> (<i>Calidris alpina</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a>, sometimes separated with the other "<a href="/wiki/Stint" title="Stint">stints</a>" in <i>Erolia</i>. The English name is a dialect form of "dunling", first recorded in 1531–2. It derives from <i>dun</i>, "dull brown", with the suffix <i>-ling</i>, meaning a person or thing with the given quality.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The genus name is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>kalidris</i> or <i>skalidris</i>, a term used by <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> for some grey-coloured waterside birds. The specific <i>alpina </i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means "of high mountains", in this case referring to the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is a circumpolar breeder in <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> or subarctic regions. Birds that breed in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">northern Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> are long-distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrants</a>, wintering south to <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">southeast Asia</a> and the Middle East. Birds that breed in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> Arctic migrate short distances to the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> coasts of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, although those nesting in northern Alaska overwinter in Asia. Many dunlins winter along the Iberian south coast.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dunlin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Spinus-dunlin-2014-11-n011123-w.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Spinus-dunlin-2014-11-n011123-w.jpg/220px-Spinus-dunlin-2014-11-n011123-w.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="122" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Spinus-dunlin-2014-11-n011123-w.jpg/330px-Spinus-dunlin-2014-11-n011123-w.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Spinus-dunlin-2014-11-n011123-w.jpg/440px-Spinus-dunlin-2014-11-n011123-w.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1888" data-file-height="1048" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Spinus-dunlin-2014-11-n011123-w.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult dunlin in winter plumage, Sandy Hook, New Jersey, USA.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dunlin_easton_bavents.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Dunlin_easton_bavents.jpg/220px-Dunlin_easton_bavents.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Dunlin_easton_bavents.jpg/330px-Dunlin_easton_bavents.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Dunlin_easton_bavents.jpg/440px-Dunlin_easton_bavents.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2348" data-file-height="1563" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dunlin_easton_bavents.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dunlin in late September at Easton Bavents, Suffolk, UK.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Measurements</b>:<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Length</b>: 6.3-8.7 in (16-22 cm)</li>
<li><b>Weight</b>: 1.7-2.7 oz (48.7-75.9 g)</li>
<li><b>Wingspan</b>: 14.2-15.0 in (36-38 cm)</li></ul>
<p>An adult dunlin in breeding plumage shows the distinctive black belly which no other similar-sized wader possesses. The winter dunlin is basically grey above and white below. Juveniles are brown above with two whitish "V" shapes on the back. They usually have black marks on the flanks or belly and show a strong white wingbar in flight.
</p><p>The legs and slightly decurved bill are black. There are a number of <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> differing mainly in the extent of rufous colouration in the breeding plumage and the bill length. Bill length varies between sexes, the females having longer bills than the males.
</p><p>On the tip of the Dunlin's bill is a soft covering that fills with blood and with many nerve endings, forming a sensitive probe that is used to locate invertebrate prey in mud and sand. Although the bill can look sharp-pointed in dead specimens, in life it is blunt.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dunlin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><i>C. a. arctica</i>, <small>(Schiøler, 1922)</small>, breeds in northeast <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>.</li>
<li><i>C. a. schinzii</i>, <small>(<a href="/wiki/Christian_Ludwig_Brehm" title="Christian Ludwig Brehm">Brehm</a> &amp; Schilling, 1822)</small>, breeds in southeast <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Baltic_region" title="Baltic region">the Baltic</a>.</li>
<li><i>C. a. alpina</i>, <small>(<a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, 1758)</small>, breeds in northern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and northwest <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>.</li>
<li><i>C. a. centralis</i>, <small>(<a href="/wiki/Sergei_Buturlin" title="Sergei Buturlin">Buturlin</a>, 1932)</small>, breeds in north-central and northeast <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>.</li>
<li><i>C. a. sakhalina</i>, <small>(<a href="/wiki/Louis_Jean_Pierre_Vieillot" title="Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot">Vieillot</a>, 1816)</small>, breeds in eastern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Chukchi_Peninsula" title="Chukchi Peninsula">Chukchi Peninsula</a>.</li>
<li><i>C. a. kistchinski</i>, <small>Tomkovich, 1986</small>, breeds around the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Okhotsk" title="Sea of Okhotsk">Sea of Okhotsk</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kuril_Islands" title="Kuril Islands">Kuril Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kamchatka" class="mw-redirect" title="Kamchatka">Kamchatka</a>.</li>
<li><i>C. a. actites</i>, <small>Nechaev &amp; Tomkovich, 1988</small>, breeds on <a href="/wiki/Sakhalin" title="Sakhalin">Sakhalin</a>.</li>
<li><i>C. a. arcticola</i>, <small>(<a href="/wiki/W._E._Clyde_Todd" title="W. E. Clyde Todd">Todd</a>, 1953)</small>, breeds from northwest <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> to northwest <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.</li>
<li><i>C. a. pacifica</i>, <small>(<a href="/wiki/Coues" class="mw-redirect" title="Coues">Coues</a>, 1861)</small>, breeds in western and southern <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>.</li>
<li><i>C. a. hudsonia</i>, <small>(<a href="/wiki/W._E._Clyde_Todd" title="W. E. Clyde Todd">Todd</a>, 1953)</small>, breeds in central <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nesting">Nesting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dunlin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nesting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Calidris_alpina_MWNH_0158.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Calidris_alpina_MWNH_0158.JPG/220px-Calidris_alpina_MWNH_0158.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Calidris_alpina_MWNH_0158.JPG/330px-Calidris_alpina_MWNH_0158.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Calidris_alpina_MWNH_0158.JPG/440px-Calidris_alpina_MWNH_0158.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4064" data-file-height="2840" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Calidris_alpina_MWNH_0158.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Calidris_alpina_schinzii_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.119.20.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Calidris_alpina_schinzii_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.119.20.jpg/220px-Calidris_alpina_schinzii_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.119.20.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Calidris_alpina_schinzii_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.119.20.jpg/330px-Calidris_alpina_schinzii_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.119.20.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Calidris_alpina_schinzii_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.119.20.jpg/440px-Calidris_alpina_schinzii_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.119.20.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3780" data-file-height="2835" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Calidris_alpina_schinzii_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.119.20.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Calidris alpina schinzii</i> - <a href="/wiki/MHNT" class="mw-redirect" title="MHNT">MHNT</a></div></div></div>
<p>The nest is a shallow scrape on the ground lined with vegetation, into which typically four eggs are laid and <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" class="mw-redirect" title="Avian incubation">incubated</a> by the male and female parents. Chicks are precocial, however are brooded during early development. They start to fly at approximately three weeks of age. The majority of brood care is provided by the male, as the female deserts the brood and often leaves the breeding area.
</p><p>The call is a typical sandpiper "peep", and the display song a harsh trill.
</p><p>The dunlin is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p><p>Apparent <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrids</a> between this species and the <a href="/wiki/White-rumped_sandpiper" title="White-rumped sandpiper">white-rumped sandpiper</a> as well as with the <a href="/wiki/Purple_sandpiper" title="Purple sandpiper">purple sandpiper</a> have been reported from the Atlantic coasts of North America<sup id="cite_ref-McLaughlin2000_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLaughlin2000-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wilson_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Millington1994_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Millington1994-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> respectively.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dunlin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The dunlin is highly gregarious in winter, sometimes forming large flocks on coastal mudflats or sandy beaches. Large numbers can often be seen swirling in synchronized flight on stop-overs during migration or on their winter habitat.
</p><p>This bird is one of the most common waders throughout its breeding and wintering ranges, and it is the species with which other waders tend to be compared. At 17–21&#160;cm (6.7–8.3&#160;in) length and with a 32–36&#160;cm (13–14&#160;in) <a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">wingspan</a>, it is similar in size to a <a href="/wiki/Common_starling" title="Common starling">common starling</a>, but stouter, with a thicker bill.
</p><p>The dunlin moves along the coastal mudflat beaches it prefers with a characteristic "sewing machine" feeding action, methodically picking small food items. <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">Insects</a> form the main part of the dunlin's diet on the nesting grounds; it eats <a href="/wiki/Mollusca" title="Mollusca">molluscs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worm" title="Worm">worms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> in coastal areas. 
</p>

<p>Dunlin are small migratory waders, however they show strong philopatry with individuals of the Southern Dunlin (<i>Calidris alpina schinzii</i>) in Sweden and Finland returning to, or very close to, their natal patches.  Habitat fragmentation has reduced the availability of habitat patches to these birds through reducing patch size and increasing patch isolation.  This reduced connectivity between patches has reduced the movements of Dunlin leaving them more susceptible to inbreeding in these locations.  Future management for the conservation of Southern Dunlin should include increasing the connectivity between habitat patches.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
