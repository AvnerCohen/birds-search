<p>The <b>black-crowned night heron</b> (<i>Nycticorax nycticorax</i>), or <b>black-capped night heron</b>, commonly shortened to just <b>night heron</b> in <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>, is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">heron</a> found throughout a large part of the world, except in the coldest regions and <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a> (where it is replaced by the closely related <a href="/wiki/Rufous_night_heron" class="mw-redirect" title="Rufous night heron">rufous night heron</a>, with which it has <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridized</a> in the area of contact).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-crowned_night_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adults have a black crown and back with the remainder of the body white or grey, red eyes, and short yellow legs. They have pale grey wings and white under parts. Two or three long white plumes, erected in greeting and courtship displays, extend from the back of the head. The sexes are similar in appearance although the males are slightly larger. Black-crowned night herons do not fit the typical body form of the heron family. They are relatively stocky with shorter bills, legs, and necks than their more familiar cousins, the egrets and "day" herons. Their resting posture is normally somewhat hunched but when hunting they extend their necks and look more like other wading birds.
</p><p>Immature birds have dull grey-brown plumage on their heads, wings, and backs, with numerous pale spots. Their underparts are paler and streaked with brown. The young birds have orange eyes and duller yellowish-green legs. They are very noisy birds in their nesting colonies, with calls that are commonly transcribed as <i>quok</i> or <i>woc</i>.
</p><p><b>Measurements</b>:<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Length</b>: 22.8-26.0 in (58-66 cm)</li>
<li><b>Weight</b>: 25.6-35.8 oz (727-1014 g)</li>
<li><b>Wingspan</b>: 45.3-46.5 in (115-118 cm)</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-crowned_night_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The breeding habitat is fresh and salt-water <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a> throughout much of the world. The subspecies <i>N. n. hoactli</i> breeds in North and South America from Canada as far south as northern <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, <i>N. n. obscurus</i> in southernmost South America, <i>N. n. falklandicus</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a>, and the nominate race <i>N. n. nycticorax</i> in Europe, Asia and Africa. Black-crowned night herons nest in <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonies</a> on platforms of sticks in a group of trees, or on the ground in protected locations such as islands or reedbeds. Three to eight eggs are laid.
</p><p>This heron is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> in the northernmost part of its range, but otherwise resident (even in the cold <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a>). The North American population winters in Mexico, the southern United States, Central America, and the West Indies, and the Old World birds winter in tropical Africa and southern Asia.
</p><p>A colony of the herons has regularly summered at the <a href="/wiki/National_Zoological_Park_(United_States)" title="National Zoological Park (United States)">National Zoo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> for more than a century.<sup id="cite_ref-Smithsonian’s_mystery_of_the_black-crowned_night_herons_solved_by_satellites_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smithsonian’s_mystery_of_the_black-crowned_night_herons_solved_by_satellites-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_in_Great_Britain">Status in Great Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-crowned_night_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Status in Great Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are two archaeological specimens of the black-crowned night heron in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>. The oldest is from the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> <a href="/wiki/London_Wall" title="London Wall">London Wall</a> and the more recent from the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>'s late medieval victualling yards in <a href="/wiki/Greenwich" title="Greenwich">Greenwich</a><sup id="cite_ref-Yalden_&amp;_Albarella_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yalden_&amp;_Albarella-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  It appears in the London <a href="/wiki/Poulterer" class="mw-redirect" title="Poulterer">poulterers</a> price lists as the Brewe, a bird which was thought to have been the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_whimbrel" title="Eurasian whimbrel">Eurasian whimbrel</a> or <a href="/wiki/Glossy_ibis" title="Glossy ibis">Glossy ibis</a>,  which has now been shown to refer to the black-crowned night heron, derived from the medieval French <i>Bihoreau</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bourne_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bourne-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>   Black-crowned night heron may have bred in the far wetter and wider landscape of pre-modern Britain.  They were certainly imported for the table so the bone specimens themselves do not prove they were part of the British avifauna.  In modern times the Black-crowned Night Heron is a vagrant and feral breeding colonies were established at <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Zoo" title="Edinburgh Zoo">Edinburgh Zoo</a> from 1950 into the 21st Century<sup id="cite_ref-Forrester_&amp;_Andrews_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forrester_&amp;_Andrews-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and at <a href="/wiki/Great_Witchingham" title="Great Witchingham">Great Witchingham</a> in Norfolk where there were 8 pairs in 2003 but breeding was not repeated in 2004 or 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> A pair of adults were seen with two recently fledged juveniles in Somerset in 2017, which is the first proven breeding record of wild black-crowned night herons in Great Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-RBA_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RBA-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-crowned_night_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>These birds stand still at the water's edge and wait to ambush prey, mainly at night or early morning. They primarily eat small <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_insect" title="Aquatic insect">aquatic insects</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, and small <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>. They are among the seven heron species observed to engage in bait fishing; luring or distracting fish by tossing edible or inedible buoyant objects into water within their striking range – a rare example of tool use among birds.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> During the day they rest in trees or bushes. <i>N. n. hoactli</i> is more gregarious outside the breeding season than the nominate race.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parasites">Parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-crowned_night_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A thorough study performed by J. Sitko and P. Heneberg in the Czech Republic in 1962–2013 suggested that the central European black-crowned night herons host 8 helminth species. The dominant species consisted of <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neogryporhynchus_cheilancristrotus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Neogryporhynchus cheilancristrotus (page does not exist)">Neogryporhynchus cheilancristrotus</a></i> (62% prevalence), <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contracaecum_microcephalum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Contracaecum microcephalum (page does not exist)">Contracaecum microcephalum</a></i> (55% prevalence) and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Opistorchis_longissimus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Opistorchis longissimus (page does not exist)">Opistorchis longissimus</a></i> (10% prevalence). The mean number of helminth species recorded per host individual reached 1.41. In Ukraine, other helminth species are often found in black-crowned night herons too, namely <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echinochasmus_beleocephalus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Echinochasmus beleocephalus (page does not exist)">Echinochasmus beleocephalus</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Echinochasmus_ruficapensis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Echinochasmus ruficapensis (page does not exist)">Echinochasmus ruficapensis</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clinostomum_complanatum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Clinostomum complanatum (page does not exist)">Clinostomum complanatum</a></i> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Posthodiplostomum_cuticola&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Posthodiplostomum cuticola (page does not exist)">Posthodiplostomum cuticola</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sitko_&amp;_Heneberg_2015_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sitko_&amp;_Heneberg_2015-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-crowned_night_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Nycticorax</i> means "night raven" and derives from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language romanization">nuktos</i>, "night", and <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language romanization">korax</i>, "raven". It refers to the largely <a href="/wiki/Nocturnal" class="mw-redirect" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a> feeding habits and croaking <a href="/wiki/Crow#Calls" title="Crow">crow-like</a> call of this species.<sup id="cite_ref-job_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a>, the bird is called "quark", which is an <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeia</a> similar to its name in many other languages, like "qua-bird" in English, "kwak" in Dutch and <a href="/wiki/West_Frisian_language" title="West Frisian language">West Frisian</a>, "kvakoš noční" in <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a>, "квак" in <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a>, "кваква" in Russian, "vạc" in <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a>, "kowak-malam" in <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">Indonesian</a>, and "waqwa" in <a href="/wiki/Quechuan_languages" title="Quechuan languages">Quechua</a>.
</p>
