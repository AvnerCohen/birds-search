<p>The <b>Little Shearwater</b> (<i>Puffinus assimilis</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Shearwater" title="Shearwater">shearwater</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> family <a href="/wiki/Procellariidae" title="Procellariidae">Procellariidae</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA" class="mw-redirect">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome <i>b</i></a> <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">sequence</a> data indicates that the former North Atlantic Little Shearwater group (<a href="/wiki/Boyd%27s_Shearwater" title="Boyd's Shearwater">Boyd's Shearwater</a>, <i>P. boydi</i> and <a href="/wiki/Barolo_Shearwater" title="Barolo Shearwater">Barolo Shearwater</a>, <i>P. baroli</i>) is closer to <a href="/wiki/Audubon%27s_Shearwater" title="Audubon's Shearwater">Audubon's Shearwater</a> (Austin 1996, Heidrich <i>et al.</i> 1998), (although many taxonomists now consider them to be distinct species), and <i>myrtae</i> being closer to the <a href="/wiki/Newell%27s_Shearwater" title="Newell's Shearwater">Newell's</a> and possibly <a href="/wiki/Townsend%27s_Shearwater" title="Townsend's Shearwater">Townsend's Shearwater</a> (Austin <i>et al.</i> 2004). <a href="/wiki/Heinroth%27s_Shearwater" title="Heinroth's Shearwater">Heinroth's Shearwater</a> was also sometimes considered a subspecies of this bird; the relationship between the Little and Audubon's Shearwater is probably not as close as long believed (Austin 1996, Heidrich <i>et al.</i> 1998, Austin <i>et al.</i> 2001, but see also Penhallurick &amp; Wink 2004, and Rheindt &amp; Austin 2005).</p>
<p>This species occurs throughout the oceans of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a> south of the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">Tropic of Capricorn</a>.</p>
<p>It breeds in colonies on islands and coastal cliffs, nesting in burrows which are only visited at night to avoid predation by large <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>.</p>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> has the typically "shearing" flight of the genus, dipping from side to side on stiff wings with few wingbeats, the wingtips almost touching the water, but in light winds has a more flapping flight than its larger relatives. This bird looks like a flying cross, with its wings held at right angles to the body, and it changes from black to white as the black upperparts and white undersides are alternately exposed as it travels low over the sea.</p>
<p>This is a gregarious species, which can been seen in large numbers from boats or headlands, especially on <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">passage</a> in autumn.</p>
<p>At 25–30 cm (9.8–12 in) in length with a 58–67&#160;cm (23–26&#160;in) wingspan, it is like a small <a href="/wiki/Manx_Shearwater" title="Manx Shearwater">Manx Shearwater</a> but has proportionally shorter and broader wings, with a pale area on the inner flight feathers. Its bill is more slender than that of Manx, and its dark eye stands out against the surrounding white area.</p>
<p>It feeds on <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc" class="mw-redirect">molluscs</a>. It does not follow boats. It is silent at sea, but at night the breeding colonies are alive with raucous cackling calls.</p>
<p>Despite the scientific name, this species is completely unrelated to the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Puffin" title="Atlantic Puffin">puffins</a>, which are <a href="/wiki/Auk" title="Auk">auks</a>, the only resemblance being that they are both burrow-nesting seabirds.</p>
