<p><b>Swinhoe's Storm Petrel</b> (<i>Oceanodroma monorhis</i>), also known as <b>Swinhoe's Petrel</b>, is a small, all-brown <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Storm_petrel" title="Storm petrel">storm petrel</a> family <a href="/wiki/Hydrobatidae" title="Hydrobatidae" class="mw-redirect">Hydrobatidae</a>.</p>
<p>It breeds on islands in the northwest <a href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific" class="mw-redirect">Pacific</a> off <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>. It nests in colonies close to the sea in rock crevices and lays a single white egg. It spends the rest of the year at sea, ranging into the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Swinhoe%27s_Storm_Petrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>Swinhoe's Storm Petrel is a small <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a>, 18–21&#160;cm in length with a 45–48&#160;cm wingspan, though distinctly larger than the <a href="/wiki/European_Storm_Petrel" title="European Storm Petrel">European Storm Petrel</a>. It is essentially dark brown in all plumages, and has a fluttering flight, pattering on the water surface as it picks <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">planktonic</a> food items from the ocean surface. Unlike the European Storm Petrel, it does not follow ships.</p>
<p>In structure it most resembles a <a href="/wiki/Leach%27s_Storm_Petrel" title="Leach's Storm Petrel">Leach's Storm Petrel</a> with its forked tail, longish wings, and flight behaviour, but does not have a white rump and the call differs. It is difficult to distinguish from other all-dark <i>Oceanodroma</i> species, and the first <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> record had to be <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>-tested to eliminate the possibility that it was a Leach's Storm Petrel, since populations of north-eastern Pacific Leach's Storm Petrels contain individuals that show completely dark rumps.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Swinhoe%27s_Storm_Petrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habits and observation">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Habits_and_observation">Habits and observation</span></h2>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Storm_petrel" title="Storm petrel">storm petrel</a> is strictly nocturnal at the breeding sites to avoid predation by <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skua" title="Skua">skuas</a>, and will even avoid coming to land on clear moonlit nights. Like most petrels, its walking ability is limited to a short shuffle to the burrow. It is strictly pelagic outside the breeding season, and this, together with its remote breeding sites, makes Swinhoe's Petrel a difficult bird to see from land. Only in storms might this species be pushed into headlands, but even then an out of range bird would probably defy definite identification.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Swinhoe%27s_Storm_Petrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h2>
<p>Widespread throughout its large range, the Swinhoe's Storm Petrel was previously evaluated as Least Concern on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> of Threatened Species, but uplisted to <a href="/wiki/Near_Threatened" title="Near Threatened">Near Threatened</a> in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Swinhoe%27s_Storm_Petrel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: North Atlantic status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="North_Atlantic_status">North Atlantic status</span></h2>
<p>In 1983, a bird was trapped on the Selvagens, <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a> on the 8th July and was confirmed to be the first record for the Atlantic Ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Since then a number of storm-petrels exhibiting plumage and structural characteristics have been recorded at sea, principally in the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" title="North Atlantic" class="mw-redirect">North Atlantic</a>, while birds were trapped during the summer months in France (1989), England (1989 [two birds], 1990 [with birds retrapped from 1991 to 1994]), Spain (1994), Norway (1996, 1997), and again Madeira (1991, 1994).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Other than the east <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" title="North Atlantic" class="mw-redirect">North Atlantic</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> a number of other individuals have been identified in the western <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" title="North Atlantic" class="mw-redirect">North Atlantic</a>. One was photographed off <a href="/wiki/Hatteras,_North_Carolina" title="Hatteras, North Carolina">Hatteras, North Carolina</a>, on August 8, 1998, but this record was not accepted by the <a href="/wiki/American_Birding_Association" title="American Birding Association">American Birding Association</a> or the <a href="/wiki/American_Ornithologists%27_Union" title="American Ornithologists' Union">American Ornithologists' Union</a> as at-sea records are not usually entirely reliable due to identification difficulties (AOU 2000). A second Hatteras record on the June 2, 2008, which was accompanied by excellent photos, is far more robust and would, pending acceptance, represent the first North American record of this species.</p>
<p>The records suggest that a small breeding population may have become established in the North Atlantic but as yet there has been no definitive proof.</p>
