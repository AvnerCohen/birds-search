<p>The <b>black-winged stilt</b> (<i>Himantopus himantopus</i>) is a widely distributed very long-legged <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Avocet" title="Avocet">avocet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stilt" title="Stilt">stilt</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> (<a href="/wiki/Recurvirostridae" title="Recurvirostridae">Recurvirostridae</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Scientific_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific name">scientific name</a> <i>H. himantopus</i> was formerly applied to a single, almost cosmopolitan <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>. It is now normally applied to the form that is widespread in <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurosiberia</a> and Africa and which was formerly regarded as the nominate subspecies of <i>Himantopus himantopus</i> <i><a href="/wiki/Sensu_lato" class="mw-redirect" title="Sensu lato">sensu lato</a></i>. The scientific name <i>Himantopus</i> comes from the Greek meaning "strap foot" or "thong foot".<sup id="cite_ref-Jobling2010_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jobling2010-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Most sources today accept 2–4 species.<sup id="cite_ref-2008a_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2008a-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2008b_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2008b-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2008c_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2008c-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BirdLifeD_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLifeD-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BirdLifeE_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLifeE-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BirdLifeF_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLifeF-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It is sometimes called pied stilt, but that name is now reserved for the Australian species, <i><a href="/wiki/Himantopus_leucocephalus" class="mw-redirect" title="Himantopus leucocephalus">Himantopus leucocephalus</a></i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-winged_stilt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Adults are 33–36&#160;cm (13–14&#160;in) long. They have long pink legs, a long thin black bill and are blackish above and white below, with a white head and neck with a varying amount of black. Males have a black back, often with greenish gloss. Females' backs have a brown hue, contrasting with the black <a href="/wiki/Remiges" class="mw-redirect" title="Remiges">remiges</a>. In the populations that have the top of the head normally white at least in winter, females tend to have less black on head and neck all year round, while males often have much black, particularly in summer. This difference is not clear-cut, however, and males usually get all-white heads in winter.
</p><p>Immature birds are grey instead of black and have a markedly sandy hue on the wings, with light feather fringes appearing as a whitish line in flight.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-winged_stilt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">taxonomy</a> of this bird is still somewhat contentious. It is one of five distinct <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> which were previously considered <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of <i>H. himantopus</i>.  <i>H. himantopus</i> <i>sensu lato</i>, is made up of one species and 5–7 subspecies, and was sometimes referred to as <b>common stilt</b>. The name black-winged stilt refers to <i>H. himantopus</i> <i><a href="/wiki/Sensu_stricto" class="mw-redirect" title="Sensu stricto">sensu stricto</a></i>, with two subspecies <i>H.h. himantopus</i> from the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> and <i>H.h. meridionalis</i> from the <a href="/wiki/Afrotropic" class="mw-redirect" title="Afrotropic">Afrotropical</a> region.<sup id="cite_ref-Avibase_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avibase-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology_and_status">Ecology and status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-winged_stilt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology and status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The breeding <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> of all these stilts is <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a>, shallow <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">ponds</a>. Some populations are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and move to the ocean coasts in winter; those in warmer regions are generally resident or short-range <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrants</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the black-winged stilt is a regular spring overshoot vagrant north of its normal range, occasionally remaining to breed in northern European countries. Pairs successfully bred in Britain in 1987,<sup id="cite_ref-Boyd1997_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boyd1997-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and after a 27-year hiatus there were two instances of successful breeding in Southern England in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> 13 young were fledged in southern England in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-Countryfile_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Countryfile-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>These birds pick up their food from sand or water. They eat mainly insects and crustaceans.
</p><p>The nest site is a bare spot on the ground near water. These birds often nest in small groups, sometimes with <a href="/wiki/Avocet" title="Avocet">avocets</a>.
</p>

<p>The black-winged stilt is one of the species to which the <i><a href="/wiki/Agreement_on_the_Conservation_of_African-Eurasian_Migratory_Waterbirds" title="Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a></i> applies.
</p>
