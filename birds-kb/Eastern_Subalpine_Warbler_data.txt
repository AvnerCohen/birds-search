<p>The <b>eastern subalpine warbler</b> (<i>Curruca cantillans</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Typical_warbler" title="Typical warbler">typical warbler</a> which breeds in the southernmost areas of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. It was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">first described</a> by the German naturalist <a href="/wiki/Peter_Simon_Pallas" title="Peter Simon Pallas">Peter Simon Pallas</a> in 1764 and given the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> <i>Motacilla cantillans</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The specific <i>cantillans</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "warbling" from <i>canere</i>, "to sing".<sup id="cite_ref-job_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Like most <i>Curruca</i> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, it has distinct male and female <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumages</a>. The adult male has a grey back and head, brick-red underparts, and white <a href="/wiki/Cheek" title="Cheek">malar</a> streaks ("moustaches"). The female is mainly brown above, with a greyer head, and whitish below with a pink flush. The subalpine warbler's song is fast and rattling, and is similar to the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_whitethroat" title="Lesser whitethroat">lesser whitethroat</a>.
</p><p>This bird seems to be related to the <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_warbler" title="Sardinian warbler">Sardinian warbler</a>-<a href="/wiki/Menetries%27_warbler" class="mw-redirect" title="Menetries&#39; warbler">Menetries' warbler</a> superspecies. They all have white <a href="/wiki/Cheek" title="Cheek">malar</a> areas, the heads being dark above in adult males, and naked eye-rings. These three species are related to a superspecies consisting of <a href="/wiki/R%C3%BCppell%27s_warbler" title="Rüppell&#39;s warbler">Rüppell's warbler</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cyprus_warbler" title="Cyprus warbler">Cyprus warbler</a>, which also share the white malar area with blackish above.(Shirihai et al. 2001,<sup id="cite_ref-Shirihai_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shirihai-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Jønsson &amp; Fjeldså 2006 <sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>)
The subalpine warbler is divided into two distinct <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> groups, which may possibly be sufficiently diverged to qualify as two separate <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> (Shirihai et al. 2001).<sup id="cite_ref-Shirihai_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shirihai-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The two groups have differing male plumages, distinctive calls, and are <a href="/wiki/Allopatric" class="mw-redirect" title="Allopatric">allopatric</a>; further study is needed. In May 2020, the IOC world bird list the split of western and eastern subalpine warbler as two distinct species.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Moltoni%27s_warbler" title="Moltoni&#39;s warbler">Moltoni's warbler</a> was formerly considered conspecific.
</p><p>Eastern subalpine warbler differs from the <a href="/wiki/Western_subalpine_warbler" title="Western subalpine warbler">western subalpine warbler</a> by its deeper blue-grey upperparts, a blackish mask on the lores and ear-coverts, brick reddish-brown coloration confined to the throat and breast and sharply demarcated from a largely white belly, paler flanks and a wider white submoustachial stripe.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This is a bird of dry open country, often on hill slopes, with bushes for nesting. The nest is built in low shrub or <a href="/wiki/Gorse" class="mw-redirect" title="Gorse">gorse</a>, and 3–5 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> are laid. Like most "<a href="/wiki/Warbler" title="Warbler">warblers</a>", it is <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a>, but will also take berries.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_ecology">Habitat and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_subalpine_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Habitat and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species prefers tall and dense heterogeneous maquis with sparse tree cover in dry Mediterranean areas, particularly maquis of holm oak (Quercus ilex) and those dominated by strawberry tree (Arbutus) and tree-heath (Erica). It is also frequently found in young cork oak (Quercus suber) forest and in dense but treeless bushy areas. It uses bushy formations dominated by brambles (Rubus fruticosus) along sunny ravines and valley bottoms and prefers the intermediate stages of post-wildfire succession. Breeding occurs from late March to late June and the species is monogamous. The male constructs several ‘cock nests’ but both sexes build the breeding nest which is a deep, robust cup of grasses, thin roots and leaves and lined with finer grasses, rootlets and hair. It is placed in low scrub, bush or a small tree, c. 30–130 cm above the ground. Clutches are three to five eggs. The diet is mostly small insects and their larvae but outside of the breeding season berries and fruits are also taken.
</p>
