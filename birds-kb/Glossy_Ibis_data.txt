<p>The <b>glossy ibis</b> (<i>Plegadis falcinellus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Wading_bird" class="mw-redirect" title="Wading bird">wading bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Ibis" title="Ibis">ibis</a> family Threskiornithidae. The scientific name derives from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>plegados</i> and <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, <i>falcis</i>, both meaning "sickle" and referring to the distinctive shape of the bill.<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glossy_ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Plegadis_falcinellus_MWNH_1952.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Plegadis_falcinellus_MWNH_1952.JPG/220px-Plegadis_falcinellus_MWNH_1952.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="232" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Plegadis_falcinellus_MWNH_1952.JPG/330px-Plegadis_falcinellus_MWNH_1952.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Plegadis_falcinellus_MWNH_1952.JPG/440px-Plegadis_falcinellus_MWNH_1952.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2585" data-file-height="2723" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Plegadis_falcinellus_MWNH_1952.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>This is the most widespread ibis species, breeding in scattered sites in warm regions of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> regions of the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Olson1981_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olson1981-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It is thought to have originated in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> and spread naturally from Africa to northern <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> in the 19th century, from where it spread to North America.<sup id="cite_ref-Patten_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Patten-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The glossy ibis was first found in the New World in 1817 (New Jersey). Audubon saw the species just once in Florida in 1832. It expanded its range substantially northwards in the 1940s and to the west in the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-Patten_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Patten-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This species is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>; most European birds winter in Africa, and in North America<sup id="cite_ref-nyt20130628_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt20130628-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> birds from north of <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">the Carolinas</a> winter farther south. Though generally suspected to be a migratory species in India, the glossy ibis is resident in western India.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Birds from other populations may disperse widely outside the breeding season. While generally declining in Europe, it has recently established a breeding colony in southern <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, and there appears to be a growing trend for the Spanish birds to winter in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, with at least 22 sightings in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-BritishBirds_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BritishBirds-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In 2014, a pair attempted to breed in <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>, the first such attempt in Britain <sup id="cite_ref-bbc_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> A few birds now spend most summers in Ireland, but as yet there is no evidence of breeding there. In <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, a few birds arrive there annually, mostly in the month of July, recently a pair bred amongst a colony of <a href="/wiki/Royal_Spoonbill" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Spoonbill">Royal Spoonbill</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NZB_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NZB-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glossy_ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Glossy ibises undertake dispersal movements after breeding and are highly nomadic. The more northerly populations are fully migratory and travel on a broad front, for example across the <a href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" class="mw-redirect" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a>. Glossy ibis ringed in the Black Sea seem to prefer the Sahel and West Africa to winter, those ringed in the Caspian Sea have been found to move to East Africa, the Arabic peninsula and as far east as Pakistan and India.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Numbers of glossy ibis in western India varied dramatically seasonally with the highest numbers being seen in the winter and summers, and drastically declining in the monsoon likely indicating local movements to a suitable area to breed.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Populations in temperate regions breed during the local spring, while tropical populations nest to coincide with the rainy season. Nesting is often in mixed-species colonies. When not nesting, flocks of over 100 individuals may occur on migration, and during the winter or dry seasons the species is usually found foraging in small flocks. Glossy ibises often roost communally at night in large flocks, with other species, occasionally in trees which can be some distance from wetland feeding areas.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glossy_ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Glossy ibises feed in very shallow water and nest in freshwater or brackish wetlands with tall dense stands of emergent vegetation such as reeds, <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a> or rushes) and low trees or bushes. They show a preference for marshes at the margins of lakes and rivers but can also be found at <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flood-plain" class="mw-redirect" title="Flood-plain">flood-plains</a>, wet <a href="/wiki/Meadow" title="Meadow">meadows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoirs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a> ponds, paddies and irrigated farmland. When using farmlands in western India, glossy ibis exhibited strong scale-dependent use of the landscape seasonally preferring using areas with &gt;200 ha of wetlands during the summer, and using areas that had intermediate amounts of wetlands (50-100 ha) in the other seasons.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It is less commonly found in coastal locations such as estuaries, deltas, <a href="/wiki/Salt_marsh" title="Salt marsh">salt marshes</a> and coastal lagoons. Preferred roosting sites are normally in large trees which may be distant from the feeding areas.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glossy_ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The nest is usually a platform of twigs and vegetation positioned at least 1&#160;m (3.3&#160;ft) above water, sometimes up to 7&#160;m (23&#160;ft) in tall, dense stands of emergent vegetation, low trees or bushes. 3 to 4 eggs (occasionally 5)  are laid, and are incubated by both male and female birds for between 20 and 23 days. The young can leave the nest after about 7 days, but the parents continue to feed them for another 6 or 7 weeks. The young fledge in about 28 days.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glossy_ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The diet of the glossy ibis is variable according to the season and is very dependent on what is available. Prey includes adult and larval <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> such as aquatic <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dragonflies" class="mw-redirect" title="Dragonflies">dragonflies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Damselflies" class="mw-redirect" title="Damselflies">damselflies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crickets" class="mw-redirect" title="Crickets">crickets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flies" class="mw-redirect" title="Flies">flies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caddis" class="mw-redirect" title="Caddis">caddisflies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Annelida" class="mw-redirect" title="Annelida">Annelida</a> including <a href="/wiki/Leech" title="Leech">leeches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molluscs" class="mw-redirect" title="Molluscs">molluscs</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Snails" class="mw-redirect" title="Snails">snails</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mussels" class="mw-redirect" title="Mussels">mussels</a>), <a href="/wiki/Crustaceans" class="mw-redirect" title="Crustaceans">crustaceans</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crayfish" title="Crayfish">crayfish</a>) and occasionally <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a> and nestling birds.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glossy_ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species is a mid-sized ibis. It is 48–66&#160;cm (19–26&#160;in) long, averaging around 59.4&#160;cm (23.4&#160;in) with an 80–105&#160;cm (31–41&#160;in) wingspan.<sup id="cite_ref-arkive_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arkive-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Culmen_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Culmen (bird)">culmen</a> measures 9.7 to 14.4&#160;cm (3.8 to 5.7&#160;in) in length, each wing measures 24.8–30.6&#160;cm (9.8–12.0&#160;in), the tail is 9–11.2&#160;cm (3.5–4.4&#160;in) and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> measures 6.8–11.3&#160;cm (2.7–4.4&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The body mass of this ibis can range from 485 to 970&#160;g (1.069 to 2.138&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Breeding adults have reddish-brown bodies and shiny bottle-green wings. Non-breeders and juveniles have duller bodies. This species has a brownish bill, dark facial skin bordered above and below in blue-gray (non-breeding) to cobalt blue (breeding), and red-brown legs. Unlike herons, ibises fly with necks outstretched, their flight being graceful and often in <a href="/wiki/V_formation" title="V formation">V formation</a>. It also has shiny feathers.
</p><p>Sounds made by this rather quiet ibis include a variety of croaks and grunts, including a hoarse <i>grrrr</i> made when breeding.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glossy_ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The glossy ibis is one of the species to which the <a href="/wiki/Agreement_on_the_Conservation_of_African-Eurasian_Migratory_Waterbirds" title="Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a> (AEWA) applies. Glossy ibises can be threatened by wetland habitat degradation and loss through drainage, <a href="/wiki/Saltwater_intrusion" title="Saltwater intrusion">increased salinity</a>, groundwater extraction and invasion by exotic plants.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common name black <a href="/wiki/Curlew" title="Curlew">curlew</a> may be a reference to the glossy ibis and this name appears in <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_literature" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon literature">Anglo-Saxon literature</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Derek_Yalden" title="Derek Yalden">Yalden</a> and Albarella do not mention this species as occurring in <a href="/wiki/Medieval_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval England">medieval England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Yalden2009_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yalden2009-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
