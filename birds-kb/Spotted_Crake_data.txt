<p>The <b>spotted crake</b> (<i>Porzana porzana</i>) is a small waterbird of the family <a href="/wiki/Rallidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Rallidae">Rallidae</a>. The scientific name is derived from <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venetian</a> terms for small rails.<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The spotted crake's breeding habitat is <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyperaceae" title="Cyperaceae">sedge</a> beds across temperate <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> into western <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. They nest in a dry location in marsh vegetation, laying 6–15 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>. This species is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>.
</p><p>At 19–22.5&#160;cm (7.5–8.9&#160;in) length, spotted crakes are slightly smaller than <a href="/wiki/Water_rail" title="Water rail">water rails</a>, from which they are readily distinguished by the short straight bill, yellow with a red base. Adults have mainly brown upperparts and blue-grey breast, with dark barring and white spots on the flanks. They have green legs with long toes, and a short tail which is buff underneath.
</p><p>Immature spotted crakes are similar, but the blue-grey is replaced by brown. The downy chicks are black, as with all <a href="/wiki/Rallidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Rallidae">rails</a>.
</p><p>The only confusion species is the <a href="/wiki/Sora_(bird)" title="Sora (bird)">sora</a>, a rare vagrant from <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. However, that species lacks the breast spotting and has an unstreaked crown stripe.
</p><p>These birds probe with their bill in mud or shallow water, also picking up food by sight. They mainly eat <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and aquatic animals.
</p><p>Spotted crakes are very secretive in the breeding season, and are then mostly heard rather than seen. They are then noisy <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, with a distinctive repetitive whiplash-like <i>hwuit, hwuit</i> call. They can be easier to see on migration.
</p><p>The spotted crake is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies. The  Western European population has declined  in recent decades, and the species is now a very rare breeding bird in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
