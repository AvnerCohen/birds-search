<p>The <b>Pied Avocet</b>, <i>Recurvirostra avosetta,</i> is a large black and white <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Avocet" title="Avocet">avocet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stilt" title="Stilt">stilt</a> family, <a href="/wiki/Recurvirostridae" title="Recurvirostridae">Recurvirostridae</a>. They breed in temperate <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and western and <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> species and most winter in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> or southern Asia. Some remain to winter in the mildest parts of their range, for example in southern <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.</p>
<p>This species gets its <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scientific_name" title="Scientific name" class="mw-redirect">scientific names</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">Venetian</a> name <i>avosetta</i>. It appeared first in Aldrovandi's <i>Ornithologia</i> (1603).<sup id="cite_ref-ODBBN_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODBBN-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> While the name may refer to black and white outfits once worn by European advocates or lawyers, the actual <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-ODBBN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODBBN-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Other <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">common names</a> include <b>Black-capped Avocet</b>, <b>Eurasian Avocet</b> or just <b>Avocet</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Pied Avocet is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Pied_Avocet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The Pied Avocet is a striking white wader with bold black markings. Adults have white <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">plumage</a> except for a black cap and black patches in the wings and on the back. They have long, upturned <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bills</a> and long, bluish legs. It is approximately 16.5–17.75 inches (42–45.1 cm) in length of which the bill is approximately 2.95–3.35 inches (7.5–8.5 cm) and the legs are approximately 3–4 inches (7.6–10 cm). Its wing-span is approximately 30–31.5 inches (76–80 cm).<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Males and females look alike. The juvenile resembles the adult but with more greyish and sepia tones.</p>
<p>The call of the Avocet is a far-carrying, liquid, melodious <i>kluit kluit</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Pied_Avocet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pied_Avocet_chick.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Pied_Avocet_chick.jpg/220px-Pied_Avocet_chick.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pied_Avocet_chick.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Pied Avocet (juveline) near Oosterend, Texel island, the Netherlands.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>These birds forage in shallow brackish water or on mud flats, often scything their bills from side to side in water (a feeding technique that is unique to the avocets<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup>). They mainly eat <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>.</p>
<p>Their <a href="/wiki/Breeding_in_the_wild" title="Breeding in the wild">breeding</a> <a href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" title="Habitat (ecology)" class="mw-redirect">habitat</a> is shallow lakes with brackish water and exposed bare mud. They nest on open ground, often in small groups, sometimes with other waders. 3-5 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> are laid in a lined scrape or on a mound of vegetation.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Pied_Avocet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In Britain">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="In_Britain">In Britain</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:69px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RSPBlogo.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/f/f3/RSPBlogo.png" width="67" height="68" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RSPBlogo.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Logo of the <a href="/wiki/RSPB" title="RSPB" class="mw-redirect">RSPB</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Avocet became <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinct</a> in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> in 1840.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Its successful recolonisation at <a href="/wiki/Minsmere_RSPB_reserve" title="Minsmere RSPB reserve">Minsmere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a> in 1947<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> led to its adoption as the logo of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Protection_of_Birds" title="Royal Society for the Protection of Birds">Royal Society for the Protection of Birds</a>.<br style="clear:both;" /></p>
