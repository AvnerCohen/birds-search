<p>The <b>pied avocet</b> (<i>Recurvirostra avosetta</i>) is a large black and white <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Avocet" title="Avocet">avocet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stilt" title="Stilt">stilt</a> family, <a href="/wiki/Recurvirostridae" title="Recurvirostridae">Recurvirostridae</a>. They breed in temperate Europe and across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> to <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> then on to the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Far_East" title="Russian Far East">Russian Far East</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> species and most winter in Africa or southern Asia. Some remain to winter in the mildest parts of their range, for example in southern Spain and southern England. The pied avocet is one of the species to which the <a href="/wiki/Agreement_on_the_Conservation_of_African-Eurasian_Migratory_Waterbirds" title="Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a> (AEWA) applies.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pied_avocet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The pied avocet was one of the many <a href="/wiki/Aves_in_the_10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="Aves in the 10th edition of Systema Naturae">bird species originally described</a> by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his landmark 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a>, where it was given the binomial name of <i>Recurvirostra avosetta</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This species gets its English and <a href="/wiki/Scientific_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific name">scientific names</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">Venetian</a> word <i>avosetta</i>. It appeared first in <a href="/wiki/Ulisse_Aldrovandi" title="Ulisse Aldrovandi">Ulisse Aldrovandi</a>'s <i>Ornithologia</i> (1603).<sup id="cite_ref-ODBBN_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODBBN-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> While the name may refer to black and white outfits once worn by European advocates or lawyers, the actual <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> is uncertain.<sup id="cite_ref-ODBBN_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODBBN-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Other <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">common names</a> include <b>black-capped avocet</b>, <b>Eurasian avocet</b> or just <b>avocet</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-avibase_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-avibase-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is one of four species of <a href="/wiki/Avocet" title="Avocet">avocet</a> that make up the genus <i>Recurvirostra</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The genus name is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>recurvus</i>, "curved backwards" and <i>rostrum</i>, "bill".<sup id="cite_ref-job_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> A 2004 study combining genetics and morphology showed that it was the most divergent species in the genus.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pied_avocet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The pied avocet is a striking white wader with bold black markings. Adults have white <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">plumage</a> except for a black cap and black patches in the wings and on the back. They have long, upturned <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bills</a> and long, bluish legs. It is approximately 16.5–17.75&#160;in (41.9–45.1&#160;cm) in length of which the bill is approximately 2.95–3.35&#160;in (7.5–8.5&#160;cm) and the legs are approximately 3–4&#160;in (7.6–10.2&#160;cm). Its wingspan is approximately 30–31.5&#160;in (76–80&#160;cm).<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Males and females look alike. The juvenile resembles the adult but with more greyish and sepia tones.
</p><p>The call of the avocet is a far-carrying, liquid, melodious <i>kluit kluit</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pied_avocet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pied_Avocet_chick.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Pied_Avocet_chick.jpg/220px-Pied_Avocet_chick.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Pied_Avocet_chick.jpg/330px-Pied_Avocet_chick.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Pied_Avocet_chick.jpg/440px-Pied_Avocet_chick.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4086" data-file-height="2724" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pied_Avocet_chick.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pied avocet (juvenile) near Oosterend, Texel island, the Netherlands</div></div></div>
<p>These birds forage in shallow brackish water or on mud flats, often scything their bills from side to side in water (a feeding technique that is unique to the avocets<sup id="cite_ref-Moreira1995_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moreira1995-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>). They mainly eat <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>.
</p><p>Their <a href="/wiki/Breeding_in_the_wild" title="Breeding in the wild">breeding</a> <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> is shallow lakes with brackish water and exposed bare mud. They nest on open ground, often in small groups, sometimes with other waders. Three to five <a href="/wiki/Egg" title="Egg">eggs</a> are laid in a lined scrape or on a mound of vegetation.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Britain">In Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pied_avocet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The pied avocet was <a href="/wiki/Local_extinction" title="Local extinction">extirpated</a> as a breeding species in Great Britain by 1840.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Its successful recolonisation at <a href="/wiki/RSPB_Minsmere" title="RSPB Minsmere">Minsmere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a>, in 1947 led to its adoption as the logo of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Protection_of_Birds" title="Royal Society for the Protection of Birds">Royal Society for the Protection of Birds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The pied avocet has spread inland and northwards and westwards in Britain since then and it has bred in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a><sup id="cite_ref-NRW_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NRW-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> in 2018 at <a href="/wiki/Skinflats" title="Skinflats">Skinflats</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RSPBScotland_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSPBScotland-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
