<p>The <b>dusky warbler</b> (<i>Phylloscopus fuscatus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Leaf_warbler" title="Leaf warbler">leaf warbler</a> which breeds in the east <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>. The genus name <i><a href="/wiki/Phylloscopus" class="mw-redirect" title="Phylloscopus">Phylloscopus</a></i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>phullon</i>, "leaf", and <i>skopos</i>, "seeker" (from <i>skopeo</i>, "to watch"). The specific <i>fuscatus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>fuscus</i> "dark".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This warbler is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and winters in <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/South-east_Asia" class="mw-redirect" title="South-east Asia">South-east Asia</a>. It sometimes occurs in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, and has also occurred in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.
</p><p>This is an abundant <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a> bogs and wet meadows. The nest is built low in a bush, and 5-6 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> are laid. Like most <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">Old World warblers</a>, this small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> is <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a>.
</p><p>The dusky warbler is prone to vagrancy as far as western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> in October, despite a 3000&#160;km distance from its breeding grounds. It has wintered in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>.
</p><p>This is a warbler similar in size and shape to a <a href="/wiki/Common_chiffchaff" title="Common chiffchaff">chiffchaff</a>. The adult has an unstreaked brown back and buff underparts. There is a prominent whitish supercilium, and the bill is fine and pointed. The sexes are identical, as with most warblers, but young birds are more olive-tinged above. Like most warblers, it is insectivorous, but will take other small food items, including berries.
</p><p>The song is a monotonous whistle, and the call is a harsh <i>check</i>. The call is often the first clue that this typically skulking species is present, away from the breeding grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
