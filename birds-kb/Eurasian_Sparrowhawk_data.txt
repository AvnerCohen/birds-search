<p>The <b>Eurasian</b> (or Northern) <b>Sparrowhawk</b> (<i>Accipiter nisus</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a>. Adult male Eurasian Sparrowhawks have bluish grey upperparts and orange-barred underparts; females and juveniles are brown above with brown barring below. The female is up to 25% larger than the male – one of the largest differences between the sexes in any bird species. Though it is a predator which specialises in catching <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodland</a> birds, the Eurasian Sparrowhawk can be found in any habitat and often hunts garden birds in towns and cities. Males tend to take smaller birds, including <a href="/wiki/Tit_(bird)" title="Tit (bird)">tits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finches</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sparrow" title="Sparrow">sparrows</a>; females catch primarily <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrushes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Starling" title="Starling">starlings</a>, but are capable of killing birds weighing 500 grams (18&#160;oz) or more.</p>
<p>The Eurasian Sparrowhawk is found throughout the temperate and subtropical parts of the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a>; while birds from the northern parts of the range <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> south for winter, their southern counterparts remain resident or make dispersive movements. Eurasian Sparrowhawks breed in suitable woodland of any type, with the nest, measuring up to 60&#160;cm (2.0&#160;ft) across, built using twigs in a tree. Four or five pale blue, brown-spotted eggs are laid; the success of the breeding attempt is dependent on the female maintaining a high weight while the male brings her food. The chicks hatch after 33 days and fledge after 24 to 28 days.</p>
<p>The proportion of juveniles surviving their first year is 34%, with 69% of adults surviving from one year to the next. Mortality in young males is greater than that of young females and the typical lifespan is four years. This species is now one of the commonest birds of prey in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, although the population crashed after the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War" class="mw-redirect">Second World War</a>. <a href="/wiki/Organochloride" title="Organochloride">Organochlorine</a> <a href="/wiki/Insecticides" title="Insecticides" class="mw-redirect">insecticides</a> used to treat seeds before sowing built up in the bird population and the concentrations in Eurasian Sparrowhawks were enough to kill some outright and incapacitate others; affected birds laid eggs with fragile shells which broke during incubation. However, its population recovered after the chemicals were banned, and it is now relatively common, classified as being of <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> by <a href="/wiki/BirdLife_International" title="BirdLife International">BirdLife International</a>.</p>
<p>The Eurasian Sparrowhawk's hunting behaviour has brought it into conflict with humans for hundreds of years, particularly <a href="/wiki/Pigeon_racing" title="Pigeon racing">racing pigeon</a> owners and people rearing <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">poultry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Game_(food)" title="Game (food)">gamebirds</a>. It has also been blamed for decreases in <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> populations; scientific research has found no link between increased numbers of Eurasian Sparrowhawks and declines in some farmland and woodland birds after World War II. Studies of racing pigeon deaths found that Eurasian Sparrowhawks were responsible for less than 1%. <a href="/wiki/Falconry" title="Falconry">Falconers</a> have utilised the Eurasian Sparrowhawk since at least the 16th century; although the species has a reputation for being difficult to train, it is also praised for its courage. The species features in <a href="/wiki/Teutonic_mythology" title="Teutonic mythology" class="mw-redirect">Teutonic mythology</a> and is mentioned in works by writers including <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alfred,_Lord_Tennyson" title="Alfred, Lord Tennyson">Alfred, Lord Tennyson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ted_Hughes" title="Ted Hughes">Ted Hughes</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Sparrowhawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>Within the Accipitridae family, the Eurasian Sparrowhawk is a member of the large <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Accipiter" title="Accipiter">Accipiter</a></i>, which consists of small to medium-sized woodland hawks. Most of the Old World members of the genus are called sparrowhawks or goshawks.<sup id="cite_ref-itis_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-itis-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The species name' dates back to the <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> word <i>sperhauk</i> and <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>spearhafoc</i>, a hawk which hunts sparrows. The <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> name for the Eurasian Sparrowhawk, <i>sparrhaukr</i>, was thought to have been coined by <a href="/wiki/Viking" title="Viking">Vikings</a> who encountered falconry in England.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> folk names for the Eurasian Sparrowhawk include Blue Hawk, referring to the adult male's colouration, as well as Hedge Hawk,<sup id="cite_ref-lore_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lore-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Spar Hawk, Spur Hawk and Stone Falcon.<sup id="cite_ref-air_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-air-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Eurasian Sparrowhawk was described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in his 18th century work, <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>, as <i>Falco nisus</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> but moved to its present genus by French zoologist <a href="/wiki/Mathurin_Jacques_Brisson" title="Mathurin Jacques Brisson">Mathurin Jacques Brisson</a> in 1760.<sup id="cite_ref-brisson_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brisson-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The current <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">scientific name</a> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>accipiter</i>, meaning 'hawk' and <i>nisus</i>, the sparrowhawk.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nisos" title="Nisos">Nisus</a>, the king of <a href="/wiki/Megara" title="Megara">Megara</a>, was turned into a sparrowhawk after his daughter, <a href="/wiki/Scylla_(princess)" title="Scylla (princess)">Scylla</a>, cut off his purple lock of hair to present to her lover (and Nisus' enemy), <a href="/wiki/Minos" title="Minos">Minos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Eurasian Sparrowhawk forms a <a href="/wiki/Superspecies" title="Superspecies">superspecies</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Rufous-chested_Sparrowhawk" title="Rufous-chested Sparrowhawk" class="mw-redirect">Rufous-chested Sparrowhawk</a> of eastern and southern Africa, and possibly the <a href="/wiki/Madagascar_Sparrowhawk" title="Madagascar Sparrowhawk">Madagascar Sparrowhawk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-fl_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fl-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Geographic variation is <a href="/wiki/Cline_(biology)" title="Cline (biology)">clinal</a>, with birds becoming larger and paler in the eastern part of the range compared to the west.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Within the species itself, six <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are generally recognised:<sup id="cite_ref-fl_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fl-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbw158_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw158-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>A. n. nisus</i>, the nominate subspecies, was described by Linnaeus in 1758.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw158_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw158-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> It breeds from <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and west <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> to western <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>; northern populations winter south to the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a>, north-east <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arabia" title="Arabia" class="mw-redirect">Arabia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>.</li>
<li><i>A. n. nisosimilis</i> was described by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Tickell" title="Samuel Tickell">Samuel Tickell</a> in 1833.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw158_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw158-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> It breeds from central and eastern Siberia east to <a href="/wiki/Kamchatka" title="Kamchatka" class="mw-redirect">Kamchatka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, and south to northern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. This subspecies is wholly migratory, wintering from <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> eastwards through <a href="/wiki/South-East_Asia" title="South-East Asia" class="mw-redirect">South-East Asia</a> and southern China to <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> and Japan; some even reach <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It is very similar to, but slightly larger than, the nominate subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>A. n. melaschistos</i> was described by <a href="/wiki/Allan_Octavian_Hume" title="Allan Octavian Hume">Allan Octavian Hume</a> in 1869.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw158_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw158-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> It breeds in mountains from <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a> to western China, and winters in the plains of South Asia. Larger<sup id="cite_ref-fl_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fl-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> and longer tailed than <i>nisosimilis</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> it has dark slate-coloured upperparts, and more distinct rufous barring on the underparts.<sup id="cite_ref-fl_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fl-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>A. n. wolterstorffi</i>, described by <a href="/wiki/Otto_Kleinschmidt" title="Otto Kleinschmidt">Otto Kleinschmidt</a> in 1900, is resident in <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw158_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw158-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> It is the smallest of all the races,<sup id="cite_ref-baker_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> darker on the upperparts and more barred below than the nominate subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>A. n. granti</i>, described by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bowdler_Sharpe" title="Richard Bowdler Sharpe">Richard Bowdler Sharpe</a> in 1890,<sup id="cite_ref-hbw158_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw158-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> is confined to <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>. It is small and dark.<sup id="cite_ref-gensbol_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gensbol-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>A. n. punicus</i>, described by Erlanger in 1897,<sup id="cite_ref-hbw158_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw158-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> is resident in north-west Africa, north of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-forsman_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forsman-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> It is very similar to <i>nisus</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> being large and pale.<sup id="cite_ref-gensbol_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gensbol-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Sparrowhawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Accipiter_nisus_-England_-immature_with_kill-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A Sparrowhawk standing on and plucking a large grey bird" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Accipiter_nisus_-England_-immature_with_kill-8.jpg/220px-Accipiter_nisus_-England_-immature_with_kill-8.jpg" width="220" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Accipiter_nisus_-England_-immature_with_kill-8.jpg/330px-Accipiter_nisus_-England_-immature_with_kill-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Accipiter_nisus_-England_-immature_with_kill-8.jpg/440px-Accipiter_nisus_-England_-immature_with_kill-8.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Accipiter_nisus_-England_-immature_with_kill-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A juvenile with a kill. Larger birds such as <a href="/wiki/Common_Wood_Pigeon" title="Common Wood Pigeon">Common Wood Pigeons</a> are more often killed by the larger female Eurasian Sparrowhawks; males usually hunt smaller prey.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Eurasian Sparrowhawk is a small bird of prey with short, broad wings and a long tail, both adaptations to manoeuvring through trees. Females can be up to 25% larger than males<sup id="cite_ref-bto_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> and weigh up to twice as much.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw56_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw56-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> When females are larger than males, it is known as reverse <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism#Sexual_dimorphism_in_birds" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a>; this is unusual in higher <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a> but typical in <a href="/wiki/Birds_of_prey" title="Birds of prey" class="mw-redirect">birds of prey</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> and most marked in birds of prey which hunt birds.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw56_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw56-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The adult male is 29–34 cm (11–13 in) long, with a <a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">wingspan</a> of 59–64 cm (23–25 in)<sup id="cite_ref-bto_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> and a mass of 110–196 g (3.9–6.9 oz).<sup id="cite_ref-hbw158_11-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw158-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> He has slate-grey upperparts (sometimes tending to bluish), with finely red-barred underparts, which can look plain orange from a distance; his <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">irides</a> are orange-yellow or orange-red. The female is much larger at 35–41 cm (14–16 in) long, with a wingspan of 67–80 cm (26–31 in),<sup id="cite_ref-bto_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> and a mass of 185–342 g (6.5–12.1 oz).<sup id="cite_ref-hbw158_11-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw158-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> She has dark brown or greyish-brown upperparts, and brown-barred underparts, and bright yellow to orange irides. The juvenile is warm brown above, with rusty fringes to the upperparts; and coarsely barred or spotted brown below, with pale yellow eyes;<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> its throat has dark streaks and lacks a mesial (midline) stripe.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Eurasian Sparrowhawk's pale underparts and darker upperparts are an example of <a href="/wiki/Countershading" title="Countershading">countershading</a>, which helps to break up the bird's outline. Countershading is exhibited by birds of prey which hunt birds and other fast-moving animals. The horizontal barring seen on adult Eurasian Sparrowhawks is typical of woodland-dwelling predatory birds, while the adult male's bluish colour is also seen in other bird-eating raptors, including the <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_Falcon" title="Peregrine Falcon">Peregrine Falcon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Merlin_(bird)" title="Merlin (bird)">Merlin</a> and other <i>Accipiters</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-newton28_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newton28-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Common Cuckoo in flight, showing barred underparts" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo.jpg/220px-Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo.jpg" width="220" height="152" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo.jpg/330px-Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo.jpg/440px-Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cuculus_canorus_vogelartinfo.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Resemblance to the Eurasian Sparrowhawk helps the <a href="/wiki/Common_Cuckoo" title="Common Cuckoo">Common Cuckoo</a> to avoid aggression from the small birds whose nest it seeks to parasitise.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A study, using <a href="/wiki/Taxidermy" title="Taxidermy">stuffed</a> bird models, found that small birds are less likely to approach <a href="/wiki/Common_Cuckoo" title="Common Cuckoo">Common Cuckoos</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasite</a>) which have barred underparts like the Eurasian Sparrowhawk. <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Reed_Warbler" title="Eurasian Reed Warbler">Eurasian Reed Warblers</a> were found to be more aggressive to cuckoos which looked less hawk-like, meaning that the resemblance to the hawk helps the cuckoo to access the nests of potential hosts.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Eurasian Sparrowhawk's small bill is used for plucking feathers and pulling prey apart, rather than killing or cutting. Its long legs and toes are an adaptation for catching and eating birds. The outer toe is <i>"fairly long and slender"</i>; the inner toe and back toe are relatively short and thick. The middle toe is very long and can be used to grasp objects, while a protuberance on the underside of the toe means that the digit can be closed without leaving a gap, which helps with gripping.<sup id="cite_ref-newton28_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newton28-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The flight is a characteristic "flap-flap-glide", with the glide creating an undulating pattern.<sup id="cite_ref-collins_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> This species is similar in size to the <a href="/wiki/Levant_Sparrowhawk" title="Levant Sparrowhawk">Levant Sparrowhawk</a>, but larger than the <a href="/wiki/Shikra" title="Shikra">Shikra</a> (the calls are however different<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup>); the male is only slightly larger than the Merlin. Because of the overlap in sizes, the female can be confused with the similarly-sized male <a href="/wiki/Northern_Goshawk" title="Northern Goshawk">Northern Goshawk</a>, but lacks the bulk of that species. Eurasian Sparrowhawks are smaller, more slender and have shorter wings, a square-ended tail and fly with faster wingbeats.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> A confusion species in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Besra" title="Besra">Besra</a>, although <i>A. n. melaschistos</i> is considerably larger.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, Eurasian Sparrowhawks living further north are bigger than their more southerly counterparts, with wing length (the most reliable indicator of body size) increasing by an average of 0.86&#160;mm (0.034&#160;in) in males, and 0.75&#160;mm (0.030&#160;in) in females, for each degree further north.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Sparrowhawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lifespan and demography">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Lifespan_and_demography">Lifespan and demography</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Accipiter_nisus_9194.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Accipiter_nisus_9194.JPG/220px-Accipiter_nisus_9194.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Accipiter_nisus_9194.JPG/330px-Accipiter_nisus_9194.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Accipiter_nisus_9194.JPG/440px-Accipiter_nisus_9194.JPG 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Accipiter_nisus_9194.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
The yellow iris of this juvenile male Eurasian Sparrowhawk (caught for <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">ringing</a>) may turn orange in later life.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The oldest known wild Eurasian Sparrowhawk lived more than two decades; it was found dead in <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> 20&#160;years and 3&#160;months after having been <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">ringed</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> The typical lifespan is four years. Data analysis by the <a href="/wiki/British_Trust_for_Ornithology" title="British Trust for Ornithology">British Trust for Ornithology</a> shows that the proportion of juveniles surviving their first year of life is 34%; adult survival from one year to the next is 69%.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Birds in their first year of life weigh less than adults, and are especially light in the first two months after reaching independence. There is probably high mortality, especially for young males, during this time.<sup id="cite_ref-weights_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weights-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> A study in southern Scotland suggested that the greater mortality in young male birds may be due to their smaller size and the smaller size of their prey, which means that they can <i>"last less long between meals."</i> Their size also means that their range of prey is restricted. It has been estimated that a female Eurasian Sparrowhawk of average weight could survive for seven days without feeding – three days longer than a male of average weight.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A study of female Eurasian Sparrowhawks found "strong evidence" that their rate of survival increased for the first three years of life, and declined for the last five to six years. <a href="/wiki/Senescence" title="Senescence">Senescence</a> (ageing) was the cause of the decline as the birds became older.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Sparrowhawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_Sparrowhawk.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Eurasian_Sparrowhawk.JPG/170px-Eurasian_Sparrowhawk.JPG" width="170" height="207" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Eurasian_Sparrowhawk.JPG/255px-Eurasian_Sparrowhawk.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Eurasian_Sparrowhawk.JPG/340px-Eurasian_Sparrowhawk.JPG 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_Sparrowhawk.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Eurasian Sparrowhawk in city <a href="/wiki/Penza" title="Penza">Penza</a>, Russia</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A widespread species throughout the temperate and subtropical parts of the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-forsman_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forsman-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> the Eurasian Sparrowhawk is resident or breeds in an estimated global range of 23,600,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (9,100,000&#160;miles<sup>2</sup>) and had an estimated population of 1.5 million birds in 2009. Although global population trends have not been analysed, numbers seem to be stable, so it has been classified as being of <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> by <a href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN" class="mw-redirect">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The race <i>granti</i>, with 100 pairs resident on <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a> and 200 pairs on the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, is threatened by loss of habitat, <a href="/wiki/Egg_collecting" title="Egg collecting" class="mw-redirect">egg-collecting</a> and illegal hunting, and is listed on Annex I of the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> <a href="/wiki/Birds_Directive" title="Birds Directive">Birds Directive</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> It is one of the most common birds of prey in Europe, along with the <a href="/wiki/Common_Kestrel" title="Common Kestrel">Common Kestrel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_Buzzard" title="Common Buzzard">Common Buzzard</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-forsman_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forsman-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norwegian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albanian</a> populations are declining and, in many parts of Europe, Eurasian Sparrowhawks are still shot. However, this low-level persecution has not affected the populations badly.<sup id="cite_ref-gensbol_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gensbol-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> In the UK, the population increased by 108% between 1970–2005, but saw a 1% decline over 1994–2006.<sup id="cite_ref-predation_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-predation-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> it is the commonest bird of prey, breeding even near the city centre of <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Buffwood_4171.JPG" class="image"><img alt="Open deciduous woodland" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Buffwood_4171.JPG/220px-Buffwood_4171.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Buffwood_4171.JPG/330px-Buffwood_4171.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Buffwood_4171.JPG/440px-Buffwood_4171.JPG 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Buffwood_4171.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Deciduous woodland is a typical breeding and hunting habitat for the Eurasian Sparrowhawk.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This species is common in most woodland types in its range and also in more open country with scattered trees.<sup id="cite_ref-jonsson_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jonsson-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> Eurasian Sparrowhawks prefer to hunt woodland edges, but migrant birds can be seen in any habitat.<sup id="cite_ref-forsman_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forsman-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The increased proportion of medium-aged stands of trees created by modern forestry techniques have benefited the species, according to a <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norwegian</a> study.<sup id="cite_ref-norway_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-norway-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> Unlike its larger relative the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Goshawk" title="Northern Goshawk">Northern Goshawk</a>, it can be seen in gardens and in urban areas<sup id="cite_ref-gbw_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gbw-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> and will even breed in city parks.<sup id="cite_ref-jonsson_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jonsson-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Eurasian Sparrowhawks from colder regions of northern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> south for the winter, some to <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">north Africa</a> (some as far as equatorial east Africa) and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>; members of the southern populations are resident or disperse. Juveniles begin their migration earlier than adults and juvenile females move before juvenile males.<sup id="cite_ref-forsman_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forsman-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Analysis of <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">ringing</a> data collected at <a href="/wiki/Heligoland" title="Heligoland">Heligoland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, found that males move further and more often than females; of migrating birds ringed at <a href="/wiki/Kaliningrad" title="Kaliningrad">Kaliningrad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, the average distance moved before recovery (when the ring is read and the bird's whereabouts reported subsequently) was 1,328&#160;km (825&#160;mi) for males and 927&#160;km (576&#160;mi) for females.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A study of Eurasian Sparrowhawks in southern <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> found that ringed birds which had been raised on "high grade" <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territories</a> were recovered in greater proportion than birds which came from "low grade" territories. This suggested that the high grade territories produced young which survived better. The recovery rate also declined with increased elevation of the ground. After the post-fledging period, female birds dispersed greater distances than did males.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Sparrowhawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Food, feeding and predation">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Food.2C_feeding_and_predation">Food, feeding and predation</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Accipiter_nisus_kill.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A Sparrowhawk plucking a small bird" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Accipiter_nisus_kill.jpg/220px-Accipiter_nisus_kill.jpg" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Accipiter_nisus_kill.jpg/330px-Accipiter_nisus_kill.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Accipiter_nisus_kill.jpg/440px-Accipiter_nisus_kill.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Accipiter_nisus_kill.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Eurasian Sparrowhawks, like this male, discard the larger feathers from their prey before eating the flesh.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Eurasian Sparrowhawk is a major predator of smaller woodland birds,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> though only 10% of its hunting attacks are successful.<sup id="cite_ref-gbb_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gbb-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> It hunts by surprise attack, using <a href="/wiki/Hedge" title="Hedge">hedges</a>, tree-belts, <a href="/wiki/Copse" title="Copse">copses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orchard" title="Orchard">orchards</a> and other cover near woodland areas; its choice of habitat is dictated by these requirements. It also makes use of gardens in built-up areas, taking advantage of the prey found there.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_14-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It waits, hidden, for birds to come near, then breaks cover and flies out fast and low. A chase may follow, with the hawk even flipping upside-down to grab the victim from below or following it on foot through vegetation. It can "stoop" onto prey from a great height.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_14-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ian_Newton" title="Ian Newton">Ian Newton</a> describes seven modes of hunting used by Eurasian Sparrowhawks:<sup id="cite_ref-newton102_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newton102-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Short-stay-perch-hunting</li>
<li>High soaring and stooping</li>
<li>Contour-hugging in flight</li>
<li>Still-hunting</li>
<li>Low quartering</li>
<li>Hunting by sound</li>
<li>Hunting on foot</li>
</ul>
<p>Male Eurasian Sparrowhawks regularly kill birds weighing up to 40&#160;g (1.4&#160;oz) and sometimes up to 120&#160;g (4.2&#160;oz); females can tackle prey up to 500&#160;g (18&#160;oz) or more. The weight of food consumed by adult birds daily is estimated to be 40–50 g (1.4–1.8 oz) for males and 50–70 g (1.8–2.5 oz) for females. During one year, a pair of Eurasian Sparrowhawks could take 2,200&#160;<a href="/wiki/House_Sparrow" title="House Sparrow">House Sparrows</a>, 600&#160;<a href="/wiki/Common_Blackbird" title="Common Blackbird">Common Blackbirds</a> or 110&#160;<a href="/wiki/Common_Wood_Pigeon" title="Common Wood Pigeon">Wood Pigeons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_14-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Species that feed in the open, far from cover, or are conspicuous by their behaviour or coloration, are taken more often by Eurasian Sparrowhawks. For example, <a href="/wiki/Great_Tit" title="Great Tit">Great Tits</a> and House Sparrows are vulnerable to attack. Eurasian Sparrowhawks may account for more than 50% of deaths in certain species, but the extent varies from area to area.<sup id="cite_ref-newton137_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newton137-38"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Males tend to take <a href="/wiki/Tit_(bird)" title="Tit (bird)">tits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sparrow" title="Sparrow">sparrows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bunting_(bird)" title="Bunting (bird)">buntings</a>; females often take <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrushes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Starling" title="Starling">starlings</a>. Larger quarry (such as <a href="/wiki/Dove" title="Dove" class="mw-redirect">doves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magpie" title="Magpie">magpies</a>) may not die immediately but succumb during feather plucking and eating. More than 120 bird species have been recorded as prey and individual Eurasian Sparrowhawks may specialise in certain prey. The birds taken are usually adults or fledglings, though chicks in the nest and <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">carrion</a> are sometimes eaten. Small mammals, including <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> are sometimes caught but insects are eaten only very rarely.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_14-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:House_Sparrow-Mindaugas_Urbonas-1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A small grey and brown bird" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/House_Sparrow-Mindaugas_Urbonas-1.jpg/220px-House_Sparrow-Mindaugas_Urbonas-1.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/House_Sparrow-Mindaugas_Urbonas-1.jpg/330px-House_Sparrow-Mindaugas_Urbonas-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/House_Sparrow-Mindaugas_Urbonas-1.jpg/440px-House_Sparrow-Mindaugas_Urbonas-1.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:House_Sparrow-Mindaugas_Urbonas-1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Birds which feed out in the open, like this <a href="/wiki/House_Sparrow" title="House Sparrow">House Sparrow</a>, are more vulnerable to predation.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Small birds are killed on impact or when squeezed by the Eurasian Sparrowhawk's foot, especially the two long claws. Victims which struggle are "kneaded" by the hawk, using its talons to squeeze and stab. When dealing with large prey species which peck and flap, the hawk's long legs help.<sup id="cite_ref-newton107_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newton107-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> It stands on top of its prey to pluck and pull it apart.<sup id="cite_ref-newton28_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newton28-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The feathers are plucked and usually the breast muscles are eaten first. The bones are left, but can be broken using the notch in the bill. Like other birds of prey, Eurasian Sparrowhawks produce <a href="/wiki/Pellet_(ornithology)" title="Pellet (ornithology)">pellets</a> containing indigestible parts of their prey. These range from 25 to 35&#160;mm (1.0–1.4&#160;inches) long and 10–18&#160;mm (0.4–0.7&#160;inches) wide and are round at one end and more narrow and pointed at the other. They are usually composed of small feathers, as the larger ones are plucked and not consumed.<sup id="cite_ref-tracks_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tracks-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>During hunting, this species can fly 2–3&#160;km (1.2–1.9&#160;miles) per day. It rises above tree level mostly to display, soar above territory and to make longer journeys.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_14-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> A study in a forested area of <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> found that the mean size of the home ranges was 9.2&#160;km² (3.5&#160;miles²) for males, and 12.3&#160;km² (4.7&#160;miles²) for females, which was larger than studies in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> had found, <i>"probably due to lower land productivity and associated lower densities of prey species in the [Norwegian study area]"</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-norway_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-norway-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A study looked at the effect on the population of <a href="/wiki/Blue_Tit" title="Blue Tit">Blue Tits</a> in an area where a pair of Eurasian Sparrowhawks began to breed in 1990. It found that the annual adult survival rate for the tits in that area dropped from 0.485 to 0.376 (the rate in adjacent plots did not change). The size of the breeding population was not changed, but there were fewer non-breeding Blue Tits in the population.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> In woodland, Eurasian Sparrowhawks account for the deaths of a third of all young Great Tits;<sup id="cite_ref-gbb_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gbb-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> the two alarm calls given by Great Tits when mobbing a predator, and when fleeing from a nearby hawk, are within the optimum hearing range of both prey and predator; however, the high-pitched alarm call given when a distant flying Eurasian Sparrowhawk is seen <i>"can only be heard well by the tit."</i><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> Research carried out in <a href="/wiki/Sussex" title="Sussex">Sussex</a>, England, found that the impact of Eurasian Sparrowhawk predation on <a href="/wiki/Grey_Partridge" title="Grey Partridge">Grey Partridges</a> was highest when the partridge density was lowest,<sup id="cite_ref-basc_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-basc-44"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> while a 10-year study in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> found that Eurasian Sparrowhawks did not select the <a href="/wiki/Common_Redshank" title="Common Redshank">Common Redshanks</a> they predated according to the waders' size or condition, probably because of the hawks' surprise-attack hunting technique.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Another study found that the risk of predation for a bird targeted by a Eurasian Sparrowhawk or <a href="/wiki/Northern_Goshawk" title="Northern Goshawk">Northern Goshawk</a> increased 25-fold if the prey was infected with the blood <a href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite" class="mw-redirect">parasite</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Leucocytozoon" title="Leucocytozoon">Leucocytozoon</a></i>, and birds with <a href="/wiki/Avian_malaria" title="Avian malaria">avian malaria</a> were 16 times more likely to be killed.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Sparrowhawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Predators">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Predators">Predators</span></h3>
<p>Natural predators of the Eurasian Sparrowhawk include the <a href="/wiki/Barn_Owl" title="Barn Owl">Barn Owl</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tawny_Owl" title="Tawny Owl">Tawny Owl</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Goshawk" title="Northern Goshawk">Northern Goshawk</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_Falcon" title="Peregrine Falcon">Peregrine Falcon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Eagle" title="Golden Eagle">Golden Eagle</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Eagle_Owl" title="Eagle Owl" class="mw-redirect">Eagle Owl</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">Fox</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Stone_Marten" title="Stone Marten" class="mw-redirect">Stone Marten</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pine_Marten" title="Pine Marten" class="mw-redirect">Pine Marten</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-historical_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-historical-47"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Sparrowhawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Accipiter_nisus_Forst_Jungfernheide_080610.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Three fluffy, white chicks in a nest" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Accipiter_nisus_Forst_Jungfernheide_080610.jpg/220px-Accipiter_nisus_Forst_Jungfernheide_080610.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Accipiter_nisus_Forst_Jungfernheide_080610.jpg/330px-Accipiter_nisus_Forst_Jungfernheide_080610.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Accipiter_nisus_Forst_Jungfernheide_080610.jpg/440px-Accipiter_nisus_Forst_Jungfernheide_080610.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Accipiter_nisus_Forst_Jungfernheide_080610.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
The chicks stay on the nest until they are 24 to 28 days old.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Eurasian Sparrowhawk breeds in well-grown, extensive areas of woodland, often coniferous or mixed, preferring forest with a structure neither too dense nor too open, to allow a choice of flight paths. The <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a> can be located in the fork of a tree, often near the trunk and where two or three branches begin, on a horizontal branch in the lower canopy, or near the top of a tall shrub. If available, <a href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer" class="mw-redirect">conifers</a> are preferred. A new nest is built every year, generally close to the nest of the previous year, and sometimes using an old Wood Pigeon (<i>A. n. melaschistos</i> frequently uses the old nests of <a href="/wiki/Jungle_Crow" title="Jungle Crow">Jungle Crows</a><sup id="cite_ref-hbk_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup>) nest as a base; the male does most of the work. The structure, made of loose twigs up to 60&#160;cm (2.0&#160;ft) long, has an average diameter of 60&#160;cm (24&#160;in). When the <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> are laid, a lining of fine twigs or bark chippings is added.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_14-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>During the breeding season, the adult male Eurasian Sparrowhawk loses a small amount of weight while feeding his mate before she lays eggs, and also when the young are large and require more food. The weight of the adult female is highest in May, when laying eggs, and lowest in August after the breeding cycle is complete. A study suggested that the number of eggs and subsequent breeding success are dependent on the female maintaining a high weight while the male is feeding her.<sup id="cite_ref-weights_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weights-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sexual_maturity" title="Sexual maturity">Sexual maturity</a> is reached at between 1–3 years.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw158_11-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw158-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Most Eurasian Sparrowhawks stay on the same territory for one breeding season, though others keep the same one for up to eight years. A change of mate usually triggers the change in territory. Older birds tend to stay in the same territory; failed breeding attempts make a move more likely. The birds which kept the same territories had higher nest success, though it did not increase between years; females which moved experienced more success the year after changing territory.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The eggs are pale blue with brown spots and each measure 35–46 x 28–35&#160;mm (1.4–1.8 x 1.1–1.4&#160;inches),<sup id="cite_ref-Eggs_1994_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eggs_1994-49"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> and weigh about 22.5&#160;g (0.8&#160;oz) of which 8% is shell in a healthy egg.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Usually a clutch of four or five eggs is laid. The eggs are generally laid in the morning with an interval of 2–3 days between each egg. If a clutch is lost, up to two further eggs may be laid that are smaller than the earlier eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_14-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Sparrowhawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Young">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Young">Young</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Altricial" title="Altricial">altricial</a>, downy chicks hatch after 33&#160;days of <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" title="Avian incubation" class="mw-redirect">incubation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> After hatching, the female cares for and feeds the chicks for the first 8–14&#160;days of life, and also during bad weather after that. The male provides food, up to six kills per day in the first week increasing to eight per day in the third and 10&#160;per day in the last week in the nest, by which time the female is also hunting.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_14-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>By 24–28&#160;days after hatching, the young birds start to perch on branches near the nest and take their first flight. They are fed by their parents for a further 28–30&#160;days, staying close to the nest while growing and practicing flying. The young hawks disperse after their parents stop provisioning them.<sup id="cite_ref-newton213_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newton213-50"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> Though they receive the same amount of food, male chicks (roughly half the size of females) mature more quickly and seem to be ready to leave the nest sooner.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> In a study in the <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Ae" title="Forest of Ae">Forest of Ae</a>, south-west <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, it was found that 21% of nestlings over two days old died, with the causes of death being starvation, wet weather, predation and desertion by the parents.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> The parasite <i>Leucocytozoon toddi</i> can be passed from parent to nestling at the nest, possibly because of the number of birds sharing a small space, thus allowing transmission.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Sparrowhawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Pollutants">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Pollutants">Pollutants</span></h3>

<p>The Eurasian Sparrowhawk population in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> crashed in the second half of the 20th Century.<sup id="cite_ref-forsman_16-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forsman-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The decline coincided with the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Cyclodiene" title="Cyclodiene" class="mw-redirect">cyclodiene</a> <a href="/wiki/Insecticide" title="Insecticide">insecticides</a> – <a href="/wiki/Aldrin" title="Aldrin">aldrin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dieldrin" title="Dieldrin">dieldrin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heptachlor" title="Heptachlor">heptachlor</a> – used as <a href="/wiki/Seed_treatment" title="Seed treatment">seed dressings</a> in <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> in 1956. The chemicals accumulated in the bodies of grain-eating birds and had two effects on <a href="/wiki/Top_predator" title="Top predator" class="mw-redirect">top predators</a> like the Eurasian Sparrowhawk and <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_Falcon" title="Peregrine Falcon">Peregrine Falcon</a>: the shells of eggs they laid were too thin, causing them to break during incubation;<sup id="cite_ref-gbw_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gbw-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> and birds were poisoned by lethal concentrations of the insecticides.<sup id="cite_ref-walker_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-walker-54"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> Sub-lethal effects of these substances include irritability, <a href="/wiki/Convulsion" title="Convulsion">convulsions</a> and disorientation.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> In west <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, around 80% of nests before the 1950s produced young, but only 54% were successful in the 1960s and '70s.<sup id="cite_ref-ibc92_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibc92-56"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, for example, the species almost became extinct in <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a>, where the chemicals were most widely used; in western and northern parts of the country, where the pesticides were not used, there were no declines.<sup id="cite_ref-walker_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-walker-54"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Protection_of_Birds" title="Royal Society for the Protection of Birds">Royal Society for the Protection of Birds</a> bought its <a href="/wiki/Coombes_Valley_RSPB_reserve" title="Coombes Valley RSPB reserve">Coombes Valley</a> <a href="/wiki/Nature_reserve" title="Nature reserve">nature reserve</a> in <a href="/wiki/Staffordshire" title="Staffordshire">Staffordshire</a> because it was the only Eurasian Sparrowhawk breeding site left in the <a href="/wiki/English_Midlands" title="English Midlands" class="mw-redirect">English Midlands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-brit_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brit-57"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the UK, the use of cyclodienes as seed dressings for autumn-sown <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereals</a> was banned in 1975 and the levels of the chemicals present in the bird population began to fall.<sup id="cite_ref-walker_54-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-walker-54"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> The population has largely recovered to pre-decline levels, with an increase seen in many areas, for example northern Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-forsman_16-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forsman-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, the population also decreased drastically from the 1950s, but recovered again once organochlorines were banned in the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the UK, the failure rate at the egg stage had decreased from 17% to 6% by the year 2000, and the population had stabilised after reaching a peak in the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> A study of the eggs of <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> Eurasian Sparrowhawks found that contamination with <a href="/wiki/Dichlorodiphenyldichloroethylene" title="Dichlorodiphenyldichloroethylene">Dichlorodiphenyldichloroethylene</a> (DDE) – a "very persistent compound" produced when DDT breaks down<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> – continued into the 1980s, though a decline in the number of clutches with broken eggs during the 1970s suggested decreasing levels of the chemical.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Body tissue samples from Eurasian Sparrowhawks are still analysed as part of the Predatory Bird Monitoring Scheme conducted by the <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Government" title="Her Majesty's Government" class="mw-redirect">UK government</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Joint_Nature_Conservation_Committee" title="Joint Nature Conservation Committee">Joint Nature Conservation Committee</a>. Although the average liver concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyl" title="Polychlorinated biphenyl">polychlorinated biphenyls</a> (PCBs) in Eurasian Sparrowhawks were lower in birds that died in 2005 compared to those that died in 2004, there was not a significant or consistent decline in residues between 2000–2005.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Sparrowhawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Conflict with human interests">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Conflict_with_human_interests">Conflict with human interests</span></h3>
<p>The Eurasian Sparrowhawk's adaptation for feeding on birds has brought it into conflict with humans;<sup id="cite_ref-gbw_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gbw-33"><span
