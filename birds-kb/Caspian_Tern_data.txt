<p>The <b>Caspian Tern</b> (<i>Hydroprogne caspia</i>, formerly <i>Sterna caspia</i>;<sup id="cite_ref-bridge_0-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bridge-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Synonym_(taxonomy)" title="Synonym (taxonomy)">syn.</a> <i>Hydroprogne tschegrava</i>, <i>Helopus caspius</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">tern</a>, with a <a href="/wiki/Cosmopolitan_distribution" title="Cosmopolitan distribution">subcosmopolitan</a> but scattered distribution. Despite its extensive range, it is <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" title="Monotypic" class="mw-redirect">monotypic</a> of its genus, and has no <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> accepted either.<sup id="cite_ref-lynx_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lynx-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> it is also known by the Maori name <b>Taranui</b>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Caspian_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>It is the world's largest tern with a length of 48–60 cm (19–24 in), a wingspan of 127–145 cm (50–57 in) and a weight of 530–782 g (1.2-1.8&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-lynx_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lynx-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Adult birds have black legs, and a long thick red-orange bill with a small black tip. They have a white head with a black cap and white neck, belly and tail. The upper wings and back are pale grey; the underwings are pale with dark primary feathers. In flight, the tail is less forked than other terns and wing tips black on the underside.<sup id="cite_ref-lynx_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lynx-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> In winter, the black cap is still present (unlike many other terns), but with some white streaking on the forehead. The call is a loud <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">heron-like</a> croak.<sup id="cite_ref-mullarney_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mullarney-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Caspian_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>Their breeding habitat is large lakes and ocean coasts in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> (including the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>), and locally in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> (mainly around the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>), <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>). North American birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> to southern coasts, the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies" class="mw-redirect">West Indies</a> and northernmost <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. European and Asian birds spend the non-breeding season in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> tropics. African and Australasian birds are resident or disperse over short distances.<sup id="cite_ref-lynx_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lynx-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The global population is about 50,000 pairs; numbers in most regions are stable, but the Baltic Sea population (1,400–1,475 pairs in the early 1990s) is declining and of conservation concern.<sup id="cite_ref-lynx_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lynx-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Caspian Tern is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Caspian_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Caspian_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<p>They feed mainly on <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, which they dive for, hovering high over the water and then plunging. They also occasionally eat large <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, the young and eggs of other birds and rodents. They may fly up to 60&#160;km from the breeding colony to catch fish; it often fishes on freshwater lakes as well as at sea.<sup id="cite_ref-lynx_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lynx-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mullarney_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mullarney-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Caspian_Tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<p>Breeding is in spring and summer, with one to three pale blue green eggs, with heavy brown spotting, being laid. They nest either together in colonies, or singly in mixed colonies of other tern and <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a> species. The nest is on the ground among gravel and sand, or sometimes on vegetation; incubation lasts for 26–28 days. The chicks are variable in plumage pattern, from pale creamy to darker grey-brown; this variation assists adults in recognizing their own chicks when returning to the colony from feeding trips. Fledging occurs after 35–45 days.<sup id="cite_ref-lynx_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lynx-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
