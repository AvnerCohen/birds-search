<p>The <b>Eurasian hoopoe</b> (<i>Upupa epops</i>) is the most widespread species of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Upupa" class="mw-redirect" title="Upupa">Upupa</a></i>, native to Europe, Asia and the northern half of Africa. Some taxonomists still consider all three species conspecific.  Some authorities also keep the African and Eurasian hoopoe together, but split the Madagascar hoopoe.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_hoopoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nine <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of Eurasian hoopoe are recognised by Kristin (in the 2001 <i><a href="/wiki/Handbook_of_the_Birds_of_the_World" title="Handbook of the Birds of the World">Handbook of the Birds of the World</a></i>). They vary mostly in size and the depth of colour in the <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>. One further subspecies is suggested- <i>U. e. orientalis</i> in northwestern India.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_hoopoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:216px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ABUBILLA_(Upupa_epops).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/ABUBILLA_%28Upupa_epops%29.jpg/214px-ABUBILLA_%28Upupa_epops%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="214" height="300" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/ABUBILLA_%28Upupa_epops%29.jpg/321px-ABUBILLA_%28Upupa_epops%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/ABUBILLA_%28Upupa_epops%29.jpg/428px-ABUBILLA_%28Upupa_epops%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="978" data-file-height="1370" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ABUBILLA_(Upupa_epops).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>U. e. epops</i> in <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a>, Spain.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Common_Hoopoe_(Upapa_epops)_at_Hodal_I_IMG_9225.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Common_Hoopoe_%28Upapa_epops%29_at_Hodal_I_IMG_9225.jpg/220px-Common_Hoopoe_%28Upapa_epops%29_at_Hodal_I_IMG_9225.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Common_Hoopoe_%28Upapa_epops%29_at_Hodal_I_IMG_9225.jpg/330px-Common_Hoopoe_%28Upapa_epops%29_at_Hodal_I_IMG_9225.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Common_Hoopoe_%28Upapa_epops%29_at_Hodal_I_IMG_9225.jpg/440px-Common_Hoopoe_%28Upapa_epops%29_at_Hodal_I_IMG_9225.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="508" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Common_Hoopoe_(Upapa_epops)_at_Hodal_I_IMG_9225.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The muscles of the head allow the hoopoe's bill to be opened when it is inserted into the ground</div></div></div>
<p>The Eurasian hoopoe is a medium-sized bird, 25–32&#160;cm (9.8–12.6&#160;in) long, with a 44–48&#160;cm (17–19&#160;in) wingspan. It weighs 46–89&#160;g (1.6–3.1&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-Kristin_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kristin-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The species is highly distinctive, with a long, thin tapering <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> that is black with a fawn base. The strengthened <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">musculature</a> of the head allows the bill to be opened when probing inside the soil. The hoopoe has broad and rounded wings capable of strong flight; these are larger in the northern <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> subspecies. The hoopoe has a characteristic undulating <a href="/wiki/Bird_flight" title="Bird flight">flight</a>, which is like that of a giant <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterfly</a>, caused by the wings half closing at the end of each beat or short sequence of beats.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Adults may begin their moult after the breeding season and continue after they have migrated for the winter.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The call is typically a trisyllabic <i>oop-oop-oop</i>, which may give rise to its English and scientific names, although two and four syllables are also common. An alternative explanation of the English and scientific names is that they are derived from the French name for the bird, <i>huppée</i>, which means crested.  In the Himalayas, the calls can be confused with that of the <a href="/wiki/Himalayan_cuckoo" title="Himalayan cuckoo">Himalayan cuckoo</a> (<i>Cuculus saturatus</i>), although the cuckoo typically produces four notes. Other calls include rasping croaks, when alarmed, and hisses. Females produce a wheezy note during <a href="/wiki/Courtship_feeding" class="mw-redirect" title="Courtship feeding">courtship feeding</a> by the male.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_hoopoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Eurasian hoopoe is widespread in Europe, Asia, and North Africa and northern Sub-Saharan Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Most European and north Asian birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> to the tropics in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In contrast, the African populations are sedentary all year. The species has been a <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <i>U. e. saturata</i> was recorded there in 1975 in the <a href="/wiki/Yukon_Delta" class="mw-redirect" title="Yukon Delta">Yukon Delta</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Heindel_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heindel-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Hoopoes have been known to breed north of their European range,<sup id="cite_ref-Pforr_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pforr-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and in southern England during warm, dry summers that provide plenty of grasshoppers and similar insects,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> although as of the early 1980s northern European populations were reported to be in the decline, possibly due to changes in climate.<sup id="cite_ref-Pforr_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pforr-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In 2015, a record numbers of hoopoes were recorded in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, with at least 50 birds recorded in the southwest of the country.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> This was the highest recorded number since 1965 when 65 individuals were sighted.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The hoopoe has two basic requirements of its habitat: bare or lightly vegetated ground on which to forage and vertical surfaces with cavities (such as trees, cliffs or even walls, nestboxes, haystacks, and abandoned burrows<sup id="cite_ref-Pforr_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pforr-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>) in which to nest. These requirements can be provided in a wide range of ecosystems, and as a consequence the hoopoe inhabits a wide range of habitats such as heathland, wooded steppes, savannas and grasslands, as well as forest glades.
</p><p>Hunting is of concern in southern Europe and Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-Heindel_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heindel-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hoopoes make seasonal movements in response to rain in some regions such as in Ceylon and in the Western Ghats.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Birds have been seen at high altitudes during migration across the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>. One was recorded at about 6,400&#160;m (21,000&#160;ft) by the first Mount Everest expedition.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_hoopoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In what was long thought to be a defensive posture, hoopoes sunbathe by spreading out their wings and tail low against the ground and tilting their head up; they often fold their wings and preen halfway through.<sup id="cite_ref-Fry1_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fry1-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> They also enjoy taking dust and sand baths.<sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet_and_feeding">Diet and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_hoopoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_hoopoe_(Upupa_epops)_juvenile_in_nest_box.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Eurasian_hoopoe_%28Upupa_epops%29_juvenile_in_nest_box.jpg/220px-Eurasian_hoopoe_%28Upupa_epops%29_juvenile_in_nest_box.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Eurasian_hoopoe_%28Upupa_epops%29_juvenile_in_nest_box.jpg/330px-Eurasian_hoopoe_%28Upupa_epops%29_juvenile_in_nest_box.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Eurasian_hoopoe_%28Upupa_epops%29_juvenile_in_nest_box.jpg/440px-Eurasian_hoopoe_%28Upupa_epops%29_juvenile_in_nest_box.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2686" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_hoopoe_(Upupa_epops)_juvenile_in_nest_box.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>juvenile in nest box, Hungary</div></div></div>
<p>The diet of the Eurasian hoopoe is mostly composed of <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, although small <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a> and plant matter such as seeds and berries are sometimes taken as well. It is a solitary forager which typically feeds on the ground. More rarely they will feed in the air, where their strong and rounded wings make them fast and manoeuvrable, in pursuit of numerous swarming insects. More commonly their foraging style is to stride over relatively open ground and periodically pause to probe the ground with the full length of their bill. Insect larvae, pupae and mole crickets are detected by the bill and either extracted or dug out with the strong feet. Hoopoes will also feed on insects on the surface, probe into piles of leaves, and even use the bill to lever large stones and flake off bark. Common diet items include crickets, locusts, beetles, earwigs, cicadas, ant lions, bugs and ants. These can range from 10 to 150&#160;mm (<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>&#8260;<sub>8</sub></span> to <span class="frac nowrap">5<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>7</sup>&#8260;<sub>8</sub></span>&#160;in) in length, with a preferred prey size of around 20–30&#160;mm (<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span>–<span class="frac nowrap">1<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>8</sub></span>&#160;in). Larger prey items are beaten against the ground or a preferred stone to kill them and remove indigestible body parts such as wings and legs.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_hoopoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Young_and_mature_hoopoe.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Young_and_mature_hoopoe.jpg/220px-Young_and_mature_hoopoe.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Young_and_mature_hoopoe.jpg/330px-Young_and_mature_hoopoe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Young_and_mature_hoopoe.jpg/440px-Young_and_mature_hoopoe.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4608" data-file-height="3072" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Young_and_mature_hoopoe.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Young and mature hoopoe in Dubai park</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Huppe_fasci%C3%A9e_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_161_Ouzouer-sur-Tr%C3%A9z%C3%A9e.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Huppe_fasci%C3%A9e_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_161_Ouzouer-sur-Tr%C3%A9z%C3%A9e.jpg/220px-Huppe_fasci%C3%A9e_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_161_Ouzouer-sur-Tr%C3%A9z%C3%A9e.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="183" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Huppe_fasci%C3%A9e_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_161_Ouzouer-sur-Tr%C3%A9z%C3%A9e.jpg/330px-Huppe_fasci%C3%A9e_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_161_Ouzouer-sur-Tr%C3%A9z%C3%A9e.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Huppe_fasci%C3%A9e_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_161_Ouzouer-sur-Tr%C3%A9z%C3%A9e.jpg/440px-Huppe_fasci%C3%A9e_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_161_Ouzouer-sur-Tr%C3%A9z%C3%A9e.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4364" data-file-height="3627" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Huppe_fasci%C3%A9e_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_161_Ouzouer-sur-Tr%C3%A9z%C3%A9e.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hoopoe eggs <a href="/wiki/MHNT" class="mw-redirect" title="MHNT">MHNT</a></div></div></div>
<p>The hoopoe genus is <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a>, although the pair bond apparently only lasts for a single season, and <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territorial</a>. The male calls frequently to advertise his ownership of the territory. Chases and fights between rival males (and sometimes females) are common and can be brutal.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  Birds will try to stab rivals with their bills, and individuals are occasionally blinded in fights.<sup id="cite_ref-Martin_Vivaldi_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin_Vivaldi-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The nest is in a hole in a tree or wall, and has a narrow entrance.<sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It may be unlined, or various scraps may be collected.<sup id="cite_ref-Pforr_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pforr-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The female alone is responsible for <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" class="mw-redirect" title="Avian incubation">incubating</a> the eggs. <a href="/wiki/Avian_clutch_size" title="Avian clutch size">Clutch size</a> varies with location: Northern Hemisphere birds lay more eggs than those in the Southern Hemisphere, and birds at higher latitudes have larger clutches than those closer to the equator. In central and northern Europe and Asia the clutch size is around 12, whereas it is around four in the tropics and seven in the subtropics. The eggs are round and milky blue when laid, but quickly discolour in the increasingly dirty nest.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> They weigh 4.5&#160;g (0.16&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-Fry1_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fry1-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> A replacement clutch is possible.<sup id="cite_ref-Pforr_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pforr-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hoopoes have well-developed anti-predator defences in the nest. The <a href="/wiki/Uropygial_gland" title="Uropygial gland">uropygial gland</a> of the incubating and brooding female is quickly modified to produce a foul-smelling liquid, and the glands of nestlings do so as well. These secretions are rubbed into the plumage. The secretion, which smells like rotting meat, is thought to help deter predators, as well as deter parasites and possibly act as an antibacterial agent.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The secretions stop soon before the young leave the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-Fry1_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fry1-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> From the age of six days, nestlings can also direct streams of faeces at intruders, and will hiss at them in a snake-like fashion.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The young also strike with their bill or with one wing.<sup id="cite_ref-Fry1_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fry1-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation" title="Egg incubation">incubation</a> period for the species is between 15 and 18 days, during which time the male feeds the female. Incubation begins as soon as the first egg is laid, so the chicks are born asynchronously. The chicks hatch with a covering of <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">downy feathers</a>. By around day three to five, feather quills emerge which will become the adult feathers. The chicks are brooded by the female for between 9 and 14 days.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The female later joins the male in the task of bringing food.<sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The young <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> in 26 to 29 days and remain with the parents for about a week more.<sup id="cite_ref-Pforr_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pforr-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Hoopoes show hatching asynchrony of eggs which is thought to allow for <a href="/wiki/Brood_reduction" title="Brood reduction">brood reduction</a> when food availability is low.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_hoopoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hoopoe_bird_watercolour.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Hoopoe_bird_watercolour.jpg/220px-Hoopoe_bird_watercolour.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="487" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Hoopoe_bird_watercolour.jpg/330px-Hoopoe_bird_watercolour.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Hoopoe_bird_watercolour.jpg/440px-Hoopoe_bird_watercolour.jpg 2x" data-file-width="987" data-file-height="2185" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hoopoe_bird_watercolour.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Hoopoe bird was recorded as residing in Britain in the 18th Century</div></div></div>
<p>The diet of the Eurasian hoopoe includes many species considered by humans to be <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">pests</a>, such as the pupae of the <a href="/wiki/Thaumetopoea_pityocampa" class="mw-redirect" title="Thaumetopoea pityocampa">processionary moth</a>, a damaging forest pest.<sup id="cite_ref-pest_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pest-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> For this reason the species is afforded protection under the law in many countries.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hoopoes are distinctive birds and have made a cultural impact over much of their range. They were considered sacred in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a>, and were "depicted on the walls of tombs and temples". During the Old Kingdom, the hoopoe was used in the iconography as a symbolic code to indicate the child was the heir and successor of his father.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> They achieved a similar standing in <a href="/wiki/Minoan_Crete" class="mw-redirect" title="Minoan Crete">Minoan Crete</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Fry1_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fry1-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leviticus" class="mw-redirect" title="Leviticus">Leviticus</a> 11:13–19, hoopoes were listed among the animals that are detestable and should not be eaten. They are also listed in <a href="/wiki/Deuteronomy" class="mw-redirect" title="Deuteronomy">Deuteronomy</a> 14:18<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> as not <a href="/wiki/Kosher" class="mw-redirect" title="Kosher">kosher</a>.
</p><p>The hoopoe also appears in the <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a> and is known as the "<a href="/wiki/Hudhud_(mythology)" title="Hudhud (mythology)">Hudhud</a>" (<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D9%87%D8%AF%D9%87%D8%AF" class="extiw" title="wikt:هدهد">هدهد</a>), in Surah Al-Naml 27:20–22: "And he <a href="/wiki/King_Solomon" class="mw-redirect" title="King Solomon">Solomon</a> sought among the birds and said: How is it that I see not the hoopoe, or is he among the absent? (20) I verily will punish him with hard punishment or I verily will slay him, or he verily shall bring me a plain excuse. (21) But he [the hoopoe] was not long in coming, and he said: I have found out (a thing) that thou apprehendest not, and I come unto thee from <a href="/wiki/Queen_of_Sheba" title="Queen of Sheba">Sheba</a> with sure tidings."
</p><p>Hoopoes were seen as a symbol of virtue in <a href="/wiki/Persia" class="mw-redirect" title="Persia">Persia</a>. A hoopoe was a leader of the birds in the Persian book of poems <i><a href="/wiki/The_Conference_of_the_Birds" title="The Conference of the Birds">The Conference of the Birds</a></i> ("Mantiq al-Tayr" by <a href="/wiki/Attar_of_Nishapur" title="Attar of Nishapur">Attar</a>) and when the birds seek a king, the hoopoe points out that the <a href="/wiki/Simurgh" title="Simurgh">Simurgh</a> was the king of the birds.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hoopoes were thought of as thieves across much of Europe, and harbingers of war in <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonian</a> tradition, hoopoes are strongly connected with death and the <a href="/wiki/Underworld" title="Underworld">underworld</a>; their song is believed to foreshadow death for many people or cattle.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The hoopoe is the king of the birds in the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> comedy <i><a href="/wiki/The_Birds_(play)" title="The Birds (play)">The Birds</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes" title="Aristophanes">Aristophanes</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Metamorphoses" title="Metamorphoses">Metamorphoses</a></i>, book 6, King <a href="/wiki/Tereus" title="Tereus">Tereus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a> rapes <a href="/wiki/Philomela" title="Philomela">Philomela</a>, his wife <a href="/wiki/Procne" title="Procne">Procne</a>'s sister, and cuts out her tongue. In revenge, Procne kills their son Itys and serves him as a stew to his father. When Tereus sees the boy's head, which is served on a platter, he grabs a sword but just as he attempts to kill the sisters, they are turned into birds—Procne into a <a href="/wiki/Swallow" title="Swallow">swallow</a> and Philomela into a <a href="/wiki/Nightingale" class="mw-redirect" title="Nightingale">nightingale</a>. Tereus himself is turned into an <i>epops</i> (6.674), translated as <i><a href="/wiki/Lapwing" title="Lapwing">lapwing</a></i> by Dryden<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and <i>lappewincke</i> (<i>lappewinge</i>) by <a href="/wiki/John_Gower" title="John Gower">John Gower</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Confessio_Amantis" title="Confessio Amantis">Confessio Amantis</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> or <i>hoopoe</i> in A.S. Kline's translation.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The bird's crest indicates his royal status, and his long, sharp beak is a symbol of his violent nature. English translators and poets probably had the <a href="/wiki/Northern_lapwing" title="Northern lapwing">northern lapwing</a> in mind, considering its crest.
</p><p>The hoopoe was chosen as the <a href="/wiki/National_bird" class="mw-redirect" title="National bird">national bird</a> of Israel in May 2008 in conjunction with the country's 60th anniversary, following a national survey of 155,000 citizens, outpolling the <a href="/wiki/White-spectacled_bulbul" title="White-spectacled bulbul">white-spectacled bulbul</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IsraelSFChronicle_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IsraelSFChronicle-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The hoopoe appears on the Logo of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Johannesburg" title="University of Johannesburg">University of Johannesburg</a> and is the official mascot of the University's sports. The municipalities of <a href="/wiki/Armstedt" title="Armstedt">Armstedt</a> and <a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brechten" class="extiw" title="de:Brechten">Brechten</a>, Germany, have hoopoes in their coats of arms.
</p><p>In Morocco, hoopoes are traded live and as medicinal products in the markets, primarily in herbalist shops. This trade is unregulated and a potential threat to local populations <sup id="cite_ref-mago_Comm_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mago_Comm-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Three CGI enhanced hoopoes, together with other birds collectively named "the tittifers", are often shown whistling a song in the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> children's television series <i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Night_Garden..." title="In the Night Garden...">In the Night Garden...</a></i>.
</p>
