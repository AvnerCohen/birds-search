<p>The <b>Hoopoe</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span title="pronunciation:"><img alt="play" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" /></span>&#160;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/</a></span><span class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ˈ</span></a></span><span class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="'h' in 'hi'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">h</span></a></span><span class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="/uː/ long 'oo' in 'food'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">uː</span></a></span><span class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="'p' in 'pie'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">p</span></a></span><span class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="/uː/ long 'oo' in 'food'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">uː</span></a></span><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/</a></span></span>) (<i>Upupa epops</i>) is a colourful bird that is found across <a href="/wiki/Afro-Eurasia" title="Afro-Eurasia">Afro-Eurasia</a>, notable for its distinctive 'crown' of feathers. It is the only <a href="/wiki/Extant_taxon" title="Extant taxon">extant</a> species in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <i>Upupidae</i>. One insular species, the <a href="/wiki/Giant_Hoopoe" title="Giant Hoopoe">Giant Hoopoe</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Helena" title="Saint Helena">Saint Helena</a>, is extinct, and the <a href="/wiki/Madagascar_Hoopoe" title="Madagascar Hoopoe">Madagascar subspecies</a> of the Hoopoe is sometimes elevated to a full species. Like the Latin name <i>upupa</i>, the English name is an <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoetic" title="Onomatopoetic" class="mw-redirect">onomatopoetic</a> form which imitates the cry of the bird.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hoopoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The Hoopoe is classified in the <a href="/wiki/Coraciiformes" title="Coraciiformes">Coraciiformes</a> <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a>, a group that also includes <a href="/wiki/Kingfisher" title="Kingfisher">kingfishers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bee-eater" title="Bee-eater">bee-eaters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roller" title="Roller">rollers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Woodhoopoe" title="Woodhoopoe" class="mw-redirect">woodhoopoes</a> (forming a clade with this one according to Hackett <i>et al.</i> (2008)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup>). A close relationship between the Hoopoe and the woodhoopoes is also supported by the shared and unique nature of their <a href="/wiki/Stapes" title="Stapes">stapes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Sibley-Ahlquist_taxonomy" title="Sibley-Ahlquist taxonomy" class="mw-redirect">Sibley-Ahlquist taxonomy</a>, the Hoopoe is separated from the <a href="/wiki/Coraciiformes" title="Coraciiformes">Coraciiformes</a> as a separate order, the <a href="/wiki/Upupiformes" title="Upupiformes" class="mw-redirect">Upupiformes</a>. Some authorities place the woodhoopoes in the Upupiformes as well.<sup id="cite_ref-Mayr_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mayr-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> record of the hoopoes is very incomplete, with the earliest fossil coming from the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">Quaternary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-St_Helena_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-St_Helena-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The fossil record of their relatives is older, with fossil <a href="/wiki/Woodhoopoe" title="Woodhoopoe" class="mw-redirect">woodhoopoes</a> dating back to the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> and those of an extinct related family, the <a href="/wiki/Messelirrisor" title="Messelirrisor">Messelirrisoridae</a>, dating from the <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mayr_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mayr-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is the only extant member of its family, although some treatments consider some of the subspecies as separate species. Several authors have separated the Madagascan subspecies (<i>U. e. marginata</i>) as a separate species, and also the resident African form <i>U. e. africana</i>. The morphological differences between the most commonly split subspecies, <i>U. e. marginata</i>, and the other subspecies are minor, and only <i>U. e. marginata</i> has distinctly different <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">vocalisations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> One accepted separate species, the <a href="/wiki/Giant_Hoopoe" title="Giant Hoopoe">Giant Hoopoe</a>, <i>U. antaios</i>, lived on the island of <a href="/wiki/St_Helena" title="St Helena" class="mw-redirect">St Helena</a> but became extinct in the sixteenth century, presumably due to introduced species.<sup id="cite_ref-St_Helena_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-St_Helena-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The genus <i>Upupa</i> was created by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in his <i>Systema naturae</i> in 1758. It then included three other species with long curved bills:<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>U. eremita</i> (now <i>Geronticus eremita</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Bald_Ibis" title="Northern Bald Ibis">Northern Bald Ibis</a></li>
<li><i>U. pyrrhocorax</i> (now <i>Pyrrhocorax pyrrhocorax</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Red-billed_Chough" title="Red-billed Chough">Red-billed Chough</a></li>
<li><i>U. paradisea</i></li>
</ul>

<p>Nine <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of Hoopoe are recognised by the Kristin 2001 (in the <i><a href="/wiki/Handbook_of_the_Birds_of_the_World" title="Handbook of the Birds of the World">Handbook of the Birds of the World</a></i>), with these subspecies varying mostly in size and the depth of colour in the <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>. Two further subspecies have been proposed, <i>U. e. minor</i> in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> and <i>U. e. orientalis</i> in north western India.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hoopoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution, habitat and migration">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution.2C_habitat_and_migration">Distribution, habitat and migration</span></h2>
<p>The Hoopoe is widespread in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, and North <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, Sub-Saharan Africa and Madagascar.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Most European and north Asian birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> to the tropics in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> In contrast the African populations are sedentary year-round. The species has been a <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>U. e. saturata</i> was recorded as being seen there in 1975 in the <a href="/wiki/Yukon_Delta" title="Yukon Delta" class="mw-redirect">Yukon Delta</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Heindel_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heindel-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Hoopoes have been known to breed north of their European range,<sup id="cite_ref-Pforr_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pforr-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> and in southern England during warm, dry summers that provide plenty of grasshoppers and similar insects,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> although as of the early 1980s northern European populations were reported to be in the decline possibly due to changes in climate.<sup id="cite_ref-Pforr_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pforr-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Hoopoe has two basic requirements in its habitat; bare or lightly vegetated ground on which to forage and vertical surfaces with cavities (such as trees, cliffs or even walls, nestboxes, haystacks, and abandoned burrows<sup id="cite_ref-Pforr_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pforr-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup>) in which to nest. These requirements can be provided in a wide range of ecosystems and as a consequence they inhabit a wide range of habitats from heathland, wooded steppes, savannas and grasslands, as well as glades inside forests. The Madagascar subspecies also makes use of more dense primary forest. The modification of natural habitats by humans for various agricultural purposes has led to them becoming common in olive groves, orchards, vineyards, parkland and farmland, although they are less common and declining in intensively farmed areas.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Hunting is of concern in southern Europe and Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-Heindel_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heindel-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Hoopoes make seasonal movements in response to rain in some regions such as in Ceylon and in the Western Ghats.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Birds have been seen at high altitudes during migration across the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> and was recorded at about 6400 m by the first Mount Everest Expedition.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hoopoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Common_Hoopoe_(Upapa_epops)_at_Hodal_I_IMG_9225.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Common_Hoopoe_%28Upapa_epops%29_at_Hodal_I_IMG_9225.jpg/220px-Common_Hoopoe_%28Upapa_epops%29_at_Hodal_I_IMG_9225.jpg" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Common_Hoopoe_%28Upapa_epops%29_at_Hodal_I_IMG_9225.jpg/330px-Common_Hoopoe_%28Upapa_epops%29_at_Hodal_I_IMG_9225.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Common_Hoopoe_%28Upapa_epops%29_at_Hodal_I_IMG_9225.jpg/440px-Common_Hoopoe_%28Upapa_epops%29_at_Hodal_I_IMG_9225.jpg 2x" /></a>

<p>The Hoopoe is a medium sized bird, 25–32&#160;cm (9.8–12.6&#160;in) long, with a 44–48&#160;cm (17.3–19&#160;in) wingspan weighing 46–89&#160;<a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">g</a> (1.6–3.1&#160;<a href="/wiki/Ounce" title="Ounce">oz</a>). The species is highly distinctive, with a long, thin tapering <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> that is black with a fawn base. The strengthened <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">musculature</a> of the head allows the bill to be opened when probing inside the soil. The hoopoe has broad and rounded wings capable of strong flight; these are larger in the northern <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> subspecies. The Hoopoe has a characteristic undulating <a href="/wiki/Bird_flight" title="Bird flight">flight</a>, which is like that of a giant <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterfly</a>, caused by the wings half closing at the end of each beat or short sequence of beats.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The call is typically a trisyllabic <i>oop-oop-oop</i>, which gives rise to its English and scientific names, although two and four syllables are also common. In the Himalayas, the calls can be confused with that of the Himalayan Cuckoo (<i><a href="/wiki/Cuculus_saturatus" title="Cuculus saturatus" class="mw-redirect">Cuculus saturatus</a></i>) although the cuckoo typically produces four notes. Other calls include rasping croaks, when alarmed, and hisses. A wheezy note is produced by females during <a href="/wiki/Courtship_feeding" title="Courtship feeding" class="mw-redirect">courtship feeding</a> by the male.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Both genders shout by disturbance rough <i>charrrrrr</i> what strongly reminds of the warning cry of the <a href="/wiki/Jay" title="Jay">Eurasian Jay</a>. The feed begging of the nestlings is a similar to <a href="/wiki/Swift" title="Swift">Common Swift</a> <i>tiiii</i>. With excitement is to be heard now and again beak-scanty.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hoopoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sbuchner_20090712_hoopoe4.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Sbuchner_20090712_hoopoe4.jpg/220px-Sbuchner_20090712_hoopoe4.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Sbuchner_20090712_hoopoe4.jpg/330px-Sbuchner_20090712_hoopoe4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Sbuchner_20090712_hoopoe4.jpg/440px-Sbuchner_20090712_hoopoe4.jpg 2x" /></a>

<p>In what was long thought to be a defensive posture, Hoopoes sunbathe by spreading out their wings and tail low against the ground and tilting their head up; they often fold their wings and preen halfway through.<sup id="cite_ref-Fry1_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fry1-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The Hoopoe also enjoys taking dust and sand baths.<sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hoopoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Diet and feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Diet_and_feeding">Diet and feeding</span></h3>
<p>The diet of the Hoopoe is mostly composed of insects, although small reptiles and frogs as well as some plant matter such as seeds and berries are sometimes taken as well. It is a solitary forager which typically feeds on the ground. More rarely they will feed in the air, in pursuit of numerous swarming insects, where their strong and rounded wings make them fast and manoeuvrable. More commonly their foraging style is to stride on relatively open ground and periodically pause to probe the ground with the full length of their bill. Insect larvae, pupae and <a href="/wiki/Mole_cricket" title="Mole cricket">mole crickets</a> are detected by the bill and either extracted or dug out with the strong feet. In addition to feeding in soil Hoopoes will feed on insects on the surface, as well as probing into piles of leaves and even using the bill to lever large stones and flake off bark. Common diet items include <a href="/wiki/Orthoptera" title="Orthoptera">crickets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Locust" title="Locust">locusts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earwig" title="Earwig">earwigs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cicada" title="Cicada">cicadas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ant_lion" title="Ant lion" class="mw-redirect">ant lions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemiptera" title="Hemiptera">bugs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ants</a>. These can range from 10 to 150&#160;mm in length, with the preferred size of prey being around 20–30&#160;mm. Larger prey items are beaten against the ground or a preferred stone in order to kill them and remove indigestible body parts such as wings and legs.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hoopoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<p>The Hoopoe is <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a>, although the pair bond apparently only lasts for a single season. They are also <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territorial</a>, with the male calling frequently to advertise his ownership of the territory. Chases and fights between rival males (and sometimes females) are common and can be brutal.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Birds will try to stab rivals with their bills, and individuals are occasionally blinded in fights.<sup id="cite_ref-Martin_Vivaldi_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin_Vivaldi-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The nest is in a hole in a tree or wall, with a narrow entrance;<sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> it may be unlined or various scraps may be collected.<sup id="cite_ref-Pforr_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pforr-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The female alone is responsible for <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" title="Avian incubation" class="mw-redirect">incubating</a> the eggs. <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">Clutch size</a> varies with location, with northern hemisphere birds laying more eggs than those in the southern hemisphere and birds in higher latitudes having larger clutches than those closer to the equator. In central and northern Europe and Asia the clutch size is around 12, whereas it is between four in the tropics and seven in the subtropics. The eggs are round and milky blue on laying but quickly discolour in the increasingly dirty nest.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> They weigh 4.5&#160;grams.<sup id="cite_ref-Fry1_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fry1-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> A replacement clutch is possible.<sup id="cite_ref-Pforr_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pforr-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Hoopoes have well-developed anti-predator defences in the nest. The <a href="/wiki/Uropygial_gland" title="Uropygial gland">uropygial gland</a> of the incubating and brooding female is quickly modified to produce a foul-smelling liquid, and the glands of nestlings do so as well. These secretions are rubbed into the plumage. The secretion, which smells like rotting meat, is thought to help deter predators, as well as deter <a href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite" class="mw-redirect">parasites</a> and possibly act as an antibacterial agent.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> The secretions stop soon before the young leave the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-Fry1_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fry1-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition to this secretion nestlings are able to direct streams of faeces at nest intruders from the age of six days, and will also hiss at intruders in a snake like fashion.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_5-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The young also strike with their bill or with one wing.<sup id="cite_ref-Fry1_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fry1-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The incubation period for the species is between 15 and 18 days. During incubation the female is fed by the male. The incubation period begins as soon as the first egg is laid, so the chicks are born asynchronously. The chicks hatch with a covering of <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">downy feathers</a>, by around day three to five feather quills emerge which become adult feathers. The chicks are brooded by the female for between 9 to 14 days.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_5-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The female later joins the male in the task of bringing food.<sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The young <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> in 26 to 29 days and remain with the parents for about a week.<sup id="cite_ref-Pforr_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pforr-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hoopoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span></h2>

<p>The diet of the Hoopoe includes many species considered to be <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">pests</a> by humans; for example the pupae of the <a href="/wiki/Thaumetopoea_pityocampa" title="Thaumetopoea pityocampa" class="mw-redirect">processionary moth</a>, a damaging forest pest.<sup id="cite_ref-pest_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pest-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> For this reason the species is afforded protection under the law in many countries.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_5-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Hoopoes are distinctive birds and have made a cultural impact over much of their range. They were considered sacred in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a>, so they were "depicted on the walls of tombs and temples". They achieved a similar standing in <a href="/wiki/Minoan_Crete" title="Minoan Crete" class="mw-redirect">Minoan Crete</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Fry1_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fry1-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leviticus" title="Leviticus" class="mw-redirect">Leviticus</a> 11:13–19, hoopoes were listed among the animals that are detestable and should not be eaten. They are also listed in <a href="/wiki/Deuteronomy" title="Deuteronomy" class="mw-redirect">Deuteronomy</a> (14:18<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup>) as not <a href="/wiki/Kosher" title="Kosher" class="mw-redirect">kosher</a>.</p>
<p>Hoopoes also appear in the <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a> in Surah Al-Naml 27:20–22 in the following context "And he <a href="/wiki/King_Solomon" title="King Solomon" class="mw-redirect">Solomon</a> sought among the birds and said: How is it that I see not the hoopoe, or is he among the absent? (20) I verily will punish him with hard punishment or I verily will slay him, or he verily shall bring me a plain excuse. (21) But he [the Hoopoe] was not long in coming, and he said: I have found out (a thing) that thou apprehendest not, and I come unto thee from <a href="/wiki/Queen_of_Sheba" title="Queen of Sheba">Sheba</a> with sure tidings."</p>
<p>Hoopoes were seen as a symbol of virtue in <a href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia" class="mw-redirect">Persia</a>. A hoopoe was the leader of the birds in the Persian book of poems <i><a href="/wiki/The_Conference_of_the_Birds" title="The Conference of the Birds">The Conference of the Birds</a></i>.</p>
<p>They were thought of as thieves across much of Europe and harbingers of war in <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Also, in <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonian</a> tradition the Hoopoes are strongly connected with death and the <a href="/wiki/Underworld" title="Underworld">underworld</a>, their song is seen as a forebode of death for many a people or cattle.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Hoopoe is the king of the birds in the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> comedy <i><a href="/wiki/The_Birds_(play)" title="The Birds (play)">The Birds</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes" title="Aristophanes">Aristophanes</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Metamorphoses" title="Metamorphoses">Metamorphoses</a></i>, book 6, King <a href="/wiki/Tereus" title="Tereus">Tereus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a>, married to Procne, rapes his wife's sister, <a href="/wiki/Philomela_(princess_of_Athens)" title="Philomela (princess of Athens)">Philomela</a> and cuts out her tongue. In revenge, Procne kills their son Itys and serves him as a stew to his father. When Tereus sees the boy's head, which is served on a platter, he grabs a sword but just as he attempts to kill the sisters, they are turned into birds—Philomela into a <a href="/wiki/Nightingale" title="Nightingale" class="mw-redirect">nightingale</a> and Procne into a <a href="/wiki/Swallow" title="Swallow">swallow</a>. Tereus himself is turned into an <i>epops</i> (6.674), translated as <i><a href="/wiki/Lapwing" title="Lapwing">lapwing</a></i> by Dryden<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> and <i>lappewincke</i> (<i>lappewinge</i>) by <a href="/wiki/John_Gower" title="John Gower">John Gower</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Confessio_Amantis" title="Confessio Amantis">Confessio Amantis</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> or <i>hoopoe</i>, in A.S. Kline's translation.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> The bird's crest indicates his royal status and his long, sharp beak is a symbol of his violent nature. English translators and poets probably had the <a href="/wiki/Northern_lapwing" title="Northern lapwing" class="mw-redirect">Northern lapwing</a> in mind, considering its crest.</p>
<p>The Hoopoe was chosen as the <a href="/wiki/National_bird" title="National bird" class="mw-redirect">national bird</a> of <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> in May 2008 in conjunction with the country's 60th anniversary, following a national survey of 155,000 citizens, outpolling the <a href="/wiki/White-spectacled_Bulbul" title="White-spectacled Bulbul">White-spectacled Bulbul</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IsraelSFChronicle_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IsraelSFChronicle-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> It is also the state-bird of <a href="/wiki/Punjab_(India)" title="Punjab (India)" class="mw-redirect">Punjab</a> province of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. The Hoopoe appears on the Logo of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Johannesburg" title="University of Johannesburg">University of Johannesburg</a>, and is the official mascot of the University's sports. The municipality of <a href="/wiki/Armstedt" title="Armstedt">Armstedt</a>, Germany has a hoopoe in its coat of arms.</p>
