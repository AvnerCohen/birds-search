<p>The <b>Purple Swamphen</b> (<i>Porphyrio porphyrio</i>), also known as the <b>Pūkeko</b>, <b>African Purple Swamphen</b>, <b>Purple Moorhen</b>, <b>Purple Gallinule</b> or <b>Purple Coot</b>, is a large bird in the family <a href="/wiki/Rallidae" title="Rallidae">Rallidae</a> (rails). From its name in French, <i>talève sultane</i>, it is also known as the <b>Sultana Bird</b>. This <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">chicken-sized</a> bird, with its huge feet, bright plumage and red bill and frontal shield is easily-recognisable in its native range. It should not be confused with the <a href="/wiki/American_Purple_Gallinule" title="American Purple Gallinule">American Purple Gallinule</a>, <i>Porphyrio martinica</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_Swamphen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and physical description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_physical_description">Taxonomy and physical description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Purple_Swamphen_-_Pukeko02.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Purple_Swamphen_-_Pukeko02.jpg/220px-Purple_Swamphen_-_Pukeko02.jpg" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Purple_Swamphen_-_Pukeko02.jpg/330px-Purple_Swamphen_-_Pukeko02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Purple_Swamphen_-_Pukeko02.jpg/440px-Purple_Swamphen_-_Pukeko02.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Purple_Swamphen_-_Pukeko02.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>Porphyrio porphyrio melanotus</i> in Victoria, Australia</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There are 13 or more subspecies of the Purple Swamphen (depending on the authority) which differ mainly in <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> colour. The subspecies groups are: <i>P. p. porphyrio</i> in the Mediterranean, <i>P. p. madagascariensis</i> in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <i>P. p. poliocephalus</i> in tropical <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pukeko" title="Pukeko">P. p. melanotus</a></i> in much of <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a>, <i>P. p. indicus</i> in <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> and <i>P. p. pulverulentis</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>. European birds are overall purple-blue, African and south Asian birds have a green back, and Australasian and Indonesian birds have black backs and heads.</p>
<p>The nominate subspecies, <i>P. p. porphyrio</i> <small>(Linnaeus, 1758),</small> is found in Iberia, France, Sardinia and North Africa to Tunisia. <i>P. p. madagascariensis</i> <small>(Latham, 1801)</small> occurs in Egypt, sub-Saharan Africa and Madagascar, and resembles the nominate but with bronze green or green-blue back and scapulars. <i>P. p. caspius</i> <small>(Hartert, 1917)</small> from the Caspian Sea area, is like <i>poliocephalus</i>, but larger.</p>
<p><i>P. p. seistanicus</i> <small>(Zarudny &amp; Harms, 1911)</small> occurs from Iraq to Pakistan, as <i>poliocephalus</i>, but larger; smaller than <i>caspius</i>. <i>P. p. poliocephalus</i> <small>(Latham, 1801)</small> is found from India and Sri Lanka to south China and north Thailand, and has been introduced to Florida. It has cerulean blue scapulars, face throat and breast. <i>P. p. indicus</i> <small>(Horsfield, 1821)</small> occurs from Sumatra to Sulawesi and Bali, and has a large shield, black upperparts, and the side of the head is blackish. <i>P. p. virdis</i> <small>(Begbie, 1834)</small> occurs in South East Asia, and resembles <i>indicus</i> but the side of the head is cerulean blue. <i>P. p. pulverulentus</i> <small>(Temminck, 1826)</small> from the Philippines has olive-chestnut mantle and scapulars, and the whole plumage is tinged with ash-grey.</p>
<p><i>P. p. melanotus</i> <small>(Temminck, 1820)</small> occurs in north and east Australia, New Zealand and the surrounding islands. It has a small shield, shorter toes, black upperparts, and a purple throat and breast. <i>P. p. pelewensis</i> <small>(Hartlaub &amp; Finsch, 1872)</small> from Palau, resembles <i>melanotus</i> but has greener upperparts and is smaller. <i>P. p. melanopterus</i> <small>(Bonaparte, 1856)</small> is found from the Lesser Sundas and Moluccas to New Guinea. It is as <i>melanotus</i> but smaller, more variable and less blue in the upperparts. <i>P. p. bellus</i> <small>(Gould, 1820)</small> from West Australia is as <i>melanotus</i> but has a cerulean blue throat and breast. <i>P. p. samoensis</i> <small>(Peale, 1848)</small> occurs from New Guinea to New Caledonia and Samoa, and is as <i>melanotus</i> but smaller, with a brown tinge on the back.</p>
<p>Some authorities separate various subspecies as full species, for example <i>P. p. madagascariensis</i> is split by Sinclair <i>et al.</i> as <b>African Purple Swamphen</b>, <i>P. madagascariensis</i>.</p>

</div>
<p>The species is highly dispersive. Purple Swamphens are considered to be the ancestors of several island species including the <a href="/wiki/Extinct" title="Extinct" class="mw-redirect">extinct</a> <a href="/wiki/Lord_Howe_Swamphen" title="Lord Howe Swamphen">Lord Howe Swamphen</a> and two species of <a href="/wiki/Takah%C4%93" title="Takahē">Takahē</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> On islands where closely related species have become extinct or declined due to human interference, such as New Zealand or New Caledonia, this species has established itself relatively recently.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_Swamphen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<p>The species makes loud, quick, bleating and hooting calls, which are hardly bird-like in tone. It is particularly noisy during the breeding season. Despite being clumsy in flight it can fly long distances, and it is a good swimmer, especially for a bird without webbed feet.</p>

<p>The Purple Swamphens are generally seasonal breeders, but the season varies across their large range, correlating with peak rainfall in many places, or summer in more temperate climes.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The Purple Swamphen breeds in warm reed beds. The male has an elaborate courtship display, holding water weeds in his bill and bowing to the female with loud chuckles.<sup id="cite_ref-ali-daniel_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ali-daniel-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> In the western parts of the range the pattern of social behaviour tends to be <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamy</a>, but cooperative breeding groups are more common in the eastern parts of the range. These groups may consist of multiple females and males sharing a nest or a male female pair with helpers drawn from previous clutches.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Pairs nest in a large pad of interwoven reed flags, etc., on a mass of floating debris or amongst matted reeds slightly above water level in swamps, clumps of rushes in paddocks or long unkempt grass. Multiple females may lay in the one nest and share the <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" title="Avian incubation" class="mw-redirect">incubation</a> duties. Each bird can lay 3–6 speckled <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>, pale yellowish stone to reddish buff, blotched and spotted with reddish brown. A communal nest may contain up to 12 eggs. The incubation period is 23–27 days, and is performed by both sexes as well as any helpers that might be present. The <a href="/wiki/Precocial" title="Precocial">precocious</a> chicks are feathered with downy black feathers and able to leave the nest soon after hatching, but will often remain in the nest for a few days. Young chicks are fed by their parents (and group members) for between 10–14 days, after which they begin to feed themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Purple_Swamphen_(Porphyrio_porphyrio)_near_Hodal_W_IMG_6626.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Feeding in community near <a href="/wiki/Hodal" title="Hodal">Hodal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faridabad" title="Faridabad">Faridabad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haryana" title="Haryana">Haryana</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.</div>

<p>The Purple Swamphen prefers wet areas with high rainfall, swamps, lake edges and damp pastures. The birds often live in pairs and larger communities. It clambers through the reeds, eating the tender shoots and vegetable-like matter. They have been known to eat eggs, ducklings, small fish and <a href="/wiki/Invertebrates" title="Invertebrates" class="mw-redirect">invertebrates</a> such as snails. They have even been known to attack large eels, however there is no consensus amongst ornithologists if they actually eat eel. They will often use one foot to bring food to their mouth rather than eat it on the ground. Where they are not persecuted they can become tame and be readily seen in towns and cities.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_Swamphen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_Swamphen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Roman times">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_times">Roman times</span></h3>
<p>Evidence from <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> and other sources shows that the Romans kept Purple Swamphens as decorative birds at large villas and expensive houses. They were regarded as noble birds and were among the few birds that Romans did not eat.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_Swamphen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Polynesia">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Polynesia">Polynesia</span></h3>
<p>The common name in New Zealand, used for the subspecies <i><a href="/wiki/Pukeko" title="Pukeko">P. p. melanotus</a></i>, is the <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="Māori language">Māori language</a> name <b>Pūkeko</b>. The purple swamphen was held in high regard as a chiefly pet in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Samoa" title="Samoa">Samoa</a>, where it is called "manuali'i" (literally, "chiefly bird"). Red was the prized color of Polynesian aristocracy and while birds with red plumage (such as the <a href="/wiki/Red-tailed_Tropicbird" title="Red-tailed Tropicbird">Red-tailed Tropicbird</a>, some <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_honeycreeper" title="Hawaiian honeycreeper">Hawaiian honeycreepers</a> like the <a href="/wiki/%27i%27iwi" title="'i'iwi" class="mw-redirect">'i'iwi</a>' and <a href="/wiki/Maroon_Shining_Parrot" title="Maroon Shining Parrot">Maroon Shining Parrot</a>) were highly prized, the swamphen was unique in deriving its prestige not from plumage but from its reddish face, beak, and legs. In old <a href="/wiki/Samoa" title="Samoa">Samoa</a> only chiefs could keep such birds as pets, and early European sailors noticed tethered and/or caged swamphens treated by Samoan chiefs as tamed pets. Some Samoans also considered the swamphen to be the incarnation of a mischievous, aggressive demon called Vave (Corey &amp; Shirley Muse, "The Birds and Birdlore of Samoa," 1982). There is no tradition of swamphens being taken as sport game or poultry food, except perhaps in time of necessity.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_Swamphen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Escapes and introductions">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Escapes_and_introductions">Escapes and introductions</span></h3>
<p>The Purple Swamphen is occasionally recorded as an escape from captivity in Britain and elsewhere. An introduced population exists in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, though state wildlife biologists are trying to eradicate the birds.</p>
<p>The Purple Swamphen was introduced to <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> in the late 1990s due to <a href="/wiki/Aviculture" title="Aviculture">avicultural</a> escapes in the <a href="/wiki/Pembroke_Pines,_Florida" title="Pembroke Pines, Florida">Pembroke Pines, Florida</a> area. The birds multiplied and can now be found in many areas of southern <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>. Ornithological authorities consider it likely that the swamphen will become an established part of Florida's avifauna.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Florida birds are mostly or entirely of the gray-headed race <i>poliocephalus</i>, native to the area around the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>.</p>
<p>The most common call from the Florida birds is a loud, high-pitched "creek," often doubled.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_Swamphen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Status and conservation">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status_and_conservation">Status and conservation</span></h3>
<p>The species is considered to be <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> globally by the IUCN. While the species as a whole is not threatened, some subspecies have declined. In New Zealand and Australia it has expanded due to the creation of new artificial lakes and ponds, but the nominate race in the Mediterranean has declined due to habitat loss, hunting and pesticide use, and requires strict protection. In <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> the species declined greatly in the 19th and 20th centuries, but has increased more recently thanks to protection and some <a href="/wiki/Reintroduction" title="Reintroduction">reintroduction</a> schemes, although it remains rare and has a fragmented distribution.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> In Africa it varies from being common to being uncommon. The subspecies endemic to Palau has been considered endangered as well,<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> although a 2005 survey found that the subspecies, while potentially threatened, is at least now still common.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
