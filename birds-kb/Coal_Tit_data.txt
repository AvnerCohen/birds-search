<p>The <b>Coal Tit</b>, <i>Periparus ater</i>, is a <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Tit_(bird)" title="Tit (bird)">tit</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Tit_(bird)" title="Tit (bird)">Paridae</a>. It is a widespread and common resident breeder throughout <a href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate" class="mw-redirect">temperate</a> to <a href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical" class="mw-redirect">subtropical</a> <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Spot-winged_Tit" title="Spot-winged Tit" class="mw-redirect">Spot-winged Tit</a> (<i>P. melanolophus</i>) is sometimes included in this <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>; this may be correct, but more research is required to determine that species' status.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_Tit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description and systematics">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_systematics">Description and systematics</span></h2>
<p>The Coal Tit is 10–11.5&#160;cm in length, and has a distinctive large white nape spot on its black head. The head, throat and neck of the adult are glossy blue-black, setting off the off-white sides of the face (tinged grey to yellow depending on <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>) and the brilliant white nape; the white tips of the <a href="/wiki/Wing_covert" title="Wing covert" class="mw-redirect">wing coverts</a> appear as two wingbars. The underparts are whitish shading through buff to rufous on the flanks. The bill is black, the legs lead-coloured, and <a href="/wiki/Iris_(eye)" title="Iris (eye)" class="mw-redirect">irides</a> dark brown.</p>
<p>The young birds are duller than the adults, lacking gloss on the black head, and with the white of nape and cheeks tinged with yellow.</p>
<p>While searching for food, Coal Tit flocks keep contact with incessant short <i>dee</i> or <i>see-see</i> calls. The species' song – if "song" it can be called – is a strident <i>if-he, if-he, if-he</i>, heard most frequently from January to June, but also in autumn. One variant of this song ends with a sharp <i>ichi</i>. North African birds also have a <i>currr</i> call similar to that of the <a href="/wiki/European_Crested_Tit" title="European Crested Tit">European Crested Tit</a> (<i>Lophophanes cristatus</i>) which is not found in Africa.</p>

<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_Tit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Periparus_ater_I.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Periparus_ater_I.jpg/220px-Periparus_ater_I.jpg" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Periparus_ater_I.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>A number of Coal Tit <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are distinguished. The differences in coloration are quite pronounced in some of them, while their differences in size are more subtle. Coal Tits from <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> follow <a href="/wiki/Bergmann%27s_Rule" title="Bergmann's Rule" class="mw-redirect">Bergmann's Rule</a>, being larger in colder regions; those from further west, however, do not, as the birds from the uplands around the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a> are larger than those from northern Europe. Across its range, tail length in relation to body length increases along a <a href="/wiki/Cline_(biology)" title="Cline (biology)">cline</a> running from southwest to northeast.<sup id="cite_ref-snow1954_0-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow1954-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">British</a> race <i>P. a. britannicus</i> has an <a href="/wiki/Olive_(colour)" title="Olive (colour)" class="mw-redirect">olive</a> hue to its brownish-grey back plumage, distinguishing it from the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">continental European</a> <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" title="Nominate subspecies" class="mw-redirect">nominate subspecies</a> <i>P. a. ater</i> and <i>P. a. abietum</i><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material in the vicinity of this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s) from May 2009">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> in which the back is bluish grey without a hint of green or brown. The Irish race <i>P. a. hibernicus</i> is distinguished from <i>britannicus</i> by the pale <a href="/wiki/Sulphur" title="Sulphur" class="mw-redirect">sulphur</a>-yellow cheeks, breast and belly. It also has a paler rump (due to light fringes of the <a href="/wiki/Uppertail_covert" title="Uppertail covert" class="mw-redirect">uppertail coverts</a>) and a larger bill than its relatives from Britain and the Continent.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North African</a> race <i>P. a. ledouci</i> has yellow underparts and cheeks, and the <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cypriot</a> <i>P. a. cypriotes</i> has a buff tinge to its upperparts, and deep buff underparts. <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> subspecies are generally rather dusky brownish except for the black-and-white head;<sup id="cite_ref-snow1954_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow1954-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> they include among others <i>P. a. michalowskii</i> of the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>, <i>P. a. phaeonotus</i> of <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, or the Himalayan Coal Tit<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>P. a. aemodius</i> of southwestern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_Tit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h3>
<p>This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in his 1758 edition of <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_naturae" title="Systema naturae" class="mw-redirect">Systema naturae</a></i>. Linnaeus' primary reference was his earlier <i>Fauna Svecica</i>, whose cumbersome pre-<a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial</a> name <i>Parus capite nigro: vertice albo, dorso cinereo, pectore albo</i> ("black-headed tit with white nape, ash-grey back, white breast") became the much simpler yet no less unequivocal <i>Parus ater</i>. This name – meaning "dusky-black tit" – was simply adopted from older <a href="/wiki/Ornithological" title="Ornithological" class="mw-redirect">ornithological</a> textbooks, ultimately going back to <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Gessner" title="Conrad Gessner">Conrad Gessner</a>'s 1555 <i><a href="/wiki/Historiae_animalium" title="Historiae animalium" class="mw-redirect">Historiae animalium</a></i>. Linnaeus' description was essentially the slightly rephrased species name from <i>Fauna Svecica</i>: <i>P[arus] capite nigro, dorso cinereo, occipite pectoreque albo.</i> – "a black-headed tit, with ash-grey back, and white back of the head and breast." He gave no <a href="/wiki/Type_locality_(biology)" title="Type locality (biology)" class="mw-redirect">type locality</a> except "Europe", but his original description refers to the population inhabiting <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> (which is consequently included in the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" title="Nominate subspecies" class="mw-redirect">nominate subspecies</a> today).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

The colorful <a href="/wiki/Great_Tit" title="Great Tit">Great Tit</a> (<i>Parus major</i>) with its bold wing-stripe. Before <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial nomenclature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naturalist" title="Naturalist" class="mw-redirect">naturalists</a> found the <a href="/wiki/Folk_taxonomy" title="Folk taxonomy">folk taxonomy</a> of this species and the Coal Tit quite confusing.</div>

<p>Gessner also notes that the Coal Tit was known as <i>Kohlmeiß</i> in <a href="/wiki/German_(language)" title="German (language)" class="mw-redirect">German</a> – the literal equivalent of its <a href="/wiki/English_(language)" title="English (language)" class="mw-redirect">English</a> name, though in its modern <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a> <i>Kohlmeise</i> it refers to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Tit" title="Great Tit">Great Tit</a> (<i>Parus major</i>). That bird was in Gessner's day usually called <i>Spiegelmeiß</i> ("multicolored tit"<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup>), <i>Brandtmeiß</i> ("burnt tit") or <i>grosse Meiß</i> ("great tit") in German. <i>Kölmeyß</i> was attested for <i>P. major</i> by <a href="/wiki/William_Turner_(ornithologist)" title="William Turner (ornithologist)" class="mw-redirect">William Turner</a>, but Turner does not list <i>P. ater</i> at all, while Gessner notes that his hunters always used <i>Kohlmeiß</i> for the present species. However, this has since changed, and the modern German name of <i>P. ater</i> is <i>Tannenmeise</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Fir" title="Fir">fir</a> tit"), after a typical <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a>. This name is attested (as <i>Tannen-Maise</i>) by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Leonhard_Frisch" title="Johann Leonhard Frisch">Johann Leonhard Frisch</a> in the early 18th century already, who furthermore records that <i>P. ater</i> was also called <i>Kleine Kohl-Maise</i> ("small coal tit") whereas <i>Kohl-Maise</i> referred unequivocally to <i>P. major</i>. Frisch collected his data in the <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> region, where the German dialect was <a href="/wiki/Benrath_line" title="Benrath line">quite different</a> from that spoken by Gessner's <a href="/wiki/Alemannic_German" title="Alemannic German">Alemannic</a> sources 200 years earlier, and heavily influenced by <a href="/wiki/Middle_Low_German" title="Middle Low German">Middle Low German</a> – the language of the northern German sources of Turner. Regarding that, <i>Tanne</i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Saxon" title="Old Saxon">Old Saxon</a> <i>danna</i>, and thus had spread through the German <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">dialect continuum</a> from north to south.&#160;<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Most authorities still treat the Coal Tit in the <a href="/wiki/Subgenus" title="Subgenus">subgenus</a> <i>Periparus</i>, but the <a href="/wiki/American_Ornithologists%27_Union" title="American Ornithologists' Union">American Ornithologists' Union</a> considers <i>Periparus</i> a distinct <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a>. This is supported by <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA" class="mw-redirect">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome <i>b</i></a> <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">sequence</a> analysis; <i>Periparus</i> seems to be closer to the <i><a href="/wiki/Poecile" title="Poecile">Poecile</a></i> tits and chickadees than to the Great Tit and its relatives. Thus, it belongs to the more advanced <a href="/wiki/Paridae" title="Paridae" class="mw-redirect">Paridae</a>, in which the bright plumage of the more <a href="/wiki/Basal_(evolution)" title="Basal (evolution)" class="mw-redirect">basal</a> lineages is dulled down <a href="/wiki/Apomorph" title="Apomorph" class="mw-redirect">apomorphically</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gilletal2005_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gilletal2005-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>In addition, the same data suggest that this species is <a href="/wiki/Paraphyletic" title="Paraphyletic" class="mw-redirect">paraphyletic</a> in regard to the closely related and <a href="/wiki/Parapatric" title="Parapatric" class="mw-redirect">parapatric</a> <a href="/wiki/Spot-winged_Tit" title="Spot-winged Tit" class="mw-redirect">Spot-winged Tit</a> (<i>P. melanolophus</i>) from <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>, which looks like a slightly crested, darker version of <i>P. ater</i>. Consequently, the Spot-winged Tit might have to be included in <i>P. ater</i>, or some Coal Tits could be considered a distinct species. As occasional <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridization</a> has been recorded between the two, mtDNA alone (which is inherited only from the mother) is insufficient to determine whether hybrid <a href="/wiki/Gene_flow" title="Gene flow">gene flow</a> or another trivial cause (such as incomplete <a href="/wiki/Lineage_sorting" title="Lineage sorting" class="mw-redirect">lineage sorting</a>) obfuscates the actual relationships, or whether <a href="/wiki/Taxonomic" title="Taxonomic" class="mw-redirect">taxonomic</a> rearrangement is indeed required. With the range of these titmice encircling the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>, without further study it cannot even be excluded that they constitute a <a href="/wiki/Ring_species" title="Ring species">ring species</a> – with gene flow occurring in <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a> but not in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> –, as has been shown for other <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerines</a> in the same region.<sup id="cite_ref-gilletal2005_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gilletal2005-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_Tit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span></h2>
<p>It is typically a bird of <a href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate" class="mw-redirect">temperate</a> <a href="/wiki/Humid" title="Humid" class="mw-redirect">humid</a> <a href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer" class="mw-redirect">conifer</a> <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a>, but apart from that shows little <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> specificity. In <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a> for example Coal Tits are fairly common residents above the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical" class="mw-redirect">subtropical</a> zone, at about 3,000-3,800 m <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" title="Above mean sea level">ASL</a>, and are found in forests dominated by <a href="/wiki/Bhutan_Fir" title="Bhutan Fir" class="mw-redirect">Bhutan Fir</a> (<i>Abies densa</i>) as well as in those characterized by <a href="/wiki/Himalayan_Hemlock" title="Himalayan Hemlock" class="mw-redirect">Himalayan Hemlock</a> (<i>Tsuga dumosa</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Rhododendron" title="Rhododendron">rhododendrons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Coal Tit is an all-year resident throughout almost all range, making only local movements in response to particularly severe weather; only the <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberian</a> birds have a more regular <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migration</a>. Very rarely, vagrants may cross longer distances; for example the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" title="Nominate subspecies" class="mw-redirect">nominate subspecies</a> of <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">continental Europe</a> was recorded in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> once in 1960 and once before that, but apparently not since then.</p>
<p>Coal Tits will form small flocks in winter with other tits. This species resembles other tits in acrobatic skill and restless activity, though it more frequently pitches on a trunk, and in little hops resembles a <a href="/wiki/Treecreeper" title="Treecreeper">treecreeper</a> (<i>Certhia</i>). Its food is similar to that of the others; it is keen on <a href="/wiki/Beechmast" title="Beechmast" class="mw-redirect">beechmast</a>, picks out the seeds from <a href="/wiki/Fir" title="Fir">fir</a> (<i>Abies</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Larch" title="Larch">larch</a> (<i>Larix</i>) cones, and joins <i><a href="/wiki/Carduelis" title="Carduelis">Carduelis</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Redpoll" title="Redpoll">redpolls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siskin" title="Siskin">siskins</a> in <a href="/wiki/Alder" title="Alder">alders</a> (<i>Alnus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birches</a> (<i>Betula</i>). It will also visit <a href="/wiki/Garden" title="Garden">gardens</a> to feed on a variety of foods put out, particularly <a href="/wiki/Sunflower_seed" title="Sunflower seed">sunflower seeds</a>.</p>
<p>A favourite nesting site is a hole in a rotting tree-stump, often low down, and the nest is deep within the hole; holes in the ground, burrows of <a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">mice</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a>, chinks between the stones in walls, old nests of <i><a href="/wiki/Pica_(genus)" title="Pica (genus)">Pica</a></i> magpies or other large birds, and <a href="/wiki/Squirrel" title="Squirrel">squirrel</a> dreys are also occupied. The materials, moss, hair and grass, are closely felted together, and rabbit fur or feathers added for lining. Seven to eleven red-spotted white <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> of the usual tit type are laid, usually in May; this species breeds usually once per year.</p>
<p>Being common and widespread, the Coal Tit is not considered a <a href="/wiki/Threatened_species" title="Threatened species">threatened species</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN" class="mw-redirect">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
