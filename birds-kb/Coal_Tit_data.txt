<p>The <b>coal tit</b> or <b>cole tit</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <i>Periparus ater</i>, is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Tit_(bird)" title="Tit (bird)">tit</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a>, Paridae. It is a widespread and common resident breeder in forests throughout the <a href="/wiki/Temperate" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate">temperate</a> to <a href="/wiki/Subtropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Subtropical">subtropical</a> <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>, including <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Black-crested_tit" title="Black-crested tit">black-crested tit</a> is now usually included in this <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_tit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his landmark 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a>. Linnaeus' primary reference was his earlier <i>Fauna Svecica</i>, whose cumbersome pre-<a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial</a> name <i>Parus capite nigro: vertice albo, dorso cinereo, pectore albo</i> ("black-headed tit with white nape, ash-grey back, white breast") became the much simpler yet no less unequivocal <i>Parus ater</i>. This name – meaning "dusky-black tit" – was simply adopted from older <a href="/wiki/Ornithological" class="mw-redirect" title="Ornithological">ornithological</a> textbooks, ultimately going back to <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Gessner" title="Conrad Gessner">Conrad Gessner</a>'s 1555 <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_animalium_(Gessner_book)" title="Historia animalium (Gessner book)">Historia animalium</a></i>. He gave no <a href="/wiki/Type_locality_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Type locality (biology)">type locality</a> except "Europe", but his original description refers to the population inhabiting <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> (which is consequently included in the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> today).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The current genus name, is <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>peri</i> plus the pre-existing genus <i><a href="/wiki/Parus" title="Parus">Parus</a></i>. The specific <i>ater</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "dull black".<sup id="cite_ref-job_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Great-Tit.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Great-Tit.jpg/220px-Great-Tit.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="172" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Great-Tit.jpg/330px-Great-Tit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Great-Tit.jpg/440px-Great-Tit.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2021" data-file-height="1581" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Great-Tit.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The colourful <a href="/wiki/Great_tit" title="Great tit">great tit</a> (<i>Parus major</i>) with its bold wing-stripe. Before <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial nomenclature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naturalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Naturalist">naturalists</a> found the <a href="/wiki/Folk_taxonomy" title="Folk taxonomy">folk taxonomy</a> of this species and the coal tit quite confusing.</div></div></div>
<p>Gessner also notes that the coal tit was known as <i>Kohlmeiß</i> in <a href="/wiki/German_(language)" class="mw-redirect" title="German (language)">German</a> – the literal equivalent of its <a href="/wiki/English_(language)" class="mw-redirect" title="English (language)">English</a> name, though in its modern <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a> <i>Kohlmeise</i> it refers to the <a href="/wiki/Great_tit" title="Great tit">great tit</a> (<i>Parus major</i>). That bird was in Gessner's day usually called <i>Spiegelmeiß</i> ("multicoloured tit"<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>), <i>Brandtmeiß</i> ("burnt tit") or <i>grosse Meiß</i> ("great tit") in German. <i>Kölmeyß</i> was attested for <i>P. major</i> by <a href="/wiki/William_Turner_(ornithologist)" class="mw-redirect" title="William Turner (ornithologist)">William Turner</a>, but Turner does not list <i>P. ater</i> at all, while Gessner notes that his hunters always used <i>Kohlmeiß</i> for the present species. However, this has since changed, and the modern German name of <i>P. ater</i> is <i>Tannenmeise</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Fir" title="Fir">fir</a> tit"), after a typical <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a>. This name is attested (as <i>Tannen-Maise</i>) by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Leonhard_Frisch" title="Johann Leonhard Frisch">Johann Leonhard Frisch</a> in the early 18th century already, who furthermore records that <i>P. ater</i> was also called <i>Kleine Kohl-Maise</i> ("small coal tit") whereas <i>Kohl-Maise</i> referred unequivocally to <i>P. major</i>. Frisch collected his data in the <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> region, where the German dialect was <a href="/wiki/Benrath_line" title="Benrath line">quite different</a> from that spoken by Gessner's <a href="/wiki/Alemannic_German" title="Alemannic German">Alemannic</a> sources 200 years earlier, and heavily influenced by <a href="/wiki/Middle_Low_German" title="Middle Low German">Middle Low German</a> – the language of the northern German sources of Turner. Regarding that, <i>Tanne</i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Saxon" title="Old Saxon">Old Saxon</a> <i>danna</i>, and thus had spread through the German <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">dialect continuum</a> from north to south.&#160;<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><span class="cleanup-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">Most authorities still treat the coal tit in the <a href="/wiki/Subgenus" title="Subgenus">subgenus</a> <i>Periparus</i>, but the <a href="/wiki/American_Ornithologists%27_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="American Ornithologists&#39; Union">American Ornithologists' Union</a> considers <i>Periparus</i> a distinct <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a>. This is supported by <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" class="mw-redirect" title="MtDNA">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome <i>b</i></a> <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" class="mw-redirect" title="DNA sequence">sequence</a> analysis; <i>Periparus</i> seems to be closer to the <i><a href="/wiki/Poecile" title="Poecile">Poecile</a></i> tits and chickadees than to the great tit and its relatives. Thus, it belongs to the more advanced <a href="/wiki/Paridae" class="mw-redirect" title="Paridae">Paridae</a>, in which the bright plumage of the more <a href="/wiki/Basal_(evolution)" class="mw-redirect" title="Basal (evolution)">basal</a> lineages is dulled down <a href="/wiki/Apomorph" class="mw-redirect" title="Apomorph">apomorphically</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gilletal2005_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gilletal2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="This paragraph is outdated - Periparus is now widely recognized as a separate genus from Parus. More recent and more comprehensive phylogenetic studies (and other relevant sources incorporating information from them) should be cited here, e.g. Johansson et al. 2013 (Mol. Phylogenet. Evol. 69(3):852-860). The same probably goes for the following paragraph. (October 2020)">needs update</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ParusCypriotesKeulemans.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/ParusCypriotesKeulemans.jpg/220px-ParusCypriotesKeulemans.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="339" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/ParusCypriotesKeulemans.jpg/330px-ParusCypriotesKeulemans.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/ParusCypriotesKeulemans.jpg/440px-ParusCypriotesKeulemans.jpg 2x" data-file-width="932" data-file-height="1437" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ParusCypriotesKeulemans.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration of <i>Parus ater cypriotes</i> by <a href="/wiki/John_Gerrard_Keulemans" title="John Gerrard Keulemans">John Gerrard Keulemans</a></div></div></div>
<p>In addition, the same data suggest that this species is <a href="/wiki/Paraphyletic" class="mw-redirect" title="Paraphyletic">paraphyletic</a> in regard to the closely related and <a href="/wiki/Parapatric" class="mw-redirect" title="Parapatric">parapatric</a> <a href="/wiki/Spot-winged_tit" class="mw-redirect" title="Spot-winged tit">spot-winged tit</a> (<i>P. melanolophus</i>) from <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>, which looks like a slightly crested, darker version of <i>P. ater</i>. Consequently, the spot-winged tit might have to be included in <i>P. ater</i>, or some coal tits could be considered a distinct species. As occasional <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridization</a> has been recorded between the two, mtDNA alone (which is inherited only from the mother) is insufficient to determine whether hybrid <a href="/wiki/Gene_flow" title="Gene flow">gene flow</a> or another trivial cause (such as <a href="/wiki/Incomplete_lineage_sorting" title="Incomplete lineage sorting">incomplete lineage sorting</a>) obfuscates the actual relationships, or whether <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">taxonomic</a> rearrangement is indeed required. With the range of these titmice encircling the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>, without further study it cannot even be excluded that they constitute a <a href="/wiki/Ring_species" title="Ring species">ring species</a> – with gene flow occurring in <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a> but not in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> – as has been shown for other <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerines</a> in the same region.<sup id="cite_ref-gilletal2005_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gilletal2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="Additional studies relevant to coal/black-crested tit species limits have been published in the past decade and a half and should probably be cited here - Tietze et al. 2011 (J. Avian Biol. 42(3): 214–230) and Martens et al. 2011 (Orn. Monogr. 70: 64–94), and maybe Martens et al. 2006 (Zool Abh. 55: 103–120). (October 2020)">needs update</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_tit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Periparus_ater_I.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Periparus_ater_I.jpg/220px-Periparus_ater_I.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Periparus_ater_I.jpg/330px-Periparus_ater_I.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Periparus_ater_I.jpg/440px-Periparus_ater_I.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1112" data-file-height="780" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Periparus_ater_I.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult continental coal tit, <i>P. a. ater</i> (note blue-grey back)</div></div></div>
<p>A number of coal tit <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are distinguished. The differences in colouration are quite pronounced in some of them, while their differences in size are more subtle. Coal tits from Asia follow <a href="/wiki/Bergmann%27s_rule" title="Bergmann&#39;s rule">Bergmann's rule</a>, being larger in colder regions; those from further west, however, do not, as the birds from the uplands around the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> are larger than those from northern Europe. Across its range, tail length in relation to body length increases along a <a href="/wiki/Cline_(biology)" title="Cline (biology)">cline</a> running from southwest to northeast.<sup id="cite_ref-snow1954_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow1954-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">British</a> race <i>P. a. britannicus</i> has an <a href="/wiki/Olive_(colour)" class="mw-redirect" title="Olive (colour)">olive</a> hue to its brownish-grey back plumage, distinguishing it from the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">continental European</a> <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> <i>P. a. ater</i> and <i>P. a. abietum</i><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (May 2009)">verification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> in which the back is bluish grey without a hint of green or brown. The Irish race <i>P. a. hibernicus</i> is distinguished from <i>britannicus</i> by the pale <a href="/wiki/Sulphur" class="mw-redirect" title="Sulphur">sulphur</a>-yellow cheeks, breast and belly. It also has a paler rump (due to light fringes of the <a href="/wiki/Uppertail_covert" class="mw-redirect" title="Uppertail covert">uppertail coverts</a>) and a larger bill than its relatives from Britain and the Continent.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North African</a> race <i>P. a. ledouci</i> has yellow underparts and cheeks, and the <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cypriot</a> <i>P. a. cypriotes</i> has a buff tinge to its upper parts, and deep buff underparts. Asian subspecies are generally rather dusky brownish except for the black-and-white head;<sup id="cite_ref-snow1954_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow1954-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> they include among others <i>P. a. michalowskii</i> of the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>, <i>P. a. phaeonotus</i> of <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, or the Himalayan coal tit<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <i>P. a. aemodius</i> of southwestern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.
</p>

<p>The coal tit is 10–11.5&#160;cm in length, and has a distinctive large white nape spot on its black head. The head, throat and neck of the adult are glossy blue-black, setting off the off-white sides of the face (tinged grey to yellow depending on <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>) and the brilliant white nape; the white tips of the <a href="/wiki/Wing_covert" class="mw-redirect" title="Wing covert">wing coverts</a> appear as two wingbars. The underparts are whitish shading through buff to rufous on the flanks. The bill is black, the legs lead-coloured, and <a href="/wiki/Iris_(eye)" class="mw-redirect" title="Iris (eye)">irides</a> dark brown.
</p><p>The young birds are duller than the adults, lacking gloss on the black head, and with the white of nape and cheeks tinged with yellow.
</p><p>While searching for food, coal tit flocks keep contact with incessant short <i>dee</i> or <i>see-see</i> calls. The species' song – if "song" it can be called – is a strident <i>if-he, if-he, if-he</i>, heard most frequently from January to June, but also in autumn. The song resembles that of the great tit, but much faster and higher in pitch.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> One variant of this song ends with a sharp <i>ichi</i>. North African birds also have a <i>currr</i> call similar to that of the <a href="/wiki/European_crested_tit" title="European crested tit">European crested tit</a> (<i>Lophophanes cristatus</i>) which is not found in Africa.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_tit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Parus_ater_MWNH_2286.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Parus_ater_MWNH_2286.JPG/220px-Parus_ater_MWNH_2286.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Parus_ater_MWNH_2286.JPG/330px-Parus_ater_MWNH_2286.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Parus_ater_MWNH_2286.JPG/440px-Parus_ater_MWNH_2286.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2932" data-file-height="2504" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Parus_ater_MWNH_2286.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>It is typically a bird of <a href="/wiki/Temperate" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate">temperate</a> <a href="/wiki/Humid" class="mw-redirect" title="Humid">humid</a> <a href="/wiki/Conifer" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifer">conifer</a> <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a>, but apart from that shows little <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> specificity. In <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a> for example coal tits are fairly common residents above the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Subtropical">subtropical</a> zone, at about 3,000–3,800&#160;m <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Above mean sea level">ASL</a>, and are found in forests dominated by <a href="/wiki/Bhutan_fir" class="mw-redirect" title="Bhutan fir">Bhutan fir</a> (<i>Abies densa</i>) as well as in those characterized by <a href="/wiki/Himalayan_hemlock" class="mw-redirect" title="Himalayan hemlock">Himalayan hemlock</a> (<i>Tsuga dumosa</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Rhododendron" title="Rhododendron">rhododendrons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The coal tit is an all-year resident throughout almost all range, making only local movements in response to particularly severe weather; only the <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberian</a> birds have a more regular <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migration</a>. Very rarely, vagrants may cross longer distances; for example the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> of <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">continental Europe</a> was recorded in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> once in 1960 and once before that, but apparently not since then.
</p><p>Coal tits will form small flocks in winter with other tits. This species resembles other tits in acrobatic skill and restless activity, though it more frequently pitches on a trunk, and in little hops resembles a <a href="/wiki/Treecreeper" title="Treecreeper">treecreeper</a> (<i>Certhia</i>). Its food is similar to that of the others; it is keen on <a href="/wiki/Beechmast" class="mw-redirect" title="Beechmast">beechmast</a>, picks out the seeds from <a href="/wiki/Fir" title="Fir">fir</a> (<i>Abies</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Larch" title="Larch">larch</a> (<i>Larix</i>) cones, and joins <i><a href="/wiki/Carduelis" title="Carduelis">Carduelis</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Redpoll" title="Redpoll">redpolls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siskin" title="Siskin">siskins</a> in <a href="/wiki/Alder" title="Alder">alders</a> (<i>Alnus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birches</a> (<i>Betula</i>). It will also visit <a href="/wiki/Garden" title="Garden">gardens</a> to feed on a variety of foods put out, particularly <a href="/wiki/Sunflower_seed" title="Sunflower seed">sunflower seeds</a>.
</p><p>Coal tits in the laboratory prefer to forage at a variable feeding site when they are in a negative energy budget.<sup id="cite_ref-Bautista2001_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bautista2001-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> They increase evening body mass in response to tawny owl calls.<sup id="cite_ref-Bautista2000_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bautista2000-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> 
After dawn the coal tits increases body mass as soon as possible if food is obtained at a low rate, increasing body mass exponentially until an inflection point when the increase of body mass is slower.<sup id="cite_ref-Polo2006b_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Polo2006b-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The inflection point of the body mass trajectory is 16.7% delayed compared to a high food availability.<sup id="cite_ref-Polo2006b_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Polo2006b-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Subordinate coal tits are excluded from feeding sites by dominants more often in the early morning than in the rest of the day, and they showed more variability in daily mass gain and body mass at dawn than dominant coal tits.<sup id="cite_ref-Polo2002_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Polo2002-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Being common and widespread, the coal tit is not considered a <a href="/wiki/Threatened_species" title="Threatened species">threatened species</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The coal tit has the dubious distinction of having the largest number of bird fleas (<i><a href="/wiki/Ceratophyllus_gallinae" title="Ceratophyllus gallinae">Ceratophyllus gallinae</a></i>) reported from a single nest, 5,754 fleas.<sup id="cite_ref-Brinck_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brinck-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_tit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A favourite nesting site is a hole in a rotting tree-stump, often low down, and the nest is deep within the hole; holes in the ground, burrows of <a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">mice</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a>, chinks between the stones in walls, old nests of <i><a href="/wiki/Pica_(genus)" title="Pica (genus)">Pica</a></i> magpies or other large birds, and <a href="/wiki/Squirrel" title="Squirrel">squirrel</a> dreys are also occupied. The materials, moss, hair and grass, are closely felted together, and rabbit fur or feathers added for lining. Seven to eleven red-spotted white <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> are laid, usually in May; this species breeds usually once per year.
</p>
