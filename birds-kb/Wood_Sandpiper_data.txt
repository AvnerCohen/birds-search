<p>The <b>wood sandpiper</b> (<i>Tringa glareola</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a>. This Eurasian <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> is the smallest of the <a href="/wiki/Tringa" title="Tringa">shanks</a>, which are mid-sized long-legged waders of the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Scolopacidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Scolopacidae">Scolopacidae</a>. The genus name <i>Tringa</i> is the <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">New Latin</a> name given to the <a href="/wiki/Green_sandpiper" title="Green sandpiper">green sandpiper</a> by <a href="/wiki/Ulisse_Aldrovandi" title="Ulisse Aldrovandi">Aldrovandus</a> in 1599 based on <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>trungas</i>, a thrush-sized, white-rumped, tail-bobbing wading bird mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>. The specific <i> glareola</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>glarea</i>, " gravel".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_systematics">Description and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wood_sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It resembles a longer-legged and more delicate <a href="/wiki/Green_sandpiper" title="Green sandpiper">green</a> (<i>T. ochropus</i>) or <a href="/wiki/Solitary_sandpiper" title="Solitary sandpiper">solitary sandpiper</a> (<i>T. solitaria</i>) with a short fine <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a>, brown back and longer yellowish legs. It differs from the first of those species in a smaller and less contrasting white rump patch, while the solitary sandpiper has no white rump patch at all.<sup id="cite_ref-haymanetal1986_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-haymanetal1986-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, it is not very closely related to these two species. Rather, its closest relative is the <a href="/wiki/Common_redshank" title="Common redshank">common redshank</a> (<i>T. totanus</i>), and these two share a <a href="/wiki/Sister_taxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Sister taxon">sister relationship</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Marsh_sandpiper" title="Marsh sandpiper">marsh sandpiper</a> (<i>T. stagnatilis</i>). These three species are a group of smallish shanks with red or yellowish legs, a breeding <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> that is generally subdued light brown above with some darker mottling and with a pattern of somewhat diffuse small brownish spots on the breast and neck.<sup id="cite_ref-haymanetal1986_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-haymanetal1986-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pereira2005_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pereira2005-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wood_sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The wood sandpiper breeds in <a href="/wiki/Subarctic" title="Subarctic">subarctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Wetlands" class="mw-redirect" title="Wetlands">wetlands</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Scottish Highlands</a> across Europe and then east across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>. They <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> to Africa, Southern Asia, particularly India, and Australia. <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">Vagrant</a> birds have been seen as far into the Pacific as the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian Islands</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Micronesia" title="Micronesia">Micronesia</a> it is a regular visitor to the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Islands" title="Mariana Islands">Mariana Islands</a> (where flocks of up to 32 birds are reported) and <a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">Palau</a>; it is recorded on <a href="/wiki/Kwajalein" class="mw-redirect" title="Kwajalein">Kwajalein</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Islands" title="Marshall Islands">Marshall Islands</a> about once per decade. This species is encountered in the western Pacific region between mid-October and mid-May.<sup id="cite_ref-haymanetal1986_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-haymanetal1986-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wiles2000_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiles2000-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-VanderWerf2006_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VanderWerf2006-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-VanderWerfetal2006_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VanderWerfetal2006-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> A slight westward expansion saw the establishment of a small but permanent breeding population in Scotland since the 1950s.
</p><p>This bird is usually found on <a href="/wiki/Freshwater" class="mw-redirect" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> during migration and wintering. They forage by probing in shallow water or on wet mud, and mainly eat <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and similar small prey. <i>T. glareola</i> nests on the ground or uses an abandoned old tree nest of another <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Fieldfare" title="Fieldfare">fieldfare</a> (<i>Turdus pilaris</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-haymanetal1986_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-haymanetal1986-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Four pale green eggs are laid between March and May.
</p><p>Adult wood sandpipers moult all their primary feathers between August and December, whilst immature birds moult varying number of outer primaries between December and April, much closer to their departure from Africa.  Immatures are also much more flexible than adults in the timing and rate of their moult and refueling. Adults and immatures which accumulate fuel loads of c.50% of their lean body mass can potentially cross distances of 2397–4490&#160;km in one  non-stop flight.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The wood sandpiper is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p><p>Widespread, it is considered a Species of <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BLI_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BLI-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><br />
</p>
