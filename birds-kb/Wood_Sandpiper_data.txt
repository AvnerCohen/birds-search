<p>The <b>Wood Sandpiper</b> (<i>Tringa glareola</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasian</a> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> is the smallest of the <a href="/wiki/Tringa" title="Tringa">shanks</a>, which are mid-sized long-legged waders of the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Scolopacidae" title="Scolopacidae" class="mw-redirect">Scolopacidae</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Wood_Sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description and systematics">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_systematics">Description and systematics</span></h2>
<p>It resembles a longer-legged and more delicate <a href="/wiki/Green_Sandpiper" title="Green Sandpiper">Green</a> (<i>T. ochropus</i>) or <a href="/wiki/Solitary_Sandpiper" title="Solitary Sandpiper">Solitary Sandpiper</a> (<i>T. solitaria</i>) with a short fine <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a>, brown back and longer yellowish legs. It differs from the first of those species in a smaller and less contrasting white rump patch, while the Solitary Sandpiper nas no white rump patch at all.<sup id="cite_ref-haymanetal1986_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-haymanetal1986-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>However, it is not very closely related to these two species. Rather, its closest relative is the <a href="/wiki/Common_Redshank" title="Common Redshank">Common Redshank</a> (<i>T. totanus</i>), and these two share a <a href="/wiki/Sister_taxon" title="Sister taxon" class="mw-redirect">sister relationship</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Marsh_Sandpiper" title="Marsh Sandpiper">Marsh Sandpiper</a> (<i>T. stagnatilis</i>). These three species are a group of smallish shanks with red or yellowish legs, a breeding <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> that is generally subdued light brown above with some darker mottling and with a pattern of somewhat diffuse small brownish spots on the breast and neck.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Wood_Sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tringa_glareola_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Tringa_glareola_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg/220px-Tringa_glareola_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tringa_glareola_-_Laem_Phak_Bia.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
In non-breeding plumage</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Wood Sandpiper breeds in <a href="/wiki/Subarctic" title="Subarctic">subarctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Wetlands" title="Wetlands" class="mw-redirect">wetlands</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Highlands</a> across <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. They <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> to <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">Vagrant</a> birds have been seen as far into the <a href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific" class="mw-redirect">Pacific</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian Islands</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Micronesia" title="Micronesia">Micronesia</a> it is a regular visitor to the <a href="/wiki/Marianas_Islands" title="Marianas Islands" class="mw-redirect">Marianas Islands</a> (where flocks of up to 32 birds are reported) and <a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">Palau</a>; it is recorded on <a href="/wiki/Kwajalein" title="Kwajalein" class="mw-redirect">Kwajalein</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Islands" title="Marshall Islands">Marshall Islands</a> about once per decade. This species is encountered in the western Pacific region between mid-October and mid-May<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This bird is usually found on <a href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater" class="mw-redirect">freshwater</a> during migration and wintering. They forage by probing in shallow water or on wet mud, and mainly eat <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and similar small prey. <i>T. glareola</i> nests on the ground or uses an abandoned old tree nest of another <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Fieldfare" title="Fieldfare">Fieldfare</a> (<i>Turdus pilaris</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-haymanetal1986_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-haymanetal1986-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Wood Sandpiper is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>
<p>Widespread, it is considered a Species of <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN" class="mw-redirect">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
