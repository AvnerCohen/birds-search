<p>The <b>bridled tern</b> (<i>Onychoprion anaethetus</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-Bridge_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bridge-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  is a <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> of the family <a href="/wiki/Laridae" title="Laridae">Laridae</a>. It is a bird of the tropical oceans. The scientific name is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>. The genus comes from <i><i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453) language text">onux</i></i> meaning "claw" or "nail", and <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453) language text">prion</i>, meaning "saw". The specific <i>anaethetus</i> means "senseless, stupid".<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridled_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is a medium-sized tern, at 30–32&#160;cm in length and with a 77–81&#160;cm wingspan similar to the <a href="/wiki/Common_tern" title="Common tern">common tern</a> in size, but more heavily built. The wings and deeply forked tail are long, and it has dark grey upperparts and white underparts. The forehead and eyebrows are white, as is a striking collar on the hindneck. It has black legs and bill. Juvenile bridled terns are scaly grey above and pale below.
</p><p>This species is unlikely to be confused with any tern apart from the similarly dark-backed <a href="/wiki/Sooty_tern" title="Sooty tern">sooty tern</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Spectacled_tern" title="Spectacled tern">spectacled tern</a> from the Tropical Pacific. It is paler-backed than that sooty, (but not as pale as the grey-backed) and has a narrower white forehead and a pale neck collar.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_movements">Distribution and movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridled_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and movements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This bird is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and dispersive, wintering more widely through the tropical oceans. It has markedly marine habits compared to most terns. The Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> <i>melanopterus</i> breeds in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> and west <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>; other races occur around the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a>, but the exact number of valid subspecies is disputed. It is a rare vagrant to western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.
These are the four subspecies listed by the <a href="/wiki/International_Ornithological_Congress" class="mw-redirect" title="International Ornithological Congress">IOC</a>:
</p>
<ul><li><i>O. a. melanopterus</i> – <small>(<a href="/wiki/William_John_Swainson" title="William John Swainson">Swainson</a>, 1837)</small>: <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>.</li>
<li><i>O. a. antarcticus</i> – <small>(<a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Lesson" title="René Lesson">Lesson</a>, 1831)</small>: <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>.</li>
<li><i>O. a. anaethetus</i> – <small>(<a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Antonio_Scopoli" title="Giovanni Antonio Scopoli">Scopoli</a>, 1786)</small>: eastern <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a> Oceans.</li>
<li><i>O. a. nelsoni</i> – <small>(<a href="/wiki/Robert_Ridgway" title="Robert Ridgway">Ridgway</a>, 1919)</small>: west coast of <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bridled_tern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species breeds in colonies on rocky islands. It nests in a ground scrape or hole and lays one egg. It feeds by plunge-diving for fish in marine environments, but will also pick from the surface like the <a href="/wiki/Black_tern" title="Black tern">black tern</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gull-billed_tern" title="Gull-billed tern">gull-billed tern</a>. It usually dives directly, and not from the "stepped-hover" favoured by the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_tern" title="Arctic tern">Arctic tern</a>. The offering of fish by the male to the female is part of the courtship display.
</p>
