<p>The <b>common quail</b> (<i>Coturnix coturnix</i>), or <b>European quail</b>, is a small ground-nesting <a href="/wiki/Galliformes" title="Galliformes">game bird</a> in the pheasant family <a href="/wiki/Phasianidae" title="Phasianidae">Phasianidae</a>. It is mainly migratory, breeding in the western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> and wintering in Africa and southern India.
</p><p>With its characteristic call of "wet my lips", this species of <a href="/wiki/Quail" title="Quail">quail</a> is more often heard than seen. It is widespread in Europe and North Africa, and is categorised by the IUCN as "least concern". It should not be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_quail" title="Japanese quail">Japanese quail</a>, <i>Coturnix japonica</i>, native to Asia, which, although visually similar, has a very distinct call. Like the Japanese quail, common quails are sometimes kept as <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">poultry</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_quail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common quail was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">formally described</a> by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">tenth edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> under the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Tetrao coturnix</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The specific epithet <i>coturnix</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word for the common quail.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This species is now placed in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Coturnix" title="Coturnix">Coturnix</a></i> that was introduced in 1764 by the French naturalist <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Alexandre_Pierre_de_Garsault" title="François Alexandre Pierre de Garsault">François Alexandre Pierre de Garsault</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ioc_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The common quail was formerly considered to be <a href="/wiki/Conspecific" class="mw-redirect" title="Conspecific">conspecific</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_quail" title="Japanese quail">Japanese quail</a> (<i>Coturnix japonica</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The ranges of the two species meet in Mongolia and near <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a> without apparent interbreeding and in captivity the offspring of crosses show reduced fertility.<sup id="cite_ref-moreau_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moreau-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1980503_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1980503-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The Japanese quail is therefore now treated as a separate species.<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Five <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are recognised:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>C. c. coturnix</i> (<a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, 1758) – breeding in Europe and northwest Africa to Mongolia and north India, wintering in Africa and central, south India</li>
<li><i>C. c. conturbans</i> <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a>, 1917 – <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a></li>
<li><i>C. c. inopinata</i> Hartert, 1917 – <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Cape Verde Islands">Cape Verde Islands</a></li>
<li><i>C. c. africana</i> <a href="/wiki/Coenraad_Jacob_Temminck" title="Coenraad Jacob Temminck">Temminck</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Schlegel" title="Hermann Schlegel">Schlegel</a>, 1848 – sub-Saharan Africa and the islands of <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Comoros" title="Comoros">Comoros</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a></li>
<li><i>C. c. erlangeri</i> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Otto_Eduard_Graf_von_Zedlitz_und_Tr%C3%BCtzschler&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Otto Eduard Graf von Zedlitz und Trützschler (page does not exist)">Zedlitz</a>, 1912 – east and northeast Africa</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_quail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common quail is a small compact <a href="/wiki/Gallinaceous" class="mw-redirect" title="Gallinaceous">gallinaceous</a> bird 16–18&#160;cm (6.3–7.1&#160;in) in length with a wingspan of 32–35&#160;cm (13–14&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1980496_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1980496-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The weight is 70 to 140&#160;g (2.5 to 4.9&#160;oz). It is greatest before migration at the end of the breeding season. The female is generally slightly heavier than the male.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1980503_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1980503-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> It is streaked brown with a white eyestripe, and, in the male, a white chin. As befits its migratory nature, it has long wings, unlike the typically short-winged gamebirds.<sup id="cite_ref-Hume1880_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hume1880-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_quail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is a terrestrial species, feeding on <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> on the ground. It is notoriously difficult to see, keeping hidden in crops, and reluctant to fly, preferring to creep away instead. Even when flushed, it keeps low and soon drops back into cover. Often the only indication of its presence is the distinctive "wet-my-lips" repetitive song of the male. The call is uttered mostly in the mornings, evenings and sometimes at night. It is a strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> <a href="/wiki/Aves" class="mw-redirect" title="Aves">bird</a>, unlike most <a href="/wiki/Game_bird" class="mw-redirect" title="Game bird">game birds</a>.
</p><p>The common quail has been introduced onto the island of <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a> on several occasions but has failed to establish itself and is now probably extinct.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_quail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_quail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Coturnix_coturnix_MWNH_2037.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Coturnix_coturnix_MWNH_2037.JPG/110px-Coturnix_coturnix_MWNH_2037.JPG" decoding="async" width="110" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Coturnix_coturnix_MWNH_2037.JPG/165px-Coturnix_coturnix_MWNH_2037.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Coturnix_coturnix_MWNH_2037.JPG/220px-Coturnix_coturnix_MWNH_2037.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2793" data-file-height="3767" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Coturnix_coturnix_MWNH_2037.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs</div></div></div>
<p>Males generally arrive in the breeding area before the females. In northern Europe laying begins from the middle of May, and with repeat laying can continue to the end of August. The female forms a shallow <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest#Scrape" title="Bird nest">scrape</a> in the ground 7–13.5&#160;cm (2.8–5.3&#160;in) in diameter which is sparsely lined with vegetation. The eggs are laid at 24-hour intervals to form a clutch of between 8 and 13 eggs. These have an off-white to creamy yellow background with dark brown spots or blotches. Their average dimensions are 30&#160;mm ×&#160;23&#160;mm (1.18&#160;in ×&#160;0.91&#160;in) with a weight of 8&#160;g (0.28&#160;oz). The eggs are incubated by the female alone beginning after all the eggs are laid. The eggs hatch synchronously after 17–20 days. The young are <a href="/wiki/Precocial" class="mw-redirect" title="Precocial">precocial</a> and shortly after hatching leave the nest and can feed themselves. They are cared for by the female who broods them while they are small. The young fledge when around 19 days of age but stay in the family group for 30–50 days. They generally first breed when one year old and only have a single brood.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1980501-502_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1980501-502-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_humans">Relationship to humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_quail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Relationship to humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common quail is heavily hunted as <a href="/wiki/Game_(food)" class="mw-redirect" title="Game (food)">game</a> on passage through the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> area. Very large numbers are caught in nets along the Mediterranean coast of Egypt. It is estimated that in 2012, during the autumn migration, 3.4 million birds were caught in northern Sinai and perhaps as many as 12.9 million in the whole of Egypt.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species over recent years has seen an increase in its <a href="/wiki/Biological_reproduction" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological reproduction">propagation</a> in the United States and Europe. However, most of this increase is with <a href="/wiki/Hobbyists" class="mw-redirect" title="Hobbyists">hobbyists</a>. It is declining in parts of its range such as Ireland.
</p><p>In 1537, Queen <a href="/wiki/Jane_Seymour" title="Jane Seymour">Jane Seymour</a>, third wife of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII" title="Henry VIII">Henry VIII</a>, then pregnant with the future King <a href="/wiki/Edward_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward VI">Edward VI</a>, developed an insatiable craving for quail, and courtiers and diplomats abroad were ordered to find sufficient supplies for the Queen.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poisoning">Poisoning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_quail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Poisoning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>If common quails have eaten certain plants, although which plant is still in debate, the meat from quail can be poisonous, with one in four who consume poisonous flesh becoming ill with <a href="/wiki/Coturnism" title="Coturnism">coturnism</a>, which is characterized by muscle soreness, and which may lead to <a href="/wiki/Rhabdomyolysis" title="Rhabdomyolysis">kidney failure</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
