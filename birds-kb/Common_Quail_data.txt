<p>The <b>Common Quail</b> (<i>Coturnix coturnix</i>) is a small bird in the pheasant family <a href="/wiki/Phasianidae" title="Phasianidae">Phasianidae</a>. It is widespread and is found in parts of Europe, (should not be confused with the domesticated <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Quail" title="Japanese Quail">Japanese Quail</a>, Coturnix Japonica, original from Asia that although visually similar have very distinct calls).</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Quail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>It is a small (17&#160;cm) rotund bird, essentially streaked brown with a white eyestripe, and, in the male, a white chin. As befits its migratory nature, it has long wings, unlike the typically short-winged gamebirds.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Quail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habits">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Habits">Habits</span></h2>
<p>This is a terrestrial species, feeding on <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> on the ground. It is notoriously difficult to see, keeping hidden in crops, and reluctant to fly, preferring to creep away instead. Even when flushed, it keeps low and soon drops back into cover. Often the only indication of its presence is the distinctive "wet-my-lips" repetitive song of the male. The call is uttered mostly in the mornings, evenings and sometimes at night. It is a strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> <a href="/wiki/Aves" title="Aves" class="mw-redirect">bird</a>, unlike most <a href="/wiki/Game_bird" title="Game bird" class="mw-redirect">game birds</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Quail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h2>
<p>Upon attaining an age of 6–8 weeks, this <a href="/wiki/Quail" title="Quail">quail</a> breeds on open <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable farmland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a> across most of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, laying 6-12 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> in a ground nest. The eggs take from 16–18 days to hatch.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Quail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Races">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Races">Races</span></h2>
<p>This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in his <i>Systema naturae</i> in 1758 as <i>Tetrao coturnix</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The Eurasian race, <i>C. c. coturnix</i>, overwinters southwards in Africa's <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a> and India. The populations on <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> belong to the nominate race. The African race, <i>C. c. africana</i>, described by <a href="/wiki/Coenraad_Jacob_Temminck" title="Coenraad Jacob Temminck">Temminck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Schlegel" title="Hermann Schlegel">Schlegel</a>, in 1849, is known as the African Quail. It overwinters within Africa, some moving northwards from South Africa. The Common Quails of <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Comoros" title="Comoros">Comoros</a> belong to the same African race, although those found around <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> make up a different subspecies, the Abyssinian Quail, <i>C. c. erlangeri</i> (Zedlitz, 1912). The fairly numerous<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> population of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> islands, belong to a separate race, <i>C. c. inopinata</i>, (described by <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a> in 1917), while those on the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> belong to race <i>C. c. conturbans</i> (Hartert, 1917).</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Quail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Utilization">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Utilization">Utilization</span></h2>
<p><a href="//en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Exodus#16:1" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Exodus">Exodus 16:1-13</a> relates how the migrating Israelites relied on migrating quail for food. It is still heavily hunted as <a href="/wiki/Game_(food)" title="Game (food)">game</a> on passage through the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a> area. This species over recent years has seen an increase in its <a href="/wiki/Biological_reproduction" title="Biological reproduction" class="mw-redirect">propagation</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. However, most of this increase is with <a href="/wiki/Hobbyists" title="Hobbyists" class="mw-redirect">hobbyists</a>.</p>
