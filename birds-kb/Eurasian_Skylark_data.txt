<p>The <b>Eurasian skylark</b> (<i>Alauda arvensis</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird in the lark family, <a href="/wiki/Alaudidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Alaudidae">Alaudidae</a>. It is a widespread species found across Europe and the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> with introduced populations in New Zealand, Australia and on the Hawaiian Islands. It is a bird of open farmland and heath, known for the song of the male, which is delivered in hovering flight from heights of 50 to 100 metres (160 to 330&#160;ft). The sexes are alike. It is streaked greyish-brown above and on the breast and has a buff-white belly.
</p><p>The female Eurasian skylark builds an open nest in a shallow depression on open ground well away from trees, bushes and hedges. She lays three to five eggs which she incubates for around 11 days. The chicks are fed by both parents but leave the nest after eight to ten days, well before they can fly. They scatter and hide in the vegetation but continue to be fed by the parents until they can fly at 18 to 20 days of age. Nests are subject to high predation rates by larger birds and small mammals. The parents can have several broods in a single season.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_skylark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Eurasian skylark was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">tenth edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> and retains its original <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial</a> name of <i>Alauda arvensis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is one of the four species placed in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Alauda" title="Alauda">Alauda</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> name is from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>alauda</i>, "<a href="/wiki/Lark" title="Lark">lark</a>". <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny</a> thought the word was originally of Celtic origin. The specific <i>arvensis</i> is also Latin, and means "of the field".<sup id="cite_ref-job_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The results of a <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular phylogenetic">molecular phylogenetic</a> study of the lark family Alaudidae published in 2013 suggested that Eurasian skylark is most closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Oriental_skylark" title="Oriental skylark">Oriental skylark</a> <i>Alauda gulgula</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-alstrom_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alstrom-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Formerly, many authorities considered the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_skylark" title="Japanese skylark">Japanese skylark</a> as a separate species. It is now usually considered a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of the Eurasian skylark.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Alternate names for the Eurasian skylark include northern skylark and sky lark.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDonald200448_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDonald200448-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_skylark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Eleven subspecies are recognized:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>A. a. arvensis</i> Linnaeus, 1758 – northern, western and central Europe</li>
<li><i>A. a. sierrae</i> <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Weigold" title="Hugo Weigold">Weigold</a>, 1913 – Portugal, central and southern Spain</li>
<li><i>A. a. harterti</i> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Whitaker_(ornithologist)" title="Joseph Whitaker (ornithologist)">Whitaker</a>, 1904 – north-western Africa</li>
<li><i>A. a. cantarella</i> <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lucien_Bonaparte" title="Charles Lucien Bonaparte">Bonaparte</a>, 1850 – southern Europe from north-eastern Spain to Turkey and the Caucasus</li>
<li><i>A. a. armenica</i> <a href="/wiki/Modest_Bogdanov" title="Modest Bogdanov">Bogdanov</a>, 1879 – south-eastern Turkey to Iran</li>
<li><i>A. a. dulcivox</i> <a href="/wiki/Allan_Octavian_Hume" title="Allan Octavian Hume">Hume</a>, 1872 – south-eastern European Russia and western Siberia to north-western China and south-western Mongolia</li>

<li><i>A. a. lonnbergi</i> <a href="/wiki/Masauji_Hachisuka" title="Masauji Hachisuka">Hachisuka</a>, 1926 – northern <a href="/wiki/Sakhalin_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Sakhalin Island">Sakhalin Island</a></li>

<p>Some authorities recognise the subspecies <i>A. a. scotia</i> <a href="/wiki/Viktor_von_Tschusi_zu_Schmidhoffen" title="Viktor von Tschusi zu Schmidhoffen">Tschusi</a>, 1903 and <i>A. a. guillelmi</i> <a href="/wiki/Harry_Forbes_Witherby" title="Harry Forbes Witherby">Witherby</a>, 1921.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In the above list <i>scotia</i> is included in the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> <i>A. a. arvensis</i> and <i>guillelmi</i> is included in <i>A. a. sierrae</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_skylark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alauda_arvensis_MHNT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Alauda_arvensis_MHNT.jpg/220px-Alauda_arvensis_MHNT.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="258" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Alauda_arvensis_MHNT.jpg/330px-Alauda_arvensis_MHNT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Alauda_arvensis_MHNT.jpg/440px-Alauda_arvensis_MHNT.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3001" data-file-height="3515" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alauda_arvensis_MHNT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i> Alauda arvensis </i> - <a href="/wiki/MHNT" class="mw-redirect" title="MHNT">MHNT</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alauda_arvensis_nest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Alauda_arvensis_nest.jpg/220px-Alauda_arvensis_nest.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Alauda_arvensis_nest.jpg/330px-Alauda_arvensis_nest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Alauda_arvensis_nest.jpg/440px-Alauda_arvensis_nest.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2272" data-file-height="1704" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alauda_arvensis_nest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nest</div></div></div>
<p>The Eurasian skylark is 18–19&#160;cm (7.1–7.5&#160;in) in length.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1988188_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1988188-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Like most other larks, the Eurasian skylark is a rather dull-looking species, being mainly brown above and paler below.  It has a short blunt crest on the head, which can be raised and lowered. In flight it shows a short tail and short broad wings. The tail and the rear edge of the wings are edged with white, which are visible when the bird is flying away, but not if it is heading towards the observer. The male has broader wings than the female. This <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adaptation</a> for more efficient hovering flight may have <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolved</a> because of female Eurasian skylarks' preference for males that sing and hover for longer periods and so demonstrate that they are likely to have good overall <a href="/wiki/Fitness_(biology)" title="Fitness (biology)">fitness</a>.
</p><p>It is known for the song of the male, which is delivered in hovering flight from heights of 50 to 100&#160;m, when the singing bird may appear as just a dot in the sky from the ground. The long, unbroken song is a clear, bubbling warble delivered high in the air while the bird is rising, circling or hovering.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The song generally lasts two to three minutes, but it tends to last longer later in the mating season, when songs can last for 20 minutes or more. At <a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">wind farm</a> sites, male skylarks have been found to sing at higher frequencies as a result of wind turbine noise.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_skylark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This lark breeds across most of Europe and Asia and in the mountains of north Africa. It is mainly resident in the west of its range, but eastern populations are more <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, moving further south in winter. Even in the milder west of its range, many birds move to lowlands and the coast in winter. Asian birds, subspecies <i>A. a. pekinensis</i>, appear as vagrants in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduced_populations">Introduced populations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_skylark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Introduced populations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 19th century multiple batches of Eurasian skylarks were released in New Zealand beginning in 1864 in <a href="/wiki/Nelson,_New_Zealand" title="Nelson, New Zealand">Nelson</a> (in the South Island) and in 1867 in <a href="/wiki/Auckland" title="Auckland">Auckland</a> (in the North Island). The wild population increased rapidly and had spread throughout both the North and South Islands by the 1920s.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHiggins_et_al.20061043_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHiggins_et_al.20061043-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Australia the Eurasian skylark was introduced on multiple occasions beginning in 1850.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHiggins_et_al.20061043_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHiggins_et_al.20061043-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It is now widespread in the southeast of the continent. In New South Wales it mostly occurs south of 33°S. It is widespread throughout <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a> and also occurs in the south-eastern corner of <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a> around <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHiggins_et_al.20061042_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHiggins_et_al.20061042-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Eurasian skylark was introduced to the southeastern <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian Islands</a> beginning in 1865. Although once common, it has declined in abundance on <a href="/wiki/Oahu" title="Oahu">Oahu</a> and is no longer found on <a href="/wiki/Kauai" title="Kauai">Kauai</a>. A study published in 1986 found European skylarks remained only on the islands of <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_(island)" title="Hawaii (island)">Hawaii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maui" title="Maui">Maui</a> and estimated a total population of 10,000 individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Eurasian skylark was introduced to <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_Island" title="Vancouver Island">Vancouver Island</a> off the west coast of Canada in 1903; additional birds were introduced in 1913.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The population grew and by 1962 there were around 1000 individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The numbers have subsequently declined due to loss of habitat, and in 2007 there were estimated to be only around 100 individuals spread over four small areas of the <a href="/wiki/Saanich_Peninsula" title="Saanich Peninsula">Saanich Peninsula</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_skylark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_skylark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Eurasian skylarks first breed when they are one year of age. Nesting may start in late March or early April. The nest is probably built by the female alone and is a shallow depression in the ground lined with grasses. The clutch is 3 to 5 eggs. The eggs of the nominate subspecies average 23.4&#160;mm ×&#160;16.8&#160;mm (0.92&#160;in ×&#160;0.66&#160;in) in size and weigh around 3.35&#160;g (0.118&#160;oz). They have a grey-white or greenish background and are covered in brown or olive spots. They are incubated only by the female beginning after the last egg is laid and hatch synchronously after 11 days.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1988201_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1988201-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a> young are cared for by both parents and for the first week are fed almost exclusively on insects.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1988194,_201_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1988194,_201-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The nestlings fledge after 18 to 20 days but they usually leave the nest after 8 to 10 days. They are independent of their parents after around 25 days. The parents can have up to 4 broods in a season.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1988201_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1988201-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_skylark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Eurasian skylark walks over the ground searching for food on the soil surface. Its diet consists of insects and plant material such as seeds and young leaves. Unlike a <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finch</a> (family Fringillidae) it swallows seeds without removing the husk. Insects form an important part of the diet in summer.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1988192_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1988192-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats">Threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_skylark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Skylark_2,_Lake_District,_England_-_June_2009.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Skylark_2%2C_Lake_District%2C_England_-_June_2009.jpg/220px-Skylark_2%2C_Lake_District%2C_England_-_June_2009.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Skylark_2%2C_Lake_District%2C_England_-_June_2009.jpg/330px-Skylark_2%2C_Lake_District%2C_England_-_June_2009.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Skylark_2%2C_Lake_District%2C_England_-_June_2009.jpg/440px-Skylark_2%2C_Lake_District%2C_England_-_June_2009.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2560" data-file-height="1920" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Skylark_2,_Lake_District,_England_-_June_2009.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eurasian skylark in the Lake District, England, with two beetles caught in its beak</div></div></div>
<p>In the UK, Eurasian skylark numbers have declined over the last 30 years, as determined by the Common Bird Census started in the early 1960s by the <a href="/wiki/British_Trust_for_Ornithology" title="British Trust for Ornithology">British Trust for Ornithology</a>.  There are now only 10% of the numbers that were present 30 years ago.  The <a href="/wiki/RSPB" class="mw-redirect" title="RSPB">RSPB</a> have shown that this large decline is mainly due to changes in farming practices and only partly due to pesticides.  In the past cereals were planted in the spring, grown through the summer and harvested in the early autumn.  Cereals are now planted in the autumn, grown through the winter and are harvested in the early summer.  The winter grown fields are much too dense in summer for the Eurasian skylark to be able to walk and run between the wheat stems to find its food.
</p><p>A list of conservation interventions that could impact Alauda arvensis can be found in the Conservation Evidence<sup id="cite_ref-CE_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CE-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> website. English farmers are now encouraged and paid to maintain and create <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> for improving the <a href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Habitat (ecology)">habitat</a> for Eurasian skylarks.  <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Stewardship" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental Stewardship">Environmental Stewardship</a> Scheme offers 5 and 10-year grants for various beneficial options.  For example, there is an option where the farmer can opt to grow a spring cereal instead of a winter one, and leave the stubble untreated with pesticide over the winter. The <a href="/wiki/British_Trust_for_Ornithology" title="British Trust for Ornithology">British Trust for Ornithology</a> likens the stubbles to "giant bird tables" – providing spilt grain and weed seed to foraging birds.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The RSPB's research, over a six-year period, of winter-planted wheat fields has shown that suitable nesting areas for Eurasian skylarks can be made by turning the seeding machine off (or lifting the drill) for a 5 to 10&#160;metres stretch as the tractor goes over the ground to briefly stop the seeds being sown.  This is repeated in several areas within the same field to make about two skylark plots per hectare.  Subsequent spraying and fertilising can be continuous over the entire field.  DEFRA suggests that Eurasian skylark plots should not be nearer than <span class="nowrap">24 m</span> to the perimeter of the field, should not be near to telegraph poles, and should not be enclosed by trees.
</p><p>When the crop grows, the Eurasian skylark plots (areas without crop seeds) become areas of low vegetation where Eurasian skylarks can easily hunt insects, and can build their well camouflaged ground nests.  These areas of low vegetation are just right for skylarks, but the wheat in the rest of the field becomes too closely packed and too tall for the bird to seek food.  At the RSPB's research farm in <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a> skylark numbers have increased threefold (from 10 pairs to 30 pairs) over six years.  Fields where Eurasian skylarks were seen the year before (or nearby) would be obvious good sites for skylark plots.  Farmers have reported that skylark plots are easy to make and the RSPB hope that this simple effective technique can be copied nationwide.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_skylark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the word "lark" is used without specification, it usually refers to this species.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Collective_noun" title="Collective noun">collective noun</a> for Eurasian skylarks is an "exaltation". Although the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> describes this usage as "fanciful", it traces it back to a quotation from <a href="/wiki/John_Lydgate" title="John Lydgate">John Lydgate</a> dating from about 1430.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The verb "skylark", originally used by sailors, means "play tricks or practical jokes; indulge in horseplay, frolic". The verb and noun "lark", with similar meaning, may be related to "skylark" or to the dialect word "laik" (<i>New Shorter OED</i>).
</p><p>The bird is the subject of poems by <a href="/wiki/Percy_Bysshe_Shelley" title="Percy Bysshe Shelley">Percy Bysshe Shelley</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/To_a_Skylark" title="To a Skylark">To a Skylark</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/George_Meredith" title="George Meredith">George Meredith</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Lark_Ascending" title="The Lark Ascending">The Lark Ascending</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Ted_Hughes" title="Ted Hughes">Ted Hughes</a> (<i>Skylarks</i>), and numerous others; of a play by <a href="/wiki/Henrik_Ibsen" title="Henrik Ibsen">Henrik Ibsen</a> entitled "<a href="/wiki/A_Doll%27s_House" title="A Doll&#39;s House">A Doll's House</a>" and of pieces of music including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lark_Ascending" title="The Lark Ascending">The Lark Ascending</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Vaughan_Williams" title="Ralph Vaughan Williams">Ralph Vaughan Williams</a> (inspired by the eponymous poem). It is also the bird emblem of <a href="/wiki/Kumamoto_Prefecture" title="Kumamoto Prefecture">Kumamoto Prefecture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_Skylark_of_Space" title="The Skylark of Space">The Skylark of Space</a> is a series of four science fiction novels by E.E. "Doc" Smith.
</p>
