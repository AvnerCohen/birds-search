<p>The <b>pink-backed pelican</b> (<i>Pelecanus rufescens</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Pelican" title="Pelican">pelican</a> family. It is a resident breeder in the <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a> and shallow lakes of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabia</a>, and southern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>; it has also apparently <a href="/wiki/Local_extinction" title="Local extinction">extirpated</a> in <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pink-backed_pelican&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>It is a relatively small pelican, although by no means is it a small bird. Its length is from 125 to 155&#160;cm (49 to 61&#160;in), wingspan is 2.15–2.9&#160;m (7.1–9.5&#160;ft) and body mass is from 4 to 7&#160;kg (8.8 to 15.4&#160;lb). The bill is 30 to 38&#160;cm (12 to 15&#160;in) in length.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The plumage is grey and white, with a pinkish hue on the back occasionally apparent (never in the deep pink of a <a href="/wiki/Flamingo" title="Flamingo">flamingo</a>). The top of the bill is yellow and the pouch is usually greyish. Breeding adults have long <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feather</a> plumes on the head.
</p><p>It shares its habitat with the <a href="/wiki/Great_white_pelican" title="Great white pelican">great white pelican</a>, which is generally larger and has white instead of greyish plumage.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_breeding">Habitat and breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pink-backed_pelican&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habitat and breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The pink-backed pelican is found in a range of aquatic habitats, but prefers quiet backwaters with shallow water, avoiding steep, vegetated lake banks. It prefers for freshwater lakes, <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a>, large slow-flowing rivers, and seasonal pools but also frequents reservoirs, seasonally flooded land and flood-plains near river mouths. It may occur on alkaline and saline lakes and lagoons, and can sometimes be found along the coast in bays and <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuaries</a> (although seldom on open seashore). The species tends to roost and breed in trees (e.g. mangroves), but will also roost on sandy islands, cliffs, <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a> and sand-dunes.
</p><p>Nesting trees have many <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nests</a> built close together. These nests are re-used every year until the trees collapse, although the birds will normally remain in the area. The species nests colonially in trees, reeds or low bushes along waterfronts as well as (less often) on the ground on sandy islands and in <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangroves</a>.
</p><p>Its nest is a large heap of sticks and may be 10–50&#160;m above the ground. The female lays two to three large white <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> and later the chicks feed by plunging their heads deep into the adult's pouch and taking the partially digested regurgitated fish.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pink-backed_pelican&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Food is usually fish (of any size up to 450&#160;g, usually in the 80-290&#160;g range) and <a href="/wiki/Amphibia" class="mw-redirect" title="Amphibia">amphibians</a>, and is usually obtained by fishing in groups. Among the fish preyed upon are <a href="/wiki/Cichlid" title="Cichlid">cichlids</a> like <i><a href="/wiki/Haplochromis" title="Haplochromis">Haplochromis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Tilapia_(genus)" title="Tilapia (genus)">Tilapia</a></i>.
</p>
