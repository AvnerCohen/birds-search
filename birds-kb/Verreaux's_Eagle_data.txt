<p><b>Verreaux's eagle</b> (<i>Aquila verreauxii</i>) is a large, mostly <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a>.  It is also called the <b>black eagle</b>, especially in <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">Southern Africa</a>, leading to potential confusion with the Indian <a href="/wiki/Black_eagle" title="Black eagle">black eagle</a> (<i>Ictinaetus malayensis</i>), which lives far to the east in Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-BEstudy_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BEstudy-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  Verreaux's eagle lives in hilly and mountainous regions of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">southern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Africa" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Africa">eastern Africa</a> (extending marginally into <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>), and very locally in <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a> and the southern <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>. It is one of the most specialized species of accipitrid in the world, with its distribution and life history revolving around its favorite prey species, the <a href="/wiki/Rock_hyrax" title="Rock hyrax">rock hyraxes</a>. When hyrax populations decline, the species have been shown to survive with mixed success on other prey, such as small <a href="/wiki/Antelope" title="Antelope">antelopes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galliformes" title="Galliformes">gamebirds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hare" title="Hare">hares</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monkey" title="Monkey">monkeys</a> and other assorted vertebrates. Despite a high degree of specialization, Verreaux's eagle has, from a conservation standpoint, been faring relatively well in historic times. One population of this species, in the <a href="/wiki/Matobo_National_Park" title="Matobo National Park">Matobo Hills</a> of <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>, is arguably the best studied eagle population in the world, having been subject to continuous detailed study since the late 1950s.<sup id="cite_ref-BEstudy_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BEstudy-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Watson_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  Like all <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a>, this species belongs to the taxonomic order <a href="/wiki/Accipitriformes" title="Accipitriformes">Accipitriformes</a> (formerly included in <a href="/wiki/Falconiformes" title="Falconiformes">Falconiformes</a>) and the family <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a>, which may be referred to colloquially as accipitrids or raptors.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verreaux%27s_eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:VerreauxsEagle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/VerreauxsEagle.jpg/170px-VerreauxsEagle.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="234" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/VerreauxsEagle.jpg/255px-VerreauxsEagle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/VerreauxsEagle.jpg/340px-VerreauxsEagle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2023" data-file-height="2783" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:VerreauxsEagle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration from the species description by Lesson published in 1830<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> was first described by <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Primev%C3%A8re_Lesson" class="mw-redirect" title="René Primevère Lesson">René Primevère Lesson</a> in his 1830 publication, <i>Centurie zoologique, ou choix d'animaux rares, nouveaux ou imparfaitement connus</i>, as <i>Aquila Verreauxii</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The species’ name commemorates the French naturalist <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verreaux" title="Jules Verreaux">Jules Verreaux</a>, who visited <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">southern Africa</a> in the early 19th century and collected the type specimen for the <a href="/wiki/French_Academy_of_Sciences" title="French Academy of Sciences">French Academy of Sciences</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mearns1988_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mearns1988-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Verreaux's eagle is part of a broad group of raptors called "<a href="/wiki/Booted_eagles_(group)" class="mw-redirect" title="Booted eagles (group)">booted eagles</a>" which are defined by the feature that all included species have feathering over their tarsus, whereas most other accipitrids have bare legs. Included in this group are all species described as "hawk eagles" including the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Spizaetus" title="Spizaetus">Spizaetus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Nisaetus" title="Nisaetus">Nisaetus</a></i>, as well as assorted <a href="/wiki/Monotypic_taxon" title="Monotypic taxon">monotypical genera</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Black-and-chestnut_eagle" title="Black-and-chestnut eagle">Oroaetus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Long-crested_eagle" title="Long-crested eagle">Lophaetus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Crowned_eagle" title="Crowned eagle">Stephanoaetus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Martial_eagle" title="Martial eagle">Polemaetus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rufous-bellied_eagle" title="Rufous-bellied eagle">Lophotriorchis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Black_eagle" title="Black eagle">Ictinaetus</a></i>. The genus <i><a href="/wiki/Aquila_(genus)" title="Aquila (genus)">Aquila</a></i> is distributed across every continent but for <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>. Up to 20 species have been classified in the genus but the taxonomic placement of some of the traditionally included species has recently been questioned. Traditionally, the <i>Aquila</i> eagles have been grouped superficially as largish, mainly brownish or dark-colored booted eagles that vary little in transition from their juvenile to their adult plumages.
</p><p>Genetic research has recently shown the Verreaux's eagle is included in a <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> with its nearest relatives, the <a href="/wiki/Sister_species" class="mw-redirect" title="Sister species">sister species</a> <a href="/wiki/Bonelli%27s_eagle" title="Bonelli&#39;s eagle">Bonelli's eagle</a> (<i>A. fasciatus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/African_hawk-eagle" title="African hawk-eagle">African hawk-eagle</a> (<i>A. spilogaster</i>), as well as the more distant sister species pair, the <a href="/wiki/Wedge-tailed_eagle" title="Wedge-tailed eagle">wedge-tailed eagle</a> (<i>A. audax</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Gurney%27s_eagle" title="Gurney&#39;s eagle">Gurney's eagle</a> (<i>A. gurneyi</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Lerner2017_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lerner2017-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Closely related to this clade are the <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">golden eagle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cassin%27s_hawk-eagle" title="Cassin&#39;s hawk-eagle">Cassin's hawk-eagle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lerner2017_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lerner2017-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Some of the relationships within this group have long been suspected based on morphological similarities among the large-bodied species.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The identification of the smaller, much paler-bellied <i>A. fasciatus</i> and <i>A. spilogaster</i> as members of the clade was a surprise, given that they were previously included in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Hieraaetus" title="Hieraaetus">Hieraaetus</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Watson_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Helbig_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helbig-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lerner_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lerner-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Cassin's hawk-eagle has been assigned to both the <i>Hieraaetus</i> group and the <i>Spizaetus/Nisaetus</i> "hawk-eagle" group but is now known based on this genetic data to also nest within <i>Aquila</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lerner2017_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lerner2017-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other largish <i>Aquila</i> species, the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_imperial_eagle" title="Eastern imperial eagle">eastern imperial eagle</a> (<i>A. heliaca</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_imperial_eagle" title="Spanish imperial eagle">Spanish imperial eagle</a> (<i>A. adaberti</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Tawny_eagle" title="Tawny eagle">tawny eagle</a> (<i>A. rapax</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Steppe_eagle" title="Steppe eagle">steppe eagles</a> (<i>A. nipalensis</i>), are now thought to be separate, close-knit clade, which attained some characteristics similar to those of the prior clade via convergent evolution.<sup id="cite_ref-Helbig_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helbig-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lerner_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lerner-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Genetically, the "spotted eagles" (<i><a href="/wiki/Lesser_spotted_eagle" title="Lesser spotted eagle">C. pomarina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_spotted_eagle" title="Indian spotted eagle">C. hasata</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Greater_spotted_eagle" title="Greater spotted eagle">C. clanga</a></i>), have been discovered to be more closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Long-crested_eagle" title="Long-crested eagle">long-crested eagle</a> (<i>Lophaetus occipitalis</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Black_eagle" title="Black eagle">black eagle</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Helbig_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helbig-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and have been transferred to the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Clanga_(genus)" title="Clanga (genus)">Clanga</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lerner2017_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lerner2017-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The genus <i>Hieraaetus</i>, traditionally including the <a href="/wiki/Booted_eagle" title="Booted eagle">booted eagle</a> (<i>H. pennatus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Little_eagle" title="Little eagle">little eagle</a> (<i>H. morphnoides</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Ayres%27s_hawk-eagle" title="Ayres&#39;s hawk-eagle">Ayres's hawk-eagle</a> (<i>H. ayresii</i>), consists of much smaller species, that are in fact the smallest birds called eagles outside of the unrelated <i><a href="/wiki/Spilornis" title="Spilornis">Spilornis</a></i> serpent-eagle genus. This genus has recently been eliminated by many authorities and is now occasionally also included in <i>Aquila</i>, although not all ornithological unions have followed this suit in this re-classification.<sup id="cite_ref-Watson_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lerner_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lerner-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The small-bodied <a href="/wiki/Wahlberg%27s_eagle" title="Wahlberg&#39;s eagle">Wahlberg's eagle</a> (<i>H. wahlbergi</i>) has been traditionally considered an <i>Aquila</i> species due to its lack of change from juvenile to adult plumage and brownish color but it is actually genetically aligned to the <i>Hieraaetus</i> lineage.<sup id="cite_ref-Helbig_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Helbig-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verreaux%27s_eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Verreaux_Eagle_AdF.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Verreaux_Eagle_AdF.jpg/300px-Verreaux_Eagle_AdF.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Verreaux_Eagle_AdF.jpg/450px-Verreaux_Eagle_AdF.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Verreaux_Eagle_AdF.jpg/600px-Verreaux_Eagle_AdF.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1920" data-file-height="1280" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Verreaux_Eagle_AdF.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Portrait of <i>Aquila verreauxii</i></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aquila_verreauxii01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Aquila_verreauxii01.jpg/300px-Aquila_verreauxii01.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Aquila_verreauxii01.jpg/450px-Aquila_verreauxii01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Aquila_verreauxii01.jpg/600px-Aquila_verreauxii01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1892" data-file-height="1419" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aquila_verreauxii01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult Verreaux's eagle in flight in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Verreaux's eagle is a very large eagle. It measures 75 to 96&#160;cm (30 to 38&#160;in) long from the bill to the tip of the tail, making it the sixth longest eagle in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Males can weigh 3 to 4.2&#160;kg (6.6 to 9.3&#160;lb) and the larger females weigh 3.1 to 7&#160;kg (6.8 to 15.4&#160;lb). The average weight is approximately 4.19&#160;kg (9.2&#160;lb), based on the weights of 21 eagles of both sexes. Other reported mean body mass measurements of Verreaux's eagles were lower however, with seven unsexed birds averaging 3.32&#160;kg (7.3&#160;lb), while four unsexed eagles in an additional study averaged 3.72&#160;kg (8.2&#160;lb). In another group of weighed eagles, four females were found to average 4.6&#160;kg (10&#160;lb), which is more than a kilogram more than the average male, It is the seventh or eighth heaviest living eagle in the world. In average mass and overall weight range, if not linear measurements, the Verreaux's is very similar in size to its occasional competitor, the <a href="/wiki/Martial_eagle" title="Martial eagle">martial eagle</a>, which is regularly titled the largest of the African eagles. It also rivals the martial and <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">golden eagles</a> as the largest extant member of the "booted eagle" clan.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CRC_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> It has a wingspan of 1.81 to 2.3&#160;m (5&#160;ft 11&#160;in to 7&#160;ft 7&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> of the male is 56.5 to 59.5&#160;cm (22.2 to 23.4&#160;in) and that of the female is 59 to 64&#160;cm (23 to 25&#160;in). Among other standard measurements in the Verreaux's eagles, both sexes measure 27.2 to 36&#160;cm (10.7 to 14.2&#160;in) in tail length and 9.5 to 11&#160;cm (3.7 to 4.3&#160;in) in <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus length</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Other than the female's slight size advantage, adult males and females are physically indistinguishable from each other.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Adult Verreaux's eagles are mostly jet-black in color.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The yellow coloration of the cere (the bill is gun-metal grey), eye-ring and “eye-brows”, all stand out in contrast to the black plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Even more prominent on flying birds when seen from above is the white on the back, rump and upper-tail coverts and part of the scapulars, which forms a V-shaped patch, although this feature is partially obscured in perched birds.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Adults also have conspicuous white windows on the wing quills at the carpal joint (at the base of the primaries) when seen flying both from above and below.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The bill is stout, the head is prominent on the relatively long neck and the legs are fully feathered.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aquila_verreauxii02.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Aquila_verreauxii02.jpg/220px-Aquila_verreauxii02.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Aquila_verreauxii02.jpg/330px-Aquila_verreauxii02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Aquila_verreauxii02.jpg/440px-Aquila_verreauxii02.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1959" data-file-height="1469" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aquila_verreauxii02.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Juvenile Verreaux's eagle.</div></div></div>
<p>Juvenile and immature plumages differ markedly from the plumage of adults. They are overall a dark brown color.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Immatures have a strongly contrasting golden crown and a rufous or ginger nape and mantle. They have small white streaks on the forehead and black on their cheeks. The throat is dark streaked, the lower throat is pale brown and the upper-chest is brown.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The rest of the underside is brown but for a blackish-blotched rufous to cream-colored abdomen and lightly marked creamy thighs and legs. The feathers of the upper-tail and upper-wing coverts are brown with white streaks in young birds, while the other tail and wing quills are nearly black. The wing quills when seen from below in flight show considerable whitish mottling, with more extensive white than is typically seen in adult plumages. The immature has a dark brown iris and yellowish feet.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Black feathers increase from 2 to 5 years of age amongst  a scattering of brown-tipped feathers, though the contrasting creamy trousers are maintained through the 3rd year. By the 4th year, they look dark grey-brown with a buff-patch on the nape and mottling of retained brownish feathers. At the end of the subadult phase at around 5 years of age, the plumage is practically indistinguishable from the adult.<sup id="cite_ref-Leslie_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Full adult plumage is probably attained in 5 to 6 years.<sup id="cite_ref-Global_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Global-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Verreaux's eagle is essentially unmistakable, especially in adulthood. No other black-colored raptor in its range approaches this species’ large size, nor possesses its distinctive patterns of white.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The golden eagle is of similar size or marginally larger size and the two species are the heaviest living <i>Aquila</i> species and measure only marginally less than the slightly lighter-weight Australasian wedge-tailed eagle in total wing and bill-to-tail length.<sup id="cite_ref-Watson_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> While the juvenile Verreaux's eagle is quite different from the adult's, its plumage is no less distinctive. No other accipitrid shares the mottled brownish body, blackish wings with large white patches or contrasting whitish, rufous and golden color around the head and neck as that of an immature eagle. The flight profile of Verreaux's eagle is also distinctive, as it is the only <i>Aquila</i> species other than the golden eagle to soar in a pronounced <a href="/wiki/Dihedral_angle" title="Dihedral angle">dihedral</a>, with the wings held slightly above the back and primaries upturned at the tip to make a V shape. In the <a href="/wiki/Bale_Mountains" title="Bale Mountains">Bale Mountains</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> and possibly in some parts of the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a> and the southwestern edge of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, the ranges of the golden and  Verreaux's eagles overlap, but the golden is a mostly brown bird and shares none of the Verreaux's black plumage. The immature golden eagle has white patches on its underwing as do Verreaux's but they are less extensive than those of the latter species. The wing shape also differs from the golden, as the Verreaux's eagle has very broad outer secondaries and a relatively narrow pinch at the base of the primaries, whereas the tapering of a golden eagle's wing is more gradual. The Verreaux's eagle wings have variously been described as paddle, spoon or leaf shaped.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Imperial eagles are another improbable but possible source of confusion, since they too have white markings on their wing coverts, but are totally different in flight profile (much flatter winged) and overall coloration (dark brown).<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verreaux%27s_eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Witkruisarend.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Witkruisarend.jpg/220px-Witkruisarend.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Witkruisarend.jpg/330px-Witkruisarend.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Witkruisarend.jpg/440px-Witkruisarend.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1557" data-file-height="2335" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Witkruisarend.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A captive Verreaux's eagle in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>This species is largely silent, though is arguably a stronger vocalist than its close cousin, the golden eagle.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Chicking and chirruping sounds like that of a young <a href="/wiki/Turkey_(bird)" title="Turkey (bird)">turkey</a> or <a href="/wiki/Francolin" title="Francolin">francolin</a>, <i>pyuck</i>, have been heard in various contexts,  such as pairs being reunited. More striking sounds are loud, ringing <i>whaeee-whaeee</i>, <i>heeeee-oh</i> or <i>keeooo-keeooo</i> calls used as contact calls or during intruder chases. Various screams, barks, yelps and mews have been heard to be issued at potential mammalian predators.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The young emits feeble chirps at first, later more likely to cluck like the adults.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_distribution">Habitat and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verreaux%27s_eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Habitat and distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Balancing_Rocks_in_Matopos_National_Park.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Balancing_Rocks_in_Matopos_National_Park.jpg/220px-Balancing_Rocks_in_Matopos_National_Park.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Balancing_Rocks_in_Matopos_National_Park.jpg/330px-Balancing_Rocks_in_Matopos_National_Park.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Balancing_Rocks_in_Matopos_National_Park.jpg/440px-Balancing_Rocks_in_Matopos_National_Park.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1608" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Balancing_Rocks_in_Matopos_National_Park.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kopje (pronounced "copy") in the <a href="/wiki/Matobo_National_Park" title="Matobo National Park">Matobo Hills</a>, home to the highest density of Verreaux's eagle.</div></div></div>
<p>Verreaux's eagle has specific habitat requirements and is rare outside of its particular habitat type. It lives in <a href="/wiki/Monadnock" class="mw-redirect" title="Monadnock">kopjes</a>, which are dry, rocky environments in anything from rocky <a href="/wiki/Hill" title="Hill">hills</a> to high <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountains</a> amongst <a href="/wiki/Cliff" title="Cliff">cliffs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canyon" title="Canyon">gorges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monadnock" class="mw-redirect" title="Monadnock">inselbergs</a> often surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">thornbush</a> and <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">sub-desert</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It is often found in dry areas with less than 60&#160;cm (24&#160;in) of average annual rainfall. It is highest ranging in elevation in Ethiopia and East Africa, where found up to 4,000&#160;m (13,000&#160;ft) above sea level.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Verreaux's eagle is found from the <a href="/wiki/Marra_Mountains" class="mw-redirect" title="Marra Mountains">Marra Mountains</a><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> southward through that country<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> to 16°N in <a href="/wiki/Eritrea" title="Eritrea">Eritrea</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> along the northern mountains of <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> in much of <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> (mostly the central, mountainous spine),<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> possibly some mountains in northeastern <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, easternmost <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a> and possibly <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Southeastern Africa is the heart  of the Verreaux's eagle range: they are found in most mountain ranges in <a href="/wiki/Malawi" title="Malawi">Malawi</a> but for the <a href="/wiki/Nyika_Plateau" title="Nyika Plateau">Nyika Plateau</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mafinga_Hills" title="Mafinga Hills">Mafinga Hills</a> and the Lulwe Hills,<sup id="cite_ref-Dowsett_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dowsett-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a> (especially the escarpments bordering <a href="/wiki/Lake_Kariba" title="Lake Kariba">Lake Kariba</a> to the gorges below <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Falls,_Zambia" class="mw-redirect" title="Victoria Falls, Zambia">Victoria Falls</a>), in <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a> (especially east of the central plateau),<sup id="cite_ref-Irwin_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Irwin-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Swaziland" class="mw-redirect" title="Swaziland">Swaziland</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lesotho" title="Lesotho">Lesotho</a> and down into <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, where they largely inhabit the <a href="/wiki/Karoo" title="Karoo">Karoo</a>, along the cliffs of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Escarpment,_Southern_Africa" title="Great Escarpment, Southern Africa">Great Escarpment</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Fold_Mountains" class="mw-redirect" title="Cape Fold Mountains">Cape Fold Mountains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Peninsula" title="Cape Peninsula">Cape Peninsula</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tarboton_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tarboton-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> A somewhat more sparse distribution is known in <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a>, western <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a> and southwestern <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a> (in the <a href="/wiki/Serra_da_Chela" title="Serra da Chela">Serra da Chela</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Oatley_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oatley-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Elsewhere in Africa, the Verreaux's eagle may be found but tends to be rare and only spottily seen, such as in eastern <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a>, northeastern <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/A%C3%AFr_Mountains" title="Aïr Mountains">Aïr Mountains</a> of <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> and southwestern <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a> (where known only as a vagrant).<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> In 1968, only a single record of Verreaux's eagle (from <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>) was known from outside of Africa, but now it is known they are a rare breeder out of their main continent. From a handful of immature records and territorial adult behavior, breeding has been inferred in <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dietary_ecology">Dietary ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verreaux%27s_eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dietary ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dassie_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Dassie_2.jpg/220px-Dassie_2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Dassie_2.jpg/330px-Dassie_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Dassie_2.jpg/440px-Dassie_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2355" data-file-height="1662" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dassie_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Few accipitrids are as specialized as the Verreaux's eagle. One of their two favored prey species: <a href="/wiki/Rock_hyrax" title="Rock hyrax">Cape hyrax</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Few other accipitrids are as singularly specialized to hunt a single prey family as Verreaux's eagles, perhaps excluding the <a href="/wiki/Snail_kite" title="Snail kite">snail kite</a> (<i>Rostrhamus sociabilis</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Slender-billed_kite" title="Slender-billed kite">slender-billed kite</a> (<i>Helicolestes hamatus</i>) with their specialization to hunt <i><a href="/wiki/Pomacea" title="Pomacea">Pomacea</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a>. Not even accipitrids named after their staple food are known to be as specialized, i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Bat_hawk" title="Bat hawk">bat hawk</a> (<i>Macheiramphus alcinus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Palm-nut_vulture" title="Palm-nut vulture">palm-nut vulture</a> (<i>Gypohierax angolensis</i>), <a href="/wiki/Lizard_buzzard" title="Lizard buzzard">lizard buzzard</a> (<i>Kaupifalco monogrammicus</i>) and perhaps the <a href="/wiki/Rufous_crab_hawk" title="Rufous crab hawk">rufous crab hawk</a> (<i>Buteogallus aequinoctialis</i>). Certainly, the Verreaux's eagle has the most conservative diet of <i>Aquila</i> species, though the diet is more diverse in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> than in <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Steyn_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steyn-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Two species comprise considerably more than half of (often more than 90% of) the Verreaux's eagle's diet: the <a href="/wiki/Rock_hyrax" title="Rock hyrax">Cape hyrax</a> (<i>Procavia capensis</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Yellow-spotted_rock_hyrax" title="Yellow-spotted rock hyrax">yellow-spotted rock hyrax</a> (<i>Heterohyrax brucei</i>). In the <a href="/wiki/Matobo_National_Park" title="Matobo National Park">Matobo Hills</a>  of <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>, these two hyraxes comprised 1,448 out of 1,550 eagle prey items recorded at eyries just after the breeding season from 1995 to 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-Chiweshe_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chiweshe-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> In the same area, from 1957 to 1990, 98.1% of the diet was made up of rock hyrax.<sup id="cite_ref-BEstudy_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BEstudy-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In a sample size of 224 from 102 nests in <a href="/wiki/Serengeti_National_Park" title="Serengeti National Park">Serengeti National Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>, 99.1% of the remains were of hyrax.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Elsewhere in Tanzania, the diet is more mixed, with 53.7% of the remains from 24 nests made up of hyrax.<sup id="cite_ref-Rowe_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rowe-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> In a nest in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, 89.1% of the remains from a sampling of 55 were of hyrax.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> No detailed statistics are known but the hyrax are likely to the main prey in every population and have been mentioned to dominate the diet in <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Malawi" title="Malawi">Malawi</a><sup id="cite_ref-Dowsett_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dowsett-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Around 400 hyrax may be taken through the year by a pair with young.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The entire distribution of the species neatly corresponds with that of the two species of rock hyrax.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> To date, there are no known instances of Verreaux's eagle hunting the two species of <a href="/wiki/Tree_hyrax" title="Tree hyrax">tree hyraxes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Steyn_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steyn-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the first 10 years of constant observation of the population from the <a href="/wiki/Matobo_National_Park" title="Matobo National Park">Matobo Hills</a>, only two kills were witnessed. However, enough hunting behavior has been ultimately observed to give a good idea how a Verreaux's eagle obtains its prey.<sup id="cite_ref-Leslie_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> This species most often forages in low-level quartering flight, with the rock hyraxes chiefly caught after a rapid, somewhat twisting dive in the few seconds after the eagle “surprises” the hyrax. Like the golden eagle, Verreaux's eagle uses natural contours of the ground in rocky and mountainous habitats to increase the element of surprise, as hyraxes (appropriately considering their diverse range of predators) tend to be highly wary.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Steyn_49-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steyn-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Verreaux's eagle have been known to hunt from a perch, which, although not abnormal, is certainly rare.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Maclean;_1993;_p._113_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maclean;_1993;_p._113-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>  Hunting hyrax cooperatively has been recorded, with one eagle of a pair flying past and distracting the prey while the other strikes from behind. Verreaux's eagle may knock hyraxes off cliffs and take arboreal prey from treetops but usually kills on the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The daily estimate food requirements of this species are around 350&#160;g (12&#160;oz), nearly a third more than that of a golden eagle despite the latter's marginally heavier body weight.<sup id="cite_ref-Leslie_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Rock hyraxes are often difficult to observe other than a quick flash for humans, but a Verreaux's eagle can fly out and then return to the nest with a kill in the matter of a few minutes.<sup id="cite_ref-Leslie_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Of the two species regularly taken, the yellow-spotted rock hyrax can weigh from 1 to 3.63&#160;kg (2.2 to 8.0&#160;lb) with an average of 2.4&#160;kg (5.3&#160;lb), although specimens from <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a> are noticeably heavier and larger than specimens from <a href="/wiki/Serengeti_National_Park" title="Serengeti National Park">Serengeti National Park</a>. Cape hyrax, weighing from 1.8 to 5.5&#160;kg (4.0 to 12.1&#160;lb) with average of around 3.14&#160;kg (6.9&#160;lb), can be even larger than the Verreaux's eagles themselves, so can be more difficult to kill. Yellow-spotted rock hyraxes are more often taken in the Matobo Hills, perhaps because of their smaller size or its more diurnal habits. Adult rock hyraxes were disproportionately selected, perhaps due to being out in the open more regularly. In Cape hyraxes, 1 to 2-year-old males are particularly vulnerable, since they are forced to disperse at sexual maturity. Juvenile hyraxes constituted from 11-33% of prey remains in the Western Cape while 18% of hyraxes killed were juveniles in Matobo Hills. Because of their greater weight, Cape hyraxes are frequently either consumed at the kill site (putting the eagle at risk of losing prey to competing predators or to attack by large mammalian carnivores) or are decapitated and brought to the nest or perch. Fewer skulls or jaws of Cape hyraxes have been found at nest sites than yellow-spotted rock hyrax skulls.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> However, the Cape hyrax has a wider distribution than the yellow-spotted and the Verreaux's eagle may hunt the Cape hyrax almost exclusively outside of the long band of eastern Africa where the smaller species is distributed.<sup id="cite_ref-Steyn_49-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steyn-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> In comparison to the golden eagle, Verreaux's eagle has a foot pad that's about 20% wider, which is possibly an adaptation to taking the bulky and broad-backed rock hyrax.<sup id="cite_ref-Davies_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> The foot of the Verreaux's eagle is reportedly even larger than a human hand.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, where the Cape hyrax is the main prey species, the estimated mean size of prey taken to the nest is around 2.6&#160;kg (5.7&#160;lb), perhaps twice as heavy as prey taken by some nesting golden eagles.<sup id="cite_ref-Davies_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> However, the mean size of prey taken by Verreaux's eagle in the Matobo Hills, with more yellow-spotted rock hyrax, was around 1.82&#160;kg (4.0&#160;lb), around the same estimated weight as prey taken by golden eagles in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and smaller than the average estimated mass of prey taken to golden eagle nests in some regions, like <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Watson_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_prey">Other prey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verreaux%27s_eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other prey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Verreaux%27s_Eagle._Black_Eagle,_Aquila_verreauxii,_at_Walter_Sisulu_National_Botanical_Garden,_Gauteng,_South_Africa_(19613704431).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Verreaux%27s_Eagle._Black_Eagle%2C_Aquila_verreauxii%2C_at_Walter_Sisulu_National_Botanical_Garden%2C_Gauteng%2C_South_Africa_%2819613704431%29.jpg/220px-Verreaux%27s_Eagle._Black_Eagle%2C_Aquila_verreauxii%2C_at_Walter_Sisulu_National_Botanical_Garden%2C_Gauteng%2C_South_Africa_%2819613704431%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Verreaux%27s_Eagle._Black_Eagle%2C_Aquila_verreauxii%2C_at_Walter_Sisulu_National_Botanical_Garden%2C_Gauteng%2C_South_Africa_%2819613704431%29.jpg/330px-Verreaux%27s_Eagle._Black_Eagle%2C_Aquila_verreauxii%2C_at_Walter_Sisulu_National_Botanical_Garden%2C_Gauteng%2C_South_Africa_%2819613704431%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Verreaux%27s_Eagle._Black_Eagle%2C_Aquila_verreauxii%2C_at_Walter_Sisulu_National_Botanical_Garden%2C_Gauteng%2C_South_Africa_%2819613704431%29.jpg/440px-Verreaux%27s_Eagle._Black_Eagle%2C_Aquila_verreauxii%2C_at_Walter_Sisulu_National_Botanical_Garden%2C_Gauteng%2C_South_Africa_%2819613704431%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3438" data-file-height="2258" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Verreaux%27s_Eagle._Black_Eagle,_Aquila_verreauxii,_at_Walter_Sisulu_National_Botanical_Garden,_Gauteng,_South_Africa_(19613704431).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An adult Verreaux's eagle carrying avian prey.</div></div></div>
<p>Verreaux's eagle are easily capable of taking diverse prey but this is infrequent in areas with healthy rock hyrax populations. Cases where more diverse food is brought to the nest are usually either considered to be areas where rock hyrax populations have declined or areas where eagles occupy home ranges which included non-rocky habitat such as savanna, which are described by Valerie Gargett as “poor food areas” due to their lack of hyrax.<sup id="cite_ref-BEstudy_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BEstudy-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kruger_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kruger-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>  In such areas, about 80% of prey is mammalian.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Verreaux's eagles that are less specialized have diets and hunting capacities that are similar to those of the golden eagle, although the latter species often subsists on <a href="/wiki/Hare" title="Hare">hares</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ground_squirrel" title="Ground squirrel">ground squirrels</a> or <a href="/wiki/Grouse" title="Grouse">grouse</a> for about half or two-thirds of its diet, a portion still comprised by rock hyrax in the Verreaux's.<sup id="cite_ref-BEstudy_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BEstudy-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Watson_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> One study accumulated records of Verreaux's eagle preying on at least 100 prey species.<sup id="cite_ref-Thompson_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Other prey types recorded have included small (mainly juvenile) <a href="/wiki/Antelope" title="Antelope">antelopes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hare" title="Hare">hares</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meerkat" title="Meerkat">meerkats</a> (<i>Suricata suricatta</i>), other <a href="/wiki/Mongoose" title="Mongoose">mongooses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monkey" title="Monkey">monkeys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squirrel" title="Squirrel">squirrels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cane_rat" title="Cane rat">cane rats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galago" title="Galago">bushbabies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">lambs</a> (<i>Ovis aries</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Goat" title="Goat">kids</a> (<i>Capra aegagrus hircus</i>). <a href="/wiki/Francolin" title="Francolin">Francolin</a> (<i>Francolinus</i> ssp.) and <a href="/wiki/Guineafowl" title="Guineafowl">guineafowl</a> (<i>Numina</i> ssp.) as well as <a href="/wiki/Waterfowl" class="mw-redirect" title="Waterfowl">waterfowl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">herons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egret" title="Egret">egrets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bustard" title="Bustard">bustards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pigeon" class="mw-redirect" title="Pigeon">pigeons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crows</a> (<i>Corvus</i> ssp.), <a href="/wiki/Dove" class="mw-redirect" title="Dove">doves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">chickens</a> (<i>Gallus gallus domesticus</i>) and a <a href="/wiki/Black_sparrowhawk" title="Black sparrowhawk">great sparrowhawk</a> (<i>Accipiter melanoleucus</i>) have been among the recorded avian prey.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> Avian prey ranging in size from 102.6&#160;g (3.62&#160;oz) <a href="/wiki/Alpine_swift" title="Alpine swift">alpine swift</a> (<i>Tachymarptis melba</i>) to 7.26&#160;kg (16.0&#160;lb) adult male <a href="/wiki/Denham%27s_bustard" title="Denham&#39;s bustard">Denham's bustards</a> (<i>Neotis denhami</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Boshoff_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boshoff-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>, out of a  sample size of 41 from 26 nests, 53.7% of remains were of hyraxes, 29.3% of francolins, guineafowl and chickens, 12.2% of antelopes, 2.4% of hares and rabbits and 2.4% of mongoose.<sup id="cite_ref-Rowe_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rowe-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tortoise" title="Tortoise">Tortoises</a> made up 145 of 5748 from 73 sites (2.5%) in South Africa. Rarely, <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a> may also be taken and even <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termites</a> have been eaten by this species.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Leslie_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In South Africa, the commonest foods were (in descending order of preference): <a href="/wiki/Rock_hyrax" title="Rock hyrax">Cape hyrax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smith%27s_red_rock_hare" title="Smith&#39;s red rock hare">Smith's red rock hare</a>  (<i>Pronolagus rupertris</i>), <a href="/wiki/Meerkat" title="Meerkat">meerkat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mountain_reedbuck" title="Mountain reedbuck">mountain reedbuck</a> (<i>Redunca fulvorufula</i>), <a href="/wiki/Goat" title="Goat">goats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scrub_hare" title="Scrub hare">scrub hare</a> (<i>Lepus saxatilis</i>), <a href="/wiki/Cape_spurfowl" title="Cape spurfowl">Cape francolin</a> (<i>Francolinus capensis</i>), <a href="/wiki/Helmeted_guineafowl" title="Helmeted guineafowl">helmeted guineafowl</a>(<i>Numida meleagris</i>), <a href="/wiki/Yellow_mongoose" title="Yellow mongoose">yellow mongoose</a> (<i>Cynictis penicillata</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Bowsprit_tortoise" class="mw-redirect" title="Bowsprit tortoise">Angulate tortoise</a> (<i>Chersina angulata</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In “poor food areas” of the Matobo Hills, three nests included 53.6% hyrax, 10.7% cane-rats, 7.1% monkeys, 7.1% mongoose and 3.6% antelope.<sup id="cite_ref-BEstudy_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BEstudy-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  From 1997 to 2005 in the same area, non-hyrax prey (each representing less than 10 out of 1550 prey items at nests) included <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_mongoose" title="White-tailed mongoose">white-tailed mongoose</a> (<i>Ichneumia albicauda</i>), <a href="/wiki/Steenbok" title="Steenbok">steenbok</a> (<i>Raphicerus campestris</i>), domestic goat, <a href="/wiki/Vervet_monkey" title="Vervet monkey">vervet monkey</a> (<i>Chlorocebus pygerythrus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Jameson%27s_red_rock_hare" title="Jameson&#39;s red rock hare">Jameson's red rock hare</a> (<i>Pronolagus randensis</i>), <a href="/wiki/Helmeted_guineafowl" title="Helmeted guineafowl">helmeted guineafowl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swainson%27s_spurfowl" title="Swainson&#39;s spurfowl">Swainson's francolin</a> (<i>Pternistis swainsonii</i>), <a href="/wiki/Natal_spurfowl" title="Natal spurfowl">Natal francolin</a> (<i>Pternistis natalensis</i>), <a href="/wiki/Southern_red-billed_hornbill" title="Southern red-billed hornbill">southern red-billed hornbill</a> (<i>Tockus rufirostris</i>), <a href="/wiki/Rock_pigeon" class="mw-redirect" title="Rock pigeon">rock pigeon</a> (<i>Columba livia</i>), <a href="/wiki/White-necked_raven" title="White-necked raven">white-necked raven</a> (<i>Corvus albicollis</i>), <a href="/wiki/Leopard_tortoise" title="Leopard tortoise">leopard tortoise</a> (<i>Stigmochelys pardalis</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Giant_plated_lizard" title="Giant plated lizard">giant plated lizard</a> (<i>Gerrhosaurus validus</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Chiweshe_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chiweshe-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Walter_Sisulu_National_Botanical_Garden" title="Walter Sisulu National Botanical Garden">Walter Sisulu National Botanical Garden</a> of <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, the primary prey found around nests after a perceptible hyrax decline has become <a href="/wiki/Helmeted_guineafowl" title="Helmeted guineafowl">helmeted guineafowl</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francolin" title="Francolin">francolins</a>, followed by cane rats, rabbits and <a href="/wiki/Dikdik" class="mw-redirect" title="Dikdik">dikdiks</a> (<i>Rhynchotragus</i> ssp.).<sup id="cite_ref-Kruger_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kruger-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Young baboons may also be hunted, even the large-bodied <a href="/wiki/Chacma_baboon" title="Chacma baboon">chacma baboons</a> (<i>Papio ursinus</i>) which have issued a predator alarm call in response to the presence of Verreaux's eagles.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> Carrion either fairly frequent or none at all.<sup id="cite_ref-BEstudy_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BEstudy-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Maclean;_1993;_p._113_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maclean;_1993;_p._113-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>  A study of the taking of domestic lambs in the <a href="/wiki/Karoo" title="Karoo">Karoo</a> found only two cases of lambs being eaten by Verreaux's eagle and these were already dead when carried off. This contrasts with the golden eagle, which in some areas may eat many dead lambs and occasionally hunt lives ones.<sup id="cite_ref-Watson_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-R.A.G._68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R.A.G.-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> Although any prey weighing over 4.5&#160;kg (9.9&#160;lb) is rarely taken, some ungulates hunted by Verreaux's eagles can be considerably larger.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Klipspringer" title="Klipspringer">Klipspringer</a> (<i>Oreotragus oreotragus</i>) brought to the nest have weighed up to an estimated 12&#160;kg (26&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-Davies_59-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> A Verreaux's eagle was observed to hunt and kill a <a href="/wiki/Mountain_reedbuck" title="Mountain reedbuck">mountain reedbuck</a> lamb estimated to weigh 15&#160;kg (33&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> The smallest known mammalian prey was a 97.6&#160;g (3.44&#160;oz) <a href="/wiki/Cape_gerbil" title="Cape gerbil">Cape gerbil</a> (<i>Gerbilliscus afra</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Boshoff_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boshoff-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interspecies_competition">Interspecies competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verreaux%27s_eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Interspecies competition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Flickr_-_Rainbirder_-_The_aerial_duel_at_dawn%5E_(View_on_large).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Flickr_-_Rainbirder_-_The_aerial_duel_at_dawn%5E_%28View_on_large%29.jpg/220px-Flickr_-_Rainbirder_-_The_aerial_duel_at_dawn%5E_%28View_on_large%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Flickr_-_Rainbirder_-_The_aerial_duel_at_dawn%5E_%28View_on_large%29.jpg/330px-Flickr_-_Rainbirder_-_The_aerial_duel_at_dawn%5E_%28View_on_large%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Flickr_-_Rainbirder_-_The_aerial_duel_at_dawn%5E_%28View_on_large%29.jpg/440px-Flickr_-_Rainbirder_-_The_aerial_duel_at_dawn%5E_%28View_on_large%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="787" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flickr_-_Rainbirder_-_The_aerial_duel_at_dawn%5E_(View_on_large).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Verreaux's eagle finds itself attacked by a <a href="/wiki/Lanner_falcon" title="Lanner falcon">lanner falcon</a> when it enters the latter's home range, but the falcon quickly ceases the attack when the eagle presents its talons.</div></div></div>
<p>Although it is the most specialized predator of rock hyrax in the world, it does not have monopoly on this prey. Many wild predators are also attracted to rock hyraxes, which thus puts them in potential competition with the Verreaux's eagles. Amongst the other very large eagles which are widely found in sub-Saharan Africa, both the crowned eagle and the martial eagle may also locally favor rock hyraxes in their diets. However, these species have highly different habitat preferences and hunting techniques. The crowned eagle, a forest-dwelling species, is primarily a perch-hunter and can spend hours watching for prey activity from a prominent tree perch. The martial eagle is a dweller mainly of lightly wooded savanna and often hunts on the wing. soaring high and watching for prey activity with its superb vision, quite unlike the contour-hunting technique used by Verreaux's eagles. While the habitats of crowned and Verreaux's eagle keep them segregated enough to likely eliminate competition, confrontations between Verreaux's and martial eagles have been recorded. Although somewhat larger and more powerful, the martial eagle is relatively less nimble in the air and there is a case where a martial eagle was robbed of rock hyrax prey by a Verreaux's eagle.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Leslie_23-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Another case of kleptoparasitism by a Verreaux's eagle involved one stealing some carrion from a <a href="/wiki/Bearded_vulture" title="Bearded vulture">lammergeier</a> (<i>Gypaetus barbatus</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Verreaux's eagles occasionally prey on other large raptors including vultures, including <a href="/wiki/White-headed_vulture" title="White-headed vulture">white-headed vulture</a> (<i>Trigonoceps occipitalis</i>), <a href="/wiki/White-backed_vulture" title="White-backed vulture">white-backed vulture</a> (<i>Gyps africanus</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Cape_vulture" title="Cape vulture">Cape vulture</a> (<i>Gyps coprotheres</i>), though the earlier cases probably refer to nestling or juvenile predation and the latter to nest defense on the part of the eagles.<sup id="cite_ref-Steyn_49-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steyn-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Thompson_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> An apparent predation attempt on a full grown juvenile <a href="/wiki/R%C3%BCppell%27s_griffon" class="mw-redirect" title="Rüppell&#39;s griffon">Rüppell's griffon</a> (<i>Gyps rueppellii</i>) was abortive.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> A unique opportunity to study Verreaux's eagle living with its closest extant cousin, the golden eagle, has been afforded in the Bale Mountains of Ethiopia. The two species, with similar habitat preferences, were observed to defend their territories from one another exclusively, with many cases of goldens chasing Verreaux's eagles out of their respective territories in flight and only one of Verreaux's chasing the goldens. However, since the golden eagles prefer hares and Verreaux prefers rock hyraxes, they actually seem to have no deleterious effect on each other's breeding activities.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/African_hawk-eagle" title="African hawk-eagle">African hawk-eagles</a> (<i>Aquila spilogaster</i>) may also take a few  hyraxes, but are likely to avoid direct conflicts with their much larger cousins, so will <a href="/wiki/Tawny_eagle" title="Tawny eagle">tawny eagles</a> (<i>Aquila rapax</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Leslie_23-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Other predators of rock hyrax may include <a href="/wiki/Felid" class="mw-redirect" title="Felid">felids</a> like <a href="/wiki/African_wildcat" title="African wildcat">African wildcats</a> (<i>Felis silvestris lybica</i>), <a href="/wiki/Serval" title="Serval">servals</a> (<i>Leptailurus serval</i>), <a href="/wiki/Caracal" title="Caracal">caracals</a> (<i>Caracal caracal</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Leopard" title="Leopard">leopards</a> (<i>Panthera pardus</i>) as well as <a href="/wiki/Jackal" title="Jackal">jackals</a> (<i>Canis</i> ssp.), <a href="/wiki/Python_sebae" class="mw-redirect" title="Python sebae">African rock pythons</a>  (<i>Python sebae</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owls</a> (mainly the large <a href="/wiki/Verreaux%27s_eagle-owl" title="Verreaux&#39;s eagle-owl">Verreaux's eagle-owl</a> (<i>Bubo lacteus</i>) or possibly the <a href="/wiki/Cape_eagle-owl" title="Cape eagle-owl">Cape eagle-owl</a> (<i>B. capensis</i>)). Neonate rock hyraxes may fall prey to <a href="/wiki/Mongoose" title="Mongoose">mongooses</a> and venomous snakes like <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_cobra" title="Egyptian cobra">Egyptian cobras</a> (<i>Naja haje</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Bitis_arietans" class="mw-redirect" title="Bitis arietans">puff adders</a> (<i>Bitis arietans</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> Due to the formidable range of competitors it pays for Verreaux's eagle to be cautious from the moment it bears down on its prey. Cases where pirating has been attempted has involved diverse carnivores like caracals and jackals. In at least one case, a Verreaux's eagle was observed to be displaced off a rodent-kill (likely a cane-rat) by an <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_wolf" title="Ethiopian wolf">Ethiopian wolf</a> (<i>Canis simensis</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> As is often the case with reintroduced eagles (i.e. seen even in the huge <a href="/wiki/Harpy_eagle" title="Harpy eagle">harpy eagle</a> (<i>Harpia harpyja</i>)), reintroduced Verreaux's eagle may lose their fear of other predators to their own detriment and one such bird fell victim to a caracal.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> Cases where Verreaux's eagles have swooped at leopards are not likely competitive but are more likely to try to displace from their territory and such attacks have occasionally had fatal results for the birds.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> This species is not normally aggressive to humans but may swoop uncomfortably close when the nest is being investigated.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verreaux%27s_eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Territoriality_and_movements">Territoriality and movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verreaux%27s_eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Territoriality and movements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A rough estimated average of home range size in Verreaux's eagle is 10.9&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (4.2&#160;sq&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-Leslie_23-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  Density of breeding pairs varies from 1 pair per 10.3&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (4.0&#160;sq&#160;mi) in the <a href="/wiki/Matobo_National_Park" title="Matobo National Park">Matobo Hills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>, 1 pair per 24&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (9.3&#160;sq&#160;mi) in the <a href="/wiki/Karoo" title="Karoo">Karoo</a>, 1 pair per 25&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (9.7&#160;sq&#160;mi) in <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a>, 1 pair per 28&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (11&#160;sq&#160;mi) in the <a href="/wiki/Bale_Mountains" title="Bale Mountains">Bale Mountains</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> to a known maximum spacing of 1 pair per 35 to 65&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (14 to 25&#160;sq&#160;mi) in the <a href="/wiki/Magaliesberg" title="Magaliesberg">Magaliesberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drakensberg" title="Drakensberg">Drakensberg</a> ranges.<sup id="cite_ref-BEstudy_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BEstudy-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Steyn_49-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steyn-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Davies2_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies2-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> The Matobo Hills reportedly has one of the greatest breeding densities known of any large eagle and territories are extremely stable through seasons and years. Such stable distributions are expected of long-lived raptors living in the tropics with a relatively stable food supply outside the seasonal variation of temperate zones.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> While Matobo Hill home ranges ranged from 6 to 14&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (2.3 to 5.4&#160;sq&#160;mi), most were observed to include about the same amount of kopje habitat (up to 5&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (1.9&#160;sq&#160;mi)).<sup id="cite_ref-BEstudy_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BEstudy-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Populations fluctuate surprisingly little despite four-fold changes between peaks and troughs in hyrax numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>  At troughs, eagles may temporarily disappear or switch to alternate prey. This is only especially marked in drought periods and on average occurs once every 20 years.<sup id="cite_ref-Gargett_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gargett-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> Some authors consider the Verreaux's eagle to be a partial migrant,<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> others describe it as sedentary.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> This is more a matter of terminology than unclear behaviour, since this species is well known to behave like almost all raptors that breed in Sub-Saharan Africa. That is the young wander relatively widely once dispersed from their parent's territory but the adults generally remain sedentary on their home range for the remainder of their lives.<sup id="cite_ref-BEstudy_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BEstudy-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Verreaux's eagle displays may potentially occur almost throughout the year. Frequently displays are in response to the presence of another soaring pair or after repelling a single intruder from the territory. They will also display if anxious about the nest when humans or other large mammals approach too closely.<sup id="cite_ref-Leslie_23-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The male's display often consists of him first flying up in an undulating flight with wings readily held spread or closed. Then, once at a great height, he plunges down as far as 305&#160;m (1,001&#160;ft) at a time, then quickly rises back up, sometimes swinging to and fro like the arms of a pendulum, at other times diving and rising along a straight line. These evolutions may be embellished with somersaults and sideways rolls at the peak before the descent.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Leslie_23-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Some displays involve pairs of eagles. A pair frequently circles or makes figures of eight over their territory. One bird may roll over and present claws in flight or the male may fly behind female with exaggeratedly upcurved wings.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It is now the prevalent thought that most displays in <i>Aquila</i> eagles are territorial, as they often occur along the boundary of a given home range rather than near the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-Watson_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Displays with talon-grappling and tumbling are often aerial fights between territorial birds and occasionally the eagles may clasp and whirl downwards (one such fight reportedly resulting in the birds plunging into the sea).<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verreaux%27s_eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Flickr_-_Rainbirder_-_Verreaux%27s_Eagle_pair.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Flickr_-_Rainbirder_-_Verreaux%27s_Eagle_pair.jpg/220px-Flickr_-_Rainbirder_-_Verreaux%27s_Eagle_pair.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Flickr_-_Rainbirder_-_Verreaux%27s_Eagle_pair.jpg/330px-Flickr_-_Rainbirder_-_Verreaux%27s_Eagle_pair.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Flickr_-_Rainbirder_-_Verreaux%27s_Eagle_pair.jpg/440px-Flickr_-_Rainbirder_-_Verreaux%27s_Eagle_pair.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flickr_-_Rainbirder_-_Verreaux%27s_Eagle_pair.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A breeding pair of Verreaux's eagles.</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>, 60 pairs may nest in 620&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (240&#160;sq&#160;mi), equivalent to 1 pair per 10.3&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (4.0&#160;sq&#160;mi), but this is exceptional. In <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a>, one pair nests each 25&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (9.7&#160;sq&#160;mi) and in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> sometimes as little as 1 pair per 10.2 to 15&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (3.9 to 5.8&#160;sq&#160;mi), but nearer 60&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (23&#160;sq&#160;mi) being more typical.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Verreaux's eagle may build from 1 to 3 nests, sometimes none over the course of a year.  In the <a href="/wiki/Matobo_National_Park" title="Matobo National Park">Matobo Hills</a>, the average number of nests built per pair is 1.4.<sup id="cite_ref-Leslie_23-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  Eagles nesting in the <a href="/wiki/Karoo" title="Karoo">Karoo</a> have much larger territories, though are subject to persecution and habitat change, more so than many other populations.<sup id="cite_ref-Leslie_23-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Matobo_National_Park" title="Matobo National Park">Matobos</a>, the species is near the breeding population capacity level with almost unlimited nests that are rather unevenly distributed among available jumbles of rocky kopjes.  In <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, nests are more scattered and Verreaux's eagle sometimes do not breed even where the habitat seems appropriate and there are good numbers of rock hyrax.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> By virtue of location on a narrow ledge, nests tend to be much broader than deep and a relatively small for the size of the eagle.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  The flattish nests, made out of green branches and lined with green leaves, are up to 1.8&#160;m (71&#160;in) across and 2&#160;m (6.6&#160;ft) deep. Though a nest depth of around 0.6&#160;m (2.0&#160;ft) is typical, one old nest was 4.1&#160;m (13&#160;ft) deep.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Typically nests are on cliffs, often in an overhung crevice or in a small cave, sometimes on an open ledge. The nest site is generally marked by a 'whitewash' which is formed by the birds' droppings.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> Verreaux's is the most cliff dependent of all eagle species, in the late 1970s only 3 known nests were in trees.<sup id="cite_ref-Leslie_23-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Very rarely, they may nest in trees, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Euphorbia" title="Euphorbia">Euphorbia</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">Acacia</a></i>, often those growing out of a cliff crevice.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  A handful of nests in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> have even been on <a href="/wiki/Transmission_tower" title="Transmission tower">electric pylons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> A new nest takes up to four months to construct, with some repair being typical upon each use. Both sexes participate in the nest construction, though the female usually takes the lead.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  It may take several hundred feet of rope for a human to reach the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Predation of young in the nest is either suspected or anecdotely reported as having been committed by <a href="/wiki/Python_sebae" class="mw-redirect" title="Python sebae">African rock pythons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baboon" title="Baboon">baboons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caracal" title="Caracal">caracals</a>. However, predation is believed to be normally quite rare, due to the combination of factors such as the inaccessibility of most nests by foot (thus cutting off all but the most nimble mammalian carnivores) and the bold defenses of the parent eagles.<sup id="cite_ref-BEstudy_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BEstudy-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Verreaux's eagle have reportedly dropped sticks on potential nest predators. This has been considered a form of tool-use, which is generally unknown in other raptorial birds and has been mostly reported in <a href="/wiki/Corvidae" title="Corvidae">corvids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">herons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Egg laying may occur from November to August in <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabia">Arabia</a>, October to May in <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>, year around in <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a> (with a peak of June to December) and anywhere from April to November in Africa from <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a> southwards.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  In an unusual behaviour for eagles, the males may bring food to females before egg laying and, more typically, males bring almost all food during the incubation stage.<sup id="cite_ref-BEstudy_2-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BEstudy-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Two eggs are generally laid, though a range of one to three is known. The eggs are rather elongated ovals and being chalky white sometimes with a bluish tinge or a few reddish-brown marking, measuring from 71 to 83.4&#160;mm (2.80 to 3.28&#160;in) in length  and 56–62&#160;mm (2.2–2.4&#160;in) in width, with an average of 76.9&#160;mm ×&#160;58.6&#160;mm (3.03&#160;in ×&#160;2.31&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The eggs are laid at three-day intervals starting in the middle of the day. Both sexes incubate, but the female takes the major share and tends to sit all night over them. Sometimes, the male may sit for 40-50% of the day with more shifts for him towards the end of incubation. These are close-sitters, which are not easily disturbed off the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Incubation is 43 to 47 days. Hatching happens at about to 2–3 days apart, with about 24 hours from the first chipping of the egg's surface to complete hatching. One egg is sometimes infertile and the second egg tends to be about 10% smaller.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The Verreaux's eagle is considered an “obligate cainist”, that is the older sibling normally kills the younger one (in more than 90% of observed nests), by either starvation or direct attack. Aggression may continue for up to 70 days after hatching.<sup id="cite_ref-BEstudy_2-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BEstudy-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Simmons_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simmons-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> At one time no cases of two young successfully reaching the fledging stage were known, however a couple of cases of two healthy fledglings from a nest have been recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Siblicide is regularly observed in raptorial birds, including unrelated families like <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skua" title="Skua">skuas</a> and is common, even typical, in <i>Aquila</i> eagles. The behaviour is most commonly explained as a kind of insurance policy, with the second nestling existing both to act as a backup if the first egg or nestling perishes and to mitigate the stressful workload demanded of the parent raptors in feeding, brooding and defending the young.<sup id="cite_ref-Simmons_90-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simmons-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> The odds of survival for the second fledging are better in the golden eagle and other temperate-breeding <i>Aquila</i> eagles, possibly due to a shorter nesting stage in these species.<sup id="cite_ref-Simmons_90-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simmons-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> In roughly 20% of golden eagle nests and in some cases, such as prey-rich areas of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, about half of the nests will successfully produce two fledglings.<sup id="cite_ref-Watson_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In the Verreaux's eagle, no food is given to the hatchling in the first 36 hours, thereafter they are regularly fed.<sup id="cite_ref-BEstudy_2-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BEstudy-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Early in the fledging stage, the young is brooded up to 90% of the time. After 20 days, parents spend up to 20% of time with their eaglet around the nest, not brooding in daylight after 21 days. Feathers appear through the down at about 34 days, by 60 days feathers cover the down.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In Equatorial Africa, the eaglet fledges from the nest at 95–99 days, though sometimes as little as 90 days further south. In the golden eagle, the fledging stage is roughly 35 days shorter.<sup id="cite_ref-Leslie_23-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The young Verreaux's eagle takes its first flight and then returns to the nest for the first fortnight.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In the early fledging period, the male brings more food, later it is largely the female. At some point during the post-fledging stage, she ceases to roost with the young one and sits with the male at some distance, a behaviour that seems to vary from nest to nest in timing. After 45–50 days, food is caught by the parents but is likely eaten by themselves and not brought to the young. After leaving the nest, family parties may be together for up to 6 months. The eaglet grows stronger after the first month and accompanies the parents on hunting forays away from the nest. 
It often breeds every year, occasionally only on every other year.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_and_status">Population and status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verreaux%27s_eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Population and status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Verreaux%27s_Eagle_-_Giant_Castle_-_South-Africa_010003_(15523992061).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Verreaux%27s_Eagle_-_Giant_Castle_-_South-Africa_010003_%2815523992061%29.jpg/220px-Verreaux%27s_Eagle_-_Giant_Castle_-_South-Africa_010003_%2815523992061%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Verreaux%27s_Eagle_-_Giant_Castle_-_South-Africa_010003_%2815523992061%29.jpg/330px-Verreaux%27s_Eagle_-_Giant_Castle_-_South-Africa_010003_%2815523992061%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Verreaux%27s_Eagle_-_Giant_Castle_-_South-Africa_010003_%2815523992061%29.jpg/440px-Verreaux%27s_Eagle_-_Giant_Castle_-_South-Africa_010003_%2815523992061%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2198" data-file-height="1500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Verreaux%27s_Eagle_-_Giant_Castle_-_South-Africa_010003_(15523992061).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Verreaux's eagle at <a href="/wiki/Giant%27s_Castle" title="Giant&#39;s Castle">Giant's Castle</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Verreaux's eagles’ nests are estimated to have a 40-50% success rate.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Success of nesting is significantly higher when hyrax are common: 0.56 young per year dropped to 0.28 young per year. In fact, in poor food areas no breeding attempt commonly occurred (66% did not attempt to breed) whereas 24% did not attempt to breed in better food areas. Frequency of breeding attempts is lower in wetter years. Near 90% attempted nesting in years with 300&#160;mm (12&#160;in)  of rainfall whereas 45% attempted during years with roughly 1,000&#160;mm (39&#160;in) rainfall.<sup id="cite_ref-Gargett_84-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gargett-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> In the Matobo Hills, persistent intrusion by an unmated adult into a pair's nesting territory seemed to have an adverse effect on nesting success.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> The estimated average lifespan is 16 years.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown_9-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Verreaux's eagle has a total population estimated very roughly to be somewhere in the tens of thousands in total. In northeastern <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, the local breeding population is estimated to include 240 pairs, while the western Cape region of the country may hold possibly more than 2,000 pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-Tarboton_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tarboton-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Davies2_81-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies2-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> Verreaux's eagle lives in kopje habitat, which is generally non-vulnerable to human destruction, unlike, say, the savanna inhabited by martial eagles or the forests inhabited by crowned eagles.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Unlike the other two big African eagles, they do not often partake of much carrion, so are at little risk of poisoning from carcass left out to control <a href="/wiki/Jackal" title="Jackal">jackals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Nonetheless, some people shoot at or otherwise persecute them when given the opportunity due to the largely mistaken belief that they are a threat to small livestock.<sup id="cite_ref-R.A.G._68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R.A.G.-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> Perhaps the greatest concern for the species is when rock hyraxes are locally hunted by humans for food and skins, leading to likely declines and requiring the eagles to either switch to other prey or have their nesting attempts fail. In <a href="/wiki/Walter_Sisulu_National_Botanical_Garden" title="Walter Sisulu National Botanical Garden">Walter Sisulu National Botanical Garden</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, despite an appreciable decline in rock hyrax populations, breeding data revealed few inconsistencies with respect to the incubation period, nestling period and post fledging dispersal period in two known pairs of the eagles which were similar to established studies on Verreaux's eagles.<sup id="cite_ref-Kruger_62-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kruger-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> In the prior area, despite it being one of the most popular nature areas in metropolitan <a href="/wiki/Johannesburg" title="Johannesburg">Johannesburg</a>, high levels of human activity have shown to have no apparent adverse effect on the eagles’ breeding behaviour (when exposed to similar levels of disturbance, golden eagles have been shown to temporarily abandon their nests).<sup id="cite_ref-Watson_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Simmons2_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simmons2-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> However, in  South Africa, the overall number of pairs declined from 78 in 1980 (25 in reserves) to 27 in 1988 (with 19 in preserves).<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_15-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Walter_Sisulu_National_Botanical_Garden" title="Walter Sisulu National Botanical Garden">Walter Sisulu National Botanical Garden</a>, it has been wondered whether artificial feeding may be necessary in order to maintain a breeding pair in the face of continuing declines of available wild prey.<sup id="cite_ref-Simmons2_92-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simmons2-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
