<p><b>Verreaux's Eagle</b> (<i>Aquila verreauxii</i>), alternatively known as the <b>Black Eagle</b> (leading to confusion with the Asian <a href="/wiki/Black_Eagle" title="Black Eagle">Black Eagle</a>), is a large <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BEstudy_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BEstudy-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> This <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagle</a> lives in hilly and mountaineous regions of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">southern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Africa" title="Eastern Africa" class="mw-redirect">eastern Africa</a> (extending marginally into <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>), and very locally in <a href="/wiki/Western_Asia" title="Western Asia">Western Asia</a>. The name commemorates the French naturalist <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verreaux" title="Jules Verreaux">Jules Verreaux</a>, who visited <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">southern Africa</a> in the early 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mearns1988_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mearns1988-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Verreaux%27s_Eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>It is 75 to 96 cm (30 to 38 in) long. Males weigh 3 to 4.2 kg (6.6 to 9.3 lb) and females weigh 3.1 to 5.8 kg (6.8 to 13 lb). It has a wingspan of 1.81 to 2.2 m (5.9 to 7.2 ft).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> It is black with a distinct white V marking on its back.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Juveniles are usually light and dark brown with a black face. Structurally, it is very similar to the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Eagle" title="Golden Eagle">Golden Eagle</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Wedge-tailed_Eagle" title="Wedge-tailed Eagle">Wedge-tailed Eagle</a> of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Verreaux%27s_Eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Verreaux%27s_Eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<p>It is a specialist hunter of <a href="/wiki/Hyrax" title="Hyrax">hyraxes</a> (or dassies). The size of its territory often inversely reflects the size of the local hyrax population. At least occasionally, it will prey on birds including <a href="/wiki/Guineafowl" title="Guineafowl">guineafowl</a> or <a href="/wiki/Francolin" title="Francolin">francolin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> of similar size to hyraxes, such as large <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>. There are reports stating that the Verreaux's Eagle also preys on <a href="/wiki/Monkey" title="Monkey">monkeys</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Antelope" title="Antelope">antelope</a> and seldomly on <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">carrion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Maclean.3B_1993.3B_p._113_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maclean.3B_1993.3B_p._113-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> It hunts its prey by swooping around the corners of cliffs and thus surprising it prey by its sudden appearance. It also stoops from a perch; although this method is used less often.<sup id="cite_ref-Maclean.3B_1993.3B_p._113_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maclean.3B_1993.3B_p._113-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> A pair of Verreaux's Eagle may hunt cooperatively. This eagle on rare occasions may <a href="/wiki/Kleptoparasitism" title="Kleptoparasitism">pirate</a> food from other raptors, including <a href="/wiki/Bearded_Vulture" title="Bearded Vulture">Bearded Vulture</a> and other eagles.</p>

<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Verreaux%27s_Eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<p>It is highly territorial and can often be seen with another Verreaux's Eagle, with whom it mates for life. The pair will lay two cream-colored <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>, four days apart in autumn, and these will hatch approximately 45 days later. In Southern Africa the breeding season stretches from April to June, sometimes into August. Its nest is a huge stick-nest platform in the shape of a platform. The nest's diameter is about 1.5-2m. The 30–40&#160;cm diameter bowl is lined with green leaves. The nest is usually situated on a cliff ledge, rarely in a tree. The nest site is generally marked by a 'whitewash' which is formed by the birds' droppings.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
