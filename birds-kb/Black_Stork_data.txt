<p>The <b>black stork</b> (<i>Ciconia nigra</i>) is a large bird in the <a href="/wiki/Stork" title="Stork">stork</a> family Ciconiidae. It was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>. Measuring on average 95 to 100&#160;cm (37 to 39&#160;in) from beak tip to end of tail with a 145-to-155&#160;cm (57-to-61&#160;in) wingspan, the adult black stork has mainly black <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>, with white underparts, long red legs and a long pointed red beak. A widespread but uncommon species, it breeds in scattered locations across Europe (predominantly in Portugal and Spain, and central and eastern parts), and east across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> to the Pacific Ocean. It is a long-distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>, with European populations wintering in tropical Sub-Saharan Africa, and Asian populations in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>. When migrating between Europe and Africa, it avoids crossing the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> and detours via the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> in the east or the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Gibraltar" title="Strait of Gibraltar">Strait of Gibraltar</a> in the west. An isolated, non-migratory, population occurs in Southern Africa.
</p><p>Unlike the closely related <a href="/wiki/White_stork" title="White stork">white stork</a>, the black stork is a shy and wary species. It is seen singly or in pairs, usually in marshy areas, rivers or inland waters. It feeds on <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, generally wading slowly in shallow water stalking its prey. Breeding pairs usually build nests in large forest trees—most commonly <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> but also <a href="/wiki/Conifer" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifer">coniferous</a>—which can be seen from long distances, as well as on large boulders, or under overhanging ledges in mountainous areas. The female lays two to five greyish-white <a href="/wiki/Egg" title="Egg">eggs</a>, which become soiled over time in the nest. <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation" title="Egg incubation">Incubation</a> takes 32 to 38 days,&#160;with both sexes sharing duties, and <a href="/wiki/Fledging" class="mw-redirect" title="Fledging">fledging</a> takes 60 to 71 days.
</p><p>The black stork is considered to be a <a href="/wiki/Species_of_least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Species of least concern">species of least concern</a> by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a>, but its actual status is uncertain. Despite its large range, it is nowhere abundant, and it appears to be declining in parts of its range, such as in India, China and parts of Western Europe, though increasing in others such as the Iberian Peninsula. Various conservation measures have been taken for the black stork, like the Conservation Action Plan for African black storks by <a href="/wiki/Wetlands_International" title="Wetlands International">Wetlands International</a>. It is also protected under the <a href="/wiki/African-Eurasian_Waterbird_Agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="African-Eurasian Waterbird Agreement">African-Eurasian Waterbird Agreement</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_International_Trade_in_Endangered_Species_of_Wild_Fauna_and_Flora" class="mw-redirect" title="Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora">Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_etymology">Taxonomy and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>English naturalist <a href="/wiki/Francis_Willughby" title="Francis Willughby">Francis Willughby</a> wrote about the black stork in the 17th century, having seen one in Frankfurt. He named it <i>Ciconia nigra</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-willughby_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-willughby-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> words for "stork" and "black" respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-Lewis1918_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis1918-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It was one of the many <a href="/wiki/Aves_in_the_10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="Aves in the 10th edition of Systema Naturae">species originally described</a> by Swedish <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoologist</a> Carl Linnaeus in the landmark 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>, where it was given the binomial name of <i>Ardea nigra</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Linnaeus1758_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linnaeus1758-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It was moved to the new <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Ciconia" title="Ciconia">Ciconia</a></i> by French <a href="/wiki/Zoologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Zoologist">zoologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Mathurin_Jacques_Brisson" title="Mathurin Jacques Brisson">Mathurin Jacques Brisson</a> two years later.<sup id="cite_ref-Brisson1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brisson1-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The word <i>stork</i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> word <i>storc</i>, thought to be related to the <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> <i>storah</i>, meaning "stork", and the Old English <i>stearc</i>, meaning "stiff".<sup id="cite_ref-Merriam-Webster_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merriam-Webster-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Black_Stork_Manas_Assam_India_December_2018.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Black_Stork_Manas_Assam_India_December_2018.jpg/220px-Black_Stork_Manas_Assam_India_December_2018.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="275" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Black_Stork_Manas_Assam_India_December_2018.jpg/330px-Black_Stork_Manas_Assam_India_December_2018.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Black_Stork_Manas_Assam_India_December_2018.jpg/440px-Black_Stork_Manas_Assam_India_December_2018.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1293" data-file-height="1614" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Black_Stork_Manas_Assam_India_December_2018.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>From Manas Tiger Reserve, <a href="/wiki/Assam" title="Assam">Assam</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The black stork is a member of the genus <i>Ciconia</i>, or typical storks, a group of seven <a href="/wiki/Extant_taxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Extant taxon">extant</a> species, characterised by straight bills and mainly black and white <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kahl_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kahl-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The black stork was long thought to be most closely related to the <a href="/wiki/White_stork" title="White stork">white stork</a> (<i>C. ciconia</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-wood_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wood-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> However, genetic analysis via <a href="/wiki/DNA%E2%80%93DNA_hybridization" title="DNA–DNA hybridization">DNA–DNA hybridization</a> and mitochondrial <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome b</a> DNA by Beth Slikas in 1997 found that it was <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basal</a> (an early offshoot) in the genus <i>Ciconia</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Slikas1997_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Slikas1997-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">Fossil</a> remains have been recovered from <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> beds on <a href="/wiki/Rusinga_Island" title="Rusinga Island">Rusinga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maboko_Island" title="Maboko Island">Maboko Islands</a> in Kenya, which are indistinguishable from the white and black storks.<sup id="cite_ref-Dykes2008_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dykes2008-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ciconia_nigra_-Dierenrijk_Europa,_Nuenen,_Noord-Brabant,_Netherlands-8a.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ciconia_nigra_-Dierenrijk_Europa%2C_Nuenen%2C_Noord-Brabant%2C_Netherlands-8a.jpg/220px-Ciconia_nigra_-Dierenrijk_Europa%2C_Nuenen%2C_Noord-Brabant%2C_Netherlands-8a.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ciconia_nigra_-Dierenrijk_Europa%2C_Nuenen%2C_Noord-Brabant%2C_Netherlands-8a.jpg/330px-Ciconia_nigra_-Dierenrijk_Europa%2C_Nuenen%2C_Noord-Brabant%2C_Netherlands-8a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ciconia_nigra_-Dierenrijk_Europa%2C_Nuenen%2C_Noord-Brabant%2C_Netherlands-8a.jpg/440px-Ciconia_nigra_-Dierenrijk_Europa%2C_Nuenen%2C_Noord-Brabant%2C_Netherlands-8a.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3640" data-file-height="2427" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ciconia_nigra_-Dierenrijk_Europa,_Nuenen,_Noord-Brabant,_Netherlands-8a.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult in a Dutch zoo</div></div></div>
<p>The black stork is a large bird, measuring between 95 and 100&#160;cm (37 and 39&#160;in) in length with a 145-to-155&#160;cm (57-to-61&#160;in) wingspan,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977323_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977323-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and weighing around 3&#160;kg (6.6&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Standing as tall as 102&#160;cm (40&#160;in),<sup id="cite_ref-Stevenson2001_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stevenson2001-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> it has long red legs, a long neck and a long, straight, pointed red <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">beak</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977323_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977323-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> It bears some resemblance to <a href="/wiki/Abdim%27s_stork" title="Abdim&#39;s stork">Abdim's stork</a> (<i>C. abdimii</i>), which can be distinguished by its much smaller build, predominantly green bill, legs and feet, and white rump and lower back.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977323_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977323-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The plumage is black with a purplish green sheen, except for the white lower breast, belly, armpits, <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#axillaries" title="Glossary of bird terms">axillaries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Covert_feather" title="Covert feather">undertail coverts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977323_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977323-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:2_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The breast feathers are long and shaggy, forming a ruff which is used in some courtship displays.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977323_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977323-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The black stork has brown <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">irises</a>, and bare red skin around its eyes.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977323_14-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977323-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The sexes are identical in appearance, except that males are larger than females on average.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977323_14-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977323-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Moulting#Birds" title="Moulting">Moulting</a> takes place in spring, with the iridescent sheen brighter in new plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> It walks slowly and steadily on the ground and like all storks, it flies with its neck outstretched.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Juvenile_(organism)" title="Juvenile (organism)">juvenile</a> resembles the adult in plumage, but the areas corresponding to the adult black feathers are browner and less glossy. The scapulars, wing and upper tail coverts have pale tips. The legs, bill and bare skin around the eyes are greyish green.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977323_14-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977323-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> It could possibly be confused with the juvenile <a href="/wiki/Yellow-billed_stork" title="Yellow-billed stork">yellow-billed stork</a>, but the latter has paler wings and mantle, a longer bill and white under the wings.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977322_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977322-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Black_stork_(Ciconia_nigra).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Black_stork_%28Ciconia_nigra%29.jpg/220px-Black_stork_%28Ciconia_nigra%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Black_stork_%28Ciconia_nigra%29.jpg/330px-Black_stork_%28Ciconia_nigra%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Black_stork_%28Ciconia_nigra%29.jpg/440px-Black_stork_%28Ciconia_nigra%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2978" data-file-height="1985" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Black_stork_(Ciconia_nigra).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Black stork in flight</div></div></div>
<p>During the summer, the black stork is found from Eastern Asia (<a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> and northern China) west to <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a>, reaching Estonia in the north, Poland, <a href="/wiki/Lower_Saxony" title="Lower Saxony">Lower Saxony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> in Germany, the Czech Republic, Hungary, Italy and Greece in the south,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977323_14-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977323-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> with an outlying population in the central-southwest region of the Iberian Peninsula (<a href="/wiki/Extremadura" title="Extremadura">Extremadura</a> and surrounding provinces of Spain, plus Portugal).<sup id="cite_ref-canoalonso06_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-canoalonso06-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in tropical Africa and Asia, although certain populations of black storks are sedentary or dispersive.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> An isolated population exists in <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">Southern Africa</a>, where the species is more numerous in the east, in eastern South Africa and Mozambique, and is also found in Zimbabwe, Swaziland, Botswana and less commonly Namibia.<sup id="cite_ref-sabap2_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sabap2-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most of the black storks that summer in Europe migrate to Africa,<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> with those from western Germany and points west heading south via the Iberian Peninsula and the rest via Turkey and the Levant. Those flying via Spain spend winter in the <a href="/wiki/Fal%C3%A9m%C3%A9_River" title="Falémé River">Falémé River</a> basin of eastern Senegal, Guinea, southern Mauritania, Ivory Coast, Sierra Leone and western and central Mali, while those flying via the Sinai end up in northern Ethiopia, the <a href="/wiki/Kotto_River" title="Kotto River">Kotto River</a> basin in the Central African Republic, the <a href="/wiki/Mbomou_River" title="Mbomou River">Mbokou river basin</a> in Chad and northeastern Nigeria.<sup id="cite_ref-bobek08_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bobek08-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Black storks summering in <a href="/wiki/Western_Asia" title="Western Asia">western Asia</a> migrate to <a href="/wiki/North_India" title="North India">northern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northeast_India" title="Northeast India">northeastern</a> India,<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> ranging mainly from <a href="/wiki/Punjab,_India" title="Punjab, India">Punjab</a> south to <a href="/wiki/Karnataka" title="Karnataka">Karnataka</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-pande07_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pande07-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> They are occasional visitors to Sri Lanka.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Those summering further east in eastern Russia and China winter mainly in <a href="/wiki/South_China" title="South China">southern China</a>, and occasionally in Hong Kong, Myanmar, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Thailand" title="Northern Thailand">northern Thailand</a>, and Laos.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> They were first recorded in western Myanmar in 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The black stork prefers more wooded areas than the better-known white stork, and breeds in large marshy <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a> with interspersed <a href="/wiki/Conifer" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifer">coniferous</a> or <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">broadleaved</a> <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodlands</a>, but also inhabits hills and mountains with sufficient networks of <a href="/wiki/Creek_(stream)" class="mw-redirect" title="Creek (stream)">creeks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977323_14-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977323-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> It usually inhabits ponds, rivers, edges of lakes, estuaries and other freshwater wetlands.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The black stork does inhabit more agricultural areas in the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Depression" title="Caspian Depression">Caspian lowlands</a>, but even here it avoids close contact with people.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977323_14-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977323-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Its wintering habitat in India comprises reservoirs or rivers with nearby scrub or forest, which provide trees that black storks can roost in at night.<sup id="cite_ref-pande07_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pande07-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In southern Africa it is found in shallow water in rivers or lakes, or swamps, but is occasionally encountered on dry land.<sup id="cite_ref-sabap2_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sabap2-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After disappearing from Belgium before the onset of the 20th century, it has returned to breed in the <a href="/wiki/Ardennes" title="Ardennes">Belgian Ardennes</a>, Luxembourg and <a href="/wiki/Burgundy" title="Burgundy">Burgundy</a>, France, by 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-waza_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waza-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> It appears to be increasing in numbers in Spain and Portugal, where the population was estimated at 405 to 483 pairs in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-canoalonso06_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-canoalonso06-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The black stork is a rare vagrant to the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, turning up in the warmer months—particularly in spring—generally in the south and east. Sightings have become more common since the 1970s as its breeding range moves northwards.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> It has been recorded in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> six times between 1946 and 1983, including from Shetland, Orkney and the Highlands, as well as the Scottish Borders (<a href="/wiki/Peebles" title="Peebles">Peebles</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> It is not abundant in the western parts of its distribution, but more densely inhabits eastern <a href="/wiki/Transcaucasia" title="Transcaucasia">Transcaucasia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977323_14-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977323-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Further east, it has been recorded from locations across Iran, though little is known about its habits there; breeding has been recorded from near Aliabad in <a href="/wiki/Fars_Province" title="Fars Province">Fars province</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baft#Khabr_National_Park" title="Baft">Khabr National Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kerman_Province" title="Kerman Province">Kerman province</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karun" title="Karun">Karun</a> river in <a href="/wiki/Khuzestan_Province" title="Khuzestan Province">Khuzestan province</a>, Qaranqu River in <a href="/wiki/East_Azerbaijan_Province" title="East Azerbaijan Province">East Azarbaijan</a> province, and Aliabad river in <a href="/wiki/Razavi_Khorasan_Province" title="Razavi Khorasan Province">Razavi Khorasan province</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Qashqaei_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Qashqaei-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The population has declined in Iran due to draining of wetlands.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> East of the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural Mountains</a>, the black stork is patchily found in forested and mountainous areas up to <a href="/wiki/60th_parallel_north" title="60th parallel north">60°</a>‒<a href="/wiki/63rd_parallel_north" title="63rd parallel north">63° N</a> across Siberia to the Pacific Ocean. South of Siberia, it breeds in <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a>, northwestern China, northern Mongolia south to the <a href="/wiki/Altai_Mountains" title="Altai Mountains">Altai Mountains</a>, and northeastern China south to the vicinity of Beijing. In the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Peninsula" title="Korean Peninsula">Korean Peninsula</a>, the black stork is an uncommon summer visitor, no longer breeding in the south since 1966. Birds have been seen in the northeast but it is not known whether they breed there. Similarly it has been seen in the summer in Afghanistan, but its breeding status is uncertain.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration">Migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ciconia_nigra_Eurasian_Migration.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Ciconia_nigra_Eurasian_Migration.png/220px-Ciconia_nigra_Eurasian_Migration.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="279" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Ciconia_nigra_Eurasian_Migration.png/330px-Ciconia_nigra_Eurasian_Migration.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Ciconia_nigra_Eurasian_Migration.png/440px-Ciconia_nigra_Eurasian_Migration.png 2x" data-file-width="642" data-file-height="815" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ciconia_nigra_Eurasian_Migration.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b> Red line</b>: Migration border<br /> <b>Orange arrow</b>: Western migration<br /><b>Yellow arrow</b>: Eastern migration<br /><b>Blue</b>: Winter location</div></div></div>
<p>Migration takes place from early August to October, with a major exodus in September.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Some of the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian</a> populations, and also those in southern Africa, are essentially non-migratory, though they may wander freely in the non-breeding areas.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> A broad-winged soaring bird, the black stork is assisted by thermals of hot air for long-distance flight, although is less dependent on them than is the white stork.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:8_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Since thermals only form over land, the black stork, together with large <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptors</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:9_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> must cross the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> at the narrowest points, and many black storks travel south through the <a href="/wiki/Bosphorus" class="mw-redirect" title="Bosphorus">Bosphorus</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:8_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> as well as the Sinai and Gibraltar. The trip is around 5,667&#160;km (3,521&#160;mi) via the western route and 7,000&#160;km (4,300&#160;mi) via the eastern route, with satellite tracking yielding an average travel time of 37 and 80 days respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-bobek08_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bobek08-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The western route goes over the <a href="/wiki/Rock_of_Gibraltar" title="Rock of Gibraltar">Rock of Gibraltar</a> or over the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Gibraltar" title="Bay of Gibraltar">Bay of Gibraltar</a>, generally on a southwesterly track that takes them to the central part of the strait, from where they reach Morocco.<sup id="cite_ref-:9_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:3_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Many birds fly around the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> next to the coast.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> About 10% of the western storks choose the passage <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> – <a href="/wiki/Cap_Bon" class="mw-redirect" title="Cap Bon">Cap Bon</a>, Tunisia.<sup id="cite_ref-:9_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Spain contains several important areas—<a href="/wiki/Monfrag%C3%BCe" title="Monfragüe">Monfragüe</a> National Park, <a href="/wiki/Sierra_de_Gredos_Regional_Park" title="Sierra de Gredos Regional Park">Sierra de Gredos Regional Park</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Hunting_Reserve_in_C%C3%ADjara&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="National Hunting Reserve in Cíjara (page does not exist)">National Hunting Reserve in Cíjara</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_Park_of_the_Sierra_Hornachuelos&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Natural Park of the Sierra Hornachuelos (page does not exist)">Natural Park of the Sierra Hornachuelos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Do%C3%B1ana_National_Park" title="Doñana National Park">Doñana National Park</a>—where black storks stop over on the western migration route. Pesticide use has threatened birdlife in nearby Doñana. Further south, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Faguibine" title="Lake Faguibine">Lake Faguibine</a> in Mali is another stopover point but it has been affected by drought in recent years.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A wary species, the black stork avoids contact with people.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> It is generally found alone or in pairs, or in flocks of up to a hundred birds when migrating,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977326_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977326-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> or during winter.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The black stork has a wider range of calls than the white stork, its main call being a <i>chee leee</i>, which sounds like a loud inhalation. It makes a hissing call as a warning or threat.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977327_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977327-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Displaying males produce a long series of wheezy raptor-like squealing calls rising in volume and then falling.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> It rarely indulges in mutual bill-clattering when adults meet at the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Adults will do so as part of their mating ritual or when angered. The young clatter their bills when aroused.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977327_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977327-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i>up-down display</i> is used for a number of interactions with other members of the species. Here a stork positions its body horizontally and quickly bobs its head up from down-facing to around 30 degrees above horizontal and back again, while displaying the white segments of its plumage prominently, and this is repeated several times. The display is used as a greeting between birds, and—more vigorously—as a <a href="/wiki/Threat_display" class="mw-redirect" title="Threat display">threat display</a>. The species' solitary nature means that this threat display is rarely witnessed.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977326_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977326-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The black stork breeds between April and May in the Northern Hemisphere,<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> with eggs usually laid in late April.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> In southern Africa, breeding takes place in the months between September and March, possibly to take advantage of abundant water prey rendered easier to catch as the rivers dry up and recede—from April and May in Zimbabwe, Botswana and northern South Africa, and as late as July further south.<sup id="cite_ref-sabap2_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sabap2-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Pairs in courtship have aerial displays that appear to be unique among the storks. Paired birds soared in parallel, usually over the nest territory early in the mornings or late afternoons with one bird splaying the white undertail coverts to the sides of the narrowed black tail and the pair calls to each other. These courtship flights are difficult to see due to the densely forested habitat in which they breed.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> The nest is large, constructed from sticks and twigs, and sometimes also large branches, at an elevation of 4–25&#160;m (13–82&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The black stork prefers to construct its nest in forest trees with large canopies where the nest can be built far from the main trunk—generally in places far from human disturbance.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> For the most part, deciduous trees are chosen for nesting sites, though conifers are used as well.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977323_14-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977323-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> A 2003 field study in Estonia found that the black stork preferred oak (<i><a href="/wiki/Quercus_robur" title="Quercus robur">Quercus robur</a></i>), European aspen (<i><a href="/wiki/Populus_tremula" title="Populus tremula">Populus tremula</a></i>), and to a lesser extent Scots pine (<i><a href="/wiki/Pinus_sylvestris" class="mw-redirect" title="Pinus sylvestris">Pinus sylvestris</a></i>), and ignored Norway spruce (<i><a href="/wiki/Picea_abies" title="Picea abies">Picea abies</a></i>), in part due to the canopy structure of the trees. Trees with nests averaged around 25.6&#160;±&#160;5.2 metres (84&#160;±&#160;17&#160;ft) high and had a <a href="/wiki/Diameter_at_breast_height" title="Diameter at breast height">diameter at breast height</a> of 66&#160;±&#160;20 centimetres (26.0&#160;±&#160;7.9&#160;in). Furthermore, 90% of the trees chosen were at least 80 years old, highlighting the importance of conserving old-growth forests.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> A 2004 field study of nesting sites in Dadia-Lefkimi-Soufli National Park in north-eastern Greece found that it preferred the Calabrian pine (<i><a href="/wiki/Pinus_brutia" title="Pinus brutia">Pinus brutia</a></i>), which had large side branches that allowed it to build the nest away from the trunk, as well as black pine (<i><a href="/wiki/Pinus_nigra" title="Pinus nigra">Pinus nigra</a></i>) and to a lesser extent Turkey oak (<i><a href="/wiki/Quercus_cerris" title="Quercus cerris">Quercus cerris</a></i>). It chose the largest trees in an area, generally on steeper ground and near streams. Trees chosen were on average over 90 years old.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> In the Iberian peninsula it nests in pine and cork oak (<i><a href="/wiki/Quercus_suber" title="Quercus suber">Quercus suber</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-canoalonso06_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-canoalonso06-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>
In steeply mountainous areas such as parts of Spain, South Africa and the <a href="/wiki/Carpathian_Mountains" title="Carpathian Mountains">Carpathian Mountains</a> it nests on cliffs, on large boulders, in caves and under overhanging ledges.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977323_14-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977323-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The black stork's solitary nests are usually at least 1&#160;km (0.6&#160;mi) apart, even where the species is numerous.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977326_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977326-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Although newly constructed nests may be significantly smaller, older nests can be 1–2&#160;m (3.3–6.6&#160;ft) in diameter. In southern Africa, the black stork may occupy the nests of other bird species such as&#160;<a href="/wiki/Hamerkop" title="Hamerkop">hamerkop</a> (<i>Scopus umbretta</i>)&#160;or <a href="/wiki/Verreaux%27s_eagle" title="Verreaux&#39;s eagle">Verreaux's eagle</a> (<i>Aquila verreauxi</i>) and commonly reuses them in successive years.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> They are repaired with earth and grass, and lined with leaves, <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">moss</a>, grass, animal fur, paper, clay and rags.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup></p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ciconia_nigra_MWNH_0881.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Ciconia_nigra_MWNH_0881.JPG/220px-Ciconia_nigra_MWNH_0881.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ciconia_nigra_MWNH_0881.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div><p> In a clutch, there are two to five, or rarely even six large oval grey-white eggs, which become soiled during incubation.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> They can be 64–70&#160;mm (2.5–2.8&#160;in) long and 50–53&#160;mm (2.0–2.1&#160;in) wide, averaging about 68&#160;mm (2.7&#160;in) in length and 52&#160;mm (2.0&#160;in) in width.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>&#160;The eggs are laid with an interval of two days.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Hatching is asynchronous,<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and takes place at the end of May.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_44-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation" title="Egg incubation">Incubation</a>&#160;takes 32 to 38 days,&#160;with both sexes sharing duties, which commence after the first or second egg is laid.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The young start flying by the end of July.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_44-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fledging" class="mw-redirect" title="Fledging">Fledging</a> takes 60 to 71 days, after which the young joins the adults at their feeding grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> However, for another two weeks, the young continue to return to the nest, to be fed and to roost at night.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At least one adult remains in the nest for two to three weeks after hatching to protect the young. Both parents feed the young by regurgitating onto the floor of the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Black stork parents have been known to kill one of their fledglings, generally the weakest, in times of food shortage to reduce brood size and hence increase the chance of survival of the remaining nestlings. Stork nestlings do not attack each other, and their parents' method of feeding them (disgorging large amounts of food at once) means that stronger siblings cannot outcompete weaker ones for food directly, hence parental infanticide is an efficient way of reducing brood size. This behaviour has only rarely been observed in the species, although the shyness of the species and difficulties in studying its nesting habits mean that it might not be an uncommon phenomenon.<sup id="cite_ref-Zielinski2002_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zielinski2002-49">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ringing recovery studies in Europe suggests that nearly 20% of chicks reach the breeding stage, around 3 years, and about 10% live beyond 10 years and about 5% beyond 20 years. Captive individuals have lived for as long as 36 years.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The black stork mainly <a href="/wiki/Piscivore" title="Piscivore">eats fish</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> including small <a href="/wiki/Cyprinidae" title="Cyprinidae">cyprinids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Esox" title="Esox">pikes</a>, roaches, <a href="/wiki/Eel" title="Eel">eels</a>, budds, <a href="/wiki/Perch" title="Perch">perches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burbot" title="Burbot">burbots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stickleback" title="Stickleback">sticklebacks</a> and muddy <a href="/wiki/Cypriniformes" title="Cypriniformes">loaches</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Misgurnus" title="Misgurnus">Misgurnus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Cobitis" title="Cobitis">Cobitis</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-:5_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-51">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> It may feed on <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, and invertebrates such as <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mollusca" title="Mollusca">molluscs</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:4_44-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:5_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-51">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> like <a href="/wiki/Water_beetle" title="Water beetle">water beetles</a> and their <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_51-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-51">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_44-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Foraging for food takes place mostly in fresh water, though the black stork may look for food on dry land at times.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The black stork wades patiently and slowly in shallow water, often alone or in a small group if food is plentiful. It has been observed shading the water with its wings while hunting.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1977326_40-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1977326-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> In India, it often forages in mixed species flocks with the white stork, <a href="/wiki/Woolly-necked_stork" title="Woolly-necked stork">woolly-necked stork</a> (<i>Ciconia episcopus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Demoiselle_crane" title="Demoiselle crane">demoiselle crane</a> (<i>Grus virgo</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Bar-headed_goose" title="Bar-headed goose">bar-headed goose</a> (<i>Anser indicus</i>). The black stork also follows large mammals such as deer and livestock, presumably to eat the invertebrates and small animals flushed by their presence.<sup id="cite_ref-pande07_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pande07-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parasites_and_symbionts">Parasites and symbionts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Parasites and symbionts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>More than 12 species of parasitic <a href="/wiki/Helminth" class="mw-redirect" title="Helminth">helminth</a> have been recorded from black storks with <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cathaemasia_hians&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cathaemasia hians (page does not exist)">Cathaemasia hians</a></i> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dicheilonema_ciconiae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dicheilonema ciconiae (page does not exist)">Dicheilonema ciconiae</a></i> reported to be the most dominant. The juvenile black stork, although having a less diverse helminth population, is parasitized more frequently than the adult.<sup id="cite_ref-Sitko_&amp;_Heneberg_2015_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sitko_&amp;_Heneberg_2015-52">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> A species of <i><a href="/wiki/Corynebacterium" title="Corynebacterium">Corynebacterium</a></i>—<i>C. ciconiae</i>—was isolated and described from the trachea of healthy black storks, and is thought to be part of the natural flora of the species.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> A herpes virus is known from black storks.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Birdlice that have been recorded on the species include <i>Neophilopterus tricolor, Colpocephalum nigrae,</i> and <i>Ardeicola maculatus.</i><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> A diverse array of predatory <a href="/wiki/Mesostigmatid" class="mw-redirect" title="Mesostigmatid">mesostigmatid</a> mites—particularly the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Dendrolaelaps" title="Dendrolaelaps">Dendrolaelaps</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Macrocheles" title="Macrocheles">Macrocheles</a></i>—have been recovered from black stork nests. Their role is unknown, though they could prey on parasitic arthropods.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_and_conservation">Status and conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_stork&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Status and conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1998, the black stork has been rated as a species of&#160;<a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>&#160;on the&#160;<a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List_of_Endangered_Species" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN Red List of Endangered Species">IUCN Red List&#160;of Endangered Species</a>. This is because it has a large range—more than 20,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>&#160;(7,700&#160;mi<sup>2</sup>)—and because its population is thought not to have declined by 30% over ten years or three generations and thus is not a rapid enough decline to warrant a <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable</a> rating. Even so, the state of the population overall is unclear,<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and although it is widespread, it is not abundant anywhere.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Black stork numbers have declined for many years in western Europe, and the species has already been extirpated from <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The population in India—a major wintering ground—is declining.<sup id="cite_ref-pande07_26-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pande07-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Previously a regular winter visitor to the <a href="/wiki/Mai_Po_Marshes" title="Mai Po Marshes">Mai Po Marshes</a>, it is now seldom seen there, and appears to be in decline in China overall.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_19-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Its habitat is changing rapidly in much of eastern Europe and Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Various conservation measures have been taken, including <a href="/wiki/Wetlands_International" title="Wetlands International">Wetlands International</a>'s Conservation Action Plan for African black storks, which focuses on improving the wintering conditions of the birds which breed in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is protected by the <a href="/wiki/Agreement_on_the_Conservation_of_African-Eurasian_Migratory_Waterbirds" title="Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a> (AEWA) and the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_International_Trade_in_Endangered_Species_of_Wild_Fauna_and_Flora" class="mw-redirect" title="Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora">Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora</a> (CITES).<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hunters threaten the black stork in some countries of southern Europe and Asia,<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> such as Pakistan, and breeding populations may have been eliminated there.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The black stork vanished from the <a href="/wiki/Ticino_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Ticino River">Ticino River</a> valley in northern Italy, with hunting a likely contributor. In 2005, black storks were released into the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parco_Lombardo_del_Ticino&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Parco Lombardo del Ticino (page does not exist)">Parco Lombardo del Ticino</a> in an attempt to re-establish the species there.<sup id="cite_ref-waza_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waza-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
