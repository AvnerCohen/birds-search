<p>The <b>grey plover</b> or <b>black-bellied plover</b> (<i>Pluvialis squatarola</i>) is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">plover</a> breeding in <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> regions. It is a long-distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>, with a nearly worldwide <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coastal</a> distribution when not breeding.<sup id="cite_ref-hayman_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayman-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The genus name is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means relating to rain, from <i>pluvia</i>, "rain".  It was believed that golden plovers flocked when rain was imminent.  The species name <i> squatarola</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latinised</a> version of   <i>Sgatarola</i>, a Venetian name for some kind of plover.<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grey_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The English <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">common name</a> generally used for this species differs in different parts of the world. It is generally known as "grey plover" in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> and "black-bellied plover" in the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grey_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>They are 27–30&#160;cm (11–12&#160;in) long with a wingspan of 71–83&#160;cm (28–33&#160;in) and a weight of 190–280&#160;g (6.7–9.9&#160;oz) (up to 345&#160;g (12.2&#160;oz) in preparation for migration). In spring and summer (late April or May to August), the adults are spotted black and white on the back and wings. The face and neck are black with a white border; they have a black breast and belly and a white rump. The tail is white with black barring. The bill and legs are black. They moult to winter plumage in mid August to early September and retain this until April; this being a fairly plain grey above, with a grey-speckled breast and white belly. The juvenile and first-winter plumages, held by young birds from fledging until about one year old, are similar to the adult winter plumage but with the back feathers blacker with creamy white edging. In all plumages, the inner flanks and axillary feathers at the base of the underwing are black, a feature which readily distinguishes it from the other three <i><a href="/wiki/Pluvialis" title="Pluvialis">Pluvialis</a></i> species in flight. On the ground, it can also be told from the other <i>Pluvialis</i> species by its larger (24–34&#160;mm, 0.94–1.34&#160;in), heavier bill.<sup id="cite_ref-hayman_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayman-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding_and_migration">Breeding and migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grey_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Breeding and migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Their breeding habitat is <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> islands and coastal areas across the northern coasts of <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. They nest on the ground in a dry open <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> with good visibility; the nest is a shallow gravel scrape. Four eggs (sometimes only three) are laid in early June, with an incubation period of 26–27 days; the chicks fledge when 35–45 days old.<sup id="cite_ref-hayman_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayman-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>They migrate to winter in coastal areas throughout the world. In the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> they winter from southwest <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> south to <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, in the western <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> from <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and southwestern <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> south throughout coastal Africa to <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, and in the eastern Old World, from southern <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> south throughout coastal southern Asia and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, with a few reaching <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. Most of the migrants to Australia are female. It makes regular non-stop transcontinental flights over Asia, Europe, and North America, but is mostly a rare vagrant on the ground in the interior of continents, only landing occasionally if forced down by severe weather, or to feed on the coast-like shores of very large <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>, where it is a common passage migrant.<sup id="cite_ref-hayman_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayman-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ng_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ng-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Young birds do not breed until two years old; they typically remain on the wintering grounds until their second summer.<sup id="cite_ref-hayman_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayman-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grey_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>They forage for food on beaches and <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tidal</a> flats, usually by sight. The food consists of small <a href="/wiki/Mollusca" title="Mollusca">molluscs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polychaete" title="Polychaete">polychaete worms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>. It is less gregarious than the other <i>Pluvialis</i> species, not forming dense feeding flocks, instead feeding widely dispersed over beaches, with birds well spaced apart. They will however form dense flocks on high tide roosts.<sup id="cite_ref-hayman_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayman-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwpc_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grey_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The grey plover is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>
