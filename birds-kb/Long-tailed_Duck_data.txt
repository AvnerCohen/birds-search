<p>The <b>long-tailed duck</b> (<i>Clangula hyemalis</i>),  commonly known in North America as <b>oldsquaw</b>, is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Sea_duck" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea duck">sea duck</a>. It is the only living member of its <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a>, <i><b>Clangula</b></i>. This was formerly used for the <a href="/wiki/Goldeneye_(duck)" title="Goldeneye (duck)">goldeneyes</a>, with the long-tailed duck being placed in <i>Harelda</i>, but the latter is the type species of the genus.<sup id="cite_ref-job90_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job90-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> An undescribed <a href="/wiki/Conspecificity" class="mw-redirect" title="Conspecificity">congener</a> is known from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Miocene" title="Middle Miocene">Middle Miocene</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Saj%C3%B3v%C3%B6lgyi_Formation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sajóvölgyi Formation (page does not exist)">Sajóvölgyi Formation</a> (Late <a href="/w/index.php?title=Badenian&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Badenian (page does not exist)">Badenian</a>, 13–12 Mya) of <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1trasz%C5%91l%C5%91s" title="Mátraszőlős">Mátraszőlős</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gal1998_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gal1998-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Adults have white underparts, though the rest of the <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> goes through a complex moulting process. The male has a long pointed tail (10 to 15&#160;cm (3.9 to 5.9&#160;in) long) and a dark grey <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> crossed by a <a href="/wiki/Pink" title="Pink">pink</a> band. In winter, the male has a dark cheek patch on a mainly white head and neck, a dark breast and mostly white body. In summer, the male is dark on the head, neck and back with a white cheek patch. The female has a brown back and a relatively short pointed tail. In winter, the female's head and neck are white with a dark crown. In summer, the head is dark. Juveniles resemble adult females in autumn plumage, though with a lighter, less distinct cheek patch.
</p>

<p>The males are vocal and have a musical yodelling call <i>ow, ow, owal-ow</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-tailed_duck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Their breeding habitat is in <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> pools and marshes, but also along sea coasts and in large mountain lakes in the North Atlantic region, <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a> is located on the ground near water; it is built using vegetation and lined with down. They are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and winter along the eastern and western coasts of North America, on the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>, coastal northern Europe and Asia, with stragglers to the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>. The most important wintering area is the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>, where a total of about 4.5 million gather.
</p><p>The long-tailed duck is gregarious, forming large flocks in winter and during migration. They feed by diving for <a href="/wiki/Mollusk" class="mw-redirect" title="Mollusk">mollusks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> and some small <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>. Although they usually feed close to the surface, they are capable of diving to depths of 60&#160;m (200&#160;ft). According to the Audubon Society Field Guide to North American Birds they can dive to 80 fathoms (146 meters or 480 feet). They are the only ducks that use their wings to dive, which gives them the ability to dive much deeper than other ducks.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-tailed_duck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/North_American_English" title="North American English">North American English</a> it is sometimes called <i>oldsquaw</i>, though this name has fallen out of favour under influence of negative connotations of the word <a href="/wiki/Squaw" title="Squaw">squaw</a> in English usage. Some biologists have also feared that this name would be offensive to some <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_the_Americas" class="mw-redirect" title="Indigenous peoples in the Americas">Native American</a> tribes involved in the conservation effort.<sup id="cite_ref-squaw_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-squaw-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The American Ornithologists' Union stated that "<a href="/wiki/Political_correctness" title="Political correctness">political correctness</a>" was not sufficient to change the name, but "to conform with English usage in other parts of the world", it officially adopted the name "Long-tailed Duck".<sup id="cite_ref-AOU_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOU-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The scientific name is derived from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>clangere</i>, "to resound", and <i>hyemalis</i>, "of winter".<sup id="cite_ref-job90_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job90-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In 2020, following months of protests against racism, the <a href="/wiki/Bird_Names_for_Birds" title="Bird Names for Birds">Bird Names for Birds</a> campaign was created to urge the <a href="/wiki/American_Ornithological_Society" title="American Ornithological Society">American Ornithological Society</a> to address the issue of eponyms and honorific bird names with derogatory or oppressive implications.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long-tailed_duck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The long-tailed duck is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
