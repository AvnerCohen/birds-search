<p>The <b>Stone-curlews</b>, also known as <b>Dikkops</b> or <b>Thick-knees</b>, consist of nine species within the family <b>Burhinidae</b>, and are found throughout the tropical and temperate parts of the world, with two species found in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. Despite the group being classified as waders, most species have a preference for arid or semi-arid habitats.</p>
<p>They are medium to large birds with strong black or yellow black bills, large yellow eyes—which give them a reptilian appearance—and cryptic plumage. The names <i>Thick-knee</i> and <i>Stone-curlew</i> are both in common use, the preference among authorities for one term or the other varying from year to year. The term <i>Stone-curlew</i> owes its origin to the broad similarities with true <a href="/wiki/Curlew" title="Curlew">curlews</a> (which are not closely related). <i>Thick-knee</i> refers to the prominent joints in the long yellow or greenish legs and apparently originated with a name coined in 1776 for <i>B. oedicnemus</i>, the Thick-kneed Bustard.</p>
<p>They are largely nocturnal, particularly when singing their loud wailing songs, which are reminiscent of true curlews.<sup id="cite_ref-EoB_0-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoB-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The diet consists mainly of <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and other invertebrates. Larger species will also take lizards and even small mammals.<sup id="cite_ref-EoB_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoB-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Most species are sedentary, but the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Stone_Curlew" title="Eurasian Stone Curlew" class="mw-redirect">Eurasian Stone Curlew</a> is a summer <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a> in the temperate European part of its range, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.</p>
