<p>The <b>great snipe</b> (<i>Gallinago media</i>) is a small stocky <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Gallinago" title="Gallinago">Gallinago</a></i>. This bird's breeding habitat is marshes and wet meadows with short vegetation in north-eastern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, including north-western <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. Great snipes are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. The European breeding population is in steep decline.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The great snipe was described by the English naturalist <a href="/wiki/John_Latham_(ornithologist)" title="John Latham (ornithologist)">John Latham</a> in 1787 with the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Scolopax media</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The name of the current genus <i>Gallinago</i> is <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">New Latin</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Woodcock" title="Woodcock">woodcock</a> or <a href="/wiki/Snipe" title="Snipe">snipe</a> from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>gallina</i>, "hen" and the suffix <i>-ago</i>, "resembling". The specific <i>media</i> is Latin for "intermediate", because this species is intermediate in size between the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_woodcock" title="Eurasian woodcock">woodcock</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Common_snipe" title="Common snipe">common snipe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-job_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dubelt2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Dubelt2.jpg/220px-Dubelt2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Dubelt2.jpg/330px-Dubelt2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Dubelt2.jpg/440px-Dubelt2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2332" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dubelt2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In flight.</div></div></div>
<p>At 26–30&#160;cm (10–12&#160;in) in length and a 42–50&#160;cm (17–20&#160;in) wingspan, adults are only slightly larger, but much bulkier, than the <a href="/wiki/Common_snipe" title="Common snipe">common snipe</a> and have a shorter bill. The body is mottled brown on top and barred underneath. They have a dark stripe through the eye. The wings are broad, and a pale wingbar is visible in flight.
</p><p>The voice is described as a faint <i>yeah</i>. Mating display calls of groups can be heard at long distances (more than 300&#160;m (330&#160;yd)) and include a rising and falling series of chirping calls and accelerating clicking noises.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gallinago_media.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Gallinago_media.jpg/220px-Gallinago_media.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="264" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Gallinago_media.jpg/330px-Gallinago_media.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Gallinago_media.jpg/440px-Gallinago_media.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1102" data-file-height="1322" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gallinago_media.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Engraving from Naumann, 1905</div></div></div>
<p>The birds are noted for their fast, non-stop flying capabilities over huge distances.<sup id="cite_ref-bio-let_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bio-let-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> They can fly up to 97&#160;km/h (60&#160;mph), with researchers finding little evidence of wind assistance. Some have been recorded to fly non-stop for 84 hours over 6,760&#160;km (4,200&#160;mi). Their wings are not especially aerodynamic, lacking pointed tips, and they typically do not stop to feed despite having opportunities. The birds instead rely on stores of fat.<sup id="cite_ref-bio-let_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bio-let-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At dusk during the breeding season, the males display at a <a href="/wiki/Lek_mating" title="Lek mating">lek</a> (arena), standing erect with chest puffed and tail fanned out. They may jump into the air, and will produce a variety of rattles, clicks, buzzes and whistles while displaying. Three to four eggs are laid in a well-hidden nest on the ground.
</p><p>These birds forage in soft mud, probing or picking up food by sight. They mainly eat <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a>, and occasional plant material. They are difficult to see, being well camouflaged in their habitat. When flushed from cover, they fly straight for a considerable distance before dropping back into vegetation.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gallinago_media_MHNT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Gallinago_media_MHNT.jpg/170px-Gallinago_media_MHNT.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="180" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Gallinago_media_MHNT.jpg/255px-Gallinago_media_MHNT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Gallinago_media_MHNT.jpg/340px-Gallinago_media_MHNT.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3541" data-file-height="3740" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gallinago_media_MHNT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg of <i> Gallinago media</i> - (<a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9um_de_Toulouse" title="Muséum de Toulouse">Muséum de Toulouse</a>)</div></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossils">Fossils</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fossils">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fossils of the great snipe have been uncovered in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, dating back to about 4.465 Ma ±0.865M. This suggests that the bird must have at some point relocated across the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-paleodb_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-paleodb-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2012, there were estimated to be between 15,000 and 40,000 great snipe in Scandinavia and between 450,000 and 1,000,000 in western Siberia and northeastern Europe. The species is experiencing a population decline, owing primarily to habitat loss, as well as to hunting in eastern Europe and in its African wintering range. The species is classified by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> as "Near Threatened".<sup id="cite_ref-status_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-status-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The great snipe is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.<sup id="cite_ref-aewa_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aewa-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
