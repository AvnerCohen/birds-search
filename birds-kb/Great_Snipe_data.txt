<p>The <b>Great Snipe</b> (<i>Gallinago media</i>) is a small stocky <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Gallinago" title="Gallinago">Gallinago</a></i>.</p>
<p>This bird's breeding habitat is marshes and wet meadows with short vegetation in north-eastern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> including north-western <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. Great Snipes are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. The European breeding population is in steep decline.</p>
<p>The birds are noted for their fast, non-stop flying capabilities over huge distances. They can fly up to 97 kilometres per hour, with researchers finding little evidence of wind assistance. Some have been recorded to fly non-stop for 48 hours over 6,760 kilometres (4,200&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-migrations_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-migrations-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Their wings are not especially aerodynamic, lacking pointed tips, and they typically do not stop to feed despite having opportunities. The birds instead rely on stores of fat.<sup id="cite_ref-migrations_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-migrations-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The males display at a dusk <a href="/wiki/Lek_(animal_behavior)" title="Lek (animal behavior)" class="mw-redirect">lek</a> during the breeding season, standing erect with chest puffed and tail fanned. They may jump into the air. They produce a variety of rattles, clicks, buzzes and whistles while displaying. 3-4 eggs are laid in a nest in a well-hidden location on the ground.</p>
<p>These birds forage in soft mud, probing or picking up food by sight. They mainly eat <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a>, and occasional plant material. They are difficult to see, being well camouflaged in their habitat. When flushed from cover, they fly straight for a considerable distance before dropping back into vegetation.</p>
<p>At 26–30&#160;cm in length and a 42–50&#160;cm wingspan, adults are only slightly larger, but much bulkier, than <a href="/wiki/Common_Snipe" title="Common Snipe">Common Snipe</a> and have a shorter bill. The body is mottled brown on top and barred underneath. They have a dark stripe through the eye. The wings are broad, and a pale wingbar is visible in flight.</p>
<p>The Great Snipe is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Fossils">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Fossils">Fossils</span></h2>
<p>Fossils of the Great Snipe have been uncovered in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, dating back to about 4.465 Ma ±0.865M. This suggests the bird must have at some point relocated across the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-paleodb_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-paleodb-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
