<p>The <b>Eurasian Collared Dove</b> (<i>Streptopelia decaocto</i>) most often simply called the <b>Collared Dove</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-snow_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ebcc_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebcc-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cramp_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cramp-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ibercaja_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibercaja-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> also sometimes hyphenated as <b>Eurasian Collared-dove</b><sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> is a species of <a href="/wiki/Dove" title="Dove" class="mw-redirect">dove</a> native to Asia and Europe, and also recently introduced in North America.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Collared_Dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Collared_Dove_-upper_body_profile-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Collared_Dove_-upper_body_profile-8.jpg/220px-Collared_Dove_-upper_body_profile-8.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Collared_Dove_-upper_body_profile-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Profile of a Collared Dove.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It is a medium sized dove, distinctly smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Common_Wood_Pigeon" title="Common Wood Pigeon">Wood Pigeon</a>, similar in length to a <a href="/wiki/Rock_Pigeon" title="Rock Pigeon" class="mw-redirect">Rock Pigeon</a> but slimmer and longer-tailed, and slightly larger than the related <a href="/wiki/Turtle_Dove" title="Turtle Dove" class="mw-redirect">Turtle Dove</a>, 30–33&#160;cm long from tip of beak to tip of tail, with a wingspan of 47–55&#160;cm, and a weight of 125–240 g. It is grey-buff to pinkish-grey overall, a little darker above than below, with a blue-grey under wing patch. The tail feathers are grey-buff above, and dark grey tipped white below; the outer tail feathers also tipped whitish above. It has a black half-collar edged with white on its nape from which it gets its name. The short legs are red and the bill is black. The iris is red, but from a distance the eyes appear to be black, as the pupil is relatively large and only a narrow rim of reddish-brown iris can be seen around the black pupil. The eye is surrounded by a small area of bare skin, which is either white or yellow. The two sexes are virtually indistinguishable; juveniles differ in having a poorly developed collar, and a brown iris.<sup id="cite_ref-snow_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cramp_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cramp-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ibercaja_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibercaja-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Island_Collared_Dove" title="Island Collared Dove">Island Collared Dove</a> of southeast Asia and the <a href="/wiki/African_Collared_Dove" title="African Collared Dove">African Collared Dove</a> of sub-Saharan Africa, forming a <a href="/wiki/Superspecies" title="Superspecies">superspecies</a> with these.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Identification from African Collared Dove is very difficult with silent birds, with the African species being marginally smaller and paler, but the calls are very distinct, a soft purring in African Collared Dove quite unlike the Eurasian Collared Dove's cooing.<sup id="cite_ref-snow_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Collared_Dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Streptopelia_decaocto_-Gran_Canaria,_Canary_Islands,_Spain_-juvenile-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Streptopelia_decaocto_-Gran_Canaria%2C_Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-juvenile-8.jpg/220px-Streptopelia_decaocto_-Gran_Canaria%2C_Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-juvenile-8.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Streptopelia_decaocto_-Gran_Canaria,_Canary_Islands,_Spain_-juvenile-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Juvenile before collar formation</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Streptopelia_decaocto_zoom.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Streptopelia_decaocto_zoom.jpg/220px-Streptopelia_decaocto_zoom.jpg" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Streptopelia_decaocto_zoom.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Juvenile with early collar development</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Collared Dove is not <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, but is strongly dispersive. Over the last century, it has been one of the great colonisers of the bird world. Its original range at the end of the 19th century was warm temperate and subtropical Asia from Turkey east to southern China and south through India to Sri Lanka. In 1838 it was reported in Bulgaria, but not until the 20th century did it expand across Europe, appearing in parts of the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a> between 1900–1920, and then spreading rapidly northwest, reaching Germany in 1945, Great Britain by 1953 (breeding for the first time in 1956), Ireland in 1959, and the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a> in the early 1970s. Subsequent spread was 'sideways' from this fast northwest spread, reaching northeast to north of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic Circle</a> in Norway and east to the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural Mountains</a> in Russia, and southwest to the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> and northern Africa from Morocco to Egypt, by the end of the 20th century. In the east of its range, it has also spread northeast to most of central and northern China, and locally (probably introduced) in Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-snow_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ebcc_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebcc-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cramp_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cramp-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> It has also reached Iceland as a vagrant (41 records up to 2006), but has not colonised successfully there.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>There are two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>, <i>Streptopelia decaocto decaocto</i> in most of the range (including all of the 20th century colonisations), and <i>Streptopelia decaocto xanthocyclus</i> in the southeast of the range from Burma east to southern China. The latter differs in having yellow skin around the eye (white in the nominate subspecies).<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Two other subspecies formerly sometimes accepted, <i>Streptopelia decaocto stoliczkae</i> from Turkestan in central Asia, and <i>Streptopelia decaocto intercedens</i> from southern India and Sri Lanka,<sup id="cite_ref-cramp_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cramp-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> are now considered synonyms of <i>S. d. decaocto</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Collared_Dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Invasive species">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Invasive_species">Invasive species</span></h3>
<p>The Collared Dove was introduced into the Bahamas in the 1970s and spread from there to <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> by 1982. It has become <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> the stronghold in North America is still the Gulf Coast, but it is now found as far south as <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">Veracruz</a>, as far west as <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, and as far north as <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>. Some of the more distantly dispersed records may refer to local escapes from captivity.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Its impact on other species there is as yet unknown; it appears to occupy an <a href="/wiki/Ecological_niche" title="Ecological niche">ecological niche</a> between that of the <a href="/wiki/Mourning_Dove" title="Mourning Dove">Mourning Dove</a> and the Rock Pigeon (also an invasive species in North America).<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from March 2012">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Collared_Dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<i>Streptopelia decaocto</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Collared Doves typically breed close to human habitation wherever food resources are abundant and there are trees for nesting; almost all nests are within a kilometre of inhabited buildings. The female lays two white <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> in a stick nest, which she incubates during the night and which the male incubates during the day. Incubation lasts between fourteen and eighteen days, with the young <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledging</a> after fifteen to nineteen days. Breeding occurs throughout the year when abundant food is available, though only rarely in winter in areas with cold winters such as northeastern Europe. Three to four broods per year is common, although up to six broods in a year has been recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-cramp_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cramp-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The male's mating display is a ritual flight, which, as many other pigeons, consists of a rapid, near-vertical climb to height followed by a long glide downward in a circle, with the wings held below the body in an inverted "V" shape. At all other times, flight is typically direct using fast and clipped wingbeats and without use of gliding.</p>
<p>The Collared Dove is not wary and often feeds very close to human habitation, including visiting bird tables; the largest populations are typically found around farms where spilt grain is frequent around grain stores or where livestock are fed. It is a gregarious species and sizable winter flocks will form where there are food supplies such as grain (its main food) as well as seeds, shoots and insects. Flocks most commonly number between ten and fifty, but flocks of up to ten thousand have been recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-cramp_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cramp-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The song is a <i>coo-COO-coo</i>, repeated many times. It is phonetically similar to the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>decaocto</i> ("eighteen"), to which the bird owes its zoological name. It also makes a harsh loud screeching call lasting about two seconds, particularly in flight just before landing. A rough way to describe the screeching sound is a <i>hah-hah.</i></p>
<p>Collared Doves cooing in early spring are sometimes mistakenly reported as the calls of early-arriving <a href="/wiki/Common_Cuckoo" title="Common Cuckoo">Cuckoos</a> and, as such, a mistaken sign of spring's return.<sup id="cite_ref-cramp_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cramp-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>They are almost always seen in pairs and, like many birds, remain loyal to their mates.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from June 2012">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
