<p>The <b>Eurasian collared dove</b> (<i>Streptopelia decaocto</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Dove" class="mw-redirect" title="Dove">dove</a> species native to Europe and Asia; it was introduced to Japan, North America and islands in the Caribbean. Because of its vast global range and increasing population trend, it has been listed as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> since 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_collared_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Columba decaocto</i> was the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific name">scientific name</a> proposed by the Hungarian naturalist <a href="/wiki/Imre_Frivaldszky" title="Imre Frivaldszky">Imre Frivaldszky</a> in 1838 who <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> a Eurasian collared dove.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Type_locality_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Type locality (biology)">type locality</a> is <a href="/wiki/Plovdiv" title="Plovdiv">Plovdiv</a> in Bulgaria.<sup id="cite_ref-fisher_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fisher-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is now placed in <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Streptopelia" title="Streptopelia">Streptopelia</a></i> that was introduced in 1855 by the French ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lucien_Bonaparte" title="Charles Lucien Bonaparte">Charles Lucien Bonaparte</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ioc_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are recognised:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>western collared dove</b> (<i>S. d. decaocto</i>) ranges from Europe to India, northeastern China and Korea</li>
<li><b>Burmese collared dove</b> (<i>S. d. xanthocycla</i>) (<a href="/wiki/Oliver_Michael_Griffiths_Newman" title="Oliver Michael Griffiths Newman">Newman</a>, 1906) – central Myanmar (Shan States) to southern China (Yunnan) and eastern China</li></ul>
<p>The Burmese collared dove is considered as a distinct species by some authors.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbwburma_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwburma-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Two other subspecies were formerly sometimes accepted, <i>S. d. stoliczkae</i> from Turkestan in central Asia and <i>S. d. intercedens</i> from southern India and Sri Lanka.<sup id="cite_ref-cramp_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cramp-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> They are now considered junior synonyms of the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> (<i>S. d. decaocto</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Eurasian collared dove is closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Sunda_collared_dove" title="Sunda collared dove">Sunda collared dove</a> of <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/African_collared_dove" title="African collared dove">African collared dove</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>, forming a <a href="/wiki/Superspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Superspecies">superspecies</a> with these.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Identification from the African collared dove is very difficult with silent birds, with the African species being marginally smaller and paler, but the calls are very distinct, a soft purring in the African collared dove quite unlike the Eurasian collared dove's cooing.<sup id="cite_ref-snow_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_collared_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The generic name is from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>streptos</i> meaning "collar" and <i>peleia</i> meaning "dove";
the <a href="/wiki/Specific_epithet" class="mw-redirect" title="Specific epithet">specific epithet</a> is Latin for "eighteen".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The number comes from a Greek myth. A maid was unhappy that she was only paid 18 pieces a year and begged the gods to let the world know how little she was rewarded by her mistress. Thereupon Zeus created this dove that has called out "Deca-octo" ever since.<sup id="cite_ref-fisher_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fisher-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_collared_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Collared_Dove_-upper_body_profile-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Collared_Dove_-upper_body_profile-8.jpg/220px-Collared_Dove_-upper_body_profile-8.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Collared_Dove_-upper_body_profile-8.jpg/330px-Collared_Dove_-upper_body_profile-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Collared_Dove_-upper_body_profile-8.jpg/440px-Collared_Dove_-upper_body_profile-8.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Collared_Dove_-upper_body_profile-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Profile of a Eurasian collared dove</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:A_paif_of_Eurasian_collared_dove_(Streptopelia_decaocto)_Photograph_by_Shantanu_Kuveskar.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/A_paif_of_Eurasian_collared_dove_%28Streptopelia_decaocto%29_Photograph_by_Shantanu_Kuveskar.jpg/220px-A_paif_of_Eurasian_collared_dove_%28Streptopelia_decaocto%29_Photograph_by_Shantanu_Kuveskar.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/A_paif_of_Eurasian_collared_dove_%28Streptopelia_decaocto%29_Photograph_by_Shantanu_Kuveskar.jpg/330px-A_paif_of_Eurasian_collared_dove_%28Streptopelia_decaocto%29_Photograph_by_Shantanu_Kuveskar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/A_paif_of_Eurasian_collared_dove_%28Streptopelia_decaocto%29_Photograph_by_Shantanu_Kuveskar.jpg/440px-A_paif_of_Eurasian_collared_dove_%28Streptopelia_decaocto%29_Photograph_by_Shantanu_Kuveskar.jpg 2x" data-file-width="750" data-file-height="1000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:A_paif_of_Eurasian_collared_dove_(Streptopelia_decaocto)_Photograph_by_Shantanu_Kuveskar.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A pair of Eurasian collared doves from Mangaon, Maharashtra, India</div></div></div>
<p>It is a medium-sized dove, distinctly smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Common_wood_pigeon" title="Common wood pigeon">wood pigeon</a>, similar in length to a <a href="/wiki/Rock_pigeon" class="mw-redirect" title="Rock pigeon">rock pigeon</a> but slimmer and longer-tailed, and slightly larger than the related <a href="/wiki/European_turtle_dove" title="European turtle dove">European turtle dove</a>, with an average length of 32&#160;cm (13&#160;in)<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell_2000_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell_2000-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> from tip of beak to tip of tail, with a wingspan of 47–55&#160;cm (19–22&#160;in), and a weight of 125–240&#160;g (4.4–8.5&#160;oz). It is grey-buff to pinkish-grey overall, a little darker above than below, with a blue-grey underwing patch. The tail feathers are grey-buff above, and dark grey and tipped white below; the outer tail feathers are also tipped whitish above. It has a black half-collar edged with white on its nape from which it gets its name. The short legs are red and the bill is black. The iris is red, but from a distance the eyes appear to be black, as the pupil is relatively large and only a narrow rim of reddish-brown iris can be seen around the black pupil. The eye is surrounded by a small area of bare skin, which is either white or yellow. The two sexes are virtually indistinguishable; juveniles differ in having a poorly developed collar, and a brown iris.<sup id="cite_ref-snow_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cramp_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cramp-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The subspecies <i>S. d. xanthocycla</i> differs in having yellow rather than white eye-rings,  darker grey on the head and the underparts a slightly darker pink.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwburma_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwburma-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The song is a <i>goo-GOO-goo</i>. The Eurasian collared dove also makes a harsh loud screeching call lasting about two seconds, particularly in flight just before landing. A rough way to describe the screeching sound is a <i>hah-hah.</i>
</p><p>Eurasian collared doves cooing in early spring are sometimes mistakenly reported as the calls of early-arriving <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common cuckoos</a> and, as such, a mistaken sign of spring's return.<sup id="cite_ref-cramp_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cramp-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_collared_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Streptopelia_decaocto_-Gran_Canaria,_Canary_Islands,_Spain_-juvenile-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Streptopelia_decaocto_-Gran_Canaria%2C_Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-juvenile-8.jpg/220px-Streptopelia_decaocto_-Gran_Canaria%2C_Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-juvenile-8.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Streptopelia_decaocto_-Gran_Canaria%2C_Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-juvenile-8.jpg/330px-Streptopelia_decaocto_-Gran_Canaria%2C_Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-juvenile-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Streptopelia_decaocto_-Gran_Canaria%2C_Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-juvenile-8.jpg/440px-Streptopelia_decaocto_-Gran_Canaria%2C_Canary_Islands%2C_Spain_-juvenile-8.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3286" data-file-height="2191" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Streptopelia_decaocto_-Gran_Canaria,_Canary_Islands,_Spain_-juvenile-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Juvenile before collar formation</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Streptopelia_decaocto_zoom.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Streptopelia_decaocto_zoom.jpg/220px-Streptopelia_decaocto_zoom.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Streptopelia_decaocto_zoom.jpg/330px-Streptopelia_decaocto_zoom.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Streptopelia_decaocto_zoom.jpg/440px-Streptopelia_decaocto_zoom.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2313" data-file-height="1476" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Streptopelia_decaocto_zoom.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Juvenile with early collar development</div></div></div>
<p>The Eurasian collared dove is not <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, but is strongly <a href="/wiki/Biological_dispersal" title="Biological dispersal">dispersive</a>. Over the last century, it has been one of the great colonisers of the bird world, travelling far beyond its native range to colonise colder countries, becoming a permanent resident in several of them. Its original range at the end of the 19th century was warm temperate and subtropical Asia from Turkey east to southern China and south through India to Sri Lanka. In 1838 it was reported in Bulgaria, but not until the 20th century did it expand across Europe, appearing in parts of the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a> between 1900–1920, and then spreading rapidly northwest, reaching Germany in 1945, Great Britain by 1953 (breeding for the first time in 1956), Ireland in 1959, and the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a> in the early 1970s. Subsequent spread was 'sideways' from this fast northwestern spread, reaching northeast to north of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic Circle</a> in Norway and east to the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural Mountains</a> in Russia, and southwest to the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> and northern Africa from Morocco to Egypt, by the end of the 20th century. In the east of its range, it has also spread northeast to most of central and northern China, and locally (probably introduced) in Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-snow_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snow-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ebcc_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebcc-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cramp_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cramp-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> It has also reached Iceland as a vagrant (41 records up to 2006), but has not colonised successfully there.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Invasive_status_in_North_America">Invasive status in North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_collared_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Invasive status in North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1974, fewer than 50 Eurasian collared doves escaped captivity in Nassau, New Providence, Bahamas.<sup id="cite_ref-what_to_do!_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-what_to_do!-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  From the Bahamas, the species spread to Florida,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and is now found in nearly every state in the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-eurasian_collared_dove_disease_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eurasian_collared_dove_disease-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> as well as in Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-new_records_guerro_mexico_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-new_records_guerro_mexico-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>  In Arkansas (the United States), the species was recorded first in 1989 and since then has grown in numbers and is now present in 42 of 75 counties in the state. It spread from the southeastern corner of the state in 1997 to the northwestern corner in five years, covering a distance of about 500&#160;km (310&#160;mi) at a rate of 100&#160;km (62&#160;mi) per year.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> This is more than double the rate of 45&#160;km (28&#160;mi) per year observed in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>  As of 2012, few negative impacts have been demonstrated in Florida, where the species is most prolific.<sup id="cite_ref-floridas_introduced_birds_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-floridas_introduced_birds-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  However, the species is known as an aggressive competitor and there is concern that as populations continue to grow, native birds will be out-competed by the invaders.<sup id="cite_ref-floridas_introduced_birds_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-floridas_introduced_birds-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> However, one study found that Eurasian collared doves are not more aggressive or competitive than native <a href="/wiki/Mourning_dove" title="Mourning dove">mourning doves</a>, despite similar dietary preferences.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Streptopelia_decaocto;_Szczecin,_Poland_3.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Streptopelia_decaocto%3B_Szczecin%2C_Poland_3.JPG/220px-Streptopelia_decaocto%3B_Szczecin%2C_Poland_3.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Streptopelia_decaocto%3B_Szczecin%2C_Poland_3.JPG/330px-Streptopelia_decaocto%3B_Szczecin%2C_Poland_3.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Streptopelia_decaocto%3B_Szczecin%2C_Poland_3.JPG/440px-Streptopelia_decaocto%3B_Szczecin%2C_Poland_3.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2848" data-file-height="2136" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Streptopelia_decaocto;_Szczecin,_Poland_3.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eurasian collared doves can become hand-tame in urban areas. Photograph taken in <a href="/wiki/Szczecin" title="Szczecin">Szczecin</a>, Poland</div></div></div>
<p>Population growth has ceased in areas where the species has long been established, such as Florida, and in these regions recent observations suggest the population is in decline.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The population is still growing exponentially in areas of more recent introduction. <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">Carrying capacities</a> appear to be highest in areas with higher temperatures and intermediate levels of development, such as suburban areas and some agricultural areas.<sup id="cite_ref-Scheidt_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scheidt-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While the spread of disease to native species has not been recorded in a study, Eurasian collared doves are known carriers of the parasite <i><a href="/wiki/Trichomonas_gallinae" title="Trichomonas gallinae">Trichomonas gallinae</a></i> as well as <a href="/wiki/Pigeon_paramyxovirus_type_1" class="mw-redirect" title="Pigeon paramyxovirus type 1">pigeon paramyxovirus type 1</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-eurasian_collared_dove_disease_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eurasian_collared_dove_disease-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-floridas_introduced_birds_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-floridas_introduced_birds-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Both <i>Trichomonas gallinae</i> and pigeon paramyxovirus type 1 can spread to native birds via commingling at feeders and by consumption of doves by predators. Pigeon paramyxovirus type 1 is an emergent disease and has the potential to affect domestic poultry, making the Eurasian collared dove a threat to not only native biodiversity, but a possible economic threat as well.<sup id="cite_ref-eurasian_collared_dove_disease_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eurasian_collared_dove_disease-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_collared_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Eurasian collared doves typically breed close to human habitation wherever food resources are abundant and there are trees for nesting; almost all nests are within 1&#160;km (0.62&#160;mi) of inhabited buildings. The female lays two white <a href="/wiki/Egg" title="Egg">eggs</a> in a stick nest, which she incubates during the night and which the male incubates during the day. Incubation lasts between 14 and 18 days, with the young <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledging</a> after 15 to 19 days. Breeding occurs throughout the year when abundant food is available, though only rarely in winter in areas with cold winters such as northeastern Europe. Three to four broods per year is common, although up to six broods in a year has been recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-cramp_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cramp-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  Eurasian collared doves are a monogamous species, and share parental duties when caring for young.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The male's mating display is a ritual flight, which, as with many other pigeons, consists of a rapid, near-vertical climb to height followed by a long glide downward in a circle, with the wings held below the body in an inverted "V" shape. At all other times, flight is typically direct using fast and clipped wing beats and without use of gliding.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_feeding">Food and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_collared_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Food and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Eurasian collared dove is not wary and often feeds very close to human habitation, including visiting bird tables; the largest populations are typically found around farms where spilt grain is frequent around grain stores or where livestock are fed. It is a gregarious species and sizeable winter flocks will form where there are food supplies such as grain (its main food) as well as seeds, shoots and insects. Flocks most commonly number between 10 and 50, but flocks of up to 10,000 have been recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-cramp_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cramp-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
