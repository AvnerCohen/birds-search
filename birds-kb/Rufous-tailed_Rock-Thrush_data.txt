<p>The <b>Common Rock Thrush</b> (<i>Monticola saxatilis</i>),<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> formerly <b>Rufour-tailed Rock Thrush</b> or <b>Rock Thrush</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Chat_(bird)" title="Chat (bird)">chat</a> belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Muscicapidae" title="Muscicapidae" class="mw-redirect">Muscicapidae</a> family. It was formerly placed in the <a href="/wiki/Turdidae" title="Turdidae" class="mw-redirect">Turdidae</a> family.</p>
<p>It breeds in southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> across central <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> to northern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. This species is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, all populations wintering in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> south of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>. It is an uncommon visitor to northern Europe. Its range has contracted somewhat at the periphery in recent decades due to <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a>. For example, in the early 20th century it bred in the <a href="/wiki/Jura_Krakowsko-Cz%C4%99stochowska" title="Jura Krakowsko-Częstochowska" class="mw-redirect">Jura Krakowsko-Częstochowska</a> (<a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>) where none occur today,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> but it is not considered globally <a href="/wiki/Endangered" title="Endangered" class="mw-redirect">endangered</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This is a medium-sized but stocky thrush 17–20&#160;cm in length. The summer male is unmistakable, with a blue-grey head, orange underparts and outer tail feathers, dark brown wings and white back. Females and immatures are much less striking, with dark brown scaly upperparts, and paler brown scaly underparts. The outer tail feathers are reddish, like the male.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species breeds in open dry hilly areas, usually above 1500&#160;m. It nests in rock cavities, laying 4–5 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>. It is omnivorous, eating a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Insects" title="Insects" class="mw-redirect">insects</a>, berries and small reptiles. The male Common Rock Thrush has a clear and tuneful song.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
