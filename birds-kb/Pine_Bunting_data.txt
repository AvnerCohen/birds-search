<p>The <b>pine bunting</b> (<i>Emberiza leucocephalos</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bunting_(bird)" title="Bunting (bird)">bunting</a> family Emberizidae, a group most modern authors now separate from the <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finches</a>, Fringillidae.  It lives in Eurosiberia east of the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Urals</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pine_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The genus name <i>Emberiza</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old German</a> <i>Embritz</i>, a bunting. The specific <i>leucocephalos</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>leukos</i>, "whites", and <i>kephalos</i>, "headed".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_song">Description and song</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pine_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description and song">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The pine bunting is a robust 16–17.5-centimetre bird, with a thick <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seed</a>-eater's <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a>. The male has a white crown and cheeks, and a chestnut forehead and throat, and a heavily streaked brown back. The female is much duller and is more streaked on its undersides. Non-breeding <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> is like that of a yellowhammer, but with all the yellow replaced by white. Its song and calls are like those of the yellowhammer.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding_and_habitat">Breeding and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pine_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Breeding and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The pine bunting breeds across much of <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">temperate Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrating</a> south to central Asia, north India and southern China in winter. It is common in all sorts of open land with some scrub or trees, including cultivation, but has a greater preference for open forest (usually <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pines</a>) than the closely related <a href="/wiki/Yellowhammer" title="Yellowhammer">yellowhammer</a>. It is a rare vagrant to western Europe, but often winters in north-east Italy and <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="Appearance_of_pine_bunting_.C3.97_yellowhammer_hybrids"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance_of_pine_bunting_×_yellowhammer_hybrids">Appearance of pine bunting × yellowhammer hybrids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pine_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Appearance of pine bunting × yellowhammer hybrids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hybrids between pine bunting and yellowhammer show a mixture of characters. One such bird, a <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_in_birds" class="mw-redirect" title="Vagrancy in birds">vagrant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> in 1982, the "Sizewell bunting", is documented and illustrated with photographs in <i><a href="/wiki/British_Birds_(magazine)" title="British Birds (magazine)">British Birds</a></i> <sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some doubt has been cast upon male birds which appear to all intents and purposes to be pure pine buntings, but show yellow primary fringes. Previously, in Britain, these were regarded as potentially hybrid birds, and not accepted by the <a href="/wiki/British_Birds_Rarities_Committee" title="British Birds Rarities Committee">British Birds Rarities Committee</a>. However, since 2004, BBRC has regarded these birds as acceptable if they also meet the following conditions:<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>the lores must be chestnut, not black or grey</li>
<li>the throat must be extensively chestnut coloured, without a dark malar line or pale submoustachial line</li>
<li>the supercilium should be chestnut or grey, but not white</li>
<li>there should be no yellow on the head, or anywhere else except the primary fringes</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pine_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The pine bunting's natural food consists seeds, and when feeding young, insects. The nest is on the ground. Four to six <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> are laid, which show the hair-like markings characteristic of buntings.
</p>
