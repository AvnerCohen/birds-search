<p>The <b>Crested Honey Buzzard</b>, <i>Pernis ptilorhynchus</i>, is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a> which also includes many other diurnal <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptors</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Kite_(bird)" title="Kite (bird)">kites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harrier_(bird)" title="Harrier (bird)">harriers</a>. This species is also known as the <b>Oriental Honey Buzzard</b>.</p>

<p>Despite its name, this species is not related to <i>Buteo</i> <a href="/wiki/Buzzard" title="Buzzard">buzzards</a>, and is taxonomically closer to the kites.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from September 2008">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It appears long-necked with a small head (resembling that of a pigeon), and soars on flat wings. The head lacks a strong supraciliary ridge giving it a very un-raptor-like facial appearance. It has a long tail and a short head crest. It is brown above, but not as dark as Honey Buzzard, and paler below. There is a dark throat stripe. Unusually for a large bird of prey, the sexes can be distinguished. The male has a blue-grey head, while the female's head is brown. She is slightly larger and darker than the male. The male has a black tail with a white band, whilst the female resembles female Honey Buzzard.</p>
<p>It breeds in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> from central <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> east to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. It is a summer <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a> to Siberia, wintering in tropical south east Asia. Elsewhere it is more-or-less resident. It is a specialist feeder, living mainly on the <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> and nests of <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">wasps</a>, although it will take other small insect prey such as cicadas.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Crested Honey Buzzard breeds in <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodland</a>, and is inconspicuous except in the spring, when the mating display includes wing-clapping. The display of roller-coasting in flight and fluttering wings at the peak of the ascent are characteristic of the genus <i>Pernis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is larger and longer winged than its western counterpart, the <a href="/wiki/European_Honey_Buzzard" title="European Honey Buzzard">European Honey Buzzard</a>, <i>Pernis apivorus</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Crested_Honey_Buzzard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mimicry">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Mimicry">Mimicry</span></h2>
<p>It has been suggested that the similarity in plumage between juvenile Crested Honey Buzzard and the <i>Spizaetus</i> <a href="/wiki/Hawk-eagle" title="Hawk-eagle" class="mw-redirect">hawk-eagles</a> has arisen as a partial protection against predation by larger raptors. The eagles have stronger bills and talons, and are likely to be less vulnerable than the <i>Pernis</i> species.</p>
<p>Similar mimicry is shown by the juveniles of the European Honey Buzzard, which resembles the <a href="/wiki/Common_Buzzard" title="Common Buzzard">Common Buzzard</a>. Although the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Goshawk" title="Northern Goshawk">Northern Goshawk</a> is capable of killing both species, it is likely to be more cautious about attacking the better protected <i>Buteo</i> species.</p>
