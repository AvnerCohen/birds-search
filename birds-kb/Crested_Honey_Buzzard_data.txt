<p>The <b>crested honey buzzard</b> (<i>Pernis ptilorhynchus</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-ITIS_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITIS-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a>, which also includes many other diurnal <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptors</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Kite_(bird)" title="Kite (bird)">kites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Harrier_(bird)" title="Harrier (bird)">harriers</a>. This species is also known as the <b>Oriental honey buzzard</b>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crested_honey_buzzard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RollDisplay.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/RollDisplay.svg/220px-RollDisplay.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="118" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/RollDisplay.svg/330px-RollDisplay.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/RollDisplay.svg/440px-RollDisplay.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="808" data-file-height="434" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RollDisplay.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Roller coaster display of <i>Pernis</i> species</div></div></div>
<p>Despite its name, this species is not related to <i>Buteo</i> <a href="/wiki/Buzzard" title="Buzzard">buzzards</a>, and is taxonomically closer to the kites.<sup id="cite_ref-Finlayson_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Finlayson-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
It appears long-necked with a small head (resembling that of a pigeon), and soars on flat wings. The head lacks a strong superciliary ridge, giving it a facial appearance very unlike a raptor. It has a long tail and a short head crest. It is brown above, but not as dark as European honey buzzard, and paler below. A dark throat stripe is present. Unusually for a large bird of prey, the sexes can be distinguished. The male has a blue-grey head, while the female's head is brown. She is slightly larger and darker than the male. The male has a black tail with a white band.
</p><p>It breeds in <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Asia</a> from central <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> east to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. It is a summer <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a> to Siberia, wintering in tropical Southeast Asia. Elsewhere, it is more-or-less resident. It is a specialist feeder, living mainly on the <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> of social bees and wasps, and eating bits of comb and honey;<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> it takes other small insect prey such as cicadas.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The crested honey buzzard breeds in <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodland</a>, and is inconspicuous except in the spring, when the mating display includes wing-clapping. The display of roller-coasting in flight and fluttering wings at the peak of the ascent are characteristic of the genus <i>Pernis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is larger and longer-winged than its western counterpart, the <a href="/wiki/European_honey_buzzard" title="European honey buzzard">European honey buzzard</a>, <i>Pernis apivorus</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mimicry">Mimicry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crested_honey_buzzard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mimicry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The similarity in plumage between juvenile crested honey buzzards and the <i>Spizaetus</i> <a href="/wiki/Hawk-eagle" class="mw-redirect" title="Hawk-eagle">hawk-eagles</a> may have arisen as a partial protection against predation by larger raptors. The eagles have stronger bills and talons, and are likely to be less vulnerable than the <i>Pernis</i> species. Similar mimicry is shown by the juveniles of the European honey buzzard, which resembles the <a href="/wiki/Common_buzzard" title="Common buzzard">common buzzard</a>. Although the <a href="/wiki/Northern_goshawk" title="Northern goshawk">northern goshawk</a> is capable of killing both species, it is likely to be more cautious about attacking the better protected <i>Buteo</i> species.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_effects_of_wind_and_climate_change_on_migratory_route">The effects of wind and climate change on migratory route</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crested_honey_buzzard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The effects of wind and climate change on migratory route">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Crested buzzards that migrate from breeding areas in Japan to wintering areas in Southeast Asia fly over the East China Sea. This 700-km nonstop flight over water is possible because during autumn, winds over the sea blow in the same direction as the birds' direction of flight (i.e. wind support).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> As climate change affects wind conditions worldwide, the wind support over the East China Sea could be reduced.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
