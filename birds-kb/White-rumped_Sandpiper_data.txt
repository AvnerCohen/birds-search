<p>The <b>White-rumped Sandpiper</b> (<i>Calidris fuscicollis</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Shorebird" title="Shorebird" class="mw-redirect">shorebird</a>.</p>
<p>Adults have black legs and a small thin dark bill. The body is dark brown on top and mainly white underneath, with brown streaks on the breast and a white rump. They have a white stripe over their eyes. This bird shows long wings in flight. In winter <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>, this species is pale gray above. This bird can be difficult to distinguish from other similar tiny shorebirds; these are known collectively as "peeps" or "<a href="/wiki/Stint" title="Stint">stints</a>".</p>
<p>One of the best identification features is the long wings, which extend beyond the tail when the bird is on the ground. Only the <a href="/wiki/Baird%27s_Sandpiper" title="Baird's Sandpiper">Baird's Sandpiper</a> also shows this, and that bird can be distinguished by the lack of a white rump.</p>
<p>Their breeding habitat is the northern tundra on Arctic islands in Canada and Alaska. They nest on the ground, usually well-concealed in vegetation.</p>
<p>They are a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">long distance migrant</a>, wintering in northern South America. They are rare but regular vagrants to western Europe. The species is a rare vagrant to Australia.</p>
<p>These birds forage by probing on mudflats or tundra or picking up food by sight in shallow water. They mainly eat insects, mollusks and marine worms, also some plant material.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">Hybrids</a> between this species and the <a href="/wiki/Dunlin" title="Dunlin">Dunlin</a> are occasionally found in northeastern North America (McLaughlin &amp; Wormington, 2000, and external link below); the White-rumped Sandpiper is also <a href="/wiki/Hybridisation_in_shorebirds#Other_hybrid_scolopacids" title="Hybridisation in shorebirds">suspected</a> to hybridize with the <a href="/wiki/Buff-breasted_Sandpiper" title="Buff-breasted Sandpiper">Buff-breasted Sandpiper</a>.</p>
