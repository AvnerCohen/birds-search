<p>The <b>Kentish plover</b> (<i>Charadrius alexandrinus</i>) is a small cosmopolitan <a href="/wiki/Shorebird" class="mw-redirect" title="Shorebird">shorebird</a> (40-44 g) of the family <a href="/wiki/Charadriidae" title="Charadriidae">Charadriidae</a> that breeds on the shores of saline lakes, lagoons, and coasts, populating sand dunes, marshes, semi-arid desert, and tundra.<sup id="cite_ref-a_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-a-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hoyo_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hoyo-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Both male and female birds have pale plumages with a white underside, grey/brown back, dark legs and a dark <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a>, however additionally the male birds also exhibit very dark incomplete breast bands, and dark markings either side of their head, therefore the Kentish plover is regarded as <a href="/wiki/Sexually_dimorphic" class="mw-redirect" title="Sexually dimorphic">sexually dimorphic</a> <sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Until 2009, the Kentish plover species was universally thought to include the North American <a href="/wiki/Snowy_plover" title="Snowy plover">snowy plover</a> species, however a novel genetic research paper suggested that they were in fact separate species.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In July 2011, the International Ornithological Congress (IOC), and the American Ornithologists' Union (AOU) pronounced the Snowy Plover as a separate species <i>Charadrius nivosus</i>.
</p><p><i>Charadrius alexandrinus</i> has a large geographical distribution, ranging from latitudes of 10º to 55º, occupying North <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, both mainland, such as Senegal, and island, such as the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> archipelago, Central <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, for example alkaline lakes in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, including small populations in Spain and Austria. Some populations are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and often winter in Africa, whereas other populations, such as various island populations, do not migrate.<sup id="cite_ref-b_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-b-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kentish plovers are ground-nesting birds, often with a preference for low, open, moist nesting sites away from thick vegetation and human activity. They use a number of materials to build their nests, mainly consisting of shells, pebbles, grass and leaves in a small scrape in the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-c_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-d_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-d-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Like most plovers, the Kentish plovers are predominantly <a href="/wiki/Insectivores" class="mw-redirect" title="Insectivores">insectivores</a>, feeding on a large range of <a href="/wiki/Arthropods" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthropods">arthropods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Invertebrates" class="mw-redirect" title="Invertebrates">invertebrates</a> depending on the environment, by using a run and stop method.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kentish_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kentish_Plover_adult.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Kentish_Plover_adult.jpg/220px-Kentish_Plover_adult.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Kentish_Plover_adult.jpg/330px-Kentish_Plover_adult.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Kentish_Plover_adult.jpg/440px-Kentish_Plover_adult.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kentish_Plover_adult.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A female adult Kentish Plover</div></div></div>
<p>The Kentish plover is a small shorebird weighing around 40g as an adult. Both male and female birds have black bills and dark legs, however adults have dimorphic plumage. During the breeding season, males have a black horizontal head bar, two incomplete dark breast-bands on each side of their breast, black ear coverts and a rufous nape and crown (although there is some variation between breeding populations), whereas the females are paler in these areas, without the dark markings.<sup id="cite_ref-e_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-e-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-f_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-f-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In the early <a href="/wiki/Breeding_season" class="mw-redirect" title="Breeding season">breeding season</a>, it is easy to distinguish between males and females since the <a href="/wiki/Biological_ornament" title="Biological ornament">ornaments</a> are very pronounced, but as the breeding season progresses, the differences between the two sexes decrease. Moreover, males have longer <a href="/wiki/Bird_anatomy" title="Bird anatomy">tarsi</a> and longer flank feathers than females.<sup id="cite_ref-f_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-f-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Longer flank feathers are thought to be an advantage for <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation" title="Egg incubation">incubation</a> and brood care, as the quality of feathers is associated with heat insulation.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
There are multiple significant predictors of plumage ornamentation in Kentish plovers. Firstly, the interaction between the advancement of the breeding season and rainfall seem to affect ornamentation. Male ornaments become more elaborated over the course of a breeding season in regions with high rainfall, whereas in regions with low rainfall, male ornaments become lighter. Secondly, the interaction between the breeding system and the sex can predict the degree of plumage ornamentation. In <a href="/wiki/Polygamous" class="mw-redirect" title="Polygamous">polygamous</a> populations, the sexual ornaments are more pronounced, generating a stronger sexual dimorphism than in <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" class="mw-redirect" title="Monogamous">monogamous</a> populations. The difference is especially witnessed in males, whereby the ornaments are darker and smaller in polygamous populations compared to monogamous populations, where males have lighter and larger ornaments. This is thought to be the result of a trade-off between the size and intensity of the ornaments.<sup id="cite_ref-e_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-e-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span id="Distribution.2C_Movement.2C_and_Habitats"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution,_Movement,_and_Habitats">Distribution, Movement, and Habitats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kentish_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution, Movement, and Habitats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kentish_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kentish plovers have an extremely wide geographical distribution and their habitats vary not just spatially but environmentally too. They are known to reside and breed in multiple types of habitat, from <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> with ground temperatures reaching 50&#160;°C to <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>. The distribution of this species’ breeding areas covers Europe, Asia and Africa,<sup id="cite_ref-b_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-b-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vin_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vin-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>). In Europe, populations are typically found in the west; although there was once a breeding population in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, Kentish plovers no longer breed there. In Africa, populations are found on the southern coast of Senegal and along the Northern coast of the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> coast. The breeding area continues along the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bahrain" title="Bahrain">Bahrain</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>. Small populations can be found on islands too, such as the Cape Verde archipelago, the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>. It is a rare vagrant in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>Some populations do not migrate, such as the Maio (Cape Verde) population, however other populations can migrate reasonable distances, for example, plovers that spend winter in North Africa have been known to migrate to <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> in the spring. Some birds breeding in western Europe are not known to travel very far, just within Europe, however some do travel, mainly to Western Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitats_and_Movement">Habitats and Movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kentish_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Habitats and Movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The breeding habitats are most commonly alkali lake shores, <a href="/wiki/Wetlands" class="mw-redirect" title="Wetlands">wetlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salt_marshes" class="mw-redirect" title="Salt marshes">salt marshes</a>, and coastland, which is fitting with the results of a study that investigated what makes an environment suitable for a breeding habitat for the Kentish plover. By analysing 4 variables of all known nests, the study found that plovers prefer to nest in areas of low elevation, low vegetation, high moisture and places faraway from human activity and settlements,.<sup id="cite_ref-c_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vin_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vin-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:KP_nest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/KP_nest.jpg/220px-KP_nest.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/KP_nest.jpg/330px-KP_nest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/KP_nest.jpg/440px-KP_nest.jpg 2x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:KP_nest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kentish Plover nest</div></div></div> 
<p>There have been observations of parents moving their chicks from poor food areas to better food areas, with chicks subsequently growing stronger in the high food areas. This suggests that parents strategically move their chicks and change habitats. Moving young has benefits: protection from predators, obtaining more food, avoiding competition for food and space, avoiding potential <a href="/wiki/Infanticide" title="Infanticide">infanticide</a> due to competition, and avoiding <a href="/wiki/Territory" title="Territory">territory</a> defences from others. However, this is a trade-off as there are also costs to moving young: moving expends a lot of energy, especially in young, therefore chick growth may be stunted as energy is used on movement rather than growth, the chance of mortality due to starvation or predation increases whilst moving through open areas and the area of high food may have a lot of predators in it already. Overall, chick growth and brood survival benefit from moving to a higher food area, therefore increasing reproductive success of parents, hence why the parents move their chicks. The study also found that the larger and heavier females were more likely to move chicks, perhaps because they could defend their chicks from neighbouring parents <sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_Ecology">Behaviour and Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kentish_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour and Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kentish_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hatching_Kentish_Plover.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Hatching_Kentish_Plover.jpg/220px-Hatching_Kentish_Plover.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Hatching_Kentish_Plover.jpg/330px-Hatching_Kentish_Plover.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Hatching_Kentish_Plover.jpg/440px-Hatching_Kentish_Plover.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hatching_Kentish_Plover.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Kentish Plover chick hatching in Maio, Cape Verde, 2016</div></div></div>
<p>The Kentish plover has an especially flexible <a href="/wiki/Breeding_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Breeding system">breeding system</a>, including both monogamous and polygamous behaviours within populations. It is known that breeding pairs return to breed with each other the following year, however mate changes have also been observed both between and within breeding seasons.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-g_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-g-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Along with mate changes, EPF's (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Extra_pair_fertilisations&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Extra pair fertilisations (page does not exist)">extra pair fertilisations</a>) are also witnessed in some populations, by females copulating with extra-pair males (EPP- extra pair paternity), or males copulating with extra pair females, who then lay their eggs in the male's nest (QP- quasi-parasitism). A theory as to why such EPC's occur is that this mechanism evolved to avoid the deleterious effects of <a href="/wiki/Inbreeding" title="Inbreeding">inbreeding</a>. This is supported by a study by Blomqvist et al.,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> showing that EPC's are more common when a breeding pair are more closely related to each other. Another theory is that females may seek out EPC's with high quality males to get the ‘good genes’ for their sons, following the ‘<a href="/wiki/Sexy_sons" class="mw-redirect" title="Sexy sons">sexy sons</a>’ hypothesis. The breeding season of Kentish plovers lasts on average between 2 and 5 months and varies in the time of year dependent on the particular population. Breeding pairs can replace failed clutches more than once per breeding season, with the same or a different mate, and both males and females can parent more than one brood, due to mate change and EPC's as mentioned above.<sup id="cite_ref-a_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-a-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Courtship" title="Courtship">courtship</a> displays also vary between populations of plovers, especially between socially monogamous and polygamous populations, for example in polygamous populations the time spent courting is significantly higher for both males and females than in monogamous populations. Courtship displays include active gestures such as flat running, building nest scrapes (small shallow cavities in the ground that are later built into nests), and fighting/running to defend a breeding territory (mainly by males) <sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Territories">Territories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kentish_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Territories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kentish plovers inhabit sandy areas or salt-marshes in close proximity to water. Inland populations can be found near alkaline or saline lakes, ponds or reservoirs. The populations inhabiting the coastal regions can be found in semi-desert habitats i.e. on barren beaches, near lagoons and sand extractions on beaches or dunes.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Salina_do_porto_ingles.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Salina_do_porto_ingles.jpg/220px-Salina_do_porto_ingles.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Salina_do_porto_ingles.jpg/330px-Salina_do_porto_ingles.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Salina_do_porto_ingles.jpg/440px-Salina_do_porto_ingles.jpg 2x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Salina_do_porto_ingles.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Salina do Porto Ingles, the habitat of Kentish Plovers in Maio, Cape Verde</div></div></div>
<p>Kentish plovers are territorial shorebirds; the male usually has a <a href="/wiki/Territory" title="Territory">territory</a> and attracts females with courtship displays. The parents are actively defending their nest territories from predators by chasing, fighting or posturing them. When approached by predators in close proximity to the nest, the Kentish plovers quickly run away from the nest and start doing distraction displays to focus the predator's attention on themselves and lure them away from the nest. These displays include calling or crawling on the ground flapping their wings. Males tend to be more aggressive than females. When a plover's territory has been invaded, it invades a neighboring family's territory. This is when fights between males frequently occur because the plovers see their broods threatened. During such fights, it occurs that chicks get injured or even killed.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> When approached by a predator, chicks usually try to find a spot where they can hide, crouch down and stay motionless to remain unseen. When they are older, they try to run away with their parents.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nesting_and_Incubation">Nesting and Incubation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kentish_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nesting and Incubation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kentish_Plover_nest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Kentish_Plover_nest.jpg/220px-Kentish_Plover_nest.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Kentish_Plover_nest.jpg/330px-Kentish_Plover_nest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Kentish_Plover_nest.jpg/440px-Kentish_Plover_nest.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2448" data-file-height="3264" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kentish_Plover_nest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Kentish Plover nest, with a standard grey card</div></div></div>
<p>Kentish plovers either nest solitarily or in a loose <a href="/wiki/Semicolonial" class="mw-redirect" title="Semicolonial">semicolonial</a> manner. They are ground-nesting birds that lay their eggs in small shallow scrapes prepared by the male during courtship on the bare ground. Selection of the breeding ground is essential for the survival of nests and broods; nests are placed near the water on bare earth or in sparse vegetation; often on slightly elevated sites in order to have a good view of the surroundings to spot predators from a distance or near small bushes, plants or grass clusters,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> where the eggs are partly sheltered from predators. Nests are filled with nest material i.e. pebble stones, small parts of shells, fish bones, small twigs, grass and other debris.<sup id="cite_ref-d_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-d-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The modal <a href="/wiki/Clutch_size" class="mw-redirect" title="Clutch size">clutch size</a> comprises three eggs, although some nests are already completed with one or two eggs. In fresh or incomplete nests, the eggs tend to be fully exposed, but as the incubation period progresses, the amount of nest material increases and the eggs become practically completely covered.<sup id="cite_ref-a_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-a-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> During the incubation period, the Kentish plover recesses for variable periods of time mainly to forage or to perform other activities essential for self-maintenance. To compensate for the resulting lack of presence and increased predation risk, they use nest materials to cover and hence camouflage the eggs and keep them insulated.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Kentish plovers regulate the amount of nest material actively. This was shown experimentally in a study by increasing or decreasing the amount of nest material artificially. Within 24hrs, the plovers had restored the amount of nest material back to original.<sup id="cite_ref-d_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-d-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> This is of advantage because nest materials help a good insulation of eggs, therefore preventing egg temperature fluctuations <sup id="cite_ref-reid_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reid-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>)  (hence avoiding embryo hypothermia) and reducing the energetic costs of incubation for the parents.<sup id="cite_ref-reid_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reid-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> By regulating the amount of nest material, the Kentish plovers balances the advantages i.e. insulation and anti-predator defence and the disadvantages of nest material i.e. overheating.<sup id="cite_ref-d_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-d-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Incubation is the process by which the eggs are kept at optimal temperature i.e. between 37&#160;°C and 38&#160;°C for the embryonic development of birds with most of the heat deriving from the incubating bird.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Kentish plover eggs are incubated for 20–25 days by both sexes; females mostly incubate during the day whilst males incubate during the night.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Female Kentish plovers usually lose mass during the day, which is unexpected since they get relieved by the males for a variable amount of time. The loss would be much higher if the females were to incubate alone. This loss is a cost of incubation due to the depletion of fat stores and the evaporation of water.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parental_Care">Parental Care</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kentish_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Parental Care">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kentish_Plover_Chick.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Kentish_Plover_Chick.jpg/220px-Kentish_Plover_Chick.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Kentish_Plover_Chick.jpg/330px-Kentish_Plover_Chick.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Kentish_Plover_Chick.jpg/440px-Kentish_Plover_Chick.jpg 2x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kentish_Plover_Chick.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Kentish Plover Chick</div></div></div>
<p>Parental care is variable within birds and the Kentish plover has a slightly different mechanism to other shorebirds. As discussed above, both parents incubate the eggs, however both parents do not always stick around once the eggs have hatched. It is not unusual for one parent to leave the chicks after a variable amount of time; this is referred to as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brood_desertion&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brood desertion (page does not exist)">brood desertion</a>. Brood desertion is the ‘termination of care, by either one or both parents, before the offspring are capable of surviving independently’ <sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and usually occurs after one week of the brood being accompanied by both parents. Brood desertion has been observed in both males and females, however females desert the brood significantly more frequently than males.<sup id="cite_ref-g_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-g-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> 
Studies have shown that both the male and female Kentish plover can provide adequate care for their brood on their own, so it is not the differences in the ability of the parents that determines which parent deserts the brood and which stays to care for the chicks. However, studies have also shown that after desertion females have a larger chance of breeding success than males, potentially due to many Kentish plover populations maintaining a male-biased OSR (operational sex ratio - the ratio of males actively breeding to females). Therefore, is it hypothesized that the amount of reproductive success gained by desertion is what actually determines who deserts the brood,.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-f_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-f-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In short, males and females can care for their brood equally, however females gain more by deserting their brood than males, resulting in a higher amount of female desertion over male. The non-deserting parent can continue to brood their chicks up to 80% of the time for over 20 days after hatching, as precocial young are vulnerable and exposed to external temperatures.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calls">Calls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kentish_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Calls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The alarm call, referred to as <i>kittup</i> call, is often heard both on the ground and in the air and can occur on its own, or paired with a <i>tweet</i>, heard as <i>too-eet</i>. The threat note is described as a "twanging, metallic, dwee-dwee-dweedweedwee sound".<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kentish_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kentish plovers either forage individually or in loose flocks of 20-30 individuals (outside the breeding season), and occasionally can incorporate into larger flocks of up to 260 individuals of multiple species.<sup id="cite_ref-h_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-h-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Their main source of food consists of miniature aquatic and terrestrial invertebrates such as insects and their larvae (e.g. beetles, grasshoppers or flies), molluscs, crustaceans, spiders and marine worms.<sup id="cite_ref-h_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-h-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> They are obligate visual foragers and often feed at the shoreline of lakes, lagoons or ponds in invertebrate-rich moist-soil areas.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> They forage by looking, stopping or running and then pecking to catch the prey, but also probe the sand to search for prey, or catch flies by holding their mouths open.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The Kentish plover's capability of identifying cues for prey is influenced by light, wind and rain.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> At night, their ability of finding prey might be restricted, but plovers have been shown to have a good nocturnal vision due to their large eyes and enhanced retinal visual sensitivity,.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_and_Conservation">Status and Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kentish_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Status and Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kentish_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Kentish plover is classified as Least Concern on the Red List Category because it has a very large population range. It is not near the threshold for ‘Vulnerable’ under the population size (&lt;10,000 mature individuals with a constant decline reaching &gt;10% within the next 10 years or three generations), since the global population is estimated being very large with 100,000 - 499,999 mature individuals. A closer estimate, needing further verification, is that the population size lies between 290,000 and 460,000 individuals. Moreover, the Kentish plover does not reach the threshold for being ‘Vulnerable’ on a level of range size (the area of occurrence being &lt;20,000km2 in combination with a decline of population size, habitat area/quality or range size) because it is distributed over an area of 70,800,000 km2. Finally, although there is a decline in global population size, it does not reach the threshold for ‘Vulnerable’ in terms of population trend meaning &gt;30% decline over 10 years or three generations.<sup id="cite_ref-i_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-i-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trends">Trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kentish_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The global population size of the Kentish plover is continuously declining although for some populations the trends are unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> The European population being estimated at 21,500-34,800 pairs, forming around 15% of the global range, is thought to decrease by less than 25% in 15 years/three generations.<sup id="cite_ref-i_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-i-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats">Threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kentish_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A major threat to this species is habitat loss and disturbance. Human activity such as tourists walking through protected areas, pollution, unsustainable harvesting and urbanisation can destroy nesting sites. Plover populations can also be affected by rural human activity, for example fishermen walking through protected plover breeding sites, bringing large numbers of dogs with them- a known predator of plover eggs. Breeding birds respond to human disturbance disproportionately when dogs are present<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>, as these situations are interpreted in a context of greater risk of predation<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>. Natural predators are also a problem, as many of these predators appear to thrive unnaturally well in the presence of plover breeding grounds, such as the brown-necked raven (<i>Corvus ruficollis</i>) in Maio, Cape Verde, the White-tailed Mongoose (<i>Ichneumia albicauda</i>) in Saudi Arabia, and the Grey Monitors (<i>Varanus griseus</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Al-Wathba_Wetland_Reserve" class="mw-redirect" title="Al-Wathba Wetland Reserve">Al-Wathba Wetland Reserve</a>. It is thought that the high amount of prey available to these predators attracts them into the breeding grounds- an effect named the 'honey pot',<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-i_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-i-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Global warming and climate change also plays a role in the decline of areas available for plovers to breed and reside in. It is known that the Kentish plover prefers to build its nests on low-elevated land close to water, and a study untaken in Saudi Arabia discovered that 11% of nests in the study site were in fact below sea level, therefore rising sea levels are predicted to have disastrous consequences for these low-sitting nests,.<sup id="cite_ref-c_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kentish_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Kentish plover is currently on the Annex I of the EU Birds Directive and Annex II of the Bern Convention.<sup id="cite_ref-i_43-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-i-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> 
Conservation actions proposed to protect the species include the conservation of their natural habitat by creating or elaborating protected areas at breeding sites. This is essential to stop pollution, land reclamation and urbanisation. Human interaction should be controlled and kept at a minimum.
</p>
