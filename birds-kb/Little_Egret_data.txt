<p>The <b>Little Egret</b> (<i><b>Egretta garzetta</b></i>) is a small white <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">heron</a>. It is the Old World counterpart to the very similar New World <a href="/wiki/Snowy_Egret" title="Snowy Egret">Snowy Egret</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span></h2>
<p>Depending on authority, two or three subspecies of Little Egret are currently accepted.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>Egretta garzetta garzetta</i> – Europe, Africa, and most of Asia except the southeast</li>
<li><i>Egretta garzetta nigripes</i> – Indonesia east to <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a></li>
<li><i>Egretta garzetta immaculata</i> – Australia and (non-breeding) New Zealand, often considered synonymous with <i>E. g. nigripes</i></li>
</ul>
<p>Three other egret <a href="/wiki/Taxa" title="Taxa" class="mw-redirect">taxa</a> have at times been classified as subspecies of the Little Egret in the past but are now regarded as two separate species. These are the <a href="/wiki/Western_Reef_Heron" title="Western Reef Heron">Western Reef Heron</a> <i>Egretta gularis</i> which occurs on the coastline of West Africa (<i>Egretta gularis gularis</i>) and from the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> to India (<i>Egretta gularis schistacea</i>), and the <a href="/wiki/Dimorphic_Egret" title="Dimorphic Egret">Dimorphic Egret</a> <i>Egretta dimorpha</i>, found in <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Comoros" title="Comoros">Comoros</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Aldabra_Islands" title="Aldabra Islands" class="mw-redirect">Aldabra Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The adult Little Egret is 55–65&#160;cm long with an 88–106&#160;cm wingspan, and weighs 350–550&#160;grams. Its plumage is all white. The subspecies <i>garzetta</i> has long black legs with yellow feet and a slim black bill. In the breeding season, the adult has two long nape plumes and gauzy plumes on the back and breast, and the bare skin between the bill and eyes becomes red or blue. Juveniles are similar to non-breeding adults but have greenish-black legs and duller yellow feet. has yellow feet and a bare patch of grey-green skin between the bill and eyes. The subspecies <i>nigripes</i> differs in having yellow skin between the bill and eye, and blackish feet.</p>
<p>Little Egrets are mostly silent but make various croaking and bubbling calls at their breeding colonies and produce a harsh alarm call when disturbed.</p>

<p>Its breeding distribution is in wetlands in warm temperate to tropical parts of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. In warmer locations, most birds are permanent residents; northern populations, including many European birds, <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> to Africa and southern Asia. They may also wander north in late summer after the breeding season, which may have assisted its current range expansion. Globally, the Little Egret is not listed as a threatened species.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Status in northwestern Europe">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status_in_northwestern_Europe">Status in northwestern Europe</span></h3>
<p>Historical research has shown that the Little Egret was once present, and probably common, in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, but became extinct there through a combination of over-hunting in the late mediaeval period and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> at the start of the <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a>. The inclusion of 1,000 egrets (among numerous other birds) in the banquet to celebrate the <a href="/wiki/Enthronement" title="Enthronement">enthronement</a> of <a href="/wiki/George_Neville_(bishop)" title="George Neville (bishop)">George Neville</a> as <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_York" title="Archbishop of York">Archbishop of York</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cawood_Castle" title="Cawood Castle">Cawood Castle</a> in 1465 indicates the presence of a sizable population in northern England at the time, and they are also listed in the coronation feast of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VI_of_England" title="Henry VI of England">King Henry VI</a> in 1429.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bourne_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bourne-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> They had disappeared by the mid 16th century, when William Gowreley, 'yeoman purveyor to the Kinges mowthe', "had to send further south" for egrets.<sup id="cite_ref-Bourne_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bourne-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Further declines occurred throughout Europe as the plumes of the Little Egret and other egrets were in demand for decorating hats. They had been used for this purpose since at least the 17th century but in the 19th century it became a major craze and the number of egret skins passing through dealers reached into the millions. Egret farms were set up where the birds could be plucked without being killed but most of the supply was obtained by hunting, which reduced the population of the species to dangerously low levels and stimulated the establishment of Britain's <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Protection_of_Birds" title="Royal Society for the Protection of Birds">Royal Society for the Protection of Birds</a> in 1889.</p>
<p>By the 1950s, the Little Egret had become restricted to southern Europe, and conservation laws protecting the species were introduced. This allowed the population to rebounded strongly; over the next few decades it became increasingly common in western <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and later on the north coast. It bred in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> in 1979 with further breeding from the 1990s onward.</p>
<p>In Britain it was a rare vagrant from its 16th century disappearance until the late twentieth century, and did not breed. It has however recently become a regular breeding species and is commonly present, often in large numbers, at favoured coastal sites. The first recent breeding record in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> was on <a href="/wiki/Brownsea_Island" title="Brownsea Island">Brownsea Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a> in 1996, and the species bred in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> for the first time in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The population increase has been rapid subsequently, with over 750 pairs breeding in nearly 70 colonies in 2008,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> and a post-breeding total of 4,540 birds in September 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> the species bred for the first time in 1997 at a site in <a href="/wiki/County_Cork" title="County Cork">County Cork</a> and the population has also expanded rapidly since, breeding in most Irish counties by 2010.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Status in Australia">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status_in_Australia">Status in Australia</span></h3>
<p>In Australia, its status varies from state to state. It is listed as 'Threatened' on the Victorian <a href="/wiki/Flora_and_Fauna_Guarantee_Act_(1988)" title="Flora and Fauna Guarantee Act (1988)" class="mw-redirect">Flora and Fauna Guarantee Act (1988)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Under this Act, an Action Statement for the recovery and future management of this species has been prepared.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> On the 2007 advisory list of threatened vertebrate fauna in Victoria, the Little Egret is listed as <a href="/wiki/Endangered" title="Endangered" class="mw-redirect">endangered</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DSE200_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DSE200-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Colonisation of the New World">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Colonisation_of_the_New_World">Colonisation of the New World</span></h3>
<p>The Little Egret has now started to colonise the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>. The first record there was on <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> in April 1954. It began breeding on the island in 1994. Birds are seen with increasing regularity and have occurred from <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> in the south to <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> in the north. Birds on the east coast of North America are thought to have moved north with Snowy Egrets from the Caribbean. In June 2011, a little egret was spotted in Maine, in the Scarborough Marsh, near the Audubon Center. &lt;<a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.wmtw.com/news/28400447/detail.html">http://www.wmtw.com/news/28400447/detail.html</a>&gt;</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aigrette_garzette_MHNT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Aigrette_garzette_MHNT.jpg/220px-Aigrette_garzette_MHNT.jpg" width="220" height="250" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aigrette_garzette_MHNT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>Egretta garzetta</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Little Egret nests in colonies, often with other wading birds, usually on platforms of sticks in trees or shrubs or in a reedbed or bamboo grove. In some locations such as the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> Islands, they nest on cliffs. Pairs defend a small breeding territory, usually extending around 3–4&#160;m from the nest. The three to five eggs are incubated by both adults for 21–25 days to hatching. They are oval in shape and have a pale, non-glossy, blue-green colour. The young birds are covered in white <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">down feathers</a>, are cared for by both parents and fledge after 40 to 45 days.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Little_Egret_(Egretta_garzetta)-_In_Breeding_plumage-actively_catching_prey_in_Kolkata_I_IMG_7962.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Little_Egret_%28Egretta_garzetta%29-_In_Breeding_plumage-actively_catching_prey_in_Kolkata_I_IMG_7962.jpg/220px-Little_Egret_%28Egretta_garzetta%29-_In_Breeding_plumage-actively_catching_prey_in_Kolkata_I_IMG_7962.jpg" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Little_Egret_(Egretta_garzetta)-_In_Breeding_plumage-actively_catching_prey_in_Kolkata_I_IMG_7962.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>E. g. garzetta</i> in Breeding plumage actively catching prey in <a href="/wiki/Kolkata" title="Kolkata">Kolkata</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Little Egrets eat <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, insects, <a href="/wiki/Amphibians" title="Amphibians" class="mw-redirect">amphibians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Reptiles" title="Reptiles" class="mw-redirect">reptiles</a>. They stalk their prey in shallow water, often running with raised wings or shuffling its feet to disturb small fish. They may also stand still and wait to ambush prey.</p>
