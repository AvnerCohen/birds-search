<p>The <b>little egret</b> (<i>Egretta garzetta</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of small <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">heron</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Ardeidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Ardeidae">Ardeidae</a>. The genus name comes from the <a href="/wiki/Proven%C3%A7al_dialect" title="Provençal dialect">Provençal French</a> <i>Aigrette</i>, "egret", a diminutive of <i>Aigron</i>," heron". The species epithet <i>garzetta</i> is from the Italian name for this bird, <i>garzetta</i> or <i>sgarzetta</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is a white bird with a slender black beak, long black legs and, in the western race, yellow feet. As an <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_bird" class="mw-redirect" title="Aquatic bird">aquatic bird</a>, it feeds in shallow water and on land, consuming a variety of small creatures. It breeds <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonially</a>, often with other species of water birds, making a platform nest of sticks in a tree, bush or reed bed. A clutch of three to  five bluish-green eggs is laid and incubated by both parents for about three weeks. The young <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> at about six weeks of age.
</p><p>Its breeding distribution is in wetlands in warm temperate to tropical parts of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. A successful colonist, its range has gradually expanded north, with stable and self-sustaining populations now present in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In warmer locations, most birds are permanent residents; northern populations, including many European birds, <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> to Africa and southern Asia to over-winter there. The birds may also wander north in late summer after the breeding season, and their tendency to disperse may have assisted in the recent expansion of the bird's range. At one time common in Western Europe, it was hunted extensively in the 19th century to provide plumes for the decoration of hats and became locally extinct in northwestern Europe and scarce in the south. Around 1950, conservation laws were introduced in southern Europe to protect the species and their numbers began to increase.  By the beginning of the 21st century the bird was breeding again in France, the Netherlands, Ireland and Britain. Its range is continuing to expand westward, and the species has begun to colonise the New World; it was first seen in Barbados in 1954 and first bred there in 1994. The <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> has assessed the bird's global conservation status as being of "<a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least concern</a>".
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Depending on authority, two or three subspecies of little egret are currently accepted:<sup id="cite_ref-Avibase_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avibase-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>E. g. garzetta</i> – <small>(Linnaeus, 1766)</small>: <a href="/wiki/Subspecies#Nominotypical_subspecies_and_subspecies_autonyms" title="Subspecies">nominate</a>, found in Europe, Africa, and most of Asia except the south-east</li>
<li><i>E. g. nigripes</i> – <small>(Temminck, 1840)</small>: found in Indonesia east to <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a></li>
<li><i>E. g. immaculata</i> – Australia and (non-breeding) New Zealand, often considered synonymous with <i>E. g. nigripes</i></li></ul>
<p>Three other egret <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxa</a> have at times been classified as subspecies of the little egret in the past but are now regarded as two separate species. These are the <a href="/wiki/Western_reef_heron" title="Western reef heron">western reef heron</a> <i>Egretta gularis</i> which occurs on the coastline of West Africa (<i>Egretta gularis gularis</i>) and from the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> to India (<i>Egretta gularis schistacea</i>), and the <a href="/wiki/Dimorphic_egret" title="Dimorphic egret">dimorphic egret</a> <i>Egretta dimorpha</i>, found in <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Comoros" title="Comoros">Comoros</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Aldabra_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Aldabra Islands">Aldabra Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The adult little egret is 55–65&#160;cm (22–26&#160;in) long with an 88–106&#160;cm (35–42&#160;in) wingspan, and weighs 350–550&#160;g (12–19&#160;oz). Its plumage is normally entirely white, although there are dark forms with largely bluish-grey plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In the breeding season, the adult has two long plumes on the nape that form a crest. These plumes are about 150&#160;mm (6&#160;in) and are pointed and very narrow. There are similar feathers on the breast, but the <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">barbs</a> are more widely spread. There are also several elongated scapular feathers that have long loose barbs and may be 200&#160;mm (8&#160;in) long. During the winter the plumage is similar but the scapulars are shorter and more normal in appearance. The bill is long and slender and it and the <a href="/wiki/Lore_(anatomy)" title="Lore (anatomy)">lores</a> are black. There is an area of greenish-grey bare skin at the base of the lower mandible and around the eye which has a yellow <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">iris</a>. The legs are black and the feet yellow. Juveniles are similar to non-breeding adults but have greenish-black legs and duller yellow feet,<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and may have a certain proportion of greyish or brownish feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  The subspecies <i>nigripes</i> differs in having yellow skin between the bill and eye, and blackish feet. During the height of courtship, the lores turn red and the feet of the yellow-footed races turn red.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Little egrets are mostly silent but make various croaking and bubbling calls at their breeding colonies and produce a harsh alarm call when disturbed. To the human ear, the sounds are indistinguishable from the <a href="/wiki/Black-crowned_night_heron" title="Black-crowned night heron">black-crowned night heron</a> (<i>Nycticorax nycticorax</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Cattle_egret" title="Cattle egret">cattle egret</a> (<i>Bubulcus ibis</i>) with which it sometimes associates.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The breeding range of the western race (<i>E. g. garzetta</i>) includes southern Europe, the Middle East, much of Africa and <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">southern Asia</a>. Northern European populations are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, mostly travelling to Africa although some remain in southern Europe, while some Asian populations migrate to the Philippines. The eastern race, (<i>E. g. nigripes</i>), is resident in Indonesia and New Guinea, while <i>E. g. immaculata</i> inhabits Australia and New Zealand, but does not breed in the latter.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> During the late twentieth century, the range of the little egret expanded northwards in Europe and into the New World, where a breeding population was established on Barbados in 1994. The birds have since spread elsewhere in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean</a> region and on the Atlantic coast of the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-Kushlan_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kushlan-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The little egret's habitat varies widely, and includes the shores of lakes, rivers, canals, ponds, lagoons, marshes and flooded land, the bird preferring open locations to dense cover. On the coast it inhabits mangrove areas, swamps, mudflats, sandy beaches and reefs. Rice fields are an important habitat in Italy, and coastal and mangrove areas are important in Africa. The bird often moves about among cattle or other hoofed mammals.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Little egrets are sociable birds and are often seen in small flocks. Nevertheless, individual birds do not tolerate others coming too close to their chosen feeding site, though this depends on the abundance of <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">prey</a>. They use a variety of methods to procure their food; they stalk their prey in shallow water, often running with raised wings or shuffling their feet to disturb small fish, or may stand still and wait to ambush prey. They make use of opportunities provided by <a href="/wiki/Cormorant" title="Cormorant">cormorants</a> disturbing fish or humans attracting fish by throwing bread into water. On land they walk or run while chasing their prey, feed on creatures disturbed by grazing <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> and ticks on the livestock, and even <a href="/wiki/Scavenger" title="Scavenger">scavenge</a>. Their diet is mainly <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> are also eaten, as well as <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mollusca" title="Mollusca">molluscs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Worm" title="Worm">worms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Little egrets nest in colonies, often with other wading birds. On the coasts of western India these colonies may be in urban areas, and associated birds include <a href="/wiki/Cattle_egret" title="Cattle egret">cattle egrets</a> (<i>Bubulcus ibis</i>), <a href="/wiki/Black-crowned_night_heron" title="Black-crowned night heron">black-crowned night herons</a> (<i>Nycticorax nycticorax</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Black-headed_ibis" title="Black-headed ibis">black-headed ibises</a> (<i>Threskiornis melanocephalus</i>). In Europe, associated species may be <a href="/wiki/Squacco_heron" title="Squacco heron">squacco herons</a> (<i>Ardeola ralloides</i>), cattle egrets, black-crowned night herons and <a href="/wiki/Glossy_ibis" title="Glossy ibis">glossy ibises</a> (<i>Plegadis falcinellus</i>). The nests are usually platforms of sticks built in trees or shrubs, or in <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed beds</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a> groves. In some locations such as the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> Islands, the birds nest on cliffs. Pairs defend a small breeding territory, usually extending around 3 to 4&#160;m (10 to 13&#160;ft) from the nest. The three to five eggs are <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation" title="Egg incubation">incubated</a> by both adults for 21 to 25 days before hatching. They are oval in shape and have a pale, non-glossy, blue-green shell colour. The young birds are covered in white <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">down feathers</a>, are cared for by both parents and <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> after 40 to 45 days.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Globally, the little egret is not listed as a threatened species and has in fact expanded its range over the last few decades.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> states that their wide distribution and large total population means that they are a species that cause them "<a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least concern</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_in_northwestern_Europe">Status in northwestern Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Status in northwestern Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Historical research has shown that the little egret was once present, and probably common, in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, but became extinct there through a combination of over-hunting in the late <a href="/wiki/Medieval_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval period">medieval period</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_(general_concept)" class="mw-redirect" title="Climate change (general concept)">climate change</a> at the start of the <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a>. The inclusion of 1,000 egrets (among numerous other birds) in the banquet to celebrate the <a href="/wiki/Enthronement" title="Enthronement">enthronement</a> of <a href="/wiki/George_Neville_(bishop)" class="mw-redirect" title="George Neville (bishop)">George Neville</a> as <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_York" title="Archbishop of York">Archbishop of York</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cawood_Castle" title="Cawood Castle">Cawood Castle</a> in 1465 indicates the presence of a sizable population in northern England at the time, and they are also listed in the coronation feast of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VI_of_England" title="Henry VI of England">King Henry VI</a> in 1429.<sup id="cite_ref-Stubbs_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stubbs-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bourne_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bourne-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> They had become scarce by the mid-16th century, when William Gowreley, "yeoman purveyor to the Kinges mowthe", "had to send further south" for egrets.<sup id="cite_ref-Bourne_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bourne-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In 1804 <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bewick" title="Thomas Bewick">Thomas Bewick</a> commented that if it were the same bird as listed in Neville's bill of fare "No wonder this species has become nearly extinct in this country!"<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Bewick_The_Little_Egret_1804.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Thomas_Bewick_The_Little_Egret_1804.jpg/220px-Thomas_Bewick_The_Little_Egret_1804.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="223" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Thomas_Bewick_The_Little_Egret_1804.jpg/330px-Thomas_Bewick_The_Little_Egret_1804.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Thomas_Bewick_The_Little_Egret_1804.jpg/440px-Thomas_Bewick_The_Little_Egret_1804.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1336" data-file-height="1354" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Bewick_The_Little_Egret_1804.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"The Little Egret" in <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bewick" title="Thomas Bewick">Thomas Bewick</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/A_History_of_British_Birds" title="A History of British Birds">A History of British Birds</a></i>, volume II, "Water Birds", 1804</div></div></div>
<p>Further declines occurred throughout Europe as the plumes of the little egret and other egrets were in demand for decorating hats. They had been used in the <a href="/wiki/Plume_trade" class="mw-redirect" title="Plume trade">plume trade</a> since at least the 17th century but in the 19th century it became a major craze and the number of egret skins passing through dealers reached into the millions.<sup id="cite_ref-Haines_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haines-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Complete statistics do not exist, but in the first three months of 1885, 750,000 egret skins were sold in London, while in 1887 one London dealer sold 2 million egret skins.<sup id="cite_ref-BB_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Egret farms were set up where the birds could be plucked without being killed but most of the supply of so-called "<a href="/wiki/Osprey" title="Osprey">Osprey</a> plumes"<sup id="cite_ref-birdnotesnews_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdnotesnews-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> was obtained by hunting, which reduced the population of the species to dangerously low levels and stimulated the establishment of Britain's <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Protection_of_Birds" title="Royal Society for the Protection of Birds">Royal Society for the Protection of Birds</a> in 1889.<sup id="cite_ref-Haines_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haines-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the 1950s, the little egret had become restricted to southern Europe, and conservation laws protecting the species were introduced. This allowed the population to rebound strongly; over the next few decades it became increasingly common in western <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and later on the north coast. It bred in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> in 1979 with further breeding from the 1990s onward. About 22,700 pairs are thought to breed in Europe, with populations stable or increasing in Spain, France and Italy but decreasing in Greece.<sup id="cite_ref-Avibirds_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avibirds-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Britain it was a rare vagrant from its 16th-century disappearance until the late 20th century, and did not breed. It has however recently become a regular breeding species and is commonly present, often in large numbers, at favoured coastal sites. The first recent breeding record in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> was on <a href="/wiki/Brownsea_Island" title="Brownsea Island">Brownsea Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a> in 1996, and the species bred in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> for the first time in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-rspb_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The population increase has been rapid subsequently, with over 750 pairs breeding in nearly 70 colonies in 2008,<sup id="cite_ref-Holling_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holling-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and a post-breeding total of 4,540 birds in September 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-Calbrade2010_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Calbrade2010-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Little egrets are especially common around the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a>, and in summer can be noticed in large numbers at <a href="/wiki/Port_Meadow,_Oxford" title="Port Meadow, Oxford">Port Meadow, Oxford</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, the species bred for the first time in 1997 at a site in <a href="/wiki/County_Cork" title="County Cork">County Cork</a> and the population has also expanded rapidly since, breeding in most Irish counties by 2010. Severe winter weather in 2010–2012 proved to be only a temporary setback, and the species continues to spread.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_in_Australia">Status in Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Status in Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Little_Egret.6.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Little_Egret.6.jpg/220px-Little_Egret.6.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="190" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Little_Egret.6.jpg/330px-Little_Egret.6.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Little_Egret.6.jpg/440px-Little_Egret.6.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3766" data-file-height="3259" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Little_Egret.6.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>E. g. immaculata</i> in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">Northern Territory</a>, Australia</div></div></div>
<p>In Australia, its status varies from state to state. It is listed as "Threatened" on the Victorian <a href="/wiki/Flora_and_Fauna_Guarantee_Act_1988" title="Flora and Fauna Guarantee Act 1988">Flora and Fauna Guarantee Act 1988</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-vic.gov.au_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vic.gov.au-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Under this act, an Action Statement for the recovery and future management of this species has been prepared.<sup id="cite_ref-vic.gov.au.2_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vic.gov.au.2-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> On the 2007 advisory list of threatened vertebrate fauna in Victoria, the little egret is listed as <a href="/wiki/Endangered" class="mw-redirect" title="Endangered">endangered</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DSE200_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DSE200-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colonisation_of_the_New_World">Colonisation of the New World</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_egret&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Colonisation of the New World">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The little egret has now started to colonise the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>. The first record there was on <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> in April 1954. The bird began breeding on the island in 1994 and now also breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Bahamas" class="mw-redirect" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Avibirds_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avibirds-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">Ringed birds</a> from Spain provide a clue to the birds' origin.<sup id="cite_ref-Kushlan_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kushlan-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The birds are very similar in appearance to the <a href="/wiki/Snowy_egret" title="Snowy egret">snowy egret</a> and share colonial nesting sites with these birds in Barbados, where they are both recent arrivals. The little egrets are larger, have more varied foraging strategies and exert dominance over feeding sites.<sup id="cite_ref-Kushlan_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kushlan-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Little egrets are seen with increasing regularity over a wider area and have been observed from <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> in the south to <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> in the north. Birds on the east coast of North America are thought to have moved north with snowy egrets from the Caribbean. In June 2011, a little egret was spotted in Maine, in the Scarborough Marsh, near the Audubon Center.<sup id="cite_ref-wmtw_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wmtw-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
