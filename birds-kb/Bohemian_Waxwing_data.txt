<p>The <b>Bohemian Waxwing</b> (<i>Bombycilla garrulus</i>) is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Waxwing" title="Waxwing">waxwing</a> family of <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerines</a>. A sleek bird, 18–21&#160;cm long with a pointed crest, it travels in large, nomadic groups with a strong, direct flight. It breeds in coniferous forests throughout the most northern parts of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and western <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. As the <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Waxwing" title="Cedar Waxwing">Cedar Waxwing</a> inhabits only North America and the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Waxwing" title="Japanese Waxwing">Japanese Waxwing</a> only Asia, the Bohemian Waxwing is the only member of this family whose range circumnavigates all the continents just below the sub-Arctic latitudes.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohemian_Waxwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>It is larger, fatter and greyer than the Cedar Waxwing and has bright yellow, black or rusty orange color on its tail feather tips and a yellow, white, red or black stripe along the wing feathers. Under tail coverts are a deep rust color. Both beak and feet are dark and the brown eyes are set in a narrow black mask underlined with white.</p>
<p>The call is a pleasant ringing sound, similar to that of the Cedar Waxwing but lower-pitched.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohemian_Waxwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h2>
<p>The preferred nest location is usually high in a <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a> tree but feeding opportunities determine the location chosen. Each bird or pair may have more than one nest in the same general area. The nests have an outer diameter of 15&#160;cm to 18&#160;cm and are lined with fine grass, moss, and down. On average, 4 to 6 eggs are laid, the egg shells having a pale bluish color with a heavy sprinkling of blackish spots and some dark, irregular lines. Incubation is around 14 days and the young leave the nest about 13 to 15 days after hatching.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohemian_Waxwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Food and movements">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_movements">Food and movements</span></h2>
<p>Like other waxwings, its diet consists primarily of <a href="/wiki/Berry" title="Berry">berries</a> supplemented by <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, especially during the breeding season.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sidensvans_Bombycilla_garrulus_03.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Sidensvans_Bombycilla_garrulus_03.jpg/220px-Sidensvans_Bombycilla_garrulus_03.jpg" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sidensvans_Bombycilla_garrulus_03.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
The distinctive red wing tips</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This species is irruptive, moving in unpredictable migration patterns from year to year, and particularly moving south, often in huge numbers, if the berry supply fails in winter.</p>
<p>Birds in winter can be very confiding and will come into gardens for berry bushes and trees, a favourite being the <a href="/wiki/Rowan" title="Rowan">rowan</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohemian_Waxwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>Its <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> name refers to the bright red bead-like tips of the secondary feathers on its wings, which look like drops of sealing wax, while 'Bohemian' refers to the <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romani</a> (gypsies), with a comparison to this bird's wandering,<sup id="cite_ref-Holloway_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holloway-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> or to its (presumed) origin from <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a> (at the time, a relatively unknown "distant, eastern" place to most English speakers).<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from May 2010">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The generic name <i>Bombycilla</i>, from Latin <i>Bombyx</i> (<a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a> / <a href="/wiki/Silk_moth" title="Silk moth" class="mw-redirect">silk moth</a>) + <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">Scientific Latin</a> <i>cilla</i> (tail),<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> is a direct translation of the <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> name 'Sidensvans', silk-tail, and refers to the silky-soft <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">plumage</a> of the bird; the species name <i>garrulus</i> means 'talkative' and refers to a resemblance to the <a href="/wiki/European_Jay" title="European Jay" class="mw-redirect">European Jay</a>, <i>Glandularius garrulus</i>,.<sup id="cite_ref-Holloway_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holloway-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
