<p>The <b>Bohemian waxwing</b> (<i>Bombycilla garrulus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Common_starling" title="Common starling">starling</a>-sized <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird that breeds in the northern forests of the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> and North America. It has mainly buff-grey <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>, black face markings and a pointed crest. Its wings are patterned with white and bright yellow, and some feather tips have the red waxy appearance that give this species its English name. The three subspecies show only minor differences in appearance. Females are similar to males, although young birds are less well-marked and have few or no waxy wingtips. Although the Bohemian waxwing's range overlaps those of the <a href="/wiki/Cedar_waxwing" title="Cedar waxwing">cedar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japanese_waxwing" title="Japanese waxwing">Japanese waxwings</a>, it is easily distinguished from them by size and plumage differences.
</p><p>The breeding habitat is <a href="/wiki/Pinophyta" title="Pinophyta">coniferous</a> forests, usually near water. The pair build a lined cup-shaped nest in a tree or bush, often close to the trunk. The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> of 3–7 eggs is <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation" title="Egg incubation">incubated</a> by the female alone for 13–14&#160;days to hatching. The chicks are <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a> and naked, and are fed by both parents, initially mostly with insects, but thereafter mainly fruit. They <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> about 14–16&#160;days after leaving the egg. Many birds desert their nesting range in winter and <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> farther south. In some years, large numbers of Bohemian waxwings <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/irruption" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:irruption">irrupt</a> well beyond their normal winter range in search of the fruit that makes up most of their diet.
</p><p>Waxwings can be very tame in winter, entering towns and gardens in search of food, <a href="/wiki/Rowan" title="Rowan">rowan</a> berries being a particular favourite. They can <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolise</a> alcohol produced in fermenting fruit, but can still become intoxicated, sometimes fatally. Other hazards include predation by <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">birds of prey</a>, infestation by parasites and collisions with cars or windows. The Bohemian waxwing's high numbers and very large breeding area mean that it is classified as being of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a> by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohemian_waxwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Waxwing" title="Waxwing">waxwings</a> are a family, Bombycillidae, of short-tailed stocky birds with soft plumage, a head crest and distinctively patterned wings and tails. There are three species, the Bohemian, cedar, and Japanese waxwings. <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> studies and shared features such as a relatively large size, grey underparts and similar undertail patterns suggest that the Japanese and Bohemian waxwings are most closely related within the genus. Although only the cedar and Bohemian waxwings normally have red tips on their wing feathers, this feature is occasionally shown by the Japanese waxwing, suggesting that this was originally a whole-family characteristic that has been lost in one species, rather than an indicator of a close relationship. <a href="/wiki/Genetic_testing" title="Genetic testing">DNA analysis</a> confirms that the cedar waxwing diverged early from the other members of the family. Outside the genus, the closest relatives of the waxwings are believed to be the <a href="/wiki/Silky-flycatcher" title="Silky-flycatcher">silky-flycatchers</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Palmchat" title="Palmchat">palmchat</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Grey_hypocolius" title="Grey hypocolius">grey hypocolius</a>, all of which have sometimes been included in the Bombycillidae.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Bohemian waxwing was described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a> as <i>Lanius Garrulus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Linn_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linn-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The waxwings were moved to their own genus, <i>Bombycilla</i>, by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Jean_Pierre_Vieillot" title="Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot">Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot</a> in 1808.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> The genus name <i>Bombycilla</i> comes from the Greek <i>bombux</i>, "silk" and the <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">Modern Latin</a> <i>cilla</i>, "tail";<sup id="cite_ref-job74_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job74-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> this is a direct translation of the German <i>Seidenschwanz</i>, "silk-tail", and refers to the silky-soft <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">plumage</a> of the bird.<sup id="cite_ref-Holloway_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holloway-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The species name <i>garrulus</i> is the Latin for <i>talkative</i> and was applied to this bird, as "Garrulus Bohemicus", by <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Gessner" title="Conrad Gessner">Conrad Gessner</a> in 1555;<sup id="cite_ref-job171_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job171-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gessner_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gessner-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> the term is a reference to a supposed likeness to the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_jay" title="Eurasian jay">Eurasian jay</a> (<i>Garrulus glandarius</i>) rather than to the waxwing's vocalisations.<sup id="cite_ref-parkes_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-parkes-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The English name "waxwing" refers to the bright red tips of the <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#secondaries" title="Flight feather">secondary feathers</a> on its wings, which look like drops of <a href="/wiki/Sealing_wax" title="Sealing wax">sealing wax</a>, while "Bohemian" follows Gessner's usage, and may refer to the <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romani</a>, alluding to the bird's wanderings,<sup id="cite_ref-Holloway_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holloway-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> or to its presumed origin from <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cocker_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cocker-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> "Waxwing" and "Bohemian waxwing" were first recorded in 1817, the former as a reference to Vieillot's separation of this bird from the "chatterers".<sup id="cite_ref-OED_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are three recognised subspecies:<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>B. g. garrulus</i> <small>(Linnaeus, 1758)</small>: the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate subspecies</a>, breeds in northern Europe from northern Sweden east to the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural Mountains</a>.</li>
<li><i>B. g. centralasiae</i> <small>(<a href="/w/index.php?title=Grigorii_Izrailevich_Poliakov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Grigorii Izrailevich Poliakov (page does not exist)">Poliakov</a>, 1915)</small>: breeds from the Urals eastwards across northern Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>B. g. pallidiceps</i> <small>(<a href="/wiki/Anton_Reichenow" title="Anton Reichenow">Reichenow</a>, 1908)</small>: breeds in northwestern North America.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The differences between these forms are small and <a href="/wiki/Cline_(biology)" title="Cline (biology)">clinal</a>, and the species could be possibly considered as <a href="/wiki/Monotypic_taxon" title="Monotypic taxon">monotypic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> record includes <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> deposits from the UK and the <a href="/wiki/Carpathian_Mountains" title="Carpathian Mountains">Carpathian Mountains</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fozy_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fozy-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohemian_waxwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sidensvans_Bombycilla_garrulus_03.jpg" class="image"><img alt="a spread wing with yellow white and red markings" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Sidensvans_Bombycilla_garrulus_03.jpg/220px-Sidensvans_Bombycilla_garrulus_03.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Sidensvans_Bombycilla_garrulus_03.jpg/330px-Sidensvans_Bombycilla_garrulus_03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Sidensvans_Bombycilla_garrulus_03.jpg/440px-Sidensvans_Bombycilla_garrulus_03.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="2227" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sidensvans_Bombycilla_garrulus_03.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The distinctive red wing tips</div></div></div>
<p>The Bohemian waxwing is a <a href="/wiki/Common_starling" title="Common starling">starling-sized</a> bird 19–23&#160;cm (7.5–9.1&#160;in) in length with a 32–35.5&#160;cm (12.6–14.0&#160;in) wingspan, and an average weight of 55&#160;g (1.9&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It is short-tailed, mainly brownish-grey, and has a conspicuous crest on its head. The male of the nominate subspecies has a black mask through the eye and a black throat. There is a white streak behind the bill and a white curve below the eye. The lower belly is a rich chestnut colour and there are cinnamon-coloured areas around the mask. The rump is grey and the tail ends in a bright yellow band with a broad black border above it. The wings are very distinctive; the <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">flight feathers</a> are black and the <a href="/wiki/Primary_feathers" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary feathers">primaries</a> have markings that produce a yellow stripe and white "fishhooks" on the closed wing. The adult's <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#secondaries" title="Flight feather">secondaries</a> end in long red appendages with the <a href="/wiki/Sealing_wax" title="Sealing wax">sealing wax</a> appearance that gives the bird its English name. The eyes are dark brown, the bill is mainly black, and the legs are dark grey or black.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In flight, the waxwing's large flocks, long wings and short tail give some resemblance to the <a href="/wiki/Common_starling" title="Common starling">common starling</a>, and its flight is similarly fast and direct.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It clambers easily through bushes and trees but only shuffles on the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The soft, dense feathers are kept in good condition by <a href="/wiki/Preening_(bird)" title="Preening (bird)">preening</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The red waxy tips are the extended and flattened ends of feather shafts, pigmented with <a href="/wiki/Astaxanthin" title="Astaxanthin">astaxanthin</a> and enclosed in a transparent sheath. A study of the cedar waxwings showed that the red tips are few or absent until the third year of life for that related species.<sup id="cite_ref-mountjoy_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mountjoy-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> All adult waxwings have a complete <a href="/wiki/Moulting" title="Moulting">moult</a> annually between August and January. Juveniles moult at the same time but retain their flight and some other wing feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The female Bohemian waxwing is very similar to the male, but has a narrower yellow terminal band to the tail, a less defined lower edge to the black throat and slightly less distinctive wing markings. Juveniles are duller than adults, with whiter underparts, only a few red wing tips, no black on the throat and a smaller black face mask. Compared to the nominate subspecies, eastern <i>B. g. centralasiae</i> is paler, greyer and has little reddish-brown behind the bill. The American subspecies <i>B. g. pallidiceps</i> has more colouring on the cheeks and forehead than the nominate form and is otherwise generally duller in appearance.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The range of the Bohemian waxwing overlaps those of both the other members of the genus. The cedar waxwing is smaller than the Bohemian; it has browner upperparts, a white undertail and a white line above the black eye patch. Adult cedar waxwings have a yellowish belly, and all ages have less strongly patterned wings than the Bohemian waxwing.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The Japanese waxwing is easily distinguished from its relatives; it has a red terminal band to the tail, the black mask extends up the rear of the crest, and there is no yellow stripe or red tips on the wings.<sup id="cite_ref-Brazil_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brazil-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Bohemian waxwing's call is a high <a href="/wiki/Trill_(music)" title="Trill (music)">trill</a> <i>sirrrr</i>. It is less wavering and lower-pitched than that of the cedar waxwing,<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sibley_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sibley-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and longer and lower-pitched than the call of the Japanese waxwing.<sup id="cite_ref-Brazil_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brazil-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Other calls are just variants of the main vocalisation; a quieter version is used by chicks to call parents, and courtship calls, also given during nest construction, have a particularly large frequency range.<sup id="cite_ref-umich_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Although not a call as such, when a flock takes off or lands, the wings make a distinctive rattling sound that can be heard 30&#160;m (98&#160;ft) away.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohemian_waxwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Helvetinj%C3%A4rvi.JPG" class="image"><img alt="conifer woodland and river" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Helvetinj%C3%A4rvi.JPG/220px-Helvetinj%C3%A4rvi.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Helvetinj%C3%A4rvi.JPG/330px-Helvetinj%C3%A4rvi.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Helvetinj%C3%A4rvi.JPG/440px-Helvetinj%C3%A4rvi.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4272" data-file-height="2848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Helvetinj%C3%A4rvi.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Northern coniferous forest breeding habitat.</div></div></div>
<p>The Bohemian waxwing has a <a href="/wiki/Subarctic" title="Subarctic">circumpolar</a> distribution, breeding in northern regions of <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> and North America. In Eurasia, its northern nesting limit is just short of the <a href="/wiki/Tree_line" title="Tree line">treeline</a>, roughly at the 10&#160;°C July <a href="/wiki/Isotherm_(contour_line)" class="mw-redirect" title="Isotherm (contour line)">isotherm</a>, and it breeds south locally to about 51°N.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_20-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Most birds breed between 60–67°N, reaching 70°N in Scandinavia.<sup id="cite_ref-french_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-french-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The North American subspecies breeds in the northwestern and north central areas of the continent, its range extending southwards beyond the US border in the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sibley_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sibley-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This waxwing is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> with much of the breeding range abandoned as the birds move south for the winter. Migration starts in September in the north of the range, a month or so later farther south. Eurasian birds normally winter from eastern Britain through northern parts of western and central Europe, Ukraine, Kazakhstan and northern China to Japan. North American breeders have a more southeasterly trend, many birds wintering in southeast Canada, with smaller numbers in the north central and northeastern US states. Birds do not usually return to the same wintering sites in successive years.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> One bird wintering in the Ukraine was found 6,000&#160;km (3,700&#160;mi) to the east in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> in the following year.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_20-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Newton406_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newton406-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bombycilla_garrulus_-Poland_-flock.jpg" class="image"><img alt="waxwings in a bare tree" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Bombycilla_garrulus_-Poland_-flock.jpg/220px-Bombycilla_garrulus_-Poland_-flock.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Bombycilla_garrulus_-Poland_-flock.jpg/330px-Bombycilla_garrulus_-Poland_-flock.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Bombycilla_garrulus_-Poland_-flock.jpg/440px-Bombycilla_garrulus_-Poland_-flock.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bombycilla_garrulus_-Poland_-flock.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A winter flock in Poland</div></div></div>
<p>In some years, this waxwing irrupts south of its normal wintering areas, sometimes in huge numbers. The fruit on which the birds depend in winter varies in abundance from year to year, and in poor years, particularly those following a good crop the previous year, the flocks move farther south until they reach adequate supplies.<sup id="cite_ref-Newton_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newton-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-koenig_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-koenig-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> They will stay until the food runs out and move on again.<sup id="cite_ref-Newton403_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newton403-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> In what may be the largest ever irruption in Europe, in the winter of 2004–2005, more than half a million waxwings were recorded in Germany alone. This invasion followed an unusually warm, dry breeding season.<sup id="cite_ref-french_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-french-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In 1908, an American flock 60–90&#160;m (200–300&#160;ft) wide was noted as taking two to three minutes to fly over.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_15-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The breeding habitat is mature conifers, often <a href="/wiki/Spruce" title="Spruce">spruce</a> although other conifers and broadleaf trees may also be present. More open, wet areas such as lakes and peat swamps with dead and drowned trees are used for feeding on insects. Lowlands, valleys and uplands are used in Eurasia, although mountains tend to be avoided. However, the North American subspecies nests in Canada at altitudes between 900–1,550&#160;m (2,950–5,090&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_20-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Campbell_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Outside the breeding season, the waxwing will occupy a wide range of habitats as long as suitable fruits such as <a href="/wiki/Rowan" title="Rowan">rowan</a> are available. It may be found by roads, in parks and gardens or along hedges or woodlands edges. It shows little fear of humans at this time.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_15-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BWP_20-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In winter, waxwings roost communally in dense trees or hedges, sometimes with <a href="/wiki/American_robin" title="American robin">American robins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fieldfare" title="Fieldfare">fieldfares</a> or other wintering species.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohemian_waxwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohemian_waxwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bombycilla_garrulus_small.jpg" class="image"><img alt="single waxwing sitting on a nest" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Bombycilla_garrulus_small.jpg/220px-Bombycilla_garrulus_small.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="178" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Bombycilla_garrulus_small.jpg/330px-Bombycilla_garrulus_small.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Bombycilla_garrulus_small.jpg/440px-Bombycilla_garrulus_small.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="646" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bombycilla_garrulus_small.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Female on nest</div></div></div>
<p>Bohemian waxwings start their return from the wintering grounds in February or March, but northern breeders do not reach their breeding areas until April or early May. This <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a> species nests mainly from mid-June to July.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_20-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bombycilla_garrulus_MWNH_1285.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Bombycilla_garrulus_MWNH_1285.JPG/220px-Bombycilla_garrulus_MWNH_1285.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="207" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Bombycilla_garrulus_MWNH_1285.JPG/330px-Bombycilla_garrulus_MWNH_1285.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Bombycilla_garrulus_MWNH_1285.JPG/440px-Bombycilla_garrulus_MWNH_1285.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1442" data-file-height="1359" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bombycilla_garrulus_MWNH_1285.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>Waxwings are not highly territorial, and, although normally solitary breeders, several pairs may nest close together where there are a number of good nest sites.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Males may sometimes deter rivals from approaching their mates, and females may squabble over nest sites. Aggression is shown by sleeking down the feathers and crest, showing the black throat, and opening the bill. The breeding display is almost the opposite of this; the male erects his body and crest feathers, and turns his head away from the female. The male may repeatedly present a gift of a small item, often food, to his partner, placing it in her open bill. In about 90% of cases, this display does not lead to copulation.<sup id="cite_ref-bb58_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb58-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Older males have more red tips to the wings and are preferred by females.<sup id="cite_ref-umich_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bombycilla_garrulus_egg.jpg" class="image"><img alt="five white eggs in a twig nest" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Bombycilla_garrulus_egg.jpg/220px-Bombycilla_garrulus_egg.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Bombycilla_garrulus_egg.jpg/330px-Bombycilla_garrulus_egg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Bombycilla_garrulus_egg.jpg/440px-Bombycilla_garrulus_egg.jpg 2x" data-file-width="656" data-file-height="660" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bombycilla_garrulus_egg.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nest and eggs</div></div></div>
<p>The nest, built by both sexes, is a cup of thin twigs lined with softer material such as fine grass, moss, fur or <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichen</a>. It is constructed 1.3–15&#160;m (4–50&#160;ft) above the ground in a <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a> or scrub, commonly close to the trunk. The eggs are a glossy pale blue spotted with black and grey and the <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is 3–7 eggs, although five or six is most common.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_15-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BWP_20-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The average size of the egg is 24&#160;mm ×&#160;18&#160;mm (0.94&#160;in ×&#160;0.71&#160;in), and it weighs 3.8&#160;g (0.13&#160;oz), of which 5% is shell. The eggs are <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation" title="Egg incubation">incubated</a> for 13–14&#160;days by the female alone.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> She is fed regurgitated berries by her mate, and rarely leaves the nest. The chicks are <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a> and naked, and have bright red mouths; they are fed by both parents, although the male brings most of the food, mainly insects, in the first few days. The young are subsequently fed largely with fruit. The chicks <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> about 14–16&#160;days after hatching.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> They are fed by the adults for about two weeks after fledging.<sup id="cite_ref-umich_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Breeding densities of this waxwing are typically low compared to other passerines, usually less than ten birds per square kilometre (26&#160;per square mile) even in good habitat, although up to 35·6&#160;birds per square kilometre (92&#160;per square mile) have been found in Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> One brood each year is normal.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Maximum recorded ages are 5&#160;years 10&#160;months in North America and more than 13&#160;years 6&#160;months in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-umich_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-euring_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euring-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The average life expectancy is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-bto_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Significant causes of death include predation,<sup id="cite_ref-umich_24-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> collisions with windows and cars,<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and poisoning by road salt consumed when drinking.<sup id="cite_ref-tilltop_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tilltop-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohemian_waxwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rowanberries_in_late_August_2004_in_Helsinki.jpg" class="image"><img alt="bunch of red berries" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Rowanberries_in_late_August_2004_in_Helsinki.jpg/220px-Rowanberries_in_late_August_2004_in_Helsinki.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Rowanberries_in_late_August_2004_in_Helsinki.jpg/330px-Rowanberries_in_late_August_2004_in_Helsinki.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Rowanberries_in_late_August_2004_in_Helsinki.jpg/440px-Rowanberries_in_late_August_2004_in_Helsinki.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="594" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rowanberries_in_late_August_2004_in_Helsinki.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sorbus_aucuparia" title="Sorbus aucuparia">European rowan</a> is a favourite food.</div></div></div>
<p>Waxwings are primarily fruit eaters, but also consume insects during the breeding season. <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">Mosquitoes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Midge" title="Midge">midges</a> are the most common prey, but many other insects and some spiders are eaten. They are caught mainly by flycatching from an open perch, but some may be picked off vegetation.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_20-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Fruit is normally picked from trees, sometimes from the ground, and is usually swallowed whole. In the summer, <i><a href="/wiki/Vaccinium" title="Vaccinium">Vaccinium</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Rubus" title="Rubus">Rubus</a></i> species and <a href="/wiki/Shepherdia_canadensis" title="Shepherdia canadensis">Canada buffaloberry</a> are important items of their diet, while <a href="/wiki/Cotoneaster" title="Cotoneaster">cotoneaster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juniper" title="Juniper">juniper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crataegus" title="Crataegus">haws</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rose_hip" title="Rose hip">rose hips</a> and apples predominate outside the breeding season. Rowan berries are a favourite food, and are eaten whenever available.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_15-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Waxwings can eat huge numbers of berries, each bird sometimes consuming several hundred a day, more than double its own weight. One individual was recorded as eating between 600 and 1,000 cotoneaster berries in six hours, and defecating every four minutes.<sup id="cite_ref-cocker_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cocker-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Waxwings travel significant distances when feeding and help to disperse the fruit seeds.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Waxwings forage in large flocks, sometimes of several hundred birds, which enables them to overwhelm birds such as <a href="/wiki/Mistle_thrush" title="Mistle thrush">mistle thrushes</a> attempting to defend their fruit trees.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bohemian_Wax_Wing.jpg" class="image"><img alt="waxwing eating berries" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Bohemian_Wax_Wing.jpg/220px-Bohemian_Wax_Wing.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Bohemian_Wax_Wing.jpg/330px-Bohemian_Wax_Wing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Bohemian_Wax_Wing.jpg/440px-Bohemian_Wax_Wing.jpg 2x" data-file-width="780" data-file-height="624" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bohemian_Wax_Wing.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Feeding on rowan</div></div></div>
<p>Fruit is rich in sugar but deficient in other nutrients, so it must be eaten in large quantities.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Bohemian waxwings have a large liver which helps to convert sugar to energy. They can <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolise</a> <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> produced from the fermentation of those sugary fruits more efficiently than humans, but may still become intoxicated,<sup id="cite_ref-umich_24-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> sometimes fatally.<sup id="cite_ref-umich_24-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Waxwings often drink water or eat snow in winter, since the sugar in their fruit diet tends to dehydrate the birds through an <a href="/wiki/Osmosis" title="Osmosis">osmotic</a> effect. In the summer, the fruits are juicier and water is less of a problem.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the past, the arrival of waxwings sometimes coincided with <a href="/wiki/Epidemic" title="Epidemic">epidemics</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a> or <a href="/wiki/Plague_(disease)" title="Plague (disease)">plague</a>, and led to the old Dutch and Flemish name <i>Pestvogel</i>, "plague bird". The <a href="/wiki/Juniper_berry" title="Juniper berry">juniper berries</a> on which they fed were thought to offer protection, and people consumed the fruit and burned branches to fumigate their houses.<sup id="cite_ref-french_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-french-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators_and_parasites">Predators and parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohemian_waxwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Predators and parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Accipiter_nisus_Meneer_Zjeroen.jpg" class="image"><img alt="perched grey bird of prey" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Accipiter_nisus_Meneer_Zjeroen.jpg/170px-Accipiter_nisus_Meneer_Zjeroen.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Accipiter_nisus_Meneer_Zjeroen.jpg/255px-Accipiter_nisus_Meneer_Zjeroen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Accipiter_nisus_Meneer_Zjeroen.jpg/340px-Accipiter_nisus_Meneer_Zjeroen.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="1500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Accipiter_nisus_Meneer_Zjeroen.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">Eurasian sparrowhawk</a> hunts Bohemian waxwings.</div></div></div>
<p>Waxwings are preyed upon by <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">birds of prey</a> including <a href="/wiki/Rough-legged_buzzard" title="Rough-legged buzzard">rough-legged buzzards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">Eurasian sparrowhawks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prairie_falcon" title="Prairie falcon">prairie falcons</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-umich_24-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Great_grey_shrike" title="Great grey shrike">great grey shrikes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bna_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bna-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Merlin_(bird)" title="Merlin (bird)">Merlins</a> attack winter flocks, including those in cities.<sup id="cite_ref-Semenchuk_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Semenchuk-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> When alarmed, Bohemian waxwings "freeze" with bill and neck pointing upwards. If this fails, they fly, calling noisily.<sup id="cite_ref-umich_24-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umich-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Bohemian waxwings are not <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasitised</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common cuckoo</a> or its relatives in Eurasia because the cuckoo's young cannot survive on a largely fruit diet. In North America, the waxwing's breeding range has little overlap with <a href="/wiki/Brown-headed_cowbird" title="Brown-headed cowbird">brown-headed cowbird</a>, another parasitic species. Nevertheless, eggs of other birds placed in a Bohemian waxwing's nest are always rejected. This suggests that in the past, perhaps 3&#160;million years ago, the ancestral waxwing was a host of a brood parasitic species, and retains the rejection behaviour acquired then.<sup id="cite_ref-peer_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peer-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Parasitic <a href="/wiki/Mite" title="Mite">mites</a> include <i>Syringophiloidus bombycillae</i>, first identified on this species,<sup id="cite_ref-Skoracki_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Skoracki-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> and the nasal mite <i>Ptilonyssus bombycillae</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Spicer_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spicer-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Blood parasites include <i><a href="/wiki/Trypanosoma" title="Trypanosoma">Trypanosoma</a></i> species, and a <i><a href="/wiki/Leucocytozoon" title="Leucocytozoon">Leucocytozoon</a></i> first identified in this waxwing.<sup id="cite_ref-stabler_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stabler-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Bohemian waxwings may carry <a href="/wiki/Trematoda" title="Trematoda">flatworms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cestoda" title="Cestoda">tapeworms</a>, but levels of <a href="/wiki/Helminths" class="mw-redirect" title="Helminths">parasitic worm</a> infestation are generally low.<sup id="cite_ref-Rausch_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rausch-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohemian_waxwing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The global population of the Bohemian waxwing has been estimated at more than three&#160;million birds, and the breeding range covers about 12.8&#160;million&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (4.9&#160;million&#160;mi<sup>2</sup>). Although this species' population, as of 2013, appears to be declining, the decrease is not rapid nor large enough to trigger conservation vulnerability criteria. Given its high numbers and huge breeding area, this waxwing is therefore classified by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> as being of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-43">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>  The woodlands used by this species are well to the north of major human populations, and the birds can use disturbed habitats, so there are no serious long-term threats to this species.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_15-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
