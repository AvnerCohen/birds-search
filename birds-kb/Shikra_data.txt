<p>The <b>shikra</b> (<i>Accipiter badius</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a> found widely distributed in Asia and Africa where it is also called the <b>little banded goshawk</b>. The African forms may represent a separate species but have usually been considered as subspecies of the shikra. The shikra is very similar in appearance to other sparrowhawk species including the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_goshawk" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese goshawk">Chinese goshawk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">Eurasian sparrowhawk</a>. They have a sharp two note call and have the typical flap and glide flight. Their calls are imitated by <a href="/wiki/Drongo" title="Drongo">drongos</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Common_hawk-cuckoo" title="Common hawk-cuckoo">common hawk-cuckoo</a> resembles it in plumage.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identification">Identification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shikra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Identification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shikra_(Female)_at_Hodal-_I2-Haryana_IMG_7970.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Shikra_%28Female%29_at_Hodal-_I2-Haryana_IMG_7970.jpg/220px-Shikra_%28Female%29_at_Hodal-_I2-Haryana_IMG_7970.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="217" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Shikra_%28Female%29_at_Hodal-_I2-Haryana_IMG_7970.jpg/330px-Shikra_%28Female%29_at_Hodal-_I2-Haryana_IMG_7970.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Shikra_%28Female%29_at_Hodal-_I2-Haryana_IMG_7970.jpg/440px-Shikra_%28Female%29_at_Hodal-_I2-Haryana_IMG_7970.jpg 2x" data-file-width="659" data-file-height="650" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shikra_(Female)_at_Hodal-_I2-Haryana_IMG_7970.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Female (<a href="/wiki/Hodal" title="Hodal">Hodal</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>)</div></div></div>
<p>The shikra is a small raptor (26–30&#160;cm long) and like most other <i>Accipiter</i> hawks, this species has short rounded wings and a narrow and somewhat long tail. Adults are whitish on the underside with fine rufous bars while the upperparts are grey. The lower belly is less barred and the thighs are whitish. Males have a red iris while the females have a less red (yellowish orange) iris and brownish upperparts apart from heavier barring on the underparts. The females are slightly larger. The mesial stripe on the throat is dark but narrow. In flight the male seen from below shows a light wing lining (underwing coverts) and has blackish wing tips. When seen from above the tail bands are faintly marked on the lateral tail feathers and not as strongly marked as in the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">Eurasian sparrowhawk</a>. The central tail feathers are unbanded and only have a dark terminal band.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Juveniles have dark streaks and spots on the upper breast and the wing is narrowly barred while the tail has dark but narrow bands. A post juvenile transitional plumage is found with very strong barring on the contour feathers of the underside.<sup id="cite_ref-molt_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-molt-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The call is <i>pee-wee</i>, the first note being higher and the second being longer. In flight the calls are shorter and sharper <i>kik-ki ... kik-ki</i>.  The <a href="/wiki/Chinese_sparrowhawk" title="Chinese sparrowhawk">Chinese sparrowhawk</a> is somewhat similar in appearance but has swollen bright orange ceres and yellow legs with the wing tips entirely black.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shikra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several subspecies are recognized across its range and some of them may be treated as full species. It has been suggested, based on differences in morphology and calls, that the African forms do not belong to this species.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Subspecies <i>cenchroides</i> is larger and paler and found in Turkestan, Afghanistan and eastern Iran. The Indian population <i>dussumieri</i> is resident on the plains and lower hills (up to 1400 m in the Himalayas). The nominate form is found in Sri Lanka and has somewhat darker grey upperparts. The Burmese shikra <i>A. b. poliopsis</i> may represent a distinct species. The population on Car Nicobar Island, earlier treated as a subspecies <i>butleri</i><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and that on Katchall Island, <i>obsoletus</i> are now treated as a sub-species within a full species, the Nicobar sparrowhawk (<i>Accipiter butleri</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbk_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The west African population <i>A. b. sphenurus</i> is migratory while the southern African <i>A. b. polyzonoides</i> is more nomadic in its movements. In Asia only <i>A. b. cenchroides</i> is migratory.<sup id="cite_ref-molt_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-molt-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shikra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shikra_(Accipiter_badius)_with_a_Garden_Lizard_W2_IMG_8979.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Shikra_%28Accipiter_badius%29_with_a_Garden_Lizard_W2_IMG_8979.jpg/220px-Shikra_%28Accipiter_badius%29_with_a_Garden_Lizard_W2_IMG_8979.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="215" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Shikra_%28Accipiter_badius%29_with_a_Garden_Lizard_W2_IMG_8979.jpg/330px-Shikra_%28Accipiter_badius%29_with_a_Garden_Lizard_W2_IMG_8979.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Shikra_%28Accipiter_badius%29_with_a_Garden_Lizard_W2_IMG_8979.jpg/440px-Shikra_%28Accipiter_badius%29_with_a_Garden_Lizard_W2_IMG_8979.jpg 2x" data-file-width="614" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shikra_(Accipiter_badius)_with_a_Garden_Lizard_W2_IMG_8979.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Immature feeding on <i><a href="/wiki/Calotes_versicolor" class="mw-redirect" title="Calotes versicolor">Calotes versicolor</a></i></div></div></div>

<p>The shikra is found in a range of habitats including forests, farmland and urban areas. They are usually seen singly or in pairs. The flight is typical with flaps and glides. During the breeding season pairs will soar on thermals and stoop at each other. Their flight usually draws alarms among smaller birds and squirrels. They feed on rodents (including <i><a href="/wiki/Meriones_hurrianae" class="mw-redirect" title="Meriones hurrianae">Meriones hurrianae</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>), squirrels, small birds, small reptiles (mainly lizards but sometimes small snakes<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>) and insects.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Small birds usually dive through foliage to avoid a shikra and a Small Blue Kingfisher has been observed diving into water to escape. Babblers have been observed to rally together to drive away a shikra.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> They will descend to the ground to feast on emerging winged termites,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> hunt at dusk for small bats<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> (such as <i>Cynopterus sphinx</i><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>) and in rare instances they may even resort to feed on carrion.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In one instance a male was found feeding on a dead chick at the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Their calls are mimicked by <a href="/wiki/Drongo" title="Drongo">drongos</a> and this behaviour is thought to aid in stealing food by alarming other birds that the drongos associate with.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The breeding season in India is in summer from March to June. The nest is a platform similar to that of crows lined with grass. Both sexes help build the nest, twigs being carried in their feet.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Like crows, they may also make use of metal wires.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The usual clutch is 3 to 4 eggs (when eggs are removed they lay replacements and one observer noted that they could lay as many as 7 in a season<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>) which are pale bluish grey stippled on the broad end in black. The incubation period is 18 to 21 days.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shikra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The shikra was a favourite among <a href="/wiki/Falconry" title="Falconry">falconers</a> in India and Pakistan due to the ease with which it could be trained and was frequently used to procure food for the more prized falcons. They were noted for their pluck and ability to take much larger birds including partrigdes, crows and even young peafowl.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-whistler_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whistler-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The word <i>shikra</i> or <i>shikara</i> means hunter in the Hindi language (the male was called <i>chipak</i> or <i>chipka</i> based on call<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>) 
The word Shikra is borrowed from the Urdu word (شِـكْـره) which is derived from the word shikari (شِكارى) meaning hunter. Hunting in Persian is referred to as shikar (شِـكار), and in Arabic the hunt master in the Mameluke era was known as Ameer Shikar (أمير شِـكار). The word Shikari may also be used in English in the sense of "hunter".<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The word is also used in the French name <i>Le Chicquera</i> which was however given to the <a href="/wiki/Red-necked_falcon" title="Red-necked falcon">red-necked falcon</a> by Levaillant in 1799.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An <a href="/wiki/Indian_Navy" title="Indian Navy">Indian Navy</a> <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopter</a> base was named <a href="/wiki/INS_Shikra" title="INS Shikra">INS Shikra</a> in 2009.
</p><p>The shikra is also the mascot for the <a href="/wiki/149_Squadron,_Republic_of_Singapore_Air_Force" title="149 Squadron, Republic of Singapore Air Force">149 Squadron</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Singapore_Air_Force" title="Republic of Singapore Air Force">Republic of Singapore Air Force</a>, which operates the <a href="/wiki/Northrop_F-5#Upgrades" title="Northrop F-5">F5S/T Tiger IIs</a> fighter jets.
</p><p>Famous Punjabi poet <a href="/wiki/Shiv_Kumar_Batalvi" title="Shiv Kumar Batalvi">Shiv Kumar Batalvi</a> wrote a poem called "Main Ik Shikra Yaar Banaya" about a lost love wherein he compares her to a shikra.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
