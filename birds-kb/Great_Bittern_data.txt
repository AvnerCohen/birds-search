<p>The <b>Eurasian Bittern</b> or <b>Great Bittern</b> (<i>Botaurus stellaris</i>) is a wading <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">heron</a> family <a href="/wiki/Ardeidae" title="Ardeidae" class="mw-redirect">Ardeidae</a>. It is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Bittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for bittern, <i>Botaurus</i>, also refers to the bull. The other part of its scientific name, <i>stellata</i> is the Latin for <i>starry</i>, in reference to its <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">plumage</a>. Its folk names include "barrel-maker", "bog-bull", "bog hen", "bog-trotter" and "mire drum", mainly with reference to its voice or habitat. "Butterbump" derives from Norfolk and refers to its high fat content when eaten as food.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Bittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>Bitterns are thickset herons with bright, pale, buffy-brown plumage covered with dark streaks and bars, similar in appearance to the to the <a href="/wiki/American_Bittern" title="American Bittern">American Bittern</a>, <i>Botaurus lentiginosa</i>. The Eurasian or Great Bittern is 69–81 cm (27–32 in) in length, with a 100–130 cm (39–51 in) wingspan and a body mass of 0.87–1.94 kg (1.9–4.3 lb).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Their most distinctive feature is the males song in spring - a loud, very deep "boom" which can be heard for about 5 km from the bird.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Bittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>Distribution in Europe as a whole is estimated at 20-44,000 males.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> It usually inhabits <i><a href="/wiki/Phragmites" title="Phragmites">Phragmites</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Reedbed" title="Reedbed" class="mw-redirect">reedbeds</a>. The population is declining in much of its temperate <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> range. It is resident in the milder west and south, but <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrates</a> south from areas where the water freezes in winter. In the UK, the main areas are <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a> with an estimated 44 breeding pairs. In Ireland it died out as a breeding species in the mid-19th century, but in 2011 a single bird was spotted in <a href="/wiki/County_Wexford" title="County Wexford">County Wexford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Bittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Races">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Races">Races</span></h3>
<p>Besides the Eurasian race, <i>Botaurus s. stellaris</i>, another race, <i>Botaurus s. capensis</i> exists in southern Africa,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> which occurs sparingly in marshes of the east coast, the <a href="/wiki/Okavango_Delta" title="Okavango Delta">Okavango Delta</a> and the upland foothills of the <a href="/wiki/Drakensberg" title="Drakensberg">Drakensberg</a>. The southern race suffered catastrophic decline during the 20th century due to wetland degradation, and unlike the northern race it is of highest conservation concern.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Bittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<p>Usually solitary, it walks stealthily as it forages. If it senses that it has been seen, it becomes motionless, with its bill pointed upward, causing it to blend into the reeds. It is most active at dawn and dusk.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Bittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<p>Males are polygamous with each mating with up to five females. The nest is built in the previous year's standing reeds and consists of a platform some 30&#160;cm across. Four or five eggs are laid in late March and April and incubated by the female bird. After hatching, the chicks spend about two weeks in the nest and then disperse amongst the reeds.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Bittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<p>Bitterns feed on fish, eels, amphibians and invertebrates, hunting along the reed margins in shallow water.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Bittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span></h3>
<p>The mating call or contact call of the male is a deep fog-horn or bull-like <i>boom</i>, easily audible from a distance of 2 or 3 miles on a calm night. This call is mainly given between January and April. Surveys of Eurasian Bitterns are carried out by noting the number of distinct male booms in a given area.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Bittern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In fiction">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="In_fiction">In fiction</span></h2>
<p>Eurasian Bittern is proposed as a rational explanation behind the mythical creature <a href="/wiki/Drekavac" title="Drekavac">drekavac</a> in short story <i>Brave Mita and drekavac from the pond</i> by <a href="/wiki/Branko_%C4%86opi%C4%87" title="Branko Ćopić">Branko Ćopić</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The species is mentioned in <a href="/wiki/George_Crabbe" title="George Crabbe">George Crabbe</a>'s 1810 narrative poem <i>The Borough</i>, to emphasise the ostracised, solitary life of the poem's villain, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Grimes" title="Peter Grimes">Peter Grimes</a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"And the loud Bittern from the bull-rush home<br />
Gave from the Salt-ditch side the bellowing boom:"</p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thomas_McDonagh" title="Thomas McDonagh" class="mw-redirect">Thomas McDonagh</a> the Irish poet executed for his part in the <a href="/wiki/Easter_Rising" title="Easter Rising">Easter Rising</a> translated a famous Gaelic poem "The Yellow Bittern". His friend and fellow poet <a href="/wiki/Francis_Ledwidge" title="Francis Ledwidge">Francis Ledwidge</a> wrote a celebrated "Lament for Thomas McDonagh" with the opening line <i>He shall not hear the bittern cry</i>.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a> novel <a href="/wiki/The_Hound_of_the_Baskervilles" title="The Hound of the Baskervilles">The Hound of the Baskervilles</a> by Sir <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Arthur Conan Doyle">Arthur Conan Doyle</a>, the villain Stapleton proposes the boom of a Bittern as an explanation for the howl attributed to the mystical hound.</p>
