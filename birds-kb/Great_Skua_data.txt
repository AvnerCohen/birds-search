<p>The <b>great skua</b> (<i>Stercorarius skua</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> in the skua family <a href="/wiki/Stercorariidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Stercorariidae">Stercorariidae</a>. It is roughly the size of a <a href="/wiki/Herring_gull" title="Herring gull">herring gull</a>. It mainly eats fish which is taken from the surface of the sea or from other birds.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_skua&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The great skua was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> by the Danish zoologist <a href="/wiki/Morten_Thrane_Br%C3%BCnnich" title="Morten Thrane Brünnich">Morten Thrane Brünnich</a> in 1764 under the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> <i>Catharacta skua</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is now placed in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Stercorarius" class="mw-redirect" title="Stercorarius">Stercorarius</a></i> that was introduced by the French zoologist <a href="/wiki/Mathurin_Jacques_Brisson" title="Mathurin Jacques Brisson">Mathurin Jacques Brisson</a> in 1760.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ioc_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  The English name and species name "skua" is believed to originate from the Faroese <i>skúvur</i> or <i>skúgvur</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[ˈskɪkvʊər]</a></span> and is the only known bird name to originate from the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroes</a> that has come into regular use elsewhere.<sup id="cite_ref-Salomonsen1935_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salomonsen1935-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OEDskua_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OEDskua-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In Britain, it is sometimes known by the name <b>bonxie</b>, a <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a> name of <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Norse</a> origin.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-scots_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scots-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The genus name <i>Stercorarius</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means "of dung"; the food disgorged by other birds when pursued by skuas was once thought to be excrement.<sup id="cite_ref-job_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The species is <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monotypic">monotypic</a>: no <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are recognised.<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_skua&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stercorarius_skua_collage,_Runde,_Norway_06-06.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Stercorarius_skua_collage%2C_Runde%2C_Norway_06-06.jpg/220px-Stercorarius_skua_collage%2C_Runde%2C_Norway_06-06.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Stercorarius_skua_collage%2C_Runde%2C_Norway_06-06.jpg/330px-Stercorarius_skua_collage%2C_Runde%2C_Norway_06-06.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Stercorarius_skua_collage%2C_Runde%2C_Norway_06-06.jpg/440px-Stercorarius_skua_collage%2C_Runde%2C_Norway_06-06.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5120" data-file-height="2880" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stercorarius_skua_collage,_Runde,_Norway_06-06.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Displaying white wing flashes</div></div></div>
<p>Great skuas measure 50–58&#160;cm (20–23&#160;in) long and have a 125–140&#160;cm (49–55&#160;in) <a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">wingspan</a>. One study found that 112 males weighed an average of 1.27&#160;kg (2.8&#160;lb) and that 125 females weighed an average of 1.41&#160;kg (3.1&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Adults are a streaked greyish brown, with a black cap, while juveniles are a warmer brown and unstreaked below. They have a short, blunt tail, and a powerful flight. The great skua's call is a harsh <i>hah-hah-hah-hah</i>; quacking and croaking noises have also been heard. Distinguishing this skua from the other North Atlantic skuas (<a href="/wiki/Parasitic_jaeger" title="Parasitic jaeger">parasitic jaeger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pomarine_jaeger" title="Pomarine jaeger">pomarine jaeger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Long-tailed_jaeger" title="Long-tailed jaeger">long-tailed jaeger</a>) is relatively straightforward. The <a href="/wiki/Herring_gull" title="Herring gull">herring gull</a> size, massive barrel chest and white wing flashes of this bird are distinctive even at a distance. It is sometimes said to give the impression of a <a href="/wiki/Common_buzzard" title="Common buzzard">common buzzard</a>. Identification of this skua is only complicated when it is necessary to distinguish it from the closely related large southern-hemisphere skuas. Despite its name, the great skua is marginally smaller on average than the other 3 large southern-hemisphere skuas, although not by enough to distinguish them by size in the field.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Some authorities still regard the great skua as conspecific with some of these southern skuas, and as a group they have sometimes been separated in the genus <i>Catharacta</i>, although currently this taxonomy is not commonly followed.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_skua&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Genetic studies have found surprising similarities between the great skua and the pomarine skua, despite their dissimilar appearance. Many ornithologists now believe either that the great skua originated as a hybrid between the pomarine skua and one of the southern-hemisphere species,<sup id="cite_ref-Furness2003_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Furness2003-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> presumably as a result of vagrancy or migration across the equator by the southern species, or that the pomarine skua evolved from hybridization of the great skua and one of the small Arctic species (see <a href="/wiki/Pomarine_jaeger" title="Pomarine jaeger">pomarine jaeger</a> for details).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_skua&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stercorarius_skua_MWNH_0302.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Stercorarius_skua_MWNH_0302.JPG/220px-Stercorarius_skua_MWNH_0302.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Stercorarius_skua_MWNH_0302.JPG/330px-Stercorarius_skua_MWNH_0302.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Stercorarius_skua_MWNH_0302.JPG/440px-Stercorarius_skua_MWNH_0302.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4172" data-file-height="2848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stercorarius_skua_MWNH_0302.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>The great skua breeds in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, and on <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> islands, with some individuals breeding on mainland Scotland and in the northwest of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. They breed on coastal moorland and rocky islands, usually laying two spotted olive-brown eggs in grass-lined nests. Like other skuas, they will fly at the head of a human or other intruder approaching its nest. Although it cannot inflict serious damage, such an experience with a bird of this size is frightening. They are a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a> species, wintering at sea in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and regularly reaching <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> waters. Vagrant to Mediterranean countries (e.g. Turkey).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_skua&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>They eat mainly <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">carrion</a>, offal, <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a>, and occasionally <a href="/wiki/Berries" class="mw-redirect" title="Berries">berries</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Skua_attacking_Gannet_near_Stac_an_Armin.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Great_Skua_attacking_Gannet_near_Stac_an_Armin.jpg/220px-Great_Skua_attacking_Gannet_near_Stac_an_Armin.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="130" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Great_Skua_attacking_Gannet_near_Stac_an_Armin.jpg/330px-Great_Skua_attacking_Gannet_near_Stac_an_Armin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Great_Skua_attacking_Gannet_near_Stac_an_Armin.jpg/440px-Great_Skua_attacking_Gannet_near_Stac_an_Armin.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2998" data-file-height="1767" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Skua_attacking_Gannet_near_Stac_an_Armin.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Great skua attacking northern gannet near Stac an Armin (<a href="/wiki/St_Kilda,_Scotland" title="St Kilda, Scotland">St Kilda, Scotland</a>)</div></div></div><p>They will often obtain fish by robbing <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">terns</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Northern_gannet" title="Northern gannet">northern gannets</a> of their catches. They will also directly attack and kill other seabirds, up to the size of Herring Gulls. Like most other skua <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, it continues this <a href="/wiki/Kleptoparasitism" title="Kleptoparasitism">piratical behaviour</a> throughout the year, showing less agility and more brute force than the smaller skuas when it harasses its victims. A common technique is to fly up to a gannet in mid-air and grab it by the wing, so that it stalls and falls into the sea, where the great skua then physically attacks it until it surrenders its catch. Due to its size, aggressive nature and fierce defence of its nest, the great skua has little to fear from other predators. While <a href="/wiki/Fledgling_(birds)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fledgling (birds)">fledglings</a> can fall prey to <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_fox" title="Arctic fox">Arctic fox</a>, healthy adults are threatened only by greater raptors such as the <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">golden eagle</a>, the <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_eagle" title="White-tailed eagle">white-tailed eagle</a>, and more rarely, by the <a href="/wiki/Orca" class="mw-redirect" title="Orca">orca</a>.
</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predation_account">Predation account</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_skua&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Predation account">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The great skua is an aggressive pirate of the seas, deliberately harassing birds as large as gannets to steal a free meal. It also readily kills and eats smaller birds such as puffins. Great skuas show little fear of humans – anybody getting close to the nest will be repeatedly dive-bombed by the angry adult.
Unusual behaviour by <a href="/wiki/St_Kilda,_Scotland" title="St Kilda, Scotland">St Kilda's</a> skuas was recorded in 2007 during research into recent falls in the <a href="/wiki/Leach%27s_storm_petrel" title="Leach&#39;s storm petrel">Leach's storm petrel</a> population. Using night vision gear, ecologists observed the skuas preying on the petrels at night, a remarkable strategy for a seabird.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
