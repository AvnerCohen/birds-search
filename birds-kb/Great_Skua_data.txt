<p>The <b>Great Skua</b> (<i>Stercorarius skua</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Skua" title="Skua">skua</a> family Stercorariidae. In Britain, it is sometimes known by the name <b>Bonxie</b>, a <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a> name of unknown origin.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Skua&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>This is a large skua, measuring 50–58 cm (20–23 in) long with a 125–140 cm (49–55 in) <a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">wingspan</a>. The adult is streaked greyish brown, with a black cap, while the juvenile is a warmer brown and unstreaked below. Its tail is short and blunt. The flight is direct and powerful. The Brown Skua's call is a harsh <i>hah-hah-hah-hah</i>; quacking and croaking noises have also been heard. Distinguishing this skua from the other North Atlantic skuas (<a href="/wiki/Arctic_Skua" title="Arctic Skua" class="mw-redirect">Arctic Skua</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pomarine_Skua" title="Pomarine Skua">Pomarine Skua</a> and <a href="/wiki/Long-tailed_Skua" title="Long-tailed Skua" class="mw-redirect">Long-tailed Skua</a>) is relatively straightforward. The <a href="/wiki/Herring_Gull" title="Herring Gull">Herring Gull</a> size, massive barrel chest and white wing flashes of this bird are distinctive even at a distance. It is sometimes said to give the impression of a <a href="/wiki/Common_Buzzard" title="Common Buzzard">Common Buzzard</a> (which in America would be called a hawk). Identification of this skua is only complicated when it is necessary to distinguish it from the closely related large southern-hemisphere skuas. Some authorities still regard the Great Skua as conspecific with some of these southern skuas, and as a group they have sometimes been separated in the genus <i>Catharacta</i>, although currently this is not commonly followed.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Skua&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span></h2>
<p>Genetic studies have found surprising similarities between the Great Skua and the Pomarine Skua, despite their dissimilar appearance. Many ornithologists now believe either that the Great Skua originated as a hybrid between the Pomarine Skua and one of the southern-hemisphere species [Furness and Hamer, 2003], presumably as a result of vagrancy or migration across the equator by the southern species, or that the Pomarine Skua evolved from hybridization of the Great Skua and one of the small Arctic species (see <a href="/wiki/Pomarine_Skua" title="Pomarine Skua">Pomarine Skua</a> for details).</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Skua&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h2>
<p>The Great Skua breeds in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> islands, with a few on mainland Scotland. It breeds on coastal moorland and rocky islands, usually laying two spotted olive-brown eggs in grass-lined nests. Like other skuas, it will fly at the head of a human or other intruder approaching its nest. Although it cannot inflict serious damage, such an experience with a bird of this size is frightening. It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>, wintering at sea in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and regularly reaching <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> waters. Vagrant to Mediterranean countries (e.g. Turkey).</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Skua&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span></h2>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> eats mainly fish, which it often obtains by robbing <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">terns</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Northern_Gannet" title="Northern Gannet">Northern Gannets</a> of their catches. It will also directly attack and kill other seabirds, up to the size of <a href="/wiki/Great_Black-backed_Gull" title="Great Black-backed Gull">Great Black-backed Gulls</a>. Like most other skua <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, it continues this <a href="/wiki/Kleptoparasitism" title="Kleptoparasitism">piratical behaviour</a> throughout the year, showing less agility and more brute force than the smaller skuas when it harasses its victims. A common technique is to fly up to a gannet in mid-air and grab it by the wing, so that it stalls and falls into the sea, where the Great Skua then physically attacks it until it surrenders its catch. It also eats small mammals, eggs, berries and carrion. Due to its size, aggressive nature and fierce defence of its nest, the Great Skua has little to fear from other predators. While <a href="/wiki/Fledgling" title="Fledgling">fledglings</a> can fall prey to <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Fox" title="Arctic Fox" class="mw-redirect">Arctic Fox</a>, healthy adults are threatened only by greater raptors such as the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Eagle" title="Golden Eagle">Golden Eagle</a> and the <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_Eagle" title="White-tailed Eagle">White-tailed Eagle</a>, and more rarely by the <a href="/wiki/Orca" title="Orca" class="mw-redirect">Orca</a> whale.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Skua&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Predation account">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Predation_account">Predation account</span></h2>
<p>Unusual behaviour by <a href="/wiki/St_Kilda,_Scotland" title="St Kilda, Scotland">St Kilda's</a> Skuas was recorded in 2007 during research into recent falls in the <a href="/wiki/Leach%27s_Storm_Petrel" title="Leach's Storm Petrel">Leach's Storm Petrel</a> population. Using night vision gear, ecologists observed the skuas preying on the petrels at night, a remarkable strategy for a seabird.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
