<p>The <b>Common Crossbill</b> (<i>Loxia curvirostra</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finch</a> family Fringillidae. It breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Spruce" title="Spruce">spruce</a> forests of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, where it is known as <b>Red Crossbill</b>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>; some populations (possibly different species) breed in <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a> forests in certain areas of all three continents, and in North America, also in <a href="/wiki/Douglas-fir" title="Douglas-fir">Douglas-fir</a>. It nests in <a href="/wiki/Pinophyta" title="Pinophyta">conifers</a>, laying 3–5 eggs.</p>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Crossbill" title="Crossbill">crossbill</a> is mainly resident, but will regularly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">irrupt</a> south if its food source fails. This species will form flocks outside the breeding season, often mixed with other crossbills.</p>
<p>The crossbills are characterised by the <a href="/wiki/Mandible" title="Mandible">mandibles</a> crossing at their tips, which gives the group its English name. They are specialist feeders on <a href="/wiki/Conifer_cone" title="Conifer cone">conifer cones</a>, particularly the various spruce species, and the unusual bill shape is an adaptation to assist the extraction of the seeds from the cone. Some populations, which may be different species, also feed on Douglas-fir and various pine species.</p>
<p>Adult males tend to be red or orange in colour, and females green or yellow, but there is much variation.</p>
<p>This species is difficult to separate from <a href="/wiki/Parrot_Crossbill" title="Parrot Crossbill">Parrot Crossbill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Crossbill" title="Scottish Crossbill">Scottish Crossbill</a>, both of which breed within its Eurasian range. The identification problem is less severe in North America, where only Red Crossbill and <a href="/wiki/Two-barred_Crossbill" title="Two-barred Crossbill">White-winged Crossbill</a> occur. However, the <a href="/wiki/South_Hills_Crossbill" title="South Hills Crossbill">South Hills Crossbill</a>, occurring in the South Hills and <a href="/wiki/Albion_Mountains" title="Albion Mountains">Albion Mountains</a> in <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a> has recently been described as a new species (<i>Loxia sinesciuris</i>). It is virtually identical to the Red Crossbill differing slightly in body dimensions and calls and shows a very low degree of hybridization with the Red Crossbill.<sup id="cite_ref-sinesciuris_0-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sinesciuris-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">Plumage</a> distinctions from Parrot and Scottish Crossbills are negligible. The head and bill are smaller than in either of the other species. Care is needed to identify this species. The <i>glip</i> or <i>chup</i> call is probably the best indicator.</p>
<p>Work on vocalisation in North America suggest that, in that continent alone, there are eight or nine populations of Red Crossbill with different calls, which rarely if ever interbreed. These forms also vary in terms of bill size and structure, and specialise on the seed cones of different species of conifer. Few <a href="/wiki/Ornithology" title="Ornithology">ornithologists</a> seem inclined to give these forms species status at present.</p>
<p>Some large-billed, pine-feeding populations currently assigned to this species in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">Mediterranean</a> area may possibly be better referred to either Parrot Crossbill or alternatively to new species in their own right, but as yet, research into them is still at a very early stage. These include Balearic Crossbill <i>L. curvirostra balearica</i> and North African Crossbill <i>L. curvirostra poliogyna</i>, feeding primarily on <a href="/wiki/Aleppo_Pine" title="Aleppo Pine" class="mw-redirect">Aleppo Pine</a> (<i>Pinus halepensis</i>); Cyprus Crossbill <i>L. curvirostra guillemardi</i>, feeding primarily on <a href="/wiki/European_black_pine" title="European black pine" class="mw-redirect">European black pine</a> (<i>Pinus nigra</i>); and an as-yet unidentified crossbill with a Parrot Crossbill-size bill feeding primarily on <a href="/wiki/Bosnian_Pine" title="Bosnian Pine">Bosnian Pine</a> (<i>Pinus heldreichii</i>) in the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>. These populations also differ on plumage, with the Balearic, North African and Cyprus races having yellower males, and the Balkan type having deep purple-pink males; this however merely reflects the differing <a href="/wiki/Anthocyanin" title="Anthocyanin">anthocyanin</a> content of the cones they feed on, as these pigments are transferred to the feathers.</p>

</ul>

</li>
<li>Type 5, corresponds with <i>Loxia c. benti/grinnelli/bendirei</i>
<ul>
<li>Recorded on <a href="/wiki/Lodgepole_Pine" title="Lodgepole Pine">Lodgepole Pine</a> <i>Pinus contorta latifolia</i></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Type 6, corresponds with <i>Loxia c. stricklandi</i>
<ul>
<li>Uses <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">Pine</a> species in Section Trifoliae</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Type 7, corresponds with <i>Loxia c. benti/grinnelli/bendirei</i>
<ul>
<li>In terms of diet this type is possibly a generalist</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Type 8, corresponds with <i>Loxia c. percna</i>
<ul>
<li>Uses <a href="/wiki/Black_Spruce" title="Black Spruce" class="mw-redirect">Black Spruce</a> <i>Picea mariana</i></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Type 9, corresponds with <a href="/wiki/South_Hills_Crossbill" title="South Hills Crossbill">South Hills Crossbill</a>
<ul>
<li>Isolated population of <a href="/wiki/Lodgepole_Pine" title="Lodgepole Pine">Lodgepole Pine</a> <i>Pinus contorta latifolia</i></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><b>Summers' list of European Common Crossbill forms (with Parrot &amp; Scottish) based on Flight/Excitement calls:</b></p>
<ul>
<li>1A, corresponds with "British" Crossbill</li>
<li>1B, corresponds with "Parakeet" Crossbill</li>
<li>2B, corresponds with "Wandering" Crossbill</li>
<li>2D, corresponds with Parrot Crossbill <i>Loxia pytyopsittacus</i></li>
<li>3C, corresponds with Scottish Crossbill <i>Loxia scotica</i></li>
<li>3D, corresponds with Balearic Crossbill</li>
<li>3E, corresponds with North African Crossbill</li>
<li>4E, corresponds with "Glip" Crossbill</li>
<li>5D, corresponds with Cyprus Crossbill</li>
</ul>
<p><b>"The Sound Approach's" classification of European Common Crossbill types based on call-types:</b>;<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>"Wandering" Crossbill (Type A - flight call "Keep")</li>
<li>"Bohemian" Crossbill (Type B - flight call "Weet")</li>
<li>"Glip" Crossbill (Type C - flight call "Glip")</li>
<li>"Phantom" Crossbill (Type D - flight call "Jip")</li>
<li>"British" Crossbill (Type E - flight call "Chip")</li>
<li>"Scarce" Crossbill (Type F - flight call "Trip")</li>
<li>"Parakeet" Crossbill (Type X - flight call "Cheep")</li>
</ul>
