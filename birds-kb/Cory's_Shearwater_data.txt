<p>The <b>Cory's Shearwater</b> (<i>Calonectris diomedea</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Shearwater" title="Shearwater">shearwater</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> family <a href="/wiki/Procellariidae" title="Procellariidae">Procellariidae</a>.</p>
<p>This species breeds on islands and cliffs in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a>, with the odd outpost on the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> coast of <a href="/wiki/Iberian_peninsula" title="Iberian peninsula" class="mw-redirect">Iberia</a>. The nest is on open ground or among rocks or less often in a burrow where one white egg is laid, and is visited at night to minimise predation from large <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>. In late summer and autumn, most <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> into the Atlantic as far north as the south-western coasts of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. They return to the Mediterranean in February. The biggest colony is located in <a href="/wiki/Savage_Islands" title="Savage Islands">Savage Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>.</p>
<p>This bird flies with long glides, and always with wings bowed and angled slightly back, unlike the stiff, straight-winged flight of the similarly-sized <a href="/wiki/Great_Shearwater" title="Great Shearwater">Great Shearwater</a>.</p>
<p>This shearwater is identifiable by its size, at 45–56&#160;cm in length and with a 112–126&#160;cm wingspan. It has brownish-grey upperparts, white underparts and yellowish bill. It lacks the brown belly patch, dark shoulder markings and black cap of Great Shearwater.</p>
<p>There are two subspecies, the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a> subspecies <i>C. d. diomedea</i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic" class="mw-redirect">Atlantic</a> subspecies <i>C. d. borealis</i>. They are closely similar in appearance, although the Atlantic race is larger, and with a stouter bill. They are best distinguished by the pattern of the underwing. Being fairly distinct (Heidrich <i>et al.</i> 1998), they are sometimes regarded as separate species, <i>C. d. diomedea</i> then being named Scopoli's Shearwater.</p>
<p>Cory's Shearwater feeds on <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc" class="mw-redirect">molluscs</a> and offal, and can dive deep (50 feet/15 meters or more) in search of prey. It readily follows fishing boats, where it indulges in noisy squabbles. This is a gregarious species, which can be seen in large numbers from ships or appropriate headlands. The <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">Bay of Biscay</a> ferries are particularly good for this species. It is silent at sea, but at night the breeding colonies are alive with raucous cackling calls.</p>
<p>This bird was named after the American ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Charles_B._Cory" title="Charles B. Cory">Charles B. Cory</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_Shearwater" title="Cape Verde Shearwater">Cape Verde Shearwater</a> <i>C. edwardsii</i> (<a href="/wiki/Emile_Oustalet" title="Emile Oustalet" class="mw-redirect">Oustalet</a>, 1883) was once considered a subspecies of Cory's Shearwater but has recently been split off as a separate species (Snow &amp; Perrins 1998). It is <a href="/wiki/Endemism_in_birds" title="Endemism in birds" class="mw-redirect">endemic</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_Islands" title="Cape Verde Islands" class="mw-redirect">Cape Verde Islands</a>. It has an all dark, slim bill, and darker head and upperparts than Cory's. The flight has been described as rather more typically shearwater-like than Cory's, with stiffer and more rapid wing beats.</p>
