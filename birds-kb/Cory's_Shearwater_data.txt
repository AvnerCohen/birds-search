<p><b>Cory's shearwater</b> (<i>Calonectris borealis</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Shearwater" title="Shearwater">shearwater</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> family <a href="/wiki/Procellariidae" title="Procellariidae">Procellariidae</a>. The genus name <i>Calonectris</i> comes from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>kalos</i>, "good" and <i>nectris</i>, "swimmer", and <i>borealis</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "northern".<sup id="cite_ref-job90_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job90-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The English name is after the American <a href="/wiki/Ornithologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Ornithologist">ornithologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Charles_B._Cory" title="Charles B. Cory">Charles B. Cory</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cory%27s_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species breeds on <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Berlengas" title="Berlengas">Berlengas</a> Archipelago in <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>. They nest on open ground or among rocks or less often in a <a href="/wiki/Burrow_nest" class="mw-redirect" title="Burrow nest">burrow</a> where one white egg is laid. The burrow is visited at night to minimise predation from large <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>. In late summer and autumn, most birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> into the Atlantic as far north as the south-western coasts of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. They return to the Mediterranean in February. The biggest colony is located in <a href="/wiki/Savage_Islands" title="Savage Islands">Savage Islands</a>, Madeira.
</p><p>This bird flies with long glides, and always with wings bowed and angled slightly back, unlike the stiff, straight-winged flight of the similarly sized <a href="/wiki/Great_shearwater" title="Great shearwater">great shearwater</a>.
</p><p>Cory's shearwater feeds on <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mollusc" class="mw-redirect" title="Mollusc">molluscs</a> and offal, and can dive deep (15&#160;m (49&#160;ft) or more) in search of prey.  It readily follows fishing boats, where it indulges in noisy squabbles. This is a gregarious species, which can be seen in large numbers from ships or appropriate headlands. The <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">Bay of Biscay</a> ferries are particularly good for this species. It is silent at sea, but at night the breeding colonies are alive with raucous cackling calls.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cory%27s_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This shearwater is identifiable by its size, at 45–56&#160;cm (18–22&#160;in) in length and with a 112–126&#160;cm (44–50&#160;in) wingspan. It has brownish-grey upperparts, white underparts and a yellowish bill. It lacks the brown belly patch, dark shoulder markings and black cap of the great shearwater.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cory%27s_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Once considered two subspecies, (the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>  <i>C. d. diomedea</i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> <i>C. d. borealis</i>), <a href="/wiki/Scopoli%27s_shearwater" title="Scopoli&#39;s shearwater">Scopoli's shearwater</a> and Cory's shearwater are now split into two distinct species. They are similar in appearance, although the Atlantic species is larger with a stouter bill. They are best distinguished by the pattern of the underwing.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_shearwater" title="Cape Verde shearwater">Cape Verde shearwater</a> <i>C. edwardsii</i> (<a href="/wiki/Emile_Oustalet" class="mw-redirect" title="Emile Oustalet">Oustalet</a>, 1883) was once considered a subspecies of Cory's shearwater but has recently been split off as a separate species (<a href="#CITEREFSnowPerrinsGillmor1998">Snow, Perrins &amp; Gillmor 1998</a>). It is <a href="/wiki/Endemism_in_birds" class="mw-redirect" title="Endemism in birds">endemic</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Cape Verde Islands">Cape Verde Islands</a>. It has an all dark, slim bill, and darker head and upperparts than Cory's. The flight has been described as rather more typically shearwater-like than the Cory's, with stiffer and more rapid wing beats.
</p>
