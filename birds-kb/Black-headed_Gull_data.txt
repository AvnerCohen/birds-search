<p>The <b>black-headed gull </b> (<i>Chroicocephalus ridibundus</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a> that breeds in much of  the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> including <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and also in coastal eastern <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Most of the population is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and winters further south, but some <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> reside in the milder westernmost areas of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. Some black-headed gulls also spend the winter in northeastern <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, where it was formerly known as the common black-headed gull. As is the case with many gulls, it was previously placed in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Larus" title="Larus">Larus</a></i>.
</p><p>The genus name <i> Chroicocephalus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>khroizo</i>, "to colour", and <i>kephale</i>, "head". The specific <i>ridibundus</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "laughing", from <i>ridere</i> "to laugh".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The black-headed gull displays a variety of compelling behaviours and adaptations. Some of these include removing eggshells from ones nest after hatching, begging coordination between siblings, differences between sexes, conspecific brood parasitism, and extra-pair paternity. They are an overwintering species, found in a variety of different habitats.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-headed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This gull is 38–44&#160;cm (15–17&#160;in) long with a 94–105&#160;cm (37–41&#160;in) wingspan and weighs from 190-400 g (6.7-14.1 oz).
</p><p>In flight, the white leading edge to the wing is a good field mark. The summer adult has a chocolate-brown head (not black, although does look black from a distance), pale grey body, black tips to the primary wing feathers, and red bill and legs. The hood is lost in winter, leaving just two dark spots. Immature birds have a mottled pattern of brown spots over most of the body.<sup id="cite_ref-Peterson_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peterson-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It breeds in colonies in large reed beds or marshes, or on islands in lakes, nesting on the ground. Like most gulls, it is highly gregarious in winter, both when feeding or in evening roosts. It is not a pelagic species and is rarely seen at sea far from coasts.
</p><p>The black-headed gull is a bold and opportunistic feeder. It eats insects, fish, seeds, worms, scraps, and carrion in towns, or invertebrates in ploughed fields with equal relish. It is a noisy species, especially in colonies, with a familiar "kree-ar" call. Its scientific name means <i>laughing gull</i>.
</p><p>This species takes two years to reach maturity. First-year birds have a black terminal tail band, more dark areas in the wings, and, in summer, a less fully developed dark hood. Like most gulls, black-headed gulls are long-lived birds, with a maximum age of at least 32.9 years recorded in the wild, in addition to an anecdote now believed of dubious authenticity regarding a 63-year-old bird.<sup id="cite_ref-anage_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anage-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-headed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To be found over much of Europe, except Spain, Italy and Greece.<sup id="cite_ref-Peterson_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peterson-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It is also found in across the Palearctic to Japan and E China.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It is an occasional visitor to the east coast of North America.
</p><p>And also in some Caribbean islands.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-headed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eggshell_removal">Eggshell removal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-headed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eggshell removal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Eggshell removal is a behaviour seen in birds once the chicks have hatched, observed mostly to reduce risk of predation.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Removing the eggshell acts as a way of camouflage to avoid predators seeing the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-:13_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The further away egg shells are from the nest, the lower the predation risk.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Black-headed gull eggs experience predation from different species of birds, foxes, stoats, and even other black-headed gulls. Although mothers show some form of aggressiveness when a predator is near, in the first 30 minutes, wet chicks can be easily taken by other black-headed gulls after hatching when the parents of the wet chick are distracted.<sup id="cite_ref-:13_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Black headed gulls also carry away other objects that do not belong in the nest. The removal of eggshells and other objects is important not only in the incubation period but also during the first few days after the eggs hatch. However, the removal process seems to increase as time goes on.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The removal is done by both the male and female parents, normally lasts a few seconds and is done three times a year.<sup id="cite_ref-:13_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A black-headed gull is able to differentiate an egg shell from an egg by acknowledging its thin, serrated, white, edge. Therefore, the weight of the egg or eggshell does not play a role when determining its value.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Earlier_eggshell_removal_hypotheses">Earlier eggshell removal hypotheses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-headed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Earlier eggshell removal hypotheses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Other hypotheses have attempted to explain the survival value of black-headed gulls removing their eggshells from the nest, including:<sup id="cite_ref-:13_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>The sharp edges of the shells after hatching could harm the chicks<sup id="cite_ref-:13_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The eggshell could somehow intrude during the brooding<sup id="cite_ref-:13_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The eggshell could slip over the unhatched egg, creating a double shell<sup id="cite_ref-:13_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Some of the moist organic material left from the shell could lead to a production of bacteria and mould<sup id="cite_ref-:13_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-headed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Begging_coordination_between_siblings">Begging coordination between siblings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-headed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Begging coordination between siblings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Black-headed gulls feed their young by regurgitating it onto the ground, rather than into each chick one at a time. The parents tend to accommodate their regurgitation amounts for how intense the nest begging is, from both an individual chick or a group of chicks begging together. Chicks who are siblings, have learned this behaviour and begin synchronizing their begging signals to decrease the costs as an individual and increase the benefits as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-:22_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:22-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The rate of parental food regurgitation to chicks increases with begging intensity.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The amount and response of begging signals differs throughout the nestling period. Usually, there is 3-5 begging events/hour, each lasting around one minute.<sup id="cite_ref-:22_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:22-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> High intensity begging behaviour appears at the end of the first week in the nest, but the coordination between multiple chicks emerge during the last week of the nestling period. The more siblings present, the more they coordinate their begging while decreasing the number of begging.<sup id="cite_ref-:22_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:22-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sex_differences">Sex differences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-headed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sex differences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Male chicks have less of a chance of survival when compared to female chicks. Black-headed gulls are a sexually size-dimorphic species, so the larger sex is at a disadvantage when the amount of food sources are low.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Male birds are more likely to be born in the first egg and female birds are more likely to be born in the third. The position of a female black-headed gull in response to the food available when laying the eggs can predict the offsprings characteristics.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Conspecific_brood_parasitism">Conspecific brood parasitism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-headed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Conspecific brood parasitism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Conspecific brood parasitism is a behaviour that occurs when females lay their eggs in another females nest, of the same species.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It can reduce the cost of incubation and nestling young by passing it on to another bird. Black-headed gulls usually lay three egg clutches, and the first two are normally larger than the third.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The third egg normally has the lowest survival rate, while the first or second are usually the parasitic eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most of the egg dumping occurs within the beginning of the egg laying period. The parasitic eggs being laid in another conspecifics nest increases the chance of hatching and may occur because of nest desertion or a nest being taken over by another bird.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Multiple eggs in a nest from different mothers may also result from intra-specific nest parasitism,<sup id="cite_ref-:4_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> joint female nesting,<sup id="cite_ref-:5_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and nest takeover.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Intra-specific nest parasitism is a disadvantage to the hosts because the female could end up taking care of the parasitic chicks over her own and therefore neglecting them and reducing their fitness. Another disadvantage for the host is that incubating more chicks than their own takes up more energy.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Extra-pair_paternity">Extra-pair paternity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-headed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Extra-pair paternity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The rate of extra-pair paternity (EPP) has a large variation between populations of black-headed gulls. It is primarily a context-dependent strategy, meaning not all black headed gulls experience this behaviour.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The variation between populations of extra-pair paternity can be explained by the variation it has on the advantages and disadvantages it has on a female, as well, as the variation in pressure on a females choice.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The differences in the rate of EPP may be determined by multiple different factors: life history traits, ecological factors or different behavioural strategies of males.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Central.E2.80.93periphery_gradient_within_colonies"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Central–periphery_gradient_within_colonies">Central–periphery gradient within colonies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-headed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Central–periphery gradient within colonies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Egg-laying can be earlier in Black-headed Gulls nesting in the centre of the colony, with central pairs tending to lay larger eggs, which have a higher hatching success, than pairs nesting at the periphery of the colony. Centrally nesting individuals have also been found to be in better condition and have higher genetic quality.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Walking_displays">Walking displays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-headed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Walking displays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Black-headed gulls display both head-bobbing walking (HBW) and non-bobbing walking (NBW). Head-bobbing walking is expressed by a hold phase and a thrust phase. The hold phase in black-headed gulls occurs mainly during the single support phase and is when the bird balances its head to equal the environment.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Head-bobbing walking occurs during a seeking type foraging by walking through water and includes benefits such as enhancing motion and pattern detection and gathering depth information from motion parallax during the thrust phase.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Non-bobbing walking occurs when black-headed gulls are displaying a waiting behaviour while foraging on flat surfaces.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synchronization">Synchronization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black-headed_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Synchronization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Observations on the behavior of black-headed gulls show that black-headed gulls individuals synchronize their vigilance activity with other black-headed gulls neighbors. Synchronization in black-headed gulls groups is dependent on the distance between the black-headed gulls members.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
