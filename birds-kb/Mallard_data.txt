class="mw-redirect">temperate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical" class="mw-redirect">subtropical</a> Americas, Europe, Asia, and North Africa, and has been <a <p>The male birds (drakes) have a glossy green head and are grey on wings and belly, while the females have mainly brown-speckled <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>. Mallards live in wetlands, eat water plants and small animals, and are gregarious. This species is the ancestor of most breeds of <a href="/wiki/Domestic_duck" title="Domestic duck">domestic ducks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Mallard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and evolution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_evolution">Taxonomy and evolution</span></h2>
<p>The Mallard was one of the many <a href="/wiki/Aves_in_the_10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="Aves in the 10th edition of Systema Naturae">bird species originally described</a> by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his 18th-century work, <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>, and still bears its original <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"Mallard" is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i>malart</i> or <i>mallart</i> "wild drake", although its ultimate derivation is unclear. It may be related to an <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> masculine proper name <i>Madelhart</i>, clues lying in the alternate English forms "maudelard" or "mawdelard".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Mallards frequently interbreed with their closest relatives in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Anas" title="Anas">Anas</a></i>, such as the <a href="/wiki/American_Black_Duck" title="American Black Duck">American Black Duck</a>, and also with species more distantly related, for example the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Pintail" title="Northern Pintail">Northern Pintail</a>, leading to various <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrids</a> that may be fully fertile.<sup id="cite_ref-Phillips_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phillips-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> This is quite unusual among different species, and apparently is because the Mallard <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolved</a> very rapidly and recently, during the <a href="/wiki/Late_Pleistocene" title="Late Pleistocene">Late Pleistocene</a>. The distinct lineages of this radiation are usually kept separate due to non-overlapping ranges and <a href="/wiki/Behaviour" title="Behaviour" class="mw-redirect">behavioural</a> cues, but are still not fully genetically incompatible.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from February 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Mallards and their domesticated <a href="/wiki/Conspecific" title="Conspecific" class="mw-redirect">conspecifics</a> are, of course, also fully interfertile.</p>
<p>Mallards appear to be closer to their <a href="/wiki/Indo-Pacific" title="Indo-Pacific">Indo-Pacific</a> relatives than to their American ones judging from <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a>. Considering <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA" class="mw-redirect">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/D-loop_replication" title="D-loop replication">D-loop</a> <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">sequence</a> data,<sup id="cite_ref-Kulikova_et_al._2005_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kulikova_et_al._2005-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> they may have evolved in the general area of <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>; Mallard bones rather abruptly appear in food remains of ancient humans and other deposits of <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> bones in Europe, without a good candidate for a local predecessor species.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from August 2010">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The large <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a> <a href="/wiki/Paleosubspecies" title="Paleosubspecies" class="mw-redirect">paleosubspecies</a> which made up at least the European and west Asian populations during the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> has been named <i>Anas platyrhynchos palaeoboschas</i>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from October 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Haplotypes" title="Haplotypes" class="mw-redirect">Haplotypes</a> typical of American Mallard relatives and <a href="/wiki/Spotbill" title="Spotbill" class="mw-redirect">Spotbills</a> can be found in Mallards around the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Sea" title="Bering Sea">Bering Sea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kulikova_et_al._2004_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kulikova_et_al._2004-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Islands" title="Aleutian Islands">Aleutian Islands</a> hold a population of Mallards that appear to be evolving towards a subspecies, as <a href="/wiki/Gene_flow" title="Gene flow">gene flow</a> with other populations is very limited.<sup id="cite_ref-Kulikova_et_al._2005_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kulikova_et_al._2005-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The size of the Mallard varies <a href="/wiki/Cline_(biology)" title="Cline (biology)">clinally</a>, and birds from <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, although larger than birds further south, have smaller bills and are stockier. They are sometimes separated as <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>, the Greenland Mallard (<i>A. p. conboschas</i>).<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from February 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Mallard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mallard_speculum.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Mallard_speculum.jpg/220px-Mallard_speculum.jpg" width="220" height="264" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Mallard_speculum.jpg/330px-Mallard_speculum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Mallard_speculum.jpg/440px-Mallard_speculum.jpg 2x" /></a>

<p>The Mallard is a medium-sized waterfowl species, although is often slightly heavier than most other <a href="/wiki/Anatinae" title="Anatinae">dabbling ducks</a>. It is 50–65 cm (20–26 in) long (of which the body makes up around two-thirds), has a wingspan of 81–98 cm (32–39 in),<sup id="cite_ref-BWP505_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP505-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> and weighs 0.72–1.58 kg (1.6–3.5 lb).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CRC_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Among standard measurements, the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> is 25.7 to 30.6 cm (10.1 to 12.0 in), the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> is 4.4 to 6.1 cm (1.7 to 2.4 in) and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> is 4.1 to 4.8 cm (1.6 to 1.9 in).<sup id="cite_ref-Madge_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The breeding male Mallard is unmistakable, with a glossy bottle-green head and white collar which demarcates the head from the purple-tinged brown breast, grey brown wings, and a pale grey belly. The rear of the male is black, with the dark tail having white borders.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP506_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP506-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The bill of the male is a yellowish orange tipped with black while that of the female is generally darker ranging from black to mottled orange. The female Mallard is predominantly mottled with each individual feather showing sharp contrast from buff to very dark brown, a coloration shared by most female dabbling ducks, and has buff cheeks, eyebrow, throat and neck with a darker crown and eye-stripe.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP506_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP506-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Both male and female Mallards have distinct iridescent purple blue <a href="/wiki/Speculum_feathers" title="Speculum feathers">speculum feathers</a> edged with white, prominent in flight or at rest, though temporarily shed during the annual summer moult. Upon hatching, the plumage colouring of the duckling is yellow on the underside and face (with streaks by the eyes) and black on the backside (with some yellow spots) all the way to the top and back of the head. Its legs and bill are also black. As it nears a month in age, the duckling's plumage will start becoming drab, looking more like the female (though its plumage is more streaked) and its legs will lose their dark grey colouring.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP506_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP506-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Two months after hatching, the fledgling period has ended and the duckling is now a juvenile. Between three to four months of age, the juvenile can finally begin flying as its wings are fully developed for flight (which can be confirmed by the sight of purple speculum feathers). Its bill will soon lose its dark grey colouring and its sex can finally be distinguished by three factors. The bill colouring is yellow in males, black and orange for females. The breast feathers are reddish-brown for males, brown for females. The centre tail feather is curled for males (called a drake feather), straight for females.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from June 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>During the final period of maturity leading up to adulthood (6–10 months of age), the plumage of female juveniles remains the same while the plumage of male juveniles slowly changes to its characteristic colours.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from April 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This plumage change also applies to adult Mallard males when they transition in and out of their non-breeding (<a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/eclipse" class="extiw" title="wikt:eclipse">eclipse</a>) plumage at the beginning and the end of the summer moulting period. The adulthood age for Mallards is 14 months and the average life expectancy is 20 years.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from October 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Several species of duck have brown-plumaged females which can be confused with the female Mallard. The female <a href="/wiki/Gadwall" title="Gadwall">Gadwall</a> (<i>A. strepera</i>) has an orange-lined bill, white belly, black and white speculum which is seen as a white square on the wings in flight, and is a smaller bird.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP506_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP506-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> More similar to the female Mallard in North America are the American Black Duck (<i>A. rubripes</i>), which is notably darker hued in both sexes than the Mallard, and the <a href="/wiki/Mottled_Duck" title="Mottled Duck">Mottled Duck</a> (<i>A. fulvigula</i>), which is somewhat darker than the female Mallard, with no white edge on the speculum and slightly different bare-part coloration.</p>
<p>In captivity, <a href="/wiki/Domestic_duck" title="Domestic duck">domestic ducks</a> come in wild-type plumages, white, and other colours. Most of these colour variants are also known in domestic Mallards not bred as livestock, but kept as pets, <a href="/wiki/Aviary" title="Aviary">aviary</a> birds, etc., where they are rare but increasing in availability.</p>
<p>A noisy species, the male has a nasal call, and a high-pitched whistle, while the female has a deeper <i>quack</i> stereotypically associated with ducks.<sup id="cite_ref-Rogers_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rogers-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BWP507_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP507-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Mallard is a rare example of both <a href="/wiki/Allen%27s_Rule" title="Allen's Rule" class="mw-redirect">Allen's Rule</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bergmann%27s_Rule" title="Bergmann's Rule" class="mw-redirect">Bergmann's Rule</a> in birds. Bergmann's Rule, which states that polar forms tend to be larger than related ones from warmer climates, has numerous examples in birds. Allen's Rule says that appendages like ears tend to be smaller in polar forms to minimize heat loss, and larger in tropical and desert equivalents to facilitate heat diffusion, and that the polar taxa are stockier overall. Examples of this rule in birds are rare, as they lack external ears. However, the bill of ducks is very well supplied with blood vessels and is vulnerable to cold.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from February 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Due to the malleability of the Mallard's genetic code, which gives it its vast interbreeding capability, mutations in the genes that decide plumage colour are very common and have resulted in a wide variety of hybrids such as Brewer's Duck (Mallard × <a href="/wiki/Gadwall" title="Gadwall">Gadwall</a>, <i>Anas strepera</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Mallard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Flying_mallard_duck_-_female.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Flying_mallard_duck_-_female.jpg/220px-Flying_mallard_duck_-_female.jpg" width="220" height="158" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Flying_mallard_duck_-_female.jpg/330px-Flying_mallard_duck_-_female.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Flying_mallard_duck_-_female.jpg/440px-Flying_mallard_duck_-_female.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flying_mallard_duck_-_female.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>The Mallard is widely distributed across the Northern Hemisphere, North America from southern and central Alaska to Mexico, the Hawaiian Islands, and across Eurasia, from Iceland and southern Greenland and parts of Morocco (North Africa) in the west, Scandinavia to the north, and to Siberia, Japan, and China in the east.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP505_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP505-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> It is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> in the northern parts of its breeding range, and winters farther south. For example, in North America it winters south to Mexico, but also regularly strays into Central America and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> between September and May.<sup id="cite_ref-Herrera_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Herrera-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Mallard inhabits a wide range of habitat and climates, from Arctic Tundra to subtropical regions. It is found in both fresh- and salt-water <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a>, including parks, small ponds, rivers, lakes and <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuaries</a>, as well as shallow inlets and open sea within sight of the coastline. Water depths of less than 1 metre (3.3&#160;ft) are preferred, birds avoiding areas more than a few metres deep. They are attracted to bodies of water with aquatic vegetation.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP507_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP507-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Mallard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Mallard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<p>The Mallard is omnivorous and very flexible in its foods choice. Its diet may vary based on several factors, including the stage of the breeding cycle, short-term variations in available food, <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrient</a> availability, and <a href="/wiki/Interspecific_competition" title="Interspecific competition">inter</a>- and <a href="/wiki/Intraspecific_competition" title="Intraspecific competition">intraspecific competition</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Krapu_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krapu-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The majority of the Mallard's diet seems to be made up of <a href="/wiki/Gastropod" title="Gastropod" class="mw-redirect">gastropods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">lepidopterans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dragonfly" title="Dragonfly">dragonflies</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Caddisfly" title="Caddisfly">caddisflies</a>), <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oligochaeta" title="Oligochaeta">worms</a>, many varieties of seeds and plant matter, and roots and <a href="/wiki/Tubers" title="Tubers" class="mw-redirect">tubers</a>. During the breeding season, male birds were recorded to have eaten 37.6% animal matter and 62.4% plant matter, most notably <i><a href="/wiki/Echinochloa_crus-galli" title="Echinochloa crus-galli">Echinochloa crus-galli</a></i>, and nonlaying females ate 37.0% animal matter and 63.0% plant matter, while laying females ate 71.9% animal matter and only 28.1% plant matter.<sup id="cite_ref-Swanson_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Swanson-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Plants generally make up a larger part of the bird's diet, especially during autumn migration and in the winter.<sup id="cite_ref-Gruenhagen_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gruenhagen-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Combs_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Combs-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It usually feeds by dabbling for plant food or grazing; there are reports of it eating <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a>. It usually nests on a river bank, but not always near water. It is highly gregarious outside of the breeding season and forms large <a href="/wiki/Flock_(birds)" title="Flock (birds)">flocks</a>, which are known as <i>sords</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Mallards usually form pairs (in October and November) only until the female lays eggs at the start of nesting season which is around the beginning of spring, at which time she is left by the male who joins up with other males to await the moulting period which begins in June. During the brief time before this, however, the males are still sexually potent and some of them either remain on standby to sire replacement clutches (for female Mallards that have lost or abandoned their previous clutch) or forcibly mate with females that appear to be isolated or unattached regardless of their species and whether or not they have a brood of ducklings.</p>
<p>The nesting period can be very stressful for the female since she lays more than half her body weight in eggs. She requires a lot of rest and a feeding/loafing area that is safe from predators. When seeking out a suitable nesting site, the female's preferences are areas that are well concealed, inaccessible to ground predators, or have few predators nearby. This can include nesting sites in urban areas such as roof gardens, enclosed courtyards, and flower boxes on window ledges and balconies more than one story up, which the ducklings cannot leave safely without human intervention. The clutch is 8–13&#160;eggs, which are incubated for 27–28&#160;days to hatching with 50–60&#160;days to fledgling. The ducklings are <a href="/wiki/Precocial" title="Precocial">precocial</a> and fully capable of swimming as soon as they hatch. However, <a href="/wiki/Imprinting_(psychology)" title="Imprinting (psychology)">filial imprinting</a> compels them to instinctively stay near the mother not only for warmth and protection but also to learn about and remember their habitat as well as how and where to forage for food. When ducklings mature into flight-capable juveniles, they learn about and remember their traditional migratory routes (unless they are born and raised in captivity). After this, the juveniles and the mother may either part or remain together until the breeding season arrives.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from September 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>When they pair off with mating partners, often one or several drakes end up "left out". This group sometimes targets an isolated female duck, even one of a different species, and proceeds to chase and peck at her until she weakens, at which point the males take turns copulating with the female. Lebret (1961) calls this behaviour "Attempted Rape Flight" and Cramp &amp; Simmons (1977) speak of "rape-intent flights". Male Mallards also occasionally chase other male ducks of a different species, and even each other, in the same way. In one documented case of "homosexual necrophilia", a male Mallard copulated with another male he was chasing after the chased male died upon flying into a glass window.<sup id="cite_ref-Moeliker_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moeliker-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> This paper was awarded with an <a href="/wiki/Ig_Nobel_Prize" title="Ig Nobel Prize">Ig Nobel Prize</a> in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-MacLeod_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacLeod-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Mallards are opportunistically targeted by <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasites</a>, occasionally having eggs laid in their nests by <a href="/wiki/Redhead_(duck)" title="Redhead (duck)" class="mw-redirect">Redheads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ruddy_Duck" title="Ruddy Duck">Ruddy Ducks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Scaup" title="Lesser Scaup">Lesser Scaup</a>, Gadwalls, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Shoveler" title="Northern Shoveler">Northern Shovelers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Pintail" title="Northern Pintail">Northern Pintails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cinnamon_Teal" title="Cinnamon Teal">Cinnamon Teal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_Goldeneye" title="Common Goldeneye">Common Goldeneyes</a>, and other Mallards. These eggs are generally accepted when they resemble the eggs of the host Mallard, although the hen may attempt to eject them or even abandon the nest if parasitism occurs during egg laying.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Mallards of all ages (but especially young ones) and in all locations must contend with a wide diversity of predators including <a href="/wiki/Raptor_birds" title="Raptor birds" class="mw-redirect">raptors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mustelidae" title="Mustelidae">mustelids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corvid" title="Corvid" class="mw-redirect">corvids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">raccoons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Opossum" title="Opossum">opossums</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skunk" title="Skunk">skunks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turtle" title="Turtle">turtles</a>, large <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Felidae" title="Felidae">felids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canidae" title="Canidae">canids</a>, including domesticated ones.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> The most prolific natural predators of adult Mallards are <a href="/wiki/Red_Fox" title="Red Fox" class="mw-redirect">Red Fox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hawk" title="Hawk">hawks</a>, although both kill far fewer than human hunters.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Mallard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anas_platyrhynchos_oustaleti_last_male.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Anas_platyrhynchos_oustaleti_last_male.jpg/220px-Anas_platyrhynchos_oustaleti_last_male.jpg" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1a/Anas_platyrhynchos_oustaleti_last_male.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1a/Anas_platyrhynchos_oustaleti_last_male.jpg 2x" /></a>

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anas_platyrhynchos_oustaleti_last_male.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>Unlike many waterfowl, Mallards have benefited from human alterations to the world. They are very adaptable, being able to live and even thrive in urban areas which may have supported more localized, sensitive species of waterfowl before development. The release of <a href="/wiki/Feral" title="Feral">feral</a> Mallards in areas where they are not native sometimes creates problems through interbreeding with indigenous <a href="/wiki/Waterfowl" title="Waterfowl">waterfowl</a>. These <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">non-migratory</a> Mallards interbreed with indigenous wild ducks from local populations of closely related species through <a href="/wiki/Genetic_pollution" title="Genetic pollution">genetic pollution</a> by producing fertile offspring. Complete <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridization</a> of various species of wild ducks gene pools could result in the extinction of many indigenous waterfowl. The wild Mallard itself is the ancestor of most <a href="/wiki/Domestic_duck" title="Domestic duck">domestic ducks</a> and its naturally evolved wild gene pool gets genetically polluted in turn by the <a href="/wiki/Domesticated" title="Domesticated" class="mw-redirect">domesticated</a> and feral populations.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-krugerpark.co.za_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-krugerpark.co.za-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Mallard is considered an <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> in New Zealand. There, and elsewhere, Mallards are spreading with increasing <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a> and hybridizing with local relatives.<sup id="cite_ref-Rhymer96_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rhymer96-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Over time, a continuum of hybrids ranging between almost typical examples of either species will develop; the <a href="/wiki/Speciation" title="Speciation">speciation</a> process beginning to reverse itself.<sup id="cite_ref-Rhymer06_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rhymer06-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> This has created <a href="/wiki/Conservation_biology" title="Conservation biology">conservation</a> concerns for relatives of the Mallard, such as the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Duck" title="Hawaiian Duck">Hawaiian Duck</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Rhymer96_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rhymer96-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Griffin_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Griffin-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> the <i>A. s. superciliosa</i> subspecies of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Black_Duck" title="Pacific Black Duck">Pacific Black Duck</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Rhymer96_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rhymer96-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gillespie_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gillespie-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rhymer94_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rhymer94-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Williams_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/American_Black_Duck" title="American Black Duck">American Black Duck</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Rhymer96_30-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rhymer96-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Johnsgard_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnsgard-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Avise_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avise-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mank_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mank-38"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Mottled_Duck" title="Mottled Duck">Mottled Duck</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Rhymer96_30-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rhymer96-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mazourek_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mazourek-39"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-McCracken_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McCracken-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Meller%27s_Duck" title="Meller's Duck">Meller's Duck</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Young_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Young-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Yellow-billed_Duck" title="Yellow-billed Duck">Yellow-billed Duck</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Rhymer06_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rhymer06-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Duck" title="Mexican Duck">Mexican Duck</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Rhymer96_30-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rhymer96-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-McCracken_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McCracken-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> in the latter case even leading to a dispute whether these birds should be considered a species<sup id="cite_ref-AOU_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOU-42"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> (and thus entitled to more conservation research and funding) or included in the Mallard.</p>
<p>The availability of Mallards, Mallard ducklings, and fertilized Mallard eggs for public sale and private ownership, either as livestock or as pets, is currently legal in the United States except for the state of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> which has currently banned domestic ownership of Mallards. This is to prevent hybridisation with the native <a href="/wiki/Mottled_Duck" title="Mottled Duck">Mottled Duck</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Mallards are also causing severe "genetic pollution" of South Africa's <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> by breeding with endemic ducks, although the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> applies to the Mallard. The hybrids of Mallards and the <a href="/wiki/Yellow-billed_Duck" title="Yellow-billed Duck">Yellow-billed Duck</a> are fertile and can produce more hybrid offspring. If this continues, only hybrids will occur and in the long term this will result in the extinction of various indigenous waterfowl. The Mallard duck can cross breed with 63 other species and is posing a severe threat to the genetic integrity of indigenous waterfowl. Mallards and their hybrids compete with indigenous birds for resources such as food, nest sites and roosting sites.<sup id="cite_ref-krugerpark.co.za_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-krugerpark.co.za-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The Eastern or Chinese <a href="/wiki/Spot-billed_Duck" title="Spot-billed Duck">Spot-billed Duck</a> is currently introgressing into the Mallard populations of the <a href="/wiki/Primorsky_Krai" title="Primorsky Krai">Primorsky Krai</a>, possibly due to <a href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" title="Habitat (ecology)" class="mw-redirect">habitat</a> changes from <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kulikova_et_al._2004_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kulikova_et_al._2004-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Mallard" title="Mariana Mallard">Mariana Mallard</a> was a resident <a href="/wiki/Allopatric" title="Allopatric" class="mw-redirect">allopatric</a> population—in most respects a good species—apparently initially derived from Mallard-<a href="/wiki/Pacific_Black_Duck" title="Pacific Black Duck">Pacific Black Duck</a> hybrids;<sup id="cite_ref-Yamashina_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yamashina-44"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> unfortunately, it became <a href="/wiki/Extinct" title="Extinct" class="mw-redirect">extinct</a> in the 1980s. In addition, feral domestic ducks interbreeding with Mallards have led to a size increase—especially in drakes—in most Mallards in urban areas. Rape flights between normal-sized females and such stronger males are liable to end with the female being drowned by the males' combined weight.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from February 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Laysan_Duck" title="Laysan Duck">Laysan Duck</a> is an insular relative of the Mallard with a very small and fluctuating population. Mallards sometimes arrive on its island home during migration, and can be expected to occasionally have remained and hybridized with Laysan Ducks as long as these species have existed. But these hybrids are less well adapted to the peculiar ecological conditions of <a href="/wiki/Laysan_Island" title="Laysan Island" class="mw-redirect">Laysan Island</a> than the local ducks, and thus have lower <a href="/wiki/Fitness_(biology)" title="Fitness (biology)">fitness</a>, and furthermore, there were—apart from a brief time in the early 20th century when the Laysan Duck was almost extinct—always many more Laysan Ducks than stray Mallards. Thus, in this case, the hybrid lineages would rapidly fail.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from February 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the cases mentioned above, however, ecological changes and hunting have led to a decline of local species; for example, the New Zealand Grey Duck population declined drastically due to overhunting in the mid-20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> Hybrid offspring of Hawaiian Ducks seem to be less well-adapted to native habitat, and utilizing them in reintroduction projects apparently reduces success.<sup id="cite_ref-Rhymer96_30-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rhymer96-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kirby_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kirby-45"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> In summary, the problems of Mallards "hybridizing away" relatives is more a consequence of local ducks declining than of Mallards spreading; <a href="/wiki/Allopatric_speciation" title="Allopatric speciation">allopatric speciation</a> and isolating behaviour have produced today's diversity of Mallard-like ducks despite the fact that in most if not all of these populations, hybridization must have occurred to some extent.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from February 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Mallard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span></h2>
<p>The Mallard is depicted in a marginal decoration of the 15th century English illuminated manuscript the <a href="/wiki/Sherborne_Missal" title="Sherborne Missal">Sherborne Missal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Since 1933, the <a href="/wiki/Peabody_Hotel" title="Peabody Hotel">Peabody Hotel</a> in <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Downtown Memphis, Tennessee">Downtown Memphis, Tennessee</a> has maintained a long tradition of keeping one Mallard drake and four Mallard hens, called <i>The Peabody Ducks</i>, as a popular hotel attraction and as guests of honor. The Mallards are provided by a local farmer and friend of the Peabody Hotel and are rotated out and returned to the farm for a new team of Mallards every three months. This tradition has also been maintained and observed at the other Peabody Hotels in <a href="/wiki/Little_Rock,_Arkansas" title="Little Rock, Arkansas">Little Rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orlando,_Florida" title="Orlando, Florida">Orlando</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The children's <a href="/wiki/Picture_book" title="Picture book">picture book</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Make_Way_for_Ducklings" title="Make Way for Ducklings">Make Way for Ducklings</a></i>, published in <a href="/wiki/1941_in_literature" title="1941 in literature">1941</a> and winner of the <a href="/wiki/Caldecott_Medal" title="Caldecott Medal">1942 Caldecott Medal</a> for its illustrations, is the story of a pair of Mallards who decide to raise their family on an island in the lagoon in <a href="/wiki/Boston_Public_Garden" title="Boston Public Garden" class="mw-redirect">Boston Public Garden</a> in <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-makeway_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-makeway-48"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Duck_Head" title="Duck Head">Duck Head</a>, a U.S. clothing brand, uses the image of a Mallard's head as its <a href="/wiki/Logo" title="Logo">logo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-brandhistory_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brandhistory-49"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
