<p>The <b>ortolan</b> (<i>Emberiza hortulana</i>), also called <b>ortolan bunting</b>, is a bird in the <a href="/wiki/Bunting_(bird)" title="Bunting (bird)">bunting</a> family <a href="/wiki/Emberizidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Emberizidae">Emberizidae</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> family now separated by most modern scholars from the <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fringillidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Fringillidae">Fringillidae</a>. The genus name <i>Emberiza</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> <i lang="goh" title="Old High German (ca. 750-1050) language text">Embritz</i>, a bunting. The specific <i>hortulana</i> is from the Italian name for this bird, <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">ortolana</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The English <i>ortolan</i> is derived from <a href="/wiki/Middle_French" title="Middle French">Middle French</a> <i lang="frm" title="Middle French (ca. 1400-1600) language text">hortolan</i>, "gardener".<sup id="cite_ref-OED_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The ortolan is served in <a href="/wiki/French_cuisine" title="French cuisine">French cuisine</a>, typically cooked and eaten whole. Traditionally diners cover their heads with their napkin, or a towel, while eating the delicacy.  The bird is so widely used that its French populations dropped dangerously low, leading to laws restricting its use in 1999. In September 2007, the French government announced its intent to enforce long-ignored laws protecting the bird.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ortolan_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ortolan bunting was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">tenth edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> and retains its original <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial</a> name of <i>Emberiza hortulana</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The species is <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monotypic">monotypic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular phylogenetic">molecular phylogenetic</a> study of the buntings published in 2008 found that the ortolan bunting is most closely related to <a href="/wiki/Cretzschmar%27s_bunting" title="Cretzschmar&#39;s bunting">Cretzschmar's bunting</a> (<i>Emberiza caesia</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ortolan_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ortolan bunting is 16–17&#160;cm (6.3–6.7&#160;in) in length and has a wing-span of 23–29&#160;cm (9.1–11.4&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994209_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrampPerrins1994209-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  In appearance and habits it much resembles its relative the <a href="/wiki/Yellowhammer" title="Yellowhammer">yellowhammer</a>, but lacks the bright colouring of that species; the ortolan's head, for instance, is greenish-grey, instead of a bright yellow. The song of the male ortolan resembles that of the yellowhammer.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ortolan_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A native of most European countries and <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">western Asia</a>.  It reaches as far north as Scandinavia and beyond the Arctic Circle, frequenting cornfields and their neighbourhoods. It is an uncommon vagrant in spring, and particularly autumn, to the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>.
</p><p>It was spotted at Kenjar Coastal Karnataka, India, in November 2018 and photographed by Birdwatchers. Some birders commented that it is the first photographic record of an ortolan bunting in India.<sup id="cite_ref-Ortolan_Bunting_Spotted_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ortolan_Bunting_Spotted-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ortolan_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ortolan nests are placed on or near the ground.
</p><p>The maximum age recorded is 6 years and 10 months for a bird found dead in Switzerland.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Seeds are the natural diet, but <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a> and other insects are taken when feeding their young.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_food">As food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ortolan_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: As food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">For centuries, a rite of passage for French gourmets was the eating of the Ortolan. These tiny birds—captured alive, force-fed, then drowned in <a href="/wiki/Armagnac_(brandy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Armagnac (brandy)">Armagnac</a>—were roasted whole and eaten that way, bones and all, while the diner draped his head with a linen napkin to preserve the precious aromas and, some believe, to hide from God.</div>

<p>The birds are caught with <a href="/wiki/Net_(textile)" title="Net (textile)">nets</a> set during their autumn migratory flight to Africa. They are then kept in covered cages or boxes. The birds react to the dark by gorging themselves on grain, usually millet seed, until they double their bulk. The birds are then thrown into a container of <a href="/wiki/Armagnac_(brandy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Armagnac (brandy)">Armagnac</a>, which both drowns and marinates the birds.<sup id="cite_ref-Telegraph_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CNBC_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNBC-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The bird is roasted for eight minutes and then plucked.  The consumer then places the bird feet first into their mouth while holding onto the bird's head.  The ortolan is then eaten whole, with or without the head, and the consumer spits out the larger bones.  The traditional way French gourmands eat ortolans is to cover their heads and face with a large napkin or towel while consuming the bird.  The purpose of the towel is debated.  Some claim it is to retain the maximum aroma with the flavour as they consume the entire bird at once, others have stated "Tradition dictates that this is to shield – from God’s eyes – the shame of such a decadent and disgraceful act",<sup id="cite_ref-Telegraph_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and others have suggested the towel hides the consumers spitting out bones.<sup id="cite_ref-ABC_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  This use of the towel was begun by a priest, a friend of <a href="/wiki/Jean_Anthelme_Brillat-Savarin" title="Jean Anthelme Brillat-Savarin">Jean Anthelme Brillat-Savarin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>At one time, the island of <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a> formed a chief depot for the export of ortolans, which were pickled in spices and vinegar and packed in casks containing from 300 to 400 each. In the early 20th century, between 400 and 500 casks were annually exported from Cyprus.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Noted_meals">Noted meals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ortolan_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Noted meals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Three_Emperors_Dinner" title="Three Emperors Dinner">Three Emperors Dinner</a> in 1867 included ortolans on toast among its 16 courses.</li>
<li>In 1975, food critic <a href="/wiki/Craig_Claiborne" title="Craig Claiborne">Craig Claiborne</a> made a winning $300 bid in an auction for a dinner for two, courtesy of <a href="/wiki/American_Express" title="American Express">American Express</a>, at any restaurant in the world that takes its credit card.  Claiborne selected Chez Denis in Paris for a $4,000 meal<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> ($18,773 in 2017) that included a course of ortolans.</li>
<li>In 1995, former French President <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Mitterrand" title="François Mitterrand">François Mitterrand</a>'s last New Year's Eve meal included this specially prepared bird.<sup id="cite_ref-Telegraph_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_status">Legal status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ortolan_bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legal status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Emberiza_hortulana_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.217_Neschers.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Emberiza_hortulana_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.217_Neschers.jpg/220px-Emberiza_hortulana_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.217_Neschers.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="211" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Emberiza_hortulana_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.217_Neschers.jpg/330px-Emberiza_hortulana_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.217_Neschers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Emberiza_hortulana_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.217_Neschers.jpg/440px-Emberiza_hortulana_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.217_Neschers.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3388" data-file-height="3245" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Emberiza_hortulana_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.217_Neschers.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ortolan eggs</div></div></div>
<p>Ortolan hunting was banned in France in 1999, but the law was poorly enforced and it is thought that up to 50,000 ortolans were illegally killed each year during the autumn migration: mostly birds from breeding grounds in Finland and the Baltic area. According to France's <a href="/wiki/BirdLife_International" title="BirdLife International">League for the Protection of Birds</a>, France's ortolan population fell 30% between 1997 and 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-Telegraph2_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph2-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In 2007, the French government vowed to strictly enforce some existing rules about banning the practice, with the maximum fine set at €6,000 (£4,800 or $6,728). Killing and cooking ortolans is banned across the EU. In 2007, the pressure from France's League for Protection of Birds and from the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> resulted in the French government promising to enforce the EU directive protecting the ortolan. After several years of active citizen watch revealing little if any change in the field situation,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> the local representative of the government repeated this statement in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>European Union member states prohibit:<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>deliberate killing or capture of these birds by any method;</li>
<li>deliberate destruction of, or damage to, their nests and eggs or removal of their nests;</li>
<li>taking their eggs in the wild and keeping these eggs;</li>
<li>deliberate disturbance of these birds particularly during the period of breeding and rearing, insofar as this would have a significant negative effect on the birds;</li>
<li>keeping birds, the hunting and capture of which is prohibited;</li>
<li>sale, transport for sale, keeping for sale and the offering for sale of live or dead birds and of any readily recognizable parts or derivatives of these birds.</li></ul>
<p>The Ortolan bunting's population as of 2018 is listed by the IUCN as Least Concern (LC).
</p>
