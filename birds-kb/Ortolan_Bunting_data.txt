<p>The <b>Ortolan</b>, or <b>Ortolan Bunting</b> (<i>Emberiza hortulana</i>) is a bird in the <a href="/wiki/Bunting_(bird)" title="Bunting (bird)">bunting</a> family <a href="/wiki/Emberizidae" title="Emberizidae">Emberizidae</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> family now separated by most modern authors from the <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fringillidae" title="Fringillidae" class="mw-redirect">Fringillidae</a>. The bird's common name is <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>hortulanus</i>, the gardener bird, (from <i>hortus</i>, a garden).</p>
<p>A native of most <a href="/wiki/European_countries" title="European countries" class="mw-redirect">European countries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Asia" title="Western Asia">western Asia</a>, the Ortolan <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrates</a> in autumn to tropical <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, returning at the end of April or beginning of May.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from December 2010">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Its distribution throughout its breeding range seems to be very local, and for this no obvious reason can be assigned. It was said in France to prefer wine-growing districts;<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from June 2008">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but it certainly does not feed upon grapes, and is found equally in countries where vineyards are unknown. It reaches as far north as Scandinavia and beyond the Arctic Circle, frequenting cornfields and their neighbourhoods. It is an uncommon vagrant in spring and particularly autumn to the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>.</p>
<p>The Ortolan is 16&#160;cm in length and weighs 20–25 grams (0.71–0.88 oz). In appearance and habits it much resembles its <a href="/wiki/Congener" title="Congener">congener</a> the <a href="/wiki/Yellowhammer" title="Yellowhammer">Yellowhammer</a>, but lacks the bright colouring of that species; the Ortolan's head, for instance, is greenish-grey, instead of a bright yellow. The somewhat monotonous<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from December 2010">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> song of the cock resembles that of the Yellowhammer.</p>
<p>Ortolan nests are placed on or near the ground; the eggs seldom<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from December 2010">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> show the hair-like markings so characteristic of most buntings' eggs.</p>
<p>Seeds are the natural diet, but <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a> and other insects are eaten when feeding young.</p>
<p>In September 2007, the French Government announced its intent to enforce long ignored laws protecting the bird.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Ortolan_Bunting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gastronomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Gastronomy">Gastronomy</span></h2>
<blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px; display:table;">
<p>For centuries, a rite of passage for French gourmets has been the eating of the Ortolan. These tiny birds—captured alive, force-fed, then drowned in <a href="/wiki/Armagnac_(drink)" title="Armagnac (drink)" class="mw-redirect">Armagnac</a>—were roasted whole and eaten that way, bones and all, while the diner draped his head with a linen napkin to preserve the precious aromas and, some believe, to hide from God.</p>
<p style="text-align: right;">— <cite><i><a href="/wiki/The_Wine_Spectator" title="The Wine Spectator" class="mw-redirect">The Wine Spectator</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></cite></p>
</blockquote>
<p>However Wine Spectator Magazine was wrong in saying the birds are force fed (as it is impractical to do with such a niche market and the creatures' size). Instead the ortolan is placed in a dark box with millet, and as a natural reaction to darkness it proceeds to eat continuously. Also saying that diners drape their head with a napkin to 'hide from God' as if they were doing something shameful is incorrect, as it is actually to hide the mess, inevitably made while eating a bird in a single bite, from your fellow dinners as a courtesy.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>One way French diners ate ortolans was to cover their heads and face with a large napkin for the gourmet's aesthetic desire to absorb the maximum odour with the flavor. This famous use of the towel was launched by a priest, a friend of <a href="/wiki/Jean_Anthelme_Brillat-Savarin" title="Jean Anthelme Brillat-Savarin">Jean Anthelme Brillat-Savarin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1958 film <i><a href="/wiki/Gigi" title="Gigi">Gigi</a></i>, Gigi attends a luncheon with her Great Aunt Alicia as part of her instruction in the life of a <a href="/wiki/Courtesan" title="Courtesan">courtesan</a>. Ortolan are served as the main course, and the young girl struggles to eat the meal and answer a series of questions put to her by her demanding instructor.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Mitterrand" title="François Mitterrand">François Mitterrand</a>'s last meal included this specially prepared bird which was illegal to prepare and eat at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Anthony_Bourdain" title="Anthony Bourdain">Anthony Bourdain</a> describes the eating of ortolans in the opening to his 2010 book <i><a href="/wiki/Medium_Raw" title="Medium Raw">Medium Raw</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1975, food critic <a href="/wiki/Craig_Claiborne" title="Craig Claiborne">Craig Claiborne</a> made a winning $300 bid in an auction for a dinner for two, courtesy of <a href="/wiki/American_Express" title="American Express">American Express</a>, at any restaurant in the world that takes its credit card. Claiborne selected Chez Denis in Paris for a $4000 meal<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> that included a course of ortolans.</p>
