<p>The <b>wrens</b> are mostly small, brownish <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> birds in the mainly <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <b>Troglodytidae</b>. There are approximately 80 <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <b>true wrens</b> in approximately 20 <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genera</a>. Only the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Wren" title="Eurasian Wren">Eurasian Wren</a> occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a>, where in Anglophone regions it is commonly known simply as the "wren" as it is the originator of the name. The name wren has been applied to other unrelated birds, particularly the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_wren" title="New Zealand wren">New Zealand wrens</a> (Acanthisittidae) and the Australian wrens (<a href="/wiki/Maluridae" title="Maluridae">Maluridae</a>).</p>
<p>Most wrens are small and rather inconspicuous, except for their loud and often complex songs. Notable exceptions are the relatively large members of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Campylorhynchus" title="Campylorhynchus">Campylorhynchus</a></i>, which can be quite bold in their behavior. Wrens have short wings that are barred in most species, and they often hold their tails upright. As far as known, wrens are primarily <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" title="Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect">insectivorous</a>, eating insects, spiders and other small <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a>, but many species also eat vegetable matter and some will take small frogs/lizards.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_0-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Wren&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name and use of the term wren">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Name_and_use_of_the_term_wren">Name and use of the term wren</span></h2>
<p>The English name <i>wren</i> derives from Middle English <i>wrenne</i>, Old English <i>wraenna</i>, attested (as <i>werna</i>) very early, in an 8th century gloss. It is cognate to <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> <i>wrendo, wrendilo</i> and Icelandic <i>rindill</i> (the latter two including an additional diminutive <i>-ilan</i> suffix). The Icelandic name is attested in <a href="/wiki/Old_Icelandic" title="Old Icelandic" class="mw-redirect">Old Icelandic</a> (<a href="/wiki/Edda" title="Edda">Eddaic</a>) <i>rindilþvari</i>. This points to a <a href="/wiki/Common_Germanic" title="Common Germanic" class="mw-redirect">Common Germanic</a> name <i>*wrandjan-</i>, but the further etymology of the name is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The wren is also known as <i>kuningilin</i> "kinglet" in Old High German, a name associated with the <a href="/wiki/Fable" title="Fable">fable</a> of the election of the "king of birds". The bird who could fly to the highest altitude would be made king. The <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagle</a> outflew all other birds, but he was beaten by a small bird who had hidden in his plumage. This fable is already known to <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> (<i>Hist. animalium</i> 9.11) and <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Plinius</a> (<i>Naturalis hist.</i> 10.74 ), and was taken up by medieval authors such as <a href="/wiki/Johann_Geiler_von_Kaisersberg" title="Johann Geiler von Kaisersberg">Johann Geiler von Kaisersberg</a>, but it concerns <i><a href="/wiki/Regulus_(genus)" title="Regulus (genus)" class="mw-redirect">Regulus</a></i>, and is apparently motivated by the yellow "crown" sported by these birds (a point noted already by <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Uhland" title="Ludwig Uhland">Ludwig Uhland</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> In modern German the name is "Zaunkönig", king of the fence (or hedge). In Dutch the name is "winterkoninkje" (little winter king).</p>
<p>The family name Troglodytidae is derived from troglodyte, which means "cave-dweller", and the wrens get their scientific name from the tendency of some species to forage in dark crevices.</p>
<p>The name "wren" is also ascribed to <a href="/wiki/Wren_(disambiguation)" title="Wren (disambiguation)">other families</a> of passerine birds throughout the world. In Europe, species of <i><a href="/wiki/Regulus_(genus)" title="Regulus (genus)" class="mw-redirect">Regulus</a></i> are commonly known as "wrens", the <a href="/wiki/Common_Firecrest" title="Common Firecrest">Common Firecrest</a> and <a href="/wiki/Goldcrest" title="Goldcrest">Goldcrest</a> as "fire-crested wren" and "golden-crested wren", respectively.</p>
<p>The 27 <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasian</a> "wren" species in the family <a href="/wiki/Maluridae" title="Maluridae">Maluridae</a> are unrelated, as are the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_wren" title="New Zealand wren">New Zealand wrens</a> in the family Acanthisittidae, the antwrens in the family <a href="/wiki/Thamnophilidae" title="Thamnophilidae" class="mw-redirect">Thamnophilidae</a>, and the wren-babblers of the family <a href="/wiki/Timaliidae" title="Timaliidae" class="mw-redirect">Timaliidae</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Wren&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>Wrens are medium-small to very small birds. The Eurasian Wren is among the smallest birds in its range, while the smaller species from the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> are among the smallest <a href="/wiki/Passerines" title="Passerines" class="mw-redirect">passerines</a> in that part of the world. They range in size from the <a href="/wiki/White-bellied_Wren" title="White-bellied Wren">White-bellied Wren</a>, which averages under 10 centimetres (3.9&#160;in) and 9 grams (0.32&#160;oz), to the <a href="/wiki/Giant_Wren" title="Giant Wren">Giant Wren</a>, which averages about 22 centimetres (8.7&#160;in) and weighs almost 50 grams (1.8&#160;oz). The dominating colours of their <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> are generally drab, composed of grey, brown, black and white, and most species show some barring, especially to tail and/or wings. There is no <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a> in the plumage of wrens, and little difference between young birds and adults.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> All have fairly long, straight to marginally decurved bills.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_0-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Wrens have loud and often complex songs, sometimes given in duet by a pair. The song of members of the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Cyphorhinus" title="Cyphorhinus">Cyphorhinus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Microcerculus" title="Microcerculus">Microcerculus</a></i> have been considered especially pleasant to the human ear, leading to common names such as <a href="/wiki/Song_Wren" title="Song Wren">Song Wren</a>, <a href="/wiki/Musician_Wren" title="Musician Wren">Musician Wren</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flutist_Wren" title="Flutist Wren">Flutist Wren</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Nightingale-Wren" title="Southern Nightingale-Wren">Southern Nightingale-Wren</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_0-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Wren&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cobb%27s_Wren.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Cobb%27s_Wren.png/220px-Cobb%27s_Wren.png" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Cobb%27s_Wren.png/330px-Cobb%27s_Wren.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/31/Cobb%27s_Wren.png 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cobb%27s_Wren.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Cobb%27s_Wren" title="Cobb's Wren">Cobb's Wren</a> is an insular <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a>, restricted to the Falkland Islands</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Wrens are principally a New World Family, distributed from <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> to southern <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, with the greatest <a href="/wiki/Species_richness" title="Species richness">species richness</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Neotropics" title="Neotropics" class="mw-redirect">Neotropics</a>. As suggested by its name, the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Wren" title="Eurasian Wren">Eurasian Wren</a> is the only species of wren found outside the Americas, as restricted to Europe, Asia and northern Africa (it was formerly considered <a href="/wiki/Conspecific" title="Conspecific" class="mw-redirect">conspecific</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Winter_Wren" title="Winter Wren">Winter Wren</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Wren" title="Pacific Wren">Pacific Wren</a> of North America). There are a number of insular species, including the <a href="/wiki/Clarion_Wren" title="Clarion Wren" class="mw-redirect">Clarion Wren</a> and <a href="/wiki/Socorro_Wren" title="Socorro Wren">Socorro Wren</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Revillagigedo_Islands" title="Revillagigedo Islands">Revillagigedo Islands</a> in the Pacific Ocean, and the <a href="/wiki/Cobb%27s_Wren" title="Cobb's Wren">Cobb's Wren</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a>, but few <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean</a> islands have a species of wren, with only the <a href="/wiki/House_Wren" title="House Wren">Southern House Wren</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>, the <a href="/wiki/House_Wren" title="House Wren">Cozumel Wren</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cozumel_Island" title="Cozumel Island" class="mw-redirect">Cozumel Island</a>, and the highly restricted <a href="/wiki/Zapata_Wren" title="Zapata Wren">Zapata Wren</a> in a single swamp in <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>.</p>
<p>The various species occur in a wide range of habitats, ranging from dry, sparsely wooded country to rainforest. Most species are mainly found at low levels, but members of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Campylorhynchus" title="Campylorhynchus">Campylorhynchus</a></i> are frequently found higher, and the two members of <i><a href="/wiki/Odontorchilus" title="Odontorchilus">Odontorchilus</a></i> are restricted to the <a href="/wiki/Forest_canopy" title="Forest canopy" class="mw-redirect">forest canopy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_0-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> A few species, notably the Eurasian Wren and the <a href="/wiki/House_Wren" title="House Wren">House Wren</a>, are often associated with humans. Most species are resident, remaining in Central and South America all year round, but the few species found in temperate regions of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> are partially migratory, spending the winter further south.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Wren&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span></h2>
<p>Wrens vary from highly secretive species such as those found in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Microcerculus" title="Microcerculus">Microcerculus</a></i> to the highly conspicuous genus <i><a href="/wiki/Campylorhynchus" title="Campylorhynchus">Campylorhynchus</a></i>, the members of which will frequently sing from exposed perches. The family as a whole exhibits a great deal of variation in their behaviour. Temperate species generally occur in pairs, but some tropical species may occur in parties of up to twenty birds.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_0-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Wrens build dome-shaped nests, and may be either <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" title="Monogamous" class="mw-redirect">monogamous</a> or <a href="/wiki/Polygamous" title="Polygamous" class="mw-redirect">polygamous</a>, depending on species.<sup id="cite_ref-EoB_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoB-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Though little is known about the feeding habits of many of the <a href="/wiki/Neotropical" title="Neotropical" class="mw-redirect">Neotropical</a> species, wrens are considered primarily <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" title="Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect">insectivorous</a>, eating insects, spiders and other small <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_0-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Many species also take vegetable matter such as seeds and berries, some (primarily the larger species) will take small frogs/lizards, the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Wren" title="Eurasian Wren">Eurasian Wren</a> has been recorded wading into shallow water to catch small fish and tadpoles, the <a href="/wiki/Sumichrast%27s_Wren" title="Sumichrast's Wren">Sumichrast's Wren</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zapata_Wren" title="Zapata Wren">Zapata Wren</a> will take snails, and the <a href="/wiki/Giant_Wren" title="Giant Wren">Giant Wren</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marsh_Wren" title="Marsh Wren">Marsh Wren</a> have been recorded attacking and eating bird eggs (in the latter species, even eggs of conspecifics).<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_0-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> A local <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> name for the Giant Wren and <a href="/wiki/Bicolored_Wren" title="Bicolored Wren">Bicolored Wren</a> is <i>chupahuevo</i> ("egg-sucker"), but whether the latter actually eats eggs is unclear.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_0-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Plain_Wren" title="Plain Wren">Plain Wren</a> and <a href="/wiki/House_Wren" title="House Wren">Northern House Wren</a> sometimes destroy bird eggs, and the <a href="/wiki/Rufous-and-white_Wren" title="Rufous-and-white Wren">Rufous-and-white Wren</a> has been recorded killing nestlings, but this is apparently to eliminate potential food competitors rather than feed on the eggs/nestlings.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_0-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Several species of Neotropical wrens sometimes participate in <a href="/wiki/Mixed-species_flock" title="Mixed-species flock" class="mw-redirect">mixed-species flocks</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ant-follower" title="Ant-follower">follow army ants</a>, and the Eurasian Wren may follow <a href="/wiki/European_Badger" title="European Badger" class="mw-redirect">Badgers</a> to catch prey items disturbed by them.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_0-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Wren&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span></h2>
<p>Revised following Martínez Gómez <i>et al.</i> (2005) and Mann <i>et al.</i> (2006). The taxonomy of some groups is highly complex, and future species-level splits are likely. Additionally, undescribed <a href="/wiki/Taxa" title="Taxa" class="mw-redirect">taxa</a> are known to exist. The <a href="/wiki/Black-capped_Donacobius" title="Black-capped Donacobius">Black-capped Donacobius</a> is an enigmatic species traditionally placed with the wrens more for lack of a more apparent alternative and/or thorough study. It was more recently determined to be most likely closer to certain "<a href="/wiki/Warblers" title="Warblers" class="mw-redirect">warblers</a>", possibly the newly established <a href="/wiki/Megaluridae" title="Megaluridae" class="mw-redirect">Megaluridae</a>, and might constitute a <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" title="Monotypic" class="mw-redirect">monotypic</a> family.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
