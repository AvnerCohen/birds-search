<p>The <b>Eurasian dotterel</b> (<i>Charadrius morinellus</i>), also known in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> as just <b>dotterel</b>, is a small <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">plover</a> family of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>.
</p><p>The dotterel is a brown and black streaked bird with a broad white eye-stripe and an orange-red chest band when in breeding plumage. The female is more colourful than the male. The bird is tame and unsuspecting and the term "dotterel" has been applied contemptuously to mean an old fool.
</p><p>The Eurasian dotterel is a migratory species, breeding in northern Europe and <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurosiberia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrating</a> south to north Africa and the Middle East in the winter. It nests in a bare scrape on the ground and lays two to four eggs. The male does the incubation and rears the chicks, the female having gone off to find another male and lay another clutch of eggs. It is a common bird with a wide range and the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> has rated it as being of "<a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least concern</a>".
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_dotterel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The English name dates from 1440 when it was used to refer to the bird and also as an insult for someone considered simple or a dotard. It is not clear which use is the oldest,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> but the link is its tame and unsuspecting nature which made it easy to catch; its <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a> name is <i>amadan-mòintich</i>, "fool of the <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moors</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> King <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">James VI and I</a> went every year to <a href="/wiki/Royston,_Hertfordshire" title="Royston, Hertfordshire">Royston, Hertfordshire</a> to shoot dotterels.<sup id="cite_ref-Quinlan2003_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Quinlan2003-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It was also easy prey for illegal poaching, which depleted its stocks.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> They were also prized as a delicacy: in 1534 Queen <a href="/wiki/Anne_Boleyn" title="Anne Boleyn">Anne Boleyn</a> was presented with "a brace of dotterels".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The genus name <i>Charadrius</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">Late Latin</a> word for a yellowish bird mentioned in the fourth-century <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a>. It derives from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> {[transl|grc|kharadrios}} a bird found in ravines and river valleys (<i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language romanization">kharadra</i>, "ravine"). The specific <i> morinellus</i> is  <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> derived from <i>moros</i> "foolish", due to the bird's trusting nature.<sup id="cite_ref-job_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_dotterel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This plover is smaller and more compact than <a href="/wiki/European_golden_plover" title="European golden plover">European golden plover</a> (<i>Pluvialis apricaria</i>). It has a striking whitish <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a> in all plumages and has plain wings in flight. Adults in summer are unmistakable, with a chestnut breast bordered above with white, black belly and warm brown back. The legs are yellow, and the short bill is black. As with the <a href="/wiki/Phalarope" title="Phalarope">phalaropes</a>, the female is brighter than the male.
</p><p>Winter birds lack the rich underpart colouration, apart from the white breast line, and are greyer above. Young birds are similar but have a scaly appearance to their backs.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_dotterel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It breeds in the Arctic tundra of northern <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Eurosiberia</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> to eastern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, and on suitable mountain plateaus such as the <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> highlands and the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>. This species is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in a narrow belt across north <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> from <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> eastwards to <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. Migration stopovers are traditional, and small parties (<i>trips</i>) of dotterels pass through each year at these usually inland arable or grassy sites. The winter habitat is semi-desert.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_dotterel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The dotterel's diet is made up of <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and other small <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> such as snails and worms and shellfish. These are obtained by a run-and-pause technique, rather than the steady probing used by other waders.
</p><p>The flight call is a soft <i>pyurr</i>. The female's song is a simple repetitive whistle.
</p><p>The male dotterel generally is responsible for incubation and looks after the chicks. In most cases the cock dotterel successfully prevents other males from getting his mate and fertilizing her eggs. He usually rears chicks that he has fathered and only 4.6% (2/44) of chicks were not the genetic offspring of the caring male, corresponding to 9.1% (2/22) broods affected.<sup id="cite_ref-Owens1995_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Owens1995-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_dotterel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is a relatively common species with a wide range. Populations seem to be declining slowly but not alarmingly so, and the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> has rated it as a "<a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least-concern species</a>". A survey published in 2015 showed a fall in dotterel numbers in Scotland between 1987 and 2011, from 980 to 423 breeding males - representing a decline of 57%.<sup id="cite_ref-BIRDSTU_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BIRDSTU-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> 
The Eurasian dotterel is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>
