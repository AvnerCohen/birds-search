<p>The <b>pied kingfisher</b> (<i>Ceryle rudis</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Water_kingfisher" title="Water kingfisher">water kingfisher</a> widely distributed across Africa and Asia. Originally described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758, it has five recognised subspecies. Its black and white plumage and crest, as well as its habit of hovering over clear lakes and rivers before diving for fish, make it distinctive. Males have a double band across the breast, while females have a single <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#gorget" title="Glossary of bird terms">gorget</a> that is often broken in the middle. They are usually found in pairs or small family groups. When perched, they often bob their head and flick up their tail.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_evolution">Taxonomy and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pied_kingfisher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The pied kingfisher was one of the many <a href="/wiki/Aves_in_the_10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="Aves in the 10th edition of Systema Naturae">bird species originally described</a> by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a> in the landmark 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>, who noted that it lived in Persia and Egypt. He named it <i>Alcedo rudis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The German naturalist <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Boie" title="Friedrich Boie">Friedrich Boie</a> erected the genus <i>Ceryle</i> in 1828.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The name is from classical Greek <i>kērulos</i>, an unidentified and probably mythical bird mentioned by Aristotle and other authors. The specific epithet <i>rudis</i> is Latin for "wild" or "rude".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The pied kingfisher is the only member of the genus <i>Ceryle</i>. Molecular analysis shows it is an early offshoot of the lineage that gave rise to American kingfishers of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Chloroceryle" class="mw-redirect" title="Chloroceryle">Chloroceryle</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-moyle_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moyle-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The pied kingfisher was initially believed to be descended from an ancestral <a href="/wiki/American_green_kingfisher" title="American green kingfisher">American green kingfisher</a> which crossed the Atlantic Ocean about one million years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> A more recent suggestion is that the pied kingfisher and the American green kingfishers are derived from an Old World species, with the pied kingfisher or its ancestor losing the metallic colouration afterwards.<sup id="cite_ref-moyle_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moyle-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are five <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>C. r. syriacus</i> <small>Roselaar, 1995</small> – Turkey to Israel east to southwest Iran (some ornithologists do not recognise this subspecies)<sup id="cite_ref-kasparek_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kasparek-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbw_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>C. r. rudis</i> <small>(Linnaeus, 1758)</small> – Egypt and Africa south of the Sahara</li>
<li><i>C. r. leucomelanurus</i> <small><a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Gustav_Reichenbach" title="Heinrich Gustav Reichenbach">Reichenbach</a>, 1851</small> – east Afghanistan through India to south China and north Indochina</li>
<li><i>C. r. travancoreensis</i> <small><a href="/wiki/Hugh_Whistler" title="Hugh Whistler">Whistler</a>, 1935</small> – southwest India<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>C. r. insignis</i> <small><a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a>, 1910</small> – east and southeast China, <a href="/wiki/Hainan" title="Hainan">Hainan Island</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pied_kingfisher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is a medium-sized kingfisher, about 25&#160;cm (9.8&#160;in) long with a white with a black mask, a white <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a> and black breast bands. The crest is neat and the upperparts are barred in black. Several subspecies are recognized within the broad distribution. The nominate race is found in sub-Saharan Africa, extending into West Asia. The subspecies <i>syriacus</i> is a larger northern bird similar to the nominate subspecies (following <a href="/wiki/Bergmann%27s_rule" title="Bergmann&#39;s rule">Bergmann's rule</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-kasparek_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kasparek-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Subspecies <i>leucomelanura</i> is found from Afghanistan east into India, Sri Lanka, Thailand and Laos. The subspecies <i>travancoreensis</i> of the Western Ghats is darker with the white reduced. The subspecies <i>C. r. insignis</i> is found in Hainan and southeastern China and has a much larger bill. Males have a narrow second breast-band while females have a single broken breast band.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pied_kingfisher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is common throughout sub-Saharan Africa and southern Asia from Turkey to India to China. It is resident, and most birds do not <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a>, other than short-distance seasonal movements.<sup id="cite_ref-fry_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fry-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In India it is distributed mainly on the plains and is replaced in the higher hills of the Himalayas by the <a href="/wiki/Crested_kingfisher" title="Crested kingfisher">crested kingfisher</a> (<i>Megaceryle lugubris</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The pied kingfisher is estimated to be one of the three most numerous kingfishers in the world; the other two are the <a href="/wiki/Common_kingfisher" title="Common kingfisher">common kingfisher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Collared_kingfisher" title="Collared kingfisher">collared kingfisher</a>. It is a noisy bird, making it hard to miss.<sup id="cite_ref-fry_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fry-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pied_kingfisher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When perched the pied kingfisher often bobs its heads up and down and will sometimes raise its tail and flick it downwards. It calls often with sharp <i>chirruk chirruk</i> notes.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Unlike some kingfishers, it is quite gregarious, and forms large roosts at night.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pied_kingfisher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This kingfisher feeds mainly on fish, although it will take <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> and large aquatic <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> such as dragonfly larvae.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> It usually hunts by hovering over the water to detect prey and diving vertically bill-first to capture fish. When not foraging, it has a straight rapid flight and have been observed flying at nearly 50&#160;km/h.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
In <a href="/wiki/Lake_Victoria" title="Lake Victoria">Lake Victoria</a> in East Africa the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Nile_perch" title="Nile perch">Nile perch</a> reduced the availability of <a href="/wiki/Haplochromine_cichlid" class="mw-redirect" title="Haplochromine cichlid">haplochromine cichlids</a> which were formerly the preferred prey of these birds.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It can deal with prey without returning to a perch, often swallowing small prey in flight, and so can hunt over large water bodies or in estuaries that lack perches that are required by other kingfishers.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pied_kingfisher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The breeding season is February to April. Its nest is a hole excavated in a vertical mud bank about five feet above water. The nest tunnel is four to five feet deep and ends in a chamber. Several birds may nest in the same vicinity. The usual clutch is three to six white eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The pied kingfisher sometimes reproduces cooperatively, with young non-breeding birds from an earlier brood assisting parents or even unrelated older birds.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In India, nestlings have been found to be prone to maggot infestations (probably by <i><a href="/wiki/Protocalliphora" title="Protocalliphora">Protocalliphora</a></i> sp.)<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and in some areas to leeches.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Nest holes may sometimes be used for roosting.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1947, British zoologist <a href="/wiki/Hugh_B._Cott" title="Hugh B. Cott">Hugh B. Cott</a> noticed while skinning birds that hornets were attracted to certain birds but avoided the flesh of pied kingfishers. This led to a comparative study of edibility of birds and he suggested that more conspicuously plumaged birds may be less palatable. This suggestion was, however, not supported by a subsequent reanalysis of his data.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
