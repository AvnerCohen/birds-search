<p>The <b>common pochard</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;p&#39; in &#39;pie&#39;">p</span><span title="/ɒ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;body&#39;">ɒ</span><span title="/tʃ/: &#39;ch&#39; in &#39;China&#39;">tʃ</span><span title="/ər/: &#39;er&#39; in &#39;letter&#39;">ər</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <i>Aythya ferina</i>) is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Diving_duck" title="Diving duck">diving duck</a>. The scientific name is derived from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i> aithuia</i> an unidentified <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> mentioned by authors including <a href="/wiki/Hesychius_of_Miletus" title="Hesychius of Miletus">Hesychius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>  <i>ferina</i>, "wild game", from <i>ferus</i>, "wild".<sup id="cite_ref-job90_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job90-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_pochard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The adult male has a long dark bill with a grey band, a red head and neck, a black breast, red eyes and a grey back. The adult female has a brown head and body and a narrower grey bill-band. The triangular head shape is distinctive. Pochards are superficially similar to the closely related <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> <a href="/wiki/Redhead_(duck)" class="mw-redirect" title="Redhead (duck)">redhead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canvasback" title="Canvasback">canvasback</a>.
</p><p>Females give hoarse growls. Males have whistles cut off by a final nasal note <i>aaoo-oo-haa</i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_pochard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Their breeding habitat consists of <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a> and lakes with a metre or more water depth. Pochards breed in much of temperate and northern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>. They are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, and spend winter in the south and west of Europe.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, birds breed in eastern <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and lowland <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, in small numbers in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> with numbers increasing gradually, and sporadically in the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, where it may also be increasing. While uncommon, individuals are also occasionally seen in the south of England, and small populations are sometimes observed on the River Thames. Large numbers stay overwinter in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, after the birds retreat from <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_pochard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>These are gregarious birds, forming large flocks in winter, often mixed with other diving ducks such as the <a href="/wiki/Tufted_duck" title="Tufted duck">tufted duck</a>, with which they are known to hybridise.
</p><p>These birds feed mainly by diving or dabbling. They eat aquatic plants with some <a href="/wiki/Mollusc" class="mw-redirect" title="Mollusc">molluscs</a>, aquatic <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and small <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>. They often feed at night, and will up-end for food as well as the more characteristic diving. According to the article 'Patterns in the diving behaviour of the pochard, <i>Aythya ferina</i>: a test of an optimality model' Pochard's have a behavioral preference when it comes to their feeding patterns. This behavioral preference is that Pochards prefer shallower water in comparison to deeper water even though the food concentration in deeper water may be higher.
</p><p>In number of countries the population of Common Pochard is decreasing mainly due to urbanization of the natural habitats and their transformation, as well as due to overhunting.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
The pochard is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>
