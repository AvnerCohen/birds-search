<p>The <b>Barbary Falcon</b> (<i>Falco pelegrinoides</i>) is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon</a> about the size of a <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crow</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> and on the coasts of north <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It is mainly resident.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbary_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of semi-desert and dry open hills. It typically lays its eggs in cliff-ledge nests.</p>
<p>The Barbary Falcon is similar to the <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_Falcon" title="Peregrine Falcon">Peregrine Falcon</a>, but smaller at 33–39&#160;cm length with a wingspan of 76–98&#160;cm. The female is larger than the male. It resembles its relative in general structure.</p>
<p>Adults have paler grey-blue upperparts than the Peregrine, and often have a buff wash to the barred underparts, whereas the larger species has a white background colour. The nape is rufous, but this is difficult to see.</p>
<p>Sexes are similar, apart from size, but the young birds have brown upperparts and streaked underparts. The streaking is lighter than in the juvenile Peregrine.</p>
<p>The call is a high-pitched "rek-rek-rek".</p>
<p>The Barbary Falcon also bears some resemblance to the Lanner Falcon, but can be distinguished from that species at rest by the head-pattern, and in flight, by the proportions, flight action and underwing pattern.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbary_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The Barbary Falcon differ in appearance from the Peregrine Falcon according to <a href="/wiki/Gloger%27s_Rule" title="Gloger's Rule" class="mw-redirect">Gloger's Rule</a>. The genetic distance is slight and the species form a close-knit and somewhat <a href="/wiki/Paraphyletic" title="Paraphyletic" class="mw-redirect">paraphyletic</a> group in DNA sequence analyses. They differ more in behavior, ecology and <a href="/wiki/Anatomy" title="Anatomy">anatomy</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> than usual for <a href="/wiki/Conspecific" title="Conspecific" class="mw-redirect">conspecifics</a>. They are able to produce fertile hybrids,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> but they are generally <a href="/wiki/Allopatric" title="Allopatric" class="mw-redirect">allopatric</a> and only co-occur during breeding season in small areas such as the <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">Maghreb</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Punjab_region" title="Punjab region">Punjab</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Khorasan</a>, and possibly the <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_Altai" title="Mongolian Altai" class="mw-redirect">Mongolian Altai</a>, and there is clear evidence of <a href="/wiki/Assortative_mating" title="Assortative mating">assortative mating</a> with hybridization hardly ever occurring under natural conditions. In short, though they occupy adjacent territories, they breed at different times of year and Barbary Falcons virtually never breed with Peregrines in nature.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wink_et_al._2000_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wink_et_al._2000-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Assuming a genetic distance of 2% in hierofalcons<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> corresponds to a divergence roughly 200,000-130,000&#160;years ago,<sup id="cite_ref-Nittinger_et_al._2005_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nittinger_et_al._2005-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> the 0.6-0.7%&#160;genetic distance in the Peregine-Barbary Falcon ("peregrinoid") complex <sup id="cite_ref-Wink_et_al._2000_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wink_et_al._2000-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> suggests its current taxa <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolved</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Late_Pleistocene" title="Late Pleistocene">Late Pleistocene</a> some 100,000&#160;years ago or less, but before the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Paleolithic" title="Upper Paleolithic">Upper Paleolithic</a>. The presumed time of divergence between Peregrine and Barbary Falcons approximately coincides with the start of the <a href="/wiki/Last_ice_age" title="Last ice age" class="mw-redirect">last ice age</a>, when <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a> was prominent in North Africa and the Middle East, and the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> became a landlocked inland sea that slowly dried up. Populations of ancestral "peregrinoid" falcons living in marginal habitat at the fringe of the African-Middle Eastern desert belt either adapted (and might have become isolated e.g. in the Persian Gulf region, which turned into semiarid habitat surrounded by vast deserts), or left for better habitat, or became extinct. During <a href="/wiki/Interstadial" title="Interstadial" class="mw-redirect">interstadials</a>, deserts receded and the aridland and <a href="/wiki/Humid" title="Humid" class="mw-redirect">humidland</a> populations could expand to contact again, causing some limited gene flow. This scenario by and large parallels the proposed evolutionary history of the <a href="/wiki/Saker_Falcon" title="Saker Falcon">Saker Falcon</a> in relation to the other hierofalcons; indeed, that group shows similar patterns of molecular paraphyly though it is of somewhat earlier origin.<sup id="cite_ref-Nittinger_et_al._2005_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nittinger_et_al._2005-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fossil_record" title="Fossil record" class="mw-redirect">fossil record</a> adds little to the issue. A <a href="/wiki/Humerus" title="Humerus">humerus</a> some 9,000&#160;years old (i.e., after the last ice age) from the <a href="/wiki/Aswan" title="Aswan">Aswan</a> area in <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, where <i>Falco peregrinus minor</i> occurs today, was identified to belong to the Peregrine.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The Barbary Falcon is one of the rare cases that may arguably be considered a species under the <a href="/wiki/Biological_species_concept" title="Biological species concept" class="mw-redirect">Biological</a>, but certainly not under the Phylogenetic <a href="/wiki/Species_concept" title="Species concept" class="mw-redirect">Species Concept</a> rather than the other way around as usual. This case demonstrates that what makes a "species" is not only its descent, but also occurs to a population in the course of evolution, how it adapts, and how this affects its <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_isolation" title="Reproductive isolation">reproductive isolation</a> (or lack thereof) from <a href="/wiki/Sister_taxa" title="Sister taxa" class="mw-redirect">sister taxa</a>.</p>

In general terms, the ability to produce fertile offspring is a <a href="/wiki/Plesiomorph" title="Plesiomorph" class="mw-redirect">plesiomorphy</a> initially shared by close relatives; the loss of ability to hybridize successfully is an <a href="/wiki/Apomorph" title="Apomorph" class="mw-redirect">apomorphy</a>. Hence, the <i>inability</i> rather than the ability to produce fertile hybrids is <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic" title="Phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect">phylogenetically</a> informative.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-4"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-4">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Schollaert &amp; Willem (2000)</span></li>
