<p>The <b>Eurasian spoonbill</b> (<i>Platalea leucorodia</i>), or <b>common spoonbill</b>, is a wading <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ibis" title="Ibis">ibis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spoonbill" title="Spoonbill">spoonbill</a> family <a href="/wiki/Threskiornithidae" title="Threskiornithidae">Threskiornithidae</a>. The genus name <i>Platalea</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means "broad", referring to the distinctive shape of the bill, and <i>leucorodia</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>leukerodios</i> "spoonbill", itself derived from <i>leukos</i>, "white" and <i>erodios</i> "heron".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In England it was traditionally known as the "shovelard", a name later used for the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Shoveller" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Shoveller">Northern Shoveller</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_spoonbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Platalea_leucorodia_-Holes_Bay,_Poole,_Dorset,_England-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Platalea_leucorodia_-Holes_Bay%2C_Poole%2C_Dorset%2C_England-8.jpg/220px-Platalea_leucorodia_-Holes_Bay%2C_Poole%2C_Dorset%2C_England-8.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Platalea_leucorodia_-Holes_Bay%2C_Poole%2C_Dorset%2C_England-8.jpg/330px-Platalea_leucorodia_-Holes_Bay%2C_Poole%2C_Dorset%2C_England-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Platalea_leucorodia_-Holes_Bay%2C_Poole%2C_Dorset%2C_England-8.jpg/440px-Platalea_leucorodia_-Holes_Bay%2C_Poole%2C_Dorset%2C_England-8.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1923" data-file-height="1081" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Platalea_leucorodia_-Holes_Bay,_Poole,_Dorset,_England-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Juvenile in <a href="/wiki/Holes_Bay" title="Holes Bay">Holes Bay</a>, Dorset, England</div></div></div>
<p>A study of <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> of the spoonbills found that the Eurasian spoonbill is sister taxon to a clade containing the <a href="/wiki/Royal_spoonbill" title="Royal spoonbill">royal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black-faced_spoonbill" title="Black-faced spoonbill">black-faced spoonbills</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chesser2010_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chesser2010-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_spoonbill.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Eurasian_spoonbill.JPG/220px-Eurasian_spoonbill.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Eurasian_spoonbill.JPG/330px-Eurasian_spoonbill.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Eurasian_spoonbill.JPG/440px-Eurasian_spoonbill.JPG 2x" data-file-width="5184" data-file-height="2920" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Eurasian_spoonbill.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Eurasian spoonbill in Bharatpur Bird sanctuary, Rajasthan, India</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Platalea_leucorodia_MWNH_1953.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Platalea_leucorodia_MWNH_1953.JPG/220px-Platalea_leucorodia_MWNH_1953.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="232" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Platalea_leucorodia_MWNH_1953.JPG/330px-Platalea_leucorodia_MWNH_1953.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Platalea_leucorodia_MWNH_1953.JPG/440px-Platalea_leucorodia_MWNH_1953.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2514" data-file-height="2656" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Platalea_leucorodia_MWNH_1953.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden,_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Museum Wiesbaden, Germany">Museum Wiesbaden, Germany</a></div></div></div>
<p>The Eurasian spoonbill has three <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-IOC_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IOC-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>P. l. leucorodia</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, 1758</small>: <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies#Nominotypical_subspecies_and_subspecies_autonyms" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate</a>, occupies all the range except as below.</li>
<li><i>P. l. balsaci</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_de_Naurois" title="René de Naurois">Naurois</a> &amp; Roux, 1974</small>: found on the islands off the <a href="/wiki/Banc_d%27Arguin" class="mw-redirect" title="Banc d&#39;Arguin">Banc d'Arguin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a>.</li>
<li><i>P. l. archeri</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/Oscar_Neumann" title="Oscar Neumann">Neumann</a>, 1928</small>: found on the coasts of the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Birds in Asia are sometimes separated as <i>P. l. major</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_spoonbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species is almost unmistakable in most of its range. The breeding bird is all white except for its dark legs, black bill with a yellow tip, and a yellow breast patch like a <a href="/wiki/Pelican" title="Pelican">pelican</a>. It has a crest in the breeding season. Non-breeders lack the crest and breast patch, and immature birds have a pale bill and black tips to the primary flight feathers. Unlike herons, spoonbills fly with their necks outstretched. The Eurasian spoonbill differs from the <a href="/wiki/African_spoonbill" title="African spoonbill">African spoonbill</a> with which in overlaps in winter, in that the latter species has a red face and legs, and no crest.
</p><p>They are mostly silent. Even at their breeding colonies the main sounds are bill snapping, occasional deep grunting and occasional trumpeting noises.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_spoonbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is a <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> species, breeding from the United Kingdom and Spain in the west through to Japan, and also in North Africa. In Europe, only the Netherlands, Spain, Austria, Hungary and Greece have sizeable populations.<sup id="cite_ref-Overdijk2002p88_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Overdijk2002p88-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Most birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> to the tropics in winter, with European breeders mainly going to Africa, but a few remaining in mild winter areas of western Europe south to the United Kingdom. It was <a href="/wiki/Local_extinction" title="Local extinction">extirpated</a> from the United Kingdom in the later part of the seventeenth century, although in the previous century it had been a widespread breeding species in the south-east, even near London. Sporadic breeding attempts in the early 21st century culminated with the formation of a colony at <a href="/wiki/Holkham" title="Holkham">Holkham</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a> in 2010. In 2011, 8 breeding pairs nested, successfully fledging 14 young.<sup id="cite_ref-naturalengland_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-naturalengland-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Eurasian spoonbills show a preference for extensive shallow, wetlands with muddy, clay or fine sandy beds. They may inhabit any type of marsh, river, lake, flooded area and mangrove swamp, whether fresh, brackish or saline, but especially those with islands for nesting or dense emergent vegetation (e.g. reedbeds) and scattered trees or shrubs (especially <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willow <i>Salix</i> spp.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak <i>Quercus </i>spp.</a> or <a href="/wiki/Populus" title="Populus">poplar <i>Populus</i> spp.</a>). Eurasian spoonbills may also frequent sheltered marine habitats during the winter such as deltas, estuaries, tidal creeks and coastal lagoons.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_spoonbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_spoonbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>More northerly breeding populations are fully migratory but may only migrate short distances while other, more southerly populations are resident and nomadic or partially migratory. In the Palearctic, the species breeds in spring (e.g. from April) but in tropical parts of its range it times breeding to coincide with rainfall. Breeding is normally in single species colonies or in small single species groups amidst mixed-species colonies of other waterbirds such as herons, egrets and cormorants. Outside the breeding season Eurasian spoonbills forage singly or in small flocks of up to 100 individuals. Migration is usually conducted in flocks of up to 100 individuals. Most activity takes place during the morning and evening (although in coastal areas it foraging is governed by tidal rhythms), they often roost communally in roosts which are up to 15&#160;km (9.3&#160;mi) away from the feeding areas.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The nest is a platform of sticks and vegetation which is either constructed on the ground on islands in lakes and rivers or in dense stands of reeds, bushes, mangroves or deciduous trees up to 5&#160;m (16&#160;ft) above the ground. Within colonies neighbouring nests are usually quite close together, no more than 1 or 2&#160;m (3.3 or 6.6&#160;ft) apart. Breeding colonies are normally sited within 10–15&#160;km (6.2–9.3&#160;mi) of feeding areas, often much less (although the species may also feed up to 35–40&#160;km (22–25&#160;mi) away).<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_spoonbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The diet consists of aquatic <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mollusks" class="mw-redirect" title="Mollusks">mollusks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newt" title="Newt">newts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, worms, leeches, <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a>, tadpoles and small fish up to 10–15&#160;cm (3.9–5.9&#160;in) long. It may also take algae or small fragments of aquatic plants (although these are possibly ingested accidentally with animal matter).<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>They use sideways sweeps of their beaks to filter out the tiny fish and shrimps.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_spoonbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Threats to the Eurasian spoonbill include habitat destruction by drainage and degradation by pollution, It is especially adversely affected by the disappearance of reed swamps. In Greece, over-fishing and disturbance have caused the population to decline, and human exploitation of eggs and nestlings for food has threatened the species in the past.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> As stated above it has recently started to breed in the United Kingdom from where it was extirpated in the 17th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Unwin2000_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Unwin2000-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
The Eurasian spoonbill international expert group (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.unep-aewa.org/en/workinggroup/aewa-international-species-expert-groups-iseg">ESIEG</a>) is one of the first research network created on a waterbird species. It was formed in 1991 and then linked to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurosite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eurosite (page does not exist)">Eurosite</a> until 2013 when the group became an <a href="/wiki/Agreement_on_the_Conservation_of_African-Eurasian_Migratory_Waterbirds" title="Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds">AEWA</a> International Species Expert Groups. An <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.unep-aewa.org/en/publication/international-single-species-action-plan-conservation-eurasian-spoonbill-complete-ts">international action plan for the Eurasian Spoonbill</a> was adopted in 2008. ESIEG is in charge of its implementation.
</p>
