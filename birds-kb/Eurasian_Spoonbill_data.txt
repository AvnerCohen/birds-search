<p>The <b>Eurasian Spoonbill</b> or <b>Common Spoonbill</b> (<i>Platalea leucorodia</i>) is a wading <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ibis" title="Ibis">ibis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spoonbill" title="Spoonbill">spoonbill</a> family <a href="/wiki/Threskiornithidae" title="Threskiornithidae">Threskiornithidae</a>,</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Spoonbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<p>This is a <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" title="Palearctic" class="mw-redirect">Palearctic</a> species, breeding from the United Kingdom and Spain in the west through to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, and also in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>. In Europe, only The Netherlands, Spain, Austria, Hungary and Greece have sizeable populations.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Most birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> to the tropics in winter, with <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> breeders mainly going to <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, but a few remaining in mild winter areas of western Europe south to the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. It was <a href="/wiki/Extirpated" title="Extirpated" class="mw-redirect">extirpated</a> from the United Kingdom but sporadic breeding attempts in the early 21st Century culminated with the formation of a colony at <a href="/wiki/Holkham" title="Holkham">Holkham</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a> in 2010. In 2011, 8 breeding pairs nested, successfully fledging 14 young.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Spoonbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>This species is almost unmistakable in most of its range. The breeding bird is all white except for its dark legs, black bill with a yellow tip, and a yellow breast patch like a <a href="/wiki/Pelican" title="Pelican">pelican</a>. It has a crest in the breeding season. Non-breeders lack the crest and breast patch, and immature birds have a pale bill and black tips to the primary flight feathers. Unlike herons, spoonbills fly with their necks outstretched. The Eurasian Spoonbill differs from the <a href="/wiki/African_Spoonbill" title="African Spoonbill">African Spoonbill</a> with which in overlaps in winter, in that the latter species has a red face and legs, and no crest. They are mostly silent. Even at their breeding colonies the main sounds are bill snapping, occasional deep grunting and occasional trumpeting noises.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Spoonbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>A study of mitochondrial DNA of the spoonbills found that the Eurasian Spoonbill is sister taxon to a clade containing the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Spoonbill" title="Royal Spoonbill">Royal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black-faced_Spoonbill" title="Black-faced Spoonbill">Black-faced Spoonbills</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Eurasian Spoonbill has three <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>P. l. leucorodia</i>, the nominate race, occupies all the range except as below.</li>
<li><i>P. l. balsaci</i>. Islands off the <a href="/wiki/Banc_d%27Arguin" title="Banc d'Arguin" class="mw-redirect">Banc d'Arguin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a>.</li>
<li><i>P. l. archeri</i>. The coasts of the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Birds in Asia are sometimes separated as <i>P. l. major</i></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Spoonbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span></h2>
<p>Eurasian Spoonbills show a preference for extensive shallow, wetlands with muddy, clay or fine sandy beds. They may inhabit any type of marsh, river, lake, flooded area and mangrove swamp, whether fresh, brackish or saline, but especially those with islands for nesting or dense emergent vegetation (e.g. reedbeds) and scattered trees or srubs (especially <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willow</a> <a href="/wiki/Salix" title="Salix" class="mw-redirect">Salix</a> spp., <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> <a href="/wiki/Quercus" title="Quercus" class="mw-redirect">Quercus</a> spp. or <a href="/wiki/Poplar" title="Poplar" class="mw-redirect">poplar</a> <a href="/wiki/Populus" title="Populus">Populus</a> spp.). Eurasian Spoonbills may also frequent sheltered marine habitats during the winter such as deltas, estuaries, tidal creeks and coastal lagoons.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Spoonbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span></h2>
<p>The diet consists of adult and larval aquatic insects, molluscs, crustaceans, worms, leeches, frogs, tadpoles and small fish up to 10–15&#160;cm long. It may also take algae or small fragments of aquatic plants (although these are possibly ingested accidentally with animal matter).<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Spoonbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<p>More northerly breeding populations are fully migratory but may only migrate short distances while other, more southerly populations are resident and nomadic or partially migratory. In the Palearctic the species breeds in spring (e.g. from April) but in tropical parts of its range it times breeding to coincide with rainfall. Breeding is normally in single species colonies or in small single species groups amidst mixed-species colonies of other waterbirds such as herons, egrets and cormorants. Outside the breeding season Eurasian Spoonbills forage singly or in small flocks of up to 100 individuals. Migration is usually conducted in flocks of up to 100 individuals. Most activity takes place during the morning and evening (although in coastal areas it foraging is governed by tidal rhythms), they often roost communally in roosts which are up to 15&#160;km away from the feeding areas.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The nest is a platform of sticks and vegetation which is either constructed on the ground on islands in lakes and rivers or in dense stands of reeds, bushes, mangroves or deciduous trees up to 5 m above the ground1. Within colonies neighbouring nests are usually quite close together, no more than 1 or 2m apart. Breeding colonies are normally sited within 10–15&#160;km of feeding areas, often much less (although the species may also feed up to 35–40&#160;km away).<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_Spoonbill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span></h2>
<p>Threats to the Eurasian Spoonbill include habitat destruction by drainage and degradation by pollution, It is especially adversely affected by the disappearance of reed swamps. In Greece over-fishing and disturbance have caused the population to decline, and human exploitation of eggs and nestlings for food has threatened the species in the past.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> As stated above it has recently started to breed in the United Kingdom from where it was extirpated in the 17th Century.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
