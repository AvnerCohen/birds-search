<p>The <b>Dead Sea Sparrow</b> (<i>Passer moabiticus</i>), as its name suggests, is a breeding <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> around the <a href="/wiki/River_Jordan" title="River Jordan" class="mw-redirect">River Jordan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a>, and into <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>. Breeding recorded in <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a> (1981/1982) but is probably extinct there now. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> or dispersive, although the regular wintering grounds of this nomadic species are largely unknown, except that the eastern race winters in <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>. Flocks of the nominate western race have been found in winter further south in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>.</p>
<p>It is a small 12–13&#160;cm long <a href="/wiki/Sparrow" title="Sparrow">sparrow</a> which breeds in dry lowlands with some shrubs, including <a href="/wiki/Tamarisk" title="Tamarisk" class="mw-redirect">tamarisk</a>, and access to water. It builds a nest in a tree, and 4-7 eggs are laid.</p>
<p>The male Dead Sea Sparrow has a grey crown, rear neck and cheeks, and a small black bib. It has a pale <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a> shading to buff at the rear, and yellow neck sides. The upperparts are dark-streaked reddish brown, and the underparts are grey-white.</p>
<p>The female is like a small <a href="/wiki/House_Sparrow" title="House Sparrow">House Sparrow</a>, with a streaked brown back, greyish head and buff-white underparts. She is paler and smaller billed than her relative, and sometimes shows yellow on the neck sides.</p>
<p>The eastern race <i>P. m. yatii</i> is sandier, and the male has a yellow wash to the underparts.</p>
<p>This species feeds principally on seeds, like other sparrows. The chirping song resembles those of House and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Sparrow" title="Spanish Sparrow">Spanish Sparrows</a>, but is weaker. The flight call is a high-pitched <i>chi-wit</i>. This species is often silent.</p>
