<p>The <b>great white pelican</b> (<i>Pelecanus onocrotalus</i>) also known as the <b>eastern white pelican</b>, <b>rosy pelican</b> or <b>white pelican</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Pelican" title="Pelican">pelican</a> family. It breeds from southeastern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> through <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and  <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a> and shallow lakes.
</p><p>The great white pelican has been rated as a species of&#160;<a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least concern</a>&#160;on the&#160;<a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List_of_Endangered_Species" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN Red List of Endangered Species">IUCN Red List&#160;of Endangered Species</a>&#160;(IUCN). It is listed under Appendix I of the&#160;<a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Conservation_of_Migratory_Species_of_Wild_Animals" title="Convention on the Conservation of Migratory Species of Wild Animals">Convention on the Conservation of Migratory Species of Wild Animals</a>, Annexure I under the EU Birds Directive on the Conservation of Wild Birds, and Appendix II of the <a href="/wiki/Berne_Convention_on_the_Conservation_of_European_Wildlife_and_Natural_Habitats" title="Berne Convention on the Conservation of European Wildlife and Natural Habitats">Berne Convention on the Conservation of European Wildlife and Natural Habitats</a>. It is also listed within 108 <a href="/wiki/Special_Protection_Area" title="Special Protection Area">Special Protection Areas</a> in the European Union. It occurs within 43 <a href="/wiki/Important_Bird_Area" title="Important Bird Area">Important Bird Areas</a> (IBAs) in its European range. It is one of the species to which the&#160;<a href="/wiki/Agreement_on_the_Conservation_of_African-Eurasian_Migratory_Waterbirds" title="Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a>&#160;(AEWA) is applied.
</p>

<p>The great white pelican is a huge bird—only the <a href="/wiki/Dalmatian_pelican" title="Dalmatian pelican">Dalmatian pelican</a> is, on average, larger among pelicans. It measures 140 to 180&#160;cm (55 to 71&#160;in) in length<sup id="cite_ref-:12_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> with a 28.9 to 47.1&#160;cm (11.4 to 18.5&#160;in) enormous pink and yellow <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:12_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and a dull pale-yellow gular pouch.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fanshawe2001_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fanshawe2001-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:22_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:22-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">wingspan</a> measures 226 to 360&#160;cm (7.41 to 11.81&#160;ft),<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> the latter measurement being the highest among extant flying animals outside of the <a href="/wiki/Great_albatross" title="Great albatross">great albatross</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wood_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The adult male measures about 175&#160;cm (69&#160;in) in length; it weighs from 9 to 15&#160;kg (20 to 33&#160;lb) <sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and larger races from the <a href="/wiki/Palaearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palaearctic">Palaearctic</a> are usually around 11&#160;kg (24&#160;lb), with few exceeding 13&#160;kg (29&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It has a bill measuring 34.7 to 47.1&#160;cm (13.7 to 18.5&#160;in). The female measures about 148&#160;cm (58&#160;in) in length, and is considerably less bulky, weighing 5.4 to 9&#160;kg (12 to 20&#160;lb), and has a bill that measures 28.9 to 40.0&#160;cm (11.4 to 15.7&#160;in) in length.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Lake_Edward" title="Lake Edward">Lake Edward</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>, the average weight of 52 males was found to 11.45&#160;kg (25.2&#160;lb) and in 22 females it was 7.59&#160;kg (16.7&#160;lb). In <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, the average weight of males was 9.6&#160;kg (21&#160;lb) and of females was 6.9&#160;kg (15&#160;lb). Thus the sexual dimorphism is especially pronounced in this species (perhaps the greatest known in any extant pelican), as at times the male can average more than 30% more massive than the female. The great white pelican rivals the <a href="/wiki/Kori_bustard" title="Kori bustard">kori bustard</a>, which has even more pronounced sexual dimorphism, as the heaviest flying bird to reside in Africa (both averaging perhaps slightly heavier than the <a href="/wiki/Cape_vulture" title="Cape vulture">cape vulture</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Wattled_crane" title="Wattled crane">wattled crane</a>). There are a small few slightly heavier flying birds in the Eurasian portions of the range.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Among standard measurements, the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> is 60 to 73&#160;cm (24 to 29&#160;in), the <a href="/wiki/Tail" title="Tail">tail</a> 16 to 21&#160;cm (6.3 to 8.3&#160;in), and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> 13 to 14.9&#160;cm (5.1 to 5.9&#160;in) long. Standard measurements from different areas indicate that pelicans from the <a href="/wiki/Western_Palaearctic" title="Western Palaearctic">Western Palaearctic</a> are somewhat larger than those from Asia and Africa.
</p><p>The male has a downward bend in the neck and the female has a shorter, straighter beak. The <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> is predominantly white except on <a href="/wiki/Remiges" class="mw-redirect" title="Remiges">remiges</a>, with a faint pink tinge on the neck and a yellowish base on the foreneck.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The primary feathers are black, with white shafts at the bases, occasionally with paler tips and narrow fringes. The secondary feathers are also black, but with a whitish fringe. The upperwing coverts, underwing coverts, and tertials are white.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The forehead is swollen and pinkish skin surrounds the bare, dark eyes having brown-red to dark brown irides.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:22_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:22-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It has fleshy-yellow legs and pointed forehead-feathers where meeting the <a href="/wiki/Culmen_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Culmen (bird)">culmen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In breeding season, the male has pinkish skin while the female has orangey skin on its face.<sup id="cite_ref-Mclachlan1978_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mclachlan1978-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The bill is mostly bluish grey, with a red tip, reddish maxilla edges, and a cream-yellow to yolk-yellow gular pouch.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The white plumage becomes tinged-pink with a yellow patch on the breast,<sup id="cite_ref-:22_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:22-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and the body is tinged yellowish-rosy.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It also has a short, shaggy crest on the nape.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The white <a href="/wiki/Covert_feather" title="Covert feather">covert feathers</a> contrast with the solid black <a href="/wiki/Primary_feathers" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary feathers">primary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Secondary_feathers" class="mw-redirect" title="Secondary feathers">secondary</a> feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-:12_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The legs are yellow-flesh to pinkish orange. Both male and female are similar, but the female is smaller and has brighter orange facial skin in the breeding season.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The juvenile has darker, brownish underparts that are palest at the rump, center of the belly, and <a href="/wiki/Uppertail_covert" class="mw-redirect" title="Uppertail covert">uppertail coverts</a>. The underwing coverts are mostly dull-white, but the greater coverts are dark and there is a dark brownish bar over the lesser coverts. The rear tertials upperwing coverts mostly have paler tips with a silvery-grey tinge on the greater secondary coverts and tertials.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It has dark <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">flight feathers</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and brown-edged wings.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The head, neck, and <a href="/wiki/Upperparts" class="mw-redirect" title="Upperparts">upperparts</a>, including the upperwing coverts, are mostly brown—this is the darkest part of the neck.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The facial skin&#160;and the bill, including its gular pouch, are greyish to dusky greyish.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The forehead, rump, and abdomen are white, and its legs and feet are grey.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Its blackish tail occasionally has a silvery-grey tinge. Its <a href="/wiki/Underparts" class="mw-redirect" title="Underparts">underparts</a> and back are initially browner and darker than those of the Dalmatian&#160;pelican, and the underwing is strongly patterned, similar to the juvenile <a href="/wiki/Brown_pelican" title="Brown pelican">brown pelican</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The great white pelican is distinguished from all other pelicans by its plumage. Its face is naked and the feathering on its forehead tapers to a fine point, whereas other species are completely feathered. In flight, the white underwing with black remiges of the adult are similar only to those of the <a href="/wiki/American_white_pelican" title="American white pelican">American white pelican</a> (<i>P. erythrorhynchos</i>), but the latter has white inner secondary feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It differs from the Dalmatian pelican in its pure white – rather than greyish-white – plumage, a bare pink facial patch around the eye, and pinkish legs.<sup id="cite_ref-:32_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:32-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Spot-billed_pelican" title="Spot-billed pelican">spot-billed pelican</a> (<i>P. philippensis</i>) of Asia is slightly smaller than the great white pelican, with greyish tinged white plumage, and a paler, duller-colored bill.<sup id="cite_ref-:22_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:22-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, the <a href="/wiki/Pink-backed_pelican" title="Pink-backed pelican">pink-backed pelican</a> (<i>P. rufescens</i>) is smaller, with brownish-grey plumage, a light pink to off-grey bill, and a pinkish wash on the back.<sup id="cite_ref-:32_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:32-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The bird is mostly silent but has a variety of low-pitched lowing, grunting, and growling calls. The flight call is a deep, quiet croak.,<sup id="cite_ref-:32_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:32-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> while at breeding colonies, it gives deep <i>moooo</i> calls.<sup id="cite_ref-:12_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_white_pelican&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The&#160;breeding range of the great white pelican extends to Ethiopia, Tanzania, Chad, northern Cameroons, and Nigeria in Africa, and has been observed or reported breeding in Zambia, Botswana, and South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In the 1990s, 6,700 to 11,000 breeding pairs in 23 to 25 breeding sites, were found in the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic_realm" title="Palearctic realm">Palearctic</a> region.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> A 1991 study noted about 3,070 to 4,300 pairs were present in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Only two breeding colonies are located in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">Mediterranean basin</a>, one having 250 to 400 pairs in Turkey and the other having 50 to 100 pairs in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Greece" title="Northern Greece">northern Greece</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The breeding colony at <a href="/wiki/Lake_Rukwa" title="Lake Rukwa">Lake Rukwa, Tanzania</a> is the largest known breeding colony in Africa, followed by the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Shala" title="Lake Shala">Lake Shala, Ethiopia</a> colony which is probably of crucial importance to the species in Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
The African population of about 75,000 pairs of the great white pelican is resident.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The ones breeding in the Palearctic region are migrants, although it is possible that the majority of the western Palearctic populations stop-over in Israel during their autumn migration.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The migration routes are only partially known.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Migratory populations are found from <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a> to Kazakhstan during the breeding season. More than 50% of Eurasian great white pelicans breed in the <a href="/wiki/Danube_Delta" title="Danube Delta">Danube Delta</a> in Romania. They also prefer staying in the <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">Lakes</a> near <a href="/wiki/Burgas" title="Burgas">Burgas</a>, Bulgaria and in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Srebarna_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Srebarna Lake (page does not exist)">Srebarna Lake</a> in Bulgaria. The pelicans arrive in the Danube in late March or early April and depart after breeding from September to late November.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Wintering locations for European pelicans are not exactly known but wintering birds may occur in northeastern Africa through Iraq to <a href="/wiki/North_India" title="North India">north India</a>, with a particularly large number of breeders from Asia wintering around Pakistan and Sri Lanka.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Northern populations migrate to China, India, Myanmar, with stragglers reaching <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bali" title="Bali">Bali</a> in Indonesia.<sup id="cite_ref-:22_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:22-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> These are birds that are found mostly in lowlands, though in <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a> and Nepal may be found living at elevations of up to 1,372&#160;m (4,501&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Overall, the great white pelican is one of the most widely distributed species. Although some areas still hold quite large colonies, it ranks behind the brown pelican and possibly the <a href="/wiki/Australian_pelican" title="Australian pelican">Australian pelican</a> in overall abundance.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Europe now holds an estimated 7,345–10,000 breeding pairs, with over 4,000 pairs that are known to nest in Russia. During migration, more than 75,000 have been observed in Israel and, in winter, over 45,000 may stay in Pakistan. In all its colonies combined, 75,000 pairs are estimated to nest in Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is possibly extinct in Serbia and Montenegro, and regionally extinct in Hungary.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Great white pelicans usually prefer shallow, (seasonally or tropical) warm <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a>. Well scattered groups of breeding pelicans occur through Eurasia from the eastern <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> to Vietnam.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In Eurasia, fresh or <a href="/wiki/Brackish_water" title="Brackish water">brackish waters</a> may be inhabited and the pelicans may be found in lakes, deltas, <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a>, usually with <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">dense reed beds</a> nearby for nesting purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, sedentary populations are found year-round in Africa, south of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" class="mw-redirect" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a> although these are patchy. In Africa, great white pelicans occur mainly around <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_lake" class="mw-redirect" title="Freshwater lake">freshwater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_lake" class="mw-redirect" title="Alkaline lake">alkaline lakes</a> and may also be found in coastal, estuarine areas.<sup id="cite_ref-Crawford_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Beyond reed beds, African pelicans have nested on <a href="/wiki/Monadnock" class="mw-redirect" title="Monadnock">inselbergs</a> and flat inshore islands off of <a href="/wiki/Banc_d%27Arguin_National_Park" title="Banc d&#39;Arguin National Park">Banc d'Arguin National Park</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_white_pelican&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Danube_Delta_pelican_flying.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Danube_Delta_pelican_flying.jpg/220px-Danube_Delta_pelican_flying.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Danube_Delta_pelican_flying.jpg/330px-Danube_Delta_pelican_flying.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Danube_Delta_pelican_flying.jpg/440px-Danube_Delta_pelican_flying.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5184" data-file-height="3456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Danube_Delta_pelican_flying.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pelican taking off from the water in the <a href="/wiki/Danube_Delta" title="Danube Delta">Danube Delta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania </a></div></div></div>
<p>The great white pelican is highly sociable and often forms large flocks.<sup id="cite_ref-:32_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:32-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It is well adapted for aquatic life. The short strong legs and webbed feet propel it in water and aid a rather awkward takeoff from the water surface. In flight, it is an elegant soaring bird, with the head held close to and aligned with the body. Its flight consists of a few slow wingbeats followed by a glide.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Once aloft, the long-winged pelicans are powerful fliers, however, and often travel in spectacular linear, circular, or V-formation groups.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_white_pelican&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The great white pelican mainly <a href="/wiki/Piscivore" title="Piscivore">eats fish</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It leaves its roost to feed early in the morning and may fly over 100&#160;km (62&#160;mi) in search of food, as has been observed in Chad and in <a href="/wiki/Mogode" title="Mogode">Mogode, Cameroon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It needs from 0.9 to 1.4&#160;kg (2.0 to 3.1&#160;lb) of fish every day,<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> which corresponds to around 28,000,000&#160;kg (62,000,000&#160;lb) annual fish consumption at the largest colony of the great white pelican, on Tanzania's Lake Rukwa (with almost 75,000 birds). Fish targeted are usually fairly large ones, in the 500–600&#160;g (1.1–1.3&#160;lb) weight range, and are taken based on regional abundance.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Common_carp" title="Common carp">Common carp</a> are preferred in Europe, <a href="/wiki/Mullet_(fish)" title="Mullet (fish)">mullets</a> in China, and <a href="/wiki/Arabian_toothcarp" title="Arabian toothcarp">Arabian toothcarp</a> in India.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In Africa, often the commonest <a href="/wiki/Cichlid" title="Cichlid">cichlids</a>, including many species in the <i><a href="/wiki/Haplochromis" title="Haplochromis">Haplochromis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Tilapia" title="Tilapia">Tilapia</a></i> genera, seem to be preferred.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The pelican's pouch serves simply as a scoop. As the pelican pushes its bill underwater, the lower bill bows out, creating a large pouch which fills with water and fish. As it lifts its head, the pouch contracts, forcing out the water but retaining the fish. A group of 6 to 8 great white pelicans gather in a horseshoe formation in water to feed together. They dip their bills in unison, creating a circle of open pouches, ready to trap every fish in the area. Most feeding is cooperative and done in groups, especially in shallow waters where fish schools can be corralled easily, though they may also forage alone as well.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Great white pelicans are not restricted to fish, however, and are often opportunistic foragers. In some situations, they eat chicks of other birds, such as the well documented case off the southwest coast of South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Here, breeding pelicans from the <a href="/wiki/Dassen_Island" title="Dassen Island">Dassen Island</a> prey on chicks weighing up to 2&#160;kg (4.4&#160;lb) from the <a href="/wiki/Cape_gannet" title="Cape gannet">Cape gannet</a> colony on <a href="/wiki/Malgas_Island" title="Malgas Island">Malgas Island</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ryan2007_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ryan2007-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, in <a href="/wiki/Walvis_Bay" title="Walvis Bay">Walvis Bay, Namibia</a> the eggs and chicks of <a href="/wiki/Cape_cormorant" title="Cape cormorant">Cape cormorants</a> are fed regularly to young pelicans. The local pelican population is so reliant on the cormorants, that when the cormorant species experienced a population decline, the numbers of pelicans appeared to decline as well.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>They also <a href="/wiki/Kleptoparasitism" title="Kleptoparasitism">rob other birds</a> of their prey. During periods of <a href="/wiki/Starvation" title="Starvation">starvation</a>, they also eat <a href="/wiki/Seagull" class="mw-redirect" title="Seagull">seagulls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Duckling" class="mw-redirect" title="Duckling">ducklings</a>. The gulls are held under water and drowned before being eaten headfirst. A flock of captive great white pelicans in <a href="/wiki/St_James%27s_Park" title="St James&#39;s Park">St James's Park</a>, London is well known for occasionally eating local <a href="/wiki/Feral_pigeon" title="Feral pigeon">pigeons</a>, despite being well-fed.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc1_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc1-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bbc2_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc2-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_white_pelican&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pelecanus_onocrotalus_MWNH_0512.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Pelecanus_onocrotalus_MWNH_0512.JPG/220px-Pelecanus_onocrotalus_MWNH_0512.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Pelecanus_onocrotalus_MWNH_0512.JPG/330px-Pelecanus_onocrotalus_MWNH_0512.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Pelecanus_onocrotalus_MWNH_0512.JPG/440px-Pelecanus_onocrotalus_MWNH_0512.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3515" data-file-height="2772" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pelecanus_onocrotalus_MWNH_0512.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>The breeding season commences in April or May in temperate zones, is essentially all year around in Africa and runs February through April in India. Large numbers of these pelicans breed together in colonies.
</p><p>Nest locations vary: some populations make stick nests in trees but a majority, including all those that breed in Africa, nest exclusively in scrapes on the ground lined with grass, sticks, feathers and other material.<sup id="cite_ref-Crawford_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The female can lay from 1 to 4 eggs in a clutch, with two being the average.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Incubation_period" title="Incubation period">Incubation</a> takes 29 to 36 days. The chicks are naked when they hatch but quickly sprout blackish-brown down. The young are cared for by both parents.  The colony gathers in "pods" around 20 to 25 days after the eggs hatch. The young <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> at 65 to 75 days of age. Around 64% of young successful reach adulthood, attaining sexual maturity at 3 to 4 years of age.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators">Predators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_white_pelican&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Predators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While white pelicans are often protected from <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird-eating raptors</a> by virtue of their own great size, <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a>, especially sympatric <i><a href="/wiki/Haliaeetus" class="mw-redirect" title="Haliaeetus">Haliaeetus</a></i> species, may prey on their eggs, nestlings, and fledglings. Occasionally, pelicans and their young are attacked at their colonies by <a href="/wiki/Carnivora" title="Carnivora">mammalian carnivores</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Jackal" title="Jackal">jackals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lions</a>. As is common in pelicans, the close approach of a large predaceous or unknown mammal, including a human, at a colony will lead the pelican to abandon its nest in self-preservation.<sup id="cite_ref-McLean_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLean-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, <a href="/wiki/Crocodile" title="Crocodile">crocodiles</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Nile_crocodile" title="Nile crocodile">Nile crocodiles</a> in Africa, readily kill and eat swimming pelicans.<sup id="cite_ref-Ross1989_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ross1989-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_and_conservation">Status and conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_white_pelican&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Status and conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1998, the great white pelican has been rated as a species of&#160;<a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>&#160;on the&#160;IUCN Red List&#160;of Endangered Species. This is because it has a large range – more than 20,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>&#160;(7700&#160;mi<sup>2</sup>) – and because its population is thought not to have declined by 30% over ten years or three generations, which is not a rapid enough decline to warrant a&#160;<a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable</a>&#160;rating. However, the state of its overall population is not known,&#160;and although it is widespread, it is not abundant anywhere.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is one of the species to which the <a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a> (AEWA) applies. It is listed under the Appendix I of the Convention on the Conservation of Migratory Species of Wild Animals, Appendix II of the Berne Convention on the Conservation of European Wildlife and Natural Habitats and Annexure I of the EU Birds Directive on the Conservation of Wild Birds. It occurs within 43 Important Bird Areas in its European range, and is listed within 108 Special Protection Areas in the European Union.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> This species is often kept in captivity in zoos or in semi-wild colonies such as that in St. James's Park, London. The ancestors of this colony were originally given to <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a> by the Russian ambassador in 1664 which initiated the tradition of ambassadors donating the birds.<sup id="cite_ref-royalparks_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-royalparks-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Today, because of <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a> in certain areas, white pelicans are forced to fly long distances to find food. Great white pelicans are exploited for many reasons. Their pouch is used to make tobacco bags, their skin is turned into leather, the <a href="/wiki/Guano" title="Guano">guano</a> is used as <a href="/wiki/Fertiliser" class="mw-redirect" title="Fertiliser">fertiliser</a>, and the fat of its young is converted into oils for traditional medicine in China and India. In Ethiopia, great white pelicans are shot for their meat. Human disturbance, loss of foraging habitat and breeding sites, as well as pollution are all contributing to the decline of the great white pelican. Declines have been particularly notable in the Palaearctic.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_3-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
