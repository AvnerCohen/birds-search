<p>The <b>striated heron</b> (<i>Butorides striata</i>) also known as <b>mangrove heron</b>, <b>little heron</b> or <b><a href="/wiki/Green-backed_heron" title="Green-backed heron">green-backed heron</a></b>, is a small <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">heron</a>, about 44&#160;cm tall. Striated herons are mostly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">non-migratory</a> and noted for some interesting behavioral traits. Their breeding habitat is small wetlands in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> tropics from west Africa to Japan and Australia, and in South America and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>. Vagrants have been recorded on Oceanic islands, such as <a href="/wiki/Chuuk_State" title="Chuuk State">Chuuk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yap" title="Yap">Yap</a> in the Federated States of Micronesia, the <a href="/wiki/Marianas" class="mw-redirect" title="Marianas">Marianas</a> and Palau; the bird recorded on Yap on February 25, 1991, was from a continental Asian rather than from a <a href="/wiki/Melanesia" title="Melanesia">Melanesian</a> population, while the origin of the bird seen on Palau on May 3, 2005 was not clear.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This bird was long considered to be <a href="/wiki/Conspecific" class="mw-redirect" title="Conspecific">conspecific</a> with the closely related North American species, the <a href="/wiki/Green_heron" title="Green heron">green heron</a>, which is now usually separated as <i>B. virescens</i>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Lava_heron" title="Lava heron">lava heron</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_Islands" title="Galápagos Islands">Galápagos Islands</a> (now <i>B. sundevalli</i>, but often included in <i>B. striata</i>, e.g. by <a href="/wiki/BirdLife_International" title="BirdLife International">BirdLife International</a><sup id="cite_ref-bli2008_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bli2008-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>); collectively they were called "<a href="/wiki/Green-backed_heron" title="Green-backed heron">green-backed herons</a>".
</p><p>The bird can also be found in <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a> around the town of <a href="/wiki/Riberalta" title="Riberalta">Riberalta</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Beni_Department" title="Beni Department">Beni Department</a> and specifically in the Aquicuana Reserve.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_ecology">Description and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Striated_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Butorides_striata_Orto_botanico_di_Pamplemousses_Mauritius_0069.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Butorides striata Orto botanico di Pamplemousses Mauritius 0069.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Butorides_striata_Orto_botanico_di_Pamplemousses_Mauritius_0069.jpg/220px-Butorides_striata_Orto_botanico_di_Pamplemousses_Mauritius_0069.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Butorides_striata_Orto_botanico_di_Pamplemousses_Mauritius_0069.jpg/330px-Butorides_striata_Orto_botanico_di_Pamplemousses_Mauritius_0069.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Butorides_striata_Orto_botanico_di_Pamplemousses_Mauritius_0069.jpg/440px-Butorides_striata_Orto_botanico_di_Pamplemousses_Mauritius_0069.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1895" data-file-height="1271" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Butorides_striata_Orto_botanico_di_Pamplemousses_Mauritius_0069.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>
<p>Adults have a blue-grey back and wings, white underparts, a black cap, a dark line extends from the bill to under the eye and short yellow legs. Juveniles are browner above and streaked below.
</p><p>These birds stand still at the water's edge and wait to ambush prey, but are easier to see than many small heron species. They mainly eat small fish, frogs and aquatic insects. They sometimes use bait, dropping a feather or leaf carefully on the water surface and picking fish that come to investigate.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>They nest in a platform of sticks measuring between 20–40&#160;cm long and 0.5–5&#160;mm thick. The entire nest measures some 40–50&#160;cm wide and 8–10&#160;cm high outside, with an inner depression 20&#160;cm wide and 4–5&#160;cm deep. It is usually built in not too high off the ground in shrubs or trees but sometimes in sheltered locations on the ground, and often near water. The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is 2–5 <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>, which are pale blue and measure around 36 by 28&#160;mm.<sup id="cite_ref-greeneyetal2006_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greeneyetal2006-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An adult bird was once observed in a peculiar and mysterious behavior: while on the nest, it would grab a stick in its bill and make a rapid back-and-forth motion with the head, like a sewing machine's needle. The significance of this behavior is completely unknown: While such movements occur in many other nesting birds where they seem to compact the nest, move the eggs, or dislodge <a href="/wiki/Parasite" class="mw-redirect" title="Parasite">parasites</a>, neither seems to have been the case in this particular striated heron.<sup id="cite_ref-greeneyetal2006_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greeneyetal2006-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Young birds will give a display when they feel threatened, by stretching out their necks and pointing the bill skywards. How far this would deter <a href="/wiki/Predator" class="mw-redirect" title="Predator">predators</a> is not known.<sup id="cite_ref-greeneyetal2006_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greeneyetal2006-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Widespread and generally common, the striated heron is classified as a species of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>; this holds true whether the <a href="/wiki/Lava_heron" title="Lava heron">lava heron</a> is included in <i>B. striata</i> or not.<sup id="cite_ref-bli2008_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bli2008-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
