<p>The <b>common rosefinch</b> (<i>Carpodacus erythrinus</i>) or <b>scarlet rosefinch</b> is the most widespread and common <a href="/wiki/Rosefinch" title="Rosefinch">rosefinch</a> of Asia and Europe.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_rosefinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a molecular <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Phylogenetic">phylogenetic</a> study of the finch family published in 2012, Zuccon and colleagues found that the common rosefinch fell outside the core <i>Carpodacus</i> rosefinch clade and was a <a href="/wiki/Sister_group" title="Sister group">sister</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Scarlet_finch" title="Scarlet finch">scarlet finch</a> (at the time <i>Haematospiza sipahi</i>). They recommended that the common rosefinch should be moved to a new <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monotypic">monotypic</a> genus with the resurrected name of <i>Erythrina</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Zuccon2012_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zuccon2012-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/British_Ornithologists%27_Union" title="British Ornithologists&#39; Union">British Ornithologists' Union</a> accepted this proposal,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> but the <a href="/wiki/International_Ornithological_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="International Ornithological Union">International Ornithological Union</a> chose instead to adopt a more inclusive <i>Carpodacus</i> that retained the common rosefinch in the rosefinch genus.<sup id="cite_ref-iou_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iou-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The genus name is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>karpos</i>, "fruit" and  <i>dakno</i>, "to bite", and the specific <i>erythrinus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>  <i>erythros</i>, "red".<sup id="cite_ref-job_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_rosefinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common rosefinch is 13–15&#160;cm (5.1–5.9&#160;in) in length.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwalive_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwalive-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It has a stout and conical bill. The mature male has brilliant rosy-carmine head, breast and rump; heavy bill; dark brown wings with two indistinct bars, and a white belly. Females and young males are dull-colored with yellowish-brown above, brighter on the rump and greyer on head; buff below.
</p><p>Adults moult in their winter quarters, between September and November. After moulting the red of male is subdued, and becomes brighter during the winter due to wear of the feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_rosefinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It has spread westward through <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> in recent decades, even breeding in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> once. Common rosefinches breed from the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> valley, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Sea" title="Bering Sea">Bering Sea</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, the western <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>; to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> between latitudes 25° and 68°. In winter they are found from southern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> to south-east China <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burma" class="mw-redirect" title="Burma">Burma</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indochina" class="mw-redirect" title="Indochina">Indochina</a>.
</p><p>They are found in summer in thickets, woodland and forest edges near rivers and in winter in gardens and orchards, wetlands and locally in dry oak woods.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

The eggs are dark blue with coarse dark brown spots, and a typical clutch contains five eggs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. </p>
