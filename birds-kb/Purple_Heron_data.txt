<p>The <b>purple heron</b> (<i>Ardea purpurea</i>) is a wide-ranging <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of wading bird in the heron family, <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">Ardeidae</a>. The scientific name comes from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>ardea</i> "heron", and <i>purpureus</i>, "coloured purple".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It breeds in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, central and southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, and southern and eastern <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Asia</a>. The Western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> populations <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> between breeding and wintering habitats whereas the African and tropical-Asian populations are primarily sedentary, except for occasional dispersive movements.
</p><p>It is similar in appearance to the more common <a href="/wiki/Grey_heron" title="Grey heron">grey heron</a> but is slightly smaller, more slender and has darker plumage. It is also a more evasive bird, favouring densely vegetated habitats near water, particularly <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed beds</a>. It hunts for a range of prey including fish, rodents, frogs and insects, either stalking them or standing waiting in ambush.
</p><p>Purple herons are <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonial</a> breeders and build a bulky nest out of dead reeds or sticks close to the water' edge among reeds or in dense vegetation. About five bluish-green eggs are laid and are <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation" title="Egg incubation">incubated</a> by both birds. The young hatch about four weeks later and <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> six weeks after that. The <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> notes that the global population trend is downwards, largely because of the drainage of wetlands, but assesses the purple heron's conservation status as being of "<a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least concern</a>".
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ardea_purpurea_-Lake_Baringo,_Great_Rift_Valley,_Kenya_-adult_and_chicks-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Ardea_purpurea_-Lake_Baringo%2C_Great_Rift_Valley%2C_Kenya_-adult_and_chicks-8.jpg/220px-Ardea_purpurea_-Lake_Baringo%2C_Great_Rift_Valley%2C_Kenya_-adult_and_chicks-8.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Ardea_purpurea_-Lake_Baringo%2C_Great_Rift_Valley%2C_Kenya_-adult_and_chicks-8.jpg/330px-Ardea_purpurea_-Lake_Baringo%2C_Great_Rift_Valley%2C_Kenya_-adult_and_chicks-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Ardea_purpurea_-Lake_Baringo%2C_Great_Rift_Valley%2C_Kenya_-adult_and_chicks-8.jpg/440px-Ardea_purpurea_-Lake_Baringo%2C_Great_Rift_Valley%2C_Kenya_-adult_and_chicks-8.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="787" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ardea_purpurea_-Lake_Baringo,_Great_Rift_Valley,_Kenya_-adult_and_chicks-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult with chicks at <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baringo" title="Lake Baringo">Lake Baringo</a>, Great Rift Valley, Kenya</div></div></div>
<p>The purple heron is a large bird, 78–97&#160;cm (31–38&#160;in) in length with a standing height from 70 to 94&#160;cm (28 to 37&#160;in) and a 120–152&#160;cm (47–60&#160;in) wingspan.<sup id="cite_ref-arkive_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arkive-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ali_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ali-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> However, it is slender for its size, weighing only 0.5 to 1.35&#160;kg (1.1 to 3.0&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It is somewhat smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Grey_heron" title="Grey heron">grey heron</a>, from which it can be distinguished by its darker reddish-brown <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>, and, in adults, its darker grey back. Adults have the forehead and the crown of the head black, with a dark stripe down the back of the neck that terminates in a slender, dangling crest. This is shorter than the crest of the grey heron and does not exceed 140&#160;mm (5.5&#160;in). The sides of the head and the neck are buffish chestnut, with dark streaks and lines down either side of the whole the neck. The mantle is oily brown and the upper scapular feathers are elongated but not the lower ones. The rest of the upper parts and the tail are brownish grey. The front of the neck is paler than the sides and there are some elongated feathers at the base of the neck which are streaked with white, chestnut and black. The breast is chestnut brown, with some blackening at the side, and the belly and under-tail coverts are black. The brownish-yellow beak is long, straight and powerful, and is brighter in colour in breeding adults. The <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">iris</a> is yellow and the legs are brown at the front and yellowish behind.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The call is a harsh "frarnk", but is quieter and more high-pitched than that of the grey heron. It is generally a less noisy bird, though similar guttural sounds are heard emanating from the <a href="/wiki/Heronry" title="Heronry">heronry</a>. It is also less robust, and appears somewhat hollow-chested. Its head and neck are more slender and snake-like than the grey heron and its toes much longer. Unlike that bird, it often adopts a posture with its neck extending obliquely, and even nestlings tend to use this stance.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are three or four <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-IOC_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IOC-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>A. p. purpurea</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, 1766</small>: <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies#Nominotypical_subspecies_and_subspecies_autonyms" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate</a>, found in Africa, Europe north to the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, and southwestern Asia east to <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>.</li>
<li><i>A. p. bournei</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_de_Naurois" title="René de Naurois">de Naurois</a>, 1966</small>: <a href="/wiki/Bourne%27s_heron" title="Bourne&#39;s heron">Bourne's heron</a>. Found in the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Cape Verde Islands">Cape Verde Islands</a> (included in <i>purpurea</i> by some authors, but treated as a distinct species <i>Ardea bournei</i> by some others).<sup id="cite_ref-ornitaxa_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ornitaxa-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>A. p. madagascariensis</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/E._D._van_Oort" class="mw-redirect" title="E. D. van Oort">van Oort</a>, 1910</small>: found in <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>.</li>
<li><i>A. p. manilensis</i> – <small>Meyen, 1834</small>: found in Asia from the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Subcontinent" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian Subcontinent">Indian Subcontinent</a> east to the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, and north to <a href="/wiki/Primorsky_Krai" title="Primorsky Krai">Primorsky Krai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SL_Bundala_NP_asv2020-01_img04.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/SL_Bundala_NP_asv2020-01_img04.jpg/170px-SL_Bundala_NP_asv2020-01_img04.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/SL_Bundala_NP_asv2020-01_img04.jpg/255px-SL_Bundala_NP_asv2020-01_img04.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/SL_Bundala_NP_asv2020-01_img04.jpg/340px-SL_Bundala_NP_asv2020-01_img04.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3669" data-file-height="2935" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SL_Bundala_NP_asv2020-01_img04.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Purple heron in the <a href="/wiki/Bundala_National_Park" title="Bundala National Park">Bundala National Park</a>, Sri Lanka</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Graureiher_Grey_Heron.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Graureiher_Grey_Heron.jpg/170px-Graureiher_Grey_Heron.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Graureiher_Grey_Heron.jpg/255px-Graureiher_Grey_Heron.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Graureiher_Grey_Heron.jpg/340px-Graureiher_Grey_Heron.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2001" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Graureiher_Grey_Heron.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Grey_heron" title="Grey heron">grey heron</a> is paler in colour and bulkier</div></div></div>
<p>The purple heron has a mostly <a href="/wiki/Palearctic_realm" title="Palearctic realm">palaearctic</a> distribution and breeds in Europe, Asia and Africa. The range of the western race extends from southern Spain and North Africa eastwards across southern and eastern Europe as far as <a href="/wiki/Lake_Balkhash" title="Lake Balkhash">Lake Balkhash</a> in Kazakhstan. In Africa, the bird breeds in Senegal, down the east coast of Africa and in Madagascar. The eastern race extends from the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Subcontinent" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian Subcontinent">Indian Subcontinent</a>, eastwards to eastern China and the Philippines, and northwards to the basins of the <a href="/wiki/Amur_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Amur River">Amur River</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ussuri_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Ussuri River">Ussuri River</a> at about 49°N. The southern race is restricted to Madagascar,<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and a small population of purple herons on the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde Islands</a> is regarded as a separate race by some authorities.<sup id="cite_ref-ornitaxa_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ornitaxa-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Between August and October, birds of the western population <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> southwards to tropical Africa, returning northwards in March. Purple herons often overshoot their normal range on their return, and are <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrants</a> to northern Europe including Britain. The eastern population is largely <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">resident</a>, though some birds from the northern part of the range fly southwards to Korea, Thailand and Malaysia. The African birds are resident.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The purple heron inhabits marshes, lagoons and lakes surrounded by dense vegetation. It may frequent mangrove swamps on the coast but it usually chooses freshwater habitats, particularly locations with <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed beds</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Phragmites" title="Phragmites">Phragmites</a></i>). It also visits mudflats, river banks, ditches and canals.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In the Cape Verde Islands, it is more likely to be seen in the open, on arid slopes.<sup id="cite_ref-Hancock_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hancock-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Flight is slow, with the neck retracted and the legs extending a long way behind the tail. This is characteristic of herons and <a href="/wiki/Bittern" title="Bittern">bitterns</a>, and distinguishes them from <a href="/wiki/Stork" title="Stork">storks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crane_(bird)" title="Crane (bird)">cranes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spoonbill" title="Spoonbill">spoonbills</a>, which extend their necks in flight. It is a secretive bird, spending less time out in the open than the grey heron and tending to skulk in <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed beds</a>. Its long toes mean it can walk on floating vegetation, and it sometimes walks over bushes in the same way, not making any attempt to grip the branches. It seldom perches in trees, preferring more terrestrial sites to rest.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is most active at dawn and dusk, roosting with other birds in the middle of the day and at night, but increasing its diurnal activity while rearing young. It feeds in shallow water, grabbing its <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">prey</a> with its powerful beak. It will often wait motionless for prey, or slowly stalk its victim. The diet consists mainly of <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>, but nestling <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, water <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a> are also eaten. Terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a> are the commonest insect prey, followed by water beetles and aquatic <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dragonfly" title="Dragonfly">dragonflies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a> also being consumed.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ardea_purpurea_MWNH_0894.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ardea_purpurea_MWNH_0894.JPG/220px-Ardea_purpurea_MWNH_0894.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="138" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ardea_purpurea_MWNH_0894.JPG/330px-Ardea_purpurea_MWNH_0894.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ardea_purpurea_MWNH_0894.JPG/440px-Ardea_purpurea_MWNH_0894.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2712" data-file-height="1699" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ardea_purpurea_MWNH_0894.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>The purple heron usually breeds in <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonies</a> but sometimes the nests are solitary. It sometimes associates with other species such as the <a href="/wiki/Goliath_heron" title="Goliath heron">goliath heron</a> (<i>Ardea goliath</i>) or grey heron at multi-species nesting colonies. The site chosen is generally in <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed beds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canebrake" title="Canebrake">canebrakes</a> or low bushes close to large lakes or other extensive wetlands. It builds a bulky nest of dead reeds, sticks or whatever is available, carelessly pulling the material together. The eggs are bluish-green, averaging 56 by 45&#160;mm (2.20 by 1.77&#160;in). The clutch is usually four or five eggs, with occasionally seven or eight eggs being laid, though these large clutches may have resulted from two females laying in the same nest. The eggs are laid at intervals of three days, and <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation" title="Egg incubation">incubation</a> may begin with the first egg, or start when the clutch is complete. Both parents share the incubation, which lasts between 24 and 28 days, and the care of the young. When an adult brings food, its beak is dragged down by the chicks and it regurgitates food from the crop onto the nest, or the young may take food directly from the beak. The young fledge at about six weeks and become independent at two months. They then <a href="/wiki/Biological_dispersal" title="Biological dispersal">disperse</a> widely.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_heron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are estimated to be a total of between 270,000 and 570,000 purple herons in the world and the population is probably decreasing slowly. The <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> has assessed its conservation status as being of "<a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least concern</a> because the rate of decline is insufficient to justify rating it in a more threatened category. The chief threat the bird faces is drainage and disturbance of its wetland habitats, particularly destruction of the reed beds.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The purple heron is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.<sup id="cite_ref-AEWA_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AEWA-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
