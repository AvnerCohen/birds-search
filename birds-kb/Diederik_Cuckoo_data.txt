<p>The <b>Diederik Cuckoo</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> (<i>Chrysococcyx caprius</i>), formerly <b>Dideric Cuckoo</b> or <b>Didric Cuckoo</b>, is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Cuckoo" title="Cuckoo">cuckoo</a> order of birds, the Cuculiformes, which also includes the <a href="/wiki/Geococcyx" title="Geococcyx">roadrunners</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ani_(bird)" title="Ani (bird)">anis</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Hoatzin" title="Hoatzin">Hoatzin</a>.</p>
<p>It is a very common resident breeder in Africa south of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" title="Sahara Desert" class="mw-redirect">Sahara Desert</a>. It is a short-distance seasonal <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>, moving with the rains. It is a solitary bird, found in open woodland, savanna and riverside bushes. It has been recorded as far north as Cyprus (1982).</p>
<p>The Diederik Cuckoo is a brood parasite. It lays a single <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">egg</a> mostly in the nests of <a href="/wiki/Ploceidae" title="Ploceidae">weavers</a>, especially the <a href="/wiki/Village_Weaver" title="Village Weaver">Village Weaver</a> and the bishops in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Euplectes" title="Euplectes">Euplectes</a></i>.</p>
<p>The Diederik Cuckoo is a smallish cuckoo at 18 to 20&#160;cm. Adult males are green above with copper-sheened areas on the back and whitish underparts. They have a broken white eyestripe, a green moustauchial stripe, and green outer tail <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a> with small white spots.</p>
<p>Females show more copper above, and have coppery barring on the flanks. The underparts are often washed brownish. Juveniles are more copper-coloured above and browner below than the females, and the flank markings are brown blotches.</p>
<p>The Diederik Cuckoo takes a variety of insects and <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a>. It is a noisy species, with the persistent and loud <i>deed-deed-deed-deed-er-ick</i> call from which it gets its name. Usually four clear, roughly identical, notes followed by a little twitter.</p>
