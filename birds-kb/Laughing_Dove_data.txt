<p>The <b>Laughing Dove</b> (<i>Spilopelia senegalensis</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Dove" title="Dove" class="mw-redirect">pigeon</a> that is a resident breeder in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan" title="Sub-Saharan" class="mw-redirect">Sub-Saharan</a> <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> east to the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Subcontinent" title="Indian Subcontinent" class="mw-redirect">Indian Subcontinent</a>. This small long-tailed dove is found in dry scrub and semi-desert habitats where pairs can often be seen feeding on the ground. A rufous and black chequered necklace gives it a distinctive pattern and is also easily distinguished from other doves by its call. Other names include <b>Palm Dove</b> and <b>Senegal Dove</b> while in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> the name of the <b>Little Brown Dove</b> is often used. It was introduced in <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a> and has established itself in the wild around <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" title="Perth, Western Australia" class="mw-redirect">Perth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fremantle,_Western_Australia" title="Fremantle, Western Australia" class="mw-redirect">Fremantle</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Laughing_Dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Laughing_Dove_I_IMG_0527.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Laughing_Dove_I_IMG_0527.jpg/220px-Laughing_Dove_I_IMG_0527.jpg" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Laughing_Dove_I_IMG_0527.jpg/330px-Laughing_Dove_I_IMG_0527.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Laughing_Dove_I_IMG_0527.jpg/440px-Laughing_Dove_I_IMG_0527.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Laughing_Dove_I_IMG_0527.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>S. s cambayensis</i> eating rice in <a href="/wiki/Bhopal" title="Bhopal">Bhopal</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a></div>

<p>The Laughing Dove is a long-tailed, slim pigeon, typically 25&#160;cm (9.8&#160;in) in length. It is pinkish brown on the underside with a lilac tinged head and neck. The head and underparts are pinkish, shading to buff on the lower <a href="/wiki/Abdomen" title="Abdomen">abdomen</a>. A chequered rufous and grey patch is found on the sides of the neck and are made up of split feathers. The upper parts are brownish with a bluish-grey band along the wing. The back is uniform and dull brown in the Indian population. The African populations <i>senegalensis</i> and <i>phoenicophila</i> have a bluish grey rump and upper tail coverts but differ in the shades of the neck and wing feathers while <i>aegyptiaca</i> is larger and the head and nape are vinous and upper wing coverts are rufous.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The tail is graduated and the outer feathers are tipped in white. The sexes are indistinguishable in the field. <a href="/wiki/Juvenile_(organism)" title="Juvenile (organism)">Young birds</a> lack the chequered neck markings. The <a href="/wiki/Leg" title="Leg">legs</a> are red. The populations vary slightly in plumage with those from more arid zones being paler.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Abnormal <a href="/wiki/Leucism" title="Leucism">leucistic</a> plumages have been noted.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The chuckling call is a low rolling <i>croo-doo-doo-doo-doo</i> with a rising and falling amplitude.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Laughing_Dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>It is a common and widespread species in <a href="/wiki/Scrubland" title="Scrubland" class="mw-redirect">scrub</a>, dry <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farmland</a> and habitation over a good deal of its range, often becoming very tame. The species is found in much of Sub-Saharan Africa, Arabia, Iran, Iraq, Afghanistan, Pakistan and India. They are mostly sedentary but some populations may make movements. Birds ringed in Gujarat have been recovered 200 km north in Pakistan and exhausted birds have been recorded landing on ships in the Arabian Sea.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbk_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The species (thought to belong to the nominate population) was introduced to Perth in 1889 and has become established around Western Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-emu76_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emu76-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Birds that land on ships may be introduced to new regions.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Laughing_Dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Systematics and taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Systematics_and_taxonomy">Systematics and taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>This species was described by Linnaeus who placed it in the genus <i>Columba</i> along with other pigeons. It was later placed in the genus <i>Streptopelia</i> but studies of molecular phylogeny indicated that this and the <a href="/wiki/Spotted_Dove" title="Spotted Dove">Spotted Dove</a> stood out from the remainder of the <i>Streptopelia</i> species<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> leading to the use of an older genus name that has been used for the species by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Jakob_Sundevall" title="Carl Jakob Sundevall">Carl Sundevall</a>. Unfortunately Sundevall used the name <i>Stigmatopelia senegalensis</i> and <i>Spilopelia</i> for the Spotted Dove (he used the genus for <i>chinensis</i> as well as for <i>suratensis</i> and <i>tigrina</i>, which are now subspecies) on the same page of his 1872 book.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Some authors have argued that <i>Stigmatopelia</i> is the valid name as it has priority due to appearing in an earlier line on the page<sup id="cite_ref-cheke_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cheke-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> but Schodde and Mason in their zoological catalogue of Australian birds chose <i>Spilopelia</i> citing clause 24(b) of the ICZN Code which supports the decision of the first reviser.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Several populations with minute plumage and size differences have been given the status of subspecies and these include:<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>S. s. phoenicophila</i> (Hartert, 1916)&#160;: Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia</li>
<li><i>S. s. aegyptiaca</i> (Latham, 1790)&#160;: Nile valley</li>
<li><i>S. s. senegalensis</i> (Linnaeus, 1766)&#160;: Senegal and Nigeria including <i>aequatorialis</i> (Erlanger, 1904)</li>
<li><i>S. s. cambayensis</i> (Gmelin, 1789)&#160;: India</li>
<li><i>S. s. ermanni</i> (Bonaparte, 1856)&#160;: Afghanistan and Turkestan</li>
<li><i>S. s. sokotrae</i> (Grant, 1914)&#160;: Socotra Island</li>
<li><i>S. s. dakhlae</i> (Meinertzhagen, 1928)&#160;: Dakhla oasis, Libya (usually included in <i>phoenicophila</i>)</li>
<li><i>S. s. thome</i> (Bannerman, 1931)&#160;: Sao Thome Island (but may possibly be an introduced population)</li>

<p>The species is usually seen in pairs or small parties and only rarely in larger groups. Larger groups are formed especially when drinking at waterholes in arid regions. Small numbers assemble on trees near waterholes before flying to the water's edge where they are able to suck up water like other members of the pigeon family.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Laughing Doves eat the fallen <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a>, mainly of <a href="/wiki/Grass" title="Grass">grasses</a>, other vegetable matter and small ground insects such as termites and beetles.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> They are fairly terrestrial, foraging on the ground in <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grasslands</a> and cultivation. Their flight is quick and direct with the regular beats and an occasional sharp flick of the <a href="/wiki/Wing" title="Wing">wings</a> which are characteristic of pigeons in general.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The male in courtship display follows the female with head bobbing displays while cooing. The male pecks its folded wings in "displacement-preening" to solicit copulation form the female. A female accepts by crouching and begging for food. The male may indulge in courtship feeding before mounting and copulating. Pairs may preen each other.<sup id="cite_ref-turkey_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turkey-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Males may also launch into the air with wing clapping sounds and then glide down in a gentle arc when displaying. The species has a spread out breeding season in Africa. Almost year round in Malawi and Turkey;<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> and mainly May to November in Zimbabwe, February to June in Egypt and Tunisia. In Turkey they breed In Australia the main breeding season is September to November.<sup id="cite_ref-emu76_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emu76-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The nest is a very flimsy platform of twigs built in a low bush and sometimes in crevices or under the eaves of houses. Both parents build the nest with males bringing the twigs which are then placed by the female. Two eggs are laid within an interval of a day between them and both parents take part in building the <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a>, incubating and feeding the young. Males spend more time incubating the nest during the day.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The eggs are incubated after the second egg is laid and the eggs hatch after about 13 to 15 days.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbk_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Nesting adults may feign injury to distract and draw predators away from the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Multiple broods may be raised by the same pair in the same nest. Seven broods by the same pair have been noted in Turkey.<sup id="cite_ref-turkey_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turkey-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The young fledge and leave the nest after about 14 to 16 days.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Jacobin_Cuckoo" title="Jacobin Cuckoo">Jacobin Cuckoo</a> sometimes lays its egg in the nests of the Laughing Dove in Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Feral populations in Australia are sometimes infected by a virus that causes symptoms similar to that produced in parrots by psittacine beak and feather disease.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Several ectoparasitic <a href="/wiki/Bird_louse" title="Bird louse">bird lice</a> have been found on the species and include those in the genera <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coloceras&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Coloceras (page does not exist)">Coloceras</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Columbicola&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Columbicola (page does not exist)">Columbicola</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonomiella&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bonomiella (page does not exist)">Bonomiella</a></i> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hohorstiella&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hohorstiella (page does not exist)">Hohorstiella</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> A blood parasite <i>Trypanosoma hannae</i> has been recorded in the species.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Southern_Grey_Shrike" title="Southern Grey Shrike">Southern Grey Shrike</a> have been observed preying on an adult Laughing Dove in northwestern India while the <a href="/wiki/Lizard_Buzzard" title="Lizard Buzzard">Lizard Buzzard</a> is a predator of the species in Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> South African birds sometimes show a beak deformity in which the upper mandible overgrowth occurs.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
