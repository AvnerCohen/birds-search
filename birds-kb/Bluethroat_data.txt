<p>The <b>bluethroat</b>  (<i>Luscinia svecica</i>) is a small passerine <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> that was formerly classed as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family Turdidae, but is now more generally considered to be an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a>, Muscicapidae. It, and similar small <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> species, are often called  <a href="/wiki/Chat_(bird)" title="Chat (bird)">chats</a>.
</p><p>It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> insectivorous species breeding in wet <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a> wood or bushy swamp in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> with a foothold in western <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. It nests in tussocks or low in dense bushes. It winters in north <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>.
</p><p>The bluethroat is similar in size to the <a href="/wiki/European_robin" title="European robin">European robin</a> at 13–14&#160;cm. It is plain brown above except for the distinctive black tail with red side patches. It has a strong white supercilium.
The male has an iridescent blue bib edged below with successive black, white and rust coloured borders. Some subspecies, such as <i>L. svecica svecica</i> (<b>red-spotted bluethroat</b>) of northern Eurasia, have a red spot in the centre of the blue bib.
</p><p>Others, such as <i>L. svecica cyanecula </i> (<b>white-spotted bluethroat</b>) of southern and central Europe, have a white spot in the centre of the blue bib. <i>L. svecica magna </i> in Turkey has no central spot.
</p><p>Females of all subspecies usually have just a blackish crescent on an otherwise cream throat and breast. Newly fledged juveniles are freckled and spotted dark brown above.
</p><p>Despite the distinctive appearance of the males, recent genetic studies show only limited variation between the forms, and confirm that this is a single species.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Moults begins in July after breeding and is completed in 40–45 days, before the birds migrate.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The male has a varied and very imitative song.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Its call is a typical chat <i>chack</i> noise.
</p>

<p>The genus name <i>Luscinia</i> is  <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Common_nightingale" title="Common nightingale">common nightingale</a> and <i>svecica</i> is <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">New Latin</a>. The colours of the male's breast were thought to evoke the Swedish flag, the yellow in the flag being more orange hued in the 17th and 18th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
