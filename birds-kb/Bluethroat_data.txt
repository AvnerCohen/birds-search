<p>The <b>Bluethroat</b> (<i>Luscinia svecica</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> that was formerly classed as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family Turdidae, but is now more generally considered to be an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a>, Muscicapidae. It, and similar small <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> species, are often called <a href="/wiki/Chat_(bird)" title="Chat (bird)">chats</a>.</p>

<p>It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> insectivorous species breeding in wet <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a> wood or bushy swamp in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> with a foothold in western <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. It nests in tussocks or low in dense bushes. It winters in north <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Subcontinent" title="Indian Subcontinent" class="mw-redirect">Indian Subcontinent</a>.</p>
<p>The Bluethroat is similar in size to the <a href="/wiki/European_Robin" title="European Robin">European Robin</a> at 13–14&#160;cm. It is plain brown above except for the distinctive black tail with red side patches. It has a strong white supercilium. The male has a blue bib edged below with successive black, white and rust coloured borders. Some races, such as <i>L. svecica svecica</i> (<b>Red-spotted Bluethroat</b>) of northern Eurasia, have a red spot in the centre of the blue bib.</p>
<p>Others, such as <i>L. svecica cyanecula</i> (<b>White-spotted Bluethroat</b>) of southern and central Europe, have a white spot in the centre of the blue bib. <i>L. svecica magna</i> in Turkey has no central spot.</p>
<p>Females of all races usually have just a blackish crescent on an otherwise cream throat and breast. Newly fledged juveniles are freckled and spotted dark brown above.</p>
<p>Despite the distinctive appearance of the males, recent genetic studies show only limited variation between the forms, and confirm that this is a single species.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The male has a varied and very <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://audioblog.sonatura.com/?p=1057">imitative song</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Its call is a typical chat “chack” noise.</p>
