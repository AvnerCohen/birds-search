<p><b>Pallas's leaf warbler</b> or <b>Pallas's warbler</b> (<i>Phylloscopus proregulus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> that breeds in mountain forests from southern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> east to northern <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northeast_China" title="Northeast China">northeast China</a>. It is named for German <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Simon_Pallas" title="Peter Simon Pallas">Peter Simon Pallas</a>, who first formally described it. This <a href="/wiki/Leaf_warbler" title="Leaf warbler">leaf warbler</a> is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering mainly in <a href="/wiki/South_China" title="South China">south China</a> and adjacent areas of <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">southeast Asia</a>, although in recent decades increasing numbers have been found in Europe in autumn.
</p><p>Pallas's leaf warbler is one of the smallest <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Warbler" title="Warbler">warblers</a>, with a relatively large head and short tail. It has greenish upperparts and white underparts, a lemon-yellow rump, and yellow double wingbars, <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilia</a> and central <a href="/wiki/Crown_(anatomy)" title="Crown (anatomy)">crown</a> stripe. It is similar in appearance to several other Asian warblers, including some that were formerly considered to be its <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>, although its distinctive <a href="/wiki/Bird_vocalization" title="Bird vocalization">vocalisations</a> aid identification.
</p><p>The female builds a <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest#Cup" title="Bird nest">cup nest</a> in a tree or bush, and <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation#Avian_incubation" title="Egg incubation">incubates</a> the four to six eggs, which <a href="/wiki/Hatchling" title="Hatchling">hatch</a> after 12–13 days. The chicks are fed mainly by the female and <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> when they are 12–14 days old; both parents then bring food for about a week. Pallas's leaf warbler is <a href="/wiki/Insectivore" title="Insectivore">insectivorous</a>, feeding on the adults, <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pupa" title="Pupa">pupa</a> of small <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a>. Birds forage in bushes and trees, picking items from leaves or catching prey in short flights or while hovering. The Pallas's leaf warbler has a large range, and its numbers are believed to be stable. It therefore is evaluated as of "<a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least concern</a>" by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pallas%27s_leaf_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The English name of Pallas's leaf warbler commemorates the German <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Simon_Pallas" title="Peter Simon Pallas">Peter Simon Pallas</a>, who found it on the <a href="/wiki/Ingoda_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Ingoda River">Ingoda River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> in May 1772.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> He named the new <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> as <i>Motacilla proregulus</i> when he finally published his findings in 1811.<sup id="cite_ref-ZRA_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZRA-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The current <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> name <i>Phylloscopus</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>phullon</i>, "leaf", and <i>skopos</i>, "seeker" (from <i>skopeo</i>, "to watch"). The specific <i>proregulus</i> is from Greek <i>pro</i>, "close to", and the name <i>regulus</i>, referring to the similar-looking <a href="/wiki/Goldcrest" title="Goldcrest">goldcrest</a>, <i>Regulus regulus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-job_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>First described by German zoologist <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Boie" title="Friedrich Boie">Friedrich Boie</a> in 1826, the genus <i>Phylloscopus</i> comprises about 50 species of small <a href="/wiki/Insectivore" title="Insectivore">insectivorous</a> Old World woodland warblers that are either greenish or brown above and yellowish, white or buff below. The genus was formerly part of the <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">Old World warbler</a> family Sylvidae, but has now been split off as a separate family, the <a href="/wiki/Phylloscopidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Phylloscopidae">Phylloscopidae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-alstrom_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alstrom-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Within the genus, Pallas's leaf warbler is one of a group of similar tiny Asiatic species characterised by a yellow rump, a strong <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a> (stripe over the eye), double wing bars and a stripe on the crown of the head,<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> once separated as the genus <i>Abrornis</i> but currently retained in <i>Phylloscopus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-gray_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gray-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Baker2_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker2-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Pallas's leaf warbler was itself formerly treated as a complex of several <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>, with the <a href="/wiki/Subspecies#Nominotypical_subspecies_and_subspecies_autonyms" title="Subspecies">nominate form</a> <i>P. p. proregulus</i> breeding in northern Asia, and other subspecies breeding much further south at high altitudes in the mountains from the western <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> east to western China (<a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a> and north to <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hebei" title="Hebei">Hebei</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-will_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-will-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although nineteenth-century field naturalists such as <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_White" title="Gilbert White">Gilbert White</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Edwin_Brooks" title="William Edwin Brooks">William Edwin Brooks</a> had noted the importance of calls in separating often very similar-looking leaf warblers,<sup id="cite_ref-white_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-white-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-brooks_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brooks-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> for many years their views were not always accepted by the ornithological establishment.<sup id="cite_ref-alex_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alex-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> More recently, vocalisations have become increasingly important in taxonomy. In the case of the former subspecies of Pallas's leaf warbler, even though they differ only slightly in <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>, the southern forms are very distinctive vocally. Their songs and calls differ from those of the nominate race, and <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> analysis from 2006 has confirmed these forms to be sufficiently distinct that they are now treated as separate species, leaving Pallas's leaf warbler as a <a href="/wiki/Monotypic_taxon" title="Monotypic taxon">monotypic taxon</a>. The split species are:<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-alstrom2006_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alstrom2006-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-martens_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-martens-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Lemon-rumped_warbler" title="Lemon-rumped warbler">Lemon-rumped warbler</a> <i>Phylloscopus chloronotus</i>. Himalayas, southwest China. Three subspecies, <i>P. c. chloronotus</i>, <i>P. c. forresti</i> and <i>P. c. simlaensis</i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gansu_leaf_warbler" title="Gansu leaf warbler">Gansu leaf warbler</a> <i>Phylloscopus kansuensis</i>. Central western China, <a href="/wiki/Monotypic_taxon" title="Monotypic taxon">monotypic</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chinese_leaf_warbler" title="Chinese leaf warbler">Chinese leaf warbler</a> <i>Phylloscopus yunnanensis</i> (<a href="/wiki/Synonym_(taxonomy)" title="Synonym (taxonomy)">synonym</a> <i>P. sichuanensis</i>). Western China, monotypic.</li></ul>
<p>Of these, <i>Phylloscopus chloronotus forresti</i> is possibly also a separate species, but further analysis is required to confirm this. The breeding ranges of the Gansu leaf warbler and the Chinese leaf warbler overlap in southern Gansu, but the species are separated <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecologically</a>: the Gansu leaf warbler is found in taller forest habitats and the Chinese leaf warbler uses lower, often scrubby habitats.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> "Lemon-rumped warbler" was sometimes used as synonym for Pallas's leaf warbler before the species' split.<sup id="cite_ref-bakerpallas_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bakerpallas-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Pallas's leaf warbler appears to have diverged from the Chinese leaf warbler 4.1–5.5&#160;million years ago, and from its other former subspecies about 1.7–3.2&#160;million years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-martens_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-martens-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pallas%27s_leaf_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pallas%27s_Leaf-Warbler_-_great_rarity_in_Italy_S4E1751_(19261546342).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Pallas%27s_Leaf-Warbler_-_great_rarity_in_Italy_S4E1751_%2819261546342%29.jpg/220px-Pallas%27s_Leaf-Warbler_-_great_rarity_in_Italy_S4E1751_%2819261546342%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Pallas%27s_Leaf-Warbler_-_great_rarity_in_Italy_S4E1751_%2819261546342%29.jpg/330px-Pallas%27s_Leaf-Warbler_-_great_rarity_in_Italy_S4E1751_%2819261546342%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Pallas%27s_Leaf-Warbler_-_great_rarity_in_Italy_S4E1751_%2819261546342%29.jpg/440px-Pallas%27s_Leaf-Warbler_-_great_rarity_in_Italy_S4E1751_%2819261546342%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1140" data-file-height="760" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pallas%27s_Leaf-Warbler_-_great_rarity_in_Italy_S4E1751_(19261546342).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Showing head stripes</div></div></div>
<p>Pallas's leaf warbler is one of the smallest warblers, with a large head and short tail.<sup id="cite_ref-Svensson_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Svensson-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> At 9–10 centimetres (3.5–3.9&#160;in) long and 4–7 grams (0.14–0.25&#160;oz) in weight, it is slightly smaller than a <a href="/wiki/Yellow-browed_warbler" title="Yellow-browed warbler">yellow-browed warbler</a> and barely any larger than a goldcrest. It has greenish upperparts and white underparts, but is very striking, with prominent pale yellow double wingbars on the wing <a href="/wiki/Covert_feather" title="Covert feather">covert feathers</a>, bold yellow supercilia and central crown stripe, and a lemon-yellow rump.<sup id="cite_ref-Svensson_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Svensson-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The bill is blackish-brown with a yellowish tinge to the cutting edges and the base of the lower <a href="/wiki/Mandible" title="Mandible">mandible</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">iris</a> is brown, and the legs are brown with a green or greyish tinge.<sup id="cite_ref-bakerpallas_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bakerpallas-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Although the yellow rump is obvious when a bird is low in vegetation or hovering, it can otherwise be hard to see.<sup id="cite_ref-Svensson_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Svensson-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Asia, Pallas's leaf warbler can be distinguished from its former subspecies by its yellower head stripes, wingbars and throat as well as its different vocalisations.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Other yellow-rumped Asiatic warblers resemble Pallas's; <a href="/wiki/Buff-barred_warbler" title="Buff-barred warbler">buff-barred</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brooks%27s_leaf_warbler" title="Brooks&#39;s leaf warbler">Brooks's leaf warblers</a> are larger, much duller green above and less strongly marked, and their wing bars are buff and white respectively, not yellow. <a href="/wiki/Ashy-throated_warbler" title="Ashy-throated warbler">Ashy-throated warbler</a> has grey head markings, face and throat, and pale yellow underparts.<sup id="cite_ref-bakerpallas_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bakerpallas-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The sexes of Pallas's leaf warbler have similar plumage, but non-breeding birds are somewhat brighter green above and have broad, bright fringes to their <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">flight feathers</a>. Juveniles are like the adults, but have a brown tinge to the upperparts, greyish-white underparts and a duller supercilium. Adults have a complete post-breeding <a href="/wiki/Moulting" title="Moulting">moult</a> in August or September before migrating south. Juveniles and pre-breeding adults have a partial moult in March or April, replacing all the body plumage and some tail feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-bakerpallas_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bakerpallas-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The song of Pallas's leaf warbler is delivered from a concealed perch near the top of a tall tree.<sup id="cite_ref-brazil_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brazil-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> It is strong and prolonged, with a medley of whistles, <i>tirrit-tirrt-tirrit-terchee-choo-choo-chee-chee-chee</i> or similar, with some phrases reminiscent of a <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_canary" title="Atlantic canary">canary</a>, and interspersed <a href="/wiki/Trill_(music)" title="Trill (music)">trills</a>. It lasts 2–4&#160;seconds and may be heard in the winter quarters as well as from breeding birds.<sup id="cite_ref-simms_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-simms-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The call is a short, soft <i>dju-ee</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-bakerpallas_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bakerpallas-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In contrast, the former subspecies have quite different songs, with sustained rattles for several seconds, or sometimes minutes. Their calls are typically sharp and monosyllabic.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwlemon_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwlemon-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbwyunnan_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwyunnan-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pallas%27s_leaf_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pinega_Krasnaya_gorka.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Pinega_Krasnaya_gorka.JPG/220px-Pinega_Krasnaya_gorka.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Pinega_Krasnaya_gorka.JPG/330px-Pinega_Krasnaya_gorka.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Pinega_Krasnaya_gorka.JPG/440px-Pinega_Krasnaya_gorka.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1984" data-file-height="1488" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pinega_Krasnaya_gorka.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Siberian taiga woodland</div></div></div>
<p>Pallas's leaf warbler breeds in <a href="/wiki/Conifer" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifer">coniferous</a> <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a> forests including <a href="/wiki/Fir" title="Fir">fir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spruce" title="Spruce">spruce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Larch" title="Larch">larch</a>, or in mixed forest with <a href="/wiki/Rhododendron" title="Rhododendron">rhododendron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quercus_semecarpifolia" title="Quercus semecarpifolia">karsu oak</a> and a high percentage of conifers. In southern Russia, it was found breeding at up to 1,500–1,700 metres (4,900–5,600&#160;ft). In winter, it uses a wider range of habitats, including broadleaf forest and scrub as well as conifers, and can be found in river valleys down to 100 metres (330&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-bakerpallas_17-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bakerpallas-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Pallas's leaf warbler breeds in Siberia from the <a href="/wiki/Altai_Mountains" title="Altai Mountains">Altai Mountains</a> east to the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Okhotsk" title="Sea of Okhotsk">Sea of Okhotsk</a>, northern Mongolia, <a href="/wiki/Northeast_China" title="Northeast China">northeast China</a> and possibly <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>. It is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> and winters mainly in subtropical <a href="/wiki/South_China" title="South China">south China</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> and elsewhere in northeastern <a href="/wiki/Indochina" class="mw-redirect" title="Indochina">Indochina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> It is rare but annual in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-brazil_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brazil-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_movements">Other movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pallas%27s_leaf_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other movements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dalmatian_regulus_gould.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Dalmatian_regulus_gould.jpg/170px-Dalmatian_regulus_gould.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Dalmatian_regulus_gould.jpg/255px-Dalmatian_regulus_gould.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Dalmatian_regulus_gould.jpg/340px-Dalmatian_regulus_gould.jpg 2x" data-file-width="371" data-file-height="496" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dalmatian_regulus_gould.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>From <a href="/wiki/John_Gould" title="John Gould">John Gould</a>'s 1837 illustration of "Dalmatian Regulus"<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;b&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Pallas's leaf warbler now occurs regularly in Europe in autumn. The first known European record was shot in 1829 in <a href="/wiki/Dalmatia" title="Dalmatia">Dalmatia</a>, now Croatia, but <a href="/wiki/John_Gould" title="John Gould">John Gould</a>, who formally described it, did not realise the species had already been discovered in Asia, and named it as the "Dalmatian Regulus", <i>Regulus modestus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-gould_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gould-26">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> German <a href="/wiki/Ornithologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Ornithologist">ornithologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_G%C3%A4tke" title="Heinrich Gätke">Heinrich Gätke</a>, who moved to the then-British island of <a href="/wiki/Heligoland" title="Heligoland">Heligoland</a> in 1837 and stayed there for some fifty years, subsequently showed that several Asiatic species, including an occasional Pallas's leaf warbler, were regularly found there in autumn.<sup id="cite_ref-seeb1_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seeb1-27">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-seeb2_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seeb2-28">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the far west of Europe, the UK's first Pallas's leaf warbler was shot in 1896,<sup id="cite_ref-zoo_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zoo-29">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> although it was not until 1951 that the second was found.<sup id="cite_ref-bb1952_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb1952-30">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Thereafter, this species became increasingly common, ceasing to be a national rarity at the end of 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-bb1992_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb1992-31">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> In 2003, for example, 313 were recorded in Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-bb2006_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb2006-32">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Pallas's leaf warbler also occurs at least annually in Sweden, Finland and Denmark.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-33">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most Pallas's leaf warblers found in Europe are first-year birds,<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-33">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and several reasons for the large increase in numbers in autumn have been proposed. In the past, these warblers were widely considered to be <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrants</a> or <a href="/wiki/Reverse_migration_(birds)" title="Reverse migration (birds)">reverse migrants</a>, but were more recently thought to be undertaking a regular migration, taking advantage of the mild <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a> on the western fringes of Europe for overwintering.<sup id="cite_ref-gilroy_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gilroy-34">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> A flaw in that theory is that many birds should winter in Spain, particularly in the northwest, but Pallas's leaf warbler is rare in that country and tends to occur in the east. Spanish ornithologist Eduardo de Juana has therefore proposed that once the warblers reach northwest Europe, they then reorientate to a south easterly direction.<sup id="cite_ref-juana_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-juana-35">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Outside Europe, Pallas's leaf warbler has been recorded as a vagrant in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">north Africa</a> (Tunisia<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and Morocco), <a href="/wiki/Western_Asia" title="Western Asia">western Asia</a> (Israel, Turkey and Iran), <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">central Asia</a> (Uzbekistan and Tajikistan), <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">southeast Asia</a> (Bangladesh and Taiwan), and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pallas%27s_leaf_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pallas's leaf warbler is not wary but its unobtrusive <a href="/wiki/Arboreal" class="mw-redirect" title="Arboreal">arboreal</a> lifestyle makes it difficult to observe, particularly in thick foliage. It is constantly in motion, and often hovers briefly like a goldcrest, although more frequently,<sup id="cite_ref-simms_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-simms-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and may sometimes hang upside-down.<sup id="cite_ref-Svensson_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Svensson-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pallas%27s_leaf_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nesting is from June to July, with eggs laid from mid-June. The nest is built by the female in a conifer, usually next to the trunk at 0.5–10 metres (1.6–32.8&#160;ft) above ground, sometimes in a bush. It is a round or elliptical cup made from twigs, leaves and other vegetation and lined with finer material including feathers, hair or fine grasses.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_19-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Four to six blue-grey flecked white eggs are laid and incubated by the female.<sup id="cite_ref-Москва_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Москва-36">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> They hatch after 12–13 days, with the chicks <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledging</a> when 12–14 days old. They are fed mainly by the female while in the nest, but by both parents for about a week after fledging. In the south of the range, a pair may sometimes raise a second brood. The breeding territory in central Siberia is usually 3–5 hectares (7.4–12.4 acres), infrequently as much as 10 hectares (25 acres).<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_19-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Pallas's leaf warbler, as with other members of its genus, is a host of the <a href="/wiki/Oriental_cuckoo" title="Oriental cuckoo">oriental cuckoo</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasite</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cuckoo_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cuckoo-37">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The cuckoo's egg is similar in appearance, though larger, to those of the host species.<sup id="cite_ref-Москва_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Москва-36">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pallas%27s_leaf_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like its relatives, Pallas's leaf warbler is insectivorous, feeding on the adults, <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pupa" title="Pupa">pupa</a> of small insects including <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">aphids</a>; <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a> are also taken. Birds forage in bushes and trees, picking items from leaves or catching prey in short flights or while hovering. When not breeding, they may join <a href="/wiki/Mixed-species_foraging_flock" title="Mixed-species foraging flock">mixed-species foraging flocks</a> together with <a href="/wiki/Tit_(bird)" title="Tit (bird)">tits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goldcrest" title="Goldcrest">goldcrests</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">warblers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_19-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-simms_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-simms-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In Asia, accompanying species may also include <a href="/wiki/White-eye" title="White-eye">white-eyes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minivet" title="Minivet">minivets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Old_World_babbler" title="Old World babbler">babblers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Zhang_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zhang-38">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pallas%27s_leaf_warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Pallas's leaf warbler has a large range, and although global population trends have not been quantified, numbers are believed to be stable. This species does not approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> (that is, declining more than 30 percent in ten years or three generations). For these reasons, it is evaluated as of "<a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least concern</a>" by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">IUCN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Pallas's leaf warbler is widespread, common and locally abundant in Russia and northeast China. Breeding densities of up to 35–50 pairs/km<sup>2</sup> (90–130 pairs/mi<sup>2</sup>) have been recorded in southeast Russia, with only slightly lower figures in Siberia. It is locally common in parts of its wintering grounds in southeast Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoyo_19-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyo-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
