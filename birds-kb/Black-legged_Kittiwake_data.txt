<p>The <b>Black-legged Kittiwake</b> (<i>Rissa tridactyla</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> species in the <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a> family Laridae.</p>
<p>This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in his <i>Systema naturae</i> in 1758 as <i>Larus tridactylus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In North America, this species is known as the <b>Black-legged Kittiwake</b> to differentiate it from the <a href="/wiki/Red-legged_Kittiwake" title="Red-legged Kittiwake">Red-legged Kittiwake</a>, but in Europe, where it is the only member of the genus, it is often known just as <b>Kittiwake</b>.</p>
<p>The adults is 37–41 cm (15–16 in) in length with a wingspan of 91–105 cm (36–41 in) and a body mass of 305–525 g (10.8–18.5 oz).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> It has a white head and body, grey back, grey wings tipped solid black, and have black legs and a yellow bill. Occasional individuals have pinky-grey to reddish legs, inviting confusion with Red-legged Kittiwake. In winter, this species acquires a dark grey smudge behind the eye and a grey hind-neck collar. The name is derived from its call, a shrill 'kittee-wa-aaake, kitte-wa-aaake'.</p>
<p>It is a coastal breeding bird around the north Pacific and north Atlantic oceans, found most commonly in North America and Europe. It breeds in large colonies on cliffs and is very noisy on the breeding ground. Cliff nesting for gulls occurs only in the <i>Rissa</i> species, and the Kittiwake is capable of utilizing the very sheerest of vertical cliffs, as is evident in their nesting sites on <a href="/wiki/Staple_Island" title="Staple Island">Staple Island</a> in the outer <a href="/wiki/Farne_Islands" title="Farne Islands">Farne Islands</a> (Hogan, 2005). One to two buff spotted eggs are laid in the nest lined with moss or seaweed. The downy young of Kittiwakes are white, since they have no need of camouflage from predators, and do not wander from the nest like <i><a href="/wiki/Larus" title="Larus">Larus</a></i> gulls for obvious safety reasons.</p>
<p>At fledging, the juveniles differ from the adults in having a black 'W' band across the length of the wings and whiter secondary and primary feathers behind the black 'W', a black hind-neck collar and a black terminal band on the tail. The old fisherman's name of "tarrock" for juvenile Kittiwakes is still occasionally used.</p>
<p>They are fish feeders, and are more <a href="/wiki/Pelagic" title="Pelagic" class="mw-redirect">pelagic</a> than <i>Larus</i> gulls outside the breeding season. They do not scavenge at tips like some other gull species.</p>
<p>There are two races of Black-legged Kittiwake:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Rissa tridactyla tridactyla</i> in the North Atlantic Ocean, which is unique among the Laridae in having only a very small or even no hind toe.</li>
<li><i>Rissa tridactyla pollicaris</i> in the North Pacific Ocean, which (as the name <i>pollex</i>, thumb, suggests) has a normally developed hind toe.</li>
</ul>
