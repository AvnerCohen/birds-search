<p>The <b>Basra reed warbler</b> (<i>Acrocephalus griseldis</i>) is a "<a href="/wiki/Warbler" title="Warbler">warbler</a>" of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Acrocephalus_(bird)" title="Acrocephalus (bird)">Acrocephalus</a></i>. It is an endemic breeder in East and southern <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> and <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a><a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.birdlife.org/news/news/2007/08/basra_israel.html">[1]</a> in extensive beds of <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phragmites" title="Phragmites">reeds</a>. It is easily mistaken for the <a href="/wiki/Great_reed_warbler" title="Great reed warbler">great reed warbler</a> but is a bit smaller, has whiter under parts and has a narrower, longer and more pointed bill. It winters in <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a>. It is a very rare vagrant in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. The call is a gruff 'chaar', deeper than a <a href="/wiki/Reed_warbler" class="mw-redirect" title="Reed warbler">reed warbler</a>'s.
</p><p>It is found in aquatic vegetation in or around shallow, fresh or brackish water, still or flowing, mainly in dense reedbeds. It is found in thickets and bushland when migrating or wintering.
</p><p>In 2007, the species was discovered as a breeding bird in northern Israel.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
