<p>The <b>Arabian Babbler</b> (<i>Turdoides squamiceps</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> belonging to the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Turdoides" title="Turdoides">Turdoides</a></i>, a genus of <a href="/wiki/Old_World_babbler" title="Old World babbler">Old World babblers</a>. It is a communally nesting resident bird of arid scrub in the Middle East which lives together in relatively stable groups with strict orders of rank.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabian_Babbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>It is one of the 26 species of the genus <i>Turdoides</i> that constitutes together with several other genera the family Timaliidae (order Passeriformes, Cramp &amp; Perrins 1993). The Timaliidae occur in the <a href="/wiki/Afrotropic_ecozone" title="Afrotropic ecozone">Ethiopian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indomalaya_ecozone" title="Indomalaya ecozone">Oriental</a> <a href="/wiki/Ecozone" title="Ecozone" class="mw-redirect">ecozones</a>, but a few species of the genus <i>Turdoides</i> have penetrated into the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" title="Palearctic" class="mw-redirect">Palearctic</a> zone north of the tropics where they live in arid areas of North and East Africa, India and the Middle East (Zahavi 1990).</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabian_Babbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>It is 26–29&#160;cm long with a wingspan of 31-33.5&#160;cm and a weight of 64-83&#160;grams. It has a fairly long curved bill, a long tail, rounded wings and strong legs and feet. The <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> is grey-brown above, paler below. There are dark streaks on the back and the throat is whitish. It has a variety of calls including whistles, trills and chattering.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabian_Babbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>The Arabian Babbler prefers to settle along dry river beds with few trees and bushes. It is found in eastern, southern and western <a href="/wiki/Arabia" title="Arabia" class="mw-redirect">Arabia</a>, occurring in the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> but absent from the central and north-eastern parts of the peninsula. Its range extends north to <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and eastern <a href="/wiki/Sinai" title="Sinai" class="mw-redirect">Sinai</a>. It inhabits arid <a href="/wiki/Scrubland" title="Scrubland" class="mw-redirect">scrubland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a>, occurring up to 2800 metres above sea-level in Yemen.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabian_Babbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Arabian_Babbler.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Arabian_Babbler.jpg/220px-Arabian_Babbler.jpg" width="220" height="138" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Arabian_Babbler.jpg/330px-Arabian_Babbler.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Arabian_Babbler.jpg/440px-Arabian_Babbler.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Arabian_Babbler.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Photographed at Al Ain, Abu Dhabi, UAE</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Babblers dance and take baths together, offer each other gifts, clean themselves, and sometimes enter into conflict with each other for the privilege of helping another babbler. They may also feed their counterparts. This peculiar behaviour made them a privileged example for <a href="/wiki/Ethology" title="Ethology">ethological</a> theories concerning <a href="/wiki/Altruism_in_animals" title="Altruism in animals">altruism among animals</a>.</p>
<p>Starting in the 1970s, <a href="/wiki/Amotz_Zahavi" title="Amotz Zahavi">Amotz Zahavi</a> observed the babbler at length, giving rise to his theory of signal and its correlative, the <a href="/wiki/Handicap_principle" title="Handicap principle">handicap principle</a>. Although babblers were considered particularly altruistic animals, Zahavi reinterpreted their behaviours according to his theory. Thus, Zahavi (1974) theorized that chick feeding by Arabian Babbler helpers acts as a signal by the helper to gain social prestige within the group.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabian_Babbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Group structure and breeding biology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Group_structure_and_breeding_biology">Group structure and breeding biology</span></h3>
<p>Arabian Babblers are cooperative breeders that live in groups and defend territories year round. Group size varies from two to 10 individuals. Their territories vary with the group size as well the presence of neighboring groups. Groups consist of a reproductive pair and other non-breeding members who may or may not be direct offspring or close relatives. Helpers usually do not reproduce (Lundy et al. 1998), but they participate in rearing the offspring, that is, in providing food, as well as other parental behavior like incubation, territory defense and defense against predators.</p>
<p>Groups are labelled according to the relatedness of helpers to the reproductive pair:</p>
<ol>
<li>Simple group: where all helpers are direct offspring of the breeding pairs.</li>
<li>Polyandrous group: where helpers include potential male breeder</li>
<li>Polygynous group: where helpers include potential female breeders</li>
<li>Complex group: Have non-related helpers of both sexes.</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabian_Babbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nest">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Nest">Nest</span></h3>
<p>Babblers build open cup-shaped nests in the dense part of the tree or bush. Their breeding period starts generally from February and varies up to July and is highly dependent on the seasonal rainfall of the region which in turn regulates the food availability. They copulate throughout the year. Eggs are laid usually from February to July. Most clutches contain four eggs, laid on consecutive days. Incubation usually starts after laying the last egg and continues for 14 days until the chicks hatch (Ostreiher 1997, 2001). The nestlings fledge about 14 days after hatching (Ostreiher 1997).</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabian_Babbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span></h3>
<p>The diet of the babblers includes a variety of invertebrates (mostly <a href="/wiki/Arthropods" title="Arthropods" class="mw-redirect">arthropods</a>), small vertebrates (lizards, <a href="/wiki/Gecko" title="Gecko">geckos</a>, snakes) and plant material, like nectar, flowers, berries, leaves and seeds (Zahavi 1990; Cramp &amp; Perrins 1993).</p>
