<p>The <b>Northern Bald Ibis</b>, <b>Hermit Ibis</b>, or <b>Waldrapp</b> (<i>Geronticus eremita</i>) is a migratory bird found in barren, semi-desert or rocky habitats, often close to running water. This 70–80&#160;cm (28–31&#160;in) glossy black <a href="/wiki/Ibis" title="Ibis">ibis</a>, which, unlike other members of the ibis family, is non-wading, has an unfeathered red face and head, and a long, curved red bill. It breeds colonially on coastal or mountain cliff ledges, where it typically lays 2–3 eggs in a stick nest, and feeds on lizards, insects, and other small animals.</p>
<p>The Northern Bald Ibis was once widespread across the Middle East, northern Africa, southern and central Europe, with a fossil record dating back at least 1.8&#160;million years. It disappeared from Europe over 300 years ago, and is now considered <a href="/wiki/Critically_Endangered" title="Critically Endangered">critically endangered</a>. There are believed to be about 500 wild birds remaining in southern <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, and fewer than 10 in <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, where it was rediscovered in 2002. To combat this ebb in numbers, recent <a href="/wiki/Reintroduction" title="Reintroduction">reintroduction</a> programs have been instituted internationally, with a semi-wild breeding colony in Turkey, as well as sites in Austria, Spain, and northern <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>.</p>
<p>The reasons for the species' long-term decline are unclear, but hunting, loss of foraging habitat, and pesticide poisoning have been implicated in the rapid loss of colonies in recent decades.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Bald_Ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The ibises are gregarious, long-legged wading birds with long down-curved bills. Along with the <a href="/wiki/Spoonbill" title="Spoonbill">spoonbills</a> they form one subfamily within the <a href="/wiki/Threskiornithidae" title="Threskiornithidae">Threskiornithidae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The Northern Bald Ibis' closest relative, and the only other member of the genus, is the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Bald_Ibis" title="Southern Bald Ibis">Southern Bald Ibis</a>, <i>G. calvus</i>, of southern Africa. The two <i>Geronticus</i> species differ from other ibises in that they have unfeathered faces and heads, breed on cliffs rather than in trees, and prefer arid habitats to the wetlands used by their relatives.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SASOL_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SASOL-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nbibis0371crop.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Nbibis0371crop.jpg/220px-Nbibis0371crop.jpg" width="220" height="177" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Nbibis0371crop.jpg/330px-Nbibis0371crop.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Nbibis0371crop.jpg/440px-Nbibis0371crop.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nbibis0371crop.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Gessner's 1555 <a href="/wiki/Woodcut" title="Woodcut">woodcut</a> of the Northern Bald Ibis<sup id="cite_ref-woodcut_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-woodcut-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>

<p>The Northern Bald Ibis was described and illustrated by Swiss naturalist <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Gessner" title="Conrad Gessner">Conrad Gessner</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Historiae_animalium_(Gesner)" title="Historiae animalium (Gesner)">Historiae animalium</a></i> in 1555,<sup id="cite_ref-Gesner_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gesner-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Yearbook09_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yearbook09-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> and given the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Upupa" title="Upupa" class="mw-redirect">Upupa</a> eremita</i> by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his 1758 <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-linn_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linn-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> It was moved to its current genus by the German <a href="/wiki/Herpetologist" title="Herpetologist" class="mw-redirect">herpetologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Johann_Georg_Wagler" title="Johann Georg Wagler">Johann Georg Wagler</a> in 1832.<sup id="cite_ref-wagler_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wagler-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> This species has an interesting history of description, oblivion and rediscovery. <sup id="cite_ref-history_confusion_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history_confusion-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The species probably split into two distinct populations at least 400 years ago and, since then, the two populations have been diverging <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphologically</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecologically</a>, and genetically;<sup id="cite_ref-serraabd_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-serraabd-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> nevertheless, the Turkish and Moroccan populations of this ibis are not currently classed as separate subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> One consistent difference between the eastern and western birds is a single mutation in the <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome b</a> <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">gene</a> of their <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pegoraro_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pegoraro-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Fossils of the Northern Bald Ibis have been found at a <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> (c. 10,000 years ago) site in southern France,<sup id="cite_ref-cecile_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cecile-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> in middle <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> (c. 900,000 ago) strata in Sicily,<sup id="cite_ref-boev2_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boev2-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a>-Pleistocene boundary (c. 1.8&#160;million years ago) deposits on the Mediterranean coast of Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-marco_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marco-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> What appears to be an ancestral form, <i>Geronticus balcanicus</i>, was found in the late Pliocene of Bulgaria,<sup id="cite_ref-Boev_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boev-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> further illustrating the early widespread presence of this genus in Europe, and suggesting that <i>Geronticus eremita</i> may have originated in southeastern Europe or the Middle East.<sup id="cite_ref-boev2_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boev2-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The genus name, <i>Geronticus</i>, is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">γέρων</span>, meaning <i>old man</i> and refers to the bald head of the aged.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Eremita</i> is <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">Late Latin</a> for <i>hermit</i>, from the Greek <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἐρημία</span>, meaning <i>desert</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> and refers to the arid habitats inhabited by this species. The alternative common name <i>Waldrapp</i> is German for <i>forest crow</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> the equivalent of the Latin <i>Corvo sylvatico</i> of Gesner,<sup id="cite_ref-Gesner_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gesner-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> adapted as <i>Corvus sylvaticus</i> by Linnaeus.<sup id="cite_ref-linn_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linn-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Bald_Ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Geronticus_eremita_close_up_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Geronticus_eremita_close_up_1.jpg/220px-Geronticus_eremita_close_up_1.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Geronticus_eremita_close_up_1.jpg/330px-Geronticus_eremita_close_up_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Geronticus_eremita_close_up_1.jpg/440px-Geronticus_eremita_close_up_1.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Geronticus_eremita_close_up_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Close-up of an adult's head</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Northern Bald Ibis is a large, glossy black bird, 70–80&#160;cm (28–31&#160;in) long with a 125–135&#160;cm (49–53&#160;in) wingspan and an average weight of 1.0–1.3&#160;kg (35–46&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The plumage is black, with bronze-green and violet iridescence, and there is a wispy ruff on the bird's hind neck. The face and head are dull red and unfeathered, and the long, curved bill and the legs are red.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> In flight, this bird has powerful, shallow, and flexible wing beats. It gives guttural <i>hrump</i> and high, hoarse <i>hyoh</i> calls at its breeding colonies, but is otherwise silent.<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The sexes are similar in plumage, although males are generally larger than females,<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and, as with other ibises that breed in colonies, have longer <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bills</a>. The longer-billed males are more successful in attracting a mate.<sup id="cite_ref-Babbitt_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Babbitt-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The downy chick has uniformly pale brown plumage, and the fledged juvenile resembles the adult except that it has a dark head, light grey legs, and a pale bill. The unfeathered areas of the young bird's head and neck gradually become red as it matures.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Moroccan birds have a significantly longer bill than Turkish birds of the same sex.<sup id="cite_ref-Siegfried_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Siegfried-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>If the eastern and western populations are considered to be separable subspecies, it is unclear which should be considered to be the <a href="/wiki/Subspecies#Nomenclature" title="Subspecies">nominate (first-named) form</a>, since the first description of this species was based on a now-extinct population from Switzerland which is of unknown race.<sup id="cite_ref-Gesner_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gesner-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Northern Bald Ibis is readily distinguished from its close relative, the Southern Bald Ibis of Southern Africa, by the southern species' whitish face.<sup id="cite_ref-SASOL_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SASOL-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The Northern Bald can also be confused with the similarly dark-plumaged <a href="/wiki/Glossy_Ibis" title="Glossy Ibis">Glossy Ibis</a>, which overlaps its range, but it is larger and stockier than that species. In flight, when the bill and face colouration may not be visible, the Bald Ibis' less rounded wings and shorter neck give it a different profile from Glossy Ibis,<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and its relatively short legs mean that its feet do not project beyond the tail, unlike those of the Glossy Ibis.<sup id="cite_ref-Mullarney_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullarney-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Bald_Ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitat and range">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_range">Habitat and range</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nbibis013.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Nbibis013.jpg/220px-Nbibis013.jpg" width="220" height="202" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Nbibis013.jpg/330px-Nbibis013.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9a/Nbibis013.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nbibis013.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Part of a flock in the Souss Massa stronghold</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Unlike other ibises, which nest in trees and feed in wetlands, the Northern Bald Ibis breeds on undisturbed cliff ledges, and forages for food in irregularly cultivated, grazed dry areas such as semi-arid <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppes</a>, and fallow fields. The close proximity of adequate steppe feeding areas to breeding cliffs is an important habitat requirement.<sup id="cite_ref-rspb2_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb2-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> The Northern Bald Ibis was once widespread across the Middle East, northern Africa, and southern and central Europe;<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> it bred along the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhone" title="Rhone">Rhone</a> Rivers, and in the mountains of Spain, Italy, Germany, Austria and Switzerland (Gessner's original description was of a Swiss bird),<sup id="cite_ref-Gesner2_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gesner2-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> and most probably also in the Upper <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Sea" title="Adriatic Sea">Adriatic</a> region.<sup id="cite_ref-PT2001_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PT2001-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> It used castle battlements as well as cliff ledges for nesting before vanishing from Europe at least three centuries ago.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> It is also extinct over most of its former range, and now almost the entirety of the wild breeding population of just over 500 birds is in Morocco, at <a href="/wiki/Souss-Massa_National_Park" title="Souss-Massa National Park">Souss-Massa National Park</a>, where there are three documented colonies, and near the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Oued_Tamri" title="Oued Tamri">Oued Tamri</a> (north of <a href="/wiki/Agadir" title="Agadir">Agadir</a>), where there is a single colony containing almost half the Moroccan breeding population. There is some movement of birds between these two sites.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Religious traditions helped this species to survive in one Turkish colony long after the species had disappeared from Europe, since it was believed that the ibis <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrated</a> each year to guide <a href="/wiki/Hajj" title="Hajj">Hajj</a> pilgrims to <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a>. The ibis was protected by its religious significance, and a festival was held annually to celebrate its return north.<sup id="cite_ref-aewa6_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aewa6-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> The Turkish ibis population was centred near the small town of <a href="/wiki/Birecik" title="Birecik">Birecik</a> in the south east of the country, and during the first half of the twentieth century, the Birecik colony maintained a relatively stable population of about 500 breeding pairs, reaching an estimated total population of about 3,000 around 1930. By the 1970s, numbers had drastically declined and a captive breeding program was initiated in 1977 with one adult pair and nine chicks taken from the wild. This program largely failed to revert the decline; there were 400 birds in 1982, five pairs in 1986, and seven pairs in 1987. Only three birds returned from their wintering grounds in 1989, and just one in 1990. The returning birds died before they could reproduce, thus rendering the species extinct in the wild in Turkey as of 1992. Once the wild Turkish population became non-viable, the colony was maintained as a flock which was free-flying for most of the year but caged in autumn to prevent migration.<sup id="cite_ref-kelaynak_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kelaynak-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ibismap_2003.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Ibismap_2003.png/220px-Ibismap_2003.png" width="220" height="199" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Ibismap_2003.png/330px-Ibismap_2003.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Ibismap_2003.png/440px-Ibismap_2003.png 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ibismap_2003.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<span style="color:red;"><b>—</b></span>Tagged Syrian breeders migrated south through Yemen in 2006 and returned via Eritrea.<sup id="cite_ref-rspb_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup><br />
<span style="color:green;"><b>—</b></span>Birds from Birecik visited the Syrian colony at Palmyra.<sup id="cite_ref-dead_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dead-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After the demise of the migratory Turkish colony, the Northern Bald Ibis was known to survive in the wild only at the Moroccan sites, although occasional sightings of birds in Yemen, Eritrea, Saudi Arabia, and Israel during the 1980s and 1990s suggested that there was still a colony somewhere in the Middle East.<sup id="cite_ref-Schulz_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schulz-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Intensive field surveys in spring 2002, based on the knowledge of <a href="/wiki/Bedouin" title="Bedouin">Bedouin</a> nomads and local hunters, revealed that the species had never become completely extinct on the Syrian desert steppes. Following systematic searches, 15 old nesting sites were found, one, near <a href="/wiki/Palmyra" title="Palmyra">Palmyra</a>, was still hosting an active breeding colony of seven individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-serraabd_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-serraabd-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Serra2003_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Serra2003-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-murdoch_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-murdoch-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> Although the ibis had been declared extinct in Syria more than 70 years earlier,<sup id="cite_ref-safriel_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-safriel-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> the bird appears to have been relatively common in the desert areas until 20 years ago, when a combination of <a href="/wiki/Overexploitation" title="Overexploitation">overexploitation</a> of its range lands and increasing hunting pressures initiated a dramatic decline.<sup id="cite_ref-Serra2003_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Serra2003-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-murdoch_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-murdoch-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Moroccan breeding birds are resident, dispersing along the coast after the nesting season. It has been suggested that coastal fog provides extra moisture for this population, and enables the ibises to remain year-round.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> In the rest of its former range, away from the Moroccan coastal locations, the Northern Bald Ibis migrated south for the winter, and formerly occurred as a vagrant to Spain, Iraq, Egypt, the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, and Cape Verde.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Satellite tagging of 13 Syrian birds in 2006 showed that the three adults in the group, plus a fourth untagged adult, wintered together from February to July in the highlands of Ethiopia, where the species had not been recorded for nearly 30 years. They travelled south on the eastern side of the Red Sea via Saudi Arabia and Yemen, and returned north through Sudan and Eritrea.<sup id="cite_ref-rspb_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-winter_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-winter-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Bald_Ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Bald_Ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Geronticus_eremita_Waldrapp.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Geronticus_eremita_Waldrapp.jpg/220px-Geronticus_eremita_Waldrapp.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Geronticus_eremita_Waldrapp.jpg/330px-Geronticus_eremita_Waldrapp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Geronticus_eremita_Waldrapp.jpg/440px-Geronticus_eremita_Waldrapp.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Geronticus_eremita_Waldrapp.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A young bird</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Northern Bald Ibis breeds in loosely spaced colonies, nesting on cliff ledges or amongst boulders on steep slopes, usually on the coast or near a river.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Volunteer climbers have created extra ledge spaces in the <a href="/wiki/Souss-Massa_National_Park" title="Souss-Massa National Park">Souss-Massa</a> colonies to ensure that breeding population is not limited by the availability of nest ledges,<sup id="cite_ref-rspb2_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb2-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> and artificial <a href="/wiki/Nest_box" title="Nest box">nest boxes</a> are used in the managed colony at <a href="/wiki/Birecik" title="Birecik">Birecik</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Birecik_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birecik-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> In the past, the birds also nested in buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This ibis starts breeding at 3–5 years of age,<sup id="cite_ref-bci_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bci-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> and pairs for life.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The male chooses a nest site, cleans it, and then advertises for a female by waving his crest and giving low rumbling calls. Once the birds have paired, the bond is reinforced through bowing displays and mutual <a href="/wiki/Personal_grooming" title="Personal grooming">preening</a>. The nest is a loose construction of twigs lined with grass or straw.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>G. eremita</i> normally lays 2–4 rough-surfaced eggs, which weigh an average of 50.16&#160;g (1.8&#160;oz),<sup id="cite_ref-egg_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-egg-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> and are initially blue-white with brown spots, becoming brown during <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" title="Avian incubation" class="mw-redirect">incubation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> An egg in the collection of the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a> was marked more thickly at the broad end, with "spots and very small blotches of yellowish brown and pale rufous". It was 0.93&#160;in long and 0.68&#160;in&#160;wide (2.37&#160;×&#160;1.73&#160;cm).<sup id="cite_ref-BrMusCat_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BrMusCat-38"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> The clutch is incubated for 24–25 days to hatching, the chicks <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> in another 40–50 days,<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and the first flight takes place at about two months.<sup id="cite_ref-beaky_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beaky-39"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> Both parents incubate and feed the chicks.<sup id="cite_ref-aewa_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aewa-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Northern Bald Ibis lives in captivity for an average of 20–25 years (oldest recorded male 37 years, oldest recorded female 30 years).<sup id="cite_ref-eep_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eep-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> The average age in the wild has been estimated as 10–15 years.<sup id="cite_ref-bci_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bci-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<div class="thumb tright">

<p>This gregarious species <a href="/wiki/Commuting" title="Commuting">commutes</a> in flocks from the cliff breeding sites or winter roosts to its feeding areas, flying in a <b>V</b>-formation. The flocks may contain up to 100 birds in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_2-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> During the breeding season, the ibises regularly forage up to 15&#160;km (10&#160;mi) from the colony, and, although <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a> not in current cultivation is preferred for feeding, they will also use <a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">fallow ground</a>, and occasionally even actively cultivated fields.<sup id="cite_ref-rspb2_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb2-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Northern Bald Ibis consumes a very wide variety of mainly animal food; <a href="/wiki/Faeces" title="Faeces" class="mw-redirect">faecal</a> analysis of the Moroccan breeding population has shown that lizards and <a href="/wiki/Darkling_beetle" title="Darkling beetle">tenebrionid beetles</a> predominate in the diet,<sup id="cite_ref-rspb2_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb2-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> although small mammals, ground-nesting birds, and invertebrates such as snails, scorpions, spiders, and caterpillars are also taken.<sup id="cite_ref-aewa_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aewa-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> Males will sometimes "scrounge" food from females.<sup id="cite_ref-waza5_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waza5-42"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> As the flock moves across the ground, the ibis uses its long bill to feel for food items in the loose, sandy soil. Since this bird hunts mainly by probing, a soft surface seems to be vital, and it is important that any vegetation is sparse, and not more than 15–20&#160;cm (6–8&#160;in) high.<sup id="cite_ref-aewa_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aewa-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Bald_Ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Birecikcolonynests.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Birecikcolonynests.JPG/220px-Birecikcolonynests.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Birecikcolonynests.JPG/330px-Birecikcolonynests.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Birecikcolonynests.JPG/440px-Birecikcolonynests.JPG 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Birecikcolonynests.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Artificial nests at the <a href="/wiki/Birecik" title="Birecik">Birecik</a> colony</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Although the Northern Bald Ibis was long extinct in Europe, many colonies in Morocco and Algeria survived until the early twentieth century, when they began to decline more rapidly, the last colony in Algeria disappearing in the late 1980s. In Morocco there were about 38 colonies in 1940 and 15 in 1975, but the last migratory populations in the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas Mountains</a> had vanished by 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-aewa_40-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aewa-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> The species is now officially <a href="/wiki/Critically_Endangered" title="Critically Endangered">critically endangered</a> according to the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">IUCN</a> scale, with an estimated population in 2008 of around 500 in the wild and over 1,000 in captivity.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-aewa_40-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aewa-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> The Northern Bald Ibis is one of the key species to which the draft <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies, and it has a detailed, internationally agreed conservation action plan under the agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-aewa_40-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aewa-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> As a species that is threatened with extinction, it is listed on Appendix 1 of <a href="/wiki/CITES" title="CITES">CITES</a> (the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora), which means that trade in wild-caught birds is illegal, and may be licensed only in exceptional circumstances.<sup id="cite_ref-citesapp_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-citesapp-43"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-citestext_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-citestext-44"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Northern Bald Ibis has declined for several centuries, at least partly as a consequence of unidentified natural causes.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The more rapid decline in the past hundred years, with a loss of 98% of the population between 1900 and 2002,<sup id="cite_ref-serraabd_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-serraabd-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> is the result of a combination of factors. These include significant human persecution, especially hunting, and also the loss of steppe and non-intensive agricultural areas (particularly in Morocco), pesticide poisoning, disturbance, and dam construction.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The discovery in Jordan of three dead adults from the Turkish colony confirmed that the overuse of pesticides is still a cause of death on migration. These birds were tracked by satellite after leaving Birecik; they stopped off briefly at the Syrian colony, and were later found dead in the Jordanian desert. Although the corpses were close to electricity pylons, the cause of death was poison, probably laid by chicken farmers to kill rodents.<sup id="cite_ref-dead_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dead-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Bald_Ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Wild populations">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Wild_populations">Wild populations</span></h3>
<p>Monitoring of Moroccan wild population is guaranteed by <a href="/wiki/BirdLife_International" title="BirdLife International">BirdLife International</a> partners, especially by <a href="/wiki/RSPB" title="RSPB" class="mw-redirect">RSPB</a> and <a href="/wiki/SEO/BirdLife" title="SEO/BirdLife" class="mw-redirect">SEO/BirdLife</a> in cooperation with <a href="/wiki/Souss-Massa_National_Park" title="Souss-Massa National Park">Souss-Massa National Park</a> administration.<sup id="cite_ref-seo-marruecos_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seo-marruecos-45"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> For the first time in the species' recorded history, there is now evidence of population growth in the wild, and the population in Morocco increased to 100 breeding pairs in the decade prior to 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-bci_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bci-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Simple site and species protection has facilitated this growth. Quantitative assessments of the importance of sites for breeding, roosting, and foraging have guided actions to prevent disturbance and the loss of key areas to mass tourism development. Wardening by members of the local community has reduced human intrusion and increased the perceived value of the birds. The provision of drinking water and the removal and deterrence of predators and competitors enhances breeding prospects, and monitoring has confirmed that <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a> and two-year <a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">fallows</a> are key feeding habitats.<sup id="cite_ref-bci_36-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bci-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Maintaining such non-intensive land uses in the future may present major management challenges, and the recovery in the <a href="/wiki/Souss-Massa_National_Park" title="Souss-Massa National Park">Souss-Massa</a> region remains precarious because the population is concentrated in just a few places. However, it could provide opportunities for natural extension of the range to formerly occupied sites further north in Morocco.<sup id="cite_ref-bci_36-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bci-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Waldrapp_Ibis_is_feeding_a_chick.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Waldrapp_Ibis_is_feeding_a_chick.jpg/170px-Waldrapp_Ibis_is_feeding_a_chick.jpg" width="170" height="243" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Waldrapp_Ibis_is_feeding_a_chick.jpg/255px-Waldrapp_Ibis_is_feeding_a_chick.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Waldrapp_Ibis_is_feeding_a_chick.jpg/340px-Waldrapp_Ibis_is_feeding_a_chick.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Waldrapp_Ibis_is_feeding_a_chick.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Adult feeding a chick</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The main cause of breeding failure at the Souss-Massa National Park is the loss of eggs to predators,<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> especially the <a href="/wiki/Common_Raven" title="Common Raven">Common Raven</a> which nest monitoring has shown to have had a serious impact at one sub-colony.<sup id="cite_ref-rspb2_22-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb2-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> The effects of predators on adult birds have not been studied, but the very similar Southern Bald Ibis, <i>Geronticus calvus</i>, is hunted by large <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptors</a>, particularly those that share its breeding cliffs.<sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-46"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> There is evidence of chick starvation in some years, but the main threats to breeding birds are human disturbance and the loss of feeding habitat.<sup id="cite_ref-rspb2_22-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb2-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> There was a dramatic mortality incident at the Moroccan colonies in May 1996, when 40 adults died or disappeared over a period of nine days. Although analyses have not identified the cause, an obscure virus, a toxin, or <a href="/wiki/Botulism" title="Botulism">botulism</a> are thought to be the most likely causes of the deaths.<sup id="cite_ref-rspb2_22-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rspb2-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Syrian Ministry of Agriculture and Agrarian Reform has now put protection measures in place to protect the tiny breeding population in that country,<sup id="cite_ref-murdoch_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-murdoch-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> and Yemen and Ethiopia, which provide migration stop-offs and wintering areas, have a good record for conservation. However, young birds do not winter nor return with the adults and their wintering grounds are still unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-winter_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-winter-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> Although the wild Syrian population is very small, the discovery that semi-wild Turkish birds on a migration trial visited the <a href="/wiki/Palmyra" title="Palmyra">Palmyra</a> colony has led to hopes that some migrants may join the Syrian breeding population and boost the numbers in that country.<sup id="cite_ref-dead_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dead-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>An intensive community-based protection program in <a href="/wiki/Palmyra" title="Palmyra">Palmyra</a> under an FAO/Italian Cooperation project during 2002–2004 recorded an average breeding performance that was higher than in Turkey and in Morocco. During period 2005–2010 the protection program, now under Birdlife Middle East management had a much lower effort and lack of constant external scientific and technical assistance to local authorities. This resulted in a very variable breeding performance; two fatal and predictable breeding failures occurred in 2005 and 2008. During 2004–2007, due to prior high breeding performance, four subadults returned to the colony and three recruited successfully, compensating the natural mortality of adults. The unsustainably high mortality of young ibises outside the breeding grounds (an estimated 85%) and the breeding failures in 2009 and 2010 (due to unknown causes) meant that the colony reached its lowest size ever in 2010, only one pair, and three adults. Extinction at this point seems unavoidable.<sup id="cite_ref-serra_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-serra-47"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Bald_Ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Turkey">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Turkey">Turkey</span></h3>
<p>With the loss of the genuinely wild Turkish population, the Ministry of Environment and Forestry's Directorate of Natural Preservation and National Parks established a new semi-wild colony at Birecik. This was heavily managed, with birds taken into captivity after the breeding season to prevent migration.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The program was successful, with numbers at 91 as of July 2006. The intention is to allow the birds to migrate once the population reaches a stable 100 pairs, excluding young.<sup id="cite_ref-kelaynak_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kelaynak-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The birds are released in late January or early February to breed outside the cages on ledges and, mainly, in the nest boxes in the breeding station compound. The ibises are free flying and forage around the Birecik area in forest nurseries, agricultural fields, and along the <a href="/wiki/Euphrates" title="Euphrates">Euphrates</a>, but supplementary food is also provided. Following the end of the breeding season, the birds are taken into cages in late July or early August to prevent migration.<sup id="cite_ref-kelaynak_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kelaynak-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> A trial migration using tagged birds confirmed the risks presented to travelling birds by pesticides.<sup id="cite_ref-dead_29-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dead-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Bald_Ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reintroductions">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Reintroductions">Reintroductions</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Waldrapp-Schoenbrunn01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Waldrapp-Schoenbrunn01.jpg/220px-Waldrapp-Schoenbrunn01.jpg" width="220" height="257" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Waldrapp-Schoenbrunn01.jpg/330px-Waldrapp-Schoenbrunn01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Waldrapp-Schoenbrunn01.jpg/440px-Waldrapp-Schoenbrunn01.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Waldrapp-Schoenbrunn01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Tiergarten_Sch%C3%B6nbrunn" title="Tiergarten Schönbrunn">Tiergarten Schönbrunn</a> in Vienna is running a successful breeding programme for reintroduction</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The guidelines for the conservation and reintroduction of the Northern Bald Ibis were established in 2003 at an International Advisory Group for Northern Bald Ibis (IAGNBI) conference in <a href="/wiki/Innsbruck" title="Innsbruck">Innsbruck</a> at the Alpenzoo, which maintains the European <a href="/wiki/Breed_registry" title="Breed registry">studbook</a> for the Northern Bald Ibis.<sup id="cite_ref-eep_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eep-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IAGNBI2003d_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IAGNBI2003d-48"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Decisions taken at the meeting included:</p>
<ul>
<li>There should be no augmenting of the wild populations at <a href="/wiki/Souss-Massa_National_Park" title="Souss-Massa National Park">Souss-Massa</a> or in <a href="/wiki/Palmyra" title="Palmyra">Palmyra</a> using zoo-bred ibises.<sup id="cite_ref-IAGNBI2003_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IAGNBI2003-49"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>There are two distinctive populations of Northern Bald Ibis, and the separate ranges of the eastern and western forms should be respected.<sup id="cite_ref-IAGNBI2003_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IAGNBI2003-49"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>In order to prepare birds for release, groups of chicks should be hand-reared by human "parents".<sup id="cite_ref-IAGNBI2003b_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IAGNBI2003b-50"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Migration routes and stop-over points will have to be taught to young birds, since it is unlikely that they will discover this information by themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-IAGNBI2003b_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IAGNBI2003b-50"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>A second conference in Spain in 2006 stressed the need to survey potential and former sites in northwest Africa and the Middle East for currently undetected colonies. The need to raise the standards of hygiene and husbandry in the <a href="/wiki/Birecik" title="Birecik">Birecik</a> aviaries was reiterated, and the prevalence of skin problems in a number of zoos reinforced the view that no zoo birds should be used for any free-flying trials.<sup id="cite_ref-IAGNBI2007_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IAGNBI2007-51"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> In future captive breeding and releasing programmes, only birds of known origin should be used.<sup id="cite_ref-Pegoraro_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pegoraro-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Bald_Ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Zoo populations">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Zoo_populations">Zoo populations</span></h4>
<p>There are 850 Northern Bald Ibises in European zoos and a further 250 in captivity in Japan and North America.<sup id="cite_ref-IAGNBI2003d_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IAGNBI2003d-48"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> The 49 European zoos keeping this species produce 80–100 young birds per year, and earlier attempts at releasing captive-bred birds included close to 150 birds between 1976-86 from an aviary at Birecik, 75 from <a href="/wiki/Tel_Aviv" title="Tel Aviv">Tel Aviv</a> Zoo in 1983, and an unspecified number from a project in <a href="/wiki/Almer%C3%ADa" title="Almería">Almería</a>, Spain, from 1991 to 1994; all these attempts were unsuccessful.<sup id="cite_ref-waza2_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waza2-52"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> All Northern Bald Ibises in zoos, other than those in Turkey, are of the western population, and were imported from Morocco. Three bloodlines exist; the earliest relates to importations to <a href="/wiki/Zoo_Basel" title="Zoo Basel">Zoo Basel</a>, Switzerland in the 1950s and 1960s, the next is the descendants of birds taken in the 1970s to stock <a href="/wiki/Rabat" title="Rabat">Rabat</a> Zoo, and the last captured wild birds were those taken to the Naturzoo, <a href="/wiki/Rheine" title="Rheine">Rheine</a>, in 1976 and 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-waza_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waza-53"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> Captive birds have a high incidence of skin problems, and 40% of those birds that had to be put down suffered from chronic ulcerative <a href="/wiki/Dermatitis" title="Dermatitis">dermatitis</a>, characterised by feather loss, rawness, and ulceration on the back, neck, and the undersides of the wings. The cause of this disease is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-Quevedo_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Quevedo-54"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> Other major disease problems reported in zoo collections have been avian <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>, gastric foreign bodies, bone disease, and heart problems.<sup id="cite_ref-greenwood_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenwood-55"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> An outbreak of <a href="/wiki/West_Nile_virus" title="West Nile virus">West Nile virus</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bronx_Park" title="Bronx Park">Bronx Park</a>, New York, involved Northern Bald Ibises amongst many other species of birds and mammals.<sup id="cite_ref-ludwig_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ludwig-56"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Bald_Ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span></h4>
<p>In 1504, a decree by <a href="/wiki/Leonhard_von_Keutschach" title="Leonhard von Keutschach">Archbishop Leonhard</a> of <a href="/wiki/Salzburg" title="Salzburg">Salzburg</a> made the Northern Bald Ibis one of the world's earliest officially protected species. Despite the decree, it died out in Austria as elsewhere in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Shuker_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shuker-57"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> There are now two ibis reintroduction projects in the country, at Grünau and Waldrapp. A research station at <a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%BCnau_im_Almtal" title="Grünau im Almtal">Grünau</a> has a breeding colony managed, like the Turkish population, as a free-flying flock which is caged at migration time. The aim here is to investigate flock interactions and hormonal status, behavioural and ecological aspects of natural foraging, and the establishment of traditions via social learning.<sup id="cite_ref-waza_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waza-53"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Graz_Schlossberg_Waldrapp_Infotafel.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Graz_Schlossberg_Waldrapp_Infotafel.jpg/220px-Graz_Schlossberg_Waldrapp_Infotafel.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Graz_Schlossberg_Waldrapp_Infotafel.jpg/330px-Graz_Schlossberg_Waldrapp_Infotafel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Graz_Schlossberg_Waldrapp_Infotafel.jpg/440px-Graz_Schlossberg_Waldrapp_Infotafel.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Graz_Schlossberg_Waldrapp_Infotafel.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
The Austrian reintroduction</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Scharnstein Project is an attempt to establish a migratory Waldrapp colony by using <a href="/wiki/Ultralight_aviation" title="Ultralight aviation">ultralight planes</a> to teach a migration route. The scheme builds on the Grünau research by developing a method to control and guide the autumn migration of a founder population, which then can pass this migration tradition to subsequent generations. In May 2002, 11 birds from the Vienna Zoo and the Grünau colony were trained to follow two microlight planes, and in 2003, a first attempt was made to lead a group of birds from <a href="/wiki/Scharnstein" title="Scharnstein">Scharnstein</a> to southern Tuscany. Due to adverse weather and technical problems, the birds had to be transported by road over a considerable part of the distance.<sup id="cite_ref-waza_53-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waza-53"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> The subsequent releases were more successful, with birds wintering in Tuscany, and, from 2005, returning to northern Austria. In 2008, a female ibis named Aurelia flew 930&#160;km (580&#160;mi) back to Austria for her fourth return to the breeding site. The hazards of the journey are shown by the loss of her two offspring and her mate while on the southern journey in the autumn of 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-Waldrapp_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Waldrapp-58"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Proyecto Eremita</i> is a Spanish reintroduction involving the release of nearly 30 birds in the Ministry of Defence training ground in <a href="/wiki/La_Janda" title="La Janda">La Janda</a> district, <a href="/wiki/Barbate" title="Barbate">Barbate</a> (<a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz_(province)" title="Cádiz (province)" class="mw-redirect">Cádiz Province</a>). It had its first success in 2008, when a pair laid two eggs. This is probably the first attempt to breed in the wild in Spain for 500 years as the last definite reference to the Northern Bald Ibis breeding in Spain is from a 15th-century falconry book.<sup id="cite_ref-elpais_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elpais-59"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> This effort has been undertaken by the <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusian</a> government's Environmental Ministry, the Spanish Ministry of Defence, and the <a href="/wiki/Jerez_de_la_Frontera" title="Jerez de la Frontera">Jerez</a> Zoo and Botanical Gardens, with the assistance of the <a href="/wiki/Do%C3%B1ana_National_Park" title="Doñana National Park">Doñana National Park</a> and volunteers from the Cádiz Natural History Society.<sup id="cite_ref-elpais_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elpais-59"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Proyectoeremita_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Proyectoeremita-60"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mde_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mde-61"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> Previously, two birds left the area in 2005 and 14 in 2006, but nothing is known of their whereabouts other than that a ringed bird from Spain was seen in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Atlas" title="Middle Atlas">Middle Atlas</a>, Morocco in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-waza_53-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waza-53"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Bald_Ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Northern Morocco">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Morocco">Northern Morocco</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Atlas-Mountains-Labeled-2_new.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Atlas-Mountains-Labeled-2_new.jpg/220px-Atlas-Mountains-Labeled-2_new.jpg" width="220" height="186" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Atlas-Mountains-Labeled-2_new.jpg/330px-Atlas-Mountains-Labeled-2_new.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Atlas-Mountains-Labeled-2_new.jpg/440px-Atlas-Mountains-Labeled-2_new.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Atlas-Mountains-Labeled-2_new.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Location of the Rif mountains</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There is a planned reintroduction of the ibis at Ain Tijja-<a href="/wiki/Mezguitem" title="Mezguitem">Mezguitem</a> in the north-east of <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>. Since the wild populations further south remain vulnerable, and the porous sandstone of their breeding ledges is exposed to erosion, the intention is to establish a non-migratory population (stocked from German, Swiss, and Austrian zoos) in an area where this species was know to have bred up to about 1980. The station in the <a href="/wiki/Rif" title="Rif">Rif</a> mountains was built in 2000, and stocked with the first group of zoo-bred birds. A second importation of zoo-bred birds and the construction of an information centre took place in 2004. Six pairs bred in 2006 subsequent to a change in the birds' diet, and six offspring from five nests were successfully reared. In 2007 there were 19 birds (13 adults and six juveniles) in the aviary.<sup id="cite_ref-waza3_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waza3-62"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The rock walls of the mountains have many potential breeding ledges, and an artificial lake provides water to the birds and to the local human population. Steppe pasture which is not exposed to herbicides or pesticides gives good foraging. Once the population reaches around 40 birds, a release will be initiated, subject to international agreement. The reintroduction site is 760&#160;km (475&#160;mi) from Agadir on the other side of the Atlas Mountains, so accidental contamination of the wild colonies is unlikely.<sup id="cite_ref-waza4_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waza4-63"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Bald_Ibis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mosaico_Trabajos_H%C3%A9rcules_(M.A.N._Madrid)_06.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Mosaico_Trabajos_H%C3%A9rcules_%28M.A.N._Madrid%29_06.jpg/170px-Mosaico_Trabajos_H%C3%A9rcules_%28M.A.N._Madrid%29_06.jpg" width="170" height="224" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Mosaico_Trabajos_H%C3%A9rcules_%28M.A.N._Madrid%29_06.jpg/255px-Mosaico_Trabajos_H%C3%A9rcules_%28M.A.N._Madrid%29_06.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Mosaico_Trabajos_H%C3%A9rcules_%28M.A.N._Madrid%29_06.jpg/340px-Mosaico_Trabajos_H%C3%A9rcules_%28M.A.N._Madrid%29_06.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mosaico_Trabajos_H%C3%A9rcules_(M.A.N._Madrid)_06.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">Heracles</a> fights the Stymphalian birds</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>According to local legend in the Birecik area, the Northern Bald Ibis was one of the first birds that <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">Noah</a> released from the <a href="/wiki/Noah%27s_Ark" title="Noah's Ark">Ark</a> as a symbol of fertility,<sup id="cite_ref-Shuker_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shuker-57"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> and a lingering religious sentiment in Turkey helped the colonies there to survive long after the demise of the species in Europe, as described above.<sup id="cite_ref-aewa6_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aewa6-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This ibis was revered as a holy bird and a symbol of brilliance and splendour in Ancient Egypt,<sup id="cite_ref-unep_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unep-64"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FAO_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-65"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> where, together with the <a href="/wiki/Sacred_Ibis" title="Sacred Ibis" class="mw-redirect">Sacred Ibis</a>, it was regarded as a reincarnation of <a href="/wiki/Thoth" title="Thoth">Thoth</a>, scribe of the gods, who was usually depicted with a man's body and the head of an ibis. The <a href="/wiki/Old_Egyptian" title="Old Egyptian" class="mw-redirect">Old Egyptian</a> word <i>akh</i>, "to be resplendent, to shine", was denoted in <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs" title="Egyptian hieroglyphs">hieroglyphs</a> by a Bald Ibis, presumably as a reference to its glossy plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-Lamy_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lamy-66"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> In a more abstract sense, <i>akh</i> stood for excellence, glory, honour, and virtue.<sup id="cite_ref-FAO_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-65"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> It has also been used to signify the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_soul" title="Egyptian soul" class="mw-redirect">soul</a> or spirit, one of five elements constituting personality.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> wrote of the man-eating <a href="/wiki/Stymphalian_birds" title="Stymphalian birds">Stymphalian birds</a>, which had wings of brass and sharp metallic feathers they could fire at their victims. Ridding <a href="/wiki/Lake_Stymphalia" title="Lake Stymphalia">Lake Stymphalia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Arcadia" title="Arcadia">Arcadia</a> of these creatures was one of the twelve <a href="/wiki/Labours_of_Hercules" title="Labours of Hercules">labours of Heracles</a>. These mythical birds are sometimes considered to be based on the Northern Bald Ibis,<sup id="cite_ref-Shuker_57-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shuker-57"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> but since they were described as marsh birds, and usually depicted without crests, the legendary species is more likely to be derived from the Sacred Ibis.<sup id="cite_ref-Hall_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hall-68"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> Some depictions, such as the 6th century BC Athenian black-figure amphora in the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>, clearly show the black head and white body of the latter species.<sup id="cite_ref-Britmus_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Britmus-69"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> After the Bald Ibis became extinct in Central Europe, some later writers thought that Gesner's description was itself one of several in his book depicting mythical creatures.<sup id="cite_ref-Shuker_57-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shuker-57"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The bird painted in 1490 in one of the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_fresco" title="Gothic fresco" class="mw-redirect">Gothic frescoes</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Trinity_Church,_Hrastovlje" title="Holy Trinity Church, Hrastovlje">Holy Trinity Church in Hrastovlje</a> (now southwestern Slovenia) in the <a href="/wiki/Kras" title="Kras">Karst</a> by <a href="/wiki/John_of_Kastav" title="John of Kastav">John of Kastav</a> was most probably the Northern Bald Ibis.<sup id="cite_ref-PT2001_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PT2001-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> It is believed that it had also been depicted at other places in <a href="/wiki/Istria" title="Istria">Istria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dalmatia" title="Dalmatia">Dalmatia</a>, where it was presumably endemic in the Middle Ages, e.g. in the local church in <a href="/wiki/Gradi%C5%A1%C4%8De_pri_Diva%C4%8Di" title="Gradišče pri Divači">Gradišče pri Divači</a> and in the coat of arms of the noble family Elio from <a href="/wiki/Koper" title="Koper">Koper</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> The portal of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lukovec_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lukovec Castle (page does not exist)">Lukovec Castle</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lukovica_pri_Brezovici" title="Lukovica pri Brezovici">Lukovica pri Brezovici</a> (central Slovenia) also features this species.<sup id="cite_ref-Delo66559_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Delo66559-71"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Several countries have produced postage stamps which depict the Northern Bald Ibis. They include Algeria, Morocco, Sudan, Syria, Turkey, and Yemen, which are breeding or migration locations; Austria, which is seeking to reintroduce the bird; and <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-dutton_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dutton-72"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> which has a small captive population.<sup id="cite_ref-stamp_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stamp-73"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
