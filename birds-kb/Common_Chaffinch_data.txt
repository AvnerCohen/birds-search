<p>The <b>common chaffinch</b> or simply the <b>chaffinch</b> (<i>Fringilla coelebs</i>) is a common and widespread small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird in the <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finch</a> family. The male is brightly coloured with a <a href="/wiki/Blue-grey" class="mw-redirect" title="Blue-grey">blue-grey</a> cap and <a href="/wiki/Rust_(color)" title="Rust (color)">rust-red</a> underparts. The female is much duller in colouring, but both sexes have two contrasting white wing bars and white sides to the tail. The male bird has a strong voice and sings from exposed perches to attract a mate.
</p><p>The chaffinch breeds in much of Europe, across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> to Siberia and in northwestern Africa. The female builds a nest with a deep cup in the fork of a tree. The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is typically four or five  eggs, which hatch in about 13&#160;days. The chicks <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> in around 14&#160;days, but are fed by both adults for several weeks after leaving the nest. Outside the breeding season, chaffinches form flocks in open countryside and forage for seeds on the ground. During the breeding season, they forage on trees for invertebrates, especially caterpillars, and feed these to their young. They are partial <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrants</a>; birds breeding in warmer regions are sedentary, while those breeding in the colder northern areas of their range winter further south.
</p><p>The eggs and nestlings of the chaffinch are taken by a variety of mammalian and avian predators. Its large numbers and huge range mean that chaffinches are classed as of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a> by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chaffinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common chaffinch was described by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> under its current <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-linnaeus_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linnaeus-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <i>Fringilla</i> is the Latin word for finch, while <i>caelebs</i> means unmarried or single. Linnaeus remarked that during the Swedish winter, only the female birds migrated south through Belgium to Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-linnaeus_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linnaeus-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The English name comes from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>ceaffinc</i>, where <i>ceaf</i> is "<a href="/wiki/Chaff" title="Chaff">chaff</a>" and <i>finc</i> "<a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finch</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Chaffinches were likely given this name because after farmers thresh their crops, these birds sometimes spend weeks picking through heaps of discarded chaff for grain. The chaffinch is one of the many birds depicted in the marginal decoration of the 15th century English illuminated manuscript the <a href="/wiki/Sherborne_Missal" title="Sherborne Missal">Sherborne Missal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The English naturalist <a href="/wiki/William_Turner_(naturalist)" title="William Turner (naturalist)">William Turner</a> described the common chaffinch in his book on birds published in 1544. Although the text is in Latin, Turner gives the English name as chaffinche and lists two folk names: sheld-appel and spink.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The word <i>sheld</i> is a dialectal word meaning pied or multicoloured (as in <a href="/wiki/Shelduck" title="Shelduck">shelduck</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Appel may be related to <i>Alp</i>, an obsolete word for a bullfinch.<sup id="cite_ref-swainson_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swainson-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The name <i>spink</i> is probably derived from the bird's call note. The names spink and shell apple are among the many folk names listed for the common chaffinch by Reverend Charles Swainson in his <i>Provincial Names and Folk Lore of British Birds</i> (1885).<sup id="cite_ref-swainson_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swainson-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Fringillidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Fringillidae">Fringillidae</a> are all seed-eaters with stout conical bills. They have similar skull morphologies, nine large <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Primaries" title="Flight feather">primaries</a>, 12 tail feathers and no <a href="/wiki/Crop_(anatomy)#Birds" title="Crop (anatomy)">crop</a>. In all species, the female builds the nest, incubates the eggs, and broods the young. Finches are divided into two subfamilies, the <a href="/wiki/Carduelinae" title="Carduelinae">Carduelinae</a>, containing around 28 <a href="/wiki/Genera" class="mw-redirect" title="Genera">genera</a> with 141 <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Fringillinae" class="mw-redirect" title="Fringillinae">Fringillinae</a> containing a single genus, <i><a href="/wiki/Fringilla" title="Fringilla">Fringilla</a></i>, with four species: the common chaffinch (<i>F. coelebs</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Gran_Canaria_blue_chaffinch" title="Gran Canaria blue chaffinch">Gran Canaria blue chaffinch</a> (<i>F. polatzeki</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Tenerife_blue_chaffinch" title="Tenerife blue chaffinch">Tenerife blue chaffinch</a> (<i>F. teydea</i>), and the <a href="/wiki/Brambling" title="Brambling">brambling</a> (<i>F. montifringilla</i>). Fringilline finches raise their young almost entirely on <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a>, while the cardueline finches raise their young on regurgitated seeds.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwfinches_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwfinches-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chaffinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A number of <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of the common chaffinch have been described, based principally on the differences in the pattern and colour of the adult male plumage. The subspecies can be divided into three groups: the "<i>coelebs</i> group" that occurs in Europe and Asia, the "<i>spondiogenys</i> group" in North Africa, and the "<i>canariensis</i> group" on the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bow_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bow-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The subspecies from <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> are placed either in the "<i>canariensis</i> group"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994448_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994448-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> or in the "<i>spondiogenys</i> group".<sup id="cite_ref-bow_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bow-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Genetic studies indicate that members of the "<i>coelebs</i> group" and the "<i>spondiogenys</i> group" are more closely related to each other than they are to members of the "<i>canariensis</i> group".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Within the "<i>spondiogenys</i> group", the gradual <a href="/wiki/Cline_(biology)" title="Cline (biology)">clinal</a> variation over the large geographic range and the extensive <a href="/wiki/Intergradation" title="Intergradation">intergradation</a> means that the geographical limits and acceptance of the various subspecies varies between authorities. The <a href="/wiki/International_Ornithological_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="International Ornithological Committee">International Ornithologists' Union</a> lists 11 subspecies from this group,<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> whereas Peter Clement in the Birds of the World lists seven and considers the features of the subspecies <i>balearica</i> (<a href="/wiki/Mallorca" title="Mallorca">Mallorca</a>), <i>caucasica</i> (the southern <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>), <i>schiebeli</i> (southern Greece, <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a> and western Turkey), and <i>tyrrhenica</i> (<a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>) to fall within the variation of the <a href="/wiki/Subspecies#Nominotypical_subspecies_and_subspecies_autonyms" title="Subspecies">nominate subspecies</a>. He also suggests that the subspecies <i>alexandrovi</i>, <i>sarda</i>, <i>solomkoi</i>, and  <i>syriaca</i> may represent variations of the nominate subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-bow_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bow-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The authors of a 2009 <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular phylogenetic">molecular phylogenetic</a> study on the three subspecies that were recognised on the Canary Islands concluded that they are sufficiently distinct in both <a href="/wiki/Genotype" title="Genotype">genotype</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotype</a> to be considered as separate species within the genus <i>Fringilla</i>. They also proposed a revised distribution of the subspecies on the islands in which the birds on <a href="/wiki/La_Palma" title="La Palma">La Palma</a> (<i>palmae</i>) and <a href="/wiki/El_Hierro" title="El Hierro">El Hierro</a> (<i>ombrioso</i>) are grouped together as a single subspecies, while the current <i>canariensis</i> subspecies is split into two, with one subspecies occurring only on <a href="/wiki/Gran_Canaria" title="Gran Canaria">Gran Canaria</a> and the other on <a href="/wiki/La_Gomera" title="La Gomera">La Gomera</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tenerife" title="Tenerife">Tenerife</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-suarez_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-suarez-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The results of a study published in 2018 confirmed the earlier findings. The authors formerly described the Gran Canaria variety as a subspecies and coined the <a href="/wiki/Trinomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Trinomial name">trinomial name</a> <i>Fringilla coelebs bakeri</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chaffinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:African_Chaffinch_(Fringilla_coelebs_africana)_Morocco.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/African_Chaffinch_%28Fringilla_coelebs_africana%29_Morocco.jpg/220px-African_Chaffinch_%28Fringilla_coelebs_africana%29_Morocco.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/African_Chaffinch_%28Fringilla_coelebs_africana%29_Morocco.jpg/330px-African_Chaffinch_%28Fringilla_coelebs_africana%29_Morocco.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/African_Chaffinch_%28Fringilla_coelebs_africana%29_Morocco.jpg/440px-African_Chaffinch_%28Fringilla_coelebs_africana%29_Morocco.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4438" data-file-height="2959" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:African_Chaffinch_(Fringilla_coelebs_africana)_Morocco.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Male <i>F. c. africana</i> in Morocco</div></div></div>
<p>The common chaffinch is about 14.5&#160;cm (5.7&#160;in) long, with a wingspan of 24.5–28.5&#160;cm (9.6–11.2&#160;in) and a weight of 18–29&#160;g (0.63–1.02&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994448_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994448-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The adult male of the nominate subspecies has a black forehead and a <a href="/wiki/Blue-grey" class="mw-redirect" title="Blue-grey">blue-grey</a> crown, nape and upper mantle. The rump is a light <a href="/wiki/Olive_(color)" title="Olive (color)">olive-green</a>; the lower mantle and scapulars form a brown saddle. The side of head, throat and breast are a dull <a href="/wiki/Rust_(color)" title="Rust (color)">rust-red</a> merging to a pale creamy-pink on the belly. The central pair of tail feathers are dark grey with a black shaft streak.  The rest of the tail is black apart from the two outer feathers on each side which have white wedges.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994467-468_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994467-468-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Each wing has a contrasting white panel on the <a href="/wiki/Covert_feather" title="Covert feather">coverts</a> and a buff-white bar on the <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Secondaries" title="Flight feather">secondaries</a> and inner primaries.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994448_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994448-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">flight feathers</a> are black with white on the basal portions of the vanes. The secondaries and inner primaries have pale yellow fringes on the outer web whereas the outer primaries have a white outer edge.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994467-468_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994467-468-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the autumn <a href="/wiki/Moulting#Birds" title="Moulting">moult</a>, the tips of the new feathers have a buff fringe that adds a brown cast to the coloured plumage. The ends of the feathers wear away over the winter so that by the spring breeding season the underlying brighter colours are displayed.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994467-468_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994467-468-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton197219_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton197219-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The eyes have dark brown <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">irises</a> and the legs are grey-brown. In winter the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> is a pale grey and slightly darker along the upper ridge or <a href="/wiki/Beak#Culmen" title="Beak">culmen</a>, but in spring the bill becomes bluish-grey with a small black tip.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994469_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994469-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fringilla_coelebs_palmae_-_Los_Tilos.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Fringilla_coelebs_palmae_-_Los_Tilos.jpg/220px-Fringilla_coelebs_palmae_-_Los_Tilos.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Fringilla_coelebs_palmae_-_Los_Tilos.jpg/330px-Fringilla_coelebs_palmae_-_Los_Tilos.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Fringilla_coelebs_palmae_-_Los_Tilos.jpg/440px-Fringilla_coelebs_palmae_-_Los_Tilos.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4752" data-file-height="3168" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fringilla_coelebs_palmae_-_Los_Tilos.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Male <i>F. c. palmae</i>, <a href="/wiki/La_Palma" title="La Palma">La Palma</a>, Canary Islands</div></div></div>
<p>The male of the subspecies resident in the British Isles (<i>F. c. gengleri</i>) closely resembles the nominate subspecies, but has a slightly darker mantle and underparts. The males of the two North African subspecies <i>F. c. africana</i> and <i>F. c. spodiogenys</i> have a blue-grey crown and nape that extends down to the sides of the head and neck, a black forehead and lore, a broken white eye-ring, a bright olive-green saddle and a pink-buff throat and breast. The males of <i>F. c. canariensis</i> and <i>F. c. palmae</i> in the Canary Islands have deep slate-blue upperparts and lack a contrasting mantle. Male chaffinches in Madeira (<i>F. c. maderensis</i>) and the Azores (<i>F. c. moreletti</i>) are similar in appearance to <i>F. c. canariensis</i>, but have a bright green mantle.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994472-473_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994472-473-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The adult female is much duller in appearance than the male. The head and most of the upperparts are shades of grey-brown. The underparts are paler. The lower back and rump are a dull olive green. The wings and tail are similar to those of the male. The juvenile resembles the female.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994449_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994449-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:FringillaCoelebsMadeirensis_3749.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/FringillaCoelebsMadeirensis_3749.jpg/220px-FringillaCoelebsMadeirensis_3749.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/FringillaCoelebsMadeirensis_3749.jpg/330px-FringillaCoelebsMadeirensis_3749.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/FringillaCoelebsMadeirensis_3749.jpg/440px-FringillaCoelebsMadeirensis_3749.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2048" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:FringillaCoelebsMadeirensis_3749.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Male <i>F. c. maderensis</i>, <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chaffinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Males typically sing two or three different song types, and there are regional dialects also.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The acquisition by the young common chaffinch of its song was the subject of an influential study by British ethologist <a href="/wiki/William_Homan_Thorpe" title="William Homan Thorpe">William Thorpe</a>. Thorpe determined that if the young common chaffinch is not exposed to the adult male's song during a certain <a href="/wiki/Critical_period" title="Critical period">critical period</a> after hatching, it will never properly learn the song. He also found that in adult common chaffinches, castration eliminates the song, but injection of testosterone induces such birds to sing even in November, when they are normally silent.<sup id="cite_ref-thorpe_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thorpe-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chaffinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common chaffinch breeds in wooded areas where the July <a href="/wiki/Isotherm_(contour_line)" class="mw-redirect" title="Isotherm (contour line)">isotherm</a> is between 12 and 30&#160;°C (54 and 86&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994450_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994450-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The breeding range includes northwestern Africa and most of Europe and extends eastwards across <a href="/wiki/Temperate_climate" title="Temperate climate">temperate</a> Asia to the <a href="/wiki/Angara_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Angara River">Angara River</a> and the southern end of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. There are also a number of distinctive subspecies on the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Madeira_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Madeira Islands">Madeira Islands</a> in the Atlantic Ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-bow_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bow-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The common chaffinch was introduced from Great Britain into several of its overseas territories in the second half of the 19th century. In New Zealand, the common chaffinch had colonised both the <a href="/wiki/North_Island" title="North Island">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Island" title="South Island">South Islands</a> by 1900 and is now one of the most widespread and common passerine species.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In South Africa, a very small breeding colony in the suburbs of <a href="/wiki/Constantia,_Cape_Town" title="Constantia, Cape Town">Constantia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hout_Bay" title="Hout Bay">Hout Bay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pinelands" class="mw-disambig" title="Pinelands">Pinelands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Camps_Bay" title="Camps Bay">Camps Bay</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a> is the only remnant of another such introduction.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This bird is not <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> in the milder parts of its range, but vacates the colder regions in winter. It forms loose flocks outside the breeding season, sometimes mixed with <a href="/wiki/Brambling" title="Brambling">bramblings</a>. It occasionally strays to eastern North America, although some sightings may be escapees.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chaffinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pinson_des_arbres_%E2%80%93_Fringilla_coelebs_moreletti_%E2%80%93_Flamengos_Fayal_A%C3%A7ores_222.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Pinson_des_arbres_%E2%80%93_Fringilla_coelebs_moreletti_%E2%80%93_Flamengos_Fayal_A%C3%A7ores_222.jpg/220px-Pinson_des_arbres_%E2%80%93_Fringilla_coelebs_moreletti_%E2%80%93_Flamengos_Fayal_A%C3%A7ores_222.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="182" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Pinson_des_arbres_%E2%80%93_Fringilla_coelebs_moreletti_%E2%80%93_Flamengos_Fayal_A%C3%A7ores_222.jpg/330px-Pinson_des_arbres_%E2%80%93_Fringilla_coelebs_moreletti_%E2%80%93_Flamengos_Fayal_A%C3%A7ores_222.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Pinson_des_arbres_%E2%80%93_Fringilla_coelebs_moreletti_%E2%80%93_Flamengos_Fayal_A%C3%A7ores_222.jpg/440px-Pinson_des_arbres_%E2%80%93_Fringilla_coelebs_moreletti_%E2%80%93_Flamengos_Fayal_A%C3%A7ores_222.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4984" data-file-height="4124" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pinson_des_arbres_%E2%80%93_Fringilla_coelebs_moreletti_%E2%80%93_Flamengos_Fayal_A%C3%A7ores_222.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs of <i>Fringilla coelebs moreletti</i></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chaffinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Common chaffinches first breed when they are 1 year old. They are mainly monogamous and the pair-bond for residential subspecies such as <i>gengleri</i> sometimes persists from one year to the next.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994457_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994457-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The date for breeding is dependent on the spring temperature and is earlier in southwest Europe and later in the northeast. In Great Britain, most <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutches</a> are laid between late April and the middle of June. A male attracts a female to his territory through song.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton1972137_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton1972137-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Nests are built entirely by the female and are usually located in the fork of a bush or a tree several metres above the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994466-467_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994466-467-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The nest has a deep cup and is lined with a layer of thin roots and feathers. The outside is covered with a layer of lichen and spider silk over an inner layer of moss and grass. The eggs are laid in early morning at daily intervals until the <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is complete.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton1972141_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton1972141-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The clutch is typically 4–5 eggs, which are smooth and slightly glossy, but very variable in colour. They range from pale-blueish green to light red with purple-brown blotches, spots or steaks. The average size of an egg is 19&#160;mm ×&#160;15&#160;mm (0.75&#160;in ×&#160;0.59&#160;in) with a weight of 2.2&#160;g (0.078&#160;oz). The eggs are incubated for 10–16 days by the female.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994466-467_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994466-467-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The chicks are <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a>, hatching nearly naked with closed eyes, and are fed by both parents but mainly by the female, who broods them for around six days.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton1972141-142_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton1972141-142-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> They are mainly fed caterpillars. The nestlings fledge 11–18 days after hatching and disperse. The young birds are then assisted with feeding by both parents for a further three weeks. The parents only very rarely start a second brood, but when they do so it is always in a new nest.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994466-467_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994466-467-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Juveniles undergo a partial <a href="/wiki/Moult" class="mw-redirect" title="Moult">moult</a> at around five weeks of age in which they replace their head, body and many of their <a href="/wiki/Covert_feather" title="Covert feather">covert</a> feathers, but not their primary and secondary flight feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994469_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994469-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> After breeding adult birds undergo a complete annual moult which lasts around ten weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994469_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994469-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton1972257,_Appendix_11_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton1972257,_Appendix_11-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In a study carried out in Britain using <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">ring-recovery</a> data, the survival rate for juveniles in their first year was 53&#160;per cent, and the adult annual survival rate was 59&#160;per cent.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> From these figures the typical lifespan is only 3 years,<sup id="cite_ref-bto_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> but the maximum age recorded is 15 years and 6 months for a bird in Switzerland.<sup id="cite_ref-euring_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euring-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chaffinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Outside the breeding season, common chaffinches mainly eat seeds and other plant material that they find on the ground.  They often forage in open country in large flocks. Common chaffinches seldom take food directly from plants and only very rarely use their feet for handling food.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994455-456_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994455-456-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> During the breeding season, their diet switches to invertebrates, especially defoliating caterpillars. They forage in trees and also occasionally make short sallies to catch insects in the air.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994455-456_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994455-456-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The young are entirely fed with invertebrates which include caterpillars, aphids, earwigs, spiders and grubs (the larvae of beetles).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994455-456_41-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994455-456-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators_and_parasites">Predators and parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chaffinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Predators and parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The eggs and nestlings of the common chaffinch are predated by <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red_squirrel" title="Red squirrel">Eurasian red</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_gray_squirrel" title="Eastern gray squirrel">eastern grey squirrels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">domestic cats</a> and probably also by <a href="/wiki/Stoat" title="Stoat">stoats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Least_weasel" title="Least weasel">weasels</a>. Clutches begun later in the spring suffer less predation, an effect that is believed to be due to the increased vegetation making nests more difficult to find.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton1972145_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton1972145-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Unlike the case for the closely related brambling, the common chaffinch is not parasitised by the <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common cuckoo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENewton197228_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewton197228-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoal</a> parasite <i><a href="/wiki/Trichomonas_gallinae" title="Trichomonas gallinae">Trichomonas gallinae</a></i> was known to infect pigeons and raptors, but beginning in Great Britain in 2005, carcasses of dead <a href="/wiki/European_greenfinch" title="European greenfinch">European greenfinches</a> and common chaffinches were found to be infected with the parasite.<sup id="cite_ref-robinson2010_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-robinson2010-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> The disease spread and in 2008, infected carcasses were found in Norway, Sweden and Finland and a year later in Germany. The spread of the disease is believed to have been mediated by common chaffinches, as large numbers of the birds breed in northern Europe and winter in Great Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-lawson2011_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lawson2011-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> In Great Britain, the number of infected carcasses recovered each year declined after a peak in 2006. There was a reduction in the number of European greenfinches but no significant decline in the overall number of common chaffinches.<sup id="cite_ref-lawson2012_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lawson2012-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> A similar pattern occurred in Finland where, after the arrival of the disease in 2008, there was a reduction in the number of European greenfinches, but only a small change in the number of common chaffinches.<sup id="cite_ref-lehikoinen2013_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lehikoinen2013-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Common chaffinches can develop tumors on their feet and legs caused by the <i>Fringilla coelebs</i> <a href="/wiki/Papillomaviridae" title="Papillomaviridae">papillomavirus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-lina1973_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lina1973-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> The size of the <a href="/wiki/Papilloma" title="Papilloma">papillomas</a> range from a small nodule on a digit to a large growth involving both the foot and the leg. The disease is uncommon: in a 1973 study undertaken in the Netherlands, of around 25,000 common chaffinches screened, only 330 bore papillomas.<sup id="cite_ref-lina1973_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lina1973-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chaffinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common chaffinch has an extensive range, estimated at 7 million square kilometres (3.7 million square miles) and a large population including an estimated 130–240 million breeding pairs in Europe. Allowing for the birds breeding in Asia, the total population lies between 530–1,400 million individuals.  There is no evidence of any serious overall decline in numbers, so the species is classified by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> as being of <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-birdlife_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> subspecies on the <a href="/wiki/Macaronesia" title="Macaronesia">Macaronesian</a> islands in the Atlantic are vulnerable to the loss of habitat, especially <i>F. c. ombriosa</i> on <a href="/wiki/El_Hierro" title="El Hierro">El Hierro</a> in the Canary Islands, where the breeding population is between 1,000-5,000 pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_humans">Relationship to humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_chaffinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Relationship to humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common chaffinch was once popular as a caged songbird and large numbers of wild birds were trapped and sold.<sup id="cite_ref-beeton_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beeton-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> At the end of the 19th century, trapping even depleted the number of birds in London parks.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> In 1882, the English publisher <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Orchart_Beeton" title="Samuel Orchart Beeton">Samuel Orchart Beeton</a> issued a guide on the care of caged birds and included the recommendation: "To parents and guardians plagued with a morose and sulky boy, my advice is, buy him a chaffinch."<sup id="cite_ref-beeton_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beeton-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Competitions were held where bets were placed on which caged common chaffinch would repeat its song the greatest number of times. The birds were sometimes blinded with a hot needle in the belief that this encouraged them to sing.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> This practice is the subject of the poem <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blinded_Bird" title="The Blinded Bird">The Blinded Bird</a></i> by the English author <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hardy" title="Thomas Hardy">Thomas Hardy</a>, which contrasts the cruelty involved in blinding the birds with their zestful song.<sup id="cite_ref-cocker_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cocker-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> In Great Britain, the practice of keeping common chaffinches as pets declined after the trapping of wild birds was outlawed by the Wild Birds Protection Acts of 1880 to 1896.<sup id="cite_ref-cocker_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cocker-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common chaffinch is still a popular pet bird in some European countries. In Belgium, the traditional sport of <a href="/wiki/Vinkenzetting" class="mw-redirect" title="Vinkenzetting">vinkenzetting</a> pits male common chaffinches against one another in a contest for the most bird calls in an hour.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
