<p>The <b>Common Chaffinch</b> (<i>Fringilla coelebs</i>), also called by a wide variety of other names, is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird in the <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finch</a> family Fringillidae.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Chaffinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The Common Chaffinch's large double white wing bars, white tail edges and greenish rump easily identify this 14–16&#160;cm long species. The breeding male is unmistakable, with his reddish underparts and a blue-grey cap. The female is drabber and greener, but still obvious.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Chaffinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The name chaffinch comes from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>ceaffinc</i>, literally "<a href="/wiki/Chaff" title="Chaff">chaff</a> <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finch</a>", and is the source of the nickname chaffy or chaffie.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-knight_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-knight-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-swainson_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swainson-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The bird is so named for its tendency to peck the grain left out in farmyards,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> a habit which has also garnered it the names wheatbird and wheatsel-bird or wheatsel bird (from "wheatsel", a rare word meaning <i><a href="/wiki/Seed_drill" title="Seed drill">wheat drilling</a></i>), the latter used primarily of male chaffinches (or "cock-chaffinches").<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The names scobby, cobby, scoppy, and scop refer to this pecking ("scop" is a <a href="/wiki/Cumbrian" title="Cumbrian" class="mw-redirect">Cumbrian</a> word meaning <i>to hit</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-macpherson_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-macpherson-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dickinson_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dickinson-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Common Chaffinch's appearance has given rise to the names whitewing, white finch, copper finch, flecky flocker, pied finch, and robinet ("little <a href="/wiki/European_Robin" title="European Robin">robin redbreast</a>").<sup id="cite_ref-dickinson_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dickinson-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-journal_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-journal-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-robinet_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-robinet-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The name shellapple or shillapple (also spelled sheldapple, sheldafle, or archaically <i>sheldaple</i>) is from "sheld", a rare word meaning <i>variegated</i>, and "dapple".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> This name also appears in the <a href="/wiki/Metathesis_(linguistics)" title="Metathesis (linguistics)">metathetic</a> forms apple-sheeler, its corruption upper shealer, and apple bird.<sup id="cite_ref-swainson_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swainson-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-muirhead_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-muirhead-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> The dialectal names shelly, skelly, sheely, shiltie, shilfer, shilfa, sheelfa, and shulfie are derived from these.<sup id="cite_ref-knight_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-knight-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-swainson_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swainson-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-macpherson_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-macpherson-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-muirhead_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-muirhead-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-morris_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morris-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Spink and the less common names pink, pinkie, and pinkety are of the same <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> origin as <i><a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finch</a></i> (confer Greek <i>spiza</i>, chaffinch, and French <i>pinson</i>, finch), and are possibly imitative of the bird's song.<sup id="cite_ref-swainson_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swainson-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-macpherson_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-macpherson-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-morris_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morris-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> This unique call has inspired the names twink, tweet, weet-weet, chink chink, chink chaffey, and pink twink.<sup id="cite_ref-swainson_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swainson-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-morris2_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morris2-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Popular belief holds that the chaffinch's song foretells rain, leading to the name wetbird.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Common Chaffinch is also known by the names beech finch, horse finch (and the variation hoose finch), buck finch, roberd, boldie, and the reduplicative shellapple shiltie.<sup id="cite_ref-knight_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-knight-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-macpherson_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-macpherson-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-journal_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-journal-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-robinet_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-robinet-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-muirhead_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-muirhead-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-morris_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morris-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-morris2_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morris2-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> English naturalist Charles Swainson recorded 36 names for the Common Chaffinch in his <i>Provincial Names and Folk Lore of British Birds</i> (1885), including brichtie, brisk finch, briskie, bullspink, bully, charbob, daffinch, maze finch, pea finch, pine finch, and snabby.<sup id="cite_ref-swainson_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swainson-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Chaffinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>This bird is widespread and very familiar throughout Europe. It is the most common finch in western Europe, and the second most common bird in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>. Its range extends into western Asia, northwestern Africa, and <a href="/wiki/Macaronesia" title="Macaronesia">Macaronesia</a>, where it has many distinctive island forms. In the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tenerife" title="Tenerife">Tenerife</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gran_Canaria" title="Gran Canaria">Gran Canaria</a>, the Common Chaffinch has colonised twice, giving rise to the <a href="/wiki/Endemism_in_birds" title="Endemism in birds" class="mw-redirect">endemic</a> species known as the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Chaffinch" title="Blue Chaffinch">Blue Chaffinch</a> and a distinctive <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>. In each of the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>, and in the rest of the Canaries there is a single species on each island.</p>
<p>It was introduced from Britain into a number of its overseas territories in the 18th and 19th centuries. In New Zealand it is a common species. In South Africa a very small breeding colony in the suburbs of <a href="/wiki/Constantia,_Cape_Town" title="Constantia, Cape Town">Constantia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hout_Bay" title="Hout Bay">Hout Bay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Camps_Bay" title="Camps Bay">Camps Bay</a> near <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a> is the only remnant of one such introduction.</p>
<p>It uses a range of habitats, but open woodland is favoured, although it is common in gardens and on farmland.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Chaffinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<p>This bird is not <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> in the milder parts of its range, but vacates the colder regions in winter. The <i>coelebs</i> part of its name means "bachelor". This species was named by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a>; in his home country of Sweden, where the females depart in winter, but the males often remain.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> This species forms loose flocks outside the breeding season, sometimes mixed with <a href="/wiki/Brambling" title="Brambling">Bramblings</a>. This bird occasionally strays to eastern North America, although some sightings may be escapees.</p>
<p>It builds its nest in a tree fork, and decorates the exterior with moss or <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichen</a> to make it less conspicuous. It lays about six eggs, which are greenish-blue with purple speckling.<sup id="cite_ref-Newton23_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newton23-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The main food of the Common Chaffinch is <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a>, but unlike most finches, the young are fed extensively on <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, and adults also eat insects in the breeding season.<sup id="cite_ref-Newton23_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newton23-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The powerful song is very well known, and its <i>fink</i> or <i>vink</i> sounding call gives the finch family its English name. Males typically sing two or three different song types, and there are regional dialects too&lt;/ref&gt;<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup>(<span class="unicode"><a href="/wiki/File:Fringilla_coelebs_short.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" /></a>&#160;<a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a1/Fringilla_coelebs_short.ogg" class="internal" title="Fringilla coelebs short.ogg">song</a><small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">&#160;(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:Fringilla_coelebs_short.ogg" title="File:Fringilla coelebs short.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span>)</p>
<p>The acquisition by the young chaffinch of its song was the subject of an influential study by British ethologist <a href="/wiki/William_Homan_Thorpe" title="William Homan Thorpe">William Thorpe</a>. Thorpe determined that if the chaffinch is not exposed to the adult male's song during a certain <a href="/wiki/Critical_period" title="Critical period">critical period</a> after hatching, it will never properly learn the song.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from January 2010">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> He also found that in adult chaffinches, castration eliminates song, but injection of testosterone induces such birds to sing even in November, when they are normally silent.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Chaffinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In captivity">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="In_captivity">In captivity</span></h2>
<p>The Common Chaffinch is a popular pet bird in many countries. In Belgium, the ancient traditional sport of <a href="/wiki/Vinkenzetting" title="Vinkenzetting" class="mw-redirect">vinkenzetting</a> pits male chaffinches against one another in a contest for the most bird calls in an hour.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_Chaffinch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span></h2>
<p>The Common Chaffinch is depicted in a marginal decoration of the 15th century English illuminated manuscript the <a href="/wiki/Sherborne_Missal" title="Sherborne Missal">Sherborne Missal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
