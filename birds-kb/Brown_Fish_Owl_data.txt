<p>The <b>brown fish owl</b> (<i>Ketupa zeylonensis</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Fish_owl" title="Fish owl">fish owl</a> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Typical_owl" class="mw-redirect" title="Typical owl">typical owls</a>, Strigidae. It is native from Turkey to <a href="/wiki/South" title="South">South</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>. Due its wide distribution it is listed as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a> on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It inhabits forests and wooded wetlands.<sup id="cite_ref-Grimmett_al2016_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grimmett_al2016-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Of the four living species of fish owl, it is the most widely distributed, most common and best-studied. It occupies a range of over 7,000&#160;km (4,300&#160;mi).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown_fish_owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SL_Yala_NP_BlockV_asv2020-01_img05.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/SL_Yala_NP_BlockV_asv2020-01_img05.jpg/220px-SL_Yala_NP_BlockV_asv2020-01_img05.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="275" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/SL_Yala_NP_BlockV_asv2020-01_img05.jpg/330px-SL_Yala_NP_BlockV_asv2020-01_img05.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/SL_Yala_NP_BlockV_asv2020-01_img05.jpg/440px-SL_Yala_NP_BlockV_asv2020-01_img05.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2400" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SL_Yala_NP_BlockV_asv2020-01_img05.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A brown fish owl of the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> <i>zeylonensis</i>, which is smaller and darker than other subspecies</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Brown_Fish_Owl_(8566795379).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Brown_Fish_Owl_%288566795379%29.jpg/220px-Brown_Fish_Owl_%288566795379%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="253" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Brown_Fish_Owl_%288566795379%29.jpg/330px-Brown_Fish_Owl_%288566795379%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Brown_Fish_Owl_%288566795379%29.jpg/440px-Brown_Fish_Owl_%288566795379%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="954" data-file-height="1096" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Brown_Fish_Owl_(8566795379).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A brown fish owl in India</div></div></div>
<p><i>Strix zeylonensis</i> was the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific name">scientific name</a> proposed by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Gmelin" title="Johann Friedrich Gmelin">Johann Friedrich Gmelin</a> in 1788 for a fish owl from <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In the 19th and 20th centuries, several brown fish owl <a href="/wiki/Zoological_specimen" title="Zoological specimen">specimens</a> were described:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Strix Leschenault</i> by <a href="/wiki/Coenraad_Jacob_Temminck" title="Coenraad Jacob Temminck">Coenraad Jacob Temminck</a> in 1820 was a fish owl collected in India by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Leschenault_de_La_Tour" title="Jean-Baptiste Leschenault de La Tour">Jean-Baptiste Leschenault de La Tour</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It was placed in <i><a href="/wiki/Ketupa" class="mw-redirect" title="Ketupa">Ketupa</a></i> proposed as <a href="/wiki/Genus_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Genus (biology)">generic</a> name by <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9-Primev%C3%A8re_Lesson" class="mw-redirect" title="René-Primevère Lesson">René-Primevère Lesson</a> in 1831 for fish owl <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> from <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a> and India. Lesson renamed it to <i>Ketupa Leschenaultii</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Ketupa semenowi</i> proposed by <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Zarudny" title="Nikolai Zarudny">Nikolai Zarudny</a> in 1905 were a male and a female fish owl from the <a href="/wiki/Zagros_Mountains" title="Zagros Mountains">Zagros Mountains</a> in western <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Ketupa ceylonensis orientalis</i> proposed by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Th%C3%A9odore_Delacour" title="Jean Théodore Delacour">Jean Théodore Delacour</a> in 1926 were two female specimens collected in <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> that slightly differed in colour from brown fish owls in Laos.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Results of a <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Phylogenetic">phylogenetic</a> analysis of nine <a href="/wiki/Horned_owl" title="Horned owl">horned owl</a> species indicate that <i>Ketupa</i> species form a <a href="/wiki/Monophyletic_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Monophyletic group">monophyletic group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Currently, the brown fish owl is classified in the genus <i>Ketupa</i> and four <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are recognised as valid taxa:<sup id="cite_ref-HBW2014_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW2014-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Sri Lankan brown fish owl (<i>K. zeylonensis zeylonensis</i>) is the smallest and darkest coloured subspecies with a <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> of 355 to 405&#160;mm (14.0 to 15.9&#160;in), a <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> of 85 to 90&#160;mm (3.3 to 3.5&#160;in), a <a href="/wiki/Tail" title="Tail">tail</a> of 177 to 206&#160;mm (7.0 to 8.1&#160;in), and a <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> of 42 to 48&#160;mm (1.7 to 1.9&#160;in). The wing length averages 92% shorter than in northern subspecies. One male was found to have weighed 1,100&#160;g (2.4&#160;lb).</li>
<li>Common brown fish owl (<i>K. z. leschenaultii</i>) occurs from the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> to Myanmar and Thailand. It is of medium hue with lighter markings than the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate subspecies</a>. It has a wing chord of 370 to 430&#160;mm (15 to 17&#160;in), a tail of 186 to 210&#160;mm (7.3 to 8.3&#160;in), a tarsus of 71 to 90&#160;mm (2.8 to 3.5&#160;in) and a bill of 49 to 52&#160;mm (1.9 to 2.0&#160;in). The wing chord of 16 males in India averaged 402&#160;mm (15.8&#160;in) and their bills averaged 49&#160;mm (1.9&#160;in) while 15 females averaged 395&#160;mm (15.6&#160;in) in wing chord and 51.5&#160;mm (2.03&#160;in) in bill length. A male was found to weigh 1,105&#160;g (2.436&#160;lb) and a female was found to weigh 1,308&#160;g (2.884&#160;lb).</li>
<li>Western brown fish owl (<i>K. z. semenowi</i>) occurs from southern Turkey through Iran and Pakistan to <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_India" class="mw-redirect" title="Northeastern India">northeastern India</a>. It is paler and slightly larger than the common brown fish owl with a somewhat tawny hue. Its wing chord is 396 to 434&#160;mm (15.6 to 17.1&#160;in) with a tarsus of 74 to 90&#160;mm (2.9 to 3.5&#160;in), a tail of 197 to 214&#160;mm (7.8 to 8.4&#160;in) and a bill of 49 to 54&#160;mm (1.9 to 2.1&#160;in).</li>
<li>Eastern brown fish owl (<i>K. z. orientalis</i>) occurs in north-eastern Myanmar, Vietnam and southeastern China and <a href="/wiki/Hainan_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Hainan Island">Hainan Island</a>. It is somewhat darker than the common brown fish owl with a larger tarsus. Its wing chord is 365 to 457&#160;mm (14.4 to 18.0&#160;in), with a tail of 195 to 210&#160;mm (7.7 to 8.3&#160;in) and a tarsus of 67 to 70&#160;mm (2.6 to 2.8&#160;in).</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_records">Fossil records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown_fish_owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fossil records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric" class="mw-redirect" title="Prehistoric">prehistoric</a> times, this species may have been present across the central and eastern <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> basin, in particular on islands. The <a href="/wiki/Late_Pleistocene" title="Late Pleistocene">Late Pleistocene</a> <i>Bubo insularis</i> is typically considered to include the fragmentary remains originally described as <i>Ophthalmomegas lamarmorae</i> due to a mix-up with the <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> <a href="/wiki/Macaque" title="Macaque">macaque</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Macaca_majori" title="Macaca majori">Macaca majori</a></i> and subsequently unstudied for many decades. Its fossil bones suggest a bird the size of a large <a href="/wiki/Spotted_eagle-owl" title="Spotted eagle-owl">spotted eagle-owl</a> (<i>B. africanus</i>), a bit smaller still than the smallest living fish owls. It was certainly smallish but long-legged by eagle-owl standards, and its wing proportions differed conspicuously from a typical horned eagle owl. On the other hand, its leg and foot bones were more similar to those of a typical eagle-owl. Some consider it a specialized <a href="/wiki/Paleosubspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleosubspecies">paleosubspecies</a> of the brown fish owl.<sup id="cite_ref-Mourer-Chauviré1986_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mourer-Chauviré1986-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mlíkovský2003_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mlíkovský2003-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
Its oldest remains date back at least to the <a href="/wiki/Early_Pliocene" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Pliocene">Early Pliocene</a>, about 5 <a href="/wiki/Million_years_ago" class="mw-redirect" title="Million years ago">million years ago</a>. It was probably widely distributed around 120,000 years ago. After the onset of the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a>, less than 100,000 years ago, it disappeared from the western part of its range. The <a href="/wiki/Late_Miocene" title="Late Miocene">Late Miocene</a>-Early Pliocene <i>"<a href="/wiki/Strix_(genus)" title="Strix (genus)">Strix</a>" perpasta</i> is unlikely to belong in that genus, and also sometimes merged with <i>B. insularis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mlíkovský2003_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mlíkovský2003-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown_fish_owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The brown fish owl has prominent ear tufts and rufous brown upperparts that are heavily streaked with black or dark brown. Its underparts are buffy-fulvous to whitish, with wavy dark brown streaks and finer brown barring. Its throat is white and conspicuously puffed. Its facial disk is indistinct, the bill dark and the <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">iris</a> golden yellow. Its featherless feet are yellow. Two-year old brown fish owls are somewhat paler than adults. Female and male differ slightly in size.<sup id="cite_ref-Mikkola_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mikkola-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In body size, it ranges from 48 to 61&#160;cm (19 to 24&#160;in) with a wingspan from 125 to 140&#160;cm (49 to 55&#160;in). Its weight varies considerably, ranging from 1.1 to 2.5&#160;kg (2.4 to 5.5&#160;lb). Some of the variability is attributed to the range of sizes across the subspecies. Also, females are invariably at least somewhat larger than males and condition of birds is variable.<sup id="cite_ref-gw9905_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gw9905-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It is slightly larger than the <a href="/wiki/Buffy_fish_owl" title="Buffy fish owl">buffy fish owl</a> with a darker brown hue.<sup id="cite_ref-Konig_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Konig-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Voous_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voous-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Compared to eagle owls of similar length, fish owls tend to be even shorter in tail length and even heavier in build, have relatively larger wings, have considerably longer legs, and have a rough texture to the bottom of their toes. At least the latter two features are clear adaptations to aid these owls in capturing fish. Diurnal raptors who feed largely on fish have similar, if not identical, rough texture under their toes, which helps these birds grasp slippery fish. Unlike diurnal raptors who capture fish such as the osprey (<i>Pandion haliaetus</i>) as compared to most terrestrial raptors, the fish owls have large, powerful, and curved talons and a longitudinal sharp keel sitting under the middle claw with all having sharp cutting edges that are very much like those of eagle owls. Also, unlike fish-eating diurnal raptors will not submerge any part of their body while hunting, preferring only to put their feet into the water, although fish owls will hunt on foot, wading into the shallows. Unlike most owls, the feathers of fish owls are not soft to the touch and they lack the comb and hair-like fringes to the primaries, which allow other owls to fly silently in order to ambush their prey. Due to the lack of these feather-specializations, fish owl wing beats make sounds. The brown fish owl in particular is said to have a noisy wing beat, sometimes described as producing a singing sound, but another description claimed they could be "as silent as any other owl" in flight. The lack of a deep facial disc in fish owls is another indication of the unimportance of sound relative to vision in these owls, as facial disc depth (as well as inner ear size) are directly related to how important sound is to an owl's hunting behavior. Also different from most any other kind of owl, the bill is placed on the face between the eyes rather below it, which is said to impart this fish owl with a "remarkably morose and sinister expression". Similar adaptations, such as unwillingness to submerge beyond their legs and lack of sound-muffling feathers are also seen in the African fishing owls, which do not seem to be directly related. The brown fish owl has sometimes been regarded as <a href="/wiki/Biological_specificity" title="Biological specificity">conspecific</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Blakiston%27s_fish_owl" title="Blakiston&#39;s fish owl">Blakiston's fish owl</a> (<i>B. blakistoni</i>), but there is an approximately 2,000&#160;km (1,200&#160;mi) gap in their distributions, not to mention a large number of physical differences not the least of which is the Blakiston's considerably greater size.<sup id="cite_ref-Voous_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voous-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Konig_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Konig-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown_fish_owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:An_adult_Brown_Fish_Owl_at_Barpeta,_Assam_by_Hedayeat_Ullah.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/An_adult_Brown_Fish_Owl_at_Barpeta%2C_Assam_by_Hedayeat_Ullah.jpg/220px-An_adult_Brown_Fish_Owl_at_Barpeta%2C_Assam_by_Hedayeat_Ullah.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/An_adult_Brown_Fish_Owl_at_Barpeta%2C_Assam_by_Hedayeat_Ullah.jpg/330px-An_adult_Brown_Fish_Owl_at_Barpeta%2C_Assam_by_Hedayeat_Ullah.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/An_adult_Brown_Fish_Owl_at_Barpeta%2C_Assam_by_Hedayeat_Ullah.jpg/440px-An_adult_Brown_Fish_Owl_at_Barpeta%2C_Assam_by_Hedayeat_Ullah.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3183" data-file-height="2275" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:An_adult_Brown_Fish_Owl_at_Barpeta,_Assam_by_Hedayeat_Ullah.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An adult Brown Fish Owl at Barpeta Assam by Hedayeat Ullah</div></div></div>
<p>The brown fish owl is an all-year resident throughout most tropical and subtropical parts of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Subcontinent" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian Subcontinent">Indian Subcontinent</a> to Southeast Asia and adjoining regions. West of its main range, it is patchily distributed to the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> (possibly extinct) and southern <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a> (recently rediscovered). The typical habitat of brown fish owls is forest and woodland bordering streams, lakes or rice fields. It inhabits mainly the lowlands, from open woodland to dense forest as well as in plantations; in the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> <a href="/wiki/Foothills" title="Foothills">foothills</a> it ranges into <a href="/wiki/Submontane" class="mw-redirect" title="Submontane">submontane</a> forest up to 1,500&#160;m (4,900&#160;ft) <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Above mean sea level">above mean sea level</a> or so but not higher. It frequently spends the day in stands of bamboo or other large shady trees. They be found around water reservoirs, along canals, on the outskirts of villages and along sea coasts. Western birds are found in <a href="/wiki/Semiarid" class="mw-redirect" title="Semiarid">semiarid</a> landscape and may breed in <a href="/wiki/Oases" class="mw-redirect" title="Oases">oases</a> in <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> regions. Regardless of habitat, it rarely strays far from larger bodies of water such as rivers and lakes.<sup id="cite_ref-gw9905_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gw9905-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Voous_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voous-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
Being a large <a href="/wiki/Predator" class="mw-redirect" title="Predator">predatory</a> bird, the brown fish owl is only rarely found at a high <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a>, an exception being Sri Lanka, where this particular owl's adaptability to human habitat change has been beneficial in continued high numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-Mikkola_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mikkola-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown_fish_owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:KetupaFoot.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/KetupaFoot.png/220px-KetupaFoot.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="228" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/KetupaFoot.png/330px-KetupaFoot.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/KetupaFoot.png/440px-KetupaFoot.png 2x" data-file-width="596" data-file-height="617" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:KetupaFoot.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Foot showing <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adaptations</a> to catch fish</div></div></div>
<p>The calls of the buffy fish owl are described as a deep humming <i>boom-uh-boom</i>, a <i>hup hup hu</i> and a loud <i>huhuhuhuhuhuhu</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW2014_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW2014-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Grimmett_al2016_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grimmett_al2016-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> A trisyllabic <i>tu-hoo-hoo</i> is seemingly the territorial song emitted before breeding. Its call has been described as comparable to that of a distant <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_bittern" title="Eurasian bittern">Eurasian bittern</a> (<i>Botaurus stellaris</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The buffy fish owl is <a href="/wiki/Nocturnal" class="mw-redirect" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a> but can often be located by the small birds that <a href="/wiki/Mobbing_behavior" class="mw-redirect" title="Mobbing behavior">mob</a> it while roosting in a tree. However, in some areas it may be semi-diurnal and has been seen hunting during daytime, especially in cloudy weather. Brown fish owl primarily hunt by stationing itself on a rock overhang or hanging perch over water, or by wading into shallow waters. It grabs food by gliding over the water, nearly skimming it with its feet and grabbing its prey by quickly extending its long legs. It feeds mainly on <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fishes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_animal" title="Aquatic animal">aquatic</a> <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, especially <i><a href="/wiki/Potamon" title="Potamon">Potamon</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a>. It usually selects the larger freshwater fish available in waterways. Compared to the tawny fish owl, which prefers flowing waters, brown fish owls frequently hunt in still or stagnant waters. By number in the <a href="/wiki/Melghat" title="Melghat">Melghat Tiger Reserve</a> in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_crab" title="Freshwater crab">freshwater crabs</a> of the family <a href="/wiki/Gecarcinucidae" title="Gecarcinucidae">Gecarcinucidae</a> (of genus <i>Barytelphusa</i>) almost totally dominated the diet.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Brown fish owls may be attracted to ornamental fish ponds or commercial fisheries in order to exploit the easily caught fish at such locations. <a href="/wiki/Amniote" title="Amniote">Amniotes</a>, in particular <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_animal" title="Terrestrial animal">terrestrial</a> ones, are seldom taken. However, other recorded foods have included <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_beetle" title="Water beetle">water beetles</a>, other <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a>) and occasionally <a href="/wiki/Water_bird" title="Water bird">water birds</a>. In Melghat, the largest biomass of food consisted of small mammals, namely <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a> (<i>Rattus</i> ssp.), other types of <a href="/wiki/Muridae" title="Muridae">murids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asian_house_shrew" title="Asian house shrew">Asian house shrews</a> (<i>Suncus murinus</i>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Birds hunted by brown fish owls have including <a href="/wiki/Lesser_whistling_duck" title="Lesser whistling duck">lesser whistling duck</a> (<i>Dendrocygna javanica</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Indian_pond_heron" title="Indian pond heron">Indian pond heron</a> (<i>Ardeola grayii</i>). One unusual prey item recorded was a 28&#160;cm (11&#160;in) long <a href="/wiki/Monitor_lizard" title="Monitor lizard">monitor lizard</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Konig_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Konig-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Competition may occur between this species and <a href="/wiki/Pallas%27s_fish_eagle" title="Pallas&#39;s fish eagle">Pallas's fish eagles</a> (<i>Haliaeetus leucoryphus</i>) as well as <a href="/wiki/Dusky_eagle_owl" class="mw-redirect" title="Dusky eagle owl">dusky eagle owls</a> (<i>B. coromandus</i>), but the brown fish owl is more terrestrial than the fish eagle and consumes more <a href="/wiki/Invertebrates" class="mw-redirect" title="Invertebrates">invertebrates</a> than either of those species, the eagle feeding mainly on fish followed by water birds and the eagle owl feeding mainly on mammals followed by land birds. If hungry, brown fish owls will scavenge <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">carrion</a>, a rare behavior for owls. A case where a putrefying <a href="/wiki/Crocodile" title="Crocodile">crocodile</a> (<i>Crocodylus</i> ssp.) carcass was consumed by this species was observed.<sup id="cite_ref-Voous_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voous-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Konig_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Konig-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gw9905_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gw9905-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As mentioned above, the <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric" class="mw-redirect" title="Prehistoric">prehistoric</a> <i>B. insularis</i> is sometimes included in the brown fish owl. If this is correct, the different foot <a href="/wiki/Anatomy" title="Anatomy">anatomy</a>, more similar to that of a typical eagle-owl, would imply that the population had shifted back to terrestrial prey. A likely prey item in this case would have been the <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_pika" title="Sardinian pika">Sardinian pika</a> (<i>Prolagus sardus</i>). It has been conjectured that the owls disappeared with their prey due to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_(general_concept)" class="mw-redirect" title="Climate change (general concept)">climate change</a>, but the giant pikas of Sardinia and <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a> still existed around 1750, finally succumbing to <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a>, introduced predatory mammals and overhunting soon thereafter.<sup id="cite_ref-Mourer-Chauviré1986_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mourer-Chauviré1986-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown_fish_owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Brown fish owls breed from November to April, with activity tending to peak a bit earlier in the north of their breeding range and a bit later in the more tropical south of their breeding range. The breeding season is concurrent with the dry season, which has the benefit of low water levels and thus crabs and fish being more readily assessible. However, most other owls and diurnal raptors also primarily breed in the dry season as well. As owls do not build nest, brown fish owls are somewhat opportunistic when it comes to nesting sites. Brown fish owls frequently nest in shady spots such as old-growth <a href="/wiki/Mangifera" title="Mangifera">mango trees</a> (<i>Mangifera</i> ssp.), <a href="/wiki/Ficus" title="Ficus">fig trees</a>, including <i><a href="/wiki/Ficus_religiosa" title="Ficus religiosa">Ficus religiosa</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Ficus_benghalensis" title="Ficus benghalensis">Ficus benghalensis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Shorea_robusta" title="Shorea robusta">Shorea robusta</a></i> and other large trees in lowland forests. Outside of the bare surface of large branches, nests are often in spots such as overgrown eroded ravines and steep riverbanks with natural holes. However, it may also nest near suitable villages, along wet roadsides, <i>jheels</i>, canals and rice fields. Usually nest are large natural holes, in hollows or at the base of large branches. Deserted nest built by fish eagles and <a href="/wiki/Old_World_vulture" title="Old World vulture">vultures</a> are also sometimes used as nesting locations. Other nesting sites have included rock ledges, caves in shady cliff faces and stone ruins. The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is two or occasionally just one egg. 10 eggs in India averaged 58.4&#160;mm ×&#160;48.9&#160;mm (2.30&#160;in ×&#160;1.93&#160;in) in sizes, with eggs of fish owls to the north averaging slightly larger. Incubation is 38 days or somewhat less, and the young <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> after about seven weeks. The downy chicks are mostly an off-white color and develop into a paler version of adults by their second year.<sup id="cite_ref-Voous_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voous-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Konig_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Konig-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gw9905_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gw9905-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mikkola_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mikkola-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats">Threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown_fish_owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Apparently in India, during the festival of <a href="/wiki/Diwali" title="Diwali">Diwali</a>, thousands of owls are killed - though programs to counter this practice are underway -   including this species due to superstitious beliefs about the evil nature of owls and as an attempt to gain the power of black magic.<sup id="cite_ref-Mikkola_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mikkola-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">Habitat destruction</a> will eventually cause the species to desert a region. Due to this, it seems to be extinct as a breeding bird in Israel nowadays, attributable largely to damming practices leading to drying up of many waterways. In Israel, it was decimated by the use of the <a href="/wiki/Rodenticide" title="Rodenticide">rodenticide</a> <a href="/wiki/Thallium(I)_sulfate" title="Thallium(I) sulfate">Thallium(I) sulfate</a>, which in addition to potential direct exposure through rodents also poisons surface waters.<sup id="cite_ref-Voous_16-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voous-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
