<p>The <b>Brown Fish Owl</b> (<i>Bubo zeylonensis</i> or <i>Ketupa zeylonensis</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owl</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> is a part of the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Typical_owl" title="Typical owl" class="mw-redirect">typical owls</a>, Strigidae, which contains most living owls. It inhabits the warm <a href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical" class="mw-redirect">subtropical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Humid" title="Humid" class="mw-redirect">humid</a> <a href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical" class="mw-redirect">tropical</a> parts of continental <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and some offshore islands.<sup id="cite_ref-grimmeteetal1999_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grimmeteetal1999-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The four fish owls were previously generally separated in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Ketupa" title="Ketupa" class="mw-redirect">Ketupa</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA" class="mw-redirect">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome <i>b</i></a> <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">sequence</a> data is equivocal on which genus name is applied for them, and today they are commonly <a href="/wiki/Lumpers_and_splitters" title="Lumpers and splitters">lumped</a> with the horned and eagle-owls (<i><a href="/wiki/Bubo_(genus)" title="Bubo (genus)" class="mw-redirect">Bubo</a></i>) – which they also resemble <a href="/wiki/Osteological" title="Osteological" class="mw-redirect">osteologically</a> very much – for sake of convenience. Depending on whether some little-studied tropical eagle-owls are closer to the fish-owls than to the typical eagle-owls, <i>Ketupa</i> might be a valid genus if these as well as the <a href="/wiki/Fishing_owl" title="Fishing owl">fishing owls</a> (formerly <i>Scotopelia</i>) are included in it.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown_Fish_Owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>It is a large <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owl</a> with prominent "ear" tufts, typically around 55 centimetres (22&#160;in) in length and weighing 2–2.5 kilograms (4.4–5.5 lb) when fully grown. <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">Subspecies</a> differ in size and males are smaller than females, with the smallest birds not quite 50&#160;cm (20&#160;in) long and weighing as little as 1,100 grams (39&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-gw9905_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gw9905-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The upperparts are reddish brown and heavily streaked with black or dark brown. The underparts are buff to whitish, with dark streaks and finer brown barring. The throat is white and can be conspicuously puffed, while the facial disk is indistinct. The <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">irides</a> are yellow, the feet a duller yellow, and the bill is dark. Sexes do not differ in appearance except for size.<sup id="cite_ref-gw9905_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gw9905-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Its calls are described as a deep <i>tu-hoo-hoo</i> or a soft <i>huphuphuphuphuphup</i> or a loud <i>huhuhuhuhuhuhu</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-gw9905_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gw9905-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown_Fish_Owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span></h3>
<p>Three or four living <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are accepted today:<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>B. z. zeylonensis</i> <small>(Gmelin, 1788)</small> – <b>Sri Lankan Brown Fish Owl</b>. <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Small and dark.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>B. z. leschenault</i><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> <small>(<a href="/wiki/Coenraad_Jacob_Temminck" title="Coenraad Jacob Temminck">Temminck</a>, 1820)</small> – <b>Common Brown Fish Owl</b>. <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> to <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar" class="mw-redirect">Myanmar</a> (except NE) and <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>. Might include <i>orientalis</i>.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Larger and lighter than <i>zeylonensis</i>.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>B. z. semenowi</i> <small>(Zarudny, 1905)</small> – <b>Western Brown Fish Owl</b>. <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> (possibly extinct) and S <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a> through <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> (no up-to-date observations) to <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Paler than <i>leschenault</i>. Possibly a separate species.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i>B. z. orientalis</i> <small><a href="/wiki/Jean_Theodore_Delacour" title="Jean Theodore Delacour" class="mw-redirect">Delacour</a>, 1926<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material in the vicinity of this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s) from June 2009">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></small> – <b>Eastern Brown Fish Owl</b>. NE Myanmar, <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> and SE <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Doubtfully distinct from <i>leschenault</i>.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Darker than <i>leschenault</i>.</dd>
</dl>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric" title="Prehistoric" class="mw-redirect">prehistoric</a> times, this species may have been present across the central and eastern <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a> basin, in particular on islands. The <a href="/wiki/Late_Pleistocene" title="Late Pleistocene">Late Pleistocene</a> <i>Bubo insularis</i> is typically considered to include the fragmentary remains originally described as <i>Ophthalmomegas lamarmorae</i> due to a mix-up with the <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> <a href="/wiki/Macaque" title="Macaque">macaque</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Macaca_majori" title="Macaca majori">Macaca majori</a></i> and subsequently unstudied for many decades. Its fossil bones suggest a bird the size of a large <a href="/wiki/Spotted_Eagle-Owl" title="Spotted Eagle-Owl">Spotted Eagle-Owl</a> (<i>B. africanus</i>), a bit smaller still than the smallest living Brown Fish Owls. It was certainly smallish but long-legged by eagle-owl standards, and its wing proportions differed conspicuously from a typical <i><a href="/wiki/Bubo_(genus)" title="Bubo (genus)" class="mw-redirect">Bubo</a></i>. On the other hand, its leg and foot bones were more similar to those of a typical eagle-owl. Some consider them a specialized <a href="/wiki/Paleosubspecies" title="Paleosubspecies" class="mw-redirect">paleosubspecies</a> of the Brown Fish Owl:<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>B. z. insularis</i><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> <small>Mourer-Chauviré &amp; Weesie, 1986</small></li>
</ul>
<p>Its oldest remains date back at least to the <a href="/wiki/Early_Pliocene" title="Early Pliocene" class="mw-redirect">Early Pliocene</a>, about 5 <a href="/wiki/Million_years_ago" title="Million years ago" class="mw-redirect">million years ago</a> (Ma). It was widely distributed around 120,000 years ago. After the onset of the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a>, less than 100,000 years ago, this population disappeared from the western part of its range, while in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> <i>semenowi</i> – <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> bones indistinguishable from which are known since about the <a href="/wiki/Gelasian" title="Gelasian">Gelasian</a>, c. 2 Ma – would have subsumed any remnants of the eastern Mediterranean population. The <a href="/wiki/Late_Miocene" title="Late Miocene">Late Miocene</a>-Early Pliocene <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxon</a> <i>"<a href="/wiki/Strix_(genus)" title="Strix (genus)">Strix</a>" perpasta</i> is unlikely to belong in that genus, and also sometimes merged with <i>B. (z.) insularis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown_Fish_Owl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:KetupaFoot.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/KetupaFoot.png/220px-KetupaFoot.png" width="220" height="228" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/KetupaFoot.png/330px-KetupaFoot.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/KetupaFoot.png/440px-KetupaFoot.png 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:KetupaFoot.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Foot showing <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adaptations</a> to catch fish.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This species is an all-year resident throughout most tropical and subtropical parts of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Subcontinent" title="Indian Subcontinent" class="mw-redirect">Indian Subcontinent</a> to <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> and southeastern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>; it is also found on <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>. West of its main range, it is patchily distributed to the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> (possibly extinct) and southern <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a> (recently rediscovered). It inhabits mainly the lowlands, in well-wooded <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a>, from open woodland to dense forest as well as in plantations; in the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> <a href="/wiki/Foothills" title="Foothills">foothills</a> it ranges into <a href="/wiki/Submontane" title="Submontane" class="mw-redirect">submontane</a> forest up to 1,500 m <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" title="Above mean sea level">ASL</a> or so but not higher. Western birds are found in <a href="/wiki/Semiarid" title="Semiarid" class="mw-redirect">semiarid</a> landscape and may breed in <a href="/wiki/Oases" title="Oases" class="mw-redirect">oases</a> in <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> regions. Regardless of habitat, it rarely strays far from larger bodies of water such as rivers and lakes.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species is very <a href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal" class="mw-redirect">nocturnal</a> but it can often be located by the small birds that <a href="/wiki/Mobbing_behavior" title="Mobbing behavior" class="mw-redirect">mob</a> it while it is roosting in a tree. It feeds mainly on <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fishes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_animal" title="Aquatic animal">aquatic</a> <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>; <a href="/wiki/Amniote" title="Amniote">amniotes</a>, in particular <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_animal" title="Terrestrial animal">terrestrial</a> ones, are seldom taken. If hungry, Brown Fish Owls will scavenge <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">carrion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gw9905_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gw9905-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>As mentioned above, the <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric" title="Prehistoric" class="mw-redirect">prehistoric</a> <i>B. insularis</i> is sometimes included in the Brown Fish Owl. If this is correct, the different foot <a href="/wiki/Anatomy" title="Anatomy">anatomy</a>, more similar to that of a typical eagle-owl, would imply that the population had shifted back to terrestrial prey. A likely prey item in this case would have been the <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_Pika" title="Sardinian Pika" class="mw-redirect">Sardinian Pika</a> (<i>Prolagus sardus</i>). It has been conjectured that the owls disappeared with their prey due to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, but the giant pikas of Sardinia and <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a> still existed around 1750, finally succumbing to <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a>, introduced predatory mammals and overhunting soon thereafter.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Brown Fish Owls breed from November to March. The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is one or two eggs, often placed in an old stick nest of other birds, otherwise in a rock crevice or similar. Incubation is 38 days or somewhat less, and the young <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> after about 7 weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-gw9905_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gw9905-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Brown Fish Owl is not considered a <a href="/wiki/Threatened_species" title="Threatened species">threatened species</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN" class="mw-redirect">IUCN</a>. Being a large <a href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator" class="mw-redirect">predatory</a> bird, it is only rarely found at a high <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a>. <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">Habitat destruction</a> will eventually cause the species to desert a region, and probably because of this (in addition to use of certain <a href="/wiki/Rodenticides" title="Rodenticides" class="mw-redirect">rodenticides</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> it seems to be extinct as a breeding bird in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> nowadays.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
