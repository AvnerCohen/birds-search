<p>The <b>Bearded Vulture</b> (<i>Gypaetus barbatus</i>), also known as the <b>Lammergeier</b> or <b>Lammergeyer</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a>, and the only member of the genus <i>Gypaetus</i>. Traditionally considered an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_vulture" title="Old World vulture">Old World vulture</a>, it actually forms a minor lineage of <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a> together with the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_Vulture" title="Egyptian Vulture">Egyptian Vulture</a> (<i>Neophron percnopterus</i>), its closest living relative. They are not much more closely related to the Old World vultures proper than to, for example, <a href="/wiki/Hawk" title="Hawk">hawks</a>, and differ from the former by their feathered neck. Although dissimilar, Egyptian and Bearded Vultures both have a <a href="/wiki/Lozenge" title="Lozenge">lozenge</a>-shaped tail that is unusual among birds of prey.</p>
<p>It eats mainly carrion and lives and breeds on crags in high mountains in southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili5_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili5-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili6_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili6-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili8_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili8-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">Southern Africa</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> Subcontinent, and <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>, laying one or two <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> in mid-winter which hatch at the beginning of spring. Populations are resident.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>The Bearded Vulture is sparsely distributed across a considerable range. It may be found in mountainous regions from <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> through much of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. In Eurasia, its found in the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> region, the <a href="/wiki/Zagros_Mountains" title="Zagros Mountains">Zagros Mountains</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Alborz" title="Alborz">Alborzs</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Altai_Mountains" title="Altai Mountains">Altai Mountains</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>, western and central <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a>. In Africa, it is found in the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas Mountains</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Highlands" title="Ethiopian Highlands">Ethiopian Highlands</a> and down from <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> to northeastern <a href="/wiki/Zaire" title="Zaire">Zaire</a>, central <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>. An isolated population inhabits the <a href="/wiki/Drakensberg" title="Drakensberg">Drakensberg</a> of <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species is almost entirely associated with mountains and <a href="/wiki/Monadnock" title="Monadnock">inselbergs</a> with plentiful cliffs, crags, precipices, canyons and gorges. They are often found near <a href="/wiki/Transhumance_in_the_Alps" title="Transhumance in the Alps">alpine pastures</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alpine_tundra" title="Alpine tundra">meadows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montane_grasslands_and_shrublands" title="Montane grasslands and shrublands">montane grassland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heath" title="Heath">heath</a>, steep-sided, rocky <a href="/wiki/Wadi" title="Wadi">wadis</a>, high <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a> and are occasional around <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a>. They seem to prefer desolate, lightly-populated areas where predators who provide many bones, such as <a href="/wiki/Wolves" title="Wolves" class="mw-redirect">wolves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle" class="mw-redirect">Golden eagles</a>, have healthy populations. In <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, they are now common at refuse tips on the outskirts of small villages and towns. Although they occasionally descend to 300–600 m (980–2,000 ft), Bearded Vultures are rare below an elevation of 1,000&#160;m (3,300&#160;ft) and normally reside above 2,000&#160;m (6,600&#160;ft) in some parts of their range. They are typically found around or above the <a href="/wiki/Tree_line" title="Tree line">tree line</a> which are often near the tops of the mountains, at up to 2,000&#160;m (6,600&#160;ft) in Europe, 4,500&#160;m (14,800&#160;ft) in Africa and 5,000&#160;m (16,000&#160;ft) in central Asia. They even have been observed living at altitudes of 7,500&#160;m (24,600&#160;ft) on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a> and been observed flying at a height of 24,000&#160;ft (7,300&#160;m).<sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili5_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili5-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili6_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili6-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili8_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili8-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>This bird is 94–125 cm (37–49 in) long with a <a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">wingspan</a> of 2.31–2.83 m (7.6–9.3 ft).<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> It weighs 4.5–7.8 kg (9.9–17 lb), with the nominate race averaging 6.21&#160;kg (13.7&#160;lb) and <i>G. b. meridionalis</i> of Africa averaging 5.7&#160;kg (13&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> In Eurasia, vultures found around the Himalayas tend to be slightly larger than those from other mountain ranges.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Females are slightly larger than males.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> It is essentially unmistakable with other vultures or indeed other birds in flight due to its long, narrow wings, with the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> measuring 71.5–91 cm (28.1–36 in), and long, wedge-shaped tail, which measures 42.7–52 cm (16.8–20 in) in length. The tarsus is relatively small for the bird's size, at 8.8–10 cm (3.5–3.9 in). The proportions of the species have been compared to a <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon</a>, upgraded to an enormous size.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike most vultures, the Bearded Vulture does not have a bald head. This species is relatively small headed, although its neck is powerful and thick. It has a generally elongated, slender shape, sometimes appearing bulkier due to the often hunched back of these birds. The gait on the ground is wattling and the feet are large and powerful. The adult is mostly dark gray, rusty and whitish in color. It is gray-blue to gray-black above. The creamy-colored forehead contrasts against a black band across the eyes and lores and bristles under the chin, which form a black beard that give the species its English name. Bearded Vultures are variably orange or rust on their head, breast and leg feathers but this is actually cosmetic. This coloration may come from dust-bathing, rubbing mud on its body or from drinking in mineral-rich waters. The tail feathers and wings are gray. The juvenile bird is dark black-brown over most of the body, with a buff-brown breast and takes five years to reach full maturity. The Lammergeier is silent, apart from shrill whistles in their breeding displays and a falcon-like <i>cheek-acheek</i> call made around the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lammergeier_with_boy,_Kabul,_1973.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/af/Lammergeier_with_boy%2C_Kabul%2C_1973.JPG/170px-Lammergeier_with_boy%2C_Kabul%2C_1973.JPG" width="170" height="237" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/af/Lammergeier_with_boy%2C_Kabul%2C_1973.JPG/255px-Lammergeier_with_boy%2C_Kabul%2C_1973.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/af/Lammergeier_with_boy%2C_Kabul%2C_1973.JPG/340px-Lammergeier_with_boy%2C_Kabul%2C_1973.JPG 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lammergeier_with_boy,_Kabul,_1973.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Boy with live Bearded Vulture, <a href="/wiki/Kabul" title="Kabul">Kabul</a>, 1973.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Like other <a href="/wiki/Vulture" title="Vulture">vultures</a> it is a <a href="/wiki/Scavenger" title="Scavenger">scavenger</a>, feeding mostly on animal matter from <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">dead animals</a>. It usually disdains the actual meat, however, and lives on a diet that is 85-90% <a href="/wiki/Bone_marrow" title="Bone marrow">bone marrow</a>. The Lammergeier can swallow whole or bite through brittle bones up to the size of a lamb's femur<sup id="cite_ref-PBS-Bhutan_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PBS-Bhutan-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> and its powerful digestive system quickly dissolves even large pieces. The Lammergeier has learned to crack bones too large to be swallowed by carrying them in flight to a height of 50–150 m (160–490 ft) above the ground and then dropping them onto rocks below, which smashes them into smaller pieces and exposes the nutritious marrow.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> They can fly with bones up to 10&#160;cm (3.9&#160;in) in diameter and weighing over 4&#160;kg (8.8&#160;lb), or nearly equal to their own weight.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> After dropping the large bones, the Bearded Vulture spirals or glides to down to inspect them and may repeat the act if the bone is not sufficiently cracked.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> This learned skill requires extensive practice by immature birds and takes up to seven years to master.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC-BeardedVulture_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-BeardedVulture-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Its old name of <i>Ossifrage</i> ("bone breaker") relates to this habit. More seldomly, these birds have been observed to try to break bones (usually of a medium size) by hammering them with their bill directly into rocks while perched.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Live prey is sometimes attacked by the Bearded Vulture, with perhaps greater regularity than any other vulture.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Among these, especially <a href="/wiki/Tortoise" title="Tortoise">tortoises</a> are targeted depending on their local abundance. Tortoises predated may be nearly as large as the predating vulture. When killing tortoise, Bearded Vultures also fly to some height and drop them to crack open the bulky reptiles' hard shells. Golden eagles have been observed to kill tortoises in the same way.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Other live animals, up to nearly their own size, been observed to be predaceously seized and dropped in flight. Among these are <a href="/wiki/Rock_hyrax" title="Rock hyrax">rock hyraxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hare" title="Hare">hares</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marmot" title="Marmot">marmots</a> and, in one case, a 62&#160;cm (24&#160;in) long <a href="/wiki/Monitor_lizard" title="Monitor lizard">monitor lizard</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PBS-Bhutan_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PBS-Bhutan-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Larger animals have been known to be attacked by Bearded Vultures, including <a href="/wiki/Ibex" title="Ibex" class="mw-redirect">ibex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capra_(genus)" title="Capra (genus)"><i>Capra</i> goats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chamois" title="Chamois">Chamois</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steenbok" title="Steenbok">Steenbok</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> These animals have been killed by being surprised by the large birds and battered with wings until they fall off precipitious rocky edges to their deaths, although in some cases these may be accidental killings when both the vulture and the mammal surprise each other.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Many large animals killed by Bearded Vultures have appeared sickly or are obviously injured.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Humans have been anecdotedly reported to have been killed in the same way, although this (if it does happen) is generally agreed to be accidental on the part of the vulture.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Occasionally smaller ground-dwelling birds, such as <a href="/wiki/Partridge" title="Partridge">partridges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pigeon" title="Pigeon" class="mw-redirect">pigeons</a>, have been reported to eaten, sometimes as fresh carrion and sometimes killed with beating wings by the vulture.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> While foraging for bones or live prey while in flight, Bearded Vultures fly fairly low over the rocky ground, staying around 2 to 4 m (6.6 to 13 ft) high.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Occasionally, breeding pairs may forage and hunt together.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Highlands" title="Ethiopian Highlands">Ethiopian Highlands</a> only, Bearded Vultures have adapted to living largely off of human refuse.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Physiology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Physiology">Physiology</span></h2>
<p>The acid concentration of the Bearded Vulture stomach has been estimated to be of pH about 1 and large bones will be digested in about 24 hours, aided by slow mixing/churning of the stomach content. The high fat content of bone marrow makes the net energy value of bone almost as good as that of muscle, even if bone is less completely digested. A skeleton left on a mountain will dehydrate and become protected from bacterial degradation and the Bearded Vulture can return to consume the remainder of a carcass even months after the soft parts have been consumed by other animals, larvae and bacteria.<sup id="cite_ref-acid_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acid-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Life history">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Life_history">Life history</span></h2>
<p>The Bearded Vulture occupies an enormous territory year-around. It may forage over two square kilometers each day. The breeding period is variable, being December through September in <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>, November to June in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Subcontinent" title="Indian Subcontinent" class="mw-redirect">Indian Subcontinent</a>, October to May in <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, throughout the year in eastern Africa and May to January in southern Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Although generally solitary, the bond between a breeding pair is often considerably close. In seldom cases, <a href="/wiki/Polyandry" title="Polyandry">polyandry</a> has been recorded in the species.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The territorial and breeding display between Bearded Vultures is often spectacular, involving the showing of talons, tumbling and spiralling while flight and the large birds regularly lock feet with each other and fall some distance through the sky.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The nest is a massive pile of sticks, that goes from around 1&#160;m (3.3&#160;ft) across and 69&#160;cm (27&#160;in) deep when first constructed up to 2.5&#160;m (8.2&#160;ft) across and 1&#160;m (3.3&#160;ft) deep, with a covering of various animal matter from food, after repeated uses. The female usually lay a clutch of 1 to 2 eggs, though 3 have been recorded on rare occasions.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> which are incubated for 53 to 60 days. After hatching the young spend 100 to 130 days in the nest before <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledging</a>. The young may be dependent on the parents for up to 2 years, forcing the birds to regularly nest in alternate years.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Typically, the Bearded Vulture nests in caves and on ledges and rock outcrops or caves on steep rock walls, so are very difficult for nest-predating mammals to access.<sup id="cite_ref-PBS-Bhutan_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PBS-Bhutan-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span></h2>
<p>The Bearded Vulture is locally threatened. It naturally occurs at low densities, with anywhere from a dozen to 500 pairs now being found in each mountain range in Eurasia where the species breeds. The species is most common in <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, where an estimated 1,400 to 2,200 are believed to breed.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Relatively large, healthy numbers seem to occur in some parts of the Himalayas as well. It was largely wiped out in Europe by the beginning of the 20th century, but has been locally reintroduced and is beginning to re-establish itself in protected areas. The Bearded Vulture has been successfully reintroduced to the Pyrenees of <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Swiss" title="Swiss" class="mw-redirect">Swiss</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> Alps, with both populations have spread themselves over into <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> They have also declined somewhat in parts of Asia and Africa as well, though less severely than in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Declines today are usually due to poisons left out for carnivores, habitat degradation, the disturbances of nests, reduced food supplies and collisions with power lines.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> It was formerly persecuted in significant numbers because people feared (without justification) that it regularly carried off children and domestic animals; the bird was also hunted as a <a href="/wiki/Trophy_hunting" title="Trophy hunting">trophy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-howstuffworks_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-howstuffworks-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Despite the declines, the species clearly occupies a large range and, as such, it is listed as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">least concern</a> on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Probably less than 10,000 pairs exist in the wild worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_5-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_naturae" title="Systema naturae" class="mw-redirect">Systema naturae</a></i> in 1758 as <i>Vultur barbatus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Linnaeus1785_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linnaeus1785-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The present scientific name means "Bearded Vulture-Eagle".</p>
<p>The name of the Lammergeier originates from <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Lämmergeier</span></i>, which means "lamb-vulture" or "lamb-hawk". The name stems from the belief that it attacked <a href="/wiki/Domestic_sheep" title="Domestic sheep" class="mw-redirect">lambs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legend">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Legend">Legend</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greek</a> playwright <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus</a> was said to have been killed in 456 or 455 BC by a <a href="/wiki/Tortoise" title="Tortoise">tortoise</a> dropped by an eagle who mistook his bald head for a stone – if this incident did occur, the Lammergeier is a likely candidate for the "eagle".</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>/<a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a>, the bearded vulture, as the ossifrage, is among the birds forbidden to be eaten (<a href="/wiki/Leviticus" title="Leviticus" class="mw-redirect">Leviticus</a> 11:13).</p>
<p>More recently, in 1945, it is said that <a href="/wiki/Shimon_Peres" title="Shimon Peres">Shimon Peres</a> (called Shimon Persky at the time) and <a href="/wiki/David_Ben-Gurion" title="David Ben-Gurion">David Ben-Gurion</a> found a nest of Bearded Vultures in the <a href="/wiki/Negev" title="Negev">Negev</a> desert. The bird is called <i><span lang="he-Latn" xml:lang="he-Latn">peres</span></i> in <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>, and Shimon Persky liked it so much he adopted it as his <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Bearded Vulture is considered a threatened species in <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. Iranian mythology considers the rare Lammergeier the symbol of luck and happiness. It was believed that if the shadow of a <i><a href="/wiki/Huma_bird" title="Huma bird">Huma</a></i> fell on one, he would rise to sovereignty.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
