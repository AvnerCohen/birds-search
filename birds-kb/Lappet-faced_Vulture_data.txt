<p>The <b>Lappet-faced Vulture</b> or <b>Nubian Vulture</b> (<i>Torgos tracheliotos</i>) is a mostly <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a> <a href="/wiki/Old_World_vulture" title="Old World vulture">Old World vulture</a> belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> order <a href="/wiki/Accipitriformes" title="Accipitriformes">Accipitriformes</a>, which also includes <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kite_(bird)" title="Kite (bird)">kites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buzzard" title="Buzzard">buzzards</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hawk" title="Hawk">hawks</a>. It is the only member of the genus <i>Torgos</i>. The Lappet-faced Vulture was formerly considered monotypical, but now is separated into two subspecies. The nominate race lives throughout Africa. The subspecies <i>T. t. negevensis</i>, differing considerably in appearance from African vultures (as described below), occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Sinai_Peninsula" title="Sinai Peninsula">Sinai</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Negev_desert" title="Negev desert" class="mw-redirect">Negev desert</a>, and probably north-west <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>. It is not closely related to the superficially similar <a href="/wiki/New_World_vulture" title="New World vulture">New World vultures</a>, and does not share the good sense of smell of some members of that group.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Lappet-faced_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<p>This species is patchily distributed through much of Africa, though it is abscent from much of the central and western parts of the continent and declining elsewhere in its range. The Lappet-faced Vulture nests in <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burkina_Faso" title="Burkina Faso">Burkina Faso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, southeastern <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwanda</a>, easternmost part of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malawi" title="Malawi">Malawi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swaziland" title="Swaziland">Swaziland</a>, northeastern <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Gambia" title="The Gambia">The Gambia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guinea" title="Guinea">Guinea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">Ivory Coast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benin" title="Benin">Benin</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Republic" title="Central African Republic">Central African Republic</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a> and possibly in <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>. Across the sea, the species nests in <a href="/wiki/Arabia" title="Arabia" class="mw-redirect">Arabia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Lappet-faced_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span></h2>
<p>This vulture prefers to live in <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">dry savannah</a>, thornbush, arid plains, deserts with scattered trees in <a href="/wiki/Wadi" title="Wadi">wadis</a>, open mountain slopes. They are usually found in undisturbed open country with a scattering of trees and apparently prefer areas with minimal grass cover. While foraging, they can wander into denser habitats and even into human habitated areas, especially if drawn to road kills. They may be found in elevation from sea-level to 4,500&#160;m (14,800&#160;ft).</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Lappet-faced_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The Lappet-faced Vulture is a huge species, ranking as the longest and largest winged vulture in its range behind the closely related <a href="/wiki/Cinereous_Vulture" title="Cinereous Vulture">Cinereous Vulture</a>, although some co-occurring <i><a href="/wiki/Gyps" title="Gyps">Gyps</a></i> vultures tend to be heavier on average, especially the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Vulture" title="Cape Vulture">Cape Vulture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Griffon" title="Eurasian Griffon" class="mw-redirect">Eurasian Griffon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_0-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> This species measures around 95–115 cm (37–45 in) in body length, with a wingspan of 2.5–2.9 m (8.2–9.5 ft). Among the standard measurements, the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> is 71.5–82.5 cm (28.1–32.5 in), the <a href="/wiki/Tail" title="Tail">tail</a> is 33–36 cm (13–14 in) and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a> is 12.2–15 cm (4.8–5.9 in).<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The bill, at up to 10&#160;cm (3.9&#160;in) long and 5&#160;cm (2.0&#160;in) deep, ranks as one of the largest of any <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">accipitrid</a>, although a reported <a href="/wiki/Culmen_(bird)" title="Culmen (bird)" class="mw-redirect">culmen</a> length of 7.2&#160;cm (2.8&#160;in) is slightly less than the culmen length of the Cinereous Vulture.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_0-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Wild vultures, of the subspecies <i>T. t. tracheliotus</i>, range from 4.4 to 9.4 kg (9.7 to 21 lb) and, in East Africa, average only 6.2&#160;kg (14&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> On the other hand, captive vultures of the larger <i>T. t. negevensis</i> subspecies, weighed 6.5–9.2 kg (14–20 lb) in males and 10.5–13.6 kg (23–30 lb) in females.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_0-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Overall, the Lappet-faced Vulture is blackish above with a strongly contrasting white thigh feathers. The black feathers on the back of African vultures are lined with brown, while Arabian birds are dark brown rather than black above. The underside can range from pure white to buff-brown. Like many vultures, it has a bald head. The head coloration can range from reddish in southern Africa to dull pink in more northern Africa to pink on the back of the head and gray on the front in the Arabian Peninisula.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_0-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The combination of the colorful head and fleshy folds on the side of it are distinctive. The bald head of the Lappet-faced Vulture is advantageous, because a feathered head would become spattered with <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> and other fluids, and thus be difficult to keep clean. While flying, Lappet-faced Vultures have large, broad wings held with the front edges held parallel and slightly pointed, serrated-looking wingtips. Compared to the somewhat similarly-marked <a href="/wiki/Hooded_Vulture" title="Hooded Vulture">Hooded Vulture</a>, it is considerably larger with a more massive bill and can only be confused at a great distance. The <i>Gyps</i> vultures are generally much paler, with less white lining the wings and more bulging secondaries. The Cinereous Vulture (which may overlap in range in the Arabian area) has a similar body shape but is all dark, with no contrasting plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_0-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Lappet-faced_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Lappet-faced_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<p>The Lappet-faced Vulture is a scavenging bird, feeding mostly from <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a> carcasses, which it finds by sight or by watching other <a href="/wiki/Vulture" title="Vulture">vultures</a>. More so than many other African vultures, they often find carrion on their own and start tearing through the skin. They are the most powerful and aggressive of the <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a> <a href="/wiki/Vulture" title="Vulture">vultures</a>, and other vultures will usually cede a carcass to the Lappet-faced Vulture if the Lappet-faced decides to assert itself. This is often beneficial to the less powerful vultures because the Lappet-face can tear through the tough hides and knotty muscles of large <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> that the others cannot penetrate, although <a href="/wiki/Hyena" title="Hyena">hyenas</a> are even more efficient in this regard (if more voracious eaters). However, Lappet-faced Vultures frequently hang around the edges of the throngs at large carcasses, waiting until the other vultures are done, to feed on remnant skin, tendons and other coarse tissues that the others will not eat. Big game animals, up to the size of <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephants</a>, are preferred as carrion, since they provide the most subsistence at a sitting. A full crop can contain up to 1.5&#160;kg (3.3&#160;lb) of meat.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_0-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Perhaps more than any other true vulture, Lappet-faced Vultures have been recorded as regularly feeding on freshy-killed smaller mammals, birds and reptiles. Some of these are probably road-kills or are pirated from <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a> or other raptors but they are also believed to occasionally attack live animals, especially young and weak animals and the nests and young of other birds. <a href="/wiki/Flamingo" title="Flamingo">Flamingo</a> colonies (including eggs, young and adults), young <a href="/wiki/Impala" title="Impala">impalas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guineafowl" title="Guineafowl">guineafowl</a> have reportedly been predated. They are believed to still-hunt from an elevated perch and then drop on their prey, stunning them with the impact and tearing them apart with their strong bills. Most remains found at nests are from small animals possibly caught alive.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_0-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Lappet-faced_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Life History">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Life_History">Life History</span></h3>
<p>Lappet-faced Vultures are generally solitary birds. They do not nest in cohesive colonies as do many smaller vultures, with one tree or area usually only having 1 to 2 nests in it, though rarely up to 10 nests have been recorded in one area. The home range of a Lappet-faced Vulture is usually at least 8 to 15 km (5.0 to 9.3 mi).<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Groups of up to 25 to 50 Lappet-faces may congregate at large carcasses or watering holes, though typically only from 1 to as many as 7 turn up per carcass.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species nests in November through July in the north of its range, throughout the year in eastern Africa and May to January in southern Africa. The huge nest, a pile of neatly-formed sticks, measures 120–220 cm (47–87 in) across and 30–70 cm (12–28 in) deep. The nest is often lined with green leaves, as well as animal hair and skins. Nests are almost always placed in the main fork or top of an <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">Acacia</a></i> tree, though <i><a href="/wiki/Balanites" title="Balanites">Balanites</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Terminalia_(plant)" title="Terminalia (plant)">Terminalia</a></i> trees are sometimes also used, at 5 to 15 m (16 to 49 ft) off the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The clutch contains 1 or 2 eggs, which are incubated by both parents over the course of 54 to 56 days. The young fledge at 124 to 135 days old, although can be dependent on their parents for up to an age of 1 year or more, sometimes forcing parents to only nest in alternate years. There is a single remarkable record of a Lappet-faced Vulture pair successfully raising a <a href="/wiki/White-headed_Vulture" title="White-headed Vulture">White-headed Vulture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_0-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The Lappet-faced Vultures does not usually breed until it is around 6 years of age.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Lappet-faced_Vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h2>
<p>The Lappet-faced Vulture's world population has been believed to have decreased perceptiously. They are declining in <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a> and several parts of their southern, northern and western distribution in Africa. They are apparently currently stable in Arabia but have a small population there and have been expirated from <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> as a breeding bird. The declines are almost entirely due to human activities, including disturbances from habitat destruction and cultivation, disturbances at the nesting site (to which the species is reportedly quite sensitive) and ingestion of pesticides, which are usually set out for <a href="/wiki/Jackal" title="Jackal">jackals</a> and other small mammalian carnivores.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Domestic <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>, who have replaced natural prey over much of the range, are now often sold off, rather than abandoned, due to the proliferation of markets and abattoirs and rarely left to die and be consumed by vultures.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Lappet-faced Vultures are also sometimes victims of direct persecution, including shooting and the use of <a href="/wiki/Strychnine" title="Strychnine">strychnine</a> and other poisons. In <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a>, 86 Lappet-faced Vultures were poisoned at once via a group of cattle carcasses, because the farmers erroneously believed they were killing and eating the cattle. In some cases the poisoning is done by poachers, who fear the presence of vultures will alert authorities to their activities, the illegal killings of protected species.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> They are considered Vulnerable at the species level, with an estimated world population of under 9,000 individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-RaptorsWorld_0-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RaptorsWorld-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
