<p>The <b>brown-headed gull</b> (<i>Chroicocephalus brunnicephalus</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a> which breeds in the high plateaus of <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">central Asia</a> from <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a> to Ordos in Inner <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering on the coasts and large inland lakes of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Subcontinent" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian Subcontinent">Indian Subcontinent</a>. As is the case with many gulls, was traditionally placed in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Larus" title="Larus">Larus</a></i>.
</p><p>This gull breeds in colonies in large reedbeds or marshes, or on islands in lakes, nesting on the ground. Like most gulls, it is highly <a href="/wiki/Sociality" title="Sociality">gregarious</a> in winter, both when feeding or in evening roosts. It is not a <a href="/wiki/Pelagic_zone#Pelagic_birds" title="Pelagic zone">pelagic</a> species, and is rarely seen at sea far from coasts.
</p><p>This is a bold and opportunist feeder, which will scavenge in towns or take invertebrates in ploughed fields with equal relish.
</p><p>The brown-headed gull is slightly larger than <a href="/wiki/Black-headed_gull" title="Black-headed gull">black-headed gull</a>. The summer adult has a pale brown head, lighter than that of black-headed, a pale grey body, and red bill and legs. The black tips to the primary wing feathers have conspicuous white "mirrors". The underwing is grey with black flight feathers. The brown hood is lost in winter, leaving just dark vertical streaks.
</p><p>This bird takes two years to reach maturity. First year birds have a black terminal tail band, more dark areas in the wings, and, in summer, a less homogeneous hood.
</p><p><br />
This is a noisy species, especially at colonies.
</p>
