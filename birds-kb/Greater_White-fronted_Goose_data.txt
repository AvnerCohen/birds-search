<p>The <b>greater white-fronted goose</b> (<i>Anser albifrons</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Goose" title="Goose">goose</a> related to the smaller <a href="/wiki/Lesser_white-fronted_goose" title="Lesser white-fronted goose">lesser white-fronted goose</a> (<i>A. erythropus</i>). It is named for the patch of white feathers bordering the base of its bill, in fact <i>albifrons </i>comes from the Latin <i>albus</i> "white" and <i>frons</i>" forehead ".<sup id="cite_ref-job90_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job90-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  In Europe it has been known as the "<b>white-fronted goose</b>"; in North America it is known as the <b>greater white-fronted goose</b> (or "greater whitefront"), and this name is also increasingly adopted internationally.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Even more distinctive are the salt-and-pepper markings on the breast of adult birds, which is why the goose is colloquially called the <b>"specklebelly"</b> in North America.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_white-fronted_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Greater white-fronted geese are 64–81&#160;cm (25–32&#160;in) in length, have a 130–165&#160;cm (51–65&#160;in) wingspan and weigh 1.93–3.31&#160;kg (4&#160;lb 4&#160;oz–7&#160;lb 5&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-AAB_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAB-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CRC_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> They have bright orange legs and mouse-coloured upper wing-coverts. They are smaller than <a href="/wiki/Greylag_goose" title="Greylag goose">greylag geese</a>. As well as being larger than the lesser white-fronted goose, the greater white-fronted goose lacks the yellow eye-ring of that species, and the white facial blaze does not extend upwards so far as in lesser.<sup id="cite_ref-Perrins1987_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Perrins1987-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The male is typical larger in size, both sexes are similar in appearance—greyish brown birds with light grey breasts dappled with dark brown to black blotches and bars. Both males and females also have a pinkish bill and orange legs and feet.<sup id="cite_ref-DU_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DU-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Greater white-fronted geese make a variation of sounds, but notably the most recognizable is the high pitched cackle that can be imitated by the sounds "he-he." There is a distinct breaking of the note from the first cackle to the second.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Differences_between_European_and_Greenland_birds">Differences between European and Greenland birds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_white-fronted_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Differences between European and Greenland birds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The appearance of European or Russian white-fronted geese, of the race <i>albifrons</i> and Greenland white-fronted geese, of the race <i>flavirostris</i>, differ in a number of ways. The Greenland white-fronted goose, in all plumages, looks darker and more 'oily-looking' than the European white-fronted goose, both at rest and in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-Kemp_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kemp-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The following are the differences which apply to first-winter plumage:<sup id="cite_ref-Kemp_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kemp-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The mantle and scapulars of <i>flavirostris</i> have narrow, indistinct pale fringes creating a uniform appearance to the birds' upperparts, whereas <i>albifrons</i> has noticeable whitish fringes creating obviously barred upperparts</li>
<li>The tertials of <i>flavirostris</i> have indistinct pale fringes, whereas these pale fringes are more noticeable on <i>albifrons</i></li>
<li>The lesser- and median-upperwing-coverts of <i>flavirostris</i> have narrow, indistinct pale fringes, creating a rather uniform appearance to the wing, whereas on <i>albifrons</i>, these fringes are prominent and broad, creating wing-bars</li>
<li>The greater-coverts of <i>flavirostris</i> are dark grey, with a narrow white tip, forming a narrow wing-bar; on <i>albifrons</i> they are blue-grey, with prominent white tips, forming a bold wing-bar</li>
<li>The flank-line is narrows and white on <i>flavirostris</i>, but broad and bright white on <i>albifrons</i></li>
<li>The tail of <i>flavirostris</i> is dark brown, with a very narrow white tip and sides; that of <i>albifrons</i> is dark grey, and the white tip and sides are at least double the width of the corresponding areas on <i>flavirostris</i></li>
<li>The bill of <i>flavirostris</i> is orange-yellow with a dark nail, compared with the bright pink bill of <i>albifrons</i> which has only a hint of dark on the nail; in addition the bill of <i>flavirostris</i> is longer and appears slimmer than that of <i>albifrons</i></li></ul>
<p>The belly-barring on adult birds is on average more extensive on <i>flavirostris</i> than on <i>albifrons</i>, but the individual variation in both forms renders this of limited use as an identification feature.<sup id="cite_ref-Kemp_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kemp-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The bill of adult Greenland white-fronts are also orange-yellow at the base, but can be more pinkish-yellow on the outer-half, thus close in colour to European white-fronts; the colour difference is more easily determined in dull, flat light rather than bright sunshine.<sup id="cite_ref-Kemp_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kemp-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_white-fronted_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The greater white-fronted goose is divided into five <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>. The nominate subspecies <i>A. a. albifrons</i> breeds in the far north of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, and winters further south and west in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.
</p><p>Three other restricted-range races occur in northern <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>: <i>A. a. gambeli</i> in interior northwest Canada, and wintering on the coast of the Gulf of Mexico, slightly larger than the nominate form, Pacific white-fronted goose,  <i>A. a. frontalis</i> and tule goose, <i>A. a. elgasi</i>, in southwest <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, largest and longest-billed of all,  both wintering in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. All these races are similar in plumage, differing only in size.<sup id="cite_ref-Carboneras_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carboneras-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The very distinct Greenland white-fronted goose, <i>A. a. flavirostris</i>, breeding in western <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, is much darker overall, with only a very narrow white tip to the tail (broader on the other races), more black barring on its belly, and usually has an orange (not pink) bill. It winters in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>.
</p><p>Birds breeding in the far east of <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> east to Arctic <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, wintering in the United States and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, have been described as <i>A. a. frontalis</i> on the basis of their slightly larger size and a marginally longer bill. Another putative East Asian subspecies <i>albicans</i> has also been described. A 2012 study has found that <i>frontalis</i> and <i>albicans</i> do not merit subspecies status, the former being synonymised with <i>gambelli</i> and the latter with the nominate subspecies; this study found that these forms had been named on the wintering grounds from specimens whose breeding grounds were unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-Banks_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Banks-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">Ecological</a> studies in 2002 suggest the Greenland birds should probably be considered a separate species from <i>A. albifrons</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Fox2002_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fox2002-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Of particular interest is its unusually long period of parental care and association, which may last several years and can include <a href="/wiki/Grandparent" title="Grandparent">grandparenting</a>, possibly uniquely among the <a href="/wiki/Anseriformes" title="Anseriformes">Anseriformes</a>.
</p>

<p>The North American midcontinent birds of the subspecies <i>A. a. gambeli</i> – which in 2010 had a fall population of about 710,000 birds – breeds from the <a href="/wiki/Alaska_North_Slope" title="Alaska North Slope">Alaska North Slope</a> across the western and central Canadian Arctic.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The Pacific white-fronted goose of the American Pacific coast, which in 2010 numbered approximately 650,000 birds,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and the tule geese, which are estimated to number 10,000 birds, nest in western Alaska. The midcontinent geese gather in early fall on the prairies of western Saskatchewan and eastern Alberta, spending several weeks feeding before heading to wintering areas near the Gulf of Mexico, into northern Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-DU_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DU-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The Pacific birds migrate south down the Pacific coast, staging primarily in the <a href="/wiki/Klamath_Basin" title="Klamath Basin">Klamath Basin</a> of southern Oregon and northern California and wintering, eventually, in California's <a href="/wiki/Central_Valley_(California)" title="Central Valley (California)">Central Valley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DU_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DU-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The tule goose is somewhat rare and has been since the latter half of the 19th century,<sup id="cite_ref-Littlejohn1916_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Littlejohn1916-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> presumably it was affected by destruction of its wintering habitat due to human settlement.<sup id="cite_ref-Carboneras_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carboneras-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, two races overwinter: Greenland birds in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, and Russian birds in England and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>. They gather on farmland at favoured traditional sites, with a famous flock gathering at <a href="/wiki/WWT_Slimbridge" title="WWT Slimbridge">WWT Slimbridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a>, England.<sup id="cite_ref-Slimbridge_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Slimbridge-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Greenland birds also overwinter in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and from late September and through the winter months, Ireland is home to almost 50% of the Greenland population of white-fronted geese.<sup id="cite_ref-Peatlands_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peatlands-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>A. a. albifrons</i> and <i>A. a. flavirostis</i> are among the <a href="/wiki/Taxa" class="mw-redirect" title="Taxa">taxa</a> to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_white-fronted_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Weather conditions are a key factor in the annual breeding success of white-fronted geese. In the Arctic, the window of opportunity for nesting, incubating eggs, and raising a brood to flight state is open briefly, for about three months. Arriving in late May or early June, white-fronted geese begin departing for fall staging areas in early September. This means that a delayed snowmelt or late spring storm can significantly reduce the birds' reproductive success.<sup id="cite_ref-DU_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DU-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_migration">Origin of migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_white-fronted_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Origin of migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Midcontinental white-fronted geese in North America have many breeding areas and each group in each breeding area differs in its migration time and wintering location. There are six breeding areas, including interior Alaska, the North Slope of Alaska, western Northwest Territories, western Nunavut, central Nunavut, and eastern Nunavut. These spatial differences lead to different departure times for white-fronted geese leaving their breeding areas. Birds from interior Alaska start migrating earlier during autumn and fly farther south to winter.<sup id="cite_ref-Geography2013_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Geography2013-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Due to their migration, white-fronted geese are commonly sought after by waterfowl hunters, all across the country.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Backtracking_technique">Backtracking technique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_white-fronted_goose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Backtracking technique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>One technique to identify the migratory flight path of individual birds using isotopes was developed by a researcher from Austrian Institute of Technology named Micha Horacek.<sup id="cite_ref-Horacek2011_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Horacek2011-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  Horacek thought the different types of feathers growing on a single migratory bird could be a way to backtrack the migratory route of individual birds infected by avian flue to help locate areas that may be become infected.This allows the affected area to be measured and marked. Bird feather growth can be used as timeline for nutritional intake during the migratory flight understand the route which the infected bird took and determine the region affected by that particular bird flu. During migratory flight, feathers on white-fronted geese gradually fall off during migration as new feathers grow (a process called moulting). Each location visited by a bird has its own distinct isotopic signature of elements (including carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, and sulfur) that can be taken up by migratory birds ingesting food from that area. These isotopes can be studied by collecting feathers and performing thermal combustion analysis. Because different types of feathers have different growth rates, the growth rates can be used to determine the relative time during which each isotope signal was picked up. Each isotope signal in turn can be compared to the signatures present in the different environments where the goose may have fed.
</p>
