<p>The <b>common wood pigeon</b> (<i>Columba palumbus</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Columbidae" title="Columbidae">dove and pigeon</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a>. It belongs to the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Columba_(genus)" title="Columba (genus)">Columba</a></i> and, like all pigeons and doves, belongs to the family <a href="/wiki/Columbidae" title="Columbidae">Columbidae</a>. It is locally known in southeast England as the "<b>culver</b>";<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This name has given rise to several areas known for keeping pigeons to be named after it, such as <a href="/wiki/Culver_Down" title="Culver Down">Culver Down</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_wood_pigeon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common wood pigeon was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">formally described</a> by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">tenth edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>. He placed it with all the other pigeons in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Columba_(genus)" title="Columba (genus)">Columba</a></i> and coined the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Columba palumbus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Specific_epithet" class="mw-redirect" title="Specific epithet">specific epithet</a> <i>palumbus</i> is from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>palumbes</i> for a wood-pigeon.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Five <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are recognised, one of which is now <a href="/wiki/Extinct" class="mw-redirect" title="Extinct">extinct</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>C. p. palumbus</i> <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, 1758 – Europe to west Siberia and Iraq; northwest Africa</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_wood_pigeon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Columba_palumbus_-Quinton,_Birmingham,_England_-head-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Columba_palumbus_-Quinton%2C_Birmingham%2C_England_-head-8.jpg/220px-Columba_palumbus_-Quinton%2C_Birmingham%2C_England_-head-8.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="230" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Columba_palumbus_-Quinton%2C_Birmingham%2C_England_-head-8.jpg/330px-Columba_palumbus_-Quinton%2C_Birmingham%2C_England_-head-8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Columba_palumbus_-Quinton%2C_Birmingham%2C_England_-head-8.jpg/440px-Columba_palumbus_-Quinton%2C_Birmingham%2C_England_-head-8.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1476" data-file-height="1540" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Columba_palumbus_-Quinton,_Birmingham,_England_-head-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult common wood pigeon, photograph taken in Birmingham, England</div></div></div>
<p>The three Western European <i><a href="/wiki/Columba_(genus)" title="Columba (genus)">Columba</a></i> pigeons, common wood pigeon, <a href="/wiki/Stock_dove" title="Stock dove">stock dove</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rock_pigeon" class="mw-redirect" title="Rock pigeon">rock pigeon</a>, though superficially alike, have very distinctive characteristics; the common wood pigeon may be identified at once by its larger size at 38–44.5&#160;cm (15.0–17.5&#160;in) and weight 300–615&#160;g (10.6–21.7&#160;oz), and the white on its neck and wing.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It is otherwise a basically grey bird, with a pinkish breast. The wingspan can range from 68 to 80&#160;cm (27 to 31&#160;in) and the <a href="/wiki/Wing_chord_(biology)" title="Wing chord (biology)">wing chord</a> measures 24 to 25.4&#160;cm (9.4 to 10.0&#160;in). The <a href="/wiki/Tail" title="Tail">tail</a> measures 13.8 to 15&#160;cm (5.4 to 5.9&#160;in), the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> is 1.9 to 2.2&#160;cm (0.75 to 0.87&#160;in) and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsometatarsus" title="Tarsometatarsus">tarsus</a> is 2.5 to 2.8&#160;cm (0.98 to 1.10&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Adult birds bear a series of green and white patches on their necks, and a pink patch on their chest.
</p><p>Juvenile birds do not have the white patches on either side of the neck. When they are about six months old (about three months out of the nest) they gain small white patches on both sides of the neck, which gradually enlarge until they are fully formed when the bird is about 6–8 months old (approx. ages only). Juvenile birds also have a greyer beak and an overall lighter grey appearance than adult birds.
The call is a characteristic cooing, <i>coo-COO-coo-coo-coo</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_wood_pigeon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the colder northern and eastern parts of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Asia</a> the common wood pigeon is a migrant, but in southern and western Europe it is a well distributed and often abundant resident. In <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> wood pigeons are commonly seen in parks and gardens<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and are seen with increasing numbers in towns and cities.
</p>

<p>Its flight is quick, performed by regular beats, with an occasional sharp flick of the wings, characteristic of pigeons in general. It takes off with a loud clattering. It perches well, and in its nuptial display walks along a horizontal branch with swelled neck, lowered wings, and fanned tail. During the display flight the bird climbs, the wings are smartly cracked like a whiplash, and the bird glides down on stiff wings. The common wood pigeon is gregarious, often forming very large flocks outside the breeding season. Like many species of pigeon, wood pigeons take advantage of trees and buildings to gain a vantage point over the surrounding area, and their distinctive call means that they are usually heard before they are seen.
</p><p>This species can be an agricultural <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">pest</a>, and it is often shot, being a legal quarry species in most European countries. It is wary in rural areas, but often quite tame where it is not persecuted.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_wood_pigeon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Two_young_Columba_Palumbus_in_nest.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Two_young_Columba_Palumbus_in_nest.JPG/220px-Two_young_Columba_Palumbus_in_nest.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Two_young_Columba_Palumbus_in_nest.JPG/330px-Two_young_Columba_Palumbus_in_nest.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Two_young_Columba_Palumbus_in_nest.JPG/440px-Two_young_Columba_Palumbus_in_nest.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Two_young_Columba_Palumbus_in_nest.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two young <i>Columba palumbus</i> in a nest</div></div></div><p> It breeds in trees in woods, parks and gardens, laying two white eggs in a simple stick nest which hatch after 17 to 19 days. Wood pigeons seem to have a preference for trees near roadways and rivers. Males exhibit aggressive behaviour towards each other during the breeding season by jumping and flapping wings at each other. Their plumage becomes much darker, especially the head, during hot summer periods. Breeding can happen year round if there is food abundant however breeding season most commonly occurs in autumn usually in the months of August and September. 
</p><p>The nests are vulnerable to attack, particularly by <a href="/wiki/Corvus_(genus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Corvus (genus)">crows</a>, because they live in the countryside, the more so early in the year when the leaf cover is not fully formed. The young usually fly at 33 to 34 days; however, if the nest is disturbed, some young may be able to survive having left the nest as early as 20 days from hatching.
</p><p>In a study carried out using <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">ring-recovery</a> data, the survival rate for juveniles in their first year was 52&#160;per cent, and the adult annual survival rate was 61&#160;per cent.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> For birds that survive the first year the typical lifespan is thus only three years,<sup id="cite_ref-bto_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> but the maximum recorded age is 17 years and 8 months for a bird ringed and recovered on the <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-euring_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euring-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_wood_pigeon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most of its diet is vegetable, round and fleshy leaves from <a href="/wiki/Caryophyllaceae" title="Caryophyllaceae">Caryophyllaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asteraceae" title="Asteraceae">Asteraceae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cruciferous_vegetables" title="Cruciferous vegetables">cruciferous vegetables</a> taken from open fields or gardens and lawns; young shoots and seedlings are favoured, and it will take grain, pine nuts, and certain fruits and berries. In the autumn they also eat figs and acorns, and in winter buds of trees and bushes. They will also eat larvae, ants, and small worms. They need open water to drink and bathe in. Young common wood pigeons swiftly become fat, as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Crop_milk" title="Crop milk">crop milk</a> they are fed by their parents. This is an extremely rich fluid that is produced in the adult birds' crops during the breeding season.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1985317,_326_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1985317,_326-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><br />
</p>
