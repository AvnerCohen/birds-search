<p>The <b>Fieldfare</b> (<i>Turdus pilaris</i>) is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family Turdidae. It breeds in woodland and scrub in northern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. It is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, with many northern birds moving south during the winter. It is a very rare breeder in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, but winters in large numbers in these countries.</p>
<p>It nests in trees, laying several <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> in a neat nest. Unusually for a thrush, they often nest in small colonies, possibly for protection from large <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crows</a>. Migrating birds and wintering birds often form large flocks, often with <a href="/wiki/Redwing" title="Redwing">Redwings</a>.</p>
<p>It is <a href="/wiki/Omnivorous" title="Omnivorous" class="mw-redirect">omnivorous</a>, eating a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a> in summer, and berries in winter.</p>
<p>The Fieldfare is 22–27&#160;cm long, with a plain brown back, white underwings, and grey rump and rear head. The breast has a reddish wash, and the rest of the underparts are White. The breast and flanks are heavily spotted. The sexes are similar.</p>
<p>The male has a simple chattering song, and a chattering flight and alarm call.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Fieldfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">common name</a> <i>fieldfare</i> dates back to at least the eleventh century. The <a href="/wiki/Old_English_language" title="Old English language" class="mw-redirect">Anglo-Saxon</a> word <i>feldefare</i> perhaps meant <i>traveller through the fields</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-dict_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dict-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Alternatively, it may be derived from Old English <i>fealu fearh</i>, literally <i>grey piglet</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODBBN_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODBBN-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The species was described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in his <i>Systema naturae</i> (1758) under its current <a href="/wiki/Scientific_name" title="Scientific name" class="mw-redirect">scientific name</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The name <i>Turdus pilaris</i> comes from two separate <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> words for <i>thrush</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-DSBN_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DSBN-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Fieldfare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h2>

<p>The Fieldfare has an extensive range, estimated at 10&#160;million&#160;square kilometres (3.8&#160;million square miles), and a large population, including an estimated 28 to 48 million individuals in Europe alone. The species is not believed to approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the IUCN Red List (i.e., declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations), and is therefore evaluated as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
