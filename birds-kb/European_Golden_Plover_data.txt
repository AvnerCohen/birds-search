<p>The <b>European golden plover</b> (<i>Pluvialis apricaria</i>), also known as the <b>Eurasian golden plover</b> or just the <b>golden plover</b> within Europe, is a largish <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">plover</a>. This species is similar to two other golden plovers: the <a href="/wiki/American_golden_plover" title="American golden plover">American golden plover</a>, <i>Pluvialis dominica</i>, and <a href="/wiki/Pacific_golden_plover" title="Pacific golden plover">Pacific golden plover</a>, <i>Pluvialis fulva</i>, which are both smaller, slimmer and relatively longer-legged than European golden plover, and both have grey rather than white <a href="/wiki/Axilla" title="Axilla">axillary</a> feathers (only properly visible in flight).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_golden_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The genus name is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means relating to rain, from <i>pluvia</i>, "rain".  It was believed that golden plovers flocked when rain was imminent.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  The species name <i> apricaria</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means to bask in the sun.<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The European golden plover is quite thickset, with its wings only being slightly longer than its tail. Its most distinct feature is a white "s"-shaped band stretching from its <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#forehead" title="Glossary of bird terms">forehead</a> to its <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#flanks" title="Glossary of bird terms">flanks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-europe_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-europe-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_golden_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European golden plover tends to breed in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_tundra" class="mw-redirect" title="Arctic tundra">Arctic tundra</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">palearctic</a> areas, ranging as far west as <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, where they are called Heiðlóa, and as far east as <a href="/wiki/Central_Siberia" class="mw-redirect" title="Central Siberia">central Siberia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-europe_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-europe-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It tends to gather in large flocks and winter in open areas, agricultural plains, ploughed land, and short meadows, ranging from <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> to <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-handbook_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-handbook-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-national_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-national-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, golden plover chicks rely on <a href="/wiki/Tipulidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Tipulidae">Tipulidae</a> for feeding, while in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> <a href="/wiki/Bibionidae" title="Bibionidae">Bibionidae</a> are more important.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The European golden plover's call is a <a href="/wiki/Monosyllabic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monosyllabic">monosyllabic</a>, slightly descending, <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">melancholic</a> "tuu".<sup id="cite_ref-europe_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-europe-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-national_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-national-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Its flight action is rapid and powerful, with regular wingbeats.<sup id="cite_ref-handbook_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-handbook-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_golden_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Folklore">Folklore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_golden_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Folklore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The European golden plover spends summers in Iceland, and in <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_folklore" class="mw-redirect" title="Icelandic folklore">Icelandic folklore</a>, the appearance of the first plover in country means that spring has arrived.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The Icelandic media always covers the first plover sighting, which in 2017, took place on March 27.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In 2020, the first golden plover was sighted on March 16.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_Guinness_World_Records">Origin of Guinness World Records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_golden_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Origin of Guinness World Records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 10 November 1951, Sir <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Beaver" title="Hugh Beaver">Hugh Beaver</a>, then the managing director of the <a href="/wiki/Guinness" title="Guinness">Guinness</a> Breweries,<sup id="cite_ref-guinness_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guinness-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> went on a shooting party in the <a href="/wiki/North_Slob" title="North Slob">North Slob</a>, by the <a href="/wiki/River_Slaney" title="River Slaney">River Slaney</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Wexford" title="County Wexford">County Wexford</a>, Ireland. After missing a shot at a Eurasian golden plover, he became involved in an argument over which was the fastest <a href="/wiki/Game_(food)" class="mw-redirect" title="Game (food)">game bird</a> in Europe, the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_golden_plover" class="mw-redirect" title="Eurasian golden plover">golden plover</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Red_grouse" title="Red grouse">red grouse</a> (the former being correct).<sup id="cite_ref-Davenport2010_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davenport2010-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> That evening at <a href="/wiki/Castlebridge" title="Castlebridge">Castlebridge</a> House, he realised that it was impossible to confirm in reference books whether or not the golden plover was Europe's fastest game bird.<sup id="cite_ref-guinness2_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guinness2-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cavendish2005_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish2005-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Beaver knew that there must be numerous other questions debated nightly in pubs throughout Ireland, but there was no book in the world with which to settle arguments about records. He realised then that a book supplying the answers to this sort of question might prove popular.<sup id="cite_ref-Guinness50th_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guinness50th-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> A Guinness employee told Sir Hugh of two twin brothers, <a href="/wiki/Norris_mcwhirter" class="mw-redirect" title="Norris mcwhirter">Norris</a>  and <a href="/wiki/Ross_McWhirter" title="Ross McWhirter">Ross McWhirter</a>, who had opened a fact checking agency in London.  Sir Hugh interviewed the brothers and, impressed by their prodigious knowledge, commissioned the book. Later, he published the first <a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness World Records</a> which became a best seller within months.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_golden_plover&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European golden plover is one of the species to which the Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>
