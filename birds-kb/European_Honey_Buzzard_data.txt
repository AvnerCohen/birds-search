<p>The <b>European honey buzzard</b> (<i>Pernis apivorus</i>), also known as the <b>pern</b> or <b>common pern</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-Cuvier1851_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cuvier1851-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_honey_buzzard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite its English name, this species is more closely related to kites of the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Leptodon" title="Leptodon">Leptodon</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Chondrohierax" title="Chondrohierax">Chondrohierax</a></i> than to true <a href="/wiki/Buzzard" title="Buzzard">buzzards</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Buteo" title="Buteo">Buteo</a></i>. The <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomen</a> is due to <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linné</a>. It is derived from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>pernes</i> <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%80%CE%B5%CF%81%CE%BD%CE%B7%CF%82" class="extiw" title="wikt:περνης">περνης</a>, a term used by <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> for a bird of prey, and <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>apivorus</i> "bee-eating", from <i>apis</i>, "<a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bee</a>" and <i>-vorus</i>, "-eating".<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In fact, bees are much less important than wasps in the birds' diet. Note that it is accordingly called <i lang="de" title="German language text">Wespenbussard</i> ("wasp buzzard") in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> and similarly in some other Germanic languages.
</p>

<p>The 52–60-centimetre (20–24&#160;in)-long honey buzzard is larger and longer winged, with a 135–150-centimetre (53–59&#160;in) wingspan, when compared to the smaller <a href="/wiki/Common_buzzard" title="Common buzzard">common buzzard</a> (<i>Buteo buteo</i>). It appears longer necked with a small head, and soars on flat wings. It has a longer tail, which has fewer bars than the <i>Buteo</i> buzzard, usually with two narrow dark bars and a broad dark subterminal bar. The sexes can be <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">distinguished on plumage</a>, which is unusual for a large bird of prey. The male has a blue-grey head, while the female's head is brown. The female is slightly larger and darker than the male.
</p><p>The soaring <a href="/wiki/Jizz_(birding)" title="Jizz (birding)">jizz</a> is quite diagnostic; the wings are held straight with the wing tips horizontal or sometimes slightly pointed down. The head protrudes forwards with a slight kink downwards and sometimes a very angular chest can be seen, similar to a <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_sparrowhawk" title="Eurasian sparrowhawk">sparrowhawk</a>, although this may not be diagnostic. The angular chest is most pronounced when seen in direct flight with tail narrowed. The call is a clear <i>peee-lu</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_honey_buzzard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European honey buzzard is a summer <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a> to  a relatively small area in the western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> from most of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> to as far east as southwestern Siberia. The eastern area boundary is not yet known exactly, it is thought to be  in the Tomsk&#8211;Novosibirsk&#8211;Barnaul area. It is seen in a wide range of habitats, but generally prefers woodland and exotic plantations. It migrates to tropical <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> for European winters.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Movements">Movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_honey_buzzard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Movements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Being a long distance migrant, the honey buzzard relies on magnetic orientation to find its way south, as well as a visual memory of remarkable geographical features such as mountain ranges and rivers, along the way. It avoids large expanses of water over which it cannot soar. Accordingly, great numbers of honey buzzards can be seen crossing the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> over its narrowest stretches, such as the <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar_Strait" class="mw-redirect" title="Gibraltar Strait">Gibraltar Strait</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Messina_Strait" class="mw-redirect" title="Messina Strait">Messina Strait</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bosphorus" class="mw-redirect" title="Bosphorus">Bosphorus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, or in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Garcia2006_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Garcia2006-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_in_Britain">Status in Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_honey_buzzard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Status in Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bird is an uncommon breeder in, and a scarce though increasing migrant to, Britain. Its most well-known summer population is in the <a href="/wiki/New_Forest" title="New Forest">New Forest</a> (Hampshire) but it is also found in the <a href="/wiki/River_Tyne" title="River Tyne">Tyne Valley</a> (Northumberland), <a href="/wiki/Wareham_Forest" title="Wareham Forest">Wareham Forest</a> (Dorset), <a href="/wiki/Swanton_Novers" title="Swanton Novers">Swanton Novers</a> Great Wood (Norfolk), the <a href="/wiki/Vale_of_Neath" title="Vale of Neath">Neath Valleys</a> (South Wales), the <a href="/wiki/Clumber_Park" title="Clumber Park">Clumber Park</a> area (Nottinghamshire), near Wykeham Forest (North Yorkshire), Haldon Forest Park (Devon) and elsewhere.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mimicry">Mimicry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_honey_buzzard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mimicry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The similarity in plumage between juvenile European honey buzzard and common buzzard may have arisen as a partial protection against predation by <a href="/wiki/Northern_goshawk" title="Northern goshawk">northern goshawks</a>. Although that formidable predator is capable of killing both species, it is likely to be more cautious about attacking the better protected <i>Buteo</i> species, with its stronger bill and talons. Similar <a href="/wiki/Batesian_mimicry" title="Batesian mimicry">Batesian mimicry</a> is shown by the <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> <i>Pernis</i> species, which resemble the <i>Spizaetus</i> <a href="/wiki/Hawk-eagle" class="mw-redirect" title="Hawk-eagle">hawk-eagles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Duff2006_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duff2006-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_honey_buzzard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is sometimes seen soaring in thermals. When flying in wooded vegetation, honey buzzards usually fly quite low and perch in midcanopy, holding the body relatively horizontal with its tail drooping. The bird also hops from branch to branch, each time flapping its wings once, and so emitting a loud clap. The bird often appears restless with much ruffling of the wings and shifting around on its perch. The honey buzzard often inspects possible locations of food from its perch, cocking its head this way and that to get a good look at possible food locations. This behaviour is reminiscent of an inquisitive <a href="/wiki/Parrot" title="Parrot">parrot</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_honey_buzzard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bondr%C3%A9e_apivore_MHNT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Bondr%C3%A9e_apivore_MHNT.jpg/180px-Bondr%C3%A9e_apivore_MHNT.jpg" decoding="async" width="180" height="196" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Bondr%C3%A9e_apivore_MHNT.jpg/270px-Bondr%C3%A9e_apivore_MHNT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Bondr%C3%A9e_apivore_MHNT.jpg/360px-Bondr%C3%A9e_apivore_MHNT.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4095" data-file-height="4468" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bondr%C3%A9e_apivore_MHNT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs are heavily marked with brown on a white or pale buff background,<sup id="cite_ref-Perrins_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Perrins-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and measure 5.1 x 4.1 cm.<sup id="cite_ref-manen_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manen-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The honey buzzard breeds in <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodland</a>, and is inconspicuous except in the spring, when the mating display includes wing-clapping. Breeding males are fiercely territorial. The clutch typically consists of two eggs, less often one or three. <a href="/wiki/Siblicide" title="Siblicide">Siblicide</a> is rarely observed.<sup id="cite_ref-manen_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manen-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_honey_buzzard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is a specialist feeder, living mainly on the larvae and nests of <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">wasps</a> and hornets, although it will take small mammals, reptiles, and birds. It is the only known predator of the <a href="/wiki/Asian_giant_hornet" title="Asian giant hornet">Asian giant hornet</a>. It spends large amounts of time on the forest floor excavating wasp nests. It is equipped with long toes and claws adapted to raking and digging, and scale-like feathering on its head, thought to be a defence against the stings of its victims.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Honey buzzards are thought to have a chemical deterrent in their feathers that protects them from wasp attacks.<sup id="cite_ref-Sievwright_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sievwright-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
