<p>The <b> masked shrike</b> (<i>Lanius nubicus</i>) is a bird in the <a href="/wiki/Shrike" title="Shrike">shrike</a> family, Laniidae. It breeds in southeastern Europe and at the eastern end of the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>, with a separate population in eastern Iraq and western Iran. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering mainly in northeast Africa. Although it is a short-range migrant, vagrants have occurred widely elsewhere, including northern and western Europe. It is the smallest member of its <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a>, long-tailed and with a hooked bill. The male has mainly black upperparts, with white on its crown, forehead and <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a> and large white patches on the shoulders and <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Primaries" title="Flight feather">wings</a>. The throat, neck sides and underparts are white, with orange flanks and breast. The female is a duller version of the male, with brownish black upperparts and a grey or buff tone to the shoulders and underparts. The juvenile has grey-brown upperparts with a paler forehead and barring from the head to rump, barred off-white underparts and brown wings аpart from the white primary patches. The species' calls are short and grating, but the song has melodic <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">warbler-like</a> components.
</p><p>The masked shrike's preferred habitat is open woodland with bushes and some large trees. It is less conspicuous than its relatives, avoiding very open country and often perching in less exposed locations. The nest is a neat cup built in a tree by both adults, and the <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is normally 4–6 eggs, which are incubated by the female for 14–16 days until hatching. The chicks are fed by both parents until they <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> 18–20&#160;days later, and remain dependent on the adults for about 3–4 weeks after leaving the nest. The masked shrike eats mainly large insects, occasionally small vertebrates; it sometimes impales its prey on thorns or barbed wire. Populations are decreasing in parts of the European range, but not rapidly enough to raise serious conservation concerns, and the species is therefore classified by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> as being of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masked_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The shrikes are a family of slender, long-tailed <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerines</a>, most of its members being in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Lanius" title="Lanius">Lanius</a></i>, the typical shrikes. They are short-necked birds with rounded wings and a hooked tip to the bill. Most occur in open habitats.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonlinefam_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonlinefam-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The affiliations of the masked shrike with other members of the genus are uncertain; the "brown" shrikes (<a href="/wiki/Brown_shrike" title="Brown shrike">brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red-backed_shrike" title="Red-backed shrike">red-backed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isabelline_shrike" title="Isabelline shrike">isabelline shrikes</a>) and tropical species like the <a href="/wiki/Somali_fiscal" title="Somali fiscal">Somali fiscal</a> have both been suggested as possible relatives. The masked shrike has no subspecies.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The masked shrike was described by German explorer and naturalist <a href="/wiki/Martin_Lichtenstein" class="mw-redirect" title="Martin Lichtenstein">Martin Lichtenstein</a> in 1823 under its current scientific name.<sup id="cite_ref-licht_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-licht-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <i>Lanius</i> is the Latin for a butcher, and comes from the shrikes' habit of impaling prey, reminiscent of a butcher hanging carcasses,<sup id="cite_ref-job219_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job219-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and <i>nubicus</i> means "Nubian" (from northeast Africa).<sup id="cite_ref-job279_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job279-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OED_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The bird was independently described by Dutch <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Coenraad_Jacob_Temminck" title="Coenraad Jacob Temminck">Coenraad Temminck</a> in 1824 as <i>Lanius personatus</i>, from the Latin <i>personatus</i> "masked",<sup id="cite_ref-job219_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job219-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> referring, as does the English name, to the bird's appearance, but the older name takes precedence. A later synonym from 1844 was <i>L. leucometopon</i> from the Greek <i>leukos</i>, "white", and <i>metopon</i>, "forehead", describing a feature of the distinctive head pattern.<sup id="cite_ref-job224_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job224-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> "Shrike", first recorded in 1545, derives from the shrill cries given by this family,<sup id="cite_ref-OED2_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and the traditional common name "butcher-bird" again refers to the characteristic prey storage,<sup id="cite_ref-job279_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job279-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and has been in use since at least 1668.<sup id="cite_ref-OED3_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED3-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masked_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Maskedshrikeinhand.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Maskedshrikeinhand.JPG/220px-Maskedshrikeinhand.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Maskedshrikeinhand.JPG/330px-Maskedshrikeinhand.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Maskedshrikeinhand.JPG/440px-Maskedshrikeinhand.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="667" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Maskedshrikeinhand.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Female in Israel</div></div></div>
<p>The masked shrike is the smallest of its genus, a slender bird which usually weighs 20–23&#160;g (0.71–0.81&#160;oz), measuring 17–18.5&#160;cm (6.7–7.3&#160;in) long with a 24–26.5&#160;cm (9.4–10.4&#160;in) wingspan. It has a long tail and relatively small bill,<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BWP_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> on each side of which is a <a href="/wiki/Tomium" title="Tomium">tomial tooth</a>; the upper mandible bears a triangular ridge which fits a corresponding notch in the lower mandible. This adaptation is otherwise only found in <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-shrike23_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shrike23-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The male has mainly black upperparts, a white crown, forehead and <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a>. There are large white patches on the shoulders and <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather#Primaries" title="Flight feather">primaries</a>, and the outermost tail feathers are also white. The throat, neck sides and underparts are white, with orange on the flanks and breast. The <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">iris</a> is brown, the bill is black and the legs are dark brown or black.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BWP_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The female is a duller version of the male, with brownish-black upperparts and a grey or buff tinge to the white shoulder patches and underparts. The juvenile has grey-brown upperparts with darker bars from the head to rump, a paler grey forehead, barred off-white underparts and brown wings with white primary patches.<sup id="cite_ref-harris_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harris-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Masked shrikes are most similar in appearance to <a href="/wiki/Woodchat_shrike" title="Woodchat shrike">woodchat shrikes</a>, but are smaller, more slender and longer-tailed. Adults of the two species are easily distinguished, since the masked shrike has white on its head and a dark rump, whereas the woodchat shrike has a black crown, rusty nape and white rump. Juveniles are more similar, but the masked shrike has a longer tail, paler face, and grey back and rump, whereas the woodchat shrike has a sandy back and pale grey rump.<sup id="cite_ref-harris_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harris-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bb1995_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb1995-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Juveniles <a href="/wiki/Moult" class="mw-redirect" title="Moult">moult</a> their head, body and some wing feathers a few weeks after fledging, and adults have a complete moult after breeding. In both cases, if the process is not complete by the time of migration it is suspended and completed on the wintering grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-harris_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harris-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">Voice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masked_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Voice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The masked shrike gives the harsh calls typical of this family, with repeated <i>tsr</i>, <i>tzr</i> or <i>shek</i> notes and some whistles, and when alarmed produces a rattling <i>krrrr</i>. The bill may be snapped when the bird is agitated. The song, up to a minute long, is soft for a shrike, with chattering sounds interspersed with rich warbles.<sup id="cite_ref-harris_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harris-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> It resembles the songs of <i><a href="/wiki/Hippolais" title="Hippolais">Hippolais</a></i> species, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Olive-tree_warbler" title="Olive-tree warbler">olive-tree warbler</a>. On rare occasions, males may sing in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-Nikolov_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nikolov-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masked_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lanius_nubicus_juvenile2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Lanius_nubicus_juvenile2.jpg/220px-Lanius_nubicus_juvenile2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="139" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Lanius_nubicus_juvenile2.jpg/330px-Lanius_nubicus_juvenile2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Lanius_nubicus_juvenile2.jpg/440px-Lanius_nubicus_juvenile2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1009" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lanius_nubicus_juvenile2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Juvenile</div></div></div>
<p>The masked shrike breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>, northeast Greece and some of the Greek islands, Turkey, Cyprus and from Syria south to Israel. It also nests in eastern Iraq and western Iran. The range in the east is uncertain, and may include Afghanistan and northern Saudi Arabia. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering south of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>, mainly in Chad, Sudan and Ethiopia. Smaller numbers are found west to eastern Mali and Nigeria, and in northern Kenya and southern Saudi Arabia. Most birds leave the breeding areas in late August and September, and return north in February and March.<sup id="cite_ref-harris_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harris-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species is seen in Egypt, Jordan and Israel much more often in spring than autumn, suggesting that the southern movement may be concentrated further east. Birds will hold small territories on about 0.5 hectares (1.2 acres) on migration, and, unlike other shrikes, may congregate in significant numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-harris_13-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harris-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> More than 100 have been seen in one locality in Israel, with five in a single bush. This shrike has occurred as a vagrant in Algeria, Finland, Kenya, Libya, Spain, Sweden,<sup id="cite_ref-harris_13-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harris-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Mauritania and Turkmenistan.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> At least three individuals have been documented in Great Britain,<sup id="cite_ref-bb98_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb98-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-stodd_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stodd-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-york_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-york-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and two individuals in Armenia.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The masked shrike's preferred habitat is open woodland with bushes and some large trees. Unlike its relatives, it avoids very open, lightly vegetated country. Orchards and other cultivated land with suitable old trees or large hedges are also used by this species. It is normally found in more wooded areas than <a href="/wiki/Sympatry" title="Sympatry">sympatric</a> shrikes. It occurs in lowlands and in hills up to 1,000&#160;m (3,300&#160;ft). In some areas breeding occurs at greater altitudes, up to 2,000&#160;m (6,600&#160;ft). It may occur in gardens and resorts on migration, and in winter again prefers open country with thorny bushes and large trees like <a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">acacia</a> or introduced <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus" title="Eucalyptus">eucalyptus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masked_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The masked shrike is a solitary species except when on migration. It maintains a breeding territory of 2–5&#160;ha (5–12 acres) and is also territorial on the wintering grounds, defending an area of about 3&#160;ha (7 acres). Although unafraid of humans, it is aggressive to its own species and other birds which infringe on its territory. Most other shrikes use high, exposed branches throughout the year, but the masked shrike only uses conspicuous locations at the start of the breeding season, otherwise choosing lower, more sheltered spots.<sup id="cite_ref-harris_13-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harris-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> It perches upright, frequently cocking its tail, and has an easy, agile flight.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> A masked shrike has been recorded as feigning injury when trapped, only to return to normal when the threat receded.<sup id="cite_ref-bb44_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb44-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masked_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lanius_nubicus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.213.Djezzin_Liban.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Lanius_nubicus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.213.Djezzin_Liban.jpg/220px-Lanius_nubicus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.213.Djezzin_Liban.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="233" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Lanius_nubicus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.213.Djezzin_Liban.jpg/330px-Lanius_nubicus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.213.Djezzin_Liban.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Lanius_nubicus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.213.Djezzin_Liban.jpg/440px-Lanius_nubicus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.213.Djezzin_Liban.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2330" data-file-height="2468" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lanius_nubicus_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.213.Djezzin_Liban.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs at the <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9um_de_Toulouse" title="Muséum de Toulouse">Muséum de Toulouse</a></div></div></div> 
<p>Male masked shrikes sing from perches in their territories from early April, sometimes chasing or competing vocally with neighbouring males.<sup id="cite_ref-harris_13-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harris-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The male's courtship display, usually accompanied by singing, starts with the bird perching erect and shivering its wings on an exposed perch, and is followed by the shrike stepping down its branch and bowing, either on the move or while temporarily halted. The male may also give a fluttering, zigzagging flight display. The female is sometimes fed by her mate while she crouches with spread wings and gives begging calls. Elements of the display are shared with other shrikes, but stepping-down and bowing on the move appear to be confined to this species.<sup id="cite_ref-Nikolov_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nikolov-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The nest, built by both sexes, is a small, neat cup of rootlets, stems and twigs, lined with wool or hair, and adorned with <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichen</a> externally.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is constructed in a tree 1.5–10&#160;m (4.9–32.8&#160;ft) above the ground and averages 170&#160;mm (6.7&#160;in) wide and 65&#160;mm (2.6&#160;in) deep, with the cup 75&#160;mm (3.0&#160;in) across and a 35&#160;mm (1.4&#160;in) in depth.<sup id="cite_ref-harris_13-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harris-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Eggs are laid from April to June, mainly in May in the lowlands and about a month later in the mountains. Replacement <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutches</a> are laid in June or July if the nest fails, and second broods appear to be common in at least some areas.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The first nest is destroyed by the pair to provide material for a replacement breeding attempt. The eggs average 20&#160;mm ×&#160;16&#160;mm (0.79&#160;in ×&#160;0.63&#160;in) in size and are variable in colour, with a background of grey, cream or yellow, diffuse grey blotches, and a ring of brown markings.<sup id="cite_ref-harris_13-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harris-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The normal clutch is 4–6 eggs, which are incubated by the female for 14–16 days until hatching. The <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a> downy chicks are fed by both parents until they <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> 18–20&#160;days later. They are dependent on the adults for about 3–4 weeks after leaving the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The masked shrike breeds in its first year, but its average life span is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-harris_13-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harris-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Vertebrate predators of young birds include cats and crows.<sup id="cite_ref-harris_13-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harris-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> This species may also be infected by parasites, such as an <a href="/wiki/Nematode" title="Nematode">eyeworm</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-AlMo_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AlMo-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Tick" title="Tick">tick</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hyalomma_marginatum" title="Hyalomma marginatum">Hyalomma marginatum</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-hoog_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hoog-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and at least two species of <i><a href="/wiki/Haemoproteus" title="Haemoproteus">Haemoproteus</a></i> blood parasites.<sup id="cite_ref-MoAl_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoAl-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masked_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like its relatives, the masked shrike hunts from a perch, typically 3–8&#160;m (10–26&#160;ft) high, although usually in less exposed locations than those favoured by most other shrikes. Prey is usually taken from the ground, but occasionally picked off foliage or caught in the air with an agile <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">flycatcher-like</a> flight. The kill may be impaled on thorns or barbed wire as a "larder" for immediate or later consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-harris_13-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harris-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Because passerines have relatively weak legs, impalement holds the corpse while it is dismembered. It was once thought that this behaviour was shown mainly by male shrikes in the breeding season, but this is not the case. Masked shrikes of both sexes are known to impale in winter and on migration.<sup id="cite_ref-bb1969_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb1969-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Individual birds may be very tame, following a gardener or feeding close to an observer.<sup id="cite_ref-harris_13-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harris-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The masked shrike feeds mainly on large insects, although other <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> and small vertebrates are also caught. Shrikes fatten up before migration, but to a lesser extent than other passerines because they can feed on the way, sometimes taking other tired migrants.<sup id="cite_ref-shrike20_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shrike20-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Despite its relatively small size, the masked shrike has been recorded as killing species such as <a href="/wiki/Lesser_whitethroat" title="Lesser whitethroat">lesser whitethroat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Little_swift" title="Little swift">little swift</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Vertebrates are killed by bill blows to the back of the head, and the tomial teeth are then used to separate the neck bones.<sup id="cite_ref-shrike23_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shrike23-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN) estimates the European population of the masked shrike to be between 105,000–300,000 individuals, suggesting a global total of 142,000–600,000 birds. Although the population appears to be declining, the decrease is not rapid enough to trigger the IUCN vulnerability criteria. The large numbers and extensive breeding range of about 353,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (136,000&#160;sq&#160;mi),<sup id="cite_ref-BirdLife_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLife-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> mean that this shrike is classified by the IUCN as being of <a href="/wiki/Least_concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least concern">least concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Numbers have declined in recent decades in Europe, although Bulgaria, Greece and Cyprus still have several thousand breeding pairs. Turkey is a stronghold with up to 90,000 pairs. The species is declining in Greece and Turkey because of habitat loss, and a large decrease in Israel is thought to be due to <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>. In Somalia this bird is now rare. Migrating birds are shot in the countries around the eastern Mediterranean, despite legal protection in most countries, and there is some persecution of breeding birds in Greece and Syria, where this species is considered to be unlucky. There are indications that this shrike is adapting to plantations instead of natural woodlands, which could help populations in the longer term.<sup id="cite_ref-hbwonline_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbwonline-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
