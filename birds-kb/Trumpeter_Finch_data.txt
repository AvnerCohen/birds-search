<p>The <b>trumpeter finch</b> (<i>Bucanetes githagineus</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finch</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Fringillidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Fringillidae">Fringillidae</a>. It is mainly a desert species which is found in North Africa and Spain through to southern Asia. It has occurred as a <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrant</a> in areas north of its breeding range.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_subspecies">Distribution and subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trumpeter_finch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution and subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The trumpeter finch breeds from the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> eastwards across North Africa, as far south as Mauritania, Mali and Chad, with isolated populations in Sudan and Ethiopia and Djibouti. In the middle east it is found in Egypt east to Iraq and south in the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a> to Yemen and Oman and north into Turkey and Armenia. In central Asia it ranges from Iran north to Kazakhstan and east to India.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It has colonised southern Spain where breeding was first proved in 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-BIP_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BIP-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are four recognised subspecies:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HBW_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>B. g. amantum</i> - <small>(<a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a>, 1903)</small> - the Canary Islands</li>
<li><i>B. g. zedlitzi</i> - <small>(<a href="/wiki/Oscar_Rudolph_Neumann" class="mw-redirect" title="Oscar Rudolph Neumann">Neumann</a>, 1907)</small> - North Africa and southeastern Spain</li>
<li><i>B. g. githagineus</i> <small>(<a href="/wiki/Hinrich_Lichtenstein" title="Hinrich Lichtenstein">Lichtenstein, MHK</a>, 1823)</small> - Egypt and Sudan</li>
<li><i>B. g. crassirostris</i> (<a href="/wiki/Edward_Blyth" title="Edward Blyth">Blyth</a>, 1847) - from Turkey and the <a href="/wiki/Sinai_Peninsula" title="Sinai Peninsula">Sinai Peninsula</a> in the west east through the Middle East and Central Asia to <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haryana" title="Haryana">Haryana</a> in India.</li></ul>
<p>It has been recorded as a vagrant in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> with the first records there both occurring in 1971 in <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Sutherland" title="Sutherland">Sutherland</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Dymond_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dymond-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Channel_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Channel Island">Channel Island</a>, Denmark, Sweden Germany and Austria. It is possibly a regular migrant in southern Europe away from Spain with records of flocks from Italy and Malta.<sup id="cite_ref-Lewington_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lewington-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> There was a population in the <a href="/wiki/Algarve" title="Algarve">Algarve</a> in Portugal which originated from escaped cage birds.<sup id="cite_ref-BIP_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BIP-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the summer of 2005 there was a notable irruption of this species into northwestern Europe, with several birds reaching as far as <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BBRC_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBRC-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trumpeter_finch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The trumpeter finch is a small, long-winged bird. It has a large head and short, very thick bill. The summer male has a red bill, grey head and neck, and pale brown upper parts. The breast, rump and tail are pink, the last having dark terminal feathers. Winter males, females and young birds are a very washed-out version of the breeding male. The song of this bird is a buzzing nasal trill, like a tin trumpet.<sup id="cite_ref-Beaman_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beaman-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trumpeter_finch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Trumpeter finches occur in desert, semi-desert and the margins of deserts. They can also be found in vast open steppe areas where there are dry desolate hills with sparse low scrubby vegetation, edges of fields, on mountain slopes, in stony plains where there are no trees, cliffs, ravines, gorges and <a href="/wiki/Wadi" title="Wadi">wadis</a>. In the desert regions of northern Africa it can also occur in villages and gardens and in regions of open sandy desert it frequents <a href="/wiki/Oasis" title="Oasis">oases</a>.  The European breeding population is found in habitats where there is no tree cover but there is sparse scrub less than a metre in height, while the birds in the Canary Islands nest on sandy plains with halophytic and xerophytic scrub, as well as in more typical habitats.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trumpeter_finch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Trumpeter finches breed from February to June in <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a> pairs. The female builds a simple nest made of a loose collection of twigs, plat stems, down and fibres such as animal hair, grass fibres and sometimes feathers. It is placed in a shallow depression in the ground, in the shade of a rock, bush or a tussock of grass. It may also be situated as high as to six metres (20&#160;ft) above ground in a pipe or wall. The clutch is normally 4–6 eggs.  They are mainly vegetarian and their diet consists of small seeds, shoots and buds of grasses and low ground-loving plants. They will eat some insects as well, mainly <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a>.  Trumpeter finches can be resident, dispersive or nomadic.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> They can occur in pairs or they form flocks of up to 20 individuals; larger flocks can form outside the breeding season, frequently made up of largely juvenile birds, rarely reaching 1,000 birds. In then Canary Islands they form mixed flocks with <a href="/wiki/Common_linnet" title="Common linnet">common linnets</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_sparrows&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Spanish sparrows (page does not exist)">Spanish sparrows</a>. They will fly quite long distances in the late afternoons and in the evenings to find drinking water.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The population in Spain is supported by birds dispersing from North Africa joining its population.<sup id="cite_ref-BIP_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BIP-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trumpeter_finch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The genus name is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>bukanetes</i>, "trumpeter", and the specific <i>githagineus</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> from <i> Githago</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Agrostemma_githago" title="Agrostemma githago">corn cockle</a> (from <i>gith</i>, "<a href="/wiki/Coriander" title="Coriander">coriander</a>", and <i>-ago</i> "resembling"). <a href="/wiki/Coenraad_Jacob_Temminck" title="Coenraad Jacob Temminck">Temminck</a> believed that the bird's name was derived from that of the plant.<sup id="cite_ref-job_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
