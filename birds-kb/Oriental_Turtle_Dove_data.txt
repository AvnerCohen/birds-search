<p>The <b>oriental turtle dove</b> or <b>rufous turtle dove</b> (<i>Streptopelia orientalis</i>) is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> family Columbidae. The species has a wide native distribution range from Europe, east across Asia to Japan. The populations show variations in the patterning of plumage and have been designated into at least six named subspecies. Populations in the higher latitudes tend to migrate south in winter while those closer to the tropics are sedentary. Vagrants have been recorded in North America. The species is predominantly granivorous and forages on the ground. 
</p>

<p>The genus name <i>Streptopelia</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>streptos</i>, "collar" and <i>peleia</i>, "dove". The specific <i>orientalis</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "eastern", in this case referring to the <a href="/wiki/Type_(biology)#type_locality" title="Type (biology)">type locality</a>, China.<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The species has a wide distribution with geographical variation in plumage that has led to at least six subspecies being designated. The distributions of many are not disjunct and gradation exists except in some island populations. Some of the populations of <i>Streptopelia turtur</i> that occur on the eastern edge can be very similar in appearance to <i>S. orientalis</i> and in the past the two species have been lumped together.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>S. o. meena</i>  <small>(<a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Sykes" title="William Henry Sykes">Sykes</a>, 1832)</small> – eastern Asia and Central Asia south to the Himalayas from Kashmir to central Nepal. Winters in India as far south as Sri Lanka.</li>
<li><i>S. o. orientalis</i>  <small>(<a href="/wiki/John_Latham_(ornithologist)" title="John Latham (ornithologist)">Latham</a>, 1790)</small> – central Siberia to Japan and Korea south to the Himalayas from Assam to Yunnan and northern Vietnam. Winters in South and Southeast Asia (includes <i>S. baicalensis</i> Buturlin).</li>
<li><i>S. o. stimpsoni</i> <small>(<a href="/wiki/Leonhard_Stejneger" title="Leonhard Stejneger">Stejneger</a>, 1887)</small> – an insular subspecies from the Ryukyu Islands.</li>
<li><i>S. o. orii</i> <small><a href="/wiki/Yoshimaro_Yamashina" title="Yoshimaro Yamashina">Yamashina</a>, 1932</small> – Taiwan.</li>
<li><i>S. o. erythrocephala</i>  <small>(<a href="/wiki/Charles_Lucien_Bonaparte" title="Charles Lucien Bonaparte">Bonaparte</a>, 1855)</small> – southern peninsular India. This subspecies is reddish-brown on the head with no grey on the forehead or crown, unlike in other subspecies. The undertail coverts and terminal tail band are slaty grey.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>S. o. agricola</i> <small>(<a href="/wiki/Samuel_Tickell" title="Samuel Tickell">Tickell</a>, 1833)</small> – from Orissa and Bengal to northeastern India and into Myanmar south to Hainan.</li></ul>
<p>The Oriental turtle dove has two distinct migratory northern subspecies, <i>S. o. orientalis</i> in the central <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberian</a> <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a>, and <i>S. o. meena</i> in the open woodlands of central <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. The differences in the tail patterns of the subspecies <i>S. o. orientalis</i> and <i>S. o. meena</i> help separate identification in their wintering grounds. The nominate form is described as having a grey tip to the tail and more black in the outer web of the outer tail feathers, while <i>meena</i> has a white tip to its tail like the European turtle dove and less black in the outer web of the outer tail feathers. However, these are not consistent differences: both forms can have white or grey tail-bands and <i>orientalis</i> can have little black in the outer webs of the outer tail feathers.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oriental_turtle_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Oriental_Turtle_Dove.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Oriental_Turtle_Dove.jpg/220px-Oriental_Turtle_Dove.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Oriental_Turtle_Dove.jpg/330px-Oriental_Turtle_Dove.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Oriental_Turtle_Dove.jpg/440px-Oriental_Turtle_Dove.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Oriental_Turtle_Dove.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>S. o. meena</i> from the central Himalayas</div></div></div>
<p>The Oriental turtle dove is very similar in plumage to the <a href="/wiki/European_turtle_dove" title="European turtle dove">European turtle dove</a>. It is a little larger than that species, particularly in the case of <i>orientalis</i>, about the same size as a <a href="/wiki/Collared_dove" class="mw-redirect" title="Collared dove">collared dove</a>. It shares the black and white striped patch on the side of its neck made of silver-tipped feathers, but the breast is less pink, and the orange-brown wing <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a> of the turtle dove are replaced with a browner hue, and darker centres giving a scaly appearance. The tail is wedge shaped, like the turtle dove.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (September 2019)">which?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The flight is more relaxed and direct than that of its relative.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (September 2019)">which?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Call">Call</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oriental_turtle_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Call">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The calls are different from the purring of the European turtle dove. It is a four-syllable <i>her-her-oo-oo</i>. There are significant call differences within the populations as well.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The habitat varies, but the Oriental turtle dove breeds in well-wooded but open habitats and winters in more open habitats but usually with good tree cover.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The westernmost race, <i>meena</i>, breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Palearctic">Western Palearctic</a> region.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The southernmost populations are resident, but most other birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> south to winter in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a>, and southern <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. They disperse widely and are known from islands such as the Lakshadweep in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <i>S. o. orientalis</i> occurs as a rarity in western <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>. It is also a vagrant to northern and western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and occurrences in Britain tend to gather much attention from <a href="/wiki/Birdwatching" title="Birdwatching">twitchers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hirschfeld86_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hirschfeld86-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oriental_turtle_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oriental_turtle_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tourterelle_orientale_MHNT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Tourterelle_orientale_MHNT.jpg/220px-Tourterelle_orientale_MHNT.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="198" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Tourterelle_orientale_MHNT.jpg/330px-Tourterelle_orientale_MHNT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Tourterelle_orientale_MHNT.jpg/440px-Tourterelle_orientale_MHNT.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2437" data-file-height="2189" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tourterelle_orientale_MHNT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg of  <i>Streptopelia orientalis</i></div></div></div>
<p>The summer breeding season in the temperate zone can be protracted. In southern India, the breeding season is in winter. In display, the male flaps its wing noisily and shoots up before gliding down with outspread tail.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Nests take about two days to build with the male gathering material and the female placing it. The nest is more substantial than in some other doves and is placed at mid-canopy height. In Japan, nests built at a greater height tended to be prone to predation.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Males were found to incubate in the day and the female by night. Two white <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a>, as for all pigeons and doves, are laid in a twig nest in a tree. Incubation begins immediately after the first egg is laid. The eggs hatch in 15 to 16 days and the chicks take about 15 to 17 days to fledge (in Japan). Both parents feed the chicks with crop milk. Multiple broods may be raised and nests built by the pair or by others may be reused.Nest reuse may possibly increase the chances of predation.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In Japan the principal predators of nestlings and eggs were crows and magpies and to a lesser extent cats and snakes.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oriental_turtle_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Oriental turtle dove is granivorous, and feeds on the seeds of <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sunflower" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunflower">sunflower</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">millet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amaranth" title="Amaranth">amaranth</a>. It also consumes <a href="/wiki/Gastropods" class="mw-redirect" title="Gastropods">gastropods</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> They forage on the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parasites_and_diseases">Parasites and diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oriental_turtle_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Parasites and diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nests in Japan that have accumulated faeces have been found to host a number of flies belonging to the families <a href="/wiki/Sphaeroceridae" title="Sphaeroceridae">Sphaeroceridae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sepsidae" title="Sepsidae">Sepsidae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stratiomyidae" title="Stratiomyidae">Stratiomyidae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Subcutaneous mites of the species <i>Hypodectes propus</i> have been recorded in Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Deaths of a number of birds due to <a href="/wiki/Avipoxvirus" title="Avipoxvirus">avian pox</a> virus have been recorded in South Korea.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_associations">Other associations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oriental_turtle_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other associations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On Hongdo Island, Korea, migratory doves were found to carry seeds of the exotic <i><a href="/wiki/Achyranthes_japonica" title="Achyranthes japonica">Achyranthes japonica</a></i> attached to their plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
