<p>The <b>blackstart</b> (<i>Oenanthe melanura</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Chat_(bird)" title="Chat (bird)">chat</a> found in <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> regions in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Resident_bird" class="mw-redirect" title="Resident bird">resident</a> throughout its <a href="/wiki/Range_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Range (biology)">range</a>.
</p><p>The blackstart is 14&#160;cm long and is named for its black tail, which is frequently fanned; the rest of its plumage is bluish-grey or grey-brown (North African races being browner, Middle Eastern races bluer). The sexes are similar. The song is a clear melancholy whistle: <i>CHURlee...TRUloo...CHURlee...TRUlur...</i>, with short phrases from the song used as a call.
</p><p>It is a species of rocky deserts and mountain slopes which nests in a rock crevice where it lays its 3–4 eggs. It feeds on insects, taken mainly on the ground. The blackstart is a confident species, unafraid of man.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackstart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">formal description</a> of the blackstart was by the Dutch zoologist <a href="/wiki/Coenraad_Jacob_Temminck" title="Coenraad Jacob Temminck">Coenraad Jacob Temminck</a> in 1824 under the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> <i>Saxicola melanura</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The blackstart was included as the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i>Cercomela</i> introduced by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lucien_Bonaparte" title="Charles Lucien Bonaparte">Charles Lucien Bonaparte</a> in 1856.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular phylogenetic">Molecular phylogenetic</a> studies published in 2010 and 2012 found that the genus <i>Cercomela</i> was <a href="/wiki/Polyphyletic" class="mw-redirect" title="Polyphyletic">polyphyletic</a> with five species, including the blackstart, phylogenetically nested within the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Oenanthe_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Oenanthe (bird)">Oenanthe</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-outlaw2010_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-outlaw2010-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> As part of a reorganization of the species to create <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monotypic">monotypic</a> genera, the blackstart was assigned to the genus <i>Oenanthe</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ioc_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific epithet</a> <i>melanura</i> is from the <a href="/wiki/Classical_Greek" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical Greek">classical Greek</a> <i>melanouros</i> meaning "with a black tail" from <i>melas</i> "black" and <i>oura</i> "tail".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are 6 <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>O. m. melanura</i> (<a href="/wiki/Coenraad_Jacob_Temminck" title="Coenraad Jacob Temminck">Temminck</a>, 1824) – northeast Egypt to Israel, Jordan and central Saudi Arabia</li>
<li><i>O. m. neumanni</i> (<a href="/wiki/Sidney_Dillon_Ripley" title="Sidney Dillon Ripley">Ripley</a>, 1952) – southwest Saudi Arabia, Yemen and Oman</li>
<li><i>O. m. lypura</i> (<a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Hemprich" title="Wilhelm Hemprich">Hemprich</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Christian_Gottfried_Ehrenberg" title="Christian Gottfried Ehrenberg">Ehrenberg</a>, 1833) – north-central Sudan to Eritrea</li>
<li><i>O. m. aussae</i> (<a href="/wiki/Wilfred_Thesiger" title="Wilfred Thesiger">Thesiger</a> &amp; Meynell, 1934) – northeast Ethiopia, Djibouti and north Somalia</li>
<li><i>O. m. airensis</i> (<a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a>, 1921) – north Niger to central Sudan</li>
<li><i>O. m. ultima</i> (<a href="/wiki/George_Latimer_Bates" title="George Latimer Bates">Bates</a>, 1933) – east Mali and west Niger</li></ul>
<p>The subspecies differ slightly in the colour of their plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackstart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The blackstart is around 14&#160;cm (5.5&#160;in) in length with a wingspan of 23–27&#160;cm (9.1–10.6&#160;in) and a weight of around 15&#160;g (0.53&#160;oz).  The <a href="/wiki/Nominate_race" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate race">nominate race</a> <i>O. m. melanura</i> has blueish ash-grey upperparts with darker wings and a black rump and tail. The belly and under-wings are greyish white; the bill and legs are black. The sexes are similar. The North African subspecies <i>O. m. lypura</i> and <i>O. m. airensis</i> are browner than the nominate.<sup id="cite_ref-cramp_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cramp-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackstart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>In Egypt, the blackstart is common in the <a href="/wiki/Sinai_peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Sinai peninsula">Sinai peninsula</a></li>
<li>In Israel the species is primarily found in the <a href="/wiki/Negev_Desert" class="mw-redirect" title="Negev Desert">Negev Desert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arava_Valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Arava Valley">Arava Valley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a> areas; it is present in areas of the <a href="/wiki/Jordan_River" title="Jordan River">Jordan River</a> valley further north, but is scarcer there.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>, the species is only found in western parts of the country, in areas from the Jordan River valley south through the Dead Sea region to the Arava Valley and Aqaba Mountains.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackstart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The blackstart is monogamous and pairs remain on their breeding territory throughout the year.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The nest is built entirely by the female and can be placed between boulders, in a rock crevice or in a disused burrow.<sup id="cite_ref-leader_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leader-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> The nest is a shallow cup of grass and leaves lined with hair and fine plant material.<sup id="cite_ref-cramp_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cramp-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The female usually places small pebbles around the entrance of the nest. The 3 or 4 pale blue eggs with fine red-brown speckles are laid at daily intervals.<sup id="cite_ref-cramp_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cramp-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-leader_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leader-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> They measure 19.6&#160;mm ×&#160;14.7&#160;mm (0.77&#160;in ×&#160;0.58&#160;in) and weigh 2.26&#160;g (0.080&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-cramp_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cramp-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> They are incubated by the female and hatch after 13–14 days. The chicks are fed by both parents and fledge after around 14 days. Up to three broods can be raised in a year.<sup id="cite_ref-leader_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leader-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A study conducted in Israel on the west shore of the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a> found that a major cause of nest failure was the predation of the eggs by <a href="/wiki/Golden_spiny_mouse" title="Golden spiny mouse">Golden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cairo_spiny_mouse" title="Cairo spiny mouse">Cairo spiny mice</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-leader_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leader-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
