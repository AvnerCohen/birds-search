<p>The <b>rock sparrow</b> or <b>rock petronia</b> (<i>Petronia petronia</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird in the sparrow family <a href="/wiki/Passeridae" class="mw-redirect" title="Passeridae">Passeridae</a>. It is the only member of the genus <i>Petronia</i>. It breeds on barren rocky hills from the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Iberian peninsula">Iberian peninsula</a> and western north Africa across southern Europe and through the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>  Siberia and north and central China. It is largely resident in the west of its range, but Asian birds <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> to more southerly areas, or move down the mountains.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_sparrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">formal description</a> of the rock sparrow was by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1766 in the <a href="/wiki/12th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="12th edition of Systema Naturae">twelfth edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>. He introduced the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Fringilla petronia</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is now the only species in the genus <i>Petronia</i> that was introduced by the German naturalist <a href="/wiki/Johann_Jakob_Kaup" title="Johann Jakob Kaup">Johann Jakob Kaup</a> in 1829.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ioc_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <i>Petronia</i> is a local name for the rock sparrow from the <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a> area of Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Seven <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are recognised:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>P. p. petronia</i> (Linnaeus, 1766) – Madeira and Canary Islands, south Europe to west Turkey</li>
<li><i>P. p. barbara</i> <a href="/wiki/Carlo_von_Erlanger" title="Carlo von Erlanger">Erlanger</a>, 1899 – northwest Africa</li>
<li><i>P. p. puteicola</i> Festa, 1894 – south Turkey to Jordan</li>
<li><i>P. p. exigua</i> (<a href="/wiki/Carl_Eduard_Hellmayr" title="Carl Eduard Hellmayr">Hellmayr</a>, 1902) – central Turkey to the Caucasus, north Iran and north Iraq</li>
<li><i>P. p. kirhizica</i> <a href="/wiki/Petr_Sushkin" title="Petr Sushkin">Sushkin</a>, 1925 – Caspian Sea to Kyrgyzstan</li>
<li><i>P. p. intermedia</i> <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Hartert" title="Ernst Hartert">Hartert</a>, 1901 – Iran and north Afghanistan to northwest China</li>
<li><i>P. p. brevirostris</i> <a href="/wiki/W%C5%82adys%C5%82aw_Taczanowski" title="Władysław Taczanowski">Taczanowski</a>, 1874 – Mongolia, south central Siberia and north and central China</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_sparrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The rock sparrow is similar in size to a <a href="/wiki/House_sparrow" title="House sparrow">house sparrow</a> but with a larger more conical bill.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994371_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994371-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It is around 14&#160;cm (5.5&#160;in) in length, with a strong whitish <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a> and weaker crown stripe. It has a patterned brown back and wings, streaked underparts, and a diagnostic, but hard-to-see, yellow throat spot. <i>Petronia petronia</i> are monochromatic, with a distinctive yellow patch on their upper breast that starkly contrasts the earth tones of their plumage. This <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">carotenoid</a>-based trait is present in both sexes, and plays an important role during the breeding season, signalling both attractiveness and social status.<sup id="cite_ref-garcia_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-garcia-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This bird has a loud wheezy song.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_sparrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is a rare vagrant north of its breeding range. There is just a single record from Great Britain, at <a href="/wiki/Cley_next_the_Sea" title="Cley next the Sea">Cley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a> on 14 June 1981.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This gregarious bird is also found in human settlements in suitable country.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_sparrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_sparrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It nests in crevices in rocks or walls, laying four to five <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994396_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994396-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Rock sparrows exhibit a variety of mating patterns, most notably monogamy and sequential and simultaneous <a href="/wiki/Polyandry" title="Polyandry">polyandry</a>; however, social monogamy is the most abundant mating pattern. The frequencies of these various mating patterns most likely vary with numerous ecological and social factors. Many studies have shown that both males and females prefer a mate with a larger yellow patch. It has also been shown that male brood defence behaviours increase with greater female ornamentation. Males also differentially allocate parental investment according to female ornamentation; this behaviour is not observed in females. In alpine colonies of <i>Petronia petronia</i>, females perform most of the provisioning. In Asian colonies, both males and females contribute equally to the care of the young. Males of larger sizes feed their young at higher rates, which suggests that larger males occupy better territories and/or are better fathers. Studies have found a positive correlation between male yellow breast patch size and nestling tarsus length, which suggests that more ornamented males are also better parents. Females increase the number of non-feeding visits to their nest as the season progresses, suggesting that because females have lesser opportunities to lay other clutches, it is most advantageous to support the survival of their current offspring. A positive relationship between the number of deserting females and the number of available males has been recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-garcia_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-garcia-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_feeding">Food and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rock_sparrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Food and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The rock sparrow mainly forages on the ground. It eats seeds throughout the year and berries in autumn. In the spring its diet includes invertebrates, particularly caterpillars and grasshoppers. These are also fed to the young.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECramp1994374_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramp1994374-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
