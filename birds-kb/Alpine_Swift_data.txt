<p>The <b>Alpine swift</b> (<i>Tachymarptis melba</i>) formerly <i>Apus melba</i>, is a species of <a href="/wiki/Swift" title="Swift">swift</a>. The genus name is from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>takhus</i>, "fast", and <i>marptis</i>, "seizer". The specific name <i>melba</i> comes from ‘melano-alba’ or ‘mel-alba’; Eigenhuis &amp; Swaab (1992) posited that ‘melba’ might be a short form for ‘melano-alba’ or ‘mel-alba’ (Gr. melas, melanos = black; L. albus = white). Linnaeus certainly referred to these two colors in his diagnosis.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Alpine swifts breed in mountains from southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Himalaya" class="mw-redirect" title="Himalaya">Himalaya</a>. Like <a href="/wiki/Common_swift" title="Common swift">common swifts</a>, they are strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, and winter much further south in southern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.
</p><p>Swifts have very short legs which are used for clinging to vertical surfaces. They never settle voluntarily on the ground, spending most of their lives in the air living on the insects they catch in their beaks. Alpine swift are able to stay aloft in the air for up to seven months at a time, even drinking water "on the wing".
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_biology">Description and biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpine_swift&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description and biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The bird is superficially similar to a large <a href="/wiki/Barn_swallow" title="Barn swallow">barn swallow</a> or <a href="/wiki/Common_house_martin" title="Common house martin">house martin</a>. It is, however, completely unrelated to those <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> species, since swifts are in the order <a href="/wiki/Apodiformes" title="Apodiformes">Apodiformes</a>. The resemblances between the groups are due to <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">convergent evolution</a>, reflecting similar life styles.
</p><p>Swifts have very short legs which they use only for clinging to vertical surfaces. The scientific name Apodidae comes from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>απους, apous</i>, meaning "without feet". They never settle voluntarily on the ground.
</p><p>Alpine swifts breed in mountains from southern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Himalaya" class="mw-redirect" title="Himalaya">Himalaya</a>. Like <a href="/wiki/Common_swift" title="Common swift">common swifts</a>, they are strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, and winter much further south in southern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. They wander widely on migration, and are regularly seen in much of southern Europe, Salford and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. The species seems to have been much more widespread during the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Last glacial period">last ice age</a>, with a large colony breeding, for example in the Late <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> Cave No 16, <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, around 18,000–40,000 years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The same situation has been found for Komarowa Cave near <a href="/wiki/Cz%C4%99stochowa" title="Częstochowa">Częstochowa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> during a period about 20,000–40,000 years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>These apodiformes build their nests in colonies in a suitable cliff hole or cave, laying two or three eggs. Swifts will return to the same sites year after year, rebuilding their nests when necessary, and pairing for life. Young swifts in the nest can drop their body temperature and become torpid if bad weather prevents their parents from catching insects nearby. They have adapted well to urban conditions, frequently nesting in old buildings in towns around the Mediterranean, where large, low-flying flocks are a familiar feature in summer. Alpine swifts have a short forked tail and very long swept-back wings that resemble a crescent or a <a href="/wiki/Boomerang" title="Boomerang">boomerang</a> but may (as in the image) be held stretched straight out. Their flight is slower and more powerful than that of their smaller relatives, with a call that is a drawn-out twittering (listen at right).
</p><p>Alpine swifts are readily distinguished from the common swifts by their larger size and their white belly and throat. They are around twice as big as most other swifts in their range, about 20 to 23&#160;cm (7.9 to 9.1&#160;in) in length, with a wingspan of 57&#160;cm (22&#160;in) and a weight of around 100&#160;g (3.5&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> By comparison, the <a href="/wiki/Common_swift" title="Common swift">common swift</a> has a wingspan of around 42&#160;cm (17&#160;in). They're largely dark brown in colour with a dark neck band that separates the white throat from the white belly.
Juveniles are similar to adults, but their feathers are pale edged.<sup id="cite_ref-Stevenson_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stevenson-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_on_the_wing">Life on the wing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpine_swift&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Life on the wing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Alpine swifts spend most of their lives in the air, living on the insects they catch in their beaks. They drink on the wing, but roost on vertical cliffs or walls. A study published in 2013 showed Alpine swifts can spend over six months flying without having to land.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> All vital physiological processes, including sleep, can be performed while in air.
</p><p>In 2011, Felix Liechti and his colleagues at the <a href="/wiki/Sempach_Bird_Observatory" class="mw-redirect" title="Sempach Bird Observatory">Swiss Ornithological Institute</a> attached electronic tags that log movement to six alpine swifts and it was discovered that the birds could stay aloft in the air for more than 200 days straight.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
