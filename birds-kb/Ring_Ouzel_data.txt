<p>The <b>ring ouzel</b> (<i>Turdus torquatus</i>) is a European member of the <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a> family, Turdidae.  It is the mountain equivalent of the closely related <a href="/wiki/Common_blackbird" title="Common blackbird">common blackbird</a>, and breeds in gullies, rocky areas or scree slopes.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ring_ouzel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Ouzel" (or "ousel") is an old name for <a href="/wiki/Common_blackbird" title="Common blackbird">common blackbird</a>. The word is cognate with German "Amsel". "Ouzel" may also be applied to a group of superficially similar but more distantly related birds, the <a href="/wiki/Dippers" class="mw-redirect" title="Dippers">dippers</a>, the European representative of which is sometimes known as the <a href="/wiki/White-throated_dipper" title="White-throated dipper">water ouzel</a>.
</p><p>As with the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> name, the scientific name also refers to the male's obvious white neck crescent, being derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> words <i>turdus</i>, "thrush", and <i>torquatus</i>, "collared".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ring_ouzel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>About 65 species of medium to large thrushes are in the genus <i>Turdus</i>, characterised by rounded heads, longish, pointed wings, and usually melodious songs. Although two European thrushes, the <a href="/wiki/Song_thrush" title="Song thrush">song thrush</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mistle_thrush" title="Mistle thrush">mistle thrush</a>, are early offshoots from the Eurasian lineage of <i>Turdus</i> thrushes after they spread north from Africa, the ring ousel is descended from ancestors that had colonised the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> islands from Africa and subsequently reached Europe from there.<sup id="cite_ref-reilly_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reilly-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ring_ouzel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The adult male is all black except for a white crescent on the breast and a yellowish bill. The wings have a silvery appearance due to white feather edgings. The male sings its loud and mournful song from trees or rocks.
</p><p>The female is similar but duller, and younger birds often lack the breast crescent. The juvenile has brown <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>.
</p><p>This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his landmark 1758 <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a> under its current scientific name.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ring_ouzel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It breeds in the higher regions of western and central Europe and also in the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> mountains. Most populations are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> region. It is declining in parts of its range, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, where breeding is now largely confined to two counties, <a href="/wiki/County_Donegal" title="County Donegal">Donegal</a> and <a href="/wiki/County_Kerry" title="County Kerry">Kerry</a>, and there are fears that it may soon become extinct there.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Turdus_torquatus_nhm.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Turdus_torquatus_nhm.jpg/220px-Turdus_torquatus_nhm.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="428" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Turdus_torquatus_nhm.jpg/330px-Turdus_torquatus_nhm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Turdus_torquatus_nhm.jpg/440px-Turdus_torquatus_nhm.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1891" data-file-height="3680" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Turdus_torquatus_nhm.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ring ouzel bird-mount preparation</div></div></div>
<p>It is territorial and normally seen alone or in pairs, although loose flocks may form on migration. When not breeding, several birds may also be loosely associated in good feeding areas, such as a fruiting tree, often with other thrushes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ring_ouzel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The ring ouzel is omnivorous, eating a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a> and berries.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nesting">Nesting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ring_ouzel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nesting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Turdus_torquatus_MWNH_2255.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Turdus_torquatus_MWNH_2255.JPG/220px-Turdus_torquatus_MWNH_2255.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Turdus_torquatus_MWNH_2255.JPG/330px-Turdus_torquatus_MWNH_2255.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Turdus_torquatus_MWNH_2255.JPG/440px-Turdus_torquatus_MWNH_2255.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Turdus_torquatus_MWNH_2255.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>The ring ouzel nests in bushes or amongst rocks, laying several pale blue <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>, mottled with brown, in a neat cup-shaped nest.
</p>
