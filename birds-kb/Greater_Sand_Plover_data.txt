<p>The <b>Greater Sand Plover</b> (<i>Charadrius leschenaultii</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Plover" title="Plover">plover</a> family of birds. The spelling is commonly given as "Greater Sandplover", but the official <a href="/wiki/British_Ornithologists%27_Union" title="British Ornithologists' Union">British Ornithologists' Union</a> spelling is "Greater Sand Plover".</p>
<p>It breeds in the semi-deserts of <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and eastwards through <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>. It nests in a bare ground <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest#Scrape" title="Bird nest">scrape</a>. This species is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering on sandy beaches in east <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, south Asia and <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a>. It is a rare <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrant</a> in western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, where it has been recorded as far west as <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>. It has been spotted twice in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">Western Hemisphere</a>, the most recent being on May 14, 2009, in <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville,_Florida" title="Jacksonville, Florida">Jacksonville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-myref34_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-myref34-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>There are three <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>: The <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" title="Nominate subspecies" class="mw-redirect">nominate</a>, <i>C. l. columbinus</i> and <i>C. l. scythicus</i>. The last was known as <i>C. l. crassirostris</i> until it was established that this name is pre-occupied by a subspecies of <a href="/wiki/Wilson%27s_Plover" title="Wilson's Plover">Wilson's Plover</a>, <i>C. w. crassirostris</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This chunky plover is long-legged and thick-billed. Breeding males have grey backs and white underparts. The breast, forehead and nape are chestnut, and there is a black eye mask. The female is duller, and winter and juvenile birds lack the chestnut, apart from a hint of <a href="/wiki/Rufous" title="Rufous">rufous</a> on the head. Legs are greenish and the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a> black.</p>
<p>In all <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumages</a>, it is very similar to <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Sand_Plover" title="Lesser Sand Plover">Lesser Sand Plover</a>, <i>Charadrius mongolus</i>. Separating the species may be straightforward in mixed wintering flocks on an <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> beach, where the difference in size and structure is obvious; it is another thing altogether to identify a lone vagrant to western Europe, where both species are very rare. The problem is compounded in that the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle Eastern</a> race of the greater sand plover is the most similar to the lesser species.</p>
<p>Its food consists of <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Annelid" title="Annelid">annelid</a> worms, which are obtained by a run-and-pause technique, rather than the steady probing of some other wader groups.</p>
<p>Its <a href="/wiki/Bird_call" title="Bird call" class="mw-redirect">flight call</a> is a soft trill.</p>
<p>Its scientific name commemorates the French botanist <a href="/wiki/Jean_Baptiste_Leschenault_de_la_Tour" title="Jean Baptiste Leschenault de la Tour" class="mw-redirect">Jean Baptiste Leschenault de la Tour</a>.</p>
<p>The Greater Sand Plover is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>
