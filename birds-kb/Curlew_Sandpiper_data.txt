<p>The <b>Curlew Sandpiper</b> (<i>Calidris ferruginea</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> that breeds on the <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> of <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. It is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering mainly in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, but also in south and southeast <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrant</a> to <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Curlew_Sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>These birds are small waders, only slightly larger than <a href="/wiki/Dunlin" title="Dunlin">Dunlin</a> at 19.5–21&#160;cm in length, but differ from Dunlin in having a longer down-curved <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a>, longer neck and legs and a white rump. The breeding adult has patterned dark grey upperparts and brick-red underparts. In winter, this bird is pale grey above and white below, and shows an obvious white <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a>. Juveniles have a grey and brown back, a white belly and a peach-coloured breast.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Curlew_Sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<p>The male Curlew Sandpiper performs an aerial display during courtship. The clutch of 3–4 eggs are laid in ground scrape in the tundra and taiga, mostly in Siberia.</p>
<p>It is extremely difficult to measure breeding success or population trends in their breeding grounds because nests are scattered over a vast region and their positions influenced by localised weather.</p>
<p>Of all shorebird species, the curlew sandpiper has the smallest breeding range in relation to its non-breeding range. After breeding these birds migrate south to Africa, Australasia or India. South Africa is at the southern limit of the migration path from Siberia, 15 000&#160;km or 130 degrees of latitude away.</p>
<p>This wader is highly gregarious, and will form flocks with other calidrid waders, particularly <a href="/wiki/Dunlin" title="Dunlin">Dunlin</a>. Despite its easterly breeding range, this species is regular on passage in western Europe, presumably because of the southwesterly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migration</a> route.</p>
<p>It <a href="/wiki/Forage" title="Forage">forages</a> in soft mud on <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a> and the coast, mainly picking up food by sight. It mostly eats <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and other small <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>.</p>
<p>The numbers of this species (and of <a href="/wiki/Little_Stint" title="Little Stint">Little Stint</a>) depend on the population of <a href="/wiki/Lemming" title="Lemming">lemmings</a>. In poor lemming years, predatory species such as <a href="/wiki/Skua" title="Skua">skuas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snowy_Owl" title="Snowy Owl">Snowy Owls</a> will take Arctic-breeding waders instead.</p>
<p>Counts of the birds in South Africa, specifically at Langebaan Lagoon where they are most numerous, indicate a 40 percent decline in numbers between 1975 and 2009. A similar trend has been noted in Australia and may be linked to effects of global warming at the breeding grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This species occasionally <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridizes</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Sharp-tailed_Sandpiper" title="Sharp-tailed Sandpiper">Sharp-tailed Sandpiper</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pectoral_Sandpiper" title="Pectoral Sandpiper">Pectoral Sandpiper</a>, producing the presumed "species" called "<b><a href="/wiki/Hybridisation_in_shorebirds#.22Cooper.27s_Sandpiper.22" title="Hybridisation in shorebirds">Cooper's Sandpiper</a></b>" (<i>"Calidris"</i> × <i>cooperi</i>) and "<b><a href="/wiki/Cox%27s_Sandpiper" title="Cox's Sandpiper">Cox's Sandpiper</a></b>" (<i>"Calidris"</i> × <i>paramelanotos</i>), respectively.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Curlew_Sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>This is a fairly unusual species, and has been proposed as <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a> of the genus <i>Erolia</i> but the <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">DNA sequence</a> data is currently insufficient to resolve its relationships (Thomas <i>et al.</i>, 2004). This matter is of taxonomic relevance since, as the Curlew Sandpiper is the type species, a close relationship with the small "<a href="/wiki/Stint" title="Stint">stint</a>" sandpipers would preclude the use of <i>Erolia</i> for the present species.</p>
<p>The Curlew Sandpiper is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies.</p>
