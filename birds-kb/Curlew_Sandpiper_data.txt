<p>The <b>curlew sandpiper</b> (<i>Calidris ferruginea</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a> that breeds on the <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> of <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The genus name is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>kalidris</i> or <i>skalidris</i>, a term used by <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> for some grey-coloured waterside birds. The specific <i>ferruginea</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>ferrugo, ferruginis</i>, "iron rust" referring to its colour in breeding plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering mainly in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, but also in south and southeast <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It is a <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(biology)" title="Vagrancy (biology)">vagrant</a> to <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.
</p>

<p>These birds are small waders, similar to <a href="/wiki/Dunlin" title="Dunlin">dunlins</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> but differ in having a longer down-curved beak, longer neck and legs and a white rump. They have a length of 18–23&#160;cm (7.1–9.1&#160;in), weight of 44-117 g<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and wingspan of 38–41&#160;cm (15–16&#160;in). The breeding adult has patterned dark grey upperparts and brick-red underparts. In winter, this bird is pale grey above and white below, and shows an obvious white <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a>. Juveniles have a grey and brown back, a white belly and a peach-coloured breast.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Curlew_sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The male curlew sandpiper performs an aerial display during courtship. The clutch of 3–4 eggs are laid in ground scrape in the tundra and taiga, mostly in Siberia.
</p><p>It is extremely difficult to measure breeding success or population trends in their breeding grounds because nests are scattered over a vast region and their positions influenced by localised weather.
</p><p>Of all shorebird species, the curlew sandpiper has the smallest breeding range in relation to its non-breeding range. After breeding these birds migrate south to Africa, Australasia or India. South Africa is at the southern limit of the migration path from Siberia, 15,000&#160;km (9,300&#160;mi) or 130° of latitude away.
</p><p>This wader is highly gregarious, and will form flocks with other calidrid waders, particularly <a href="/wiki/Dunlin" title="Dunlin">dunlin</a>. Despite its easterly breeding range, this species is regular on passage in western Europe, presumably because of the southwesterly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migration</a> route.
</p><p>It <a href="/wiki/Forage" title="Forage">forages</a> in soft mud on <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a> and the coast, mainly picking up food by sight. It mostly eats <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and other small <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>.
</p><p>The numbers of this species (and of <a href="/wiki/Little_stint" title="Little stint">little stint</a>) depend on the population of <a href="/wiki/Lemming" title="Lemming">lemmings</a>. In poor lemming years, predatory species such as <a href="/wiki/Skua" title="Skua">skuas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snowy_owl" title="Snowy owl">snowy owls</a> will take Arctic-breeding waders instead.
</p><p>Counts of the birds in South Africa, specifically at Langebaan Lagoon where they are most numerous, indicate a 40% decline in numbers between 1975 and 2009. A similar trend has been noted in Australia and may be linked to <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Effects of global warming">effects of global warming</a> at the breeding grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-deVilliers2009_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deVilliers2009-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This species occasionally <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridizes</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Sharp-tailed_sandpiper" title="Sharp-tailed sandpiper">sharp-tailed sandpiper</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pectoral_sandpiper" title="Pectoral sandpiper">pectoral sandpiper</a>, producing the presumed "species" called "<a href="/wiki/Hybridisation_in_shorebirds#Cooper&#39;s_sandpiper" title="Hybridisation in shorebirds">Cooper's sandpiper</a>" (<i>"Calidris"</i> × <i>cooperi</i>) and "<a href="/wiki/Cox%27s_sandpiper" title="Cox&#39;s sandpiper">Cox's sandpiper</a>" (<i>"Calidris"</i> × <i>paramelanotos</i>), respectively.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Curlew_sandpiper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is a fairly unusual species, and has been proposed as <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a> of the genus <i>Erolia</i> but the <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" class="mw-redirect" title="DNA sequence">DNA sequence</a> data is currently insufficient to resolve its relationships.<sup id="cite_ref-Thomas2004_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas2004-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This matter is of taxonomic relevance since, as the curlew sandpiper is the type species, a close relationship with the small "<a href="/wiki/Stint" title="Stint">stint</a>" sandpipers would preclude the use of <i>Erolia</i> for the present species.
</p><p>The curlew sandpiper is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>
