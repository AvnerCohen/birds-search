<p>The <b>tree pipit</b> (<i>Anthus trivialis</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> which breeds across most of Europe and the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> as far East as the <a href="/wiki/East_Siberian_Mountains" title="East Siberian Mountains">East Siberian Mountains</a>. It is a long-distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a> moving in winter to Africa and southern Asia. The scientific name is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>. <i>Anthus</i> is the name for a small bird of grasslands, and the specific <i> trivialis</i> means "common", from <i>trivium</i>, "public street".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This is a small <a href="/wiki/Pipit" title="Pipit">pipit</a>, which resembles <a href="/wiki/Meadow_pipit" title="Meadow pipit">meadow pipit</a>. It is an undistinguished-looking species, streaked brown above and with black markings on a white belly and buff breast below. It can be distinguished from the slightly smaller meadow pipit by its heavier bill and greater contrast between its buff breast and white belly. Tree pipits more readily perch in trees.
</p><p>The call is a strong <i>spek</i>, unlike the weak call of its relative. The song flight is unmistakable. The bird rises a short distance up from a tree, and then parachutes down on stiff wings, the song becoming more drawn out towards the end.
</p><p>The breeding habitat is open woodland and scrub. The nest is on the ground, with 4–8 eggs being laid. This species is insectivorous, like its relatives, but will also take seeds.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle">Life cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tree_pipit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>mid-September to mid-April: lives in sub Saharan Africa</li>
<li>mid April to beginning of May: migrates and arrives in countries such as the United Kingdom</li>
<li>beginning of May to August: breeding season, two <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">broods</a></li>
<li>August to mid September: flies back to Saharan Africas</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management_and_conservation">Management and conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tree_pipit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Management and conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>They breed in habitats with a wooded component, including <a href="/wiki/Lowland_heath" title="Lowland heath">Lowland heath</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coppice" class="mw-redirect" title="Coppice">coppice</a>.  They are found mostly in open <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a> woodland on the boundary with <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moorland</a>, or open structured oak woodland – therefore heavy thinning is required to produce a gappy character.  They prefer low canopy medium-sized trees, where there is low-growing scrub and bramble less than 2 metres high, so that horizontal visibility is relatively high.  They like a mosaic of grass and bracken, but not very grazed short turf, so light to moderate grazing is preferred.  Glades are also valuable, and streams are preferred.
</p><p>Once they have arrived they nest on the ground amongst <a href="/wiki/Grass" class="mw-redirect" title="Grass">grass</a> or <a href="/wiki/Calluna" title="Calluna">heather</a> tussocks. They forage on <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> found in the ground vegetation.
</p><p>They need scattered trees as song perches.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
