<p>The <b>Siberian stonechat</b> or <b>Asian stonechat</b> (<i>Saxicola maurus</i>) is a recently validated <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">Old World flycatcher</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> (Muscicapidae). Like the other <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a>-like <a href="/wiki/Old_World_flycatcher" title="Old World flycatcher">flycatchers</a>, it was often placed in the <a href="/wiki/Turdidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Turdidae">Turdidae</a> in the past. It breeds in the East <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> including in easternmost Europe and winters in the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>.
</p>

<h2><span id="Description.2C_systematics_and_taxonomy"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Description,_systematics_and_taxonomy">Description, systematics and taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siberian_stonechat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description, systematics and taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It resembles its closest living relative the <a href="/wiki/European_stonechat" title="European stonechat">European stonechat</a> (<i>S. rubicola</i>), but is typically darker above and paler below, with a white rump and whiter underparts with less orange on the breast. The male in breeding plumage has black upperparts and head (lacking the brownish tones of the European stonechat), a conspicuous white collar, scapular patch and rump, and a restricted area of orange on the throat.<sup id="cite_ref-rsub779202_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rsub779202-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The female has pale brown upperparts and head, white neck patches (not a full collar), and a pale, unstreaked pinkish-yellow rump. Males in winter plumage are intermediate between summer males and females, with a <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a> resembling the <a href="/wiki/Whinchat" title="Whinchat">whinchat</a> (<i>S. rubetra</i>); from this species and the female it can be distinguished by the full white collar.<sup id="cite_ref-rsub779202_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rsub779202-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>If seen at close distance, it can be recognized that its primary <a href="/wiki/Remiges" class="mw-redirect" title="Remiges">remiges</a> are distinctly longer than in <i>S. rubicola</i>. In this, it closely resembles the whinchat, which like <i>S. maurus</i> is adapted to long-distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rsub779202_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rsub779202-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The male has a clicking call, like two <a href="/wiki/Pebble" title="Pebble">pebbles</a> knocked together. The song is high and twittering like the <a href="/wiki/Dunnock" title="Dunnock">dunnock</a> (<i>Prunella modularis</i>), an unrelated <a href="/wiki/Passerida" title="Passerida">passeridan</a> <a href="/wiki/Songbird" title="Songbird">songbird</a> belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Passeroidea" class="mw-redirect" title="Passeroidea">Passeroidea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ub2002_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ub2002-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are five or six <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>, with <i>S. m. maurus</i> (described above) and the distinct but similar <i>S. m. stejnegeri</i> found across northern and central Asia. The southern <i>S. m. variegatus</i> (west of the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>), <i>S. m. armenicus</i> (eastern Turkey to Iran), <i>S. m. indicus</i> (<a href="/wiki/Himalaya" class="mw-redirect" title="Himalaya">Himalaya</a>) and the <b>Turkestan stonechat</b> <i>S. m. przewalskii</i> (southwest China) are distinguished by larger white areas on the plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the past, <i>S. maurus</i> was usually included in <i>S. torquatus</i> as part of the "<a href="/wiki/Common_stonechat" title="Common stonechat">common stonechat</a>", but that <a href="/wiki/Scientific_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific name">scientific name</a> nowadays is restricted to the <a href="/wiki/African_stonechat" title="African stonechat">African stonechat</a>. Analysis of <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" class="mw-redirect" title="MtDNA">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome <i>b</i></a> <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" class="mw-redirect" title="DNA sequence">sequence</a> and <a href="/wiki/NDNA" class="mw-redirect" title="NDNA">nDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Microsatellite_(genetics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Microsatellite (genetics)">microsatellite</a> <a href="/wiki/DNA_fingerprinting" class="mw-redirect" title="DNA fingerprinting">fingerprinting</a> data, though not unequivocal, together with the evidence from <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Behaviour" class="mw-redirect" title="Behaviour">behaviour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a> seems to indicate that the present bird can be considered a distinct <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>. The European stonechat is its western <a href="/wiki/Sister_species" class="mw-redirect" title="Sister species">sister species</a> in the Eurasian lineage of stonechats; their ancestors separated during the <a href="/wiki/Late_Pliocene" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Pliocene">Late Pliocene</a> or <a href="/wiki/Early_Pleistocene" title="Early Pleistocene">Early Pleistocene</a>, roughly 1.5-2.5 <a href="/wiki/Million_years_ago" class="mw-redirect" title="Million years ago">million years ago</a> at the onset of the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">Quaternary glaciation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Its scientific name means "dark rock-dweller". <i>Saxicola</i> derives from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>saxum</i> "rock" + <i>incola</i> "inhabitant"; <i>maurus</i> is Latinized from <a href="/wiki/Greek_(language)" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek (language)">Greek</a> <i>maúros</i> (μαύρος) "black" (cf. "<a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">moor</a>"),<sup id="cite_ref-job_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> in reference to the upperpart colour as compared to <i>S. rubicola</i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_ecology">Distribution and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siberian_stonechat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The breeding range covers most of <a href="/wiki/Temperate" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate">temperate</a> <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, from about <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> 71°N in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> south to the <a href="/wiki/Himalaya" class="mw-redirect" title="Himalaya">Himalaya</a> and southwest China, and west to eastern Turkey and the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a> area. It also breeds in the far northeast of Europe, mainly in Russia but occasionally as far west as Finland.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The wintering range of the <a href="/wiki/Migratory_bird" class="mw-redirect" title="Migratory bird">migratory bird</a> is from southern Japan south to Thailand and India, and west to northeast Africa. On migration, small numbers reach as far west as western Europe, and exceptionally as far east as Alaska in North America.<sup id="cite_ref-rsub779202_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rsub779202-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Siberian stonechat is <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a>. It breeds in open rough scrubland or rough grassland with scattered <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrubs</a>, from sea level to about 4,000 m <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Above mean sea level">ASL</a> or more. The birds seem to avoid even cool <a href="/wiki/Temperate" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate">temperate</a> conditions and stay up north only during the hot <a href="/wiki/Continental_climate" title="Continental climate">continental summer</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Montane" class="mw-redirect" title="Montane">montane</a> regions of the <a href="/wiki/Himalaya" class="mw-redirect" title="Himalaya">Himalaya</a> foothills of Bhutan, migrants can on occasion be seen foraging in fields and pastures more than 2,000 m <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Above mean sea level">ASL</a>, but most move further down and south to winter in <a href="/wiki/Tropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical">tropical</a> regions.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Though it is not considered a distinct <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>, it is widespread and common and would not be considered a threatened species.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
