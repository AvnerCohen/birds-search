<p>The <b>Osprey</b> (<i>Pandion haliaetus</i>), sometimes known as the <b>sea hawk</b>, <b>fish eagle</b> or <b>fish hawk</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Diurnality" title="Diurnality">diurnal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish-eating</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a>. It is a large <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptor</a>, reaching more than 60&#160;cm (24&#160;in) in length and 180&#160;cm (71&#160;in) across the wings. It is brown on the upperparts and predominantly greyish on the head and underparts, with a black eye patch and wings.</p>
<p>The Osprey tolerates a wide variety of <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitats</a>, nesting in any location near a body of water providing an adequate food supply. It is found on all continents except Antarctica although in South America it occurs only as a non-breeding <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>.</p>
<p>As its other common name suggests, the Osprey's diet consists almost exclusively of fish. It possesses specialised physical characteristics and exhibits unique behaviour to assist in hunting and catching <a href="/wiki/Prey" title="Prey" class="mw-redirect">prey</a>. As a result of these unique characteristics, it has been given its own <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy" title="Taxonomy">taxonomic</a> <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a>, <i><b>Pandion</b></i> and <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a>, <b>Pandionidae</b>. Four <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are usually recognized, one of which has recently been given full species status (see below). Despite its propensity to nest near water, the Osprey is not a <a href="/wiki/Sea_eagle" title="Sea eagle">sea-eagle</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The Osprey was one of the many species described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Carolus Linnaeus</a> in his 18th-century work, <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>, and named as <i>Falco haliaeetus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The genus, <i>Pandion</i>, is the sole member of the family of Pandionidae, and used to contain only one species Osprey (<i>P. haliaetus</i>). The genus <i>Pandion</i> was described by the French zoologist <a href="/wiki/Marie_Jules_C%C3%A9sar_Savigny" title="Marie Jules César Savigny">Marie Jules César Savigny</a> in 1809, and is taken from a mythical Greek king, Pandion.<sup id="cite_ref-ITIS_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITIS-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Osprey differs in several respects from other <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_animal" title="Diurnal animal" class="mw-redirect">diurnal</a> birds of prey. Its toes are of equal length, its <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsi</a> are <a href="/wiki/Net_(device)" title="Net (device)">reticulate</a>, and its talons are rounded, rather than grooved. The Osprey and owls are the only raptors whose outer toe is reversible, allowing them to grasp their prey with two toes in front and two behind. This is particularly helpful when they grab slippery fish.<sup id="cite_ref-Terres_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Terres-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> It has always presented something of a riddle to taxonomists, but here it is treated as the sole <a href="/wiki/Extant_taxon" title="Extant taxon">living member</a> of the family Pandionidae, and the family listed in its traditional place as part of the order <a href="/wiki/Falconiformes" title="Falconiformes">Falconiformes</a>. Other schemes place it alongside the hawks and eagles in the family <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a>—which itself can be regarded as making up the bulk of the order <a href="/wiki/Accipitriformes" title="Accipitriformes">Accipitriformes</a> or else be lumped with the <a href="/wiki/Falconidae" title="Falconidae">Falconidae</a> into <a href="/wiki/Falconiformes" title="Falconiformes">Falconiformes</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Sibley-Ahlquist_taxonomy" title="Sibley-Ahlquist taxonomy" class="mw-redirect">Sibley-Ahlquist taxonomy</a> has placed it together with the other diurnal raptors in a greatly enlarged <a href="/wiki/Ciconiiformes" title="Ciconiiformes">Ciconiiformes</a>, but this results in an unnatural <a href="/wiki/Paraphyletic" title="Paraphyletic" class="mw-redirect">paraphyletic</a> classification.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span></h3>

<p>The Osprey is unusual in that it is a single living species that occurs nearly worldwide. Even the few <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are not unequivocally separable. There are four generally recognised subspecies, although differences are small, and <a href="/wiki/Integrated_Taxonomic_Information_System" title="Integrated Taxonomic Information System">ITIS</a> only lists the first two.<sup id="cite_ref-ITIS_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITIS-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>P. h. haliaetus</i> (<a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a>, 1758), <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tesky_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tesky-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. h. carolinensis</i> (<a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Gmelin" title="Johann Friedrich Gmelin">Gmelin</a>, 1788), <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. This form is larger, darker bodied and has a paler breast than nominate <i>haliaetus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tesky_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tesky-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. h. ridgwayi</i> (<a href="/wiki/Charles_Johnson_Maynard" title="Charles Johnson Maynard">Maynard</a>, 1887), <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> islands. This form has a very pale head and breast compared with nominate <i>haliaetus</i>, with only a weak eye mask.<sup id="cite_ref-Tesky_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tesky-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> It is non-migratory. Its scientific name commemorates American <a href="/wiki/Ornithologist" title="Ornithologist" class="mw-redirect">ornithologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Ridgway" title="Robert Ridgway">Robert Ridgway</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. h. cristatus</i> (<a href="/wiki/Louis_Jean_Pierre_Vieillot" title="Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot">Vieillot</a>, 1816), coastline and some large rivers of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>. The smallest and most distinctive subspecies, also non-migratory.<sup id="cite_ref-Tesky_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tesky-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Recently, <i>P. h. cristatus</i> has been given full species status<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> as <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Osprey" title="Eastern Osprey" class="mw-redirect">Eastern Osprey</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fossil record">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_record">Fossil record</span></h3>
<p>To date there have been two <a href="/wiki/Extinct" title="Extinct" class="mw-redirect">extinct</a> species named from the fossil record.<sup id="cite_ref-Avibase_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avibase-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pandion_homalopteron&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pandion homalopteron (page does not exist)">Pandion homalopteron</a></i> was named by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stuart_L._Warter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stuart L. Warter (page does not exist)">Stuart L. Warter</a> in 1976 from fossils of <a href="/wiki/Middle_Miocene" title="Middle Miocene">Middle Miocene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barstovian" title="Barstovian">Barstovian</a> age, found in marine deposits in the southern part of the U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. The second named species <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pandion_lovensis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pandion lovensis (page does not exist)">Pandion lovensis</a></i>, was described in 1985 by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_J._Becker&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jonathan J. Becker (page does not exist)">Jonathan J. Becker</a> from fossils found in the U.S state of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> and dating to the <a href="/wiki/Clarendonian" title="Clarendonian">latest Clarendonian</a> and possibly representing a separate lineage from that of <i>P. homalopteron</i> and <i>P. haliaetus</i>. A number of claw fossils have been recovered from Pliocene and Pleistocene sediments in Florida and <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, USA. The oldest recognized Pandionidae family fossils have been recovered from the Oligocene age <a href="/wiki/Jebel_Qatrani_Formation" title="Jebel Qatrani Formation">Jebel Qatrani Formation</a>, of <a href="/wiki/Faiyum_Governorate" title="Faiyum Governorate">Faiyum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>. However they are not complete enough to assign to a specific genus.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Another Pandionidae claw fossil was recovered from <a href="/wiki/Early_Oligocene" title="Early Oligocene" class="mw-redirect">Early Oligocene</a> deposits in the <a href="/wiki/Mainz" title="Mainz">Mainz</a> basin, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, and was described in 2006 by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerald_Mayr&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gerald Mayr (page does not exist)">Gerald Mayr</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mayr2006_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mayr2006-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h3>
<p>The genus name <i>Pandion</i> is after the mythical Greek king <a href="/wiki/Pandion_II" title="Pandion II">Pandion</a> of <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a> and grandfather of <a href="/wiki/Theseus" title="Theseus">Theseus</a>, who was transformed into an eagle.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The specific epithet <i>haliaetus</i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἁλιάετος</span> "<a href="/wiki/Sea_eagle" title="Sea eagle">sea eagle</a>/Osprey".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The origins of <i>Osprey</i> are obscure;<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> the word itself was first recorded around 1460, derived via the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">Anglo-French</a> <i>ospriet</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> <i>avis prede</i> "bird of prey," from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>avis praedæ</i> though the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a> notes a connection with the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>ossifraga</i> or "bone breaker" of <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> However, this term referred to the <a href="/wiki/Lammergeier" title="Lammergeier" class="mw-redirect">Lammergeier</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The Osprey is 0.9–2.1 kg (2.0–4.6 lb) in weight and 50–66 cm (20–26 in) with a 127–180 cm (50–71 in) wingspan. The subspecies are fairly close in size, with the nominate subspecies averaging 1.53&#160;kg (3.4&#160;lb), <i>P. h. carolinensis</i> averaging 1.7&#160;kg (3.7&#160;lb) and <i>P. h. cristatus</i> averaging 1.25&#160;kg (2.8&#160;lb). The wing chord measures 38 to 52 cm (15 to 20 in), the tail measures 16.5 to 24 cm (6.5 to 9.4 in) and the tarsus is 5.2–6.6 cm (2.0–2.6 in).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The upperparts are a deep, glossy brown, while the breast is white and sometimes streaked with brown, and the underparts are pure white. The head is white with a dark mask across the eyes, reaching to the sides of the neck.<sup id="cite_ref-CTDEP_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CTDEP-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> The irises of the eyes are golden to brown, and the transparent nictitating membrane is pale blue. The bill is black, with a blue <a href="/wiki/Cere" title="Cere" class="mw-redirect">cere</a>, and the feet are white with black talons.<sup id="cite_ref-Terres_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Terres-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> A short tail and long, narrow wings with four long, finger-like feathers, and a shorter fifth, give it a very distinctive appearance.<sup id="cite_ref-Forsman_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forsman-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The sexes appear fairly similar, but the adult male can be distinguished from the female by its slimmer body and narrower wings. The breast band of the male is also weaker than that of the female, or is non-existent, and the underwing coverts of the male are more uniformly pale. It is straightforward to determine the sex in a breeding pair, but harder with individual birds.<sup id="cite_ref-Forsman_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forsman-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The juvenile Osprey may be identified by buff fringes to the plumage of the upperparts, a buff tone to the underparts, and streaked feathers on the head. During spring, barring on the underwings and flight feathers is a better indicator of a young bird, due to wear on the upperparts.<sup id="cite_ref-CTDEP_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CTDEP-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In flight, the Osprey has arched wings and drooping "hands", giving it a <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull-like</a> appearance. The call is a series of sharp whistles, described as <i>cheep, cheep</i> or <i>yewk, yewk</i>. If disturbed by activity near the nest, the call is a frenzied <i>cheereek</i>!<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> <span class="unicode"><a href="/wiki/File:Pandion_haliaetus.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" /></a>&#160;<a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a1/Pandion_haliaetus.ogg" class="internal" title="Pandion haliaetus.ogg">Osprey&#160;call</a><small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">&#160;(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:Pandion_haliaetus.ogg" title="File:Pandion haliaetus.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<div class="rellink boilerplate seealso">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ospreys_in_Britain" title="Ospreys in Britain">Ospreys in Britain</a></div>
<p>The Osprey is the second most widely distributed raptor species, after the <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_Falcon" title="Peregrine Falcon">Peregrine Falcon</a>. It has a worldwide distribution and is found in temperate and tropical regions of all continents except <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>. In <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> it breeds from <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a> south to the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast" title="Gulf Coast" class="mw-redirect">Gulf Coast</a> and <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, wintering further south from the southern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> through to <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bull87_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bull87-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> It is found in summer throughout Europe north into <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, though not Iceland, and winters in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> it is mainly <a href="/wiki/Sedentism" title="Sedentism">sedentary</a> and found patchily around the coastline, though it is a non-breeding visitor to eastern <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Simpsday_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simpsday-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> There is a 1000&#160;km gap, corresponding with the coast of the <a href="/wiki/Nullarbor_Plain" title="Nullarbor Plain">Nullarbor Plain</a>, between its westernmost breeding site in <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a> and the nearest breeding sites to the west in <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dennis2007a_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dennis2007a-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> In the islands of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a> it is found in the <a href="/wiki/Bismarck_Islands" title="Bismarck Islands" class="mw-redirect">Bismarck Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> remains of adults and juveniles have been found in <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a>, where it probably was wiped out by arriving humans.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> It is possible it may once have ranged across <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a> as well. It is an uncommon to fairly common winter visitor to all parts of <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> from <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar" class="mw-redirect">Myanmar</a> through to <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">Indochina</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<p>Ospreys have been known to exhibit great joint flexibility. Their limbs (wings and legs) have adapted to have greater flexibility over time. An example of this is when flying towards bright lights (e.g., sun)—they are able to bend the joint in their wing to shield their eyes from the light to aid safety while flying.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from September 2012">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Osprey_with_fish.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Osprey_with_fish.jpg/220px-Osprey_with_fish.jpg" width="220" height="275" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Osprey_with_fish.jpg/330px-Osprey_with_fish.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Osprey_with_fish.jpg/440px-Osprey_with_fish.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Osprey_with_fish.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Eating a fish</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Fish make up 99% of the Osprey's diet.<sup id="cite_ref-evans82_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-evans82-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> It typically takes fish weighing 150–300 g (5.3–11 oz) and about 25–35 cm (9.8–14 in) in length, but the weight can range from 50 to 2,000 g (1.8 to 71 oz). Virtually any type of fish in that size range are taken.</p>
<p>Ospreys have vision that is well adapted to detecting underwater objects from the air. Prey is first sighted when the Osprey is 10–40 m (33–130 ft) above the water, after which the bird hovers momentarily then plunges feet first into the water.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Osprey is particularly well adapted to this diet, with reversible outer toes, sharp spicules on the underside of the toes,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> closable nostrils to keep out water during dives, and backwards-facing scales on the talons which act as barbs to help hold its catch.</p>
<p>Occasionally, the Osprey may prey on <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>, rabbits, hares, amphibians, other <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> and small <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-UM_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UM-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span></h3>
<p>The Osprey breeds by freshwater lakes, and sometimes on coastal brackish waters. Rocky outcrops just offshore are used in <a href="/wiki/Rottnest_Island" title="Rottnest Island">Rottnest Island</a> off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>, where there are 14 or so similar nesting sites of which five to seven are used in any one year. Many are renovated each season, and some have been used for 70 years. The nest is a large heap of sticks, driftwood and seaweed built in forks of trees, rocky outcrops, utility poles, artificial platforms or offshore islets.<sup id="cite_ref-evans82_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-evans82-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Berulds03_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berulds03-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Generally, Ospreys reach sexual maturity and begin breeding around the age of three to four, though in some regions with high Osprey densities, such as <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a>, they may not start breeding until five to seven years old, and there may be a shortage of suitable tall structures. If there are no nesting sites available, young Ospreys may be forced to delay breeding. To ease this problem, posts are sometimes erected to provide more sites suitable for nest building.<sup id="cite_ref-chesapeake_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chesapeake-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The platform design developed by one organization, <a href="/wiki/Citizens_United_to_Protect_the_Maurice_River_and_Its_Tributaries,_Inc." title="Citizens United to Protect the Maurice River and Its Tributaries, Inc.">Citizens United to Protect the Maurice River and Its Tributaries, Inc.</a> has become the official design of the <a href="/wiki/State_of_New_Jersey" title="State of New Jersey" class="mw-redirect">State of New Jersey</a>, U.S.A. The platform plans and materials list, available online, have been utilized by people from a number of different geographical regions.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Ospreys usually mate for life. Rarely, <a href="/wiki/Polyandry" title="Polyandry">polyandry</a> has been recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-Dennis2007b_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dennis2007b-39"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> The breeding season varies according to latitude; spring (September–October) in southern Australia, April to July in northern Australia and winter (June–August) in southern Queensland.<sup id="cite_ref-Berulds03_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berulds03-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> In spring the pair begins a five-month period of partnership to raise their young. The female lays two to four <a href="/wiki/The_biology_of_eggs" title="The biology of eggs" class="mw-redirect">eggs</a> within a month, and relies on the size of the nest to conserve heat. The eggs are whitish with bold splotches of reddish-brown and are about 6.2 by 4.5 cm (2.4 by 1.8 in) and weigh about 65&#160;g (2.3&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-Berulds03_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berulds03-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> The eggs are incubated for about 5 weeks to hatching.</p>
<p>The newly hatched chicks weigh only 50–60 g (1.8–2.1 oz), but fledge in 8–10 weeks. A study on <a href="/wiki/Kangaroo_Island" title="Kangaroo Island">Kangaroo Island</a>, South Australia, had an average time between hatching and fledging of 69 days. The same study found an average of 0.66 young fledged per year per occupied territory, and 0.92 young fledged per year per active nest. Some 22% of surviving young either remained on the island, or returned at maturity to join the breeding population.<sup id="cite_ref-Dennis2007b_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dennis2007b-39"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> When food is scarce, the first chicks to hatch are most likely to survive. The typical lifespan is 7–10 years, though rarely individuals can grow to as old as 20–25 years. The oldest European wild osprey on record lived to be over thirty years of age. In North America <a href="/wiki/Horned_Owl" title="Horned Owl" class="mw-redirect"><i>Bubo</i> owls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bald_Eagle" title="Bald Eagle">Bald Eagles</a> (and possibly other <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a> of comparable size) are the only major predators of both nests and sub adults.<sup id="cite_ref-UM_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UM-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Kleptoparasitism" title="Kleptoparasitism">kleptoparasitism</a> by Bald Eagles, where the larger raptor steals the Osprey's catch, is more common than predation. Endoparasitic <a href="/wiki/Trematode" title="Trematode" class="mw-redirect">trematodes</a> (<i>Scaphanocephalus expansus</i> and <i>Neodiplostomum</i> spp.) have been recorded in wild Ospreys.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Migration">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Migration">Migration</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> breeders winter in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> breeders winter in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, although some stay in the southernmost <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> states such as <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> and <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-microwave_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-microwave-42"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Some Ospreys from Florida migrate to South America.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasian</a> Ospreys tend not to <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a>.</p>
<p>Studies of Swedish Ospreys showed that females tend to migrate to Africa earlier than the males. More stopovers are made during their autumn migration. The variation of timing and duration in autumn was more variable than in spring. Although migrating predominantly in the day, they sometimes fly in the dark hours particularly in crossings over water and cover on average 260–280 km (160–170 mi) per day with a maximum of 431&#160;km (268&#160;mi) per day.<sup id="cite_ref-Alerstam2006_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alerstam2006-44"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> European birds may also winter in South Asia, an Osprey ringed in Norway has been recovered in western India.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Juvenile_osprey_on_nest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Juvenile_osprey_on_nest.jpg/220px-Juvenile_osprey_on_nest.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Juvenile_osprey_on_nest.jpg/330px-Juvenile_osprey_on_nest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Juvenile_osprey_on_nest.jpg/440px-Juvenile_osprey_on_nest.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Juvenile_osprey_on_nest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A juvenile on a man-made nest</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Osprey has a large range, covering 9,670,000 square kilometres (3,730,000&#160;sq&#160;mi) in just Africa and the Americas, and has a large global population estimated at 460,000 individuals. Although global population trends have not been quantified, the species is not believed to approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the IUCN Red List (i.e., declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations), and for these reasons, the species is evaluated as Least Concern.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> There is evidence for regional decline in South Australia where former territories at locations in the <a href="/wiki/Spencer_Gulf" title="Spencer Gulf">Spencer Gulf</a> and along the lower <a href="/wiki/Murray_River" title="Murray River">Murray River</a> have been vacant for decades.<sup id="cite_ref-Dennis2007a_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dennis2007a-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the main threats to Osprey populations were egg collectors and hunting of the adults along with other birds of prey,<sup id="cite_ref-UM_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UM-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-46"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> but Osprey populations declined drastically in many areas in the 1950s and 1960s; this appeared to be in part due to the toxic effects of insecticides such as <a href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a> on reproduction.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> The pesticide interfered with the bird's <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> metabolism which resulted in thin-shelled, easily broken or infertile eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-Bull87_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bull87-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Possibly because of the banning of DDT in many countries in the early 1970s, together with reduced persecution, the Osprey, as well as other affected <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> species, have made significant recoveries.<sup id="cite_ref-evans82_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-evans82-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> In South Australia, nesting sites on the <a href="/wiki/Eyre_Peninsula" title="Eyre Peninsula">Eyre Peninsula</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kangaroo_Island" title="Kangaroo Island">Kangaroo Island</a> are vulnerable to unmanaged coastal recreation and encroaching urban development.<sup id="cite_ref-Dennis2007a_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dennis2007a-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Osprey is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Canadian_provincial_and_territorial_symbols" title="List of Canadian provincial and territorial symbols">provincial bird</a> of both <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>, Canada and <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B6dermanland" title="Södermanland">Södermanland</a>, Sweden.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cultural depictions">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_depictions">Cultural depictions</span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nisos" title="Nisos">Nisos</a>, a king of <a href="/wiki/Megara" title="Megara">Megara</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, became a <a href="/wiki/Sea_eagle" title="Sea eagle">sea eagle</a> or Osprey, to attack his daughter after she fell in love with <a href="/wiki/Minos" title="Minos">Minos</a>, king of <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Roman writer <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> reported that parent Ospreys made their young fly up to the sun as a test, and dispatch any that failed.<sup id="cite_ref-deVries76_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deVries76-49"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Another odd legend regarding this fish-eating bird of prey, derived from the writings of <a href="/wiki/Albertus_Magnus" title="Albertus Magnus">Albertus Magnus</a> and recorded in <a href="/wiki/Raphael_Holinshed" title="Raphael Holinshed">Holinshed's</a> <i>Chronicles</i>, was that it had one webbed foot and one taloned foot.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-46"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cooper92_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper92-50"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>There was a medieval belief that fish were so mesmerised by the Osprey that they turned belly-up in surrender,<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_46-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-46"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> and this is referenced by <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a> in Act 4 Scene 5 of <i><a href="/wiki/Coriolanus_(play)" title="Coriolanus (play)" class="mw-redirect">Coriolanus</a></i>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I think he'll be to Rome<br />
As is the osprey to the fish, who takes it<br />
By sovereignty of nature.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The Irish poet <a href="/wiki/William_Butler_Yeats" title="William Butler Yeats" class="mw-redirect">William Butler Yeats</a> used a grey wandering Osprey as a representation of sorrow in <i>The Wanderings of Oisin and Other Poems</i> (1889).<sup id="cite_ref-deVries76_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deVries76-49"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Osprey is depicted as a white eagle in <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">heraldry</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Cooper92_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper92-50"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> and more recently has become a symbol of positive responses to nature,<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_46-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-46"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> and has been featured on more than 50 <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamp" title="Postage stamp">postage stamps</a><sup id="cite_ref-stamp_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stamp-51"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> used as a brand name for various products and sports teams. (Examples include the <a href="/wiki/Ospreys_(rugby_team)" title="Ospreys (rugby team)">Ospreys</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Rugby_Union" title="Rugby Union" class="mw-redirect">Rugby Union</a> team; the <a href="/wiki/Missoula_Osprey" title="Missoula Osprey">Missoula Osprey</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Minor_league_baseball" title="Minor league baseball" class="mw-redirect">minor league baseball</a> team; the <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Seahawks" title="Seattle Seahawks">Seattle Seahawks</a>, an <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a> team; and the <a href="/wiki/North_Florida_Ospreys" title="North Florida Ospreys">North Florida Ospreys</a>) or as a mascot (examples include the Springs School Ospreys in Springs, New York; <a href="/wiki/Geraldton" title="Geraldton">Geraldton</a> skiing team in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>; the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Florida" title="University of North Florida">University of North Florida</a>; <a href="/wiki/Salve_Regina_University" title="Salve Regina University">Salve Regina University</a>; <a href="/wiki/Wagner_College" title="Wagner College">Wagner College</a>; the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina_at_Wilmington" title="University of North Carolina at Wilmington" class="mw-redirect">University of North Carolina at Wilmington</a>; <a href="/wiki/Richard_Stockton_College" title="Richard Stockton College" class="mw-redirect">Richard Stockton College</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Wells_International_School" title="Wells International School">Wells International School</a> in Bangkok, Thailand.)<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> Osprey was the device of the arms of former parish of <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A4%C3%A4ksm%C3%A4ki" title="Sääksmäki">Sääksmäki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, and also the emblem of the 4th Flight of the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_Air_Force" title="Finnish Air Force">Finnish Air Force</a> elite unit, <a href="/wiki/LeLv_24" title="LeLv 24" class="mw-redirect">LeLv 24</a>.</p>
