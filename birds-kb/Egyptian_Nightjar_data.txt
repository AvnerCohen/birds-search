<p>The <b>Egyptian nightjar</b> (<i>Caprimulgus aegyptius</i>) is a medium-small <a href="/wiki/Nightjar" title="Nightjar">nightjar</a> which occurs in south west <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and north <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">winters</a> in tropical Africa. This is a fairly common species with a wide distribution which faces no obvious threats apart from <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a>, so the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> has rated its conservation status as being of "<a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least concern</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Egyptian_nightjar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The genus name <i>Caprimulgus</i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>capra</i>, "nanny goat", and <i>mulgere</i>, "to milk", referring to an old myth that nightjars suck milk from goats. The specific <i>aegyptius</i> is Latin for <i>Egyptian</i>.".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The common name "nightjar", first recorded in 1630, refers to the nocturnal habits of the bird, the second part of the name deriving from the distinctive churring song.<sup id="cite_ref-OEDnight_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OEDnight-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Egyptian_nightjar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Caprimulgus_aegyptius.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Caprimulgus_aegyptius.jpg/220px-Caprimulgus_aegyptius.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Caprimulgus_aegyptius.jpg/330px-Caprimulgus_aegyptius.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Caprimulgus_aegyptius.jpg/440px-Caprimulgus_aegyptius.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1100" data-file-height="733" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Caprimulgus_aegyptius.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Resting during the day with chick</div></div></div>
<p>The variegated plumage is much paler than the <a href="/wiki/European_nightjar" title="European nightjar">European nightjar</a>. The adult is sand-colours, barred and streaked with buff and brown. The under parts are sandy or whitish. It is smaller, but relatively longer-winged and longer-tailed than the more widespread species. Like other nightjars, it has a wide gape, long wings, soft downy plumage and nocturnal habits. The male has tiny white wing spots. The length is 25&#160;cm, and the wingspan 55&#160;cm. Its call is a repetitive mechanical <i>kroo-kroo-kroo…</i>, which rises and falls as the bird turns its head from side to side.<sup id="cite_ref-Cleere_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleere-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Egyptian_nightjar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Egyptian nightjar is native to northern Africa, the Arabian Peninsula and the Middle East. Its range includes Afghanistan, Algeria, Bahrain, Chad, Egypt, Iran, Iraq, Israel, Jordan, Kazakhstan, Kuwait, Libya, Mali, Mauritania, Morocco, Nigeria, Oman, Pakistan, Saudi Arabia, Senegal, South Sudan, Sudan, Tajikistan, Tunisia, Turkmenistan, United Arab Emirates, Uzbekistan and Yemen. It is a rare visitor to <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, and has occurred as a vagrant in Denmark, Germany, Italy, Malta, Sweden and the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Its typical habitat is open desert with a few scattered trees or bushes. It is often found near water in areas with sparse vegetation and scrub. Over-wintering birds in Sudan can be found in areas with long grass.<sup id="cite_ref-Cleere_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleere-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Egyptian_nightjar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the day, this <a href="/wiki/Crepuscular" class="mw-redirect" title="Crepuscular">crepuscular</a> nightjar lies silent upon the ground, concealed by its plumage; it is difficult to detect, blending in with the sandy soil. It flies at dusk, most often at sundown, with an easy, silent <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moth</a>-like flight; its strong and deliberate wingbeats alternate with sweeps and glides with motionless wings. Crepuscular insects, such as <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moths</a>, are its food. No nest is made; the two elongated and elliptical eggs are placed upon the bare ground; the brooding bird, sitting closely, is their best protection. The population in northwestern Africa breeds in the desert and sandy steppe where there are areas with limestone outcrops, and overwinter in dry countryside with <i><a href="/wiki/Artemisia_(genus)" title="Artemisia (genus)">Artemisia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tamarix" title="Tamarix">Tamarix</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Salsola" title="Salsola">Salsola</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cleere_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleere-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
