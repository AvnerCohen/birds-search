<p>The <b>Grey Wagtail</b> (<i>Motacilla cinerea</i>) is a small member of the <a href="/wiki/Wagtail" title="Wagtail">wagtail</a> family, <a href="/wiki/Motacillidae" title="Motacillidae">Motacillidae</a>. The species looks similar to the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow_Wagtail_(disambiguation)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Yellow Wagtail (disambiguation) (page does not exist)">Yellow Wagtail</a> but has the yellow on its underside restricted to the throat and <a href="/wiki/Cloaca" title="Cloaca">vent</a>. Breeding males have a black throat. The species is widely distributed, with several populations breeding in Europe and Asia and migrating to tropical regions in Asia and Africa. They are usually seen on open marshy ground or meadows where they walk solitarily or in pairs along the ground, capturing insects that are disturbed. Like other wagtails, they frequently wag their tail and fly low with undulations and they have a sharp call that is often given in flight.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Grey_Wagtail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Grey_Wagtail-_Breeding_Male_I_IMG_6705.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Grey_Wagtail-_Breeding_Male_I_IMG_6705.jpg/220px-Grey_Wagtail-_Breeding_Male_I_IMG_6705.jpg" width="220" height="173" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Grey_Wagtail-_Breeding_Male_I_IMG_6705.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Male <i>M. c. melanope</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This slim wagtail has a narrow white supercilium and a broken eye ring. The upperparts are grey and the yellow vent contrasting with whitish underparts makes it distinctive. The breeding male has a black throat that is edged by whitish moustachial stripes. They forage singly or in pairs on meadows or on shallow water marshes. They also use rocks in water and will often perch on trees. They have a clear sharp call note and the song consists of trills.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Grey_Wagtail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>
<p>The bird is widely distributed across the Palearctic region with several well marked populations. The nominate form (includes <i>caspica</i> of Iran, Turkey and the Caucasus) is from western Europe including the British Isles, Scandinavia and Mediterranean region. Race <i>melanope</i>, which is not well separated from the nominate subspecies, is described as the population breeding in eastern Europe and central Asia mainly along the mountain chains of the Urals, Tien Shan and along the Himalayas.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> They winter in Africa and Asia. Race <i>robusta</i> breeds along the northeastern parts of Asia in Siberia extending to Korea and Japan. These winter in Southeast Asia. Island forms include <i>patriciae</i> of the Azores, <i>schmitzi</i> of Madeira and <i>canariensis</i> of the Canary Islands.</p>
<p>They sometimes occur on the islands to the West of Alaska but have been known to occur further south in California as a vagrant.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Grey_Wagtail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span></h2>
<p>This species was first described by <a href="/wiki/Marmaduke_Tunstall" title="Marmaduke Tunstall">Marmaduke Tunstall</a> in his 1771 Ornithologia Britannica.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The relationships of this species are not well resolved; it belongs to the non-<a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a> <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> of wagtails, these are confusing in their external <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA" class="mw-redirect">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome <i>b</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/NADH_dehydrogenase" title="NADH dehydrogenase">NADH dehydrogenase</a> <a href="/wiki/Protein_subunit" title="Protein subunit">subunit</a> 2 <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">sequence</a> data are not able to robustly resolve their relationships. While the present species is probably most closely related to <a href="/wiki/Citrine_Wagtail" title="Citrine Wagtail">Citrine Wagtails</a> and some <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yellow_Wagtail_(disambiguation)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Yellow Wagtail (disambiguation) (page does not exist)">Blue-headed Wagtails</a>, the exact nature of this relationship is unclear.<sup id="cite_ref-Voelker_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voelker-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Grey_Wagtail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Motacilla_cinerea_1_Luc_Viatour.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Motacilla_cinerea_1_Luc_Viatour.jpg/220px-Motacilla_cinerea_1_Luc_Viatour.jpg" width="220" height="152" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Motacilla_cinerea_1_Luc_Viatour.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Nominate race (Belgium)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The breeding season is April to July and the nest is placed near fast running streams or rivers on an embankment between stones and roots.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The male in display, makes short flights up into the air and descends slowly with fluttering flight accompanied by a rapid series of chipping high notes.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> In Europe the nests are often made in holes in manmade structures. The clutch consists of 3-6 speckled eggs and multiple broods may be raised with declining numbers in the clutch in subsequent broods.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The usual clutch size is five in Ireland and the breeding success is about 80% with predation of eggs or chicks being the main cause of breeding failure.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The Canary Islands population typically have smaller clutches and the breeding season is not as short and well marked as in populations at higher latitudes.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The incubation period is about two weeks with chicks fledging within a fortnight. They live for a maximum of 8 years in the wild.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In some parts of the its range the <a href="/wiki/White-throated_Dipper" title="White-throated Dipper">White-throated Dipper</a> nests in the same habitats as the Grey Wagtail and there are some records of <a href="/wiki/Interspecific_feeding" title="Interspecific feeding">interspecific feeding</a> of dipper chicks by nesting wagtails.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>These birds feed on a variety of aquatic invertebrates including adult flies, mayflies, beetles, crustacea and molluscs.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> They often forage along roadsides in winter, flushing with a sharp <i>chi-cheep</i> call and flying up further along the road but after some distance turning back to return to the original location.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In winter, they roost in small groups.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Wintering birds have been known to return to the same sites, sometimes a small urban garden, each year.<sup id="cite_ref-hbk_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Adults often have parasitic ticks, <i><a href="/wiki/Ixodes_ricinus" title="Ixodes ricinus">Ixodes ricinus</a></i>, which can harbour <i><a href="/wiki/Borrelia" title="Borrelia">Borrelia</a></i> and thus can potentially disperse Lyme disease over a wide region.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Coccidia such as <i><a href="/wiki/Isospora" title="Isospora">Isospora</a></i> sp. are known in this species.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Common_Cuckoo" title="Common Cuckoo">Common Cuckoo</a> is sometimes a <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasitism" title="Brood parasitism" class="mw-redirect">brood parasite</a> of this species.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Kestrel" title="Kestrel">Kestrels</a> may sometimes prey on them.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
