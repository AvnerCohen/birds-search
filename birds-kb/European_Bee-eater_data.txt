<p>The <b>European Bee-eater</b> (<i>Merops apiaster</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Near_passerine" title="Near passerine">near passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bee-eater" title="Bee-eater">bee-eater</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <i>Meropidae</i>. It breeds in southern Europe and in parts of north Africa and western Asia. It is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in tropical Africa, India and Sri Lanka. This species occurs as a spring overshoot north of its range, with occasional breeding in northwest Europe.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Bee-eater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>This species, like other bee-eaters, is a richly-coloured, slender bird. It has brown and yellow upper parts, whilst the wings are green and the beak is black. It can reach a length of 27–29 cm (10.6–11.4 in), including the two elongated central tail feathers. Sexes are alike.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Bee-eater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Food">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Food">Food</span></h2>
<p>This is a bird which breeds in open country in warmer climates. Just as the name suggests, bee-eaters predominantly eat <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">wasps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hornet" title="Hornet">hornets</a> which are caught in the air by sorties from an open perch. Before eating its meal, a European Bee-eater removes the sting by repeatedly hitting the insect on a hard surface. It eats some 250 bees daily.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from October 2012">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Lizards and frogs are also taken.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from October 2012">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The most important prey item in their diet are <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">Hymenoptera</a>, mostly <a href="/wiki/Apis_mellifera" title="Apis mellifera" class="mw-redirect">Apis mellifera</a>; a study in Spain found that these comprise 69.4% to 82% of the European bee-eaters' diet.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Their impact on bee populations however is small; they eat less than 1% of the worker bees in the area in which they live.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A study found that European bee-eaters "convert food to body weight more efficiently if they are fed a mixture of bees and dragonflies than if they eat only bees or only dragonfiles."<sup id="cite_ref-GoodenoughMcGuire2009_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GoodenoughMcGuire2009-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Bee-eater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span></h2>
<p>These bee-eaters are gregarious, nesting <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonially</a> in sandy banks, preferably near river shores, usually at the beginning of May. They make a relatively long tunnel in which the 5 to 8, spherical white eggs are laid around the beginning of June. Both the male and the female take care of the eggs, which are brooded for about 3 weeks. These birds also feed and roost communally.</p>
<p>During courtship the male feeds large items to the female while eating the small ones himself.<sup id="cite_ref-f_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-f-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Most males are monogamous, but occasional bigamy has been encountered.<sup id="cite_ref-f_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-f-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The call is a pleasant distinctive trill.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Bee-eater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reported UK breeding attempts">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Reported_UK_breeding_attempts">Reported UK breeding attempts</span></h2>
<p>European Bee-eater has attempted to nest on five occasions in Britain:</p>
<ul>
<li>In 1920, a pair made a nesting attempt in a sand bank of the <a href="/wiki/River_Esk,_Lothian" title="River Esk, Lothian">River Esk</a> at <a href="/wiki/Musselburgh" title="Musselburgh">Musselburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. A local gardener captured the female, keeping her in a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouse</a>, and she died two days later, after laying a single <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">egg</a>.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>In 1955, three pairs of Bee-eaters nested in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Streat_sand_quarry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Streat sand quarry (page does not exist)">Streat sand quarry</a> near <a href="/wiki/Plumpton,_East_Sussex" title="Plumpton, East Sussex">Plumpton, East Sussex</a>. The birds were first found on 12 June, although the birds' presence only became widely known at the start of August. One nest was accidentally destroyed by machinery in July, but seven young fledged from the two remaining nests towards the end of August. An <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Protection_of_Birds" title="Royal Society for the Protection of Birds">RSPB</a> wardening operation was instigated, and in total over 1,000 people visited the site. The birds remained until 24 September.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>A pair nested at <a href="/wiki/Bishop_Middleham_Quarry" title="Bishop Middleham Quarry">Bishop Middleham Quarry</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a> in 2002. The birds were first found on 2 June, and within a few days started to undertake <a href="/wiki/Courtship_feeding" title="Courtship feeding" class="mw-redirect">courtship feeding</a> and copulation; five chicks hatched, but one died in the nest, one died before fledging, and a third disappeared and was also believed to have died. <a href="/wiki/Durham_Wildlife_Trust" title="Durham Wildlife Trust">Durham Wildlife Trust</a> (with RSPB assistance) set up a wardening post during the period when the birds were nesting. News was released to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rare_bird_information_services&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rare bird information services (page does not exist)">rare bird information services</a>, and the national news media also reported on the birds' presence. In total, some 15,000 people visited the site during their stay; the adults and both fledged young were seen to leave on 28 August, when they flew off high to the south.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>A pair took up residence on farmland adjacent to the <a href="/wiki/River_Wye" title="River Wye">River Wye</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Bishop" title="Hampton Bishop">Hampton Bishop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a> in summer 2005; by mid-July the adults were bringing insect food to the riverbank nest-hole confirming that eggs had hatched. A wardening operation was set up by the RSPB, with public access granted, resulting in c. 2,000 people seeing the birds. However, on the evening of 29 July, <a href="/wiki/Red_Fox" title="Red Fox" class="mw-redirect">foxes</a> predated the nest, and the birds soon left the site.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>A pair excavated a nest hole at a coastal site in <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a> in 2006, but this attempt failed (<a href="/wiki/Birdwatch_(magazine)" title="Birdwatch (magazine)">Birdwatch</a> no. 173 p.&#160;23).</li>
</ul>
