<p>The <b>European bee-eater</b> (<i>Merops apiaster</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Near_passerine" title="Near passerine">near passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bee-eater" title="Bee-eater">bee-eater</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meropidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Meropidae">Meropidae</a>. It breeds in southern Europe and in parts of north Africa and western Asia. It is strongly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in tropical Africa. This species occurs as a spring overshoot north of its range, with occasional breeding in northwest Europe.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_bee-eater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European bee-eater was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">formally described</a> by the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">tenth edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> under its current <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial name</a> <i>Merops apiaster</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  The genus name <i><a href="/wiki/Merops_(genus)" title="Merops (genus)">Merops</a></i> is <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> for "bee-eater", and <i>apiaster</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, also meaning "bee-eater", from <i>apis</i>, "bee".<sup id="cite_ref-job_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_bee-eater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species, like other bee-eaters, is a richly coloured, slender bird. It has brown and yellow upper parts, whilst the wings are green and the beak is black. It can reach a length of 27–29&#160;cm (10.6–11.4&#160;in), including the two elongated central tail feathers. Sexes are alike. Female tends to have greener rather than gold feathers on shoulders. Non-breeding plumage is much duller and with a blue-green back and no elongated central tail feathers. Juvenile resembles a non-breeding adult, but with less variation in the feather colours. Adults begin to moult in June or July and complete the process by August or September. There is a further moult into breeding plumage in winter in Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_bee-eater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_bee-eater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These bee-eaters are gregarious—nesting <a href="/wiki/Bird_colony" title="Bird colony">colonially</a> in sandy banks, preferably near river shores, usually at the beginning of May. They make a relatively long tunnel, in which they lay five to eight spherical white eggs around the beginning of June. Both male and female care for the eggs, which they brood for about three weeks. They also feed and roost communally.
</p><p>During courtship, the male feeds large items to the female while eating the small ones himself.<sup id="cite_ref-avery1988_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-avery1988-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Most males are monogamous, but occasional bigamy has been encountered.<sup id="cite_ref-avery1988_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-avery1988-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Their typical call is a distinctive, mellow, liquid and burry <i>prreee</i> or <i>prruup</i>.
</p>

<p>European bee-eaters have attempted to nest in Britain on at least 5 occasions:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<ul><li>In 1920, a pair tried to nest in a sand bank of the <a href="/wiki/River_Esk,_Lothian" title="River Esk, Lothian">River Esk</a> at <a href="/wiki/Musselburgh" title="Musselburgh">Musselburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. A local gardener captured the female and kept her in a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouse</a>. She died two days later, after laying a single <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">egg</a>.</li>
<li>In 1955, three pairs of bee-eaters nested in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Streat_sand_quarry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Streat sand quarry (page does not exist)">Streat sand quarry</a> near <a href="/wiki/Plumpton,_East_Sussex" title="Plumpton, East Sussex">Plumpton, East Sussex</a>. The birds were first found on 12 June, though the birds' presence only became widely known at the start of August. One nest was accidentally destroyed by machinery in July, but seven young fledged from the two remaining nests towards the end of August. The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Protection_of_Birds" title="Royal Society for the Protection of Birds">RSPB</a> instigated a wardening operation, and over 1,000 people visited the site. The birds remained until 24 September.</li>
<li>A pair nested at <a href="/wiki/Bishop_Middleham_Quarry" title="Bishop Middleham Quarry">Bishop Middleham Quarry</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a> in 2002. The birds were first found on 2 June, and within a few days started to undertake <a href="/wiki/Courtship_feeding" class="mw-redirect" title="Courtship feeding">courtship feeding</a> and copulation. Five chicks hatched, but one died in the nest, one died before fledging, and a third disappeared and probably died. <a href="/wiki/Durham_Wildlife_Trust" title="Durham Wildlife Trust">Durham Wildlife Trust</a> (with RSPB assistance) set up a wardening post when the birds were nesting. They released news to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rare_bird_information_services&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rare bird information services (page does not exist)">rare bird information services</a>, and the national news media also reported on the birds' presence. Around 15,000 people visited the site during their stay. The adults and both fledged young left on 28 August, flying off high to the south.</li>
<li>A pair took up residence on farmland adjacent to the <a href="/wiki/River_Wye" title="River Wye">River Wye</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Bishop" title="Hampton Bishop">Hampton Bishop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a> in summer 2005. By mid-July, the adults began bringing insect food to the riverbank nest-hole, confirming that eggs had hatched. The RSPB began a wardening operation with public access. Around 2,000 people came to see the birds. However, on the evening of 29 July, <a href="/wiki/Red_fox" title="Red fox">foxes</a> predated the nest, and the birds soon left.</li>
<li>A pair excavated a nest hole at a coastal site in <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a> in 2006, but failed.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>in 2014, two pairs nested in the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a>, one nest fledged 3 chicks, the other 5. One nest had earlier been discovered and protected but the other was not found until late August.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>in 2015, two breeding pairs were found at a quarry in Cumbria.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>in 2017 seven nested at CEMEX quarry <a href="/wiki/East_Leake" title="East Leake">East Leake</a>, Nottinghamshire, attracting thousands of bird-watchers.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_bee-eater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pair_of_Merops_apiaster_feeding.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Pair_of_Merops_apiaster_feeding.jpg/220px-Pair_of_Merops_apiaster_feeding.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Pair_of_Merops_apiaster_feeding.jpg/330px-Pair_of_Merops_apiaster_feeding.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Pair_of_Merops_apiaster_feeding.jpg/440px-Pair_of_Merops_apiaster_feeding.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3570" data-file-height="2378" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pair_of_Merops_apiaster_feeding.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Feeding bee-eater—the female (in front) waits for the male's offering</div></div></div>
<p>This bird breeds in open country in warmer climates. As the name suggests, bee-eaters predominantly eat <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">wasps</a>, and  <a href="/wiki/Hornet" title="Hornet">hornets</a>. They catch insects in flight, in sorties from an open perch. Before eating a bee, the European bee-eater removes the sting by repeatedly hitting the insect on a hard surface. It can eat around 250 bees a day.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The most important prey item in their diet is <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">Hymenoptera</a>, mostly <i><a href="/wiki/Apis_mellifera" class="mw-redirect" title="Apis mellifera">Apis mellifera</a></i>. A study in Spain found that these comprise 69.4% to 82% of the European bee-eaters' diet.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Their impact on bee populations, however, is small. They eat less than 1% of the worker bees in areas where they live.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A study found that European bee-eaters "convert food to body weight more efficiently if they are fed a mixture of bees and <a href="/wiki/Dragonflies" class="mw-redirect" title="Dragonflies">dragonflies</a> than if they eat only bees or only dragonflies."<sup id="cite_ref-GoodenoughMcGuire2009_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GoodenoughMcGuire2009-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Predation_of_honey_bees">Predation of honey bees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_bee-eater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Predation of honey bees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Colonie_de_prigorii_(Merops_apiaster)_vandalizat%C4%83_de_apicultori.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Colonie_de_prigorii_%28Merops_apiaster%29_vandalizat%C4%83_de_apicultori.jpg/220px-Colonie_de_prigorii_%28Merops_apiaster%29_vandalizat%C4%83_de_apicultori.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Colonie_de_prigorii_%28Merops_apiaster%29_vandalizat%C4%83_de_apicultori.jpg/330px-Colonie_de_prigorii_%28Merops_apiaster%29_vandalizat%C4%83_de_apicultori.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Colonie_de_prigorii_%28Merops_apiaster%29_vandalizat%C4%83_de_apicultori.jpg/440px-Colonie_de_prigorii_%28Merops_apiaster%29_vandalizat%C4%83_de_apicultori.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1920" data-file-height="1280" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Colonie_de_prigorii_(Merops_apiaster)_vandalizat%C4%83_de_apicultori.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bee-eater colony destroyed by bee-keepers. The entrance into the bee eater's galleries were deliberately blocked with stones</div></div></div><p>If an <a href="/wiki/Apiary" title="Apiary">apiary</a> is set up close to a bee-eater colony, a larger number of honey bees are eaten because they are more abundant. However, studies show the bee-eaters do not intentionally fly into the apiary, rather they feed on the insects caught on pastures and meadows within a radius of 12&#160;km (7.5&#160;mi) from the colony, this maximum distance being reached only when there is a lack of food. Observations show that the birds actually enter the apiary only in cold and rainy periods, when the bees do not leave the hive and other insect prey are harder for the bee-eaters to detect.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many <a href="/wiki/Bee-keeper" class="mw-redirect" title="Bee-keeper">bee-keepers</a> believe that the bee-eaters are the main obstacle causing worker bees not to forage, and instead stay inside the hives for much of the day between May and the end of August. However, a study carried out in <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus" title="Eucalyptus">eucalyptus</a> forest in the Alalous region, 80&#160;km (50&#160;mi) east of <a href="/wiki/Tripoli" title="Tripoli">Tripoli</a> Libya, showed that the bee-eaters were not the main obstacle of bee foraging, which is the opposite of what beekeepers think. The foraging rate was higher in presence of the birds than in their absence in some cases. The average bird meal consisted of 90.8&#160;% honey bees and 9.2&#160;% beetles.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Predation is more likely when the bees are queening or during peak migrations, from late March till mid-April, and in mid-September. Hives close to or under trees or overhead cables are also at increased risk as the birds pounce on flying insects from these perches.<sup id="cite_ref-toM_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-toM-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
