<p>The <b>ashy drongo</b> (<i>Dicrurus leucophaeus</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Drongo" title="Drongo">drongo</a> family <a href="/wiki/Dicruridae" class="mw-redirect" title="Dicruridae">Dicruridae</a>. It is found widely distributed across South and Southeast Asia with several populations that vary in the shade of grey, migration patterns and in the size or presence of  white patches around the eye.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashy_drongo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ashy_Drongo_salangensis_MG_8541_GarimaBhatia.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Ashy_Drongo_salangensis_MG_8541_GarimaBhatia.jpg/170px-Ashy_Drongo_salangensis_MG_8541_GarimaBhatia.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="128" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Ashy_Drongo_salangensis_MG_8541_GarimaBhatia.jpg/255px-Ashy_Drongo_salangensis_MG_8541_GarimaBhatia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Ashy_Drongo_salangensis_MG_8541_GarimaBhatia.jpg/340px-Ashy_Drongo_salangensis_MG_8541_GarimaBhatia.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2400" data-file-height="1800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ashy_Drongo_salangensis_MG_8541_GarimaBhatia.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Subspecies <i>salangensis</i> from Thailand</div></div></div>
<p>The adult ashy drongo is mainly dark grey, and the tail is long and deeply forked, There are a number of subspecies varying in the shade of the grey <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">plumage</a>. Some subspecies have white markings on the head. Young birds are dull brownish grey.
</p><p>Subspecies <i>longicaudatus</i> of India (which includes <i>beavani</i> of the Himalayas that winters on the peninsula, with one breeding population in central India that Vaurie separates as <i>longicaudatus</i> in the restricted sense) is very dark and almost like the <a href="/wiki/Black_drongo" title="Black drongo">black drongo</a> although this bird is slimmer and has a somewhat longer and less-splayed tail. It is found in more tall forest habitat, has dark grey underside lacking the sheen of black drongo. The iris is crimson and there is no white rictal spot. Subspecies <i>leucogenis</i> and <i>salangensis</i> have a white eye-patch as do several of the island forms that breed further south. The calls are a little more nasal and twangy than that of the black drongo.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><br />
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashy_drongo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ashy drongo breeds in the hills of tropical southern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> from eastern <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> east to southern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ryukyu_Islands" title="Ryukyu Islands">Ryukyu Islands</a> in southern <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> (particularly <a href="/wiki/Okinawa" class="mw-redirect" title="Okinawa">Okinawa</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>. Many populations in the northern part of its range are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>. <a href="/wiki/Charles_Vaurie" title="Charles Vaurie">Charles Vaurie</a> described subspecies <i>beavani</i> (after <a href="/wiki/Robert_Cecil_Beavan" title="Robert Cecil Beavan">Robert Cecil Beavan</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>) as the population that breeds along the Himalayas that wintered in peninsular India. However, later workers include this as part of <i>longicaudatus</i> which also has a population that breeds in central India.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In winter, the species is particularly fond of hill forests.<sup id="cite_ref-whistler_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whistler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/E._C._Stuart_Baker" title="E. C. Stuart Baker">E. C. Stuart Baker</a> described <i>stevensi</i> which Vaurie considered as being either <i>beavani</i> or <i>hopwoodi</i> of the eastern Himalayas. To the east of the range of <i>hopwoodi</i> is <i>mouhouti</i> of Thailand and Myanmar. To the north of this range are <i>leucogenis</i> and <i>salangensis</i> (both migratory mainly to areas further south but also known from Nagaland<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>) while <i>bondi</i> is found to the south. Along the southeast Asian island chain, there are number of insular populations including <i>periophthalmus</i>, <i>ryukyuensis</i>, <i>batakensis</i>, <i>phaedrus</i>, <i>siberu</i> and <i>nigrescens</i>. The nominate form is said to be found on Simalur, Java, Bali, Lombok, Palawan, and Balabac Islands.<sup id="cite_ref-vaurie_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vaurie-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashy_drongo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ashy drongo has short legs and sits very upright while perched prominently, often high on a tree. It is insectivorous and forages by making <a href="/wiki/Hawking_(birds)" title="Hawking (birds)">aerial sallies</a> but sometimes gleans from tree trunks.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> They are found singly, in pairs or small groups. During migration they fly in small flocks.<sup id="cite_ref-whistler_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whistler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A common call that they make is described as <i>drangh gip</i> or <i>gip-gip-drangh</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-whistler_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whistler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> They can imitate the calls of other birds and are capable of imitating the whistling notes of a <a href="/wiki/Common_iora" title="Common iora">common iora</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The breeding season is May to June with a clutch of three or four reddish or brown <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> laid in a loose cup nest in a tree.<sup id="cite_ref-whistler_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whistler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbk_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbk-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
