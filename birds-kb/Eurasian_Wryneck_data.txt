<p>The <b>Eurasian wryneck</b> or <b>northern wryneck</b> (<i>Jynx torquilla</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Wryneck" title="Wryneck">wryneck</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Woodpecker" title="Woodpecker">woodpecker</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This species mainly breeds in temperate regions of Europe and Asia. Most populations are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in tropical Africa and in southern Asia from Iran to the Indian subcontinent, but some are resident in northwestern Africa. It is a bird of open countryside, woodland and orchards.
</p><p>Eurasian wrynecks measure about 16.5&#160;cm (6.5&#160;in) in length and have bills shorter and less dagger-like than those of other woodpeckers. Their upperparts are barred and mottled in shades of pale brown with rufous and blackish bars and wider black streaks. Their underparts are cream speckled and spotted with brown. Their chief <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">prey</a> is ants and other insects, which they find in decaying wood or on the ground. The eggs are white as is the case with many birds that nest in holes and a clutch of seven to ten eggs is laid during May and June.
</p><p>These birds get their English name from their ability to turn their heads through almost 180 degrees. When disturbed at the nest, they use this <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snake</a>-like head twisting and hissing as a threat display. This odd behaviour led to their use in <a href="/wiki/Witchcraft" title="Witchcraft">witchcraft</a>, hence to put a "<a href="/wiki/Jinx" title="Jinx">jinx</a>" on someone.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_etymology">Taxonomy and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_wryneck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Eurasian wryneck was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in the <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">tenth edition</a> of his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> in 1758. The <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a> came from Sweden.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The genus name <i>Jynx</i> is from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> name for this bird, <i>iunx</i>. The specific <i>torquilla</i> is <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> derived from <i>torquere</i>, to twist, referring to the strange snake-head movements.<sup id="cite_ref-job_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The bird was used as a charm to bring back an errant lover, the bird being tied to a piece of string and whirled around.<sup id="cite_ref-job_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The English "wryneck" refers to the same twisting movement and was first recorded in 1585.<sup id="cite_ref-OEDnight_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OEDnight-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The family Picidae has four subfamilies, the Picinae (woodpeckers), the Picumninae (piculets), the Jynginae (wrynecks) and the <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monotypic">monotypic</a> Nesoctitinae (<a href="/wiki/Antillean_piculet" title="Antillean piculet">Antillean piculet</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Benz_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Benz-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Based on <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a> and behaviour, the Picumninae was considered to be the sister <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> of the Picinae. This has now been confirmed by <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">phylogenetic analysis</a> and the Jynginae are placed basal to the Picinae, Nesoctitinae and Picumninae.<sup id="cite_ref-Benz_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Benz-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Jynginae includes one genus (<i><a href="/wiki/Jynx" title="Jynx">Jynx</a></i>) and two species, the Eurasian wryneck and the <a href="/wiki/Red-throated_wryneck" title="Red-throated wryneck">red-throated wryneck</a> (<i>Jynx ruficollis</i>), resident in sub-Saharan Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> There are six subspecies of <i>Jynx torquilla</i>:
<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>Jynx torquilla chinensis</i> Hesse, 1911</li>
<li><i>Jynx torquilla himalayana</i> Vaurie, 1959</li>
<li><i>Jynx torquilla mauretanica</i> Rothschild, 1909</li>
<li><i>Jynx torquilla sarudnyi</i> Loudon, 1912</li>
<li><i>Jynx torquilla torquilla</i> Linnaeus, 1758</li>
<li><i>Jynx torquilla tschusii</i> O. Kleinschmidt, 1907</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_wryneck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jynx_torquilla_-Norway-8_(1).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Jynx_torquilla_-Norway-8_%281%29.jpg/220px-Jynx_torquilla_-Norway-8_%281%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Jynx_torquilla_-Norway-8_%281%29.jpg/330px-Jynx_torquilla_-Norway-8_%281%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Jynx_torquilla_-Norway-8_%281%29.jpg/440px-Jynx_torquilla_-Norway-8_%281%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1463" data-file-height="1005" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jynx_torquilla_-Norway-8_(1).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In Norway</div></div></div>
<p>The Eurasian wryneck grows to about 17&#160;cm (6.7&#160;in) in length.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The subspecies <i>Jynx torquilla tschusii</i> weighs 26 to 50&#160;g (0.92 to 1.76&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> It is a slim, elongated-looking bird with a body shape more like a thrush than a woodpecker. The upperparts are barred and mottled in shades of pale brown with rufous and blackish bars and wider black streaks. The rump and upper tail coverts are grey with speckles and irregular bands of brown. The rounded tail is grey, speckled with brown, with faint bands of greyish-brown and a few more clearly defined bands of brownish-black. The cheeks and throat are buff barred with brown. The underparts are creamy white with brown markings shaped like arrow-heads which are reduced to spots on the lower breast and belly. The flanks are buff with similar markings and the under-tail coverts are buff with narrow brown bars. The primaries and secondaries are brown with rufous-buff markings. The beak is brown, long and slender with a broad base and sharp tip. The irises are hazel and the slender legs and feet are pale brown. The first and second toes are shorter than the others. The first and fourth toes point backwards and the second and third point forwards, a good arrangement for clinging to vertical surfaces.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The call of the Eurasian wryneck is a series of repeated harsh, shrill notes <i>quee-quee-quee-quee</i> lasting for several seconds and is reminiscent of the voice of the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_spotted_woodpecker" title="Lesser spotted woodpecker">lesser spotted woodpecker</a>. Its alarm call is a short series of staccato "tuck"s and when disturbed on the nest it hisses.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The Eurasian wryneck has a <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">palearctic</a> distribution. The breeding range of the nominate subspecies includes all of Europe from Britain to the Urals. In the north it reaches the Arctic Circle and the range includes Spain in the southwest. In the south and east it intergrades with <i>J. t. tschusii</i> (smaller and more reddish brown) which is found in Corsica, Italy, Dalmatia and parts of the Balkans. <i>J. t. mauretanica</i> (also smaller than the nominate form, light, with whitish throat and breast) is resident in Algeria and Morocco and possibly also the Balearic Islands, Sardinia and parts of Sicily. <i>J. t. sarudnyi</i> (considerably paler than the nominate with fainter markings) occurs in the Urals and then in a wide strip of Asia through southern Siberia, Central Asia, including the north-western Himalayas to the Pacific coast. <i>J. t. chinensis</i> breeds in eastern Siberia and northeastern and central China while <i>J. t. himalayana</i> breeds in Pakistan and the northwestern Himalayas.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Eurasian wrynecks also inhabit the island of Sakhalin,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Japan and the coastal areas of southern China.<sup id="cite_ref-BirdLife_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdLife-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Eurasian wryneck is the only European woodpecker to undertake long distance migrations. The wintering area of European species is located south of the Sahara, in a wide strip across Africa extending from Senegal, Gambia and Sierra Leone in the west to Ethiopia in the east. Its southern limit extends to the Democratic Republic of Congo and Cameroon. The populations from West Asia use the same wintering areas. The Central and East Asian breeding birds winter in the Indian subcontinent or southern East Asia including southern Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the summer the bird is found in open countryside, parkland, gardens, orchards, heaths and hedgerows, especially where there are some old trees. It may also inhabit deciduous woodland and in Scandinavia it also occurs in coniferous forests.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The Eurasian wryneck sometimes forms small groups during migration and in its winter quarters but in the summer is usually found in pairs. It characteristically holds its head high with its beak pointing slightly upwards. A mutual display that occurs at any time of year involves two birds perched facing each other with their heads far back and beaks wide open, bobbing their heads up and down. Sometimes the head is allowed to slump sideways and hang limply. On other occasions, when excited, the head is shaken and twisted about violently. When disturbed on the nest or held in the hand, the neck contorts and twists in all directions. The bird sometimes feigns death and hangs limply with eyes closed.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jynx_torquilla_1_(Martin_Mecnarowski).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Jynx_torquilla_1_%28Martin_Mecnarowski%29.jpg/170px-Jynx_torquilla_1_%28Martin_Mecnarowski%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="235" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Jynx_torquilla_1_%28Martin_Mecnarowski%29.jpg/255px-Jynx_torquilla_1_%28Martin_Mecnarowski%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Jynx_torquilla_1_%28Martin_Mecnarowski%29.jpg/340px-Jynx_torquilla_1_%28Martin_Mecnarowski%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="615" data-file-height="850" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jynx_torquilla_1_(Martin_Mecnarowski).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eurasian wrynecks use their necks in display</div></div></div>
<p>On returning to the breeding area after migration, the birds set up <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territories</a>. On farmland in Switzerland it has been found that old <a href="/wiki/Pear" title="Pear">pear</a> orchards with large numbers of ant nests are preferentially selected over other habitats. Areas used for vegetable cultivation provided useful habitat when they include areas of bare ground on which the birds can forage.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Territories are not chosen at random as arriving birds favoured certain areas over others with the same territories being colonised first year after year. The presence of other Eurasian wrynecks in the vicinity is also a positive influence. Orchards in general, and older ones in particular, provide favoured territories, probably because the dense foliage is more likely to support high numbers of <a href="/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">aphids</a> and the ground beneath has scant vegetation cover, both of which factors increase the availability of ants, the birds' main prey. Despite some territories being consistently chosen over others, reproductive success in these territories was no higher than in others.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Limiting factors for such crevice-nesting species as Eurasian wrynecks are both the availability of nesting sites and the number of ants and their ease of discovery. Modern farming practices such as the removal of hedges, forest patches and isolated trees and the increasing use of fertilisers and pesticides are disadvantageous to such birds.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The diet of the Eurasian wryneck consists chiefly of ants but <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a> and their <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woodlice" class="mw-redirect" title="Woodlice">woodlice</a> are also eaten. Although much time is spent in the upper branches of trees, the bird sometimes perches in low bushes and mostly forages on the ground, moving around with short hops with its tail held in a raised position. It can cling to tree trunks, often moving obliquely, and sometimes pressing its tail against the surface as a prop. It does not make holes in bark with its beak but picks up prey with a rapid extension and retraction of its tongue and it sometimes catches insects while on the wing. Its flight is rather slow and undulating.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_wryneck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jynx_torquilla_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_162_Le_monetier_les_bains.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Jynx_torquilla_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_162_Le_monetier_les_bains.jpg/220px-Jynx_torquilla_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_162_Le_monetier_les_bains.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="206" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Jynx_torquilla_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_162_Le_monetier_les_bains.jpg/330px-Jynx_torquilla_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_162_Le_monetier_les_bains.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Jynx_torquilla_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_162_Le_monetier_les_bains.jpg/440px-Jynx_torquilla_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_162_Le_monetier_les_bains.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4138" data-file-height="3872" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jynx_torquilla_MHNT_ZOO_2010_11_162_Le_monetier_les_bains.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs of <i>Jynx torquilla</i> <a href="/wiki/MHNT" class="mw-redirect" title="MHNT">MHNT</a></div></div></div>
<p>The nesting site is variable and may be in a pre-existing hole in a tree trunk, a crevice in a wall, a hole in a bank, a <a href="/wiki/Sand_martin" title="Sand martin">sand martin</a>'s burrow or a nesting box.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In its search for a safe, protected site out of reach of <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predators</a>, it sometimes evicts a previous occupant, its eggs and nestlings.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> It uses no nesting material and a clutch of normally seven to ten eggs is laid (occasionally five, six, eleven or twelve). The eggs average 20.8 by 15.4 millimetres (0.82&#160;in ×&#160;0.61&#160;in) and weigh about 0.2&#160;g (0.007&#160;oz). They are a dull white colour and partially opaque. Both sexes are involved in <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" class="mw-redirect" title="Avian incubation">incubation</a> which takes twelve days, but the female plays the greater part. Both parents feed the chicks for about twenty days before they <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a>. There is usually a single brood.<sup id="cite_ref-Witherby_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witherby-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_wryneck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> lists the Eurasian wryneck as being of "<a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" class="mw-redirect" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>" in its <a href="/wiki/Red_List_of_Threatened_Species" class="mw-redirect" title="Red List of Threatened Species">Red List of Threatened Species</a>. This is because it has a world population estimated at up to fifteen million individual birds and a very wide geographical range. The population may be decreasing to a certain extent but not at such a rate as to make the bird reach the threshold for a more threatened category.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In continental Europe, the largest populations are in Spain, Italy, Germany, Poland, Romania, Hungary, Belarus and Ukraine, and only in Romania is the population trend believed to be upward. In Russia, where there are believed to be 300,000 to 800,000 individuals, the population trend is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In the United Kingdom the numbers of bird are on the decrease and it is protected under Schedule 1 of the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 and is listed on Appendix II of the Bern Convention. It is protected as a migratory species under the <a href="/wiki/Birds_Directive" title="Birds Directive">Birds Directive</a> in the European Union.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In Switzerland, the population has also been decreasing, but the species has reacted positively to conservation measures such as the addition of nestboxes in suitable habitats.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
