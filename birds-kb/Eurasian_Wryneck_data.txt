<p>The <b>Eurasian Wryneck</b> (<i>Jynx torquilla</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Wryneck" title="Wryneck">wryneck</a> in the family of <a href="/wiki/Woodpecker" title="Woodpecker">woodpeckers</a>.</p>
<p>This species breeds in temperate regions of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in tropical <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. It is a bird of open woodland and orchards. On migration it is frequently seen in sandy areas, where it forages for <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ants</a>.</p>

<p>This species measure 16–18 cm (6.3–7.1 in) in length and weighs 26–50 g (0.92–1.8 oz).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Their bills are shorter and less dagger-like than in the other woodpeckers, but their chief prey is <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ants</a> and other insects, which they find in decaying wood or almost bare soil. They re-use woodpecker holes for nesting, rather than making their own holes. The eggs are white, as with many hole nesters.</p>
<p>These <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> get their <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> name from their ability to turn their heads almost 180 degrees. When disturbed at the nest, they use this <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snake</a>-like head twisting and hissing as a threat display. This odd behaviour led to their use in witchcraft, hence to put a "<a href="/wiki/Jinx" title="Jinx">jinx</a>" on someone.</p>
<p>There are six subspecies:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Jynx torquilla chinensis</i> Hesse, 1911</li>
<li><i>Jynx torquilla himalayana</i> Vaurie, 1959</li>
<li><i>Jynx torquilla mauretanica</i> Rothschild, 1909</li>
<li><i>Jynx torquilla sarudnyi</i> Loudon, 1912</li>
<li><i>Jynx torquilla torquilla</i> Linnaeus, 1758</li>
<li><i>Jynx torquilla tschusii</i> O. Kleinschmidt,1907</li>
</ul>
