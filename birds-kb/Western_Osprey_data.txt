<p>The <b>osprey</b> or more specifically the <b>western osprey</b> (<i>Pandion haliaetus</i>) — also called <b>sea hawk</b>, <b>river hawk</b>, and <b>fish hawk</b> — is a <a href="/wiki/Diurnality" title="Diurnality">diurnal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piscivore" title="Piscivore">fish-eating</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Cosmopolitan_range" class="mw-redirect" title="Cosmopolitan range">cosmopolitan range</a>. It is a large <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptor</a>, reaching more than 60&#160;cm (24&#160;in) in length and 180&#160;cm (71&#160;in) across the wings. It is brown on the upperparts and predominantly greyish on the head and underparts.
</p><p>The osprey tolerates a wide variety of <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitats</a>, nesting in any location near a body of water providing an adequate food supply. It is found on all continents except Antarctica, although in South America it occurs only as a non-breeding <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>.
</p><p>As its other common names suggest, the osprey's diet consists almost exclusively of fish. It possesses specialised physical characteristics and exhibits unique behaviour to assist in hunting and catching <a href="/wiki/Prey" class="mw-redirect" title="Prey">prey</a>. As a result of these unique characteristics, it has been given its own <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">taxonomic</a> <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a>, <i>Pandion</i> and <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a>, Pandionidae. Three <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are usually recognized; one of the former subspecies, <i>cristatus</i>, has recently been given full species status and is referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_osprey" title="Eastern osprey">eastern osprey</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The osprey was one of the many species described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his 18th-century work, <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i>, and named as <i>Falco haliaeetus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Linnaeus1758_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linnaeus1758-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The genus, <i>Pandion</i>, is the sole member of the family Pandionidae, and used to contain only one species, the osprey (<i>P. haliaetus</i>). The genus <i>Pandion</i> was described by the French zoologist <a href="/wiki/Marie_Jules_C%C3%A9sar_Savigny" title="Marie Jules César Savigny">Marie Jules César Savigny</a> in 1809.<sup id="cite_ref-ITIS_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITIS-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Graves1955_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Graves1955-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most taxonomic authorities consider the species cosmopolitan and conspecific.  A few authorities split the osprey into two species, the <b>western osprey</b> and the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_osprey" title="Eastern osprey">eastern osprey</a>.
</p><p>The osprey differs in several respects from other <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_animal" class="mw-redirect" title="Diurnal animal">diurnal</a> birds of prey. Its toes are of equal length, its <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsi</a> are reticulate, and its talons are rounded, rather than grooved. The osprey and owls are the only raptors whose outer toe is reversible, allowing them to grasp their prey with two toes in front and two behind. This is particularly helpful when they grab slippery fish.<sup id="cite_ref-Terres_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Terres-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It has always presented something of a riddle to taxonomists, but here it is treated as the sole <a href="/wiki/Extant_taxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Extant taxon">living member</a> of the family Pandionidae, and the family listed in its traditional place as part of the order <a href="/wiki/Falconiformes" title="Falconiformes">Falconiformes</a>.
</p><p>Other schemes place it alongside the hawks and eagles in the family <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a>—which itself can be regarded as making up the bulk of the order <a href="/wiki/Accipitriformes" title="Accipitriformes">Accipitriformes</a> or else be lumped with the <a href="/wiki/Falconidae" title="Falconidae">Falconidae</a> into <a href="/wiki/Falconiformes" title="Falconiformes">Falconiformes</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Sibley-Ahlquist_taxonomy" class="mw-redirect" title="Sibley-Ahlquist taxonomy">Sibley-Ahlquist taxonomy</a> has placed it together with the other diurnal raptors in a greatly enlarged <a href="/wiki/Ciconiiformes" class="mw-redirect" title="Ciconiiformes">Ciconiiformes</a>, but this results in an unnatural <a href="/wiki/Paraphyletic" class="mw-redirect" title="Paraphyletic">paraphyletic</a> classification.<sup id="cite_ref-Salzman1993_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salzman1993-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Osprey_mg_9605.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Osprey_mg_9605.jpg/170px-Osprey_mg_9605.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Osprey_mg_9605.jpg/255px-Osprey_mg_9605.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Osprey_mg_9605.jpg/340px-Osprey_mg_9605.jpg 2x" data-file-width="768" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Osprey_mg_9605.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>American subspecies</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wild_Pandion.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Wild_Pandion.jpg/220px-Wild_Pandion.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="181" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Wild_Pandion.jpg/330px-Wild_Pandion.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Wild_Pandion.jpg/440px-Wild_Pandion.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1476" data-file-height="1212" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wild_Pandion.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Australasian subspecies is the most distinctive</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pandion_haliaetus_-San_Francisco_Bay,_California,_USA-head-8_(2).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Pandion_haliaetus_-San_Francisco_Bay%2C_California%2C_USA-head-8_%282%29.jpg/220px-Pandion_haliaetus_-San_Francisco_Bay%2C_California%2C_USA-head-8_%282%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="180" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Pandion_haliaetus_-San_Francisco_Bay%2C_California%2C_USA-head-8_%282%29.jpg/330px-Pandion_haliaetus_-San_Francisco_Bay%2C_California%2C_USA-head-8_%282%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Pandion_haliaetus_-San_Francisco_Bay%2C_California%2C_USA-head-8_%282%29.jpg/440px-Pandion_haliaetus_-San_Francisco_Bay%2C_California%2C_USA-head-8_%282%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2086" data-file-height="1706" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pandion_haliaetus_-San_Francisco_Bay,_California,_USA-head-8_(2).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Californian bird with scraps of fish on its beak</div></div></div>
<p>The osprey is unusual in that it is a single living species that occurs nearly worldwide. Even the few <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are not unequivocally separable. There are four generally recognised subspecies, although differences are small, and <a href="/wiki/Integrated_Taxonomic_Information_System" title="Integrated Taxonomic Information System">ITIS</a> lists only the first two.<sup id="cite_ref-ITIS_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITIS-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>Pandion haliaetus haliaetus</i> – <small>(<a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">1758</a>)</small>: the nominate subspecies, occurring in the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic_realm" title="Palearctic realm">Palearctic realm</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tesky_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tesky-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. haliaetus carolinensis</i> – <small>(<a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Gmelin" title="Johann Friedrich Gmelin">Gmelin</a>, 1788)</small>: <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. This form is larger, darker bodied and has a paler breast than the type of the first description.<sup id="cite_ref-Tesky_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tesky-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. haliaetus ridgwayi</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/Charles_Johnson_Maynard" title="Charles Johnson Maynard">Maynard</a>, 1887</small>: <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> islands. This form has a very pale head and breast compared with nominate <i>haliaetus</i>, with only a weak eye mask.<sup id="cite_ref-Tesky_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tesky-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It is non-migratory. Its scientific name commemorates American <a href="/wiki/Ornithologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Ornithologist">ornithologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Ridgway" title="Robert Ridgway">Robert Ridgway</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Barrow1998_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barrow1998-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Pandion_haliaetus_cristatus" class="mw-redirect" title="Pandion haliaetus cristatus">P. haliaetus cristatus</a></i> – <small>(<a href="/wiki/Louis_Jean_Pierre_Vieillot" title="Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot">Vieillot</a>, 1816)</small>: coastline and some large rivers of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>. The smallest and most distinctive subspecies, also non-migratory.<sup id="cite_ref-Tesky_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tesky-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Some authorities have assigned it full species status<sup id="cite_ref-Christidis2008_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christidis2008-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> as <i><a href="/wiki/Pandion_cristatus" class="mw-redirect" title="Pandion cristatus">Pandion cristatus</a></i>, known as the eastern osprey.<sup id="cite_ref-avibase_cristatus_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-avibase_cristatus-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_record">Fossil record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fossil record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To date there have been two <a href="/wiki/Extinct" class="mw-redirect" title="Extinct">extinct</a> species named from the fossil record.<sup id="cite_ref-Avibase_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avibase-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pandion_homalopteron&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pandion homalopteron (page does not exist)">Pandion homalopteron</a></i> was named by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stuart_L._Warter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stuart L. Warter (page does not exist)">Stuart L. Warter</a> in 1976 from fossils of <a href="/wiki/Middle_Miocene" title="Middle Miocene">Middle Miocene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barstovian" title="Barstovian">Barstovian</a> age, found in marine deposits in the southern part of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. The second named species <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pandion_lovensis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pandion lovensis (page does not exist)">Pandion lovensis</a></i>, was described in 1985 by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_J._Becker&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jonathan J. Becker (page does not exist)">Jonathan J. Becker</a> from fossils found in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> and dating to the <a href="/wiki/Clarendonian" title="Clarendonian">latest Clarendonian</a> and possibly representing a separate lineage from that of <i>P. homalopteron</i> and <i>P. haliaetus</i>. A number of claw fossils have been recovered from Pliocene and Pleistocene sediments in Florida and <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>.
</p><p>The oldest recognized family Pandionidae fossils have been recovered from the Oligocene age <a href="/wiki/Jebel_Qatrani_Formation" title="Jebel Qatrani Formation">Jebel Qatrani Formation</a>, of <a href="/wiki/Faiyum_Governorate" title="Faiyum Governorate">Faiyum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>. However they are not complete enough to assign to a specific genus.<sup id="cite_ref-Olson1985_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olson1985-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Another Pandionidae claw fossil was recovered from <a href="/wiki/Early_Oligocene" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Oligocene">Early Oligocene</a> deposits in the <a href="/wiki/Mainz" title="Mainz">Mainz</a> basin, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, and was described in 2006 by <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Mayr" title="Gerald Mayr">Gerald Mayr</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mayr2006_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mayr2006-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The genus name <i>Pandion</i> derives from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">mythical</a> Greek <a href="/wiki/King_of_Athens" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Athens">king of Athens</a> and grandfather of <a href="/wiki/Theseus" title="Theseus">Theseus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pandion_II" title="Pandion II">Pandion II</a>. Although Pandion II was not used to name a <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nisos" title="Nisos">Nisus</a>, a king of <a href="/wiki/Megara" title="Megara">Megara</a>, was used for the genus.<sup id="cite_ref-job_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The species name <i>haliaetus</i> comes from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>haliaietos</i> <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453) language text">ἁλιάετος</span><sup id="cite_ref-lsj_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lsj-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> from <i>hali-</i> <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453) language text">ἁλι-</span>, "sea-" and <i>aetos</i> <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453) language text">αετός</span>, "eagle".<sup id="cite_ref-job_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The origins of <i>osprey</i> are obscure;<sup id="cite_ref-Livingston1943_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Livingston1943-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> the word itself was first recorded around 1460, derived via the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">Anglo-French</a> <i>ospriet</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> <i>avis prede</i> "bird of prey," from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>avis praedæ</i> though the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a> notes a connection with the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>ossifraga</i> or "bone breaker" of <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Morris1969_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morris1969-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-etymonline_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etymonline-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> However, this term referred to the <a href="/wiki/Lammergeier" class="mw-redirect" title="Lammergeier">Bearded vulture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The osprey is 0.9–2.1&#160;kg (2.0–4.6&#160;lb) in weight and 50–66&#160;cm (20–26&#160;in) in length with a 127–180&#160;cm (50–71&#160;in) wingspan. It is, thus, of similar size to the largest members of the <i><a href="/wiki/Buteo" title="Buteo">Buteo</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Falco_(bird)" class="mw-redirect" title="Falco (bird)">Falco</a></i> genera. The subspecies are fairly close in size, with the nominate subspecies averaging 1.53&#160;kg (3.4&#160;lb), <i>P. h. carolinensis</i> averaging 1.7&#160;kg (3.7&#160;lb) and <i>P. h. cristatus</i> averaging 1.25&#160;kg (2.8&#160;lb). The wing chord measures 38 to 52&#160;cm (15 to 20&#160;in), the tail measures 16.5 to 24&#160;cm (6.5 to 9.4&#160;in) and the tarsus is 5.2–6.6&#160;cm (2.0–2.6&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson-Lees2001_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-Lees2001-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AAB_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAB-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The upperparts are a deep, glossy brown, while the breast is white and sometimes streaked with brown, and the underparts are pure white. The head is white with a dark mask across the eyes, reaching to the sides of the neck.<sup id="cite_ref-CTDEP_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CTDEP-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The irises of the eyes are golden to brown, and the transparent nictitating membrane is pale blue. The bill is black, with a blue <a href="/wiki/Cere" class="mw-redirect" title="Cere">cere</a>, and the feet are white with black talons.<sup id="cite_ref-Terres_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Terres-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> A short tail and long, narrow wings with four long, finger-like feathers, and a shorter fifth, give it a very distinctive appearance.<sup id="cite_ref-Forsman_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forsman-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Osprey_-_Lee_Point.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Osprey_-_Lee_Point.jpg/220px-Osprey_-_Lee_Point.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Osprey_-_Lee_Point.jpg/330px-Osprey_-_Lee_Point.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Osprey_-_Lee_Point.jpg/440px-Osprey_-_Lee_Point.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4367" data-file-height="2911" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Osprey_-_Lee_Point.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In flight, Northern Territory, Australia</div></div></div>
<p>The sexes appear fairly similar, but the adult male can be distinguished from the female by its slimmer body and narrower wings. The breast band of the male is also weaker than that of the female, or is non-existent, and the underwing coverts of the male are more uniformly pale. It is straightforward to determine the sex in a breeding pair, but harder with individual birds.<sup id="cite_ref-Forsman_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forsman-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The juvenile osprey may be identified by buff fringes to the plumage of the upperparts, a buff tone to the underparts, and streaked feathers on the head. During spring, barring on the underwings and flight feathers is a better indicator of a young bird, due to wear on the upperparts.<sup id="cite_ref-CTDEP_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CTDEP-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In flight, the osprey has arched wings and drooping "hands", giving it a <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull-like</a> appearance. The call is a series of sharp whistles, described as <i>cheep, cheep</i> or <i>yewk, yewk</i>. If disturbed by activity near the nest, the call is a frenzied <i>cheereek</i>!<sup id="cite_ref-Peterson1999_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peterson1999-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pandion_haliaetus.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" decoding="async" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a></span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a1/Pandion_haliaetus.ogg" class="internal" title="Pandion haliaetus.ogg"> Osprey&#160;call</a></span>&#160;<small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:Pandion_haliaetus.ogg" title="File:Pandion haliaetus.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ospreys_in_Britain" title="Ospreys in Britain">Ospreys in Britain</a></div>
<p>One of only six land-birds with a cosmopolitan distribution. The osprey is the second most widely distributed raptor species, after the <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">peregrine falcon</a>. It has a worldwide distribution and is found in temperate and tropical regions of all continents except <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>. In <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> it breeds from <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a> south to the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast" class="mw-redirect" title="Gulf Coast">Gulf Coast</a> and <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, wintering further south from the southern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> through to <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bull87_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bull87-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> It is found in summer throughout Europe north into <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> though not Iceland, and winters in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hume2002_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hume2002-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> it is mainly <a href="/wiki/Sedentism" title="Sedentism">sedentary</a> and found patchily around the coastline, though it is a non-breeding visitor to eastern <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Simpsday_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simpsday-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is a 1,000&#160;km (620&#160;mi) gap, corresponding with the coast of the <a href="/wiki/Nullarbor_Plain" title="Nullarbor Plain">Nullarbor Plain</a>, between its westernmost breeding site in <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a> and the nearest breeding sites to the west in <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dennis2007a_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dennis2007a-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> In the islands of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a> it is found in the <a href="/wiki/Bismarck_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Bismarck Islands">Bismarck Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> remains of adults and juveniles have been found in <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a>, where it probably was wiped out by arriving humans.<sup id="cite_ref-Steadman2006_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steadman2006-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> It is possible it may once have ranged across <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a> as well. It is an uncommon to fairly common winter visitor to all parts of <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Rasmussen2005_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rasmussen2005-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> from <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a> through to <a href="/wiki/Indochina" class="mw-redirect" title="Indochina">Indochina</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Strange2000_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Strange2000-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The worldwide distribution of the species is unusual for land-based birds, and only recognised in five other species.<sup id="cite_ref-Monti2015_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monti2015-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The osprey is <a href="/wiki/Piscivore" title="Piscivore">piscivorous</a>, with fish making up 99% of its diet.<sup id="cite_ref-evans82_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-evans82-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> It typically takes fish weighing 150–300&#160;g (5.3–10.6&#160;oz) and about 25–35&#160;cm (9.8–13.8&#160;in) in length, but the weight can range from 50&#160;g (1.8&#160;oz) to 2&#160;kg (4.4&#160;lb). Virtually any type of fish in that size range are taken.
</p><p>Ospreys have vision that is well adapted to detecting underwater objects from the air. Prey is first sighted when the osprey is 10–40&#160;m (33–131&#160;ft) above the water, after which the bird hovers momentarily then plunges feet first into the water.<sup id="cite_ref-BNA_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNA-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Occasionally, the osprey may prey on <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hare" title="Hare">hares</a>, other <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Goenka1985_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goenka1985-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> and small <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-UM_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UM-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">Adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The osprey has several adaptations that suit its <a href="/wiki/Piscivore" title="Piscivore">piscivorous</a> lifestyle:
</p>
<ul><li>reversible outer toes<sup id="cite_ref-Clark1987_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clark1987-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>sharp <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/spicule" class="extiw" title="wikt:spicule">spicules</a> on the underside of the toes<sup id="cite_ref-Clark1987_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clark1987-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>closable nostrils to keep out water during dives</li>
<li>backwards-facing scales on the talons which act as barbs to help hold its catch</li>
<li>dense plumage which is oily and prevents its feathers from getting waterlogged.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Osprey_prepare_to_mate.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Osprey_prepare_to_mate.jpg/220px-Osprey_prepare_to_mate.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Osprey_prepare_to_mate.jpg/330px-Osprey_prepare_to_mate.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Osprey_prepare_to_mate.jpg/440px-Osprey_prepare_to_mate.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2104" data-file-height="1504" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Osprey_prepare_to_mate.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Preparing to mate on the nest</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Osprey_with_its_nest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Osprey_with_its_nest.jpg/220px-Osprey_with_its_nest.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Osprey_with_its_nest.jpg/330px-Osprey_with_its_nest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Osprey_with_its_nest.jpg/440px-Osprey_with_its_nest.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="684" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Osprey_with_its_nest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Osprey standing next to its nest showing their relative sizes</div></div></div>
<p>The osprey breeds near freshwater lakes and rivers, and sometimes on coastal brackish waters. Rocky outcrops just offshore are used in <a href="/wiki/Rottnest_Island" title="Rottnest Island">Rottnest Island</a> off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>, where there are 14 or so similar nesting sites of which five to seven are used in any one year. Many are renovated each season, and some have been used for 70 years. The nest is a large heap of sticks, driftwood, turf or seaweed built in forks of trees, rocky outcrops, utility poles, artificial platforms or offshore islets.<sup id="cite_ref-evans82_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-evans82-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Berulds03_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berulds03-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> As wide as 2 meters and weighing about 135&#160;kg, large nests on utility poles may be <a href="/wiki/Fire_hazard" class="mw-redirect" title="Fire hazard">fire hazards</a> and have caused <a href="/wiki/Power_outage" title="Power outage">power outages</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Generally, ospreys reach sexual maturity and begin breeding around the age of three to four, though in some regions with high osprey densities, such as <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a>, they may not start breeding until five to seven years old, and there may be a shortage of suitable tall structures. If there are no nesting sites available, young ospreys may be forced to delay breeding. To ease this problem, posts are sometimes erected to provide more sites suitable for nest building.<sup id="cite_ref-chesapeake_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chesapeake-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> In some regions ospreys prefer transmission towers as nesting sites, e.g. in East Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pandion_haliaetus_MWNH_0705.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Pandion_haliaetus_MWNH_0705.JPG/170px-Pandion_haliaetus_MWNH_0705.JPG" decoding="async" width="170" height="184" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Pandion_haliaetus_MWNH_0705.JPG/255px-Pandion_haliaetus_MWNH_0705.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Pandion_haliaetus_MWNH_0705.JPG/340px-Pandion_haliaetus_MWNH_0705.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2409" data-file-height="2612" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pandion_haliaetus_MWNH_0705.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>The platform design developed by one organization, <a href="/wiki/Citizens_United_to_Protect_the_Maurice_River_and_Its_Tributaries,_Inc." title="Citizens United to Protect the Maurice River and Its Tributaries, Inc.">Citizens United to Protect the Maurice River and Its Tributaries, Inc.</a> has become the official design of the <a href="/wiki/State_of_New_Jersey" class="mw-redirect" title="State of New Jersey">State of New Jersey</a>, U.S. The platform plans and materials list, available online, have been utilized by people from a number of different geographical regions.<sup id="cite_ref-cumauriceriver_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cumauriceriver-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.osprey-watch.org/">Osprey-watch.org</a> is the global site for mapping osprey nest locations and logging observations on reproductive success.<sup id="cite_ref-osprey-watch_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-osprey-watch-45">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ospreys usually mate for life. Rarely, <a href="/wiki/Polyandry" title="Polyandry">polyandry</a> has been recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-Dennis2007b_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dennis2007b-46">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> The breeding season varies according to latitude; spring (September–October) in southern Australia, April to July in northern Australia and winter (June–August) in southern Queensland.<sup id="cite_ref-Berulds03_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berulds03-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> In spring the pair begins a five-month period of partnership to raise their young. The female lays two to four <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> within a month, and relies on the size of the nest to conserve heat. The eggs are whitish with bold splotches of reddish-brown and are about 6.2&#160;cm ×&#160;4.5&#160;cm (2.4&#160;in ×&#160;1.8&#160;in) and weigh about 65&#160;g (2.3&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-Berulds03_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berulds03-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The eggs are incubated for about 35–43 days to hatching.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The newly hatched chicks weigh only 50–60&#160;g (1.8–2.1&#160;oz), but fledge in 8–10 weeks. A study on <a href="/wiki/Kangaroo_Island" title="Kangaroo Island">Kangaroo Island</a>, South Australia, had an average time between hatching and fledging of 69 days. The same study found an average of 0.66 young fledged per year per occupied territory, and 0.92 young fledged per year per active nest. Some 22% of surviving young either remained on the island or returned at maturity to join the breeding population.<sup id="cite_ref-Dennis2007b_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dennis2007b-46">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> When food is scarce, the first chicks to hatch are most likely to survive. The typical lifespan is 7–10 years, though rarely individuals can grow to as old as 20–25 years.
</p><p>The oldest European wild osprey on record lived to be over thirty years of age. In North America, <a href="/wiki/Great_horned_owl" title="Great horned owl">great horned owls</a> (<i>Bubo virginianus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">golden eagles</a> (<i>Aquila chrysaetos</i>), and <a href="/wiki/Bald_eagle" title="Bald eagle">bald eagles</a> (<i>Haliaeetus leucocephalus</i>) are the only major predators of ospreys, capable of taking both nestlings and adults.<sup id="cite_ref-UM_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UM-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Flemming1990_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Flemming1990-48">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Macdonald1994_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macdonald1994-49">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cold1993_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cold1993-50">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lafontaine1976_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lafontaine1976-51">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Kleptoparasitism" title="Kleptoparasitism">kleptoparasitism</a> by bald eagles, where the larger raptor steals the osprey's catch, is more common than predation. The <a href="/wiki/White-tailed_eagle" title="White-tailed eagle">white-tailed eagle</a> (<i>Haliaeetus albicilla</i>), which is very similar to the bald eagle, may harass or prey on the osprey in Eurasia.<sup id="cite_ref-Willgohs1961_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Willgohs1961-52">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">Raccoons</a> (<i>Procyon lotor</i>) can be a serious threat to nestlings or eggs if they can access the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-Reese1969_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reese1969-53">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Endoparasitic <a href="/wiki/Trematode" class="mw-redirect" title="Trematode">trematodes</a> (<i>Scaphanocephalus expansus</i> and <i>Neodiplostomum</i> spp.) have been recorded in wild ospreys.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoffman1953_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoffman1953-54">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration">Migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> breeders winter in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-55">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> breeders winter in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, although some stay in the southernmost U.S. states such as <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> and <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-microwave_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-microwave-56">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Some ospreys from Florida migrate to South America.<sup id="cite_ref-Martell2004_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martell2004-57">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasian</a> ospreys tend not to <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a>.
</p><p>Studies of Swedish ospreys showed that females tend to migrate to Africa earlier than the males. More stopovers are made during their autumn migration. The variation of timing and duration in autumn was more variable than in spring. Although migrating predominantly in the day, they sometimes fly in the dark hours particularly in crossings over water and cover on average 260–280&#160;km (160–170&#160;mi) per day with a maximum of 431&#160;km (268&#160;mi) per day.<sup id="cite_ref-Alerstam2006_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alerstam2006-58">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> European birds may also winter in South Asia, indicated by an osprey tagged in Norway being monitored in western India.<sup id="cite_ref-Mundkur1988_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mundkur1988-59">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> In the Mediterranean, Ospreys show partial migratory behaviour with some individuals remaining resident, whilst others undertake relatively short migration trips.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mortality">Mortality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mortality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Swedish ospreys have a significantly higher mortality rate during migration seasons than during stationary periods, with more than half of the total annual mortality occurring during migration.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> These deaths can also be categorized into spatial patterns: Spring mortality occurs mainly in Africa, which can be traced to crossing the <a href="/wiki/Sahara_desert" class="mw-redirect" title="Sahara desert">Sahara desert</a>. Mortality can also occur through mishaps with human utilities, such as nesting near <a href="/wiki/Electrical_wiring" title="Electrical wiring">electrical wiring</a> or collisions with aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_and_conservation">Status and conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Status and conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Juvenile_osprey_on_nest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Juvenile_osprey_on_nest.jpg/220px-Juvenile_osprey_on_nest.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Juvenile_osprey_on_nest.jpg/330px-Juvenile_osprey_on_nest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Juvenile_osprey_on_nest.jpg/440px-Juvenile_osprey_on_nest.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1221" data-file-height="814" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Juvenile_osprey_on_nest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Juvenile on a man-made nest</div></div></div>
<p>The osprey has a large range, covering 9,670,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (3,730,000&#160;sq&#160;mi) in just Africa and the Americas, and has a large global population estimated at 460,000 individuals. Although global population trends have not been quantified, the species is not believed to approach the thresholds for the population decline criterion of the IUCN Red List (i.e., declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations), and for these reasons, the species is evaluated as Least Concern.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> There is evidence for regional decline in South Australia where former territories at locations in the <a href="/wiki/Spencer_Gulf" title="Spencer Gulf">Spencer Gulf</a> and along the lower <a href="/wiki/Murray_River" title="Murray River">Murray River</a> have been vacant for decades.<sup id="cite_ref-Dennis2007a_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dennis2007a-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the main threats to osprey populations were egg collectors and hunting of the adults along with other birds of prey,<sup id="cite_ref-UM_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UM-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-63">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> but osprey populations declined drastically in many areas in the 1950s and 1960s; this appeared to be in part due to the toxic effects of insecticides such as <a href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a> on reproduction.<sup id="cite_ref-Ames1966_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ames1966-64">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> The pesticide interfered with the bird's <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> metabolism which resulted in thin-shelled, easily broken or infertile eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-Bull87_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bull87-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Possibly because of the banning of DDT in many countries in the early 1970s, together with reduced persecution, the osprey, as well as other affected <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> species, have made significant recoveries.<sup id="cite_ref-evans82_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-evans82-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> In South Australia, nesting sites on the <a href="/wiki/Eyre_Peninsula" title="Eyre Peninsula">Eyre Peninsula</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kangaroo_Island" title="Kangaroo Island">Kangaroo Island</a> are vulnerable to unmanaged coastal recreation and encroaching urban development.<sup id="cite_ref-Dennis2007a_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dennis2007a-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_depictions">Cultural depictions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cultural depictions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>The Roman writer <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> reported that parent ospreys made their young fly up to the sun as a test, and dispatched any that failed.<sup id="cite_ref-deVries76_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deVries76-65">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Another odd legend regarding this fish-eating bird of prey, derived from the writings of <a href="/wiki/Albertus_Magnus" title="Albertus Magnus">Albertus Magnus</a> and recorded in <a href="/wiki/Raphael_Holinshed" title="Raphael Holinshed">Holinshed's</a> <i>Chronicles</i>, was that it had one webbed foot and one taloned foot.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-63">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cooper92_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper92-66">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The osprey is mentioned in the famous Chinese folk poem "guan guan ju jiu" (關關雎鳩); "ju jiu" 雎鳩 refers to the osprey, and "guan guan" (關關) to its voice. In the poem, the osprey is considered to be an icon of fidelity and harmony between wife and husband, due to its highly monogamous habits. Some commentators have claimed that "ju jiu" in the poem is not the osprey but the <a href="/wiki/Mallard_duck" class="mw-redirect" title="Mallard duck">mallard duck</a>, since the osprey cannot make the sound "guan guan".<sup id="cite_ref-vogel_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vogel-67">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jiang_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jiang-68">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The Irish poet <a href="/wiki/William_Butler_Yeats" class="mw-redirect" title="William Butler Yeats">William Butler Yeats</a> used a grey wandering osprey as a representation of sorrow in <i>The Wanderings of Oisin and Other Poems</i> (1889).<sup id="cite_ref-deVries76_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deVries76-65">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>There was a medieval belief that fish were so mesmerised by the osprey that they turned belly-up in surrender,<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_63-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-63">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> and this is referenced by <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a> in Act 4 Scene 5 of <i><a href="/wiki/Coriolanus_(play)" class="mw-redirect" title="Coriolanus (play)">Coriolanus</a></i>:</li></ul>
<blockquote>
<p>I think he'll be to Rome<br />
As is the osprey to the fish, who takes it<br />
By sovereignty of nature.
</p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Buddhism, the osprey is sometimes represented as the "King of Birds", especially in <i> 'The Jātaka: Or, Stories of the Buddha’s Former Births' </i>, no. 486.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iconography">Iconography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Iconography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Selous-Scouts-cap-badge.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Selous-Scouts-cap-badge.png/220px-Selous-Scouts-cap-badge.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="187" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Selous-Scouts-cap-badge.png/330px-Selous-Scouts-cap-badge.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3d/Selous-Scouts-cap-badge.png 2x" data-file-width="379" data-file-height="323" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Selous-Scouts-cap-badge.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Cap_badge" title="Cap badge">Cap badge</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Selous_Scouts" title="Selous Scouts">Selous Scouts</a> was a stylized osprey</div></div></div>
<ul><li>In <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">heraldry</a>, the osprey is typically depicted as a white eagle,<sup id="cite_ref-Cooper92_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper92-66">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> often maintaining a fish in its talons or beak, and termed a "sea-eagle." It is historically regarded as a symbol of vision and abundance; more recently it has become a symbol of positive responses to nature,<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_63-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-63">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> and has been featured on more than 50 international <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamp" title="Postage stamp">postage stamps</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-stamp_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stamp-69">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><span class="anchor" id="Provincial_bird"></span>In 1994, the osprey was declared the provincial bird of <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gov.ns.ca_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gov.ns.ca-70">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>It is also the official bird of <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B6dermanland" title="Södermanland">Södermanland</a>, Sweden.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Cap_badge" title="Cap badge">cap badge</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rhodesia" title="Rhodesia">Rhodesia</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Selous_Scouts" title="Selous Scouts">Selous Scouts</a> (1973-1980) was a stylized osprey.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The osprey is used as a brand name for various products and sports teams, such as the <a href="/wiki/Ospreys_(rugby_union)" title="Ospreys (rugby union)">Ospreys</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Rugby_Union" title="Welsh Rugby Union">Welsh</a> <a href="/wiki/Rugby_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="Rugby Union">Rugby</a> team) and the <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Seahawks" title="Seattle Seahawks">Seattle Seahawks</a> (an <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a> team of the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osprey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>So-called "osprey" plumes were an important item in the <a href="/wiki/Plume_trade" class="mw-redirect" title="Plume trade">plume trade</a> of the late 19th century and used in hats including those used as part of the army uniform. Despite their name, these plumes were actually obtained from <a href="/wiki/Egret" title="Egret">egrets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-birdnotesnews_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdnotesnews-71">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
