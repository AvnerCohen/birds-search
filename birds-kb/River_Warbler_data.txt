<p>The <b>river warbler</b> (<i>Locustella fluviatilis</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">Old World warbler</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Grass_warbler" title="Grass warbler">grass warbler</a> genus <i>Locustella</i>. It breeds in east and central <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> into the western <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, wintering in east <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. The genus name <i>Locustella</i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and is a diminutive of <i>locusta</i>,  "grasshopper".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This refers to the song of the <a href="/wiki/Common_grasshopper_warbler" title="Common grasshopper warbler">common grasshopper warbler</a> and some others in this genus.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The specific <i>fluviatilis</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "of a river".<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This small <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> bird is a species found in dense deciduous vegetation close to water in bogs or near a river. Five to seven eggs are laid in a nest in a tussock or on the ground. This species is a rare vagrant to western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. In Britain, a small number of males have set up <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territories</a> in spring, including a bird in <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester" title="Greater Manchester">Greater Manchester</a> in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> One exceptional vagrant was photographed in <a href="/wiki/Gambell,_Alaska" title="Gambell, Alaska">Gambell, Alaska</a> in October 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This is a largish warbler. The adult has an unstreaked grey-brown back, whitish grey underparts, and a darker undertail, which has white feather tips giving a contrasting pattern. The sexes are identical, as with most warblers, but young birds are yellower below. Like most warblers, it is <a href="/wiki/Insectivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Insectivorous">insectivorous</a>.
</p><p>Some birds can show reduced dark markings on the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Undertail-covert&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Undertail-covert (page does not exist)">undertail-coverts</a> (caused by more extensive than usual white tips) and thus are closer in appearance to <a href="/wiki/Savi%27s_warbler" title="Savi&#39;s warbler">Savi's warbler</a> than typical birds. However, they typically still have a streaked breast and more olive colouration on the upperparts.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This is a skulky species which is very difficult to see except sometimes when singing. It creeps through grass and low foliage.
</p><p>The song is a monotonous mechanical <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insect</a>-like reeling, often given at dusk. It is similar to the song of other species in the group, but has more of a sewing machine quality, and may be produced for long periods.
</p>
