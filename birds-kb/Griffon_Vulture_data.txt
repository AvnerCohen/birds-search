<p>The <b>griffon vulture</b> (<i>Gyps fulvus</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Old_World_vulture" title="Old World vulture">Old World vulture</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> family <a href="/wiki/Accipitridae" title="Accipitridae">Accipitridae</a>. It is also known as the <b>Eurasian griffon</b>. It is not to be confused with a different species, <a href="/wiki/R%C3%BCppell%27s_vulture" title="Rüppell&#39;s vulture">Rüppell's griffon vulture</a> (<i>Gyps rueppellii</i>). It is closely related to the <a href="/wiki/White-backed_vulture" title="White-backed vulture">white-backed vulture</a> (<i>Gyps africanus</i>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Griffon_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The griffon vulture is 93–122&#160;cm (37–48&#160;in) long with a 2.3–2.8&#160;m (7.5–9.2&#160;ft) wingspan. In the nominate race the males weigh 6.2 to 10.5&#160;kg (14 to 23&#160;lb) and females typically weigh 6.5 to 10.5&#160;kg (14 to 23&#160;lb), while in the Indian subspecies (<i>G. f. fulvescens</i>), the vultures average 7.1&#160;kg (16&#160;lb). Extreme adult weights have been reported from 4.5 to 15&#160;kg (9.9 to 33.1&#160;lb), the latter likely a weight attained in captivity.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson-Lees2001_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson-Lees2001-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ali1996_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ali1996-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Hatched naked, it is a typical Old World vulture in appearance, with a very white head, very broad wings and short tail feathers. It has a white neck ruff and yellow bill. The buff body and wing coverts contrast with the dark flight <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Griffon_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like other <a href="/wiki/Vulture" title="Vulture">vultures</a>, it is a <a href="/wiki/Scavenger" title="Scavenger">scavenger</a>, feeding mostly from carcasses of dead animals which it finds by soaring over open areas, often moving in flocks. It establishes nesting colonies in cliffs that are undisturbed by humans while coverage of open areas and availability of dead animals within dozens of kilometres of these cliffs is high.<sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili5_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili5-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili6_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili6-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It grunts and hisses at roosts or when feeding on <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">carrion</a>.
</p><p>The maximum recorded lifespan of the griffon vulture is 41.4 years for an individual in captivity.<sup id="cite_ref-Carey_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carey-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It breeds on crags in mountains in southern Europe, north Africa, and Asia, laying one egg. Griffon vultures may form loose colonies. The population is mostly resident. Juveniles and immature individuals may migrate far or embark on long-distance movements.<sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili2_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili2-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gavashelishvili12_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gavashelishvili12-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Density Dependence in this colonial species has been shown to affect annual reproductive success with eyries in protected location (caves, potholes and sheltered ledges) producing more fledglings, and used preferentially, than low-quality eyries (exposed ledges and open crevices), which were only used when the number of breeding individuals increased.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<ul><li>In Italy, the species managed to survive only in the island of <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a>, but a few attempts at <a href="/wiki/Reintroduction" class="mw-redirect" title="Reintroduction">reintroducing</a> the griffon in the peninsula have been recently made, too. As a result, several specimens have been spotted again in August 2006 on the <a href="/wiki/Gran_Sasso_d%27Italia" title="Gran Sasso d&#39;Italia">Gran Sasso</a> massif (central Italy). Populations in Italy are thought to be undergoing a vigorous increase, thanks to reintroduction schemes in neighbouring countries taking effect, and a ban on hunting the species.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>, a colony of griffon vultures can be found near the town of <a href="/wiki/Beli_(Croatia)" class="mw-redirect" title="Beli (Croatia)">Beli</a> on the island of <a href="/wiki/Cres" title="Cres">Cres</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-wildlifeextra_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wildlifeextra-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> There they breed at lower elevations, with some nests just 10&#160;m (33&#160;ft) above sea level. Therefore, contact with people is common. The population makes frequent incursions in the Slovenian territory, especially in the mountain <a href="/wiki/Stol_(Karawanks)" title="Stol (Karawanks)">Stol</a> above <a href="/wiki/Kobarid" title="Kobarid">Kobarid</a>. The bird is protected in an area called Kuntrep on the Croatian island of <a href="/wiki/Krk" title="Krk">Krk</a><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, the first record of a Griffon Vulture occurred in 1843 in Cork.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> More recently, in 2000 a vulture took up residence on the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Island</a> of <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, there is an unsustainable colony of fewer than 30 birds (2016) at <a href="/wiki/Episkopi,_Limassol" title="Episkopi, Limassol">Episkopi</a>, in the south of the island.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> colonies of griffon vultures can be found in northern <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Golan_Heights" title="Golan Heights">Golan Heights</a>, where a large colony breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Mount_Carmel" title="Mount Carmel">Carmel Mountains</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Negev" title="Negev">Negev</a> desert and especially at <a href="/wiki/Gamla" title="Gamla">Gamla</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Reintroduction" class="mw-redirect" title="Reintroduction">reintroduction</a> projects are being carried out at breeding centres in the <a href="/wiki/Carmel_Hai-Bar_Nature_Reserve" title="Carmel Hai-Bar Nature Reserve">Carmel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yotvata_Hai-Bar_Nature_Reserve" title="Yotvata Hai-Bar Nature Reserve">Negev</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, there are nearly 1000 birds. On <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a> they can be found in most mountainous areas, sometimes in groups of up to 20.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Griffon vultures have been <a href="/wiki/Reintroduction" class="mw-redirect" title="Reintroduction">reintroduced</a> successfully into the <a href="/wiki/Massif_Central" title="Massif Central">Massif Central</a> in France; about 500 are now found there. Griffon vultures are regularly spotted over the <a href="/wiki/Millau_bridge" class="mw-redirect" title="Millau bridge">Millau bridge</a>, and since 2015 also in the <a href="/wiki/Mounts_of_Cantal" title="Mounts of Cantal">Cantal Mountains</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, around 100 birds were present in the summer of 2007. These were vagrants from the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a> population (see below).<sup id="cite_ref-n-tv2007_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-n-tv2007-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In Germany, the species died out in the mid-18th century. Some 200 vagrant birds, probably from the Pyrenees, were sighted in 2006,<sup id="cite_ref-handelsblatt2006_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-handelsblatt2006-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and several dozen of the vagrants sighted in Belgium the following year crossed into Germany in search for food.<sup id="cite_ref-n-tv2007-2_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-n-tv2007-2-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> There are plans to reintroduce the species in the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>. In September 2008, pieces of a griffon vulture bone, about 35,000 years old, were excavated from <a href="/wiki/Hohle_Fels" title="Hohle Fels">Hohle Fels</a> cave in southern Germany, which are believed to form a <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">flute</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ap_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ap-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sc_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sc-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>, there are around 60–65 pairs of griffon vultures in the western parts of the country, around <a href="/wiki/Zlatar_(mountain)" title="Zlatar (mountain)">Zlatar mountain</a> and also 35 birds in the canyon of the <a href="/wiki/Tre%C5%A1njica" title="Trešnjica">Trešnjica river</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ugradu_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ugradu-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> They are under legal protection from hunting.<sup id="cite_ref-zlatar_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zlatar-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gyps_fulvus_MWNH_0732.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Gyps_fulvus_MWNH_0732.JPG/220px-Gyps_fulvus_MWNH_0732.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="222" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Gyps_fulvus_MWNH_0732.JPG/330px-Gyps_fulvus_MWNH_0732.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Gyps_fulvus_MWNH_0732.JPG/440px-Gyps_fulvus_MWNH_0732.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2624" data-file-height="2651" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gyps_fulvus_MWNH_0732.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg</div></div></div>
<ul><li>In <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, there is a population of several dozen birds.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, there is a remnant population around <a href="/wiki/Salzburg_Zoo" title="Salzburg Zoo">Salzburg Zoo</a>, and vagrants from the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a> are often seen.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, in 2008, there were 25,000 birds, from a low of a few thousand around 1980. Spain has the biggest colony of Griffon vultures in all Europe. It is located at <a href="/wiki/Hoces_del_R%C3%ADo_Durat%C3%B3n_Natural_Park" title="Hoces del Río Duratón Natural Park">Hoces del Río Duratón Natural Park</a> (<a href="/wiki/Province_of_Segovia" title="Province of Segovia">Province of Segovia</a>).</li>
<li>The Pyrenees population has apparently been affected by an <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">EC</a> ruling that due to danger of <a href="/wiki/Bovine_spongiform_encephalopathy" title="Bovine spongiform encephalopathy">BSE</a> transmission, no carcasses must be left on the fields for the time being. This has critically lowered food availability, and consequently, <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a>. Although the griffon vulture does not normally attack larger living prey, there are reports of Spanish griffon vultures killing weak, young or unhealthy living animals as they do not find enough carrion to eat.<sup id="cite_ref-newscientist2007_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newscientist2007-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In May 2013, a 52-year-old woman who was hiking in the Pyrenees and had fallen off a cliff to her death was eaten by griffon vultures before rescue workers were able to recover her body, leaving only her clothes and a few of her bones. Due to her being the first human to be documented being eaten by griffon vultures, the story brought worldwide attention to the griffon vulture problems in Southern Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-inquisitr_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inquisitr-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a> there are 46-54 pairs according to last estimation of population; the trend demonstrates slight increasing.<sup id="cite_ref-ABCC_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABCC-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In Russia, nests on the northern slopes of the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Caucasus" title="Greater Caucasus">Greater Caucasus</a>.</li></ul>
<p>The main cause of the rapid decline in the griffon vulture population is the consumption of poisoned baits set out by people. Wildlife conservation efforts have attempted to increase awareness of the lethal consequences of using illegally poisoned baits through education about the issue.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physiology">Physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Griffon_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Griffon vultures have been used as model organisms for the study of soaring and thermoregulation.  The energy costs of level flight tend to be high, prompting alternatives to flapping in larger birds. Vultures in particular utilize more efficient flying methods such as soaring. Compared to other birds, which elevate their metabolic rate to upwards of 16 times their basal metabolic rate in flight,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> soaring griffon vultures expend about 1.43 times their basal metabolic rate in flight. Griffon vultures are also efficient flyers in their ability to return to a resting heart rate after flight within ten minutes.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As large scavengers, griffon vultures have not been observed to seek shelter for thermoregulation. Vultures use their bald heads as a means to thermoregulate in both extreme cold and hot temperatures. Changes in posture can increase bare skin exposure from 7% to 32%. This change allows for the more than doubling of convective heat loss in still air.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Griffon vultures have also been found to tolerate increased body temperatures as a response to high ambient temperatures. By allowing their internal body temperature to change independently of their metabolic rate, griffon vultures minimize their loss of water and energy in thermoregulating.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> One study in particular (Bahat 1995) found that these adaptations have allowed the Griffon vulture to have one of the widest thermal neutral zones of any bird.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Intraspecific_competition">Intraspecific competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Griffon_vulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Intraspecific competition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gyps_fulvus.002_-_Natural_History_Museum_of_London.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Gyps_fulvus.002_-_Natural_History_Museum_of_London.JPG/220px-Gyps_fulvus.002_-_Natural_History_Museum_of_London.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Gyps_fulvus.002_-_Natural_History_Museum_of_London.JPG/330px-Gyps_fulvus.002_-_Natural_History_Museum_of_London.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Gyps_fulvus.002_-_Natural_History_Museum_of_London.JPG/440px-Gyps_fulvus.002_-_Natural_History_Museum_of_London.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gyps_fulvus.002_-_Natural_History_Museum_of_London.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Taxidermy" title="Taxidermy">mounted specimen</a> alongside numerous other birds of prey, <a href="/wiki/Natural_History_Museum,_London" title="Natural History Museum, London">Natural History Museum, London</a></div></div></div>
<p>In respect to varying age ranges, the griffon vultures evidently show no difference in feeding rates. Inevitably, as resource availability increases, feeding rates tend to follow the same pattern. Upon studying the reintroduction of this species and its impact on the intraspecific competition, old adults are more inclined to display aggressive behavior and signs of dominance in comparison to the other age ranges. In terms of comparing the male and female sexes, there are no observed differences in competitive behaviors. Lastly, the reintroduced individuals of the species and the wild-bred do not differ in dominance or feeding rate despite the differences in upbringing.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
