<p>The <b>Peregrine Falcon</b> (<i>Falco peregrinus</i>), also known as the <b>Peregrine</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> and historically as the <b>Duck Hawk</b> in North America,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> is a widespread <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Falconidae" title="Falconidae">Falconidae</a>. A large, <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crow</a>-sized <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon</a>, it has a blue-grey back, barred white underparts, and a black head and "moustache". As is typical of <a href="/wiki/Avivore" title="Avivore">bird-eating</a> raptors, Peregrine Falcons are <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexually dimorphic</a>, females being considerably larger than males.<sup id="cite_ref-White94_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwp_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The Peregrine is renowned for its speed, reaching over 322&#160;km/h (200&#160;mph) during its characteristic hunting stoop (high speed dive),<sup id="cite_ref-F.26WL_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-F.26WL-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> making it the fastest member of the animal kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Smithsonian_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smithsonian-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Peregrine's breeding range includes land regions from the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_tundra" title="Arctic tundra" class="mw-redirect">Arctic tundra</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>. It can be found nearly everywhere on Earth, except extreme <a href="/wiki/Polar_region" title="Polar region">polar regions</a>, very high mountains, and most <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">tropical rainforests</a>; the only major ice-free landmass from which it is entirely absent is <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. This makes it the world's most widespread raptor<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> and one of the most widely found bird species. In fact, the only land-based bird species found over a larger geographic area is not always naturally occurring but one widely introduced by humans, the <a href="/wiki/Rock_Pigeon" title="Rock Pigeon" class="mw-redirect">Rock Pigeon</a>, which in turn now supports many Peregrine populations as a prey species. Both the English and <a href="/wiki/Scientific_name" title="Scientific name" class="mw-redirect">scientific names</a> of this <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> mean "wandering falcon", referring to the <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> habits of many northern populations. Experts recognize 17 to 19 <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> which vary in appearance and range; there is disagreement over whether the distinctive <a href="/wiki/Barbary_Falcon" title="Barbary Falcon">Barbary Falcon</a> is represented by two subspecies of <i>Falco peregrinus,</i> or is a separate species, <i>F. pelegrinoides.</i></p>
<p>While its diet consists almost exclusively of medium-sized birds, the Peregrine will occasionally hunt small mammals, small reptiles, or even insects. Reaching sexual maturity at one year, it mates for life and nests in a <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest#Scrape" title="Bird nest">scrape</a>, normally on cliff edges or, in recent times, on tall human-made structures.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The Peregrine Falcon became an endangered species in many areas because of pesticides, especially <a href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a>. Since the ban on DDT from the early 1970s, populations have recovered, supported by large-scale protection of nesting places and releases to the wild.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Peregrine_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The Peregrine Falcon has a body length of 34 to 58 centimetres (13–23 in) and a wingspan from 74 to 120 centimetres (29–47 in).<sup id="cite_ref-White94_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dewey_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dewey-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The male and female have similar markings and <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>, but as in many <a href="/wiki/Birds_of_prey" title="Birds of prey" class="mw-redirect">birds of prey</a> the Peregrine Falcon displays marked reverse <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a> in size, with the female measuring up to 30% larger than the male.<sup id="cite_ref-Scholz_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scholz-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Males weigh 424 to 750 grams (0.93–1.7 lb) and the noticeably larger females weigh 910 to 1,500 grams (2.0–3.3 lb); for variation in weight between subspecies, see below. The standard linear measurements of Peregrines are: the wing chord measures 26.5–39 cm (10.4–15 in), the tail measures 13–19 cm (5.1–7.5 in) and the tarsus measures 4.5 to 5.6 cm (1.8 to 2.2 in).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The back and the long pointed wings of the adult are usually bluish black to slate grey with indistinct darker barring (see "Subspecies" <a href="#species">below</a>); the wingtips are black.<sup id="cite_ref-Dewey_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dewey-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The white to rusty underparts are barred with thin clean bands of dark brown or black.<sup id="cite_ref-Lees_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lees-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The tail, coloured like the back but with thin clean bars, is long, narrow, and rounded at the end with a black tip and a white band at the very end. The top of the head and a "moustache" along the cheeks are black, contrasting sharply with the pale sides of the neck and white throat.<sup id="cite_ref-Terres91_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Terres91-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Cere" title="Cere" class="mw-redirect">cere</a> is yellow, as are the feet, and the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">beak</a> and <a href="/wiki/Claw" title="Claw">claws</a> are black.<sup id="cite_ref-Beckstead_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beckstead-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The upper beak is notched near the tip, an <a href="/wiki/Adaptation_(biology)" title="Adaptation (biology)" class="mw-redirect">adaptation</a> which enables falcons to kill prey by severing the <a href="/wiki/Cervical_vertebrae" title="Cervical vertebrae">spinal column</a> at the neck.<sup id="cite_ref-White94_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwp_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-F.26WL_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-F.26WL-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The immature bird is much browner with streaked, rather than barred, underparts, and has a pale bluish cere and orbital ring.<sup id="cite_ref-White94_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Peregrine_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Audubon-peregrinus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Audubon-peregrinus.jpg/220px-Audubon-peregrinus.jpg" width="220" height="130" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Audubon-peregrinus.jpg/330px-Audubon-peregrinus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Audubon-peregrinus.jpg/440px-Audubon-peregrinus.jpg 2x" /></a>

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Audubon-peregrinus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p><i>Falco peregrinus</i> was first described under its current binomial name by English ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Marmaduke_Tunstall" title="Marmaduke Tunstall">Marmaduke Tunstall</a> in his 1771 work <i>Ornithologia Britannica</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> The scientific name <i>Falco peregrinus</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> phrase that was used by <a href="/wiki/Albertus_Magnus" title="Albertus Magnus">Albertus Magnus</a> in 1225. The specific name taken from the fact that juvenile birds were taken while journeying to their breeding location rather than from the nest, as falcon nests were difficult to get at.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The Latin term for falcon, <i>falco</i>, is related to <i>falx</i>, the Latin word meaning <a href="/wiki/Sickle" title="Sickle">sickle</a>, in reference to the silhouette of the falcon's long, pointed wings in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-F.26WL_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-F.26WL-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Peregrine Falcon belongs to a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> whose lineage includes the <a href="/wiki/Hierofalcon" title="Hierofalcon">hierofalcons</a><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Prairie_Falcon" title="Prairie Falcon">Prairie Falcon</a> (<i>F.&#160;mexicanus</i>). This lineage probably diverged from other falcons towards the end of the <a href="/wiki/Late_Miocene" title="Late Miocene">Late Miocene</a> or in the <a href="/wiki/Early_Pliocene" title="Early Pliocene" class="mw-redirect">Early Pliocene</a>, about 5-8&#160;<a href="/wiki/Million_years_ago" title="Million years ago" class="mw-redirect">million years ago</a> (mya). As the Peregrine-hierofalcon group includes both <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> and North American species, it is likely that the lineage originated in western <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> or Africa. Its relationship to other falcons is not clear; the issue is complicated by widespread <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridization</a> confounding <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA" class="mw-redirect">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">sequence</a> analyses; for example a genetic lineage of the <a href="/wiki/Saker_Falcon" title="Saker Falcon">Saker Falcon</a> (<i>F.&#160;cherrug</i>) is known<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> which originated from a male Saker producing fertile young with a female Peregrine ancestor, and the descendants further breeding with Sakers.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Today, Peregrines are regularly paired in captivity with other species such as the <a href="/wiki/Lanner_Falcon" title="Lanner Falcon">Lanner Falcon</a> (<i>F.&#160;biarmicus</i>) to produce the "<a href="/wiki/Perilanner" title="Perilanner">perilanner</a>", a somewhat popular bird in <a href="/wiki/Falconry" title="Falconry">falconry</a> as it combines the Peregrine's hunting skill with the Lanner's hardiness, or the <a href="/wiki/Gyrfalcon" title="Gyrfalcon">Gyrfalcon</a> to produce large, strikingly coloured birds for the use of falconers. As can be seen, the Peregrine is still genetically close to the hierofalcons, though their lineages diverged in the <a href="/wiki/Late_Pliocene" title="Late Pliocene" class="mw-redirect">Late Pliocene</a> (maybe some 2.5–2&#160;mya in the <a href="/wiki/Gelasian" title="Gelasian">Gelasian</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Peregrine_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span></h3>
<p>Numerous <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of <i>Falco peregrinus</i> have been described, with 19 accepted by the 1994 <i><a href="/wiki/Handbook_of_the_Birds_of_the_World" title="Handbook of the Birds of the World">Handbook of the Birds of the World</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-White94_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwp_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Vaurie_.281961.29_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vaurie_.281961.29-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> which considers the <a href="/wiki/Barbary_Falcon" title="Barbary Falcon">Barbary Falcon</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> and coastal north <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> to be two subspecies <i>(pelegrinoides</i> and <i>babylonicus)</i> of <i>Falco peregrinus,</i> rather than a distinct species, <i>F. pelegrinoides.</i> The following map shows the general ranges of these 19 subspecies:</p>

<ul>
<li><i><b>Falco peregrinus anatum</b>,</i> described by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lucien_Jules_Laurent_Bonaparte" title="Charles Lucien Jules Laurent Bonaparte" class="mw-redirect">Bonaparte</a> in 1838,<sup id="cite_ref-AOU164_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOU164-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> is known as the American Peregrine Falcon, or "Duck Hawk"; its scientific name means "Duck Peregrine Falcon". At one time, it was partly included in <i>leucogenys</i>. It is mainly found in the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a> today. It was formerly common throughout North America between the tundra and northern <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, where current <a href="/wiki/Reintroduction" title="Reintroduction">reintroduction</a> efforts seek to restore the population.<sup id="cite_ref-AOU164_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOU164-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Most mature <i>anatum</i>, except those that breed in more northern areas, winter in their breeding range. Most vagrants that reach western Europe seem to belong to the more northern and strongly migratory <i>tundrius</i>, only considered distinct since 1968. It is similar to <i>peregrinus</i> but is slightly smaller; adults are somewhat paler and less patterned below, but juveniles are darker and more patterned below. Males weigh 500 to 700 grams (1.1–1.5 lb), while females weigh 800 to 1,100 grams (1.8–2.4 lb).<sup id="cite_ref-Whiteetal2002_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whiteetal2002-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> It has become extinct in eastern North America, and populations there are hybrids as a result of reintroductions of birds from elsewhere.<sup id="cite_ref-lehr2000_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lehr2000-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li><i><b>Falco peregrinus babylonicus</b>,</i> described by <a href="/wiki/Philip_Sclater" title="Philip Sclater">P.L. Sclater</a> in 1861, is found in eastern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Hindu_Kush" title="Hindu Kush">Hindu Kush</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tian_Shan" title="Tian Shan">Tian Shan</a> to <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_Altai" title="Mongolian Altai" class="mw-redirect">Mongolian Altai</a> ranges. A few birds winter in northern and northwestern India, mainly in dry semi-desert habitats.<sup id="cite_ref-Pande_et_al_2007_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pande_et_al_2007-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> It is paler than <i>pelegrinoides</i>, and somewhat similar to a small, pale <a href="/wiki/Lanner_Falcon" title="Lanner Falcon">Lanner Falcon</a> <i>(Falco biarmicus).</i> Males weigh 330 to 400 grams (12 to 14 oz), while females weigh 513 to 765 grams (18.1 to 27.0 oz).<sup id="cite_ref-bwp_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Falco peregrinus brookei</b>,</i> described by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bowdler_Sharpe" title="Richard Bowdler Sharpe">Sharpe</a> in 1873, is also known as the Mediterranean Peregrine Falcon or the Maltese Falcon.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> It includes <i>caucasicus</i> and most specimens of the proposed race <i>punicus</i>, though others may be <i>pelegrinoides</i>, Barbary Falcons (see also below), or perhaps the rare hybrids between these two which might occur around <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>. They occur from the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> around the Mediterranean, except in <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> regions, to the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>. They are non-migratory. It is smaller than the nominate subspecies, and the underside usually has rusty hue.<sup id="cite_ref-Lees_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lees-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Males weigh around 445 grams (0.98&#160;lb), while females weigh up to 920 grams (2.0&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-bwp_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Falco peregrinus calidus</b>,</i> described by <a href="/wiki/John_Latham_(ornithologist)" title="John Latham (ornithologist)">John Latham</a> in 1790, was formerly called <i>leucogenys</i> and includes <i>caeruleiceps</i>. It breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> tundra of Eurasia, from <a href="/wiki/Murmansk_Oblast" title="Murmansk Oblast">Murmansk Oblast</a> to roughly <a href="/wiki/Yana_River" title="Yana River">Yana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indigirka_River" title="Indigirka River">Indigirka Rivers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. It is completely migratory, and travels south in winter as far as <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a>. It is often seen around wetland habitats.<sup id="cite_ref-pcr_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcr-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> It is paler than <i>peregrinus</i>, especially on the crown. Males weigh 588 to 740 grams (1.30–1.6 lb), while females weigh 925 to 1,333 grams (2.04–2.94 lb).<sup id="cite_ref-bwp_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Falco peregrinus cassini</b>,</i> described by Sharpe in 1873, is also known as the Austral Peregrine Falcon. It includes <i>kreyenborgi</i>, the Pallid Falcon<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Leucistic" title="Leucistic" class="mw-redirect">leucistic</a> <a href="/wiki/Morph_(zoology)" title="Morph (zoology)" class="mw-redirect">morph</a> occurring in southernmost South America, which was long believed to be a distinct species.<sup id="cite_ref-Ellis83_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ellis83-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> Its range includes South America from <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a> through <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> to <a href="/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego" title="Tierra del Fuego">Tierra del Fuego</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lees_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lees-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> It is non-migratory. It is similar to nominate, but slightly smaller with a black ear region. The variation <i>kreyenborgi</i> is medium grey above, has little barring below, and has a head pattern like the <a href="/wiki/Saker_Falcon" title="Saker Falcon">Saker Falcon</a>, but the ear region is white.<sup id="cite_ref-Ellis83_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ellis83-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Falco peregrinus ernesti</b>,</i> described by Sharpe in 1894, is found from <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> and south to <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a> and the nearby <a href="/wiki/Bismarck_Archipelago" title="Bismarck Archipelago">Bismarck Archipelago</a>. Its geographical separation from <i>nesiotes</i> requires confirmation. It is non-migratory. It differs from the nominate subspecies in the very dark, dense barring on its underside and its black ear coverts.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Falco peregrinus furuitii</b>,</i> described by Momiyama in 1927, is found on the <a href="/wiki/Izu_Islands" title="Izu Islands">Izu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ogasawara_Islands" title="Ogasawara Islands" class="mw-redirect">Ogasawara Islands</a> south of <a href="/wiki/Honsh%C5%AB" title="Honshū" class="mw-redirect">Honshū</a>, Japan. It is non-migratory. It is very rare, and may only remain on a single island.<sup id="cite_ref-White94_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> It is a dark form, resembling <i>pealei</i> in colour, but darker, especially on tail.<sup id="cite_ref-Lees_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lees-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Falco peregrinus japonensis</b>,</i> described by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Gmelin" title="Johann Friedrich Gmelin">Gmelin</a> in 1788, includes <i>kleinschmidti</i>, <i>pleskei</i>, and <i>harterti,</i> and seems to refer to intergrades with <i>calidus</i>. It is found from northeast <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kamchatka" title="Kamchatka" class="mw-redirect">Kamchatka</a> (though it is possibly replaced by <i>pealei</i> on the coast there) and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. Northern populations are migratory, while those of Japan are resident. It is similar to <i>peregrinus</i>, but the young are even darker than those of <i>anatum</i>.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li><i><b>Falco peregrinus macropus</b>,</i> described by <a href="/wiki/William_John_Swainson" title="William John Swainson">Swainson</a> in 1837, is the Australian Peregrine Falcon. It is found in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> in all regions except the southwest. It is non-migratory. It is similar to <i>brookei</i> in appearance, but is slightly smaller and the ear region is entirely black. The feet are proportionally large.<sup id="cite_ref-Lees_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lees-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Falco peregrinus madens</b>,</i> described by <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Dillon_Ripley" title="Sidney Dillon Ripley">Ripley</a> and Watson in 1963, is unusual in having some <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dichromatism" title="Sexual dichromatism" class="mw-redirect">sexual dichromatism</a>. If the Barbary Falcon (see below) is considered a distinct species, it is sometimes placed therein. It is found in the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_Islands" title="Cape Verde Islands" class="mw-redirect">Cape Verde Islands</a>, and is non-migratory;<sup id="cite_ref-Lees_14-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lees-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> it is endangered with only six to eight pairs surviving.<sup id="cite_ref-White94_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Males have a rufous wash on crown, nape, ears, and back; underside conspicuously washed pinkish-brown. Females are tinged rich brown overall, especially on the crown and nape.<sup id="cite_ref-Lees_14-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lees-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li><i><b>Falco peregrinus minor</b>,</i> first described by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lucien_Jules_Laurent_Bonaparte" title="Charles Lucien Jules Laurent Bonaparte" class="mw-redirect">Bonaparte</a> in 1850. It was formerly often <i>perconfusus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> It is sparsely and patchily distributed throughout much of <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a> and widespread in <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">Southern Africa</a>. It apparently reaches north along the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic" class="mw-redirect">Atlantic</a> coast as far as <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>. It is non-migratory, crow-sized, and dark coloured.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Falco peregrinus nesiotes</b>,</i> described by <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Mayr" title="Ernst Mayr">Mayr</a> in 1941,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> is found in <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a> and probably also <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a>. It is non-migratory.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li><i><b>Falco peregrinus pealei</b>,</i> described by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Ridgway" title="Robert Ridgway">Ridgway</a> in 1873, is also known as <a href="/wiki/Peale%27s_Falcon" title="Peale's Falcon">Peale's Falcon</a>, and includes <i>rudolfi</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-AOU165_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOU165-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> It is found in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a> of North America, northwards from the <a href="/wiki/Puget_Sound" title="Puget Sound">Puget Sound</a> along the <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> coast (including the <a href="/wiki/Queen_Charlotte_Islands" title="Queen Charlotte Islands" class="mw-redirect">Queen Charlotte Islands</a>), along the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Alaska" title="Gulf of Alaska">Gulf of Alaska</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Islands" title="Aleutian Islands">Aleutian Islands</a> to the far eastern <a href="/wiki/Bering_Sea" title="Bering Sea">Bering Sea</a> coast of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-AOU165_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOU165-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> and may also occur on the <a href="/wiki/Kuril_Islands" title="Kuril Islands">Kuril Islands</a> and the coasts of <a href="/wiki/Kamchatka" title="Kamchatka" class="mw-redirect">Kamchatka</a>. It is non-migratory. It is the largest subspecies, and it looks like an oversized and darker <i>tundrius</i> or like a strongly barred and large <i>anatum</i>. The bill is very wide.<sup id="cite_ref-Proctor_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Proctor-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Juveniles occasionally have pale crowns. Males weigh 700 to 1,000 grams (1.5–2.2 lb), while females weigh 1,000 to 1,500 grams (2.2–3.3 lb).<sup id="cite_ref-Whiteetal2002_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whiteetal2002-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Falco peregrinus pelegrinoides</b>,</i> first described by <a href="/wiki/Coenraad_Jacob_Temminck" title="Coenraad Jacob Temminck">Temminck</a> in 1829, is found in the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> through north Africa and the <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">Near East</a> to <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>. It is most similar to <i>brookei</i>, but is markedly paler above, with a rusty neck, and is a light buff with reduced barring below. It is smaller than the nominate subspecies; females weigh around 610 grams (1.3&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-bwp_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i><b><a href="/wiki/Falco_peregrinus_peregrinator" title="Falco peregrinus peregrinator" class="mw-redirect">Falco peregrinus peregrinator</a></b>,</i> described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Jakob_Sundevall" title="Carl Jakob Sundevall">Sundevall</a> in 1837, is known as the Indian Peregrine Falcon, Black Shaheen, Indian Shaheen <sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Shaheen_Falcon" title="Shaheen Falcon">Shaheen Falcon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-A_Photographic_Guide_to_Birds_of_Sri_Lanka_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A_Photographic_Guide_to_Birds_of_Sri_Lanka-38"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> It was formerly sometimes known as <i>Falco atriceps</i> or <i>Falco shaheen</i>. Its range includes <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> from <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> across <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> and Southeastern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. In India, the Shaheen is reported from all states except <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">Uttar Pradesh</a>, mainly from rocky and hilly regions. The Shaheen is also reported from the <a href="/wiki/Andaman_and_Nicobar_Islands" title="Andaman and Nicobar Islands">Andaman and Nicobar Islands</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pande_et_al_2007_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pande_et_al_2007-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> It has a clutch size of 3 to 4 eggs, with the chicks fledging time of 48 days with an average nesting success of 1.32 chicks per nest. In India, apart from nesting on cliffs, it has also been recorded as nesting on man-made structures such as buildings and cellphone transmission towers.<sup id="cite_ref-Pande_et_al_2007_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pande_et_al_2007-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> A population estimate of 40 breeding pairs in Sri Lanka was made in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> It is non-migratory, and is small and dark, with rufous underparts. In <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> this species is found to favour the higher hills while the migrant <i>calidus</i> is more often seen along the coast.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Falco peregrinus peregrinus</b>,</i> the <a href="/wiki/Nominate" title="Nominate" class="mw-redirect">nominate</a> (first-named) subspecies, described by <a href="/wiki/Marmaduke_Tunstall" title="Marmaduke Tunstall">Tunstall</a> in 1771, breeds over much of temperate <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> in the north and the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a> region and <a href="/wiki/Alpide_belt" title="Alpide belt">Alpide belt</a> in the south.<sup id="cite_ref-AOU164_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOU164-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> It is mainly non-migratory in Europe, but <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> in Scandinavia and Asia. Males weigh 580 to 750 grams (1.3–1.7 lb), while females weigh 925 to 1,300 grams (2.04–2.9 lb).<sup id="cite_ref-bwp_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> It includes <i>brevirostris</i>, <i>germanicus</i>, <i>rhenanus</i>, and <i>riphaeus</i>.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Falco peregrinus radama</b>,</i> described by <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Hartlaub" title="Gustav Hartlaub">Hartlaub</a> in 1861, is found in <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Comoros" title="Comoros">Comoros</a>. It is non-migratory.<sup id="cite_ref-Lees_14-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lees-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Falco peregrinus submelanogenys</b>,</i> described by <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Mathews" title="Gregory Mathews">Mathews</a> in 1912, is the Southwest Australian Peregrine Falcon. It is found in southwest Australia and is non-migratory.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Falco peregrinus tundrius</b>,</i> described by C.M. White in 1968, was at one time included in <i>leucogenys</i> It is found in the Arctic tundra of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> to <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, and migrates to wintering grounds in <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Proctor_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Proctor-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Most vagrants that reach western Europe belong to this subspecies, which was previously united with <i>anatum</i>. It is the New World equivalent to <i>calidus</i>. It is smaller than <i>anatum</i>. It is also paler than <i>anatum</i>; most have a conspicuous white forehead and white in ear region, but the crown and "moustache" are very dark, unlike in <i>calidus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Proctor_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Proctor-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Juveniles are browner, and less grey, than in <i>calidus</i>, and paler, sometimes almost sandy, than in <i>anatum</i>. Males weigh 500 to 700 grams (1.1–1.5 lb), while females weigh 800 to 1,100 grams (1.8–2.4 lb).<sup id="cite_ref-Whiteetal2002_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whiteetal2002-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Peregrine_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Barbary Falcon">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Barbary_Falcon">Barbary Falcon</span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Barbary_Falcon" title="Barbary Falcon">Barbary Falcon</a></div>
<p>Two of the subspecies listed above (<i>Falco peregrinus pelegrinoides</i> and <i>F. p. babylonicus)</i> are often instead treated together as a distinct <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, <i>Falco pelegrinoides</i> (<a href="/wiki/Barbary_Falcon" title="Barbary Falcon">Barbary Falcon</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-bwp_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> although they were included within <i>F. peregrinus</i> in the 1994 <i><a href="/wiki/Handbook_of_the_Birds_of_the_World" title="Handbook of the Birds of the World">Handbook of the Birds of the World</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-White94_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> These birds inhabit <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> regions from the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> along the rim of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> to <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>.</p>
<p>Barbary Falcons have a red neck patch but otherwise differ in appearance from the Peregrine proper merely according to <a href="/wiki/Gloger%27s_Rule" title="Gloger's Rule" class="mw-redirect">Gloger's Rule</a>, relating <a href="/wiki/Pigmentation" title="Pigmentation" class="mw-redirect">pigmentation</a> to <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">environmental humidity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> The Barbary Falcon has a peculiar way of flying, beating only the outer part of its wings like <a href="/wiki/Fulmar" title="Fulmar">fulmars</a> sometimes do; this also occurs in the Peregrine, but less often and far less pronounced.<sup id="cite_ref-bwp_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The Barbary Falcon's <a href="/wiki/Shoulder" title="Shoulder">shoulder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pelvis" title="Pelvis">pelvis</a> bones are stout by comparison with the Peregrine, and its feet are smaller.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Barbary Falcons breed at different times of year than neighboring Peregrine Falcon subspecies,<sup id="cite_ref-bwp_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Vaurie_.281961.29_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vaurie_.281961.29-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wink_.27.27et_al..27.27_.282000.29_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wink_.27.27et_al..27.27_.282000.29-43"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> but there are no postzygotic reproduction barriers in place.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> There is a 0.6–0.7%&#160;genetic distance in the Peregine-Barbary Falcon ("peregrinoid") complex.<sup id="cite_ref-Wink_.27.27et_al..27.27_.282000.29_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wink_.27.27et_al..27.27_.282000.29-43"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Another subspecies of <i>Falco peregrinus, madens,</i> has also sometimes been treated instead within a separately recognized <i>F. pelegrinoides.</i><sup id="cite_ref-Lees_14-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lees-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>The Peregrine Falcon lives mostly along <a href="/wiki/Mountain_range" title="Mountain range">mountain ranges</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_valley" title="River valley" class="mw-redirect">river valleys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coastline" title="Coastline" class="mw-redirect">coastlines</a>, and increasingly in <a href="/wiki/Cities" title="Cities" class="mw-redirect">cities</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lees_14-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lees-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> In mild-winter regions, it is usually a permanent resident, and some individuals, especially adult males, will remain on the breeding territory. Only populations that breed in Arctic <a href="/wiki/Clime" title="Clime">climes</a> typically migrate great distances during the northern winter.<sup id="cite_ref-DOI95_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOI95-49"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Peregrine Falcon reaches faster speeds than any other animal on the planet when performing the stoop,<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> which involves soaring to a great height and then diving steeply at speeds of over 320&#160;km/h (200&#160;mph), hitting one wing of its prey so as not to harm itself on impact.<sup id="cite_ref-F.26WL_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-F.26WL-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The air pressure from a 200&#160;mph (320&#160;km/h) dive could possibly damage a bird's <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a>, but small bony tubercles on a falcon's nostrils guide the powerful airflow away from the nostrils, enabling the bird to breathe more easily while diving by reducing the change in air pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-Wisconsin_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wisconsin-50"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> To protect their eyes, the falcons use their <a href="/wiki/Nictitating_membrane" title="Nictitating membrane">nictitating membranes</a> (third eyelids) to spread tears and clear debris from their eyes while maintaining vision. A study testing the flight physics of an "ideal falcon" found a theoretical speed limit at 400&#160;km/h (250&#160;mph) for low altitude flight and 625&#160;km/h (390&#160;mph) for high altitude flight.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2005, Ken Franklin recorded a falcon stooping at a top speed of 389&#160;km/h (242&#160;mph).<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> A video of one of the dives can be seen in <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.extremescience.com/zoom/index.php/videos/180-peregrine-falcon-video">this link</a>.</p>
<p>The life span of Peregrine Falcons in the wild is up to 15.5&#160;years.<sup id="cite_ref-bwp_4-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Mortality in the first year is 59–70%, declining to 25–32% annually in adults.<sup id="cite_ref-bwp_4-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Apart from such <a href="/wiki/Anthropogenic" title="Anthropogenic" class="mw-redirect">anthropogenic</a> threats as collision with human-made objects, the Peregrine may be killed by <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a> or large <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owls</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Michigan_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Michigan-53"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Peregrine Falcon is <a href="/wiki/Host_(biology)" title="Host (biology)">host</a> to a range of <a href="/wiki/Parasitism" title="Parasitism">parasites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Vector_(epidemiology)" title="Vector (epidemiology)">vector</a> for <a href="/wiki/Avipoxvirus" title="Avipoxvirus">Avipoxvirus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_disease_virus" title="Newcastle disease virus" class="mw-redirect">Newcastle disease virus</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Falconid_herpesvirus_1&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Falconid herpesvirus 1 (page does not exist)">Falconid herpesvirus 1</a> (and possibly other <a href="/wiki/Herpesviridae" title="Herpesviridae">Herpesviridae</a>), and some <a href="/wiki/Mycoses" title="Mycoses" class="mw-redirect">mycoses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bacterial_infection" title="Bacterial infection" class="mw-redirect">bacterial infections</a>. <a href="/wiki/Endoparasite" title="Endoparasite" class="mw-redirect">Endoparasites</a> include <i><a href="/wiki/Plasmodium_relictum" title="Plasmodium relictum" class="mw-redirect">Plasmodium relictum</a></i> (usually not causing <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> in the Peregrine Falcon), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Strigeidae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Strigeidae (page does not exist)">Strigeidae</a> <a href="/wiki/Trematode" title="Trematode" class="mw-redirect">trematodes</a>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serratospiculum_amaculata&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Serratospiculum amaculata (page does not exist)">Serratospiculum amaculata</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Nematode" title="Nematode">nematode</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Tapeworm" title="Tapeworm" class="mw-redirect">tapeworms</a>. Known Peregrine Falcon <a href="/wiki/Ectoparasite" title="Ectoparasite" class="mw-redirect">ectoparasites</a> are <a href="/wiki/Chewing_lice" title="Chewing lice" class="mw-redirect">chewing lice</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ceratophyllus_garei&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ceratophyllus garei (page does not exist)">Ceratophyllus garei</a></i> (a <a href="/wiki/Flea" title="Flea">flea</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Hippoboscidae" title="Hippoboscidae">Hippoboscidae</a> flies (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Icosta_nigra&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Icosta nigra (page does not exist)">Icosta nigra</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ornithoctona_erythrocephala&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ornithoctona erythrocephala (page does not exist)">Ornithoctona erythrocephala</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Peregrine_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Falco_peregrinus_on_ship_USFWS.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Falco_peregrinus_on_ship_USFWS.jpg/220px-Falco_peregrinus_on_ship_USFWS.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Falco_peregrinus_on_ship_USFWS.jpg/330px-Falco_peregrinus_on_ship_USFWS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Falco_peregrinus_on_ship_USFWS.jpg/440px-Falco_peregrinus_on_ship_USFWS.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Falco_peregrinus_on_ship_USFWS.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
An immature Peregrine eating its prey on the deck of a ship</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Peregrine Falcon feeds almost exclusively on medium-sized birds such as <a href="/wiki/Columbidae" title="Columbidae">pigeons and doves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waterfowl" title="Waterfowl">waterfowl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Songbird" title="Songbird">songbirds</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">waders</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Beckstead_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beckstead-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Worldwide, it is estimated that between 1,500 and 2,000 bird species (up to roughly a fifth of the world's bird species) are predated somewhere by these falcons. In <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, prey has varied in size from 3-g <a href="/wiki/Hummingbird" title="Hummingbird">hummingbirds</a> to a 3.1-kg <a href="/wiki/Sandhill_Crane" title="Sandhill Crane">Sandhill Crane</a> (killed by a peregrine in a swoop).<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> Smaller raptors are regularly predated, including smaller falcons such as the <a href="/wiki/American_Kestrel" title="American Kestrel">American Kestrel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> In urban areas, the main component of the Peregrine's diet is the <a href="/wiki/Rock_Pigeon" title="Rock Pigeon" class="mw-redirect">Rock</a> or <a href="/wiki/Feral_Pigeon" title="Feral Pigeon">Feral Pigeon</a>, which comprise 80% or more of the dietary intake for peregrines in some cities. Other common city birds are also taken regularly, including <a href="/wiki/Mourning_Dove" title="Mourning Dove">Mourning Doves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_Wood_Pigeon" title="Common Wood Pigeon">Common Wood Pigeons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_Swift" title="Common Swift">Common Swifts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Flicker" title="Northern Flicker">Northern Flickers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_Starling" title="Common Starling">Common Starlings</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Robin" title="American Robin">American Robins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_Blackbird" title="Common Blackbird">Common Blackbirds</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Corvid" title="Corvid" class="mw-redirect">corvids</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Magpie" title="Magpie">magpies</a> or <a href="/wiki/Carrion_Crow" title="Carrion Crow">Carrion</a>, <a href="/wiki/House_Crow" title="House Crow">House</a>, and <a href="/wiki/American_Crow" title="American Crow">American Crows</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-BB101_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB101-58"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> Other than bats taken at night,<sup id="cite_ref-BB101_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB101-58"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> the Peregrine rarely hunts mammals, but will on occasion take small species such as <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vole" title="Vole">voles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hare" title="Hare">hares</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shrew" title="Shrew">shrews</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">mice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Squirrel" title="Squirrel">squirrels</a>. Coastal populations of the large subspecies <i>pealei</i> feed almost exclusively on <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Terres91_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Terres91-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> In the Brazilian <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangrove</a> swamp of <a href="/wiki/Cubat%C3%A3o" title="Cubatão">Cubatão</a>, a wintering falcon of the subspecies <i>tundrius</i> was observed while successfully hunting a juvenile <a href="/wiki/Scarlet_Ibis" title="Scarlet Ibis">Scarlet Ibis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> Insects and reptiles make up a small proportion of the diet, which varies greatly depending on what prey is available.<sup id="cite_ref-Beckstead_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beckstead-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Peregrine Falcon hunts at dawn and dusk, when prey are most active, but also nocturnally in cities, particularly during migration periods when hunting at night may become prevalent. Nocturnal migrants taken by Peregrines include species as diverse as <a href="/wiki/Yellow-billed_Cuckoo" title="Yellow-billed Cuckoo">Yellow-billed Cuckoo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black-necked_Grebe" title="Black-necked Grebe">Black-necked Grebe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Rail" title="Virginia Rail">Virginia Rail</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Common_Quail" title="Common Quail">Common Quail</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BB101_58-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BB101-58"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> The Peregrine requires open space in order to hunt, and therefore often hunts over open water, <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Valley" title="Valley">valleys</a>, fields, and <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>, searching for prey either from a high perch or from the air.<sup id="cite_ref-Ehrlich92_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrlich92-60"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> Large congregations of migrants, especially species that gather in the open like <a href="/wiki/Shorebird" title="Shorebird" class="mw-redirect">shorebirds</a>, can be quite attractive to hunting Peregrines. Once prey is spotted, it begins its stoop, folding back the tail and wings, with feet tucked.<sup id="cite_ref-Terres91_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Terres91-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Prey is struck and captured in mid-air; the Peregrine Falcon strikes its prey with a clenched foot, stunning or killing it with the impact, then turns to catch it in mid-air.<sup id="cite_ref-Ehrlich92_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrlich92-60"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> If its prey is too heavy to carry, a Peregrine will drop it to the ground and eat it there. Prey is plucked before consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-Wisconsin_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wisconsin-50"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Peregrine_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Faucon_pelerin_7_mai.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Faucon_pelerin_7_mai.jpg/220px-Faucon_pelerin_7_mai.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Faucon_pelerin_7_mai.jpg/330px-Faucon_pelerin_7_mai.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Faucon_pelerin_7_mai.jpg/440px-Faucon_pelerin_7_mai.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Faucon_pelerin_7_mai.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
At nest, France</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Peregrine Falcon is sexually mature at one to three years of age, but in healthy populations they breed after two to three years of age. A pair mates for life and returns to the same nesting spot annually. The courtship flight includes a mix of aerial acrobatics, precise spirals, and steep dives.<sup id="cite_ref-Dewey_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dewey-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The male passes prey it has caught to the female in mid-air. To make this possible, the female actually flies upside-down to receive the food from the male's talons.</p>
<p>During the breeding season, the Peregrine Falcon is territorial; nesting pairs are usually more than 1&#160;km (0.62&#160;mi) apart, and often much farther, even in areas with large numbers of pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-Blood01_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blood01-61"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> The distance between nests ensures sufficient food supply for pairs and their chicks. Within a breeding territory, a pair may have several nesting ledges; the number used by a pair can vary from one or two to seven in a 16&#160;year period.</p>
<p>The Peregrine Falcon nests in a scrape, normally on cliff edges. The female chooses a nest site, where she scrapes a shallow hollow in the loose soil, sand, gravel, or dead vegetation in which to lay eggs. No nest materials are added.<sup id="cite_ref-Dewey_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dewey-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Cliff nests are generally located under an overhang, on ledges with vegetation, and south-facing sites are favored.<sup id="cite_ref-Terres91_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Terres91-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> In some regions, as in parts of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and on the west coast of Northern North America, large tree hollows are used for nesting. Before the demise of most European peregrines, a large population of peregrines in central and western Europe used the disused nests of other large birds.<sup id="cite_ref-Beckstead_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beckstead-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> In remote, undisturbed areas such as the Arctic, steep slopes and even low rocks and mounds may be used as nest sites. In many parts of its range, Peregrines now also nest regularly on tall buildings or bridges; these human-made structures used for breeding closely resemble the natural cliff ledges that the Peregrine prefers for its nesting locations.<sup id="cite_ref-White94_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Blood01_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blood01-61"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The pair defends the chosen nest site against other Peregrines, and often against <a href="/wiki/Raven" title="Raven">ravens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">herons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a>, and if ground-nesting, also such mammals as <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">foxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wolverine" title="Wolverine">wolverines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Felid" title="Felid" class="mw-redirect">felids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bear" title="Bear">bears</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gray_wolf" title="Gray wolf">wolves</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Blood01_61-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blood01-61"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> Both nests and (less frequently) adults are predated by larger-bodied raptorial birds like <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a>, large <a href="/wiki/Horned_owl" title="Horned owl">owls</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Gyrfalcon" title="Gyrfalcon">Gyrfalcons</a>. Peregrines defending their nests have managed to kill raptors as large as <a href="/wiki/Golden_Eagle" title="Golden Eagle">Golden Eagles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bald_Eagle" title="Bald Eagle">Bald Eagles</a> (both of which they normally avoid as potential predators) that have come too close to the nest.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The date of egg-laying varies according to locality, but is generally from February to March in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>, and from July to August in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>, although the Australian subspecies <i>macropus</i> may breed as late as November, and <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equatorial</a> populations may nest anytime between June and December. If the eggs are lost early in the nesting season, the female usually lays another clutch, although this is extremely rare in the Arctic due to the short summer season. Generally three to four eggs, but sometimes as few as one or as many as five, are laid in the scrape.<sup id="cite_ref-Peterson_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peterson-63"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> The eggs are white to buff with red or brown markings.<sup id="cite_ref-Peterson_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peterson-63"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> They are incubated for 29 to 33 days, mainly by the female,<sup id="cite_ref-Terres91_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Terres91-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> with the male also helping with the incubation of the eggs during the day, but only the female incubating them at night. The average number of young found in nests is 2.5, and the average number that fledge is about 1.5, due to the occasional production of infertile eggs and various natural losses of nestlings.<sup id="cite_ref-White94_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wisconsin_50-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wisconsin-50"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Michigan_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Michigan-53"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After hatching, the chicks (called "eyases"<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup>) are covered with creamy-white down and have disproportionately large feet.<sup id="cite_ref-Blood01_61-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blood01-61"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> The male (called the "tiercel") and the female (simply called the "falcon") both leave the nest to gather prey to feed the young.<sup id="cite_ref-Wisconsin_50-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wisconsin-50"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> The hunting territory of the parents can extend a radius of 19 to 24&#160;km (12–15 miles) from the nest site.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> Chicks <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> 42 to 46 days after hatching, and remain dependent on their parents for up to two months.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_.281994.29_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow_.281994.29-66"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Peregrine_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">Relationship with humans</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Peregrine_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Falconry">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Falconry">Falconry</span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Falconry" title="Falconry">Falconry</a></div>
<p>The Peregrine Falcon has been used in <a href="/wiki/Falconry" title="Falconry">falconry</a> for more than 3,000 years, beginning with nomads in central Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-Blood01_61-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blood01-61"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> Due to its ability to dive at high speeds, it is highly sought-after and generally used by experienced falconers.<sup id="cite_ref-Scholz_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scholz-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Peregrine Falcons are also occasionally used to scare away birds at airports to reduce the risk of <a href="/wiki/Bird_strike" title="Bird strike">bird-plane strikes</a>, improving air-traffic safety,<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> and were used to intercept homing pigeons during World War II.<sup id="cite_ref-Enderson_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Enderson-68"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Peregrine Falcons have been successfully bred in captivity, both for falconry and for release back into the wild.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> Until 2004 nearly all Peregrines used for falconry in the US were captive-bred from the progeny of falcons taken before the US <a href="/wiki/Endangered_Species_Act" title="Endangered Species Act">Endangered Species Act</a> was enacted and from those few infusions of wild genes available from Canada and special circumstances. Peregrine Falcons were removed from the United States' endangered species list in 1999. The successful recovery program was aided by the effort and knowledge of falconers – in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/The_Peregrine_Fund" title="The Peregrine Fund">The Peregrine Fund</a> and state and federal agencies – through a technique called <a href="/wiki/Hack_(falconry)" title="Hack (falconry)">hacking</a>. Finally, after years of close work with the US Fish and Wildlife Service, a limited take of wild Peregrines was allowed in 2004, the first wild Peregrines taken specifically for falconry in over 30 years. Since Peregrine eggs and chicks are still often targeted by illegal collectors,<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> it is common practice not to publicize unprotected nest locations.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Peregrine_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Decline due to pesticides">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Decline_due_to_pesticides">Decline due to pesticides</span></h3>
<p>The Peregrine Falcon became an endangered species because of the use of <a href="/wiki/Organochlorine_pesticide" title="Organochlorine pesticide" class="mw-redirect">organochlorine pesticides</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a>, during the 1950s, 60s, and 70s.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> Pesticide <a href="/wiki/Biomagnification" title="Biomagnification">biomagnification</a> caused <a href="/wiki/Organochlorine" title="Organochlorine" class="mw-redirect">organochlorine</a> to build up in the falcons' fat tissues, reducing the amount of calcium in their eggshells. With thinner shells, fewer falcon eggs survived to hatching.<sup id="cite_ref-Ehrlich92_60-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrlich92-60"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup> In several parts of the world, such as the eastern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, this species became <a href="/wiki/Local_extinction" title="Local extinction">extirpated</a> (locally extinct) as a result.<sup id="cite_ref-Snow_.281994.29_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snow_.281994.29-66"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> An alternate point of view is that populations in the eastern North America had vanished due to hunting and egg collection.<sup id="cite_ref-lehr2000_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lehr2000-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Peregrine_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Recovery efforts">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Recovery_efforts">Recovery efforts</span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, wildlife services in Peregrine Falcon recovery teams breed the species in captivity.<sup id="cite_ref-RDigest_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RDigest-74"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup> The chicks are usually fed through a chute or with a <a href="/wiki/Hand_puppet" title="Hand puppet">hand puppet</a> mimicking a Peregrine's head, so they cannot see to <a href="/wiki/Imprinting_(psychology)" title="Imprinting (psychology)">imprint</a> on the human trainers.<sup id="cite_ref-DOI95_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOI95-49"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> Then, when they are old enough, the rearing box is opened, allowing the bird to train its wings. As the fledgling gets stronger, feeding is reduced forcing the bird to learn to hunt. This procedure is called <a href="/wiki/Hack_(falconry)" title="Hack (falconry)">hacking back to the wild</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Aitken_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aitken-75"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup> To release a captive-bred falcon, the bird is placed in a special cage at the top of a tower or cliff ledge for some days or so, allowing it to acclimate itself to its future environment.<sup id="cite_ref-Aitken_75-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aitken-75"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Worldwide recovery efforts have been remarkably successful.<sup id="cite_ref-RDigest_74-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RDigest-74"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup> The widespread restriction of DDT use eventually allowed released birds to breed successfully.<sup id="cite_ref-DOI95_49-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOI95-49"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> The Peregrine Falcon was removed from the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> <a href="/wiki/Endangered_Species" title="Endangered Species" class="mw-redirect">Endangered Species</a> list on August 25, 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-DOI95_49-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOI95-49"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Some controversy has existed over the origins of captive breeding stock used by <a href="/wiki/The_Peregrine_Fund" title="The Peregrine Fund">The Peregrine Fund</a> in the recovery of peregrine falcons throughout the contiguous United States. Several peregrine subspecies were included in the breeding stock, including birds of Eurasian origin. Due to the <a href="/wiki/Local_extinction" title="Local extinction">extirpation</a> of the Eastern <i>anatum</i> (<i>Falco peregrinus anatum</i>), the near extirpation of the <i>anatum</i> in the Midwest, and the limited gene pool within North American breeding stock, the inclusion of non-native <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> was justified to optimize the <a href="/wiki/Genetic_diversity" title="Genetic diversity">genetic diversity</a> found within the species as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Peregrine_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: The Peregrine by J A Baker">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Peregrine_by_J_A_Baker"><i>The Peregrine</i> by J A Baker</span></h3>
<p>This book by <a href="/wiki/J._A._Baker" title="J. A. Baker">John Alec Baker</a> <sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup>was first published in 1967 and recounts in diary form his observations of peregrines (and their interaction with other birds) near his home in <a href="/wiki/Chelmsford" title="Chelmsford">Chelmsford</a>, Essex, England, over a single winter from October to April (probably the very cold winter of 1962/3). It is widely regarded as masterpiece of nature writing. <a href="/wiki/Mark_Cocker" title="Mark Cocker">Mark Cocker</a>, for example, regards the book as <i>one of the most outstanding books on nature in the twentieth century</i><sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup>. Cocker continues <i>There is an occasional metaphysical density to the language, but more often he (Baker) described the falcon's actions in passages of radiant lyricism that both express his own burning obession and restate all the qualities that make the bird such a totem species</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Peregrine_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Current status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Current_status">Current status</span></h3>
<p>Populations of the Peregrine Falcon have bounced back in most parts of the world. In <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a>, there has been a recovery of populations since the crash of the 1960s. This has been greatly assisted by conservation and protection work led by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Protection_of_Birds" title="Royal Society for the Protection of Birds">Royal Society for the Protection of Birds</a>. The RSPB has estimated that there are 1,402 breeding pairs in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup> Peregrines now breed in many mountainous and coastal areas, especially in the west and north, and nest in some urban areas, capitalising on the urban <a href="/wiki/Feral_Pigeon" title="Feral Pigeon">Feral Pigeon</a> populations for food.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup> In many parts of the world Peregrine Falcons have adapted to urban habitats, nesting on <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">cathedrals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skyscraper" title="Skyscraper">skyscraper</a> window ledges, tower blocks,<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup> and the towers of <a href="/wiki/Suspension_bridge" title="Suspension bridge">suspension bridges</a>. Many of these nesting birds are encouraged, sometimes gathering media attention and often monitored by cameras.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Peregrine_Falcon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cultural significance">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_significance">Cultural significance</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2007_ID_Proof_Rev.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/2007_ID_Proof_Rev.png/170px-2007_ID_Proof_Rev.png" width="170" height="170" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/2007_ID_Proof_Rev.png/255px-2007_ID_Proof_Rev.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/2007_ID_Proof_Rev.png/340px-2007_ID_Proof_Rev.png 2x" /></a>

<p>Due to its striking hunting technique, the Peregrine has often been associated with aggression and martial prowess. Native Americans of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippian_culture" title="Mississippian culture">Mississippian culture</a> (c. 800–1500) used the Peregrine, along with other several birds of prey, in imagery as a symbol of "aerial (celestial) power" and buried men of high status in costumes associating to the ferocity of "raptorial" birds.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87"><span>[</span>88<span>]</span></a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">late Middle Ages</a>, the Western European nobility that used Peregrines for hunting, considered the bird associated with <a href="/wiki/Prince" title="Prince">princes</a> in formal hierarchies of birds of prey, just below the <a href="/wiki/Gyrfalcon" title="Gyrfalcon">Gyrfalcon</a> associated with <a href="/wiki/King" title="King" class="mw-redirect">kings</a>. It was considered "a royal bird, more armed by its courage than its claws". Terminology used by Peregrine breeders also used the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> term <i>gentil</i>, "of noble birth; aristocratic", particularly with the Peregrine.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88"><span>[</span>89<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Peregrine Falcon is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_national_animals" title="List of national animals">national animal</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>. Since 1927, the Peregrine Falcon has been the official mascot of <a href="/wiki/Bowling_Green_State_University" title="Bowling Green State University">Bowling Green State University</a> in Bowling Green, Ohio.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89"><span>[</span>90<span>]</span></a></sup> The 2007 U.S. <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a> <a href="/wiki/50_State_Quarters" title="50 State Quarters">state quarter</a> features a Peregrine Falcon.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90"><span>[</span>91<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
