<p>The <b>Alpine Chough</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span title="pronunciation:"><img alt="play" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" /></span>&#160;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/</a></span><span class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ˈ</span></a></span><span class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="/tʃ/ 'ch' in 'china'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">tʃ</span></a></span><span class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="/ʌ/ short 'u' in 'bud'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ʌ</span></a></span><span class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="'f' in 'find'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">f</span></a></span><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/</a></span></span>), or <b>Yellow-billed Chough</b>, (<i>Pyrrhocorax graculus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crow</a> family, one of only two species in the genus <i>Pyrrhocorax</i>. Its two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> breed in high mountains from <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> east through southern Europe and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> to <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, India and China, and it may nest at a higher altitude than any other bird. The eggs have adaptations to the thin atmosphere that improve oxygen take-up and reduce water loss.</p>
<p>This bird has glossy black <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>, a yellow bill, red legs, and distinctive calls. It has a buoyant acrobatic flight with widely spread <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">flight feathers</a>. The Alpine Chough <a href="/wiki/Pair_bond" title="Pair bond">pairs</a> for life and displays <a href="/wiki/Philopatry" title="Philopatry">fidelity</a> to its breeding site, which is usually a cave or crevice in a cliff face. It builds a lined stick nest and lays three to five brown-blotched whitish eggs. It feeds, usually in flocks, on short grazed grassland, taking mainly <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrate</a> prey in summer and fruit in winter; it will readily approach tourist sites to find supplementary food.</p>
<p>Although it is subject to predation and parasitism, and changes in agricultural practices have caused local population declines, this widespread and abundant species is not threatened globally. Climate change may present a long-term threat, by shifting the necessary alpine habitat to higher altitudes.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpine_Chough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The Alpine Chough was first described as <i>Corvus graculus</i> by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> in 1766.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> It was moved to its current genus, <i>Pyrrhocorax</i>, by English <a href="/wiki/Ornithology" title="Ornithology">ornithologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Marmaduke_Tunstall" title="Marmaduke Tunstall">Marmaduke Tunstall</a> in his 1771 <i>Ornithologia Britannica</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> along with the only other member of the genus, the <a href="/wiki/Red-billed_Chough" title="Red-billed Chough">Red-billed Chough</a>, <i>P.&#160;pyrrhocorax</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The closest relatives of the choughs were formerly thought to be the typical <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crows</a>, <i>Corvus</i>, especially the jackdaws in the subgenus <i><a href="/wiki/Coloeus" title="Coloeus">Coloeus</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-goodwin_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goodwin-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> but <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome&#160;b</a> analysis shows that the genus <i>Pyrrhocorax</i>, along with the <a href="/wiki/Ratchet-tailed_Treepie" title="Ratchet-tailed Treepie">Ratchet-tailed Treepie</a> (genus <i>Temnurus</i>), diverged early from the rest of the <a href="/wiki/Corvidae" title="Corvidae">Corvidae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ericson_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ericson-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The genus name is derived from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>πύρρος (purrhos)</i>, "flame-coloured", and <i>κόραξ (korax)</i>, "raven".<sup id="cite_ref-BTO_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The species epithet <i>graculus</i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for a jackdaw.<sup id="cite_ref-woodhouse_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-woodhouse-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The current binomial name of the Alpine Chough was formerly sometimes applied to the Red-billed Chough.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The English word "chough" was originally an alternative <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeic</a> name for the <a href="/wiki/Jackdaw" title="Jackdaw" class="mw-redirect">Jackdaw</a>, <i>Corvus monedula</i>, based on its call. The Red-billed Chough, formerly particularly common in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> and known initially as the "Cornish Chough", eventually became just "Chough", the name transferring from one genus to another.<sup id="cite_ref-Cocker_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cocker-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Alpine Chough has two extant subspecies.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>P. g. graculus</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Subspecies#Nomenclature" title="Subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> in Europe, north Africa, Turkey, the Caucusus and northern Iran.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><i>P. g. digitatus</i>, described by the German naturalists <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Hemprich" title="Wilhelm Hemprich">Wilhelm Hemprich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_Gottfried_Ehrenberg" title="Christian Gottfried Ehrenberg">Christian Gottfried Ehrenberg</a> as <i>P.&#160;alpinus</i> var. <i>digitatus</i> in 1833,<sup id="cite_ref-dickinson_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dickinson-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> is larger and has stronger feet than the nominate race.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> It breeds in the rest of the depicted Asian range, mainly in the Himalayas.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravian</a> <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">palaeontologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Stoliczka" title="Ferdinand Stoliczka">Ferdinand Stoliczka</a> separated the Himalayan population as a third subspecies, <i>P.&#160;g.&#160;forsythi</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-stoliczka_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stoliczka-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> but this has not been widely accepted and is usually treated as <a href="/wiki/Synonym" title="Synonym">synonymous</a> with <i>digitatus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> form from Europe was similar to the extant subspecies, and is sometimes categorised as <i>P.&#160;g.&#160;vetus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> <a href="/wiki/White-winged_Chough" title="White-winged Chough">White-winged Chough</a>, <i>Corcorax melanorhamphos</i>, despite its similar bill shape and black plumage, is only distantly related to the true choughs.<sup id="cite_ref-ITIS2_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITIS2-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpine_Chough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/w/index.php?title=File:ChoughsDiff.svg&amp;page=1" class="image"><img alt=" Diagrams of the two chough species perched and in flight" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/ChoughsDiff.svg/170px-ChoughsDiff.svg.png" width="170" height="204" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ChoughsDiff.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Red-billed Chough (left) has deeper wing "fingers" and tail wedge in flight than Alpine (on right), and its wings extend to or beyond the tail tip when standing.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The adult of the nominate subspecies of the Alpine Chough has glossy black <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>, a short yellow bill, dark brown <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">irises</a>, and red legs.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> It is slightly smaller than Red-billed Chough, at 37–39&#160;centimetres (14.6–15.3&#160;in) length with a 12–14 cm (4.7–5.5 in) tail and a 75–85 cm (30–33 in) wingspan, but has a proportionally longer tail and shorter wings than its relative. It has a similar buoyant and easy flight.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The sexes are identical in appearance although the male averages slightly larger than the female. The juvenile is duller than the adult with a dull yellow bill and brownish legs.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The Alpine Chough is unlikely to be confused with any other species; although the Jackdaw and Red-billed Chough share its range, the Jackdaw is smaller and has unglossed grey plumage, and the Red-billed Chough has a long red bill.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The subspecies <i>P.&#160;g.&#160;digitatus</i> averages slightly larger than the nominate form, weighing 191–244&#160;grams (6.8–8.7&#160;oz) against 188–252&#160;g (6.7–9.0&#160;oz) for <i>P.&#160;g.&#160;graculus</i>, and it has stronger feet.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BWP_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> This is in accordance with <a href="/wiki/Bergmann%27s_rule" title="Bergmann's rule">Bergmann's rule</a>, which predicts that the largest birds should be found higher elevations or in colder and more arid regions. The extremities of the body, the bill and <a href="/wiki/Tarsus_(skeleton)" title="Tarsus (skeleton)">tarsus</a>, were longer in warmer areas, in line with <a href="/wiki/Allen%27s_rule" title="Allen's rule">Allen's rule</a>. Temperature seemed to be the most important cause of body variation in the Alpine Chough.<sup id="cite_ref-Ecogeographic_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ecogeographic-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The flight of the Alpine Chough is swift and acrobatic with loose deep wing beats. Its high manoeuvrability is accomplished by fanning the tail, folding its wings, and soaring in the <a href="/wiki/Vertical_draft" title="Vertical draft">updraughts</a> at cliff faces. Even in flight, it can be distinguished from Red-billed Chough by its less rectangular wings, and longer, less square-ended tail.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-burton_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-burton-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Trill_(music)" title="Trill (music)">rippling</a> <i>preep</i> and whistled <i>sweeeooo</i> calls of the Alpine Chough are quite different from the more typically <a href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">crow-like</a> <i>chee-ow</i> vocalisations of the Jackdaw and the Red-billed Chough. It also has a rolling <i>churr</i> <a href="/wiki/Alarm_signal" title="Alarm signal">alarm call</a>, and a variety of quiet warbles and squeaks given by resting or feeding birds.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> In a study of chough calls throughout the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic_ecozone" title="Palearctic ecozone">Palearctic region</a> it was found that call <a href="/wiki/Audio_frequency" title="Audio frequency">frequencies</a> in the Alpine Chough showed an <a href="/wiki/Inverse_relationship" title="Inverse relationship">inverse relationship</a> between body size and frequency, being higher-pitched in smaller-bodied populations.<sup id="cite_ref-Laiolo2001_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laiolo2001-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpine_Chough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Picos393.JPG" class="image"><img alt=" high grey bare cliffs behind a meadow" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Picos393.JPG/220px-Picos393.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Picos393.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Cliffs with nearby alpine pasture provide nesting sites.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Alpine Chough breeds in mountains from Spain eastwards through southern Europe and the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> across <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> to western China. There are also populations in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">non-migratory resident</a> throughout its range, although Moroccan birds have established a small colony near <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga" title="Málaga">Málaga</a> in southern Spain, and wanderers have reached <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This is a high-altitude species normally breeding between 1,260–2,880 metres (4,130–9,450 ft) in Europe, 2,880–3,900 m (9,450–12,800 ft) in Morocco, and 3,500–5,000 m (11,500–16,000 ft) in the Himalayas.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> It has nested at 6,500&#160;m (21,300&#160;ft), higher than any other bird species,<sup id="cite_ref-bahn_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bahn-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> even surpassing the Red-billed Chough which has a diet less well adapted to the highest altitudes.<sup id="cite_ref-Rolando2_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rolando2-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> It has been observed following mountaineers ascending <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a> at an altitude of 8,200&#160;m (26,900&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> It usually nests in cavities and fissures on inaccessible rock faces, although locally it will use holes between rocks in fields,<sup id="cite_ref-Baumgart_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baumgart-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> and forages in open habitats such as <a href="/wiki/Alpine_meadow" title="Alpine meadow" class="mw-redirect">alpine meadows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scree" title="Scree">scree slopes</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Tree_line" title="Tree line">tree line</a> or lower, and in winter will often congregate around human settlements, <a href="/wiki/Ski_resort" title="Ski resort">ski resorts</a>, hotels and other tourist facilities.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Its penchant for waiting by hotel windows for food is popular with tourists, but less so with hotel owners.<sup id="cite_ref-goodwin_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goodwin-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpine_Chough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour and ecology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour_and_ecology">Behaviour and ecology</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpine_Chough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:128px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alpendohle_Ei-3c.jpg" class="image"><img alt=" A cream egg with light buff blotches" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/de/Alpendohle_Ei-3c.jpg" width="126" height="126" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alpendohle_Ei-3c.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Eggs vary from cream to <a href="/wiki/Buff_(colour)" title="Buff (colour)">buff</a> to light-green, and have brown speckles.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Alpine Chough is socially <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a>, showing high partner fidelity in summer and winter and from year to year.<sup id="cite_ref-delestradesty_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-delestradesty-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Nesting typically starts in early May, and is non-colonial, although in suitable habitat several pairs may nest in close proximity.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_3-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The bulky nests are composed of roots, sticks and plant stems lined with grass, fine twiglets or hair, and may be constructed on ledges, in a cave or similar fissure in a cliff face, or in an abandoned building. The <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is 3–5 glossy whitish eggs, averaging 33.9 by 24.9 millimetres (1.33&#160;in ×&#160;0.98&#160;in) in size,<sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> which are tinged with buff, cream or light-green and marked with small brown blotches;<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_3-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> they are incubated by the female for 14–21 days before hatching.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_12-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The chicks hatch with a dense covering of natal <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">down</a>, in contrast to those of the Red-billed Chough which are almost naked,<sup id="cite_ref-agd_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-agd-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> in a further 29–31 days from hatching.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_12-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The young birds are fed by both parents, and may also be fed by other adults when they have fledged and joined the flock.<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_3-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Breeding is possible in the high mountains because chough eggs have relatively fewer pores than those of lowland species, and lose less water by evaporation at low atmospheric pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryos</a> of bird species that breed at high altitude also have <a href="/wiki/Haemoglobin" title="Haemoglobin" class="mw-redirect">haemoglobin</a> with a genetically determined high affinity for oxygen.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the western Italian Alps, the Alpine Chough nests in a greater variety of sites than Red-billed Chough, using natural cliffs, pot-holes and abandoned buildings, whereas the Red-billed uses only natural cliffs (although it nests in old buildings elsewhere).<sup id="cite_ref-Madge94_3-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madge94-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rolando2_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rolando2-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Blanco5_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blanco5-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> The Alpine Chough lays its eggs about one month later than its relative, although breeding success and reproductive behaviour are similar. The similarities between the two species presumably arose because of the same strong environmental constraints on breeding behaviour.<sup id="cite_ref-Rolando2_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rolando2-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A study of three different European populations showed a mean clutch size of 3.6 eggs, producing 2.6 chicks, of which 1.9 fledged. Adult survival rate varied from 83 to 92%, with no significant difference detected between males and females. Survival of first-year birds was, at 77%, lower than that of adults. The availability or otherwise of human food supplied from tourist activities did not affect breeding success.<sup id="cite_ref-delestradesty_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-delestradesty-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpine_Chough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pyrrhocorax_graculus_-Gornergrat_Observatory,_Switzerland_-alps-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt=" Visitors sitting at tables on a large balcony high in the Swiss Alps, and a chough is perching on a railing beside them." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Pyrrhocorax_graculus_-Gornergrat_Observatory%2C_Switzerland_-alps-8.jpg/220px-Pyrrhocorax_graculus_-Gornergrat_Observatory%2C_Switzerland_-alps-8.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pyrrhocorax_graculus_-Gornergrat_Observatory,_Switzerland_-alps-8.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A chough probably looking for supplementary food is perching on a railing alongside visitors to <a href="/wiki/Gornergrat" title="Gornergrat">Gornergrat</a>, high in the Swiss Alps</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the summer, the Alpine Chough feeds mainly on <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> collected from pasture, such as <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Selatosomus_aeneus" title="Selatosomus aeneus">Selatosomus aeneus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Otiorhynchus_morio" title="Otiorhynchus morio">Otiorhynchus morio</a></i> have been recorded from <a href="/wiki/Pellet_(ornithology)" title="Pellet (ornithology)">pellets</a>), <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">fly</a> <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-goodwin_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goodwin-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The diet in autumn, winter and early spring becomes mainly fruit, including <a href="/wiki/Berry" title="Berry">berries</a> such as the European Hackberry (<i><a href="/wiki/Celtis_australis" title="Celtis australis">Celtis australis</a></i>) and Sea-buckthorn (<i><a href="/wiki/Sea-buckthorn" title="Sea-buckthorn">Hippophae rhamnoides</a></i>),<sup id="cite_ref-goodwin_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goodwin-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rose_hip" title="Rose hip">rose hips</a>, and domesticated crops such as apples, grapes and pears where available.<sup id="cite_ref-LaioloCarisio_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LaioloCarisio-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> It has been observed eating flowers of <i><a href="/wiki/Crocus_vernus" title="Crocus vernus">Crocus vernus albiflorus</a></i>, including the <a href="/wiki/Gynoecium" title="Gynoecium">pistils</a>, perhaps as a source of <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">carotenoids</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BCbirds_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BCbirds-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> The chough will readily supplement its winter diet with food provided by tourist activities in mountain regions, including ski resorts, <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">refuse dumps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Picnic" title="Picnic">picnic areas</a>. Where additional food is available, winter flocks are larger and contain a high proportion of immature birds. The young birds principally frequent the sites with the greatest food availability, such as refuse dumps.<sup id="cite_ref-Delestrade_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Delestrade-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> Both chough species will hide food in cracks and fissures, concealing the cache with a few pebbles.<sup id="cite_ref-wall_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wall-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This bird always forages in groups, which are larger in winter than summer, and have constant composition in each season. Where food resources are restricted, adults dominate young birds, and males outrank females.<sup id="cite_ref-delestradesty_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-delestradesty-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Foraging areas change altitudinally through the year, depending on climatic factors, food availability and food quality. During the breeding season, birds remain above the <a href="/wiki/Tree_line" title="Tree line">tree line</a>, although they may use food provided by tourists at refuges and picnic areas.<sup id="cite_ref-LaioloCarisio_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LaioloCarisio-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Movement to lower levels begins after the first snowfalls, and feeding by day is mainly in or near valley bottoms when the snow cover deepens, although the birds return to the mountains to roost. In March and April the choughs frequent villages at valley tops or forage in snow-free patches prior to their return to the high meadows.<sup id="cite_ref-LaioloCarisio_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LaioloCarisio-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> Feeding trips may cover 20&#160;km (12&#160;mi) distance and 1,600&#160;m (5,200&#160;ft) in altitude. In the Alps, the development of skiing above 3,000&#160;m (9,800&#160;ft) has enabled more birds to remain at high levels in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-BWP_12-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BWP-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Where their ranges overlap, the two chough species may feed together in the summer, although there is only limited competition for food. An Italian study showed that the vegetable part of the winter diet for the Red-billed Chough was almost exclusively <i><a href="/wiki/Gagea" title="Gagea">Gagea</a></i> bulbs dug from the ground, whilst the Alpine Chough took berries and hips. In June, Red-billed Choughs fed mainly on caterpillars whereas Alpine Choughs ate <a href="/wiki/Crane_fly" title="Crane fly">crane fly</a> <a href="/wiki/Pupa" title="Pupa">pupae</a>. Later in the summer, the Alpine Chough consumed large numbers of grasshoppers, while the Red-billed Chough added cranefly pupae, fly larvae and beetles to its diet.<sup id="cite_ref-Rolando2_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rolando2-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> In the eastern Himalayas in November, Alpine Choughs occur mainly in <a href="/wiki/Juniper" title="Juniper">juniper</a> forests where they feed on <a href="/wiki/Juniper_berry" title="Juniper berry">juniper berries</a>, differing ecologically from the Red-billed Choughs in the same region and at the same time of year, which feed by digging in the soil of terraced pastures of villages.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpine_Chough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Natural threats">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_threats">Natural threats</span></h3>
<p>Predators of the choughs include the <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_Falcon" title="Peregrine Falcon">Peregrine Falcon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Golden_Eagle" title="Golden Eagle">Golden Eagle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Eagle-owl" title="Eurasian Eagle-owl" class="mw-redirect">Eurasian Eagle-owl</a>, while the <a href="/wiki/Common_Raven" title="Common Raven">Common Raven</a> will take nestlings.<sup id="cite_ref-birdwatchireland_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdwatchireland-38"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OperationChough_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OperationChough-39"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rolando_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rolando-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Blanco_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blanco-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> Alpine Choughs have been observed diving at a Tibetan <a href="/wiki/Red_fox" title="Red fox">red fox</a>. It seems likely that this "<a href="/wiki/Mobbing" title="Mobbing">mobbing</a>" behaviour may be play activity to give practice for when genuine defensive measures may be needed to protect eggs or young.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Alpine Chough is a host of the widespread bird <a href="/wiki/Flea" title="Flea">flea</a> <i>Ceratophyllus vagabunda</i>, two specialist chough fleas <i>Frontopsylla frontalis</i> and <i>F.&#160;laetus</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Cestoda" title="Cestoda">cestode</a> <i>Choanotaenia pirinica</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> and various species of <a href="/wiki/Chewing_louse" title="Chewing louse" class="mw-redirect">chewing lice</a> in the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Brueelia" title="Brueelia">Brueelia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Menacanthus" title="Menacanthus">Menacanthus</a></i> and <i>Philopterus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpine_Chough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The Alpine Chough has an extensive though sometimes fragmented range, estimated at 1–10&#160;million&#160;square kilometres (0.4–3.8&#160;million sq mi), and a large population, including an estimated 260,000 to 620,000 individuals in Europe. The Corsican population has been estimated to comprise about 2,500 birds.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> Over its range as a whole, the species is not believed to approach the thresholds for the global population decline criteria of the IUCN Red List (i.e., declining more than 30% in ten years or three generations), and is therefore evaluated as <a href="/wiki/Least_Concern" title="Least Concern">Least Concern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>At the greatest extent of the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a> around 18,000 years ago, southern Europe was characterised by cold open habitats, and the Alpine Chough was found as far as south as southern Italy, well outside its current range.<sup id="cite_ref-yalden_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yalden-47"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> Some of these peripheral prehistoric populations persisted until recently, only to disappear within the last couple of centuries. In the <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a> <a href="/wiki/Tatra_Mountains" title="Tatra Mountains">Tatra Mountains</a>, where a population had survived since the glacial period, it was not found as a breeding bird after the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-tomek_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tomek-48"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> In Bulgaria, the number of breeding sites fell from 77 between 1950 and 1981 to just 14 in the 1996 to 2006 period, and the number of pairs in the remaining colonies were much smaller. The decline was thought to be due to the loss of former open grasslands which had reverted to scrubby vegetation once extensive cattle grazing ceased.<sup id="cite_ref-Stoyanov_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stoyanov-49"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> Foraging habitat can also be lost to human activities such as the construction of ski resorts and other tourist development on former alpine meadows.<sup id="cite_ref-rolandopatterson_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rolandopatterson-50"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> Populations of choughs are stable or increasing in areas where traditional pastoral or other low intensity agriculture persists, but are declining or have become locally extinct where <a href="/wiki/Intensive_farming" title="Intensive farming">intensive farming</a> methods have been introduced, such as <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>, England, south-west Portugal and mainland Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-pain_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pain-51"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Choughs can be locally threatened by the accumulation of <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_(chemistry)" title="Heavy metal (chemistry)">heavy metals</a> in the mountain soils, heavy rain, shooting and other human disturbances,<sup id="cite_ref-Stoyanov_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stoyanov-49"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> but a longer-term threat comes from <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, which would cause the species' preferred <a href="/wiki/Alpine_climate" title="Alpine climate">Alpine climate</a> zone to shift to higher, more restricted areas, or locally to disappear entirely.<sup id="cite_ref-Sekercioglu_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sekercioglu-52"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> Fossils of both chough species were found in the mountains of the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>. The local extinction of the Alpine Chough and the reduced range of Red-billed Chough in the islands may have been due to climate change or human activity.<sup id="cite_ref-rando_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rando-53"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
