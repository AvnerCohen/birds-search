<p>The <b>Little Bustard</b> (<i>Tetrax tetrax</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Aves" title="Aves" class="mw-redirect">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bustard" title="Bustard">bustard</a> family, the only member of the genus <i><b>Tetrax</b></i>. It breeds in southern Europe and in western and central Asia. Southernmost European birds are mainly resident, but other populations <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> further south in winter. The central European population once breeding in the grassland of Hungary went extinct several decades ago.</p>
<p>This species is declining due to habitat loss throughout its range. It used to breed more widely, for example ranging north to <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> occasionally (Tomek &amp; Bocheński 2005). It is only a very rare vagrant to Great Britain despite breeding in France.</p>
<p>Although the smallest <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" title="Palearctic" class="mw-redirect">Palearctic</a> bustard, Little Bustard is still <a href="/wiki/Pheasant" title="Pheasant">pheasant</a>-sized at 42–45&#160;cm long with a 90–110&#160;cm wingspan and a weight of 830g.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> In flight, the long wings are extensively white. The breeding male is brown above and white below, with a grey head and a black neck bordered above and below by white.</p>
<p>The female and non-breeding male lack the dramatic neck pattern, and the female is marked darker below than the male. Immature bustards resemble females. Both sexes are usually silent, although the male has a distinctive "raspberry-blowing" call "prrt".</p>
<p>This species is <a href="/wiki/Omnivorous" title="Omnivorous" class="mw-redirect">omnivorous</a>, taking seeds, insects, rodents and reptiles. Like other bustards, the male Little Bustard has a flamboyant display with foot stamping and leaping in the air. Females lay 3 to 5 eggs on the ground.</p>
<p>This bird's habitat is open grassland and undisturbed cultivation, with plants tall enough for cover. It has a stately slow walk, and tends to run when disturbed rather than fly. It is gregarious, especially in winter.</p>
