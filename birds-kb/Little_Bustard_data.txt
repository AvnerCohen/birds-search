<p>The <b>little bustard</b> (<i>Tetrax tetrax</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Aves" class="mw-redirect" title="Aves">bird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bustard" title="Bustard">bustard</a> family, the only member of the genus <i><b>Tetrax</b></i>. The genus name is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> and refers to a <a href="/wiki/Gamebird" class="mw-redirect" title="Gamebird">gamebird</a> mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes" title="Aristophanes">Aristophanes</a> and others.<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It breeds in southern Europe and in western and central <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Asia</a>. Southernmost European birds are mainly resident, but other populations <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> further south in winter. The central European population once breeding in the grassland of Hungary became extinct several decades ago.
</p><p>This species is declining due to habitat loss throughout its range. It used to breed more widely, for example ranging north to <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> occasionally.<sup id="cite_ref-Tomek2005_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tomek2005-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is only a very rare vagrant to Great Britain despite breeding in France.
</p><p>Although the smallest <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a> bustard, the little bustard is still <a href="/wiki/Pheasant" title="Pheasant">pheasant</a>-sized at 42–45&#160;cm (17–18&#160;in) long with a 90–110&#160;cm (35–43&#160;in) wingspan and a weight of 830&#160;g (29&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-bto_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bto-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In flight, the long wings are extensively white. The breeding male is brown above and white below, with a grey head and a black neck bordered above and below by white.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tetrax_tetrax_MWNH_0049.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Tetrax_tetrax_MWNH_0049.JPG/220px-Tetrax_tetrax_MWNH_0049.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="304" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Tetrax_tetrax_MWNH_0049.JPG/330px-Tetrax_tetrax_MWNH_0049.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Tetrax_tetrax_MWNH_0049.JPG/440px-Tetrax_tetrax_MWNH_0049.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2337" data-file-height="3229" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tetrax_tetrax_MWNH_0049.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>The female and non-breeding male lack the dramatic neck pattern, and the female is marked darker below than the male. Immature bustards resemble females. Both sexes are usually silent, although the male has a distinctive "raspberry-blowing" call: <i>prrt</i>.
</p><p>This species is <a href="/wiki/Omnivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Omnivorous">omnivorous</a>, taking seeds, insects, rodents and reptiles. Like other bustards, the male little bustard has a flamboyant display with foot stamping and leaping in the air. Females lay 3 to 5 eggs on the ground.
</p><p>This bird's habitat is open grassland and undisturbed cultivation, with plants tall enough for cover. Males and females do not differ markedly in habitat selection.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It has a stately slow walk, and tends to run when disturbed rather than fly. It is gregarious, especially in winter.
</p><p>Tracking of male Little Bustards has revealed that they are nocturnal migrants that make frequent stopovers in non-irrigated and irrigated croplands to reach more productive agricultural post-breeding areas. <sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 20 December 2013, the Cypriot newspapers 'Fileleftheros' and 'Politis', as well as news website 'SigmaLive', reported the discovery of a dead little bustard in the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Buffer_Zone_in_Cyprus" title="United Nations Buffer Zone in Cyprus">United Nations Buffer Zone</a>. The bird had been shot by poachers hunting illegally in the zone. The shooting was particularly controversial amongst conservationists and birders since the little bustard is a very rare visitor to Cyprus and had not been officially recorded in Cyprus since December 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-cbt2_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbt2-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
