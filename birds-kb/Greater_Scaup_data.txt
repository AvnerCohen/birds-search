<p>The <b>Greater Scaup</b> (<i>Aythya marila</i>), just <b>Scaup</b> in Europe, or colloquially "Bluebill" in North America for its bright blue bill,<sup id="cite_ref-Bluebills_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bluebills-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> is a mid-sized diving duck though it is larger than the closely related <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Scaup" title="Lesser Scaup">Lesser Scaup</a>. It is a circumpolar species, which means that its range circles one of Earth's poles. It spends the summer months breeding in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, northern Canada, <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, and the northernmost reaches of Europe. During the winter, it <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrates</a> south down the coasts of North America, Europe, and Japan.</p>
<p>Drake Greater Scaup are larger and have more rounded heads than females; they have a bright blue bill and yellow eyes. They have dark heads with a glossy green tint, white undersides and wings with white on the tips. The females are mostly brown, with white bands located on their wingtips. They have a blue bill that is slightly duller then the drake's.</p>
<p>Greater Scaup nest near water, typically on islands in northern lakes or on floating mats of vegetation. They begin breeding at age two, but start building nests in the first year. The drakes have a complex <a href="/wiki/Courtship" title="Courtship">courtship</a> procedure, which takes place on the return migration to the summer breeding grounds and concludes with the formation of <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" title="Monogamous" class="mw-redirect">monogamous</a> pairs. Females lay a <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> of six to nine olive-buff colored eggs. The eggs hatch in 24 to 28 days. The <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">down-covered</a> ducklings are able to follow their mother in her search for food immediately after hatching.</p>
<p>Greater Scaup eat aquatic <a href="/wiki/Mollusks" title="Mollusks" class="mw-redirect">mollusks</a>, plants, and insects, which they obtain by diving underwater. They form large groups, called "rafts", that can number in the thousands. Their main threat is human development, although they are preyed upon by owls, skunks, raccoons, foxes, coyotes, and humans. Greater Scaup populations have been declining since the 1980s; however, they are still listed as a species of least concern on the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_Scaup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The genus name <i>Aythya</i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>aithuia</i> which refers to a seabird mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> and others and is thought to refer to a duck, auklet or other seabird. The species name <i>marila</i> is from the Greek word for charcoal embers or coal dust.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The Greater Scaup was first studied by <a href="/wiki/Linnaeus" title="Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Linnaeus</a> in 1761.<sup id="cite_ref-USFauna_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USFauna-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Male Greater Scaup from America are distinguishable from those in Europe and Asia by the stronger vermiculations, worm-like carvings or marks<sup id="cite_ref-Dictionary_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dictionary-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> on the mantle and <a href="/wiki/Scapula" title="Scapula">scapulars</a>, and are considered a separate subspecies, <i>A. m. nearctica</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiley_Online_Library_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiley_Online_Library-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Females of the two subspecies are indistinguishable.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Based on size differences, a <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Paleosubspecies" title="Paleosubspecies" class="mw-redirect">paleosubspecies</a>, <i>Aythya marila asphaltica</i>, has also been described by Serebrovskij in 1941 from <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> recovered at <a href="/wiki/Binagady" title="Binagady" class="mw-redirect">Binagady</a>, Azerbaijan. The Greater Scaup's name may come from "scalp", a <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scottish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_English" title="Northern English">Northern English</a> word for a shellfish bed,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> or from the duck's mating call: "scaup scaup".</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic" title="Phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect">phylogenetic</a> analysis of the diving ducks, examining the skeletal anatomy and skin, found that the Greater and Lesser Scaups are each other's closest relatives, with the <a href="/wiki/Tufted_Duck" title="Tufted Duck">Tufted Duck</a> as the next closest relative of the pair.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_Scaup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>The adult Greater Scaup is 39–56 cm (15–22 in) long with a 71–84 cm (28–33 in) wingspan and a body mass of 726–1,360 g (1.60–3.0 lb). It has a blue bill and yellow eyes and is 20% heavier and 10% longer than the closely related Lesser Scaup.<sup id="cite_ref-Birdweb_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birdweb-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The male has a dark head with a green sheen, a black breast, a light back, a black tail, and a white underside. The drake or male Greater Scaup is larger and has a more rounded head than the female. The drake's belly and flanks are a bright white. Its neck, upper chest, and tail feathers are a glossy black, while its lower flanks are gray. The drake also has a white <a href="/wiki/Speculum_feathers" title="Speculum feathers">speculum</a> on its wings. The adult female has a white band and brown oval shaped patches at the base of the bill, which is a slightly duller shade of blue than the drake's bill. Females have grey on both their legs and feet. They have a brown body and head, with white bands on their wingtips.<sup id="cite_ref-DucksUnlimited_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DucksUnlimited-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Juvenile Greater Scaup look similar to adult females. The Greater Scaup drake's <a href="/wiki/Plumage#Eclipse_plumage" title="Plumage">eclipse plumage</a> looks similar to its breeding plumage, except the pale parts of the plumage are a buffy gray.<sup id="cite_ref-enature_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enature-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Distinguishing Greater from Lesser scaups can be extraordinarily difficult in the field, especially in terms of plumage, although (depending on posture) their general shape is slightly different.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_Scaup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>The Greater Scaup has a circumpolar distribution, breeding within the Arctic Circle both in the Old World (the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic_ecozone" title="Palearctic ecozone">Palearctic</a>) and North America (the <a href="/wiki/Nearctic_ecozone" title="Nearctic ecozone">Nearctic</a>). It spends the summer months in Alaska, Siberia. and the northern parts of Europe. It is also found in Asia, and is present in the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Islands" title="Aleutian Islands">Aleutian Islands</a> year round.<sup id="cite_ref-Birdlife_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birdlife-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The summer habitat is marshy lowland <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> and islands in fresh water lakes. In the fall, the Greater Scaup populations start their migration south for the winter. They winter along the Pacific and Atlantic coasts of North America, the coasts of northwest Europe, the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>, the coast of Japan, <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Sea" title="Yellow Sea">Yellow Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_China_Sea" title="East China Sea">East China Sea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EuropeBirdguide_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EuropeBirdguide-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> During the winter months, they are found in coastal bays, <a href="/wiki/Estuaries" title="Estuaries" class="mw-redirect">estuaries</a>, and sometimes inland lakes,<sup id="cite_ref-BirdsNorthAmerica_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdsNorthAmerica-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> such as the lakes of <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EuropeBirdguide_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EuropeBirdguide-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In Europe, the Greater Scaup breeds in Iceland, the northern coasts of the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_peninsula" title="Scandinavian peninsula" class="mw-redirect">Scandinavian peninsula</a>, including much of the northern parts of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>, the higher mountains of Scandinavia and the areas close to the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Sea" title="Arctic Sea" class="mw-redirect">Arctic Sea</a> in Russia. These birds spend the winters in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, western Norway, the southern tip of Sweden, the coast from <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> to Poland, including all of Denmark, the Alps, the eastern <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Sea" title="Adriatic Sea">Adriatic Sea</a>, the northern and western Black sea and the southwestern Caspian Sea.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In North America, the Greater Scaup summers in <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Ungava_Bay" title="Ungava Bay">Ungava Bay</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Bay" title="Hudson Bay">Hudson Bay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Winnipeg" title="Lake Winnipeg">Lake Winnipeg</a>, Northern <a href="/wiki/Yukon" title="Yukon">Yukon</a>, Northern <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a>, and Northern <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>. It winters in <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a> <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>, and the entire west coast of British Columbia. It will also winter along the entire west coast of North America, including the <a href="/wiki/Baja_Peninsula" title="Baja Peninsula" class="mw-redirect">Baja Peninsula</a>. The Greater Scaup also winters along the shores of the Great Lakes, the Gulf of Mexico and the entire US east coast from <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> to <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EuropeBirdguide_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EuropeBirdguide-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_Scaup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_Scaup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<p>Greater Scaup breed all the way from Iceland to northern Scandinavia, northern Russia, northern Siberia and the western <a href="/wiki/North_American_Arctic" title="North American Arctic">North American Arctic</a>. They breed in the tundra and the <a href="/wiki/Boreal_forest" title="Boreal forest" class="mw-redirect">boreal forest</a>; it is estimated that 75% of the North American population breed in Alaska. Greater Scaup typically nest on islands in large northern lakes. Greater Scaup begin breeding when they are two years old, although they may start nesting at age one. Drake Greater Scaup have a soft quick whistle to get the attention of hens during their courtship which takes place from late winter to early spring, on the way back to their northern breeding grounds. Female Greater Scaup have a single pitch, a raspy “arrr-arrr-arrr-arrr-arrr” sounding vocalization.<sup id="cite_ref-Greater_Scaup_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greater_Scaup-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The courtship procedure is complex and results in the formation of monogamous pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-Utahwildlife_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Utahwildlife-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Pairs nest in close proximity to each other in large colonies, usually near water, on an island or shoreline, or on a raft of floating vegetation. The nest consists of a shallow depression made by the female and lined with her down.<sup id="cite_ref-Birdweb_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birdweb-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> After the female lays the eggs, the drake abandons the female.<sup id="cite_ref-Utahwildlife_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Utahwildlife-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Once the drakes leave the females, they go to a large, isolated lake, in order to molt. These lakes can be very close to the breeding grounds, or miles away. The lakes chosen are used yearly by the same ducks. The optimal molting lake is fairly shallow, has an abundance of food sources, and cover.<sup id="cite_ref-All_about_birds_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-All_about_birds-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The female lays six to nine olive-buff colored eggs,<sup id="cite_ref-Audubonbirds_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Audubonbirds-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> which she <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation" title="Egg incubation">incubates</a> for 24–28 days.<sup id="cite_ref-Ohio.gov_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ohio.gov-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> A large clutch could indicates <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasitism" title="Brood parasitism" class="mw-redirect">brood parasitism</a> by other Greater Scaups or even ducks of other species.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Newly hatched chicks are covered with down and are soon able to walk, swim, and feed themselves; however, they are not able to fly until 40–45&#160;days after hatching.<sup id="cite_ref-EuropeBirdguide_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EuropeBirdguide-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Utahwildlife_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Utahwildlife-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The vulnerable small chicks follow their mother, who protects them from predators.<sup id="cite_ref-Utahwildlife_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Utahwildlife-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_Scaup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>

<p>The Greater Scaup dive to obtain food, which they eat on the surface.<sup id="cite_ref-Birdweb_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birdweb-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> They mainly eat <a href="/wiki/Mollusk" title="Mollusk" class="mw-redirect">mollusks</a>, aquatic plants, and aquatic insects,.<sup id="cite_ref-Greater_Scaup_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greater_Scaup-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> During the summer months, the Greater Scaup will eat small aquatic <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Birdweb_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birdweb-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> There is a report of four Greater Scaups swallowing <a href="/wiki/Leopard_frog" title="Leopard frog">leopard frogs</a> (with body length about 5&#160;cm (2&#160;inches)), which they dredged out of a roadside freshwater pond.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> In freshwater <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, the Greater Scaup will eat seeds, leaves, stems and roots, along with sedges, pondweeds, <a href="/wiki/Muskgrass" title="Muskgrass" class="mw-redirect">muskgrass</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wild_celery" title="Wild celery" class="mw-redirect">wild celery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DucksUnlimited_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DucksUnlimited-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Owing to the Greater Scaup's webbed feet and weight, it can dive up to 6 metres (20&#160;ft) and stay submerged for up to a minute, allowing it to reach food sources that are unobtainable to other diving ducks.<sup id="cite_ref-Feeding_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Feeding-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> The Greater Scaup forms large flocks, some of which can contain thousands of birds. When flocks are in water, they will face the current, and as the ducks float backwards, some fly to the front of the flock to maintain position.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_Scaup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Threats">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Threats">Threats</span></h2>
<p>Common predators of the Greater Scaup are <a href="/wiki/Owls" title="Owls" class="mw-redirect">owls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skunks" title="Skunks" class="mw-redirect">skunks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">raccoons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Foxes" title="Foxes" class="mw-redirect">foxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coyotes" title="Coyotes" class="mw-redirect">coyotes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Humans" title="Humans" class="mw-redirect">humans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-USFauna_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USFauna-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Greater Scaup often find themselves entangled in fishing nets, thus large numbers of them drown in nets each year. Greater Scaup can catch <a href="/wiki/Avian_influenza" title="Avian influenza">avian influenza</a>, so future outbreaks have the potential to threaten Greater Scaup populations.<sup id="cite_ref-Birdlife_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birdlife-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Although the Greater Scaup faces numerous threats, the most significant challenge to their survival is habitat degradation caused by a mix of human development and runoff.<sup id="cite_ref-NBII_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NBII-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Greater Scaup, when moulting and during the winter, are threatened by escalated levels of organochloride contaminants. Oil and sewage pollution also threaten this duck. Since 80% of the Greater Scaup population winters in the urbanized part of the Atlantic Flyway, these ducks are subject to high levels of organic contaminates, along with increased levels of heavy metals in foods and habitat.<sup id="cite_ref-BirdsNorthAmerica_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirdsNorthAmerica-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A joint group of American and Canadian scientists researching Scaup migration across the Great Lakes found that 100% of female Greater Scaup, and 77% of female Lesser Scaup, had escalated levels of <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a> in their bodies. Selenium is an occurring <a href="/wiki/Semimetal" title="Semimetal">semimetallic</a> <a href="/wiki/Trace_element" title="Trace element">trace element</a> that occurs naturally in some soils and minute amounts are necessary for animal life. However excessive selenium can cause reproductive harm and is highly toxic. On their migration across the Great Lakes, Greater Scaups are at risk of ingesting selenium by eating the invasive <a href="/wiki/Zebra_mussels" title="Zebra mussels" class="mw-redirect">zebra mussels</a>, which can render a hen <a href="/wiki/Infertile" title="Infertile" class="mw-redirect">infertile</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bluebills_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bluebills-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> This sterilization of hens is causing the population to decrease.</p>
<p>In a study of 107 Scaup, they all had traces of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> in their tissue samples with varying concentrations of metals in different types of tissues. Further analysis revealed that the kidneys had the highest levels of cadmium, the liver had the highest levels of cooper and manganese, the liver and the stomach had the highest levels of zinc, and the lungs and liver had the highest levels of iron.<sup id="cite_ref-Trace_metals_in_the_soft_tissues_of_scaup_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trace_metals_in_the_soft_tissues_of_scaup-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> There was no difference in concentration when comparing genders.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_Scaup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span></h2>

<p>Greater Scaup are rated as a species of least concern by the IUCN Redlist.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> During aerial population surveys Greater and Lesser Scaup are counted together, because they look almost identical from the air. It was estimated that the Greater Scaup made up about 11% of the continental Scaup population. Since the 1980s, Scaup populations have been steadily decreasing. Some of the primary factors contributing to this decline are <a href="/wiki/Habitat_loss" title="Habitat loss" class="mw-redirect">habitat loss</a>, contaminants, changes in breeding habitat, and a lower female survival rate. The 2010 American Scaup population survey was 4.2&#160;million Scaup,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> however, the worldwide Greater Scaup population survey estimated 1,200,000 to 1,400,000 mature Greater Scaup.<sup id="cite_ref-Birdlife_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birdlife-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Along with the aerial population surveys, there is a <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">banding program</a> for the Greater Scaup. Metal leg bands are placed on them, so that if the Scaup is killed by a hunter or if it is captured by another banding group, the number on the band can be reported to biologists and wildlife organizations. These banding programs yield valuable data about migration patterns, harvest rates, and survival rates.<sup id="cite_ref-FlywaysUS_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FlywaysUS-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Greater Scaup are a popular <a href="/wiki/Game_bird" title="Game bird" class="mw-redirect">game bird</a> in North America and Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-eumanagement_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eumanagement-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> They are hunted in Denmark, Germany, Greece, France, the United Kingdom, and Ireland,<sup id="cite_ref-eumanagement_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eumanagement-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> and in Iran for both sport and commercial reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-EuropeBirdguide_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EuropeBirdguide-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Greater Scaup are hunted with <a href="/wiki/Shotguns" title="Shotguns" class="mw-redirect">shotguns</a>, because they must be shot on the fly, a very challenging task, as they can fly at up to 121&#160;km/h (75&#160;mph). Greater Scaup are hunted from shorelines and in open water <a href="/wiki/Hunting_blind" title="Hunting blind">hunting blinds</a> or <a href="/wiki/Layout_boat" title="Layout boat">layout boats</a>, low-profile <a href="/wiki/Kayak" title="Kayak">kayak</a>-like boats that hunters lie inside. Hunters frequently use <a href="/wiki/Decoy" title="Decoy">decoys</a> to attract the birds, often arranged to simulate a raft of Greater Scaup and featuring an open area to attract the birds to land.<sup id="cite_ref-discoveroutdoors_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-discoveroutdoors-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> In most countries where Greater Scaup are hunted, a <a href="/wiki/Duck_stamp" title="Duck stamp" class="mw-redirect">duck stamp</a> is required along with the normal hunting licences that are required to pursue other game. In America and Canada, waterfowl must be hunted with non-toxic shot.</p>
