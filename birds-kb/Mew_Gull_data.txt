<p>The <b>common gull</b> (<i>Larus canus</i>) or <b>sea mew</b> is a medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gull</a> that breeds in the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, and northwestern <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. The North American subspecies is commonly referred to as the <b>mew gull</b>, although that name is also used by some authorities for the whole species.<sup id="cite_ref-IOC_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IOC-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Many common gulls <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> further south in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> There are differing accounts as to how the species acquired its vernacular name (see <a href="#Etymology">Etymology</a> section below).
</p><p>The name "sea mew" is a <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">calque</a> of the Dutch name "zee meeuw"
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Adult common gulls are 40–46&#160;cm (16–18&#160;in) long, noticeably smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Herring_gull" title="Herring gull">herring gull</a> and slightly smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Ring-billed_gull" title="Ring-billed gull">ring-billed gull</a>. It is further distinguished from the ring-billed gull by its shorter, more tapered bill, which is a more greenish shade of yellow and is unmarked during the breeding season. The body is grey above and white below. The legs are greenish-yellow. In winter, the head is streaked grey and the bill often has a poorly defined blackish band near the tip, which is sometimes sufficiently obvious to cause confusion with ring-billed gull. They have black wingtips with large white "mirrors". Young birds have scaly black-brown upperparts and a neat wing pattern, and grey legs. They take two to three years to reach maturity. The call is a high-pitched "laughing" cry.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bwp_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwp-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are four <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>, two of which are considered distinct species by some authorities:<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-olsen_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-olsen-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<ul><li><i>L. c. canus</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">1758</a></small> – <b>common gull</b>. <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies#Nominotypical_subspecies_and_subspecies_autonyms" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate</a>, found in Europe and western Asia. Small; mantle medium grey (palest subspecies); wingtips with extensive black; iris dark. Wingspan 110–125&#160;cm (43–49&#160;in); mass 290–480&#160;g (10–17&#160;oz).</li>
<li><i>L. c. heinei</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/Eugen_Ferdinand_von_Homeyer" title="Eugen Ferdinand von Homeyer">Homeyer</a>, 1853</small> – <b>Russian common gull</b>. Found in central northern Asia. Medium size; mantle dark grey (darkest subspecies); wingtips with extensive black; iris dark. Mass 315–550&#160;g (11.1–19.4&#160;oz).</li>
<li><i>L. c. kamtschatschensis</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/Charles_Lucien_Bonaparte" title="Charles Lucien Bonaparte">Bonaparte</a>, 1857</small>; syn. <i>L. kamtschatschensis</i> – <b>Kamchatka gull</b>. Found in northeastern Asia. Large; mantle medium-dark grey; wingtips with extensive black; iris pale. Mass 394–586&#160;g (13.9–20.7&#160;oz).</li>
<li><i>L. c. brachyrhynchus</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/John_Richardson_(naturalist)" title="John Richardson (naturalist)">Richardson</a>, 1831</small>; syn. <i>L. brachyrhynchus</i> – <b>mew gull</b> or <b>short-billed gull</b>. Found in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Small; mantle medium-dark grey; wingtips with little black and much white; iris pale. Wingspan 96–102&#160;cm (38–40&#160;in); mass 320–550&#160;g (11–19&#160;oz).</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Both common and mew gulls breed colonially near water or in marshes, making a lined nest on the ground or in a small tree; <a href="/wiki/Group_size_measures" title="Group size measures">colony size</a> varies from 2 to 320 or even more pairs. Usually three eggs are laid (sometimes just one or two); they hatch after 24–26 days, with the chicks fledging after a further 30–35 days. Like most gulls, they are omnivores and will scavenge as well as hunt small prey. The global population is estimated to be about one million pairs; they are most numerous in Europe, with over half (possibly as much as 80-90%) of the world population.<sup id="cite_ref-ebcc_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebcc-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> By contrast, the Alaskan population is only about 10,000 pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-hbw_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbw-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vagrancy">Vagrancy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vagrancy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common gull occurs as a scarce winter visitor to coastal eastern Canada and as a vagrant to the northeastern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">USA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sibley2000_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sibley2000-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> There is one recent record of a mew gull in Europe, on the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-Alfrey2007_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alfrey2007-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_gull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The scientific name is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>. <i>Larus</i> appears to have referred to a gull or other large seabird, and <i>canus</i> is "grey".<sup id="cite_ref-job_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The name "common gull" was coined by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Pennant" title="Thomas Pennant">Thomas Pennant</a> in 1768 because he considered it the most numerous of its <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODBBN_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODBBN-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Others assert that the name does not indicate its abundance, but that during the winter it feeds on <a href="/wiki/Common_land" title="Common land">common land</a>, short pasture used for grazing.<sup id="cite_ref-Okill2004_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Okill2004-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Ray" title="John Ray">John Ray</a> earlier used the name <i>common sea-mall</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODBBN_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODBBN-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It is said that <i>uncommon gull</i> is a more accurate description. There are many old British regional names for this species, typically variations on <i>maa</i>, <i>mar</i> and <i>mew.</i><sup id="cite_ref-BNB_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNB-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
