<p>The <b>Sedge Warbler</b> (<i>Acrocephalus schoenobaenus</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Old_World_warbler" title="Old World warbler">Old World warbler</a> in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Acrocephalus" title="Acrocephalus">Acrocephalus</a></i>. It is a medium-sized warbler with a brown, streaked back and wings and a distinct pale <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a>. Sedge Warblers are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, crossing the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> to get from their <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> breeding grounds to spend winter in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. The male's song is composed of random chattering phrases and can include mimicry of other species. The Sedge Warbler is mostly <a href="/wiki/Insectivore" title="Insectivore">insectivorous</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sedge_Warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>This is a medium-sized warbler, <span class="nowrap">11.5-13 cm</span> long and weighing around 12 g.<sup id="cite_ref-birdfacts_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdfacts-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> It has a streaked brown back and wings, and pale underparts. The rump is warm brown and unstreaked, contrasting with the duller wings. The forehead is flattened, there is a prominent whitish <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a>, the crown is streaked with black, and the bill is strong and pointed. The legs are greyish.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> of the sexes is identical, although they can be told apart when caught for <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">ringing</a> by the presence of a <a href="/wiki/Brood_patch" title="Brood patch">brood patch</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cloaca" title="Cloaca">cloacal protuberance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-svensson_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-svensson-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Juvenile birds have dark spots on the breast. They can be easier to confuse with <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_Warbler" title="Aquatic Warbler">Aquatic Warblers</a> due to an apparent pale central crown stripe contrasting with the darker edges.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Other similar species include <a href="/wiki/Moustached_Warbler" title="Moustached Warbler">Moustached Warblers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pallas%27s_Grasshopper_Warbler" title="Pallas's Grasshopper Warbler">Pallas's Grasshopper Warblers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-collins_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The oldest recorded Sedge Warbler was a bird ringed in <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> which reached the age of 10 years, 1 month. The typical lifespan is 2 years.<sup id="cite_ref-birdfacts_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdfacts-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sedge_Warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The Sedge Warbler was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect">Carolus Linnaeus</a> in his <i>Systema naturae</i> in 1758 (British <a href="/wiki/Ornithologist" title="Ornithologist" class="mw-redirect">ornithologists</a> did not distinguish the species from the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Reed_Warbler" title="Eurasian Reed Warbler">Eurasian Reed Warbler</a> until the 18th Century).<sup id="cite_ref-brit_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brit-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Its scientific name is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>akros</i>, meaning 'pointed', and <i>kephale</i> ('head'), <i>skhoiniklos</i> ('reed') and <i>baino</i> ('to walk'). It is <a href="/wiki/Monotype_(biology)" title="Monotype (biology)" class="mw-redirect">monotypic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-birdfacts_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdfacts-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sedge_Warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>The Sedge Warbler has a large range and an estimated Global Extent of Occurrence of 10 million square kilometres, with a large global population including between 8.8 million and 15 million birds in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. Data analysis by the <a href="/wiki/British_Trust_for_Ornithology" title="British Trust for Ornithology">British Trust for Ornithology</a> has shown that fluctuations in the Sedge Warbler population stem from the adult survival rate, due to changes in rainfall on the birds' wintering grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Global changes in population have not been measured, but the Sedge Warbler's status is designated 'of Least Concern' by <a href="/wiki/BirdLife_International" title="BirdLife International">BirdLife International</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>It breeds across <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and western and central <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and is <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>. After feeding up post-breeding, they migrate quickly across southern Europe and the Sahara from August to September. Studies in <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a> suggest that Sedge Warblers return to spend winter at the same sites, year after year.<sup id="cite_ref-migatlas_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-migatlas-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>All Sedge Warblers spend winter in sub-<a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Saharan</a> Africa, from <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> in the west to <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> in the east, and as far south as the eastern <a href="/wiki/Cape_Province" title="Cape Province">Cape Province</a> of <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a>. The route taken on the southward migration, and eventual wintering grounds, correspond to the birds' breeding grounds. Birds ringed in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> are later found from south-west <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>; birds from <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> are recovered in central Europe and Italy; while <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finnish</a> birds are found in north-east Italy and <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a> east to the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean</a> region. Sedge Warblers from the former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> take routes via the eastern <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Loss of wetland areas for feeding on migration, and the expansion of the Sahara desert, pose threats to the Sedge Warbler's breeding population.<sup id="cite_ref-migatlas_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-migatlas-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Birds begin leaving Africa in late February, fatten up at wetlands before and probably after crossing the Sahara, and arrive in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> from March onwards.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike other members of the <a href="/wiki/Acrocephalus" title="Acrocephalus">Acrocephalus</a> genus, the Sedge Warbler's range stretches from the Arctic to mid-latitudes. It is adapted to cool, cloudy and moist conditions. Though it is often found in wetlands, it can breed 500 metres or more away from water.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> During the breeding season, this is a species found in <a href="/wiki/Reedbed" title="Reedbed" class="mw-redirect">reedbeds</a>, often with scrub, ditches and habitats away from water including <a href="/wiki/Hedgerow" title="Hedgerow" class="mw-redirect">hedgerows</a>, patches of <a href="/wiki/Stinging_Nettle" title="Stinging Nettle" class="mw-redirect">Stinging Nettles</a>, and arable crops.<sup id="cite_ref-bin_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bin-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> On the African wintering grounds, habitats such as reeds at wetlands, papyrus, grass, sedge and reedmace and tall elephant grass are used. It can be found at altitudes of 1,800–2,400 metres above sea level in Ethiopia.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sedge_Warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sedge_Warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Acrocephalus_schoenobaenus_singing.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Acrocephalus_schoenobaenus_singing.jpg/220px-Acrocephalus_schoenobaenus_singing.jpg" width="220" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Acrocephalus_schoenobaenus_singing.jpg/330px-Acrocephalus_schoenobaenus_singing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Acrocephalus_schoenobaenus_singing.jpg/440px-Acrocephalus_schoenobaenus_singing.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Acrocephalus_schoenobaenus_singing.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Sedge Warbler singing</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The song is varied, rushed and chattering, with sweeter phrases and some mimicry,<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-collins_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> typical of the <i>Acrocephalus</i> warblers. It is composed of phrases in random order, so that it is never the same. Male Sedge Warblers which have the widest repertoire mate with the largest number of females.<sup id="cite_ref-birdfacts_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdfacts-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Male Sedge Warblers commence singing only a few hours after arriving on their breeding territory. The song is given from a bare perch such as a reed stem or bush, or from cover and during routine flights within their territory. Song-flights are also performed: while singing, the bird takes off, rises to a height of around 2–5 metres and then after a short circling flight, makes a slow, 'parachuting' descent, often with the wings held up in a 'v' shape. The song has the function of attracting a mate, rather than keeping other males away, and is stopped as soon as a mate is found. Contact calls are described as <i>chirr</i> or similar to <a href="/wiki/Black-headed_Gull" title="Black-headed Gull">Black-headed Gull</a> <i>keerrr</i>; these calls are repeated quickly to form a rattling alarm call.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Studies have found that male Sedge Warblers are often faithful to their breeding sites, but females are less so. A study in <a href="/wiki/Nottinghamshire" title="Nottinghamshire">Nottinghamshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, showed that a third of males which returned bred within 50 m or less of their previous breeding site, and only 14% bred further than 400 m away.<sup id="cite_ref-migatlas_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-migatlas-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Pairs are usually monogamous, but not strictly so.<sup id="cite_ref-leisler_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leisler-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Males occasionally practice bigamy and promiscuity. If eggs or chicks are predated, the female will often leave the male, who then resumes singing and pairs with another female.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">Hybridisation</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Reed_Warbler" title="Eurasian Reed Warbler">Eurasian Reed Warbler</a> has been recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The male defends a territory of around 0.1 to 0.2 hectares; in a study at <a href="/wiki/Zaanstad" title="Zaanstad">Zaanstad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, 68 territories were studied, with an average size of 1064 metres square one year, and 904 m sq the next.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from January 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The nest, built by the female, is in vegetation on the ground or up to a height of 50&#160;cm. The cup-shaped structure has an outer layer of grass, stems and leaves, plus spiders' webs, with a thick, finer layer inside including reed flowers, animal hair and plant down. It is woven around vertical plant stems. Between 3-5 greenish-yellow and brown-mottled<sup id="cite_ref-obs_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obs-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> are laid, measuring 18 x 13&#160;mm and weighing 1.6 g each. They are incubated by the female for 14 days; the chicks are <a href="/wiki/Altricial" title="Altricial">altricial</a> and naked.<sup id="cite_ref-birdfacts_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdfacts-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Both male and female care for the chicks, which fledge after 13–14 days. After leaving the nest, young Sedge Warblers continue begging for food from their parents for between 1–2 weeks after learning to fly.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sedge_Warbler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Food and feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_feeding">Food and feeding</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Acrocephalus_schoenobaenus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Acrocephalus_schoenobaenus.jpg/220px-Acrocephalus_schoenobaenus.jpg" width="220" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Acrocephalus_schoenobaenus.jpg/330px-Acrocephalus_schoenobaenus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Acrocephalus_schoenobaenus.jpg/440px-Acrocephalus_schoenobaenus.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Acrocephalus_schoenobaenus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Sedge Warbler carrying food</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Prey taken by Sedge Warblers includes <a href="/wiki/Ephemeroptera" title="Ephemeroptera" class="mw-redirect">mayflies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Odonata" title="Odonata">dragonflies and damselflies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orthoptera" title="Orthoptera">grasshoppers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemiptera" title="Hemiptera">bugs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neuroptera" title="Neuroptera">lacewings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">moths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coleopter" title="Coleopter">beetles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">flies</a>. Vegetable material includes <a href="/wiki/Sambucus" title="Sambucus">elderberries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rubus" title="Rubus">blackberries</a>. On their wintering grounds food includes <a href="/wiki/Chironomidae" title="Chironomidae">non-biting midges</a> and flowers and berries from the <a href="/wiki/Salvadora_persica" title="Salvadora persica">Toothbrush Tree</a>.</p>
<p>In late July, prior to migration, it appears that Sedge Warblers seek out sites with large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Aphididae" title="Aphididae">Plum-reed Aphids</a> and stay longer there than at other places; ringing studies show that birds may move considerable distances (e.g. from southern <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> to northern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>) in search of food before beginning their actual migration. In <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, the aphid supply dries up too early for Sedge Warblers to utilise it, so many birds do not stop off there and are already heavy with fat when they pass through. Birds with the heaviest fat reserves built up before migration are capable of non-stop flights from Africa to southern Britain, or from <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a> to <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, for example. Some double their normal weight when 'fuelling' for migration. Lighter birds are forced to make the journey in several shorter parts.</p>
<p>Sedge Warblers feed in low, thick vegetation, especially reeds and rushes, but also in arable fields and around bushes. A study at <a href="/wiki/Attenborough,_Nottinghamshire" title="Attenborough, Nottinghamshire">Attenborough, Nottinghamshire</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, found that the habitats used for foraging during the breeding season were 47% marshland, mostly <i><a href="/wiki/Glyceria" title="Glyceria">Glyceria</a></i> grasses, 26% shrub, 21% field vegetation and 6% woodland. Feeding techniques include 'picking' insects from vegetation while perched or sometimes hovering, and 'leap-catching', when the bird grabs flying insects as it flies between perches. Sedge Warblers tend to hop between plant stems and pick insects from underneath leaves; they take advantage of the low temperatures around dusk and dawn which make their prey less mobile.<sup id="cite_ref-bwpi_2-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bwpi-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
