<p><b>Temminck's lark</b> (<i>Eremophila bilopha</i>) or <b>Temminck's horned lark</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a>&#160;species of the family <a href="/wiki/Alaudidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Alaudidae">Alaudidae</a>. It breeds across much of north Africa, through northern Saudi Arabia to western Iraq. It is mainly resident, but some populations of this <a href="/wiki/Passeriformes" class="mw-redirect" title="Passeriformes">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> are partially <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, moving further south in winter. This bird's common name commemorates the Dutch naturalist <a href="/wiki/Coenraad_Jacob_Temminck" title="Coenraad Jacob Temminck">Coenraad Jacob Temminck</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The population is declining in Israel and may also be declining elsewhere, probably as a result of habitat loss. Nevertheless, this is a common bird in many parts of its wide range and the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> has rated its conservation status as being of "<a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least concern</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Temminck%27s_lark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Temminck's lark was originally placed in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Alauda" title="Alauda">Alauda</a></i> before being re-classified to <i>Eremophila</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Temminck%27s_lark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Unlike most other larks, Temminck's lark is a distinctive looking species on the ground, similar to the other, larger, member of its genus, the <a href="/wiki/Horned_lark" title="Horned lark">horned lark</a>. The 14 to 15&#160;cm (5.5 to 5.9&#160;in) adult is mainly reddish brown-grey above and pale below, and it has a striking black and white face pattern and a distinctive black patch on its breast. The summer male has black "horns", which give this species its alternative name. The juvenile of this species is reddish above and pale below, quite unlike the juvenile horned lark. The adult Temminck's lark differs from the horned lark in its reddish, rather than brown-grey <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">plumage</a>, and the lack of yellow in the face pattern. It has a similar but less harsh metallic call.<sup id="cite_ref-Beaman_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beaman-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Temminck%27s_lark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Temminck's lark is native to North Africa and the Middle East. Its range includes Algeria, Egypt, Iraq, Israel, Jordan, Kuwait, Libya, Mauritania, Morocco, Saudi Arabia, Syria, Tunisia and Western Sahara.<sup id="cite_ref-iucn_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This <a href="/wiki/Lark" title="Lark">lark</a> is a bird of open stony semi-desert. Its nest is on the ground, with two to four <a href="/wiki/Bird_egg" title="Bird egg">eggs</a> being laid. Its food is seeds supplemented with insects in the breeding season.<sup id="cite_ref-HBWA_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBWA-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
