<p>The <b>Lesser Kestrel</b> (<i>Falco naumanni</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">falcon</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> breeds from the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a> across southern central <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>. It is a summer <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and sometimes even to India and Iraq. It is rare north of its breeding range, and declining in its <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> range. The scientific name of this bird commemorates the German naturalist <a href="/wiki/Johann_Andreas_Naumann" title="Johann Andreas Naumann">Johann Andreas Naumann</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Female_Lesser_Kestrel_in_flight.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Female_Lesser_Kestrel_in_flight.jpg/220px-Female_Lesser_Kestrel_in_flight.jpg" width="220" height="162" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Female_Lesser_Kestrel_in_flight.jpg/330px-Female_Lesser_Kestrel_in_flight.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Female_Lesser_Kestrel_in_flight.jpg/440px-Female_Lesser_Kestrel_in_flight.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Female_Lesser_Kestrel_in_flight.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Female in flight showing whitish talons</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It is a small <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a>, 27–33&#160;cm in length with a 63–72&#160;cm wingspan. It looks very much like the larger <a href="/wiki/Common_Kestrel" title="Common Kestrel">Common Kestrel</a> but has proportionally shorter wings and tail. It shares a brown back and barred grey underparts with the larger species. The male has a grey head and tail like male Common Kestrels, but lacks the dark spotting on the back, the black <a href="/wiki/Cheek" title="Cheek">malar</a> stripe, and has grey patches in the wings.</p>
<p>The female and young birds are slightly paler than their relative, but are so similar that call and structure are better guides than plumage. The call is a diagnostic harsh <i>chay-chay-chay</i>, unlike the Common Kestrel's <i>kee-kee-kee</i>. Both sexes do not have dark <a href="/wiki/Claw" title="Claw">talons</a> as usual in falcons; those of this species are a peculiar whitish-horn color. This, however, is only conspicuous when seen birds at very close range, e.g. in captivity.</p>
<p>Despite its outward similarity, this species appears not to be closely related to the Common Kestrel. In fact, <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA" class="mw-redirect">mtDNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytochrome_b" title="Cytochrome b">cytochrome <i>b</i></a> <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence" class="mw-redirect">sequence</a> analysis (Groombridge <i>et al.</i> 2002) places it at a <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basal</a> position with regards to the other "true" kestrels (i.e., excluding the <a href="/wiki/American_Kestrel" title="American Kestrel">American Kestrel</a> and probably the grey <a href="/w/index.php?title=African_kestrels&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="African kestrels (page does not exist)">African kestrels</a> too).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Its divergence is tentatively placed to around the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a>-<a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a> boundary (<a href="/wiki/Messinian" title="Messinian">Messinian</a> to <a href="/wiki/Zanclean" title="Zanclean">Zanclean</a>, or about 7-3.5 <a href="/wiki/Mya_(unit)" title="Mya (unit)" class="mw-redirect">mya</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphological</a> similarity with the Common Kestrel is most puzzling, but still it appears to betray the present species' actual relationships: the lack of a <a href="/wiki/Cheek" title="Cheek">malar</a> stripe seems ancestral for kestrels, and the grey wing colour unites the Lesser Kestrel with most other <i>Falco</i> species, but not the other true kestrels.</p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lesser_Kestrel.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Lesser Kestrel- a very rare winter migrant to India</div>

<p>The Lesser Kestrel is, as the name implies, a smaller and more delicate bird than the Common Kestrel, and it is entirely <a href="/wiki/Sympatric" title="Sympatric" class="mw-redirect">sympatric</a> in its breeding range with it; they compete to a limited extent. Thus, the possibility that there is some form of adaptive advantage to the similar coloration deserves study. Considering that the Lesser Kestrel would in fact have an advantage if some would-be <a href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator" class="mw-redirect">predators</a> confuse it with the larger species and consequently avoid it, it might be a case of <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCllerian_mimicry" title="Müllerian mimicry">Müllerian mimicry</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:LesserKestrelGould.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/LesserKestrelGould.jpg/170px-LesserKestrelGould.jpg" width="170" height="239" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/LesserKestrelGould.jpg/255px-LesserKestrelGould.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/LesserKestrelGould.jpg/340px-LesserKestrelGould.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:LesserKestrelGould.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf2/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
From <a href="/wiki/John_Gould" title="John Gould">John Gould</a>'s <i>Birds of Europe</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Lesser Kestrel eats <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, but also small birds, reptiles and rodents (especially mice),<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> which are often taken on the ground. It nests colonially on buildings, cliffs, or in tree holes, laying up to 3-6 eggs. No nest structure is built, which is typical for falcons. Recent surveys (January 2007) by <a href="/wiki/LPO" title="LPO">LPO</a><sup class="plainlinks noprint Inline-Template" style="vertical-align:text-top;white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="//toolserver.org/%7Edispenser/cgi-bin/dab_solver.py?page=Lesser_Kestrel&amp;editintro=Template:Disambiguation_needed/editintro&amp;client=Template:Dn"><span title="Link needs disambiguation from October 2012">disambiguation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> have revealed that in their wintering grounds, Lesser Kestrels roost communally - sometimes in huge numbers. A roost discovered in Senegal during one of these surveys held 28,600 birds, together with 16,000 <a href="/wiki/Scissor-tailed_Kite" title="Scissor-tailed Kite">Scissor-tailed Kites</a> <i>Chelictinia riocourii</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It is widespread and plentiful on a global scale, and the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN" class="mw-redirect">IUCN</a> have classed it as Least Concern.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN2012_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN2012-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Apart from possible <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a>, it appears that indiscriminate use of <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a> has a strong effect on this species due to its insectivorous habits.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
