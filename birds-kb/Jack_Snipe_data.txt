<p>The <b>jack snipe</b> or <b>jacksnipe</b> (<i>Lymnocryptes minimus</i>) is a small stocky <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a>. It is the smallest <a href="/wiki/Snipe" title="Snipe">snipe</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Monotypic_taxon" title="Monotypic taxon">only member</a> of the genus <i><b>Lymnocryptes</b></i>. Features such as its <a href="/wiki/Sternum" title="Sternum">sternum</a> make it quite distinct from other snipes or <a href="/wiki/Woodcock" title="Woodcock">woodcocks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-brit1911_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brit1911-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Thomas2004_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas2004-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The common name has been said to come from the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> word for a snipe, <i>giach</i> (pronounced with a hard <i>g</i>),<sup id="cite_ref-century_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-century-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> but modern dictionaries say it comes from the masculine name <a href="/wiki/Jack_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jack (name)">Jack</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dictionary_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dictionary-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NSOED_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NSOED-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Newton" title="Alfred Newton">Alfred Newton</a> hypothesized that, "It may be, as in <a href="/wiki/Donkey" title="Donkey">Jackass</a>, an indication of sex, for it is a popular belief that the Jack-Snipe is the male of the common species; or, again, it may refer to the comparatively small size of the bird, as the 'jack' in the game of <a href="/wiki/Bowls" title="Bowls">bowls</a> is the smallest of the balls used, and as fishermen call the smaller <a href="/wiki/Esox" title="Esox">Pikes</a> Jacks."<sup id="cite_ref-brit1911_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brit1911-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The genus name <i>Lymnocryptes</i> is from  <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>limne</i>, "marsh" and <i>kruptos</i>, "hidden". The species name <i> minimus </i> is from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and means "smallest".<sup id="cite_ref-job_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adults are smaller than <a href="/wiki/Common_snipe" title="Common snipe">common snipe</a> and have relatively shorter <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a>. Length is 18–25&#160;cm (7.1–9.8&#160;in), wingspan is 30–41&#160;cm (12–16&#160;in) and weight is 33–73&#160;g (1.2–2.6&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The body is mottled brown on top and pale underneath. They have a dark stripe through the eye. The wings are pointed and narrow, and yellow back stripes are visible in flight. When seen, the distinctive bobbing movement, as if the <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> is on springs, has an almost hypnotic quality.
</p><p>The head pattern of jack snipe differs from common snipe and other species in the genus <i>Gallinago</i>, in that there is no central crown-stripe; instead, there are two pale lateral crown-stripes, which are separated from the <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a> by an area of dark <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jack snipes are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, spending the non-breeding period in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, Atlantic and <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> coastal Europe, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. The jack snipe is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies. Their breeding habitat is <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> and wet meadows with short vegetation in northern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lymnocryptes_minimus_MHNT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Lymnocryptes_minimus_MHNT.jpg/220px-Lymnocryptes_minimus_MHNT.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="224" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Lymnocryptes_minimus_MHNT.jpg/330px-Lymnocryptes_minimus_MHNT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Lymnocryptes_minimus_MHNT.jpg/440px-Lymnocryptes_minimus_MHNT.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3347" data-file-height="3415" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lymnocryptes_minimus_MHNT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Jack snipe egg</div></div></div>
<p>Jack snipe can be secretive in their non-breeding areas and are difficult to observe, being well <a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">camouflaged</a> in their habitat. Consequently, <a href="/wiki/Birdwatcher" class="mw-redirect" title="Birdwatcher">birdwatchers</a> have developed a specialised technique for finding them. This involves walking through its marshy habitat until a bird is disturbed and flies up. Jack snipe will squat down and not flush from cover until an intruder is quite close.<sup id="cite_ref-brit1911_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brit1911-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> They then quietly fly a short distance before dropping back into vegetation.<sup id="cite_ref-brit1911_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brit1911-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>They forage in soft mud, probing or picking up food by sight. They mainly eat insects and earthworms, also plant material.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The male performs an aerial display during courtship, during which it makes a <a href="/wiki/Drumming_(snipe)" title="Drumming (snipe)">distinctive sound</a> like a galloping horse. It is silent in winter. They nest in a well-hidden location on the ground, laying 3–4 eggs.
</p>
