<p>The <b>Jack Snipe</b> (<i>Lymnocryptes minimus</i>) is a small stocky <a href="/wiki/Wader" title="Wader">wader</a>. It is the smallest <a href="/wiki/Snipe" title="Snipe">snipe</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Monotypic_taxon" title="Monotypic taxon">only member</a> of the genus <i>Lymnocryptes</i>. Features such as its sternum make it quite distinct from other snipes or <a href="/wiki/Woodcock" title="Woodcock">woodcocks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-brit1911_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brit1911-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_Snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The common name has been said to come from the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> word for a snipe, <i>giach</i> (pronounced with a hard <i>g</i>),<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> but modern dictionaries say it comes from the masculine name <a href="/wiki/Jack_(name)" title="Jack (name)" class="mw-redirect">Jack</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Newton" title="Alfred Newton">Alfred Newton</a> hypothesized that, "It may be, as in <a href="/wiki/Donkey" title="Donkey">Jackass</a>, an indication of sex, for it is a popular belief that the Jack-Snipe is the male of the common species; or, again, it may refer to the comparatively small size of the bird, as the 'jack' in the game of <a href="/wiki/Bowls" title="Bowls">bowls</a> is the smallest of the balls used, and as fishermen call the smaller <a href="/wiki/Esox" title="Esox">Pikes</a> Jacks."<sup id="cite_ref-brit1911_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brit1911-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_Snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>Adults are smaller than <a href="/wiki/Common_Snipe" title="Common Snipe">Common Snipe</a> and have relatively shorter <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">bill</a>. Length is 18–25 cm (7.1–9.8 in), wingspan is 30–41 cm (12–16 in) and weight is 33–73 g (1.2–2.6 oz).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The body is mottled brown on top and pale underneath. They have a dark stripe through the eye. The wings are pointed and narrow, and yellow back stripes are visible in flight. When seen, the distinctive bobbing movement, as if the <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> is on springs, has an almost hypnotic quality.</p>
<p>The head pattern of Jack Snipe differs from Common Snipe and other species in the genus <i>Gallinago</i>, in that there is no central crown-stripe; instead, there are two pale lateral crown-stripes, which are separated from the <a href="/wiki/Supercilium" title="Supercilium">supercilium</a> by an area of dark <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_Snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span></h2>
<p>Jack Snipes are <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a>, spending the non-breeding period in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, Atlantic and <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect">Mediterranean</a> coastal Europe, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. The Jack Snipe is one of the species to which the <i>Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</i> (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" title="AEWA" class="mw-redirect">AEWA</a>) applies. Their breeding habitat is <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> and wet meadows with short vegetation in northern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_Snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lymnocryptes_minimus_MHNT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Lymnocryptes_minimus_MHNT.jpg/220px-Lymnocryptes_minimus_MHNT.jpg" width="220" height="224" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lymnocryptes_minimus_MHNT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.21wmf1/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>Lymnocryptes minimus</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Jack Snipe can be secretive in their non-breeding areas and are difficult to observe, being well <a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">camouflaged</a> in their habitat. Consequently, <a href="/wiki/Birdwatcher" title="Birdwatcher" class="mw-redirect">birdwatchers</a> have developed a specialised technique for finding them. This involves walking through its marshy habitat until a bird is disturbed and flies up. Jack Snipe will squat down and not flush from cover until an intruder is quite close.<sup id="cite_ref-brit1911_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brit1911-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> They then quietly fly a short distance before dropping back into vegetation.<sup id="cite_ref-brit1911_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brit1911-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_Snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span></h3>
<p>They forage in soft mud, probing or picking up food by sight. They mainly eat insects and earthworms, also plant material.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_Snipe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span></h3>
<p>The male performs an aerial display during courtship, during which it makes a <a href="/wiki/Drumming_(snipe)" title="Drumming (snipe)">distinctive sound</a> like a galloping horse. It is silent in winter. They nest in a well-hidden location on the ground, laying 3–4 eggs.</p>
