<p>The <b>Eurasian stone-curlew</b>, <b>Eurasian thick-knee</b>, or simply <b>stone-curlew</b> (<i>Burhinus oedicnemus</i>) is a northern species of the <a href="/wiki/Burhinidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Burhinidae">Burhinidae</a> (<a href="/wiki/Stone-curlew" title="Stone-curlew">stone-curlew</a>) <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> family.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_stone-curlew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> name <i>Burhinus</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i><i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language romanization">bous</i></i>, ox, and <i><i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language romanization">rhis</i></i>, nose. The <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> name <i>oedicnemus</i> comes from the Greek <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language romanization">oidio</i>, to swell, and <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language romanization">kneme</i>, the shin or leg, referring to the bird's prominent <a href="/wiki/Tibiotarsal_joint" title="Tibiotarsal joint">tibiotarsal joints</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-job_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> which also give it the common name of "thick-knee". This is an abbreviated form of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Pennant" title="Thomas Pennant">Pennant's</a> 1776 coinage "thick kneed <a href="/wiki/Bustard" title="Bustard">bustard</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-PDBBN_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PDBBN-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The name "stone curlew" was first recorded by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Willughby" title="Francis Willughby">Francis Willughby</a> in 1667 as a "<i>third sort of <a href="/wiki/Godwit" title="Godwit">Godwit</a>, which in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> they call the Stone-Curlew, differing from the precedent in that it hath a much shorter and slenderer Bill than either of them</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-Penhallurick1969_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penhallurick1969-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It derives from the bird's nocturnal calls sounding like the unrelated <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_curlew" title="Eurasian curlew">Eurasian curlew</a> <i>Numenius arquata</i> and its preference for barren stony <a href="/wiki/Heath_(habitat)" class="mw-redirect" title="Heath (habitat)">heaths</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-PDBBN_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PDBBN-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In his <i>Bird Watching</i> (1901) <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Selous" title="Edmund Selous">Edmund Selous</a> uses the name "great or Norfolk plover"  (<i>Œdicnemus Crepitans</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Selous1901_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Selous1901-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_stone-curlew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is a fairly large wader though is mid-sized by the standards of its <a href="/wiki/Burhinidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Burhinidae">family</a>. Length ranges from 38 to 46&#160;cm (15 to 18&#160;in), wingspan from 76 to 88&#160;cm (30 to 35&#160;in) and weight from 290 to 535&#160;g (10.2 to 18.9&#160;oz).<sup id="cite_ref-birdsinbulgaria_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birdsinbulgaria-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CRC_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> with a strong yellow and black beak, large yellow eyes (which give it a "<a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptilian</a>", or "goggle-eyed" appearance), and cryptic plumage. The bird is striking in flight, with black and white wing markings.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habits">Habits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_stone-curlew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Burhinus_oedicnemus_MWNH_0235.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Burhinus_oedicnemus_MWNH_0235.JPG/180px-Burhinus_oedicnemus_MWNH_0235.JPG" decoding="async" width="180" height="271" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Burhinus_oedicnemus_MWNH_0235.JPG/270px-Burhinus_oedicnemus_MWNH_0235.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Burhinus_oedicnemus_MWNH_0235.JPG/360px-Burhinus_oedicnemus_MWNH_0235.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2848" data-file-height="4288" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Burhinus_oedicnemus_MWNH_0235.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs in the <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden,_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Museum Wiesbaden, Germany">Museum Wiesbaden</a> collection</div></div></div>
<p>Despite being classed as a wader, this species prefers dry open habitats with some bare ground. It is largely <a href="/wiki/Nocturnal" class="mw-redirect" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a>, particularly when singing its loud wailing songs, which are reminiscent of that of <a href="/wiki/Curlew" title="Curlew">curlews</a>. Food consists of <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and other small <a href="/wiki/Invertebrates" class="mw-redirect" title="Invertebrates">invertebrates</a>, and occasionally small <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>. It lays 2–3 eggs in a narrow scrape in the ground.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Range_and_status">Range and status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_stone-curlew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Range and status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Eurasian stone curlew occurs throughout <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, north <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">southwestern Asia</a>. It is a summer <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a> in the more temperate European and Asian parts of its range, wintering in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. Although the species is of Least Concern, some populations are showing declines due to agricultural intensification. For example, a French population has declined with 26% over 14 years.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subspecies">Subspecies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurasian_stone-curlew&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Subspecies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are five <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of <i>Burhinus oedicnemus</i>:<sup id="cite_ref-zoonomen_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zoonomen-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Indian_stone-curlew" title="Indian stone-curlew">Indian stone-curlew</a> <i>Burhinus indicus</i> was previously considered a subspecies. 
</p>
<ul><li><i>Burhinus oedicnemus distinctus</i> <small>(Bannerman, 1914)</small> – Found on the central and western <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a><sup id="cite_ref-Tosco1992_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tosco1992-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>B. o. harterti</i> <small>Vaurie, 1963</small> – Found from west <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> to <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and northwestern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a></li>
<li><i>B. o. insularum</i> <small>(Sassi, 1908)</small> – Found on the eastern Canary Islands<sup id="cite_ref-Tosco1992_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tosco1992-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>B. o. oedicnemus</i> <small>(<a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">1758</a>)</small> – Found in western and southern Europe to the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a></li>
<li><i>B. o. saharae</i> <small>(Reichenow, 1894)</small> – Found in northern Africa and the Mediterranean islands to <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a></li></ul>
