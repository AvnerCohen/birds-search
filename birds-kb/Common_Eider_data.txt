<p>The <b>common eider</b> (pronounced <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span><span title="/./: syllable break">.</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="/ər/: &#39;er&#39; in &#39;letter&#39;">ər</span></span>/</a></span></span>) (<i>Somateria mollissima</i>), also called <b>St. Cuthbert's duck</b> or <b>Cuddy's duck</b>, is a large (50–71&#160;cm (20–28&#160;in) in body length) <a href="/wiki/Merginae" class="mw-redirect" title="Merginae">sea-duck</a> that is distributed over the northern coasts of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and eastern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. It breeds in <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> and some northern temperate regions, but <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">winters</a> somewhat farther south in <a href="/wiki/Temperate" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate">temperate</a> zones, when it can form large flocks on coastal waters. It can fly at speeds up to 113&#160;km/h (70&#160;mph).<sup id="cite_ref-birds-speed_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birds-speed-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The eider's nest is built close to the sea and is lined with <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">eiderdown</a>, plucked from the female's breast. This soft and warm lining has long been harvested for filling pillows and quilts, but in more recent years has been largely replaced by down from domestic farm-geese and synthetic alternatives. Although eiderdown pillows or quilts are now a rarity, eiderdown harvesting continues and is sustainable, as it can be done after the ducklings leave the nest with no harm to the birds. The scientific name of the duck is derived from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>sōma</i> "body" and <i>erion</i> "wool", and <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>mollissimus</i> "very soft", all referring to its <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">down feathers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-job90_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job90-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_eider&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Common_Eider_(Somateria_mollissima)_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Common_Eider_%28Somateria_mollissima%29_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG/220px-Common_Eider_%28Somateria_mollissima%29_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Common_Eider_%28Somateria_mollissima%29_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG/330px-Common_Eider_%28Somateria_mollissima%29_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Common_Eider_%28Somateria_mollissima%29_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG/440px-Common_Eider_%28Somateria_mollissima%29_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3372" data-file-height="2292" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Common_Eider_(Somateria_mollissima)_skull_at_the_Royal_Veterinary_College_anatomy_museum.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A common eider skull</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Somateria_mollissima_MWNH_1023.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Somateria_mollissima_MWNH_1023.JPG/220px-Somateria_mollissima_MWNH_1023.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="133" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Somateria_mollissima_MWNH_1023.JPG/330px-Somateria_mollissima_MWNH_1023.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Somateria_mollissima_MWNH_1023.JPG/440px-Somateria_mollissima_MWNH_1023.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3531" data-file-height="2140" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Somateria_mollissima_MWNH_1023.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>The common eider is both the largest of the four <a href="/wiki/Eider" title="Eider">eider</a> species and the largest <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">duck</a> found in Europe, and is exceeded in North America only by smatterings of the <a href="/wiki/Muscovy_duck" title="Muscovy duck">Muscovy duck</a>, which only reaches North America in a wild state in southernmost Texas and south Florida. It measures 50 to 71&#160;cm (20 to 28&#160;in) in length, weighs 0.81 to 3.04&#160;kg (1.8 to 6.7&#160;lb) and spans 80–110&#160;cm (31–43&#160;in) across the wings.<sup id="cite_ref-AAB_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAB-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ogilvie2004_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ogilvie2004-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The average weight of 22 males in the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a> was 2.21&#160;kg (4.9&#160;lb) while 32 females weighed an average of 1.92&#160;kg (4.2&#160;lb).<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It is characterized by its bulky shape and large, wedge-shaped bill. The male is unmistakable, with its black and white plumage and green nape. The female is a brown bird, but can still be readily distinguished from all ducks, except other eider species, on the basis of size and head shape. The drake's display call is a strange almost human-like "ah-ooo," while the hen utters hoarse quacks. The species is often readily approachable.
</p><p>Drakes of the European, eastern North American and Asia/western North American races can be distinguished by minor differences in plumage and bill colour. Some authorities place the subspecies <i>v-nigra</i> as a separate species.
</p><p>This species dives for crustaceans and molluscs, with <a href="/wiki/Mussel" title="Mussel">mussels</a> being a favoured food. The eider will eat mussels by swallowing them whole; the shells are then crushed in their <a href="/wiki/Gizzard" title="Gizzard">gizzard</a> and excreted. When eating a crab, the eider will remove all of its claws and legs, and then eat the body in a similar fashion.
</p><p>It is abundant, with populations of about 1.5–2 million birds in both North America and Europe, and also large but unknown numbers in eastern Siberia (<a href="/wiki/Handbook_of_the_Birds_of_the_World" title="Handbook of the Birds of the World">HBW</a>).
</p><p>A particularly famous colony of eiders lives on the <a href="/wiki/Farne_Islands" title="Farne Islands">Farne Islands</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. These birds were the subject of one of the first ever bird protection laws, established by <a href="/wiki/Cuthbert_of_Lindisfarne" class="mw-redirect" title="Cuthbert of Lindisfarne">Saint Cuthbert</a> in the year 676.<sup id="cite_ref-Waltho_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Waltho-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> About 1,000 pairs still nest there every year. Because St. Cuthbert is the patron saint of Northumberland, it was natural that the eider should be chosen as the county's emblem bird; the birds are still often called <b>Cuddy's ducks</b> in the area, "Cuddy" being the familiar form of "Cuthbert".
</p><p>In Canada's Hudson Bay, important eider die-offs were observed in the 1990s by local populations due to quickly changing ice flow patterns. The Canadian Wildlife Service has spent several years gathering up-to-date information on their populations, and preliminary results seem to show a population recovery.<sup id="cite_ref-seaduckjv_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seaduckjv-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Henri2012_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Henri2012-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chaulk2012_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chaulk2012-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The common eider is the object of the 2011 documentary <i><a href="/wiki/People_of_a_Feather" title="People of a Feather">People of a Feather</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-imdb_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-imdb-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> which studies the historical relationship between the <a href="/wiki/Sanikiluaq" title="Sanikiluaq">Sanikiluaq</a> community and eiders, as well as various aspects of their ecology.<sup id="cite_ref-peopleofafeather_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peopleofafeather-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common eider is one of the species to which the Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds (<a href="/wiki/AEWA" class="mw-redirect" title="AEWA">AEWA</a>) applies.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_behaviour">Social behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Common_eider&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Social behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eiders are colonial breeders. They nest on coastal islands in colonies ranging in size of less than 100 to upwards of 10,000-15,000 individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapdelaine1986a_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapdelaine1986a-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Female eiders frequently exhibit a high degree of natal <a href="/wiki/Philopatry" title="Philopatry">philopatry</a>, where they return to breed on the same island where they were hatched. This can lead to a high degree of relatedness between individuals nesting on the same island, as well as the development of <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kin</a>-based female social structures.<sup id="cite_ref-McKinnon2006_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKinnon2006-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> This relatedness has likely played a role in the evolution of co-operative breeding behaviours amongst eiders. Examples of these behaviours include laying eggs in the nests of related individuals<sup id="cite_ref-Andersson2007_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Andersson2007-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Cr%C3%A8che_(zoology)" title="Crèche (zoology)">crèching</a>, where female eiders team up and share the work of rearing ducklings.<sup id="cite_ref-Amnat_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amnat-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
