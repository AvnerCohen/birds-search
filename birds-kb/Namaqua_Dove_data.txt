<p>The <b>Namaqua dove</b> (<i>Oena capensis</i>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Columbidae" title="Columbidae">pigeon</a>.  It is the <a href="/wiki/Monotypic_taxon" title="Monotypic taxon">only species</a> in the genus <i>Oena.</i> It is found over much of <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabia">Arabia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Namaqua_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1760 the French zoologist <a href="/wiki/Mathurin_Jacques_Brisson" title="Mathurin Jacques Brisson">Mathurin Jacques Brisson</a> included a description of the Namaqua dove in his six volume <i>Ornithologie</i> based on a specimen collected near the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a> in South Africa. He used the French name <i>La tourterelle du Cap de Bonne Espérance</i> and the Latin <i>Turtur capitis bonae spei</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-brisson_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brisson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Although Brisson coined Latin names, these do not conform to the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial system</a> and are not recognised by the <a href="/wiki/International_Commission_on_Zoological_Nomenclature" title="International Commission on Zoological Nomenclature">International Commission on Zoological Nomenclature</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-allen_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allen-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> When in 1766 the Swedish naturalist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> updated his <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/12th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="12th edition of Systema Naturae">twelfth edition</a>, he added 240 species that had been previously described by Brisson.<sup id="cite_ref-allen_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allen-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> One of these was the Namaqua dove which he placed with the other pigeons in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Columba_(genus)" title="Columba (genus)">Columba</a></i>. Linnaeus included a brief description, coined the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a> <i>Columba capensis</i> and cited Brisson's work.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific name</a> <i>capensis</i> denotes the Cape of Good Hope.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Namaqua dove is placed in its own genus <i>Oena</i> that was introduced by the English naturalist <a href="/wiki/William_John_Swainson" title="William John Swainson">William John Swainson</a> in 1837.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ioc_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The genus name is from the Ancient Greek <i>oinas</i> meaning "pigeon".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are recognised:<sup id="cite_ref-ioc_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ioc-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>O. c. capensis</i> (<a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>, 1766) – Africa south of the Sahara, island of <a href="/wiki/Socotra" title="Socotra">Socotra</a>, Arabia</li>
<li><i>O. c. aliena</i> <a href="/wiki/Outram_Bangs" title="Outram Bangs">Bangs</a>, 1918 – Madagascar</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Namaqua_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Namaqua dove is a tiny <a href="/wiki/Old_World_sparrow" title="Old World sparrow">sparrow</a>-sized pigeon, typically 22&#160;cm in length with a 28–33&#160;cm wingspan, and weighing 40g. It has a very long black tapered tail, and the size and shape have led to comparison with the <a href="/wiki/Budgerigar" title="Budgerigar">budgerigar</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">plumage</a> is mostly grey apart from a white belly, and chestnut primary feathers which are visible in flight.
</p><p>The adult male has a yellow and red <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">beak</a> and a black face, throat and breast. The adult female lacks the black and has a red-based grey bill. Young birds are dark blotched on the wings and shoulders, and otherwise resemble the females.
</p><p>The song is a quiet, short, double <i>hoo</i>, higher on the longer second note <i>kuh-whooo</i>, mournful and frequently repeated.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Namaqua_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The dove is a widespread resident breeding bird in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> with its range extending into the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a> southern <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a> and as far north as <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>.  It is found in near desert with <a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">acacia</a> and bushes.
The namaqua dove is prone to wander out its original range, it is now being recorded south Asian countries. In Pakistan this species is recorded near shore waters off Paradise Point, Karachi on Friday, 14 October 2016. In India a female namaqua dove is recorded at khijadiya bird sanctuary near jamnagar,Gujarat on 17 December 2017<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> However, the bird is thought to be in trade in India, and a cage escapee can not be denied for its record from Western India.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Namaqua_dove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The dove is quite terrestrial, and usually forages on open ground and roadsides. The food is almost exclusively minute seeds, such as those of <a href="/wiki/Grass" class="mw-redirect" title="Grass">grasses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyperaceae" title="Cyperaceae">sedges</a> and weeds.  It is not gregarious, being encountered singly or in pairs, though they may form larger flocks at waterholes.  The flight is fast with clipped beats and a tendency to stay low.  It builds a stick <a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">nest</a> in a bush, and lays two white <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>, which are incubated for 16 days in typical pigeon fashion; the female at night and early morning and the male from mid morning till late afternoon.
