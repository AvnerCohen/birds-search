<p>The <b>great grey shrike</b> (<i>Lanius excubitor</i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Songbird" title="Songbird">songbird</a> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Shrike" title="Shrike">shrike</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> (Laniidae). It forms a <a href="/wiki/Superspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Superspecies">superspecies</a> with its <a href="/wiki/Parapatric" class="mw-redirect" title="Parapatric">parapatric</a> southern relatives, the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_grey_shrike" title="Iberian grey shrike">Iberian grey shrike</a> (<i>L. meridionalis</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_grey_shrike" title="Chinese grey shrike">Chinese grey shrike</a> (<i>L. sphenocerus</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Loggerhead_shrike" title="Loggerhead shrike">loggerhead shrike</a> (<i>L. ludovicianus</i>). Males and females are similar in plumage, pearly grey above with a black eye-mask and white underparts.
</p><p>Breeding takes place generally north of <a href="/wiki/50%C2%B0_northern_latitude" class="mw-redirect" title="50° northern latitude">50° northern latitude</a> in northern Europe and <a href="/wiki/Fauna_of_Asia" title="Fauna of Asia">Asia</a>. Most populations <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> south in winter to <a href="/wiki/Temperate" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate">temperate</a> regions.<sup id="cite_ref-hf2000p152153_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hf2000p152153-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The great grey shrike is carnivorous, with <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a> making up over half its diet.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_grey_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The species was first <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">scientifically described</a> by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his 1758 edition of <i><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i> under the current <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomial name</a>. His description is <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">L[anius] cauda cuneiformi lateribus alba, dorso cano, alis nigris macula alba</i><span class="lang-comment" style="font-style: normal; display: none; color: #33aa33; margin-left: 0.3em;">code: lat promoted to code: la </span> – "a shrike with a wedge-shaped white-bordered tail, back grey, wings black with white spot".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> At that time, none of the other grey shrikes – including the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_grey_shrike" title="Lesser grey shrike">lesser grey shrike</a> (<i>L. minor</i>), for which the description of the tail pattern is incorrect and which some authors already recognized as distinct – were considered separate species by Linnaeus, but that was to change soon.
</p><p>Linnaeus' binomial name replaced the cumbersome and confusing descriptive names of the earlier <a href="/wiki/Naturalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Naturalist">naturalist</a> books he gives as his sources: in his own <i>Fauna Svecica</i> he named it <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">ampelis caerulescens, alis caudaque nigricantibus</i> ("light-blue <a href="/wiki/Waxwing" title="Waxwing">waxwing</a>, wings and tail blackish"), while it is called <i>pica cinerea sive lanius major</i> ("ash-grey <a href="/wiki/Magpie" title="Magpie">magpie</a> or greater shrike") by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Leonhard_Frisch" title="Johann Leonhard Frisch">Johann Leonhard Frisch</a>, who in his splendid colour plate confused male and female. But most authors cited by Linnaeus – <a href="/wiki/Eleazar_Albin" title="Eleazar Albin">Eleazar Albin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ulisse_Aldrovandi" title="Ulisse Aldrovandi">Ulisse Aldrovandi</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Ray" title="John Ray">John Ray</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Willughby" title="Francis Willughby">Francis Willughby</a> – called it <i>lanius cinereus major</i> or similar, which is a near-literal equivalent of the common name "great grey shrike". The <a href="/wiki/Type_locality_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Type locality (biology)">type locality</a> of Linnaeus is simply given as <i>Europa</i> ("Europe").<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The scientific name of the great grey shrike literally means "sentinel butcher": <i>Lanius</i> is the Latin term for a butcher, while <i>excubitor</i> is Latin for a watchman or sentinel.<sup id="cite_ref-job_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This refers to the birds' two most conspicuous behaviours – storing food animals by impaling them on thorns, and using exposed tree-tops or poles to watch the surrounding area for possible prey. Use of the former by <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Gessner" title="Conrad Gessner">Conrad Gessner</a> established the quasi-scientific term <i>lanius</i> for the shrikes. Linnaeus chose his <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">specific name</a> because the species "observes approaching <a href="/wiki/Hawk" title="Hawk">hawks</a> and announces [the presence] of songbirds"<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> as he put it. This habit was also put to use in <a href="/wiki/Falconry" title="Falconry">falconry</a>, as fancifully recorded by <a href="/wiki/William_Yarrell" title="William Yarrell">William Yarrell</a> later.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The common English name "shrike" is from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>scríc</i>, "shriek", referring to the shrill call.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vernacular_names">Vernacular names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_grey_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vernacular names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ulisse_Aldrovandi" title="Ulisse Aldrovandi">Ulisse Aldrovandi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Gessner" title="Conrad Gessner">Conrad Gessner</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Ray" title="John Ray">John Ray</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Willughby" title="Francis Willughby">Francis Willughby</a> also reported old <a href="/wiki/Folk_taxonomy" title="Folk taxonomy">folk names</a>, mainly from <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic languages</a>: <i>Wereangel</i> or <i>Wierangel</i> from the <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennines</a> of England (where the bird was noted as a <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_in_birds" class="mw-redirect" title="Vagrancy in birds">vagrant</a>) as well as <i>Warkangel, Werkengel</i> or <i>Wurchangel</i> in various German dialects (e.g. around <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt/Main" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt/Main">Frankfurt/Main</a> and <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a>) probably mean "choking <a href="/wiki/Angel" title="Angel">angel</a>" (cf. <a href="/wiki/Standard_German" title="Standard German">Standard German</a> <i>Würgeengel</i>). These names are unlikely to significantly pre-date the times of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Boniface" title="Saint Boniface">Saint Boniface</a> (c. 700 AD) because of their <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> connotation; the related <i>Werkenvogel</i> ("choking bird") might, however, do so. The English version, having become <i>wariangle</i> or <i>weirangle</i>, was eventually transferred to the native <a href="/wiki/Red-backed_shrike" title="Red-backed shrike">red-backed shrike</a> (<i>L. collurio</i>) and lingered on into modern times in <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>. Along the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Rhine" title="Upper Rhine">Upper Rhine</a>, between Strasbourg and <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a> for example, <i>Linkenom</i> is attested; its origin is unclear. <a href="/wiki/Low_German" title="Low German">Low German</a> <i>Neghen-doer</i> and <a href="/wiki/Middle_Low_German" title="Middle Low German">Middle German</a> <i>Nünmörder</i> were also used; this has today evolved into <i>Neuntöter</i> and specifically means the red-backed shrike, but could in earlier times refer to any native <i><a href="/wiki/Lanius" title="Lanius">Lanius</a></i>. It literally means "killer of nine [prey animals]" and refers to the food caches.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Falconry" title="Falconry">falconer</a>'s name for the great grey shrike was <i>mattages(s)(e)</i>, which is related to <i>mat'agasse</i> from the western <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>. These terms may mean "<a href="/wiki/Magpie" title="Magpie">magpie</a> killer", due to their use for luring carnivorous birds to hunters – but perhaps more likely "killer magpie", considering that the bird was believed to be a peculiar sort of magpie by Johann Leonhard Frisch and others, and that another vernacular English name was "murdering pie". <i>Shrike</i>, meanwhile, is of Germanic origin also and dates back at least to <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle</a> or <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_English" title="Early Modern English">Early Modern English</a> <i lang="enm" title="Middle English (1100-1500) language text">schricum</i>. This is related to such words as Norwegian and Swedish <i lang="sv" title="Swedish language text">skrika</i> ("shriek, <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/skrike" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:skrike">skrike</a>"), German <i lang="de" title="German language text">Schrei</i> ("scream") or Icelandic <i lang="is" title="Icelandic language text">shrikja</i> ("shrieker"). But it seems to have become the dominant term only in rather recent times, for as late as the 18th century, the species was still widely known as "greater butcher-bird" in English, just like it was known as the <i lang="fr" title="French language text">boucher</i> ("butcher") in the <a href="/wiki/French_Jura" class="mw-redirect" title="French Jura">French Jura</a>. In Norway a vernacular name for the bird is <i lang="no" title="Norwegian language text">varsler</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> A whimsical name – presumably from <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> or nearby England – was "white <a href="/wiki/Whisky" title="Whisky">wisky</a> John" in reference to its wavy and somewhat unelegant flight, during which its large areas of light <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> are conspicuous.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships_and_evolution">Relationships and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_grey_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relationships and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The shrike <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> (Laniidae) is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Corvoidea" title="Corvoidea">Corvoidea</a>, the most ancient of the four large songbird <a href="/wiki/Superfamily_(zoology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Superfamily (zoology)">superfamilies</a>. Among its superfamily, the closest relatives of the Laniidae are probably the <a href="/wiki/Corvidae" title="Corvidae">Corvidae</a> (crows and allies). Little reliable data exists on its <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>; certainly (even though the supposed ancestral shrike <i>"Lanius" miocaenus</i> might not belong in the Laniidae, and probably does not belong in the same genus as <i>L. excubitor</i>) the genus dates back to <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> times. A <i>Lanius</i> <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Late_Miocene" title="Late Miocene">Late Miocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Turolian" title="Turolian">Turolian</a> age, c. 6 <a href="/wiki/Million_years_ago" class="mw-redirect" title="Million years ago">million years ago</a>, has been found at <a href="/wiki/Polg%C3%A1rdi" title="Polgárdi">Polgárdi</a>, Hungary. Its relationship to the modern species is unclear. However, all things considered, the grey shrike lineage probably represents the <a href="/wiki/Holarctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Holarctic">Holarctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Sister_taxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Sister taxon">sister</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a> radiation of fiscal shrikes. These two seem to have originated in a west- or southwestward expansion from the genus' origin, which (considering the <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a> of living <i>Lanius</i> lineages) was probably somewhere between <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a> and Central Asia. At the time of the Polgárdi fossil, it is rather likely that the grey shrikes were a distinct lineage already; given that they and the fiscals generally follow <a href="/wiki/Bergmann%27s_Rule" class="mw-redirect" title="Bergmann&#39;s Rule">Bergmann's Rule</a>, the smallish fossil makes an unlikely ancestor to the large grey shrikes even when taking into account the somewhat warmer Miocene <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The grey shrike <a href="/wiki/Superspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Superspecies">superspecies</a> consists of <i>L. excubitor</i> and its <a href="/wiki/Parapatric" class="mw-redirect" title="Parapatric">parapatric</a> southern relatives. As mentioned <a href="#Similar_species">above</a>, the other members of this group are the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_grey_shrike" title="Iberian grey shrike">Iberian grey shrike</a> (<i>L. meridionalis</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Steppe_grey_shrike" title="Steppe grey shrike">steppe grey shrike</a> (<i>L. pallidirostris</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_grey_shrike" title="Chinese grey shrike">Chinese grey shrike</a> (<i>L. sphenocerus</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Loggerhead_shrike" title="Loggerhead shrike">loggerhead shrike</a> (<i>L. ludovicianus</i>). The centre of this group's radiation is probably in the eastern Mediterranean region, and the southern grey shrike represents the <a href="/wiki/Basal_(evolution)" class="mw-redirect" title="Basal (evolution)">basalmost</a> form. The other three only diverged during the expansion into temperate regions. This must have happened fairly recently, because <a href="/wiki/Lineage_sorting" class="mw-redirect" title="Lineage sorting">lineage sorting</a> is not complete in the grey shrikes, and most of the present-day <a href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Habitat (ecology)">habitat</a> of <i>L. excubitor</i> was uninhabitable during much of the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">Quaternary glaciation</a>. Because of the <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Phylogenetic">phylogenetic</a> uncertainties surrounding this close-knit group in the absence of a good fossil record, some refrain from splitting them up into distinct species; most modern authors do so however.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_grey_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An adult great grey shrike is a medium-sized passerine about as large as a big <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrush</a>, measuring from 22 to 26&#160;cm (8.7 to 10.2&#160;in) long. It typically weighs around 60 to 70&#160;g (2.1 to 2.5&#160;oz), although some <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> are noticeably smaller or larger, and even in the <a href="/wiki/Nominate_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominate subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> adult weights between 48 and 81&#160;g (1.7 and 2.9&#160;oz) are recorded. The wings are around 11.4&#160;cm (4.5&#160;in) and the tail around 10.9&#160;cm (4.3&#160;in) long in the nominate subspecies, its bill measures about 23&#160;mm (0.91&#160;in) from tip to skull, and the <a href="/wiki/Tarsometatarsus" title="Tarsometatarsus">tarsometatarsus</a> part of its "legs" (actually feet) is around 27.4&#160;mm (1.08&#160;in) long.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Wingspan can range from 30 to 36&#160;cm (12 to 14&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Greatgreyshrike.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Greatgreyshrike.JPG/170px-Greatgreyshrike.JPG" decoding="async" width="170" height="243" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Greatgreyshrike.JPG/255px-Greatgreyshrike.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Greatgreyshrike.JPG/340px-Greatgreyshrike.JPG 2x" data-file-width="536" data-file-height="767" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Greatgreyshrike.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult male (top) and female <i>L. e. excubitor</i> with fledging young (bottom)</div></div></div>
<p>The general colour of the upperparts is pearl grey, tinged brownish towards the east of its Eurasian range. The cheeks and chin as well as a thin and often hard-to-see stripe above the eye are white, and a deep black mask extends from the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">beak</a> through the eye to the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ear_coverts&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ear coverts (page does not exist)">ear coverts</a>; the area immediately above the beak is grey. The scapulars (shoulder feathers) are white, and the wings are black with a white bar made up by the bases of the primary <a href="/wiki/Remiges" class="mw-redirect" title="Remiges">remiges</a>, continuing slightly offset onto the bases of the secondary remiges in some regions. The tail is black, long, and pointed at the tip; the outer <a href="/wiki/Rectrices" class="mw-redirect" title="Rectrices">rectrices</a> have white outer <a href="/wiki/Pennaceous_feather" title="Pennaceous feather">vanes</a>. The underparts are white, slightly tinged with grey in most <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>. In particular the breast is usually darker and sometimes browner than the rest of the light underside, and may appear as an indistinct band between the lighter belly and white throat. In the subspecies around the <a href="/wiki/North_Pacific" class="mw-redirect" title="North Pacific">North Pacific</a> in particular and in females elsewhere too, there may be faint brownish bars on the breast. The bill is large and hooked at the tip and coloured nearly black, but pale at the base of the under <a href="/wiki/Mandible" title="Mandible">mandible</a> (though the extent varies <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasonally</a>). The legs and feet are blackish.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Males and females are about the same size, and do not differ conspicuously in appearance except by direct comparison. In the female the underparts are greyer and are usually visibly barred greyish-brown, and the white wing and tail markings are characteristically less in extent (though this is rarely clearly visible except in flight). <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">Fledged</a> young birds are heavily tinged greyish-brown all over, with barring on the upperside and indistinct <a href="/wiki/Buffy_(color)" class="mw-redirect" title="Buffy (color)">buffy</a>-white markings. The tips of the tertiary remiges and the <a href="/wiki/Wing_coverts" class="mw-redirect" title="Wing coverts">wing coverts</a> are also buffy, with a black band in the latter. In the North American subspecies <i>borealis</i>, the fledglings are tinged quite brown indeed on upperside and wings, and have sharp and dark underside bars. In Eurasia, fledglings <a href="/wiki/Moult" class="mw-redirect" title="Moult">moult</a> into a female-like plumage with the tertiary bars usually remaining in autumn. Across its range, the young acquire the adult plumage in their first spring.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocalizations">Vocalizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_grey_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vocalizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The male's <a href="/wiki/Birdsong" class="mw-redirect" title="Birdsong">song</a> consists of short pleasant warbling strophes, interspersed with fluid whistles. The individual phrases may go like <i>tu-tu-krr-pree-pree</i> or <i>trr-turit trr-turit...</i>. To announce that it has become aware of someone straying into its <a href="/wiki/Territory_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Territory (biology)">territory</a> – be it a female or male of its species or a large <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammal</a> – it gives long shrill raspy whistles like <i>trrii(u)</i> or <i>(t')kwiiet</i>. To announce to females, it often mixes these whistles with a strophe of song. A softer whistle goes like <i>trüü(t)</i>. These whistles are also used in duets between mates in winter and neighbours in the breeding season. Various contact calls have been described as <i>chlie(p)</i>, <i>gihrrr</i>, <i>kwä</i> or <i>wuut</i>. These are frequently heard during courtship, interspersed with song phrases as the harsh whistles are in pre-courtship. The song becomes softer and more warbling as the male shows the female around his territory, and at potential nest sites the male gives a lively chatter containing fluting <i>tli-tli</i>, <i>prrr</i> trills and <i>kwiw...püh</i> calls.<sup id="cite_ref-hf2000p153155_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hf2000p153155-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When disturbed, its alarm note is a harsh <a href="/wiki/Jay" title="Jay">jay</a>-like <i>k(w)eee</i>, <i>greee</i> or <i>jaaa</i>, often repeated twice. The more excited the birds become, the higher and faster the calls get, via <i>chek-chek-chek</i> to a rattle <i>trr-trr-trr</i> or an explosive <i>aak-aak-aak</i>. A <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> alert is given with a whistle <i>breezeek</i>. <i>Knuk</i> calls are given by adults confronted with a potential threat to their young. To beg for food – young to adults or mates to each other –, rows of <i>waik</i> calls are given. This species sometimes tries to attract small songbirds by <a href="/wiki/Aggressive_mimicry" title="Aggressive mimicry">mimicking</a> their calls, so it may attempt to catch them for food.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_species">Similar species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_grey_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Similar species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Indian_Great_Grey_Shrike_(Lanius_meridionalis_lahtora)_Photograph_by_Shantanu_Kuveskar.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Indian_Great_Grey_Shrike_%28Lanius_meridionalis_lahtora%29_Photograph_by_Shantanu_Kuveskar.jpg/220px-Indian_Great_Grey_Shrike_%28Lanius_meridionalis_lahtora%29_Photograph_by_Shantanu_Kuveskar.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Indian_Great_Grey_Shrike_%28Lanius_meridionalis_lahtora%29_Photograph_by_Shantanu_Kuveskar.jpg/330px-Indian_Great_Grey_Shrike_%28Lanius_meridionalis_lahtora%29_Photograph_by_Shantanu_Kuveskar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Indian_Great_Grey_Shrike_%28Lanius_meridionalis_lahtora%29_Photograph_by_Shantanu_Kuveskar.jpg/440px-Indian_Great_Grey_Shrike_%28Lanius_meridionalis_lahtora%29_Photograph_by_Shantanu_Kuveskar.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="750" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Indian_Great_Grey_Shrike_(Lanius_meridionalis_lahtora)_Photograph_by_Shantanu_Kuveskar.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_Great_Grey_Shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Indian Great Grey Shrike (page does not exist)">Indian Great Grey Shrike</a><br /><i>Lanius meridionalis lahtora</i></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Iberian_grey_shrike" title="Iberian grey shrike">Iberian grey shrike</a> (<i>L. meridionalis</i>) was formerly included in the great grey shrike as <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>. It occurs in south western Europe (<a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> and France). It prefers different <a href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Habitat (ecology)">habitat</a> – lightly wooded grassland in the great, more <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> <a href="/wiki/Shrubland" title="Shrubland">shrubland</a> in the southern grey shrike – and where the species' ranges overlap, they do not <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridize</a> at present (though they may have done so in past <a href="/wiki/Millennia" class="mw-redirect" title="Millennia">millennia</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Loggerhead128.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Loggerhead128.jpg/220px-Loggerhead128.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="230" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Loggerhead128.jpg/330px-Loggerhead128.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Loggerhead128.jpg/440px-Loggerhead128.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1296" data-file-height="1355" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Loggerhead128.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Loggerhead_shrike" title="Loggerhead shrike">Loggerhead shrike</a><br /><i>Lanius ludovicianus</i></div></div></div>
<p>Elsewhere, the <a href="/wiki/Parapatric" class="mw-redirect" title="Parapatric">parapatric</a> relatives of the <i>L. excubitor</i> are the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_grey_shrike" title="Chinese grey shrike">Chinese grey shrike</a> (<i>L. sphenocerus</i>) from <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Northern_shrike" title="Northern shrike">northern shrike</a> (<i>L. borealis</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Loggerhead_shrike" title="Loggerhead shrike">loggerhead shrike</a> (<i>L. ludovicianus</i>) from North America. The northern grey shrike is <a href="/wiki/Sympatric" class="mw-redirect" title="Sympatric">sympatric</a> in winter quarters with each of its three close relatives at the north of their range. Their overall colouration is – apparently <a href="/wiki/Plesiomorph" class="mw-redirect" title="Plesiomorph">plesiomorphically</a> – shared in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a> by the somewhat more distantly related <a href="/wiki/Grey-backed_fiscal" title="Grey-backed fiscal">grey-backed fiscal</a> (<i>L. excubitoroides</i>) which is found from the <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a> eastwards, and <a href="/wiki/Mackinnon%27s_fiscal" class="mw-redirect" title="Mackinnon&#39;s fiscal">Mackinnon's fiscal</a> (<i>L. mackinnoni</i>) of the <a href="/wiki/Congo_Basin" title="Congo Basin">Congo Basin</a> region. The <a href="/wiki/Lesser_grey_shrike" title="Lesser grey shrike">lesser grey shrike</a> (<i>L. minor</i>, <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a> to Central Asia) seems to be quite distinct indeed and is <a href="/wiki/Sympatric" class="mw-redirect" title="Sympatric">sympatric</a> with the grey shrike <a href="/wiki/Superspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Superspecies">superspecies</a> between Eastern Europe and Central Asia; it may be more closely related to the small brown shrikes and resemble the bold, aggressive and hard-to-catch grey shrikes because of <a href="/wiki/Batesian_mimicry" title="Batesian mimicry">Batesian mimicry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lesser_Grey_Shrike_by_Daniel_Bastaja.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Lesser_Grey_Shrike_by_Daniel_Bastaja.jpg/220px-Lesser_Grey_Shrike_by_Daniel_Bastaja.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Lesser_Grey_Shrike_by_Daniel_Bastaja.jpg/330px-Lesser_Grey_Shrike_by_Daniel_Bastaja.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Lesser_Grey_Shrike_by_Daniel_Bastaja.jpg/440px-Lesser_Grey_Shrike_by_Daniel_Bastaja.jpg 2x" data-file-width="648" data-file-height="555" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lesser_Grey_Shrike_by_Daniel_Bastaja.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lesser_grey_shrike" title="Lesser grey shrike">Lesser grey shrike</a><br /><i>Lanius minor</i></div></div></div>
<p>The Iberian grey shrike is clearer and usually darker grey above, and not tinged grey but often decidedly pinkish on the belly and particular breast; the white "eyebrow" extends to over the beak, which has typically a larger pale base. The barring pattern is less developed at all ages, hardly ever present even in females, and slighter in the otherwise very similar <a href="/wiki/Fledgling_(birds)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fledgling (birds)">fledglings</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>East Asian <i>L. excubitor</i> are barely sympatric with the Chinese grey shrike. The latter is larger and generally differs from the northern species as the southern does, and in addition has much larger white areas in wings and tail.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The loggerhead shrike is hard to distinguish, but the proportion of the head to the beak (which seems stubby in <i>L. ludovicianus</i> by comparison and is all-dark) is usually reliable. Indeed, the word <i><a href="/wiki/Loggerhead_shrike" title="Loggerhead shrike">loggerhead</a></i> refers to the relatively larger head of the southern species.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The lesser grey shrike is a smaller and comparatively short-tailed bird. It can best be recognized by the rather large black area above the bill, almost reaching to the forehead and without a white stripe above it. In flight, the wide instead of pointed black tail end of <i>L. minor</i> is characteristic. The African species are completely <a href="/wiki/Allopatric" class="mw-redirect" title="Allopatric">allopatric</a> with <i>L. excubitor</i>; they lack white scapulars (grey-backed fiscal) or wingspots (Mackinnon's fiscal) and differ in some other details, particularly the tail pattern.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_grey_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Generally, its breeding range is found in Eurasia and northern Africa. In the high mountains of the <a href="/wiki/Altai_Mountains" title="Altai Mountains">Altai</a>-<a href="/wiki/Tian_Shan" title="Tian Shan">Tian Shan</a> region, it ranges south perhaps as far as <a href="/wiki/42%C2%B0_northern_latitude" class="mw-redirect" title="42° northern latitude">42° northern latitude</a>. Its northern limit is generally <a href="/wiki/70%C2%B0_northern_latitude" class="mw-redirect" title="70° northern latitude">70° northern latitude</a>. It is only found as a <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_in_birds" class="mw-redirect" title="Vagrancy in birds">vagrant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, the Mediterranean region (excluding the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> and perhaps <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> but including <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>. There do not appear to be breeding records from the entire <a href="/wiki/Kamchatka_Peninsula" title="Kamchatka Peninsula">Kamchatka Peninsula</a>; in Switzerland, the present day <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a> and southern Germany small populations were found in the mid-20th century but have declined or even disappeared since then.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Except for the <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> <i>bianchii</i> which is largely all-year resident, and subspecies <i>excubitor</i> in the temperate European parts of its range with their mild <a href="/wiki/Maritime_climate" class="mw-redirect" title="Maritime climate">maritime climate</a>, the species is a short-distance <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrant</a>. The migrations are triggered by scarcity of food and therefore, according to prey population levels, the winter range might little extend south beyond the breeding range, or be entirely <a href="/wiki/Parapatric" class="mw-redirect" title="Parapatric">parapatric</a> to it. The populations of the Central Asian mountains mostly migrate downslope rather than southwards. Females are more prone to migration than males; they do not appear to migrate, on average, longer or shorter distances than males, and consequently are the dominant sex in many parts of the winter range. Birds leave for winter quarters a more or less short time after breeding – July to October, with most birds staying to September – and return to nest mainly in March/April, but some only arrive in May. In recent decades, the number of birds remaining on the breeding grounds all year has been noted to increase e.g. in <a href="/wiki/Fennoscandia" title="Fennoscandia">Fennoscandia</a>, whereas for example <i>borealis</i> seems to be as rare a winter visitor in northern <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> as it was a century ago.<sup id="cite_ref-hf2000p152153_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hf2000p152153-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The preferred <a href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Habitat (ecology)">habitat</a> is generally open <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a>, perhaps with <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrubs</a> interspersed, and adjacent lookout points. These are normally trees – at forest edges in much of the habitat, but single trees or small stands at the <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a>-<a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> border. In <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a>, it will utilize any isolated perch, be it fence posts, <a href="/wiki/Power_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Power line">power lines</a> or rocks. In general, some 5–15 perching sites per <a href="/wiki/Hectar" class="mw-redirect" title="Hectar">hectare</a> habitat seem to be required. It avoids low grassland with no lookouts and nesting opportunities (trees or large shrubs), as well as dense forest with no hunting ground. Apart from grassland, the birds will utilize a variety of hunting habitats, including <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glade_(geography)" title="Glade (geography)">clearings</a> or non-industrially farmed <a href="/wiki/Field_(agriculture)" title="Field (agriculture)">fields</a>. Breeding birds appear to have different <a href="/wiki/Microhabitat" class="mw-redirect" title="Microhabitat">microhabitat</a> desires, but little detail is known yet.<sup id="cite_ref-hf2000p152_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hf2000p152-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_grey_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lanius.excubitor.-.lindsey.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Lanius.excubitor.-.lindsey.jpg/220px-Lanius.excubitor.-.lindsey.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Lanius.excubitor.-.lindsey.jpg/330px-Lanius.excubitor.-.lindsey.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Lanius.excubitor.-.lindsey.jpg/440px-Lanius.excubitor.-.lindsey.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="426" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lanius.excubitor.-.lindsey.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Perching sites are important features of great grey shrike habitat</div></div></div>
<p>This species is <a href="/wiki/Territory_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Territory (biology)">territorial</a>, but likes to breed in dispersed groups of a good half-dozen adults. It is not known to what extent the birds in such groups are related. In the temperate parts of its range, groups are perhaps 5&#160;km (3.1&#160;mi) apart, while individual territories within each group may be as small as 20&#160;ha (49 acres) but more typically are about twice that size. In less hospitable climes, territories may be more than 350&#160;ha (1.4&#160;sq&#160;mi). Throughout the breeding season, in prime habitat, territories are held by mated pairs and single males looking for a partner. In less productive habitat, "floaters" hold territories more <a href="/wiki/Ephemeral" class="mw-redirect" title="Ephemeral">ephemerally</a>. This leads to shifts in <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> between regions, as "floaters" move between groups of territorial birds in search of a bountiful unclaimed territory to settle down and/or a partner to mate with. On the wintering grounds, pairs separate to account for the lower amount of food available at that time, but if both members migrate they tend to have their wintering grounds not far apart. As it seems, once an individual great grey shrike has found a wintering territory it likes, it will return there subsequently and perhaps even try to defend it against competitors just like a summer territory. Throughout the year, the birds regularly but briefly move through a range up to three times larger than their territory; this is tolerated by territory owners in winter more easily than in summer, and the parts of Europe where all-year residents and winter visitors co-occur typically have population densities around eight<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (January 2011)">verification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> birds/km<sup>2</sup> (about thirty<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (January 2011)">verification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> per square mile) and occasionally more in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-hf2000p153154_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hf2000p153154-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lanius_excubitor_srokosz_AM.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Lanius_excubitor_srokosz_AM.jpg/170px-Lanius_excubitor_srokosz_AM.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="211" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Lanius_excubitor_srokosz_AM.jpg/255px-Lanius_excubitor_srokosz_AM.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Lanius_excubitor_srokosz_AM.jpg/340px-Lanius_excubitor_srokosz_AM.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1337" data-file-height="1659" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lanius_excubitor_srokosz_AM.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An alert <i>L. e. excubitor</i> perching on a wire in <a href="/wiki/Lasy_Janowskie" class="mw-redirect" title="Lasy Janowskie">Lasy Janowskie</a>, Poland</div></div></div>
<p><span class="anchor" id="Ecology"></span>
Before and after the nesting season, groups of breeding birds will sometimes initiate gatherings; these seem to occur at the boundary of the group's combined range or in the unclaimed land separating it from neighbouring groups. The initiation signal is a conspicuous display flight given by a bird surveying its territory: it spirals tens of meters/yards high into the air, usually briefly does a fluttering hover at the top of the spiral, and then glides down. Group neighbours will respond by performing the same type of flight, and eventually about half the group's members will depart to the meeting location where they will spend several tens of minutes – sometimes more than an hour – chattering, calling, duetting, and excitedly moving about the meeting site (which typically is some small tree or shrubbery). In winter, birds will often assemble in small groups and roost together, particularly to keep warm during the night; this is apparently not initiated with a specific assembly display however.<sup id="cite_ref-hf2000p153_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hf2000p153-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The flight of the great grey shrike is undulating and rather heavy, but its dash is straight and determined. It is, as noted above, also capable of <a href="/wiki/Bird_flight#Hovering" title="Bird flight">hovering</a> flights, which last briefly but may be repeated time after time because of the birds' considerable stamina. It will usually stay low above the ground in flight, approaching perches from below and landing in an upward swoop. In social interactions, birds signal an aggressive stance by a bold upright posture, fanning and then flicking the tail and eventually the wings also as the bird gets more excited. It signals its readiness to strike at an intruder by shifting to a <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_plane" class="mw-redirect" title="Horizontal plane">horizontal</a> pose and fluffing its feathers, raising them into a small <a href="/wiki/Crest_(feathers)" title="Crest (feathers)">crest</a> along the top of the head. Birds appease <a href="/wiki/Conspecific" class="mw-redirect" title="Conspecific">conspecifics</a> by head-turns away from them (if close by), or by imitating the crouching fluttering pose and calls given by <a href="/wiki/Fledgling_(birds)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fledgling (birds)">fledglings</a> <a href="/wiki/Begging_behavior_in_animals" class="mw-redirect" title="Begging behavior in animals">begging for food</a> (if sitting father apart). The <a href="/wiki/Submission" class="mw-redirect" title="Submission">submission</a> gesture to prevent an imminent attack by a conspecific is pointing the <a href="/wiki/Beak" title="Beak">beak</a> straight up.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Fledgelings moult part of their juvenile plumage before their first winter, and the rest in spring. Adults moult on their breeding grounds before going on migration, or before the depth of winter if they are resident. Sometimes adults also seem to moult some feathers before attempting to breed. As moult requires a considerable investment of energy, some significant <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolutionary</a> benefits to offset this are likely. Reducing feather wear and <a href="/wiki/Parasite" class="mw-redirect" title="Parasite">parasite</a> load, moulting can make a bird more physically attractive and healthy, and may thus increase its chance of successful reproduction. The phenomenon is not well understood, however.<sup id="cite_ref-hf2000p152153_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hf2000p152153-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_feeding">Food and feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_grey_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Food and feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Suffolk-stoats.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Suffolk-stoats.jpg/220px-Suffolk-stoats.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="191" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Suffolk-stoats.jpg/330px-Suffolk-stoats.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Suffolk-stoats.jpg/440px-Suffolk-stoats.jpg 2x" data-file-width="625" data-file-height="542" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Suffolk-stoats.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Occasionally, animals as large as a young <a href="/wiki/Stoat" title="Stoat">stoat</a> (<i>Mustela erminea</i>) are killed and eaten by great grey shrikes</div></div></div>
<p>The great grey shrike eats small <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a> and large <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>. To hunt, this bird perches on the topmost branch of a tree, <a href="/wiki/Utility_pole" title="Utility pole">utility pole</a> or similar elevated spot in a characteristic upright stance some metres/yards (at least one and up to 18&#160;m/20&#160;yd) above ground. Alternatively, it may scan the grassland below from flight, essentially staying in one place during prolonged bouts of mainly hovering flight that may last up to 20 minutes. It will drop down in a light glide for <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_animal" title="Terrestrial animal">terrestrial</a> prey or swoop <a href="/wiki/Hawk" title="Hawk">hawk</a>-like on a flying <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insect</a>. Small birds are sometimes caught in flight too, usually by approaching them from below and behind and seizing their feet with the beak. If no prey ventures out in the open, great grey shrikes will rummage through the undergrowth or sit near hiding places and flash their white wing and tail markings to scare small animals into coming out. As noted <a href="#Vocalizations">above</a>, it will sometimes <a href="/wiki/Aggressive_mimicry" title="Aggressive mimicry">mimic</a> songbirds to entice them to come within striking distance.<sup id="cite_ref-hf2000p153_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hf2000p153-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lanius.excubitor5.-.lindsey.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Lanius.excubitor5.-.lindsey.jpg/170px-Lanius.excubitor5.-.lindsey.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Lanius.excubitor5.-.lindsey.jpg/255px-Lanius.excubitor5.-.lindsey.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Lanius.excubitor5.-.lindsey.jpg/340px-Lanius.excubitor5.-.lindsey.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lanius.excubitor5.-.lindsey.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bumblebee" title="Bumblebee">Bumblebee</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Bombus_lucorum" title="Bombus lucorum">Bombus lucorum</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Bombus_terrestris" title="Bombus terrestris">B. terrestris</a></i>) stuck on <a href="/wiki/Barbed_wire" title="Barbed wire">barbed wire</a> in a great grey shrike's "<a href="/wiki/Larder" title="Larder">larder</a>"</div></div></div>
<p>Typically, at least half the prey <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> is made up from small <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Cricetidae" title="Cricetidae">Cricetidae</a> (<a href="/wiki/Vole" title="Vole">voles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lemming" title="Lemming">lemmings</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Murinae" title="Murinae">Murinae</a> (Eurasian mice and sometimes young Eurasian <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a>). <a href="/wiki/Shrew" title="Shrew">Shrews</a>, songbirds, <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toad" title="Toad">toads</a> (typically as <a href="/wiki/Tadpole" title="Tadpole">tadpoles</a>) make up most of the remaining vertebrate prey. Birds are generally of little importance however, except in spring when male songbirds are engaged in <a href="/wiki/Courtship_display" title="Courtship display">courtship display</a> and often rather oblivious of their surroundings, in late summer when inexperienced <a href="/wiki/Fledgling_(birds)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fledgling (birds)">fledglings</a> abound, and in winter when most small mammals <a href="/wiki/Hibernate" class="mw-redirect" title="Hibernate">hibernate</a>. Occasionally <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newt" title="Newt">newts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salamander" title="Salamander">salamanders</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> are eaten. Prey animals may exceptionally be almost as large as the birds themselves, for example chicks of the <a href="/wiki/Willow_ptarmigan" title="Willow ptarmigan">willow ptarmigan</a> (<i>Lagopus lagopus</i>) or a young <a href="/wiki/Stoat" title="Stoat">stoat</a>. Large <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> are the second-most important prey by quantity, though not by biomass; in the latter respect they are only a bit more important than birds, except as food for <a href="/wiki/Nestling" class="mw-redirect" title="Nestling">nestlings</a> where they usually form a substantial part of the diet. Most important among invertebrate prey are <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crickets" class="mw-redirect" title="Crickets">crickets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bumblebee" title="Bumblebee">bumblebees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">wasps</a>. Invertebrate prey of minor importance are <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scorpion" title="Scorpion">scorpions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crayfish" title="Crayfish">crayfish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isopod" class="mw-redirect" title="Isopod">isopods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oligochaete" class="mw-redirect" title="Oligochaete">oligochaete</a> worms. <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">Carrion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Berries" class="mw-redirect" title="Berries">berries</a> are rarely if ever eaten; though it might occasionally plunder songbird nests this is not well documented and it is not known to eat <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hf2000p153154_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hf2000p153154-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Antczak_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Antczak-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Prey is killed by hitting it with the hooked beak, aiming for the skull in vertebrates. If too large to swallow in one or a few chunks, it is transported to a feeding site by carrying it in the beak or (if too large) in the feet. The feet are not suited for tearing up prey, however. It is rather impaled upon a sharp point – <a href="/wiki/Thorns,_spines,_and_prickles" title="Thorns, spines, and prickles">thorns</a> or the barbs of <a href="/wiki/Barbed_wire" title="Barbed wire">barbed wire</a> – or wedged firmly between forking <a href="/wiki/Branch" title="Branch">branches</a>. Thus secured, the food can be ripped into bite-sized pieces with the beak. Orthoptera that the birds have recognized as containing noxious chemicals are left impaled in the larder for several days, until the chemicals that usually deter predators have been degraded. Great grey shrikes have also been observed to impale <a href="/wiki/Common_toad" title="Common toad">common toads</a> (<i>Bufo bufo</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Skinning" title="Skinning">skin</a> them – by ripping open the back skin and pulling it over the head – to avoid contamination of the meat by the toxic skin secretions. Large bones and similar inedible parts of prey animals are usually not ingested, but smaller ones such as tiny bones or the <a href="/wiki/Elytra" class="mw-redirect" title="Elytra">elytra</a> of beetles are eaten and later <a href="/wiki/Regurgitation_(digestion)" title="Regurgitation (digestion)">regurgitated</a> as <a href="/wiki/Pellet_(ornithology)" title="Pellet (ornithology)">pellets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hf2000p153_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hf2000p153-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Antczak_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Antczak-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The basic <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_rate" class="mw-redirect" title="Metabolic rate">metabolic rate</a> of the great grey shrike is around 800 <a href="/wiki/Milliwatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Milliwatt">milliwatts</a>, or somewhat more than 11 milliwatts per gram of <a href="/wiki/Body_mass" class="mw-redirect" title="Body mass">body mass</a>. An adult of this species needs about 50&#160;g (1.8&#160;oz) of prey a day, probably somewhat more in winter. Under most circumstances, this would thus translate to one or two <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>, one or two additional vertebrate prey animals (including rodents), and up to a single vertebrate prey item's worth of invertebrates. Surplus food may be impaled for storage. These "<a href="/wiki/Larder" title="Larder">larders</a>" are typically around 1&#160;m (3.3&#160;ft) above ground and can be found anywhere within the birds' territory, but tend to be rather in the general vicinity of nest sites than far away from them.<sup id="cite_ref-hf2000p153_31-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hf2000p153-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AnAge_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AnAge-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding_and_life_history">Breeding and life history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_grey_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Breeding and life history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Great grey shrikes breed during the summer, typically once per year. In exceptionally good conditions, they raise two broods a year, and if the first <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> is destroyed before hatching they are usually able to produce a second one. Their <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" class="mw-redirect" title="Monogamous">monogamous</a> <a href="/wiki/Pair_bond" title="Pair bond">pair bond</a> is strong during the breeding season and loosens over winter; birds often choose a different mate than the year before. To seek out potential mates, males will venture outside their breeding territories. If a female thus encountered finds a male to her liking, she will visit to see whether they get along well and inspect the nesting sites he can offer. The <a href="/wiki/Courtship_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Courtship (biology)">courtship</a> period is generally longer than in the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_grey_shrike" title="Iberian grey shrike">Iberian grey shrike</a> (<i>L. meridionalis</i>), usually starting about March and lasting to April/May. At first, the female rebuffs the male, only allowing him to feed her. Males give increasingly vocal displays and show off the white markings of the wings in flight and of the tail by fanning it and turning away from the female. He also occasionally turns to sit at a right angle to her. Eventually, the female will join in the male's displays, and the songs will become duets. To feed females and to show off their hunting prowess, males will make their food caches in conspicuous places during this time. When presenting nesting sites, males give the variety of calls described <a href="#Vocalizations">above</a> and jerk their head and fanned tail.<sup id="cite_ref-hf2000p154155_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hf2000p154155-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fieldfare_4.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Fieldfare_4.jpg/220px-Fieldfare_4.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Fieldfare_4.jpg/330px-Fieldfare_4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Fieldfare_4.jpg/440px-Fieldfare_4.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2049" data-file-height="1537" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fieldfare_4.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nesting <a href="/wiki/Fieldfare" title="Fieldfare">fieldfares</a> (<i>Turdus pilaris</i>) and great grey shrikes apparently cooperate to protect their offspring from <a href="/wiki/Predator" class="mw-redirect" title="Predator">predators</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Animal_sexual_behavior" class="mw-redirect" title="Animal sexual behavior">Copulation</a> is typically initiated by the male bringing an attractive prey item to the female. With both giving begging calls, they approach until they are side by side. The male then raises and swings his body left and right a few times, and passes the prey to the female, followed by the actual copulation. The gatherings of neighbour groups (see <a href="#Ecology">above</a>) cease when nesting is underway, and when the eggs are nearly ready to lay, the male guards his partner closely, perching higher than her to watch for threats and frequently feeding her. This apparently ensures her physical well-being rather than preventing <a href="/wiki/Extra-pair_copulation" title="Extra-pair copulation">extra-pair copulations</a>, as neighbouring males will stray through each other's territory to snatch a quick fling with the resident females. In this, they have almost a one-in-three chance of success, and consequently the average grey shrike nest is very likely to contain offspring of more than one male. Females may deposit their eggs in neighbours' nests, but this seems to occur more rarely; in general, mated females are fairly reclusive after their eggs have started developing. A full <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(eggs)" title="Clutch (eggs)">clutch</a> of eggs can be produced by a female in about 10–15 days.<sup id="cite_ref-hf2000p154155_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hf2000p154155-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bird_nest" title="Bird nest">Nests</a> are built in April or May more than 1&#160;m (3.3&#160;ft) above ground in trees. This height varies according to habitat, but while nests have been found almost 40&#160;m (44&#160;yd) up, most are 2–16&#160;m above ground. Presence of <a href="/wiki/Mistletoe" title="Mistletoe">mistletoes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vine" title="Vine">vines</a> like <a href="/wiki/Common_ivy" class="mw-redirect" title="Common ivy">common ivy</a> (<i>Hedera helix</i>) on side branches near the trunk (where nests are preferentially built) will make a tree markedly more attractive. <a href="/wiki/Fieldfare" title="Fieldfare">Fieldfares</a> (<i>Turdus pilaris</i>) nesting in the vicinity will also increase the desirability of nest sites to great grey shrikes, which moreover often refuse to prey upon these <a href="/wiki/Thrush_(bird)" title="Thrush (bird)">thrushes</a>' nestlings though the opportunity is there. Apparently, the two species are more efficient in spotting potential nest predators – in particular <a href="/wiki/Corvid" class="mw-redirect" title="Corvid">corvids</a> – early on and <a href="/wiki/Mobbing_behaviour" class="mw-redirect" title="Mobbing behaviour">mobbing</a> them off cooperatively than either is on its own. Otherwise, there is no clear preference for particular <a href="/wiki/Taxa" class="mw-redirect" title="Taxa">taxa</a> of nesting trees, provided they are sufficiently dense. <a href="/wiki/Conifer" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifer">Conifers</a> seem to have become more popular with European <i>L. excubitor</i> in recent decades, but a diversity of <a href="/wiki/Deciduous_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Deciduous tree">deciduous trees</a> is used just as well. Far more rarely, large and especially <a href="/wiki/Thorns,_spines,_and_prickles" title="Thorns, spines, and prickles">thorny</a> shrubs are used for nesting. The actual nesting site is chosen by the male; the courtship visits of the female are mainly to form and strengthen the pair bond. Also, though the partners build the nest together, the male collects most of the nesting material. The <a href="/wiki/Cup_nest" class="mw-redirect" title="Cup nest">cup nest</a> is quite sizable, measuring 20–28&#160;cm (7.9–11.0&#160;in) in outer diameter. Its body is constructed of coarse vegetable material – mainly large twigs and chunks of <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">moss</a>, though bits of fabric and rubbish may be added. The interior cup is 8–12&#160;cm (3.1–4.7&#160;in) in diameter and 10–15&#160;cm (3.9–5.9&#160;in) deep; it is lined with fine twigs and roots, <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichen</a>, hair and feathers. Building a nest from scratch takes a pair one to two weeks, but if nests of the previous year in good locations remain usable, they are repaired rather than discarded.<sup id="cite_ref-hf2000p155_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hf2000p155-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg/170px-Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="239" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg/255px-Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg/340px-Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg 2x" data-file-width="446" data-file-height="628" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Reed_warbler_cuckoo.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Unlike <a href="/wiki/Reed-warbler" class="mw-redirect" title="Reed-warbler">reed-warblers</a> (<i>Acrocephalus</i>), the great grey shrike seems to have <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_arms_race" title="Evolutionary arms race">out-evolved</a> the <a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">common cuckoo</a> (<i>Cuculus canorus</i>) for the time being</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lanius_excubitor_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.212.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Lanius_excubitor_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.212.jpg/220px-Lanius_excubitor_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.212.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="248" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Lanius_excubitor_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.212.jpg/330px-Lanius_excubitor_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.212.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Lanius_excubitor_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.212.jpg/440px-Lanius_excubitor_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.212.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3874" data-file-height="4359" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lanius_excubitor_MHNT.ZOO.2010.11.212.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eggs from <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9nil-en-Xaintois" title="Ménil-en-Xaintois">Ménil-en-Xaintois</a> (France) at <a href="/wiki/MHNT" class="mw-redirect" title="MHNT">MHNT</a></div></div></div>
<p>Laying usually takes place in May. The clutch numbers three to nine eggs, typically around seven, with North American clutches tending to be larger on average than European ones. If a second clutch is produced in one breeding season, it is smaller than the first one. The eggs have a white background colour, usually with a grey hue and sometimes with a blue one; they are patterned with blotches of yellowish- to reddish-brown and purplish-grey, often denser around the blunt end. They measure around 26&#160;mm (1.0&#160;in) in length and 19.5&#160;mm (0.77&#160;in) in width. <a href="/wiki/Avian_incubation" class="mw-redirect" title="Avian incubation">Incubation</a> takes around 16 days but may be closer to three weeks for large clutches; it is generally done only by the female. While the male may briefly take over incubating, his task during this time is to provide food. The <a href="/wiki/Altricial" class="mw-redirect" title="Altricial">altricial</a> <a href="/wiki/Nestling" class="mw-redirect" title="Nestling">nestlings</a> hatch naked, blind and pink-skinned, weighing c. 4&#160;g (0.14&#160;oz); their skin turns darker after a few days. The inside of their beak is pink and they probably lack spots or other prominent marks; the wattles at the corners of the mouth are yellow as in many <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerines</a>. As the nestlings grow, the female broods them, and later on assists in providing food. The young <a href="/wiki/Fledge" title="Fledge">fledge</a> after 2–3 weeks, typically in late June or early July; they become independent of their parents about 3–6 weeks later. Sometimes, a parent will single out particular fledglings (possibly the weakest ones) and focus their care and feeding on these during this time. Other adults have occasionally been recorded assisting in feeding a pair's offspring; it is not clear whether these <a href="/wiki/Helpers_at_the_nest" title="Helpers at the nest">helpers at the nest</a> are offspring of previous years, or unrelated non-breeding "floaters" or breeding neighbours.<sup id="cite_ref-AnAge_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AnAge-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hf2000p155_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hf2000p155-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Common_cuckoo" title="Common cuckoo">Common cuckoos</a> (<i>Cuculus canorus</i>) have been noted as regular <a href="/wiki/Brood_parasite" title="Brood parasite">brood parasites</a> of <i>L. e. excubitor</i> in the past; for reasons unknown this has ceased since the late 1970s or so. It may well be that the cuckoo's <a href="/wiki/Gens_(behaviour)" title="Gens (behaviour)">gens</a> laying eggs similar to those of the great grey shrike has become <a href="/wiki/Extinct" class="mw-redirect" title="Extinct">extinct</a>. Among <a href="/wiki/Predator" class="mw-redirect" title="Predator">predators</a> of eggs and nestlings, <a href="/wiki/Corvid" class="mw-redirect" title="Corvid">corvids</a> (Corvidae) – extremely close relatives of the shrikes (Laniidae) as it happens<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> – are most significant.<sup id="cite_ref-hf2000p155_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hf2000p155-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Usually more than half of all nests manage to hatch at least one young, and around three-quarters of all eggs laid hatch, suggesting that if eggs are lost before hatching, it usually is the entire clutch. Half to three-quarters of the hatched young successfully fledge under most circumstances. They will become sexually mature in their first spring and often attempt to breed right away. On average, great grey shrikes get a chance at four breeding attempts during their life, with most birds in the wild getting eaten by a <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">bird of prey</a> or <a href="/wiki/Carnivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Carnivorous">carnivorous</a> <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammal</a> or dying of other causes before the end of their fifth winter. Raptorial birds are the main threat to shrikes after fledging, with regular predators including species as small as <a href="/wiki/Little_owl" title="Little owl">little owls</a> (which are close to the same size as the shrike).<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The maximum documented lifespan, however, is 12 years.<sup id="cite_ref-AnAge_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AnAge-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_grey_shrike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As remarked <a href="#Distribution_and_habitat">above</a>, the great grey shrike has apparently become <a href="/wiki/Extinct" class="mw-redirect" title="Extinct">extinct</a> as a breeding bird in Switzerland and the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>. Overall, its stocks seem to be declining in the European part of its range since the 1970s. In North America, the populations seem to have been stable by contrast, except in the east. The increase and decline seem to be reactions to changing land use, with an increase as the number of agricultural workers declined after World War II and land fell <a href="/wiki/Fallow" title="Fallow">fallow</a>, declining again when land consolidation (see e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Flurbereinigung" title="Flurbereinigung">Flurbereinigung</a></i>) had seriously depleted the number of <a href="/wiki/Hedgerow" class="mw-redirect" title="Hedgerow">hedgerows</a> and similar elevated growth formerly common amidst the agricultural landscape. For such a <a href="/wiki/Predator" class="mw-redirect" title="Predator">predatory</a> bird, the indiscriminate use of <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a> (which will accumulate in adult carnivores and inhibit breeding success) around the 1960s probably had a detrimental influence on stocks too.<sup id="cite_ref-hf2000p152_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hf2000p152-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Altogether, the great grey shrike is common and widespread and not considered a <a href="/wiki/Threatened_species" title="Threatened species">threatened species</a> by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> (though they still include <i><a href="/wiki/Lanius_meridionalis" class="mw-redirect" title="Lanius meridionalis">L. meridionalis</a></i> in <i>L. excubitor</i>). Wherever it occurs, its numbers are usually many hundreds or even thousands per country. Its stronghold is the region around Sweden, where at least almost 20,000, perhaps as many as 50,000 were believed to live in the late 20th century. However, in some countries it is not robustly established; in <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> only a few hundred are found, with less than 200 in Belgium and some more or less than 100 in <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a>, respectively. The few dozen in the Netherlands and the 10 birds or so in Denmark might disappear because of a few years of adverse circumstances. By contrast, in <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a> plentiful high-quality habitat is found; though the number of great grey shrikes in this tiny country is necessarily limited, the average <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> there is 25 times as high as in Lithuania.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hf2000p152_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hf2000p152-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
