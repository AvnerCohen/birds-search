<p>The <b>Bearded Reedling</b> (<i>Panurus biarmicus</i>) is a small, <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexually dimorphic</a> <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed-bed</a> <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a>. It is frequently known as the <b>Bearded Tit</b>, due to some similarities to the <a href="/wiki/Long-tailed_Tit" title="Long-tailed Tit">Long-tailed Tit</a>, or the <b>Bearded Parrotbill</b>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_Reedling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span></h2>
<p>The Bearded Reedling was placed with the <a href="/wiki/Parrotbill" title="Parrotbill">parrotbills</a> in the family Paradoxornithidae, after they were removed from the <a href="/w/index.php?title=True_tit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="True tit (page does not exist)">true tits</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> Paridae. However, according to more recent research, it is actually a unique <a href="/wiki/Songbird" title="Songbird">songbird</a> - no other living <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> seems to be particularly closely related to it. Thus, it seems that the <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" title="Monotypic" class="mw-redirect">monotypic</a> family <b>Panuridae</b> must again be recognized.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_Reedling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>This is a small orange-brown bird with a long tail and an undulating flight. The male has a grey head and black moustaches (not a beard). Flocks often betray their presence in a reedbed by their characteristic "ping" call.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_Reedling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitat and distribution">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_distribution">Habitat and distribution</span></h2>
<p>This species is a wetland specialist, breeding colonially in large <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed beds</a> by lakes or swamps. It eats reed <a href="/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">aphids</a> in summer, and <a href="/wiki/Phragmites" title="Phragmites">reed</a> seeds in winter, its digestive system changing to cope with the very different seasonal diets.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Bearded Reedling is a species of <a href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate" class="mw-redirect">temperate</a> <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. It is resident, and most birds do not <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> other than eruptive or cold weather movements. It is vulnerable to hard winters, which may kill many birds. The <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> population of about 500 pairs is largely confined to the south and east with a small population in <a href="/wiki/Leighton_Moss" title="Leighton Moss" class="mw-redirect">Leighton Moss</a> in north <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> a handful of pairs breed in <a href="/wiki/County_Wexford" title="County Wexford">County Wexford</a>. The largest single population in Great Britain is to be found in the reedbeds at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/River_Tay" title="River Tay">River Tay</a> in <a href="/wiki/Perth_and_Kinross" title="Perth and Kinross">Perth and Kinross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, where there may be in excess of 250 pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
