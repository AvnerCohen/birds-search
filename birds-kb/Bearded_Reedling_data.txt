<p>The <b>bearded reedling</b> (<i>Panurus biarmicus</i>) is a small, <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexually dimorphic</a> <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed-bed</a> <a href="/wiki/Passerine" title="Passerine">passerine</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a>. It is frequently known as the <b>bearded tit</b>, due to some similarities to the <a href="/wiki/Long-tailed_tit" title="Long-tailed tit">long-tailed tit</a>, or the <b>bearded parrotbill</b>. It is the only species in the family <b>Panuridae</b>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_systematics">Taxonomy and systematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_reedling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and systematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The bearded reedling was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in his <a href="/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="10th edition of Systema Naturae">10th edition of <i>Systema Naturae</i></a> in 1758. He placed it in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Parus" title="Parus">Parus</a></i> with the <a href="/wiki/Tit_(bird)" title="Tit (bird)">tits</a>. The species has since been placed with the <a href="/wiki/Parrotbill" title="Parrotbill">parrotbills</a> in the family Paradoxornithidae, after they were removed from the true tits in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a>. More recent research suggests it is a unique <a href="/wiki/Songbird" title="Songbird">songbird</a> – no other living <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> seems to be particularly closely related to it.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The species is now placed in the <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monotypic">monotypic</a> family Panuridae. <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular phylogenetic">Molecular phylogenetic</a> studies have shown that the bearded reedling is most closely related to the lark family <a href="/wiki/Alaudidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Alaudidae">Alaudidae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-oliveros_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oliveros-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The current genus name, <i>Panurus</i>, is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>panu</i>, "exceedingly", and <i>ουρά</i>, "tail". The specific <i>biarmicus</i> is from "Biarmia", a Latinised form of <a href="/wiki/Bjarmaland" title="Bjarmaland">Bjarmaland</a>, formerly part of what is now the <a href="/wiki/Arkhangelsk_Oblast" title="Arkhangelsk Oblast">Arkhangelsk Oblast</a> area of Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-job_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-job-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_reedling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is a small orange-brown bird, L 16.5&#160;cm, with a long tail and an undulating flight. The bill is yellow-orange. The male has a grey head and black moustaches (not a beard); the lower tail coverts are also black. The female is generally paler, with no black moustache. Flocks often betray their presence in a reedbed by their characteristic "ping" call.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearded_reedling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Panurus_biarmicus_MWNH_1404.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Panurus_biarmicus_MWNH_1404.JPG/220px-Panurus_biarmicus_MWNH_1404.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="319" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Panurus_biarmicus_MWNH_1404.JPG/330px-Panurus_biarmicus_MWNH_1404.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Panurus_biarmicus_MWNH_1404.JPG/440px-Panurus_biarmicus_MWNH_1404.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1342" data-file-height="1947" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Panurus_biarmicus_MWNH_1404.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egg, Collection <a href="/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden" title="Museum Wiesbaden">Museum Wiesbaden</a></div></div></div>
<p>This species is a wetland specialist, breeding colonially in large <a href="/wiki/Reed_bed" title="Reed bed">reed beds</a> by lakes or swamps. It eats reed <a href="/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">aphids</a> in summer, and <a href="/wiki/Phragmites" title="Phragmites">reed</a> seeds in winter, its digestive system changing to cope with the very different seasonal diets.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The bearded reedling is a species of <a href="/wiki/Temperate" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate">temperate</a> Europe and across the <a href="/wiki/Palearctic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palearctic">Palearctic</a>. It is resident, and most birds do not <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> other than eruptive or cold weather movements. It is vulnerable to hard winters, which may kill many birds. The English population of about 500 pairs is largely confined to the south and east with a small population in <a href="/wiki/Leighton_Moss" class="mw-redirect" title="Leighton Moss">Leighton Moss</a> in north <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> there  is a small but growing population mainly in  <a href="/wiki/County_Wexford" title="County Wexford">County Wexford</a>. The largest single population in Great Britain is to be found in the reedbeds at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/River_Tay" title="River Tay">River Tay</a> in <a href="/wiki/Perth_and_Kinross" title="Perth and Kinross">Perth and Kinross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, where there may be in excess of 250 pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
